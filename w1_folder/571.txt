<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Boiler (power generation)</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Boiler_(disambiguation)" title="Boiler (disambiguation)">boiler (disambiguation)</a>.</div>

<p>A <b>boiler</b> or <b>steam generator</b> is a device used to create <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a> by applying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_energy" title="Heat energy">heat energy</a> to <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>.  Although the definitions are somewhat flexible, it can be said that older steam generators were commonly termed <i>boilers</i> and worked at low to medium pressure (7–2,000 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">kPa</a> or 1–290 <a href="/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch" title="Pounds per square inch">psi</a>) but, at pressures above this, it is more usual to speak of a <i>steam generator</i>.
</p><p>A boiler or steam generator is used wherever a source of steam is required. The form and size depends on the application: mobile <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engines</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Steam_locomotive" title="Steam locomotive">steam locomotives</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portable_engine" title="Portable engine">portable engines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Traction_engine" title="Traction engine">steam-powered road vehicles</a> typically use a smaller boiler that forms an integral part of the vehicle; <a href="/wiki/Stationary_steam_engine" title="Stationary steam engine">stationary steam engines</a>, industrial installations and power stations will usually have a larger separate steam generating facility connected to the point-of-use by piping. A notable exception is the steam-powered <a href="/wiki/Fireless_locomotive" title="Fireless locomotive">fireless locomotive</a>, where separately-generated steam is transferred to a receiver (tank) on the locomotive.
</p>

<h2><span id="Steam_generator_.28component_of_prime_mover.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Steam_generator_(component_of_prime_mover)">Steam generator (component of prime mover)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boiler_(power_generation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Steam generator (component of prime mover)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:305px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Steam_Generator.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="290" data-file-width="303" height="290" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e2/Steam_Generator.png" width="303"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption">Type of <b>steam generator</b> unit used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_power_plant" title="Fossil fuel power plant">coal-fired power plants</a></div></div></div>
<p>The steam generator or boiler is an integral component of a <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engine</a> when considered as a <a href="/wiki/Prime_mover_(locomotive)" title="Prime mover (locomotive)">prime mover</a>.  However it needs to be treated separately, as to some extent a variety of generator types can be combined with a variety of engine units. A boiler incorporates a <a href="/wiki/Firebox_(steam_engine)" title="Firebox (steam engine)">firebox</a> or <a href="/wiki/Furnace" title="Furnace">furnace</a> in order to burn the fuel and generate <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a>.  The generated heat is transferred to water to make <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a>, the process of <a href="/wiki/Boiling" title="Boiling">boiling</a>. This produces <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturated_steam" title="Saturated steam">saturated steam</a> at a rate which can vary according to the pressure above the boiling water. The higher the furnace temperature, the faster the steam production. The saturated steam thus produced can then either be used immediately to produce power via a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turbogenerator" title="Turbogenerator">turbine and alternator</a>, or else may be further <a href="/wiki/Superheater" title="Superheater">superheated</a> to a higher temperature; this notably reduces suspended water content making a given volume of steam produce more work and creates a greater temperature gradient, which helps reduce the potential to form <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensation</a>. Any remaining heat in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combustion_gas" title="Combustion gas">combustion gases</a> can then either be evacuated or made to pass through an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economiser" title="Economiser">economiser</a>, the role of which is to warm the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feed_water" title="Feed water">feed water</a> before it reaches the boiler.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Boiler_types">Boiler types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boiler_(power_generation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Boiler types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Fire-tube_boiler" title="Fire-tube boiler">Fire-tube boiler</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Haycock_and_wagon_top_boilers">Haycock and wagon top boilers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boiler_(power_generation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Haycock and wagon top boilers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newcomen_engine" title="Newcomen engine">Newcomen engine</a> of 1712, the boiler was little more than large brewer's <a href="/wiki/Kettle" title="Kettle">kettle</a> installed beneath the power cylinder. Because the engine's power was derived from the <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a> produced by condensation of the steam, the requirement was for large volumes of steam at very low pressure hardly more than 1 <a href="/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch" title="Pounds per square inch">psi</a> (6.9 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">kPa</a>) The whole boiler was set into <a href="/wiki/Brickwork" title="Brickwork">brickwork</a> which retained some heat. A voluminous coal fire was lit on a grate beneath the slightly dished pan which gave a very small heating surface; there was therefore a great deal of heat wasted up the <a href="/wiki/Chimney" title="Chimney">chimney</a>. In later models, notably by <a href="/wiki/John_Smeaton" title="John Smeaton">John Smeaton</a>, heating surface was considerably increased by making the gases heat the boiler sides, passing through a <a href="/wiki/Flue" title="Flue">flue</a>. Smeaton further lengthened the path of the gases by means of a spiral labyrinth flue beneath the boiler. These under-fired boilers were used in various forms throughout the 18th Century. Some were of round section (haycock). A longer version on a rectangular plan was developed around 1775 by Boulton and Watt (wagon top boiler). This is what is today known as a three-pass boiler, the fire heating the underside, the gases then passing through a central square-section tubular flue and finally around the boiler sides.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cylindrical_fire-tube_boiler">Cylindrical fire-tube boiler</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boiler_(power_generation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cylindrical fire-tube boiler">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Flued_boiler" title="Flued boiler">Flued boiler</a></div>
<p>An early proponent of the cylindrical form was the British engineer John Blakey, who proposed his design in 1774.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Another early proponent was the American engineer, <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Evans" title="Oliver Evans">Oliver Evans</a>, who rightly recognised that the cylindrical form was the best from the point of view of mechanical resistance and towards the end of the 18th Century began to incorporate it into his projects.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Probably inspired by the writings on Leupold's "high-pressure" engine scheme that appeared in encyclopaedic works from 1725, Evans favoured "strong steam" i.e. non condensing engines in which the steam pressure alone drove the <a href="/wiki/Piston" title="Piston">piston</a> and was then exhausted to atmosphere. The advantage of strong steam as he saw it was that more work could be done by smaller volumes of steam; this enabled all the components to be reduced in size and engines could be adapted to transport and small installations. To this end he developed a long cylindrical <a href="/wiki/Wrought_iron" title="Wrought iron">wrought iron</a> horizontal boiler into which was incorporated a single fire tube, at one end of which was placed the <a href="/wiki/Grate_firing" title="Grate firing">fire grate</a>. The gas flow was then reversed into a passage or flue beneath the boiler barrel, then divided to return through side flues to join again at the chimney (Columbian engine boiler). Evans incorporated his cylindrical boiler into several engines, both stationary and mobile. Due to space and weight considerations the latter were one-pass exhausting directly from fire tube to chimney. Another proponent of "strong steam" at that time was the Cornishman, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Trevithick" title="Richard Trevithick">Richard Trevithick</a>. His boilers worked at 40–50 psi (276–345 kPa) and were at first of hemispherical then cylindrical form. From 1804 onwards Trevithick produced a small two-pass or return flue boiler for semi-portable and locomotive engines. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cornish_boiler" title="Cornish boiler">Cornish boiler</a> developed around 1812 by Richard Trevithick was both stronger and more efficient than the simple boilers which preceded it. It consisted of a cylindrical water tank around 27 feet (8.2 m) long and 7 feet (2.1 m) in diameter, and had a coal fire grate placed at one end of a single cylindrical tube about three feet wide which passed longitudinally inside the tank. The fire was tended from one end and the hot gases from it travelled along the tube and out of the other end, to be circulated back along flues running along the outside then a third time beneath the boiler barrel before being expelled into a chimney. This was later improved upon by another 3-pass boiler, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lancashire_boiler" title="Lancashire boiler">Lancashire boiler</a> which had a pair of furnaces in separate tubes side-by-side. This was an important improvement since each furnace could be stoked at different times, allowing one to be cleaned while the other was operating.
</p><p>Railway locomotive boilers were usually of the 1-pass type, although in early days, 2-pass "return flue" boilers were common, especially with locomotives built by <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Hackworth" title="Timothy Hackworth">Timothy Hackworth</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Multi-tube_boilers">Multi-tube boilers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boiler_(power_generation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Multi-tube boilers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A significant step forward came in France in 1828 when <a href="/wiki/Marc_Seguin" title="Marc Seguin">Marc Seguin</a> devised a two-pass boiler of which the second pass was formed by a bundle of multiple tubes. A similar design with natural induction used for marine purposes was the popular <a href="/wiki/Scotch_marine_boiler" title="Scotch marine boiler">Scotch marine boiler</a>.
</p><p>Prior to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainhill_trials" title="Rainhill trials">Rainhill trials</a> of 1829 <a href="/wiki/Henry_Booth" title="Henry Booth">Henry Booth</a>, treasurer of the <a href="/wiki/Liverpool_and_Manchester_Railway" title="Liverpool and Manchester Railway">Liverpool and Manchester Railway</a> suggested to <a href="/wiki/George_Stephenson" title="George Stephenson">George Stephenson</a>, a scheme for a multi-tube one-pass horizontal boiler made up of two units: a <a href="/wiki/Firebox_(steam_engine)" title="Firebox (steam engine)">firebox</a> surrounded by water spaces and a boiler barrel consisting of two telescopic rings inside which were mounted 25 copper tubes; the tube bundle occupied much of the water space in the barrel and vastly improved <a href="/wiki/Heat_transfer" title="Heat transfer">heat transfer</a>. Old George immediately communicated the scheme to his son Robert and this was the boiler used on <a href="/wiki/Stephenson%27s_Rocket" title="Stephenson's Rocket">Stephenson's Rocket</a>, outright winner of the trial. The design formed the basis for all subsequent Stephensonian-built locomotives, being immediately taken up by other constructors; this pattern of fire-tube boiler has been built ever since.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structural_resistance">Structural resistance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boiler_(power_generation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Structural resistance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 1712 boiler was assembled from riveted copper plates with a domed top made of lead in the first examples. Later boilers were made of small wrought iron plates riveted together. The problem was producing big enough plates, so that even pressures of around 50 <a href="/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch" title="Pounds per square inch">psi</a> (344.7 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">kPa</a>) were not absolutely safe, nor was the cast iron hemispherical boiler initially used by Richard Trevithick. This construction with small plates persisted until the 1820s, when larger plates became feasible and could be rolled into a cylindrical form with just one butt-jointed seam reinforced by a <a href="/wiki/Gusset_plate" title="Gusset plate">gusset</a>; Timothy Hackworth's <i>Sans Pareil 11</i> of 1849 had a longitudinal welded seam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Welded construction for locomotive boilers was extremely slow to take hold.
</p><p>Once-through monotubular water tube boilers as used by Doble, Lamont and Pritchard are capable of withstanding considerable pressure and of releasing it without danger of explosion.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Combustion">Combustion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boiler_(power_generation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Combustion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">Combustion</a></div>
<p>The source of heat for a boiler is combustion of any of several fuels, such as <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oil" title="Oil">oil</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>. <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">Nuclear fission</a> is also used as a heat source for generating steam. <a href="/wiki/Heat_recovery_steam_generator" title="Heat recovery steam generator">Heat recovery steam generators</a> (HRSGs) use the heat rejected from other processes such as <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbines</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solid_fuel_firing">Solid fuel firing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boiler_(power_generation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Solid fuel firing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In order to create optimum burning characteristics of the <a href="/wiki/Fire" title="Fire">fire</a>, air needs to be supplied both through the grate, and above the fire. Most boilers now depend on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanical_draft" title="Mechanical draft">mechanical draft</a> equipment rather than natural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chimney_draught" title="Chimney draught">draught</a>. This is because natural draught is subject to outside air conditions and temperature of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flue_gases" title="Flue gases">flue gases</a> leaving the furnace, as well as chimney height. All these factors make effective draught hard to attain and therefore make mechanical draught equipment much more economical. There are three types of mechanical draught: 
</p>
<ol><li>Induced draught: This is obtained one of three ways, the first being the "stack effect" of a heated chimney, in which the <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">flue gas</a> is less dense than the ambient air surrounding the boiler. The denser column of ambient air forces combustion air into and through the boiler. The second method is through use of a steam jet. The steam jet or ejector oriented in the direction of flue gas flow induces flue gases into the stack and allows for a greater flue gas velocity increasing the overall draught in the furnace. This method was common on steam driven locomotives which could not have tall chimneys. The third method is by simply using an induced draught fan (ID fan) which sucks flue gases out of the furnace and up the stack. Almost all induced draught furnaces have a negative pressure.</li>
<li>Forced draught: draught is obtained by forcing air into the furnace by means of a fan (FD fan) and duct-work. Air is often passed through an air heater; which, as the name suggests, heats the air going into the furnace in order to increase the overall efficiency of the boiler. Dampers are used to control the quantity of air admitted to the furnace. Forced draught furnaces usually have a positive pressure.</li>
<li>Balanced draught: Balanced draught is obtained through use of both induced and forced draft. This is more common with larger boilers where the flue gases have to travel a long distance through many boiler passes. The induced draft fan works in conjunction with the forced draft fan allowing the furnace pressure to be maintained slightly below atmospheric.</li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Firetube_boiler">Firetube boiler</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boiler_(power_generation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Firetube boiler">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fire-tube_boiler" title="Fire-tube boiler">Fire-tube boiler</a></div>
<p>The next stage in the process is to boil water and make steam. The goal is to make the heat flow as completely as possible from the heat source to the water. The water is confined in a restricted space heated by the fire. The steam produced has lower density than the water and therefore will accumulate at the highest level in the vessel; its temperature will remain at boiling point and will only increase as pressure increases. Steam in this state (in equilibrium with the liquid water which is being evaporated within the boiler) is named "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturated_steam" title="Saturated steam">saturated steam</a>". For example, saturated steam at atmospheric pressure boils at 100 °C (212 °F).  Saturated steam taken from the boiler may contain entrained water droplets, however a well designed boiler will supply virtually "dry" saturated steam, with very little entrained water.  Continued heating of the saturated steam will bring the steam to a "superheated" state, where the steam is heated to a temperature above the saturation temperature, and no liquid water can exist under this condition. Most reciprocating steam engines of the 19th century used saturated steam, however modern steam power plants universally use <a href="/wiki/Superheated_steam" title="Superheated steam">superheated steam</a> which allows higher <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steam_cycle" title="Steam cycle">steam cycle</a> efficiency.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Superheater">Superheater</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boiler_(power_generation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Superheater">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Superheater" title="Superheater">Superheater</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Superheater.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1531" data-file-width="2300" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Superheater.jpg/250px-Superheater.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Superheater.jpg/375px-Superheater.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Superheater.jpg/500px-Superheater.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Superheater.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A superheated boiler on a steam locomotive.</div></div></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L.D._Porta" title="L.D. Porta">L.D. Porta</a> gives the following equation determining the efficiency of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steam_locomotives" title="Steam locomotives">steam locomotive</a>, applicable to <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engines</a> of all kinds: power (kW) = steam Production (kg h<sup>−1</sup>)/Specific steam consumption (kg/kW h).
</p><p>A greater quantity of steam can be generated from a given quantity of water by superheating it. As the fire is burning at a much higher temperature than the saturated steam it produces, far more heat can be transferred to the once-formed steam by superheating it and turning the water droplets suspended therein into more steam and greatly reducing water consumption.
</p><p>The superheater works like coils on an <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">air conditioning</a> unit, however to a different end.  The steam piping (with steam flowing through it) is directed through the flue gas path in the boiler furnace.  This area typically is between 1,300–1,600 <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">°C</a> (2,372–2,912 <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">°F</a>). Some superheaters are radiant type (absorb heat by <a href="/wiki/Thermal_radiation" title="Thermal radiation">thermal radiation</a>), others are <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a> type (absorb heat via a fluid i.e. gas) and some are a combination of the two.  So whether by convection or radiation the extreme heat in the boiler furnace/flue gas path will also heat the superheater steam piping and the steam within as well.  While the temperature of the steam in the superheater is raised, the pressure of the steam is not: the <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">turbine</a> or moving <a href="/wiki/Piston" title="Piston">pistons</a> offer a "continuously expanding space" and the pressure remains the same as that of the boiler.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The process of superheating steam is most importantly designed to remove all droplets entrained in the steam to prevent damage to the turbine blading and/or associated piping. Superheating the steam expands the volume of steam, which allows a given quantity (by weight) of steam to generate more power.
</p><p>When the totality of the droplets is eliminated, the steam is said to be in a superheated state.
</p><p>In a Stephensonian firetube locomotive boiler, this entails routing the saturated steam through small diameter pipes suspended inside large diameter firetubes putting them in contact with the hot gases exiting the firebox; the saturated steam flows backwards from the wet header towards the firebox, then forwards again to the dry header. Superheating only began to be generally adopted for locomotives around the year 1900 due to problems of overheating of and <a href="/wiki/Lubrication" title="Lubrication">lubrication</a> of the moving parts in the cylinders and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steam_chest" title="Steam chest">steam chests</a>.
Many firetube boilers heat water until it boils, and then the steam is used at saturation temperature in other words the temperature of the boiling point of water at a given pressure (saturated steam); this still contains a large proportion of water in suspension. Saturated steam can and has been directly used by an engine, but as the suspended water cannot expand and do work and work implies temperature drop, much of the working fluid is wasted along with the fuel expended to produce it.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_tube_boiler">Water tube boiler</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boiler_(power_generation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Water tube boiler">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Water-tube_boiler" title="Water-tube boiler">Water-tube boiler</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Steam_Boiler_3_english.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="675" data-file-width="450" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Steam_Boiler_3_english.png/150px-Steam_Boiler_3_english.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Steam_Boiler_3_english.png/225px-Steam_Boiler_3_english.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Steam_Boiler_3_english.png/300px-Steam_Boiler_3_english.png 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Steam_Boiler_3_english.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diagram of a water-tube boiler.</div></div></div>
<p>Another way to rapidly produce steam is to feed the water under pressure into a tube or tubes surrounded by the combustion gases. The earliest example of this was developed by <a href="/wiki/Goldsworthy_Gurney" title="Goldsworthy Gurney">Goldsworthy Gurney</a> in the late 1820s for use in steam road carriages. This boiler was ultra-compact and light in weight and this arrangement has since become the norm for marine and stationary applications. The tubes frequently have a large number of bends and sometimes fins to maximize the surface area. This type of boiler is generally preferred in high pressure applications since the high pressure water/steam is contained within narrow pipes which can contain the pressure with a thinner wall. It can however be susceptible to damage by vibration in surface transport appliances. In a <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">cast iron</a> sectional boiler, sometimes called a "pork chop boiler" the water is contained inside cast iron sections. These sections are mechanically assembled on site to create the finished boiler.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Supercritical_steam_generator">Supercritical steam generator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boiler_(power_generation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Supercritical steam generator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Turmkessel02.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="465" data-file-width="484" height="240" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Turmkessel02.png/250px-Turmkessel02.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Turmkessel02.png/375px-Turmkessel02.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/59/Turmkessel02.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Turmkessel02.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Supercritical steam generator - note the absence of a boiler drum.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_steam_generator" title="Supercritical steam generator">Supercritical steam generator</a></div>
<p>Supercritical steam generators are frequently used for the production of <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">electric power</a>. They operate <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_fluid" title="Supercritical fluid">at supercritical</a> pressure. In contrast to a "subcritical boiler", a supercritical steam generator operates at such a high pressure (over 3,200 <a href="/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch" title="Pounds per square inch">psi</a> or 22.06 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">MPa</a>) that actual boiling ceases to occur, the boiler has no liquid water - steam separation. There is no generation of steam bubbles within the water, because the pressure is above the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Critical_temperature_and_pressure" title="Critical temperature and pressure">critical pressure</a> at which steam bubbles can form. It passes below the critical point as it does work in a high-pressure turbine and enters the generator's <a href="/wiki/Condenser_(heat_transfer)" title="Condenser (heat transfer)">condenser</a>. This results in slightly less fuel use and therefore less <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> production. The term "boiler" should not be used for a supercritical pressure steam generator, as no "boiling" actually occurs in this device.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_treatment">Water treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boiler_(power_generation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Water treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cation_anion_ion_exchange.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2373" data-file-width="3208" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Cation_anion_ion_exchange.jpg/220px-Cation_anion_ion_exchange.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Cation_anion_ion_exchange.jpg/330px-Cation_anion_ion_exchange.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Cation_anion_ion_exchange.jpg/440px-Cation_anion_ion_exchange.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cation_anion_ion_exchange.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Large cation/anion <a href="/wiki/Ion_exchange" title="Ion exchange">ion exchangers</a> used in demineralization of boiler feedwater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Boiler_feedwater" title="Boiler feedwater">Boiler feedwater</a></div>
<p>Feed water for boilers needs to be as pure as possible with a minimum of suspended solids and dissolved impurities which cause <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foaming" title="Foaming">foaming</a> and water <a href="/wiki/Carryover_with_steam" title="Carryover with steam">carryover</a>. The most common options for demineralization of boiler feedwater are <a href="/wiki/Reverse_osmosis" title="Reverse osmosis">reverse osmosis</a> (RO) and <a href="/wiki/Ion_exchange" title="Ion exchange">ion exchange</a> (IX).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Boiler_safety">Boiler safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boiler_(power_generation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Boiler safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When water is converted to steam it expands in volume over 1,000 times and travels down steam pipes at over 25 m/s. Because of this, steam is a good way of moving energy and heat around a site from a central boiler house to where it is needed, but without the right boiler feed water treatment, a steam-raising plant will suffer from scale formation and corrosion. At best, this increases energy costs and can lead to poor quality steam, reduced efficiency, shorter plant life and an operation which is unreliable. At worst, it can lead to catastrophic failure and loss of life. While variations in standards may exist in different countries, stringent legal, testing, training and certification is applied to try to minimise or prevent such occurrences. Failure modes include:
</p>
<ul><li>overpressurisation of the boiler</li>
<li>insufficient water in the boiler causing overheating and vessel failure</li>
<li>pressure vessel failure of the boiler due to inadequate construction or maintenance.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Doble_boiler">Doble boiler</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boiler_(power_generation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Doble boiler">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Doble_steam_car" title="Doble steam car">Doble steam car</a> uses a once-through type contra-flow generator, consisting of a continuous tube. The fire here is on top of the coil instead of underneath. Water is pumped into the tube at the bottom and the steam is drawn off at the top. This means that every particle of water and steam must necessarily pass through every part of the generator causing an intense circulation which prevents any <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fouling" title="Fouling">scale</a> from forming on the inside of the tube. Water enters the bottom of this tube at the <a href="/wiki/Volumetric_flow_rate" title="Volumetric flow rate">flow rate</a> of 600 feet (183 m) a second with less than two quarts of water in the tube at any one time.
</p><p>	 As the hot gases pass down between the coils, they gradually cool, as the heat is being absorbed by the water. The last portion of the generator with which the gases come into contact remains the cold incoming water.
	The fire is positively cut off when the pressure reaches a pre-determined point, usually set at 750 psi (5.2 MPa), cold water pressure; a <a href="/wiki/Safety_valve" title="Safety valve">safety valve</a> set at 1,200 lb (544 kg) provides added protection. The fire is automatically cut off by temperature as well as pressure, so in case the boiler were completely dry it would be impossible to damage the coil as the fire would be automatically cut off by the temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Similar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forced-circulation_boiler" title="Forced-circulation boiler">forced circulation generators</a>, such as the Pritchard and Lamont and Velox boilers present the same advantages.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Steam_accessories">Steam accessories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boiler_(power_generation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Steam accessories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>Main steam stop valve</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Application_of_steam_boilers">Application of steam boilers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boiler_(power_generation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Application of steam boilers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Steam boilers are used where steam and hot steam is needed. Hence, steam boilers are used as generators to produce electricity in the energy business. Besides many different application areas in the industry for example in heating systems or for <a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">cement</a> production, steam boilers are used in <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> as well for <a href="/wiki/Soil_steam_sterilization" title="Soil steam sterilization">soil steaming</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Testing_steam_generators">Testing steam generators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boiler_(power_generation)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Testing steam generators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The preeminent code for testing fired steam generators in the USA is the American Society of Mechanical Engineers (ASME) performance test code, PTC 4. A related component is the regenerative air heater. A major revision to the performance test code for air heaters will be published in 2013. Copies of the draft are available for review.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The European standards for acceptance test of steam boilers are EN 12952-15<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and EN 12953-11.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The British standards BS 845-1 and BS 845-2 remain also in use in the UK.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
