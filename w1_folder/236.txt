<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enzymology" title="Enzymology">enzymology</a>, <b>carbon monoxide dehydrogenase</b> (<a href="/wiki/Enzyme_Commission_number" title="Enzyme Commission number">EC</a> <a class="external text" href="https://enzyme.expasy.org/EC/1.2.7.4" rel="nofollow">1.2.7.4</a>) is an <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzyme</a> that <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalyzes</a> the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reaction</a>
</p>

<p>The 3 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Substrate_(biochemistry)" title="Substrate (biochemistry)">substrates</a> of this enzyme are <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">CO</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">H<sub>2</sub>O</a>, and A, whereas its two <a href="/wiki/Product_(chemistry)" title="Product (chemistry)">products</a> are <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> and AH<sub>2</sub>.
</p><p>This enzyme belongs to the family of <a href="/wiki/Oxidoreductase" title="Oxidoreductase">oxidoreductases</a>, specifically those acting on the aldehyde or oxo group of donor with other acceptors.  The <a href="/wiki/List_of_enzymes" title="List of enzymes">systematic name</a> of this enzyme class is carbon-monoxide:acceptor oxidoreductase. Other names in common use include anaerobic carbon monoxide dehydrogenase, carbon monoxide oxygenase, carbon-monoxide dehydrogenase, and carbon-monoxide:(acceptor) oxidoreductase.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classes">Classes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_monoxide_dehydrogenase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Two major classes of the carbon monoxide dehydrogenase (CODH) enzymes have been identified. CODH containing a Mo-[2Fe-2S]-FAD active site have been found in aerobic bacteria, while a distinct class of Ni-[3Fe-4S]  CODH enzymes have been purified from anaerobic bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dobbek_2001_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dobbek_2001-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn1-84755-915-8_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn1-84755-915-8-3">[3]</a></sup> Both classes of CODH catalyze the reversible conversion between carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>) and carbonmonoxide (CO). CODH exists in both monofuctional and bifunctional forms. In the latter case, CODH forms a bifunctional cluster with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acetyl-CoA_synthase" title="Acetyl-CoA synthase">acetyl-CoA synthase</a>, as has been well characterized in the anaerobic bacteria <i>Moorella thermoacetica</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Doukov_2008_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Doukov_2008-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tan_2006_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tan_2006-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_monoxide_dehydrogenase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CODH_M.thermoacetica.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="493" data-file-width="793" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6c/CODH_M.thermoacetica.jpg/300px-CODH_M.thermoacetica.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6c/CODH_M.thermoacetica.jpg/450px-CODH_M.thermoacetica.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6c/CODH_M.thermoacetica.jpg/600px-CODH_M.thermoacetica.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CODH_M.thermoacetica.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Structure of CODH/ACS in <i>M.thermoacetica." Alpha (ACS) and beta (CODH) subunits are shown.  <b>(1)</b>The A-cluster Ni-[4Fe-4S].  <b>(2)</b>C-cluster Ni-[3Fe-4S].  <b>(3)</b> B-Cluster [4Fe-4S].  <b>(4)</b> D-cluster [4Fe-4S]. Designed from <a class="external text" href="https://www.ebi.ac.uk/thornton-srv/databases/cgi-bin/pdbsum/GetPage.pl?pdbcode=3I01" rel="nofollow">3I01</a></i></div></div></div>
<p>Multiple research groups have proposed crystal structures for the α<sub>2</sub>β<sub>2</sub> tetrameric enzyme CODH/ACS from the acetogenic bacteria <i>M. thermoacetica</i>, including two recent examples since 2009: <a class="external text" href="https://www.ebi.ac.uk/thornton-srv/databases/cgi-bin/pdbsum/GetPage.pl?pdbcode=3I01" rel="nofollow">3I01</a> <a class="external text" href="https://www.ebi.ac.uk/thornton-srv/databases/cgi-bin/pdbsum/GetPage.pl?pdbcode=2Z8Y" rel="nofollow">2Z8Y</a>. The two β units are the site of CODH activity and form the central core of the enzyme.  In total, the 310 kDa enzyme contains seven iron-sulfur [4Fe-4S] clusters.  Each α unit contains a single metal cluster. Together, the two β units contains five clusters of three types. CODH catalytic activity occurs at the Ni-[3Fe-4S] C-clusters while the interior [4Fe-4S] B and D clusters transfer electrons away from the C-cluster to external electron carriers such as <a href="/wiki/Ferredoxin" title="Ferredoxin">ferredoxin</a>. The ACS activity occurs in A-cluster located in the outer two α units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dobbek_2001_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dobbek_2001-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn1-84755-915-8_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-isbn1-84755-915-8-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>A noteworthy feature of the <i>M.thermoacetica</i> CODH/ACS is an internal gas tunnel connecting the multiple active sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid18293927_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid18293927-6">[6]</a></sup> The full role of the gas channel in regulating the rate catalytic activity is still a subject of investigation, but several studies support the notion that molecules of CO do in fact travel directly from the C-cluster to the ACS active site without leaving the enzyme. For instance, the rate of acetyl-CoA synthase activity in the bifunctional enzyme is not affected by the addition of hemoglobin, which would compete for CO in bulk solution,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and isotopic labeling studies show that carbon monoxide derived from the C-cluster is preferentially used at the A-cluster over unlabeled CO in solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Protein engineering of the CODH/ACS in  <i>M.thermoacetica</i> revealed that mutating residues, so as to functionally block the tunnel, stopped acety-CoA synthesis when only CO<sub>2</sub> was present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
The discovery of a functional CO tunnel places CODH on a growing list of enzymes that independently evolved this strategy to transfer reactive intermediates from one active site to another.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction_mechanisms">Reaction mechanisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_monoxide_dehydrogenase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reaction mechanisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxidative">Oxidative</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_monoxide_dehydrogenase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Oxidative">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The CODH catalytic site, referred to as the C-cluster, is a [3Fe-4S] cluster bonded to a Ni-Fe moiety. Two basic amino acids (Lys587 and His 113 in <i>M.thermoacetica</i>) reside in proximity to the C-cluster and facilitate acid-base chemistry required for enzyme activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid16895330_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid16895330-11">[11]</a></sup>  Based on IR spectra suggesting the presence of an Ni-CO complex, the proposed first step in the oxidative catalysis of CO to CO<sub>2</sub> involves the binding of CO to Ni<sup>2+</sup> and corresponding complexing of Fe<sup>2+</sup> to a water molecule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The binding of CO molecule causes a shift in the coordination of the Ni atom from a square-planar to square pyramidal geometry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Dobbek et al. further propose that movement of the Nickel atom’s cysteine ligand brings the CO into close proximity to the hydroxyl group, and facilitate a base-catalyzed, nucleophillic attack by the iron-bound hydroxy group. A carboxy bridge between the Ni and Fe atom has been proposed as an intermediate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> A decarboxylation leads to the release of CO<sub>2</sub> and the reduction of the cluster. Although the resulting intermediate oxidation state of the Ni and the degree to which electrons are distributed throughout the Ni-[3Fe-4S] cluster is subject of some debate, the electrons in the reduced C-cluster are transferred to nearby B and D [4Fe-4S] clusters, returning the Ni-[3Fe-4S] C-cluster to an oxidized state and reducing the single electron carrier <a href="/wiki/Ferredoxin" title="Ferredoxin">ferredoxin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ragsdale_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ragsdale-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reductive">Reductive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_monoxide_dehydrogenase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reductive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Given CODH's role in CO<sub>2</sub> fixation identified in diverse autotrophic bacteria and archaea, it is common in the biochemistry literature for the reductive mechanism to be inferred as the “direct reverse” of the oxidative mechanism by the ”principal of microreversibility.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid18801467_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid18801467-17">[17]</a></sup> In the process of reducing carbon dioxide, the enzyme's C-cluster must first be activated from an oxidized to a reduced state before the Ni-CO<sub>2</sub> bond is formed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid14769031_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid14769031-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_monoxide_dehydrogenase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carbon monoxide dehydrogenase participates in diverse prokaryotic biochemical pathways, including the metabolism of methanogenic, aerobic carboxidotrophic, acetogenic, sulfate-reducing, and hydrogenogenic bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dobbek_2001_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dobbek_2001-2">[2]</a></sup> The bidirectional reaction catalyzed by CODH plays a role in the carbon cycle allowing organisms to both make use of CO as a source of energy and utilize CO<sub>2</sub> as a source of carbon.  CODH can form a monofunctional enzyme, as is the case in <i>Rhodospirillum rubram</i>, or can form a cluster with acetyl-CoA synthase as has been shown in <i>M.thermoacetica</i>. When acting in concert, either as structurally independent enzymes or in a bifunctional CODH/ACS unit, the two catalytic sites are key to carbon fixation in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reductive_acetyl-CoA_pathway" title="Reductive acetyl-CoA pathway">reductive acetyl-CoA pathway</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hegg_2004_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hegg_2004-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_relevance">Environmental relevance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_monoxide_dehydrogenase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Environmental relevance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>CODH is important for maintaining current atmospheric conditions. Microbial metabolism of CO maintains ambient CO at levels safe for other forms of life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
