<p><b>Cellular respiration</b> is a set of <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolic</a> reactions and processes that take place in the <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> of <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a> to convert <a href="/wiki/Energy#Energy_and_life" title="Energy">biochemical energy</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nutrients" title="Nutrients">nutrients</a> into <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">adenosine triphosphate</a> (ATP), and then release waste products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The reactions involved in respiration are <a href="/wiki/Catabolism" title="Catabolism">catabolic reactions</a>, which break large molecules into smaller ones, releasing energy in the process, as weak so-called "high-energy" bonds are replaced by stronger bonds in the products. Respiration is one of the key ways a cell releases chemical energy to fuel cellular activity. Cellular respiration is considered an <a href="/wiki/Exothermic_reaction" title="Exothermic reaction">exothermic</a> <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">redox reaction</a> which releases heat. The overall reaction occurs in a series of biochemical steps, most of which are redox reactions themselves. Although cellular respiration is technically a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combustion_reaction" title="Combustion reaction">combustion reaction</a>, it clearly does not resemble one when it occurs in a living cell because of the slow release of energy from the series of reactions.
</p><p>Nutrients that are commonly used by animal and plant cells in respiration include <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amino_acids" title="Amino acids">amino acids</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatty_acids" title="Fatty acids">fatty acids</a>, and the most common <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_agent" title="Oxidizing agent">oxidizing agent</a> (<a href="/wiki/Electron_acceptor" title="Electron acceptor">electron acceptor</a>) is molecular <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> (O<sub>2</sub>). The chemical energy stored in ATP (its third phosphate group is weakly bonded to the rest of the molecule and is cheaply broken allowing stronger bonds to form, thereby transferring energy for use by the cell) can then be used to drive processes requiring energy, including <a href="/wiki/Biosynthesis" title="Biosynthesis">biosynthesis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Motion_(physics)#Cells" title="Motion (physics)">locomotion</a> or transportation of molecules across <a href="/wiki/Cell_membrane" title="Cell membrane">cell membranes</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aerobic_respiration">Aerobic respiration</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Auto-and_heterotrophs.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3167" data-file-width="2722" height="256" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Auto-and_heterotrophs.png/220px-Auto-and_heterotrophs.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Auto-and_heterotrophs.png/330px-Auto-and_heterotrophs.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Auto-and_heterotrophs.png/440px-Auto-and_heterotrophs.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Auto-and_heterotrophs.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Aerobic respiration</i> (<span style="color:red;">red arrows</span>) is the main means by which both fungi and animals utilize chemical energy in the form of organic compounds that were previously created through <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> (<span style="color:green;">green arrow</span>).</div></div></div>
<p><i>Aerobic respiration</i> requires <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> (O<sub>2</sub>) in order to create <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a>. Although <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbohydrates" title="Carbohydrates">carbohydrates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fats</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proteins" title="Proteins">proteins</a>  are consumed as reactants, it is the preferred method of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyruvate" title="Pyruvate">pyruvate</a> breakdown in <a href="/wiki/Glycolysis" title="Glycolysis">glycolysis</a> and requires that pyruvate enter the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitochondria" title="Mitochondria">mitochondria</a> in order to be fully oxidized by the <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid_cycle" title="Citric acid cycle">Krebs cycle</a>. The products of this process are carbon dioxide and water, but the energy transferred is used to break bonds in ADP as the third phosphate group is added to form  ATP (<a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">adenosine triphosphate</a>), by <a href="/wiki/Substrate-level_phosphorylation" title="Substrate-level phosphorylation">substrate-level phosphorylation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NADH_dehydrogenase_(ubiquinone)" title="NADH dehydrogenase (ubiquinone)">NADH</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FADH2" title="FADH2">FADH<sub>2</sub></a>
</p>
<table>
<tbody><tr>
<td rowspan="2"><b>Simplified reaction:</b>
</td>
<td>C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>12</sub>O<sub>6</sub> (s) + 6 O<sub>2</sub> (g) → 6 CO<sub>2</sub> (g) + 6 H<sub>2</sub>O (l) + heat
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td>ΔG = −2880 kJ per mol of C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>12</sub>O<sub>6</sub>
</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The negative ΔG indicates that the reaction can occur spontaneously.
</p><p>The potential of NADH and FADH<sub>2</sub> is converted to more ATP through an <a href="/wiki/Electron_transport_chain" title="Electron transport chain">electron transport chain</a> with oxygen as the "terminal electron acceptor". Most of the ATP produced by aerobic cellular respiration is made by <a href="/wiki/Oxidative_phosphorylation" title="Oxidative phosphorylation">oxidative phosphorylation</a>. This works by the energy released in the consumption of pyruvate being used to create a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemiosmotic_potential" title="Chemiosmotic potential">chemiosmotic potential</a> by pumping <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> across a membrane. This potential is then used to drive ATP synthase and produce ATP from <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_diphosphate" title="Adenosine diphosphate">ADP</a> and a phosphate group. Biology textbooks often state that 38 ATP molecules can be made per oxidised glucose molecule during cellular respiration (2 from glycolysis, 2 from the Krebs cycle, and about 34 from the electron transport system).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rich_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rich-2">[2]</a></sup> However, this maximum yield is never quite reached because of losses due to leaky membranes as well as the cost of moving pyruvate and ADP into the mitochondrial matrix, and current estimates range around 29 to 30 ATP per glucose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rich_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rich-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Aerobic metabolism is up to 15 times more efficient than anaerobic metabolism (which yields 2 molecules ATP per 1 molecule glucose).  However some anaerobic organisms, such as <a href="/wiki/Methanogen" title="Methanogen">methanogens</a> are able to continue with <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_respiration" title="Anaerobic respiration">anaerobic respiration</a>, yielding more ATP by using other inorganic molecules (not oxygen) as final electron acceptors in the electron transport chain.  They share the initial pathway of <a href="/wiki/Glycolysis" title="Glycolysis">glycolysis</a> but aerobic metabolism continues with the Krebs cycle and oxidative phosphorylation. The post-glycolytic reactions take place in the mitochondria in <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryotic cells</a>, and in the <a href="/wiki/Cytoplasm" title="Cytoplasm">cytoplasm</a> in <a href="/wiki/Prokaryote" title="Prokaryote">prokaryotic cells</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aerobic_respiration_summed_up">Aerobic respiration summed up</span></h3>
<p>1. Glycolysis:
</p><p>--- <i>2 ATPs  +  Glucose  →  2 Pyruvic Acid  +  4 Hydrogen  +  4 ATPs</i>
</p><p>2. Formation of Acetyl CoA:
</p><p>--- <i>2 Pyruvic Acid  +  2 CoA  →  2 Acetyl CoA  +  2 Carbon Dioxide  +  2 Hydrogen</i>
</p><p>3. Krebs Cycle:
</p><p>--- <i>2 Acetyl CoA  +  3 O2  →  6 Hydrogen  +  4 Carbon Dioxide  +  2 ATPs</i>
</p><p>4. Electron Transport System:
</p><p>--- <i>12 Hydrogen  +  3 O2  →  6 Water  +  32 ATPs</i>
</p><p>Overall Reaction:
</p><p>--- <i>Glucose  +  6 O2  →  6 Carbon Dioxide  +  6 Water  +  36 ATPs</i>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Glycolysis">Glycolysis</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Respiration_diagram.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="960" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Respiration_diagram.png/220px-Respiration_diagram.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Respiration_diagram.png/330px-Respiration_diagram.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Respiration_diagram.png/440px-Respiration_diagram.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Respiration_diagram.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Out of the cytoplasm it goes into the Krebs cycle with the acetyl CoA. It then mixes with CO<sub>2</sub> and makes 2 ATP, NADH, and FADH. From there the NADH and FADH go into the NADH reductase, which produces the enzyme. The NADH pulls the enzyme's electrons to send through the electron transport chain. The electron transport chain pulls H<sup>+</sup> ions through the chain. From the electron transport chain, the released hydrogen ions make ADP for an end result of 32 ATP. O<sub>2</sub> attracts itself to the left over electron to make water. Lastly, ATP leaves through the ATP channel and out of the mitochondria.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Glycolysis" title="Glycolysis">Glycolysis</a></div>
<p>Glycolysis is a <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_pathway" title="Metabolic pathway">metabolic pathway</a> that takes place in the <a href="/wiki/Cytosol" title="Cytosol">cytosol</a> of cells in all living organisms. This pathway can function with or without the presence of oxygen. In humans, aerobic conditions produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyruvate" title="Pyruvate">pyruvate</a> and anaerobic conditions produce <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactate</a>. In aerobic conditions, the process converts one molecule of <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> into two molecules of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyruvate" title="Pyruvate">pyruvate</a> (pyruvic acid), generating energy in the form of two net molecules of <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a>. Four molecules of ATP per glucose are actually produced, however, two are consumed as part of the <a href="/wiki/Glycolysis#Preparatory_phase" title="Glycolysis">preparatory phase</a>. The initial <a href="/wiki/Phosphorylation" title="Phosphorylation">phosphorylation</a> of glucose is required to increase the reactivity (decrease its stability) in order for the molecule to be cleaved into two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyruvate" title="Pyruvate">pyruvate</a> molecules by the enzyme <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aldolase" title="Aldolase">aldolase</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/Glycolysis#Pay-off_phase" title="Glycolysis">pay-off phase</a> of glycolysis, four <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a> groups are transferred to ADP by <a href="/wiki/Substrate-level_phosphorylation" title="Substrate-level phosphorylation">substrate-level phosphorylation</a> to make four ATP, and two NADH are produced when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyruvate" title="Pyruvate">pyruvate</a> are oxidized. The overall reaction can be expressed this way:
</p>
<dl><dd>Glucose + 2 NAD<sup>+</sup> + 2 P<sub>i</sub> + 2 ADP → 2 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyruvate" title="Pyruvate">pyruvate</a> + 2 NADH + 2 ATP + 2 H<sup>+</sup> + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O + heat</dd></dl>
<p>Starting with glucose,  1 ATP is used to donate a phosphate to glucose to produce glucose 6-phosphate. Glycogen can be converted into glucose 6-phosphate as well with the help of glycogen phosphorylase. During energy metabolism, glucose 6-phosphate becomes fructose 6-phosphate. An additional ATP is used to phosphorylate fructose 6-phosphate into fructose 1,6-disphosphate by the help of phosphofructokinase. Fructose 1,6-diphosphate then splits into two phosphorylated molecules with three carbon chains which later degrades into pyruvate.
</p><p>Glycolysis can be literally translated as "sugar splitting".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxidative_decarboxylation_of_pyruvate">Oxidative decarboxylation of pyruvate</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pyruvate_decarboxylation" title="Pyruvate decarboxylation">Pyruvate decarboxylation</a></div>
<p>Pyruvate is oxidized to acetyl-CoA and CO<sub>2</sub> by the <a href="/wiki/Pyruvate_dehydrogenase_complex" title="Pyruvate dehydrogenase complex">pyruvate dehydrogenase complex</a> (PDC).  The PDC contains multiple copies of three enzymes and is located in the <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_matrix" title="Mitochondrial matrix">mitochondria</a> of eukaryotic cells and in the cytosol of prokaryotes. In the conversion of pyruvate to acetyl-CoA, one molecule of NADH and one molecule of CO<sub>2</sub> is formed.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Citric_acid_cycle">Citric acid cycle</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid_cycle" title="Citric acid cycle">Citric acid cycle</a></div>
<p>This is also called the <i>Krebs cycle</i> or the <i>tricarboxylic acid cycle</i>. When oxygen is present, <a href="/wiki/Acetyl-CoA" title="Acetyl-CoA">acetyl-CoA</a> is produced from the pyruvate molecules created from glycolysis. Once <a href="/wiki/Acetyl-CoA" title="Acetyl-CoA">acetyl-CoA</a> is formed, aerobic or anaerobic respiration can occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup> When oxygen is present, the mitochondria will undergo aerobic respiration which leads to the Krebs cycle.  However, if oxygen is not present, fermentation of the pyruvate molecule will occur.  In the presence of oxygen, when acetyl-CoA is produced, the molecule then enters the <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid_cycle" title="Citric acid cycle">citric acid cycle</a> (Krebs cycle) inside the mitochondrial matrix, and is oxidized to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> while at the same time reducing <a href="/wiki/Nicotinamide_adenine_dinucleotide" title="Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide">NAD</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NADH" title="NADH">NADH</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NADH" title="NADH">NADH</a> can be used by the <a href="/wiki/Electron_transport_chain" title="Electron transport chain">electron transport chain</a> to create further <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a> as part of oxidative phosphorylation. To fully oxidize the equivalent of one glucose molecule, two acetyl-CoA must be metabolized by the Krebs cycle. Two <a href="/wiki/Cellular_waste_product" title="Cellular waste product">waste products</a>, H<sub>2</sub>O and CO<sub>2</sub>, are created during this cycle.
</p><p>The citric acid cycle is an 8-step process involving 18 different enzymes and co-enzymes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup> During the cycle, acetyl-CoA (2 carbons) + oxaloacetate (4 carbons) yields citrate (6 carbons), which is rearranged to a more reactive form called isocitrate (6 carbons). Isocitrate is modified to become α-ketoglutarate (5 carbons), succinyl-CoA, succinate, fumarate, malate, and, finally, oxaloacetate.
</p><p>The net gain of high-energy compounds from one cycle is 3 NADH, 1 FADH<sub>2</sub>, and 1 GTP; the GTP may subsequently be used to produce ATP. Thus, the total yield from 1 glucose molecule (2 pyruvate molecules) is 6 NADH, 2 FADH<sub>2</sub>, and 2 ATP.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxidative_phosphorylation">Oxidative phosphorylation</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Oxidative_phosphorylation" title="Oxidative phosphorylation">Oxidative phosphorylation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electron_transport_chain" title="Electron transport chain">Electron transport chain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrochemical_gradient" title="Electrochemical gradient">Electrochemical gradient</a>, and <a href="/wiki/ATP_synthase" title="ATP synthase">ATP synthase</a></div>
<p>In eukaryotes, oxidative phosphorylation occurs in the mitochondrial <a href="/wiki/Crista" title="Crista">cristae</a>. It comprises the electron transport chain that establishes a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proton_gradient" title="Proton gradient">proton gradient</a> (chemiosmotic potential) across the boundary of inner membrane by oxidizing the NADH produced from the Krebs cycle. ATP is synthesized by the ATP synthase enzyme when the chemiosmotic gradient is used to drive the phosphorylation of ADP. The electrons are finally transferred to exogenous oxygen and, with the addition of two protons, water is formed.
</p>

<p>Although there is a theoretical yield of 38 ATP molecules per glucose during cellular respiration, such conditions are generally not realized because of losses such as the cost of moving pyruvate (from glycolysis), phosphate, and ADP (substrates for ATP synthesis) into the mitochondria. All are actively transported using carriers that utilize the stored energy in the proton <a href="/wiki/Electrochemical_gradient" title="Electrochemical gradient">electrochemical gradient</a>.
</p>
<ul><li>Pyruvate is taken up by a specific, low Km transporter to bring it into the mitochondrial matrix for oxidation by the pyruvate dehydrogenase complex.</li>
<li>The <b><a href="/wiki/SLC25A3" title="SLC25A3">phosphate carrier</a></b> (PiC) mediates the electroneutral exchange (<a href="/wiki/Antiporter" title="Antiporter">antiport</a>) of phosphate (H<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>4</sub><sup>−</sup>; P<sub>i</sub>) for OH<sup>−</sup> or <a href="/wiki/Symporter" title="Symporter">symport</a> of phosphate and protons (H<sup>+</sup>) across the inner membrane, and the driving force for moving phosphate ions into the mitochondria is the <a href="/wiki/Chemiosmosis#proton_motive_force" title="Chemiosmosis">proton motive force</a>.</li>
<li>The <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ATP-ADP_translocase" title="ATP-ADP translocase">ATP-ADP translocase</a></b> (also called <a href="/wiki/Adenine_nucleotide_translocator" title="Adenine nucleotide translocator">adenine nucleotide translocase, ANT</a>) is an <a href="/wiki/Antiporter" title="Antiporter">antiporter</a> and exchanges ADP and ATP across the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_nuclear_membrane" title="Inner nuclear membrane">inner membrane</a>. The driving force is due to the ATP (−4) having a more negative charge than the ADP (−3), and thus it dissipates some of the electrical component of the proton electrochemical gradient.</li></ul>
<p>The outcome of these transport processes using the proton electrochemical gradient is that more than 3 H<sup>+</sup> are needed to make 1 ATP. Obviously this reduces the theoretical efficiency of the whole process and the likely maximum is closer to 28–30 ATP molecules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rich_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rich-2">[2]</a></sup> In practice the efficiency may be even lower because the inner membrane of the mitochondria is slightly leaky to protons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Other factors may also dissipate the proton gradient creating an apparently leaky mitochondria. An uncoupling protein known as <a href="/wiki/Thermogenin" title="Thermogenin">thermogenin</a> is expressed in some cell types and is a channel that can transport protons. When this protein is active in the inner membrane it short circuits the coupling between the <a href="/wiki/Electron_transport_chain" title="Electron transport chain">electron transport chain</a> and <a href="/wiki/ATP_synthase" title="ATP synthase">ATP synthesis</a>. The potential energy from the proton gradient is not used to make ATP but generates heat. This is particularly important in <a href="/wiki/Brown_adipose_tissue" title="Brown adipose tissue">brown fat</a> thermogenesis of newborn and hibernating mammals.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cellular_respiration.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="887" data-file-width="880" height="222" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Cellular_respiration.gif/220px-Cellular_respiration.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Cellular_respiration.gif/330px-Cellular_respiration.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Cellular_respiration.gif/440px-Cellular_respiration.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cellular_respiration.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Stoichiometry of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerobic_respiration" title="Aerobic respiration">aerobic respiration</a> and most known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">fermentation</a> types in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eucaryota" title="Eucaryota">eucaryotic</a> cell. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stryer95_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stryer95-6">[6]</a></sup> Numbers in circles indicate counts of carbon atoms in molecules, C6 is <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>12</sub>O<sub>6</sub>, C1 <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> CO<sub>2</sub>. <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrion" title="Mitochondrion">Mitochondrial</a> outer membrane is omitted.</div></div></div>
<p>According to some of newer sources the ATP yield during aerobic respiration is not 36–38, but only about 30–32 ATP molecules / 1 molecule of glucose <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stryer95_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stryer95-6">[6]</a></sup>, because:
</p>
<ul><li>ATP : NADH+H<sup>+</sup> and ATP : FADH<sub>2</sub> ratios during the <a href="/wiki/Oxidative_phosphorylation" title="Oxidative phosphorylation">oxidative phosphorylation</a> appear to be not 3 and 2, but 2.5 and 1.5 respectively. Unlike in the <a href="/wiki/Substrate-level_phosphorylation" title="Substrate-level phosphorylation">substrate-level phosphorylation</a>, the stoichiometry here is difficult to establish.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/ATP_synthase" title="ATP synthase">ATP synthase</a> produces 1 ATP / 3 H<sup>+</sup>. However the exchange of matrix ATP  for cytosolic ADP and Pi (antiport with OH<sup>−</sup> or symport with H<sup>+</sup>) mediated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ATP%E2%80%93ADP_translocase" title="ATP–ADP translocase">ATP–ADP translocase</a> and <a href="/wiki/SLC25A3" title="SLC25A3">phosphate carrier</a> consumes 1 H<sup>+</sup> / 1 ATP as a result of regeneration of the transmembrane potential changed during this transfer, so the net ratio is 1 ATP : 4 H<sup>+</sup>.</li>
<li>The mitochondrial <a href="/wiki/Electron_transport_chain" title="Electron transport chain">electron transport chain</a> <a href="/wiki/Proton_pump" title="Proton pump">proton pump</a> transfers across the inner membrane 10 H<sup>+</sup> / 1 NADH+H<sup>+</sup> (4 + 2 + 4) or 6 H<sup>+</sup> / 1 FADH<sub>2</sub> (2 + 4).</li></ul></li></ul>
<dl><dd>So the final stoichiometry is</dd>
<dd>1 NADH+H<sup>+</sup> : 10 H<sup>+</sup> : 10/4 ATP = 1 NADH+H<sup>+</sup> : 2.5 ATP</dd>
<dd>1 FADH<sub>2</sub> : 6 H<sup>+</sup> : 6/4 ATP = 1 FADH<sub>2</sub> : 1.5 ATP</dd></dl>
<ul><li>ATP : NADH+H<sup>+</sup> coming from glycolysis ratio during the oxidative phosphorylation is
<ul><li>1.5, as for FADH<sub>2</sub>, if hydrogen atoms (2H<sup>+</sup>+2e<sup>−</sup>) are transferred from cytosolic NADH+H<sup>+</sup> to mitochondrial FAD by the <a href="/wiki/Glycerol_phosphate_shuttle" title="Glycerol phosphate shuttle">glycerol phosphate shuttle</a> located in the inner mitochondrial membrane.</li>
<li>2.5 in case of <a href="/wiki/Malate-aspartate_shuttle" title="Malate-aspartate shuttle">malate-aspartate shuttle</a> transferring hydrogen atoms from cytosolic NADH+H<sup>+</sup> to mitochondrial NAD<sup>+</sup></li></ul></li></ul>
<p>So finally we have, per molecule of glucose
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Substrate-level_phosphorylation" title="Substrate-level phosphorylation">Substrate-level phosphorylation</a>: 2 ATP from <a href="/wiki/Glycolysis" title="Glycolysis">glycolysis</a> + 2 ATP (directly GTP) from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Krebs_cycle" title="Krebs cycle">Krebs cycle</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Oxidative_phosphorylation" title="Oxidative phosphorylation">Oxidative phosphorylation</a>
<ul><li>2 NADH+H<sup>+</sup> from glycolysis: 2 × 1.5 ATP (if glycerol phosphate shuttle transfers hydrogen atoms) or 2 × 2.5 ATP (malate-aspartate shuttle)</li>
<li>2 NADH+H<sup>+</sup> from the <a href="/wiki/Pyruvate_decarboxylation" title="Pyruvate decarboxylation">oxidative decarboxylation of pyruvate</a> and 6 from Krebs cycle: 8 × 2.5 ATP</li>
<li>2 FADH<sub>2</sub> from the Krebs cycle: 2 × 1.5 ATP</li></ul></li></ul>
<p>Altogether this gives 4 + 3 (or 5) + 20 + 3 = 30 (or 32) ATP per molecule of glucose
</p><p>The total ATP yield in ethanol or lactic acid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">fermentation</a> is only 2 molecules coming from <a href="/wiki/Glycolysis" title="Glycolysis">glycolysis</a>, because pyruvate is not transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrion" title="Mitochondrion">mitochondrion</a> and finally oxidized to the carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>), but reduced to <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fermentation" title="Ethanol fermentation">ethanol</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid_fermentation" title="Lactic acid fermentation">lactic acid</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Cytoplasm" title="Cytoplasm">cytoplasm</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stryer95_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Stryer95-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fermentation">Fermentation</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fermentation" title="Fermentation">Fermentation</a></div>
<p>Without oxygen, pyruvate (<a href="/wiki/Pyruvic_acid" title="Pyruvic acid">pyruvic acid</a>) is not metabolized by cellular respiration but undergoes a process of fermentation. The pyruvate is not transported into the mitochondrion, but remains in the cytoplasm, where it is converted to <a href="/wiki/Cellular_waste_product" title="Cellular waste product">waste products</a> that may be removed from the cell. This serves the purpose of oxidizing the electron carriers so that they can perform glycolysis again and removing the excess pyruvate. Fermentation oxidizes NADH to NAD+ so it can be re-used in glycolysis.  In the absence of oxygen, fermentation prevents the buildup of NADH in the cytoplasm and provides NAD+ for glycolysis.  This waste product varies depending on the organism. In skeletal muscles, the waste product is <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactic acid</a>. This type of fermentation is called <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid_fermentation" title="Lactic acid fermentation">lactic acid fermentation</a>. In strenuous exercise, when energy demands exceed energy supply, the respiratory chain cannot process all of the hydrogen atoms joined by NADH. During anaerobic glycolysis, NAD+ regenerates when pairs of hydrogen combine with pyruvate to form lactate. Lactate formation is catalyzed by lactate dehydrogenase in a reversible reaction. Lactate can also be used as an indirect precursor for liver glycogen. During recovery, when oxygen becomes available, NAD+ attaches to hydrogen from lactate to form ATP. In yeast, the waste products are <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. This type of fermentation is known as alcoholic or <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fermentation" title="Ethanol fermentation">ethanol fermentation</a>. The ATP generated in this process is made by <a href="/wiki/Substrate-level_phosphorylation" title="Substrate-level phosphorylation">substrate-level phosphorylation</a>, which does not require oxygen.
</p><p>Fermentation is less efficient at using the energy from glucose: only 2 ATP are produced per glucose, compared to the 38 ATP per glucose nominally produced by aerobic respiration. This is because the <a href="/wiki/Cellular_waste_product" title="Cellular waste product">waste products</a> of fermentation still contain chemical potential energy that can be released by oxidation. <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">Ethanol</a>, for example, can be burned in an internal combustion engine like gasoline. Glycolytic ATP, however, is created more quickly. For prokaryotes to continue a rapid growth rate when they are shifted from an aerobic environment to an anaerobic environment, they must increase the rate of the glycolytic reactions. For multicellular organisms, during short bursts of strenuous activity, muscle cells use fermentation to supplement the ATP production from the slower aerobic respiration, so fermentation may be used by a cell even before the oxygen levels are depleted, as is the case in sports that do not require athletes to pace themselves, such as <a href="/wiki/Sprint_(running)" title="Sprint (running)">sprinting</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anaerobic_respiration">Anaerobic respiration</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_respiration" title="Anaerobic respiration">Anaerobic respiration</a></div>
<p>Cellular respiration is the process by which biological fuels are oxidised in the presence of an inorganic electron acceptor (such as oxygen) to produce large amounts of energy, to drive the bulk production of ATP.
</p><p><b>Anaerobic respiration</b> is used by some microorganisms in which neither oxygen (aerobic respiration) nor pyruvate derivatives (fermentation) is the final electron acceptor.  Rather, an inorganic acceptor such as <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfate</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrate</a> is used.
Such organisms are typically found in unusual places such as underwater caves or near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_vents" title="Hydrothermal vents">hydrothermal vents</a> at the bottom of the ocean.
</p>
