<p>The <b>Directorate-General for Environment</b>  (DG Environment) is a <a href="/wiki/Directorate-General" title="Directorate-General">Directorate-General</a> of the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a>, responsible for the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> policy area of the environment.
</p><p>In February 2010, departments relating to climate change were split off from the environment, industry and external relations DGs to form the <i>DG Climate Action</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC2010PR_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-EC2010PR-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission">Mission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Directorate-General_for_the_Environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The DG's main role is to initiate and define new environmental legislation
and to ensure that measures, which have been agreed, are actually put into practice in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Member_states_of_the_European_Union" title="Member states of the European Union">member states of the European Union</a>. The overall mission statement for 2005 is: "Protecting, preserving and improving the environment for present and future generations, and promoting sustainable development". The mission statement is divided into the following sub-statements:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DGMission_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-DGMission-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>To maintain and improve the quality of life through a high level of protection of our <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_resources" title="Natural resources">natural resources</a>, effective <a href="/wiki/Risk_assessment" title="Risk assessment">risk assessment</a> and management and the timely implementation of <a href="/wiki/European_Union_law" title="European Union law">Community legislation</a>.</li>
<li>To foster resource-efficiency in production, consumption and waste-disposal measures.</li>
<li>To integrate environmental concerns into other EU policy areas.</li>
<li>To promote growth in the EU that takes account of the economic, social and environmental needs both of our citizens and of future generations.</li>
<li>To address the global challenges facing us notably combating <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> and the international conservation of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>.</li>
<li>To ensure that all policies and measures in the above areas are based on a multi-sectoral approach, involve all <a href="/wiki/Project_stakeholder" title="Project stakeholder">stakeholders</a> in the process and are communicated in an effective way.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Directorate-General_for_the_Environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Environment DG is based in <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>.
</p><p>The Directorate-General is organised into an Office of the Director-General, Deputy Director-General and 6 directorates:
</p>
<ul><li>Directorate A: Policy, Coordination and Resources</li>
<li>Directorate B: Circular Economy and Green Growth</li>
<li>Directorate C: Quality of Life</li>
<li>Directorate D: Natural Capital</li>
<li>Directorate E: Implementation and Support to Member States</li>
<li>Directorate F: Global Sustainable Development</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Directorate-General_for_the_Environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EU_environmental_policy" title="EU environmental policy">EU environmental policy</a> started as a collection of disparate laws which had no specific Treaty basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> There was no department dedicated to environmental issues for the first 15 years of the European Commission’s existence. It was only in 1973 that an environmental unit was created within DG Industry; a fully-fledged Environment Directorate-General (DG) was then set up in 1981.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schön-Quinlivan_2012_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schön-Quinlivan_2012-4">[4]</a></sup> However, it remained a relatively weak DG in the Commissioner for several years due to its lack of institutional experience and human resources;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Having started with 5 officials in 1973, there were still only about 60 officials in the 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schön-Quinlivan_2012_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schön-Quinlivan_2012-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In its early years, DG Environment hired specialists with technical knowledge who had a different culture to the other Commission’s officials. This ‘gave [it] a reputation for being dominated by […] “ecological freaks”'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> However, over time DG Environment matured and settled into the Commission’s ways of working. In particular this meant taking politics more into consideration when formulating legislation so that it could be adopted and better implemented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Fifth Environmental Action Programme<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> which came into force on 1 January 1993 marked a sea-change in DG environment’s approach to policy-making. It attempted to present the DG and its policies in a more contemporary and constructive light. The programme demonstrated that legislation was no longer to be made solely behind closed doors, but together with all social and economic partners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 2016 the EC harmonised the names of all the DGs to make them more consistent and dropped "the" from the name, changing from "Directorate-General for the Environment" to "Directorate-General for Environment".
</p>
