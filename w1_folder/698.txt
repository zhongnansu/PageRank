<p><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Good_(economics)" title="Good (economics)">Goods</a></b> are items that are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tangible" title="Tangible">tangible</a>, such as pens, salt, apples, and hats. <b><a href="/wiki/Service_(economics)" title="Service (economics)">Services</a></b> are activities provided by other people, who include doctors, lawn care workers, dentists, barbers, waiters, or online servers. Taken together, it is the <a href="/wiki/Production_(economics)" title="Production (economics)">production</a>, <a href="/wiki/Distribution_(economics)" title="Distribution (economics)">distribution</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Consumption_(economics)" title="Consumption (economics)">consumption</a> of goods and services which underpins all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic_activity" title="Economic activity">economic activity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trade" title="Trade">trade</a>. According to economic theory, <a href="/wiki/Consumption_(economics)" title="Consumption (economics)">consumption</a> of goods and services is assumed to provide <a href="/wiki/Utility" title="Utility">utility</a> (satisfaction) to the consumer or end-user, although <a href="/wiki/Distribution_(marketing)#Channels_and_intermediaries" title="Distribution (marketing)"> businesses also consume goods and services in the course of producing other goods and services.</a>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goods_and_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Productive_and_unproductive_labour" title="Productive and unproductive labour">Productive and unproductive labour</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Physiocracy" title="Physiocracy">Physiocratic economists</a> categorized production into productive labour and unproductive labour. <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a> expanded this thought by arguing that any economic activities directly related on material products (goods) were productive, and those activities which involved non-material production (services) were unproductive.  This emphasis on material production was adapted by <a href="/wiki/David_Ricardo" title="David Ricardo">David Ricardo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Robert_Malthus" title="Thomas Robert Malthus">Thomas Robert Malthus</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Stuart_Mill" title="John Stuart Mill">John Stuart Mill</a>, and influenced later <a href="/wiki/Marxian_economics" title="Marxian economics">Marxian economics</a>. Other, mainly Italian, 18th century economists maintained that all desired goods and services were productive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shubik2014_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shubik2014-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_service-goods_continuum">The service-goods continuum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goods_and_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The service-goods continuum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Service-goods_continuum.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="220" height="400" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9c/Service-goods_continuum.png" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Service-goods_continuum.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Service-goods continuum</div></div></div>
<p>The division of <a href="/wiki/Consumables" title="Consumables">consumables</a> into services is a simplification: these are not discrete categories. Most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Business_theorist" title="Business theorist">business theorists</a> see a continuum with pure service at one endpoint and pure <a href="/wiki/Commodity" title="Commodity">commodity</a> goods at the other. Most <a href="/wiki/Product_(business)" title="Product (business)">products</a> fall between these two extremes. For example, a <a href="/wiki/Restaurant" title="Restaurant">restaurant</a> provides a physical good (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Processed_food" title="Processed food">prepared food</a>), but also provides services in the form of ambience, the setting and clearing of the table, etc. Although some utilities, such as <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Communications_service_provider" title="Communications service provider">communications service providers</a>, exclusively provide services, other utilities deliver physical goods, such as <a href="/wiki/Water_industry" title="Water industry">water utilities</a>. For <a href="/wiki/Government_procurement" title="Government procurement">public sector contracting</a> purposes, <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> supply is defined among goods rather than services in the <a href="/wiki/Government_procurement_in_the_European_Union" title="Government procurement in the European Union">European Union</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> whereas under United States <a href="/wiki/Government_procurement_in_the_United_States" title="Government procurement in the United States">federal procurement regulations</a> it is treated as a service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Goods are normally structural and can be transferred in an instant while services are delivered over a period of time. Goods can be returned while a service once delivered cannot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Goods are not always tangible and may be virtual e.g. a book may be paper or electronic.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Marketing" title="Marketing">Marketing</a> theory makes use of the service-goods continuum as an important concept<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> which 'enables marketers to see the relative goods/services composition of total products'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>In a narrower sense, service refers to <a href="/wiki/Quality_(business)" title="Quality (business)">quality</a> of <a href="/wiki/Customer_service" title="Customer service">customer service</a>: the measured appropriateness of assistance and support provided to a customer. This particular usage occurs frequently in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retailing" title="Retailing">retailing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Goods_and_services_in_international_law">Goods and services in international law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goods_and_services&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Goods and services in international law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/International_(Nice)_Classification_of_Goods_and_Services" title="International (Nice) Classification of Goods and Services">International (Nice) Classification of Goods and Services</a></div>
<p>Distinctions are made between goods and services in the context of <a href="/wiki/International_trade_law" title="International trade law">international trade liberalization</a>. For example, the <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Organization" title="World Trade Organization">World Trade Organization</a>'s <a href="/wiki/General_Agreement_on_Tariffs_and_Trade" title="General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade">General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade</a> (GATT) covers international trade in goods<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/General_Agreement_on_Trade_in_Services" title="General Agreement on Trade in Services">General Agreement on Trade in Services</a> (GATS) covers the <a href="/wiki/Tertiary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Tertiary sector of the economy">services sector</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
