<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Heat of combustion</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This article <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Heat_of_combustion&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span> <small><i>(June 2015)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The <b>heating value</b> (or <b>energy value</b> or <b>calorific value</b>) of a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_substance" title="Chemical substance">substance</a>, usually a <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a> or <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Food_energy" title="Food energy">food energy</a>), is the amount of <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> released during the combustion of a specified amount of it.
</p><p>The <i>calorific value</i> is the total <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> released as <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> when a substance undergoes complete <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> with <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_conditions" title="Standard conditions">standard conditions</a>. The chemical reaction is typically a <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a> or other organic molecule reacting with oxygen to form <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> and release heat. It may be expressed with the quantities:
</p>
<ul><li>energy/<a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mole</a> of fuel</li>
<li>energy/mass of fuel</li>
<li>energy/volume of the fuel</li></ul>
<p>There are two kinds of heat of combustion, called higher and lower heating value, depending on how much the products are allowed to cool and whether compounds like <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O</span> are allowed to condense.
The values are conventionally measured with a <a href="/wiki/Calorimeter#Bomb_calorimeters" title="Calorimeter">bomb calorimeter</a>. They may also be calculated as the difference between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_enthalpy_change_of_formation" title="Standard enthalpy change of formation">heat of formation</a> Δ<i>H</i><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"><s>o</s></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">f</sub></span> of the products and reactants (though this approach is somewhat artificial since most heats of formation are calculated from measured heats of combustion). For a fuel of composition C<sub><i>c</i></sub>H<sub><i>h</i></sub>O<sub><i>o</i></sub>N<sub><i>n</i></sub>, the (higher) heat of combustion is <span class="nowrap">418 kJ/mol (<i>c</i> + 0.3 <i>h</i> – 0.5 <i>o</i>)</span> usually to a good approximation (±3%),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmidt-Rohr_15_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schmidt-Rohr_15-1">[1]</a></sup> though it can be drastically wrong if <i>o</i> + <i>n</i> &gt; <i>c</i> (for instance in the case of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitroglycerine" title="Nitroglycerine">nitroglycerine</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">C<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">5</sub></span>N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">9</sub></span></span>) this formula would predict a heat of combustion of 0<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>). The value corresponds to an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermic</a> reaction (a negative change in <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy" title="Enthalpy">enthalpy</a>) because the double <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">bond</a> in molecular oxygen is much weaker than other double bonds or pairs of single bonds, particularly those in the combustion products carbon dioxide and water; conversion of the weak bonds in <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> to the stronger bonds in <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> releases energy as heat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmidt-Rohr_15_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schmidt-Rohr_15-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>By convention, the heat of combustion is defined to be the heat released for the complete combustion of a compound in its standard state to form stable products in their standard states:  hydrogen is converted to water (in its liquid state), carbon is converted to carbon dioxide gas, and nitrogen is converted to nitrogen gas.  That is, the heat of combustion, Δ<i>H°</i><sub>comb</sub>, is the heat of reaction of the following process:  
</p>
<dl><dd>C<i><sub>x</sub></i>H<i><sub>y</sub></i>N<i><sub>z</sub></i>O<i><sub>n</sub></i> (std.) + O<sub>2</sub> (g, xs.) → <i>x</i>CO<sub>2</sub> (g) + (<i>y</i>/2)H<sub>2</sub>O (l) + (<i>z</i>/2)N<sub>2</sub> (g)</dd></dl>
<p>Chlorine and sulfur are not quite standardized; they are usually assumed to convert to hydrogen chloride gas and SO<sub>2</sub> or SO<sub>3</sub> gas, respectively, or to dilute aqueous hydrochloric and sulfuric acids, respectively, when the combustion is conducted in a bomb containing some water quantity of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Higher_heating_value">Higher heating value</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_of_combustion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Higher heating value">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The quantity known as higher heating value  (<b>HHV</b>) (or <i>gross energy</i> or <i>upper heating value</i> or <i>gross calorific value</i> (<b>GCV</b>) or <i>higher calorific value</i>  (<b>HCV</b>)) is determined by bringing all the products of combustion back to the original pre-combustion temperature, and in particular condensing any vapor produced. Such measurements often use a standard temperature of 15 °C (59 °F; 288 K)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. This is the same as the thermodynamic heat of combustion since the <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy" title="Enthalpy">enthalpy</a> change for the reaction assumes a common temperature of the compounds before and after combustion, in which case the water produced by combustion is condensed to a liquid. The higher heating value takes into account the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latent_heat_of_vaporization" title="Latent heat of vaporization">latent heat of vaporization</a> of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> in the combustion products, and is useful in calculating heating values for fuels where <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensation</a> of the reaction products is practical (e.g., in a gas-fired <a href="/wiki/Boiler" title="Boiler">boiler</a> used for space heat). In other words, HHV assumes all the water component is in liquid state at the end of combustion (in product of combustion) and that heat delivered at temperatures below 150 °C (302 °F) can be put to use
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lower_heating_value">Lower heating value</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_of_combustion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lower heating value">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The quantity known as lower heating value (<b>LHV</b>) (<i>net calorific value</i> (<b>NCV</b>) or <i>lower calorific value</i> (<b>LCV</b>)) is determined by subtracting the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_of_vaporization" title="Heat of vaporization">heat of vaporization</a> of the water from the higher heating value.  This treats any H<sub>2</sub>O formed as a vapor. The energy required to vaporize the water therefore is not released as heat.
</p><p>LHV calculations assume that the water component of a combustion process is in vapor state at the end of combustion, as opposed to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Higher_heating_value" title="Higher heating value">higher heating value</a> (HHV) (a.k.a. <i>gross calorific value</i> or <i>gross CV</i>) which assumes that all of the water in a combustion process is in a liquid state after a combustion process.
</p><p>The LHV assumes that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latent_heat_of_vaporization" title="Latent heat of vaporization">latent heat of vaporization</a> of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> in the fuel and the reaction products is not recovered. It is useful in comparing fuels where condensation of the combustion products is impractical, or heat at a temperature below 150 °C (302 °F) cannot be put to use.
</p><p>The above is but one definition of lower heating value adopted by the <a href="/wiki/American_Petroleum_Institute" title="American Petroleum Institute">American Petroleum Institute</a> (API) and uses a reference temperature of 60 °F (16 °C; 289 K).
</p><p>Another definition, used by Gas Processors Suppliers Association (GPSA) and originally used by API (data collected for API research project 44), is the <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy" title="Enthalpy">enthalpy</a> of all combustion products minus the enthalpy of the fuel at the reference temperature (API research project 44 used 25 °C. GPSA currently uses 60 °F), minus the enthalpy of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stoichiometric" title="Stoichiometric">stoichiometric</a> <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> (O<sub>2</sub>) at the reference temperature, minus the heat of vaporization of the vapor content of the combustion products.
</p><p>The distinction between the two is that this second definition assumes that the combustion products are all returned to the reference temperature and the heat content from the condensing vapor is considered not to be useful.
This is more easily calculated from the higher heating value than when using the preceding definition and will in fact give a slightly different answer.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gross_heating_value">Gross heating value</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_of_combustion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Gross heating value">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Gross heating value</b> (see <b><a href="#AR">AR</a></b>) accounts for water in the exhaust leaving as vapor, and includes liquid water in the fuel prior to combustion. This value is important for fuels like <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a> or <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, which will usually contain some amount of water prior to burning.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measuring_heating_values">Measuring heating values</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_of_combustion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Measuring heating values">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The higher heating value is experimentally determined in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bomb_calorimeter" title="Bomb calorimeter">bomb calorimeter</a>. The combustion of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stoichiometric" title="Stoichiometric">stoichiometric</a> mixture of fuel and oxidizer (e.g. two moles of hydrogen and one mole of oxygen) in a steel container at 25 °C (77 °F) is initiated by an ignition device and the reactions allowed to complete. When hydrogen and oxygen react during combustion, water vapor is produced. The vessel and its contents are then cooled to the original 25 °C and the higher heating value is determined as the heat released between identical initial and final temperatures.
</p><p>When the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lower_heating_value" title="Lower heating value">lower heating value</a> (LHV) is determined, cooling is stopped at 150 °C and the reaction heat is only partially recovered. The limit of 150 °C is based on <a href="/wiki/Acid_gas" title="Acid gas">acid gas</a> dew-point.
</p><p>Note: Higher heating value (HHV) is calculated with the <b>product of water being in liquid form</b> while lower heating value (LHV) is calculated with the <b>product of water being in vapor form</b>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_between_heating_values">Relation between heating values</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_of_combustion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Relation between heating values">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The difference between the two heating values depends on the chemical composition of the fuel. In the case of pure carbon or carbon monoxide, the two heating values are almost identical, the difference being the sensible heat content of carbon dioxide between 150 °C and 25 °C (<a href="/wiki/Sensible_heat" title="Sensible heat">sensible heat</a> exchange causes a change of temperature. In contrast, <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">latent heat</a> is added or subtracted for <a href="/wiki/Phase_transition" title="Phase transition">phase transitions</a> at constant temperature. Examples: <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_vaporization" title="Enthalpy of vaporization">heat of vaporization</a> or <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_fusion" title="Enthalpy of fusion">heat of fusion</a>). For <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> the difference is much more significant as it includes the sensible heat of water vapor between 150 °C and 100 °C, the latent heat of condensation at 100 °C, and the sensible heat of the condensed water between 100 °C and 25 °C. All in all, the higher heating value of hydrogen is 18.2% above its lower heating value (142 MJ/kg vs. 120 MJ/kg). For <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrocarbons" title="Hydrocarbons">hydrocarbons</a> the difference depends on the hydrogen content of the fuel. For <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel</a> the higher heating value exceeds the lower heating value by about 10% and 7% respectively, and for natural gas about 11%.
</p><p>A common method of relating HHV to LHV is:

<p>where <i>H</i><sub>v</sub> is the heat of vaporization of water, n<sub>H<sub>2</sub>O,out</sub> is the moles of water vaporized and n<sub>fuel,in</sub> is the number of moles of fuel combusted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Most applications that burn fuel produce water vapor, which is unused and thus wastes its heat content. In such applications, the lower heating value must be used to give a 'benchmark' for the process.</li>
<li>However, for true energy calculations in some specific cases, the higher heating value is correct. This is particularly relevant for <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, whose high <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> content produces much water, when it is burned in <a href="/wiki/Condensing_boiler" title="Condensing boiler">condensing boilers</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_plants" title="Power plants">power plants</a> with <a href="/wiki/Flue-gas_condensation" title="Flue-gas condensation">flue-gas condensation</a> that condense the water vapor produced by combustion, recovering heat which would otherwise be wasted.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_of_terms">Usage of terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_of_combustion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Usage of terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many engine manufacturers rate their engine fuel consumption by the lower heating values. American consumers should be aware that the corresponding fuel-consumption figure based on the higher heating value will be somewhat higher.
</p><p>The difference between HHV and LHV definitions causes endless confusion when quoters do not bother to state the convention being used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> since there is typically a 10% difference between the two methods for a power plant burning natural gas. For simply benchmarking part of a reaction the LHV may be appropriate, but HHV should be used for overall energy efficiency calculations, if only to avoid confusion, and in any case the value or convention should be clearly stated.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accounting_for_moisture">Accounting for moisture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_of_combustion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Accounting for moisture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Both HHV and LHV can be expressed in terms of AR (all moisture counted), MF and MAF (only water from combustion of hydrogen). AR, MF, and MAF are commonly used for indicating the heating values of coal:
</p>
<ul><li><span id="AR"></span> <b>AR</b> (as received) indicates that the fuel heating value has been measured with all moisture- and ash-forming minerals present.</li>
<li><b>MF</b> (moisture-free) or <b>dry</b> indicates that the fuel heating value has been measured after the fuel has been dried of all inherent moisture but still retaining its ash-forming minerals.</li>
<li><b>MAF</b> (moisture- and ash-free) or <b>DAF</b> (dry and ash-free) indicates that the fuel heating value has been measured in the absence of inherent moisture- and ash-forming minerals.</li></ul>
