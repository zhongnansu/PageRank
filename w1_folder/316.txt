<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Brittleness</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Brittle" redirects here. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Brittle_(disambiguation)" title="Brittle (disambiguation)">Brittle (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<ol><li class="mw-empty-elt"></li></ol>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Glass_fracture.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Glass_fracture.jpg/200px-Glass_fracture.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Glass_fracture.jpg/300px-Glass_fracture.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Glass_fracture.jpg/400px-Glass_fracture.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Glass_fracture.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Brittle fracture in <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cast_iron_tensile_test.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="861" height="279" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Cast_iron_tensile_test.JPG/200px-Cast_iron_tensile_test.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Cast_iron_tensile_test.JPG/300px-Cast_iron_tensile_test.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Cast_iron_tensile_test.JPG/400px-Cast_iron_tensile_test.JPG 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cast_iron_tensile_test.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Brittle fracture in <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">cast iron</a> tensile testpieces</div></div></div><p>A material is <b>brittle</b> if, when subjected to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stress_(physics)" title="Stress (physics)">stress</a>, it breaks without significant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plastic_Deformation" title="Plastic Deformation">plastic deformation</a>. Brittle materials absorb relatively little <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> prior to fracture, even those of high <a href="/wiki/Strength_of_materials" title="Strength of materials">strength</a>. Breaking is often accompanied by a snapping sound. Brittle materials include most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceramic_materials" title="Ceramic materials">ceramics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glasses</a> (which do not deform plastically) and some <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymers</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polymethyl_methacrylate" title="Polymethyl methacrylate">PMMA</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polystyrene" title="Polystyrene">polystyrene</a>. Many <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steels</a> become brittle at low temperatures (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ductile-brittle_transition#Ductile-brittle_transition_temperature" title="Ductile-brittle transition">ductile-brittle transition temperature</a>), depending on their composition and processing.
</p><p>When used in <a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">materials science</a>, it is generally applied to materials that fail when there is little or no <a href="/wiki/Plasticity_(physics)" title="Plasticity (physics)">plastic deformation</a> before failure. One proof is to match the broken halves, which should fit exactly since no plastic deformation has occurred.
</p><p>When a material has reached the limit of its strength, it usually has the option of either deformation or fracture.  A naturally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malleable" title="Malleable">malleable</a> metal can be made stronger by impeding the mechanisms of plastic deformation (reducing <a href="/wiki/Grain_size" title="Grain size">grain size</a>, <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_hardening" title="Precipitation hardening">precipitation hardening</a>, <a href="/wiki/Work_hardening" title="Work hardening">work hardening</a>, etc.), but if this is taken to an extreme, fracture becomes the more likely outcome, and the material can become brittle.  Improving material <a href="/wiki/Toughness" title="Toughness">toughness</a> is therefore a balancing act.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toughening">Toughening</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brittleness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Toughening">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brittle_v_ductile_stress-strain_behaviour.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="750" data-file-width="660" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Brittle_v_ductile_stress-strain_behaviour.png/200px-Brittle_v_ductile_stress-strain_behaviour.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Brittle_v_ductile_stress-strain_behaviour.png/300px-Brittle_v_ductile_stress-strain_behaviour.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Brittle_v_ductile_stress-strain_behaviour.png/400px-Brittle_v_ductile_stress-strain_behaviour.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brittle_v_ductile_stress-strain_behaviour.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Graph comparing <a href="/wiki/Stress%E2%80%93strain_curve" title="Stress–strain curve">stress–strain curves</a> for brittle and ductile materials</div></div></div><p>This principle generalizes to other classes of material.  Naturally brittle materials, such as  <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>, are not difficult to toughen effectively.  Most such techniques involve one of two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fracture_toughening_mechanisms" title="Fracture toughening mechanisms">mechanisms</a>: to deflect or absorb the tip of a propagating crack, or to create carefully controlled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stress_(physics)#Residual_stress" title="Stress (physics)">residual stresses</a> so that cracks from certain predictable sources will be forced closed. The first principle is used in <a href="/wiki/Laminated_glass" title="Laminated glass">laminated glass</a> where two sheets of glass are separated by an interlayer of <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_butyral" title="Polyvinyl butyral">polyvinyl butyral</a>, which as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viscoelastic" title="Viscoelastic">viscoelastic</a> polymer absorbs the growing crack. The second method is used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toughened_glass" title="Toughened glass">toughened glass</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-stressed_concrete" title="Pre-stressed concrete">pre-stressed concrete</a>. A demonstration of glass toughening is provided by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Rupert%27s_Drop" title="Prince Rupert's Drop">Prince Rupert's Drop</a>. Brittle <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polymers" title="Polymers">polymers</a> can be toughened by using metal particles to initiate crazes when a sample is stressed, a good example being <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_impact_polystyrene" title="High impact polystyrene">high-impact polystyrene</a> or HIPS. The least brittle structural ceramics are <a href="/wiki/Silicon_carbide" title="Silicon carbide">silicon carbide</a> (mainly by virtue of its high strength) and transformation-toughened <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zirconia" title="Zirconia">zirconia</a>.
</p><p>A different philosophy is used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Composite_materials" title="Composite materials">composite materials</a>, where brittle <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glass_fibre" title="Glass fibre">glass fibres</a>, for example, are embedded in a ductile matrix such as <a href="/wiki/Polyester_resin" title="Polyester resin">polyester resin</a>. When strained, cracks are formed at the glass–matrix interface, but so many are formed that much energy is absorbed and the material is thereby toughened. The same principle is used in creating <a href="/wiki/Metal_matrix_composite" title="Metal matrix composite">metal matrix composites</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_of_pressure">Effect of pressure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brittleness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Effect of pressure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Generally, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brittle_strength" title="Brittle strength">brittle strength</a></i> of a material can be increased by <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a>. This happens as an example in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brittle-ductile_transition_zone" title="Brittle-ductile transition zone">brittle-ductile transition zone</a> at an approximate depth of 10 kilometres (6.2 mi) in the <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">Earth's crust</a>, at which rock becomes less likely to fracture, and more likely to deform <a href="/wiki/Ductility" title="Ductility">ductilely</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Rheid" title="Rheid">rheid</a>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Crack_growth">Crack growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brittleness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Crack growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Supersonic_fracture" title="Supersonic fracture">Supersonic fracture</a> is crack motion faster than the speed of sound in a brittle material. Buehler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Huajian_Gao" title="Huajian Gao">Huajian Gao</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IBM_Almaden_Research_Center" title="IBM Almaden Research Center">IBM Almaden Research Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/San_Jose,_California" title="San Jose, California">San Jose</a>, California (<a href="/wiki/Farid_F._Abraham" title="Farid F. Abraham">Farid F. Abraham</a>).
</p>
