<p>In <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, an <b>organic compound</b> is generally any <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> that contains <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>.  Due to carbon's ability to <a href="/wiki/Catenation" title="Catenation">catenate</a> (form chains with other carbon atoms), millions of organic compounds are known. Study of the properties and synthesis of organic compounds is the discipline known as <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a>.  For historical reasons, a few classes of carbon-containing compounds (e.g., carbonates and cyanides), along with a handful of other exceptions (e.g., carbon dioxide), are not classified as organic compounds and are considered inorganic.  No consensus exists among chemists on precisely which carbon-containing compounds are excluded, making the definition of an organic compound elusive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-text_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-text-1">[1]</a></sup>  Although organic compounds only make up a small percentage of the Earth's crust, they are of central importance because all known life is based on organic compounds. Most synthetically produced organic compounds are ultimately derived from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petrochemicals" title="Petrochemicals">petrochemicals</a> consisting mainly of <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions_of_organic_vs_inorganic">Definitions of organic vs inorganic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions of organic vs inorganic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For historical reasons discussed below, a few types of carbon-containing compounds, such as <a href="/wiki/Carbide" title="Carbide">carbides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonates</a>, simple <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxides" title="Oxides">oxides</a> of carbon (for example, CO and CO<sub>2</sub>), and <a href="/wiki/Cyanide" title="Cyanide">cyanides</a> are considered <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic</a>. <a href="/wiki/Allotropes_of_carbon" title="Allotropes of carbon">Allotropes of carbon</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">diamond</a>, <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fullerene" title="Fullerene">fullerenes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_nanotube" title="Carbon nanotube">carbon nanotubes</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> are also excluded because they are simple substances composed of only a single element and therefore are not generally considered to be chemical <i>compounds</i>.  
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vitalism">Vitalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Vitalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Vitalism" title="Vitalism">Vitalism</a></div>
<p>For many centuries, Western physicians and chemists believed in <a href="/wiki/Vitalism" title="Vitalism">vitalism</a>. This was the widespread conception that substances found in organic nature are created from the chemical elements by the action of a "vital force" or "life-force" (<i>vis vitalis</i>) that only living organisms possess. Vitalism taught that these "organic" compounds were fundamentally different from the "inorganic" compounds that could be obtained from the elements by chemical manipulations.
</p><p>Vitalism survived for a while even after the rise of modern ideas about the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_theory" title="Atomic theory">atomic theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical elements</a>. It first came under question in 1824, when <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_W%C3%B6hler" title="Friedrich Wöhler">Friedrich Wöhler</a> synthesized <a href="/wiki/Oxalic_acid" title="Oxalic acid">oxalic acid</a>, a compound known to occur only in living organisms, from <a href="/wiki/Cyanogen" title="Cyanogen">cyanogen</a>. A more decisive experiment was <a href="/wiki/W%C3%B6hler_synthesis" title="Wöhler synthesis">Wöhler's 1828 synthesis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Urea" title="Urea">urea</a> from the inorganic <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a> <a href="/wiki/Potassium_cyanate" title="Potassium cyanate">potassium cyanate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_sulfate" title="Ammonium sulfate">ammonium sulfate</a>. Urea had long been considered an "organic" compound, as it was known to occur only in the urine of living organisms. Wöhler's experiments were followed by many others, in which increasingly complex "organic" substances were produced from "inorganic" ones without the involvement of any living organism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_classification_and_ambiguities">Modern classification and ambiguities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Modern classification and ambiguities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:L-isoleucine-3D-balls.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1100" data-file-width="1086" height="223" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/L-isoleucine-3D-balls.png/220px-L-isoleucine-3D-balls.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/L-isoleucine-3D-balls.png/330px-L-isoleucine-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/L-isoleucine-3D-balls.png/440px-L-isoleucine-3D-balls.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:L-isoleucine-3D-balls.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Isoleucine" title="Isoleucine"><small>L</small>-isoleucine</a> molecule, C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>13</sub>NO<sub>2</sub>, showing features typical of organic compounds. Carbon atoms are in black, hydrogens gray, oxygens red, and nitrogen blue.</div></div></div>
<p>Even though vitalism has been discredited, scientific nomenclature retains the distinction between <i>organic</i> and <i>inorganic</i> compounds. The modern meaning of <i>organic compound</i> is any compound that contains a significant amount of carbon—even though many of the organic compounds known today have no connection to any substance found in living organisms.  The term <i>carbogenic</i> has been proposed by E. J. Corey as a modern alternative to <i>organic</i>, but this neologism remains relatively obscure.
</p><p>The organic compound <a href="/wiki/Isoleucine" title="Isoleucine"><small>L</small>-isoleucine</a> molecule presents some features typical of organic compounds:  <a href="/wiki/Carbon%E2%80%93carbon_bond" title="Carbon–carbon bond">carbon–carbon bonds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon%E2%80%93hydrogen_bond" title="Carbon–hydrogen bond">carbon–hydrogen bonds</a>, as well as covalent bonds from carbon to oxygen and to nitrogen.
</p><p>As described in detail below, any definition of organic compound that uses simple, broadly applicable criteria turns out to be unsatisfactory, to varying degrees.  The modern, commonly accepted definition of organic compound essentially amounts to any carbon containing compound, excluding several classes of substances traditionally considered as 'inorganic'.  However, the list of substances so excluded varies from author to author.  Still, it is generally agreed upon that there are (at least) a few carbon containing compounds that should not be considered organic.  For instance, almost all authorities would require the exclusion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alloys" title="Alloys">alloys</a> that contain carbon, including <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> (which contains <a href="/wiki/Cementite" title="Cementite">cementite</a>, Fe<sub>3</sub>C), as well as other metal and semimetal carbides (including "ionic" carbides, e.g, Al<sub>4</sub>C<sub>3</sub> and <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbide" title="Calcium carbide">CaC<sub>2</sub></a> and "covalent" carbides, e.g. B<sub>4</sub>C and SiC, and graphite intercalation compounds, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Graphite_intercalation_compound" title="Graphite intercalation compound">KC<sub>8</sub></a>). Other compounds and materials that are considered 'inorganic' by most authorities include: metal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonates" title="Carbonates">carbonates</a>, simple <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxides" title="Oxides">oxides</a> (CO, CO<sub>2</sub>, and arguably, C<sub>3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allotropes" title="Allotropes">allotropes</a> of carbon, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyanides" title="Cyanides">cyanide</a> derivatives not containing an organic residue (e.g., KCN, (CN)<sub>2</sub>, BrCN, CNO<sup>−</sup>, etc.), and heavier analogs thereof (e.g., CP<sup>−</sup> '<a href="/wiki/Cyaphide" title="Cyaphide">cyaphide</a> anion', CSe, COS; although CS<sub>2</sub> '<a href="/wiki/Carbon_disulfide" title="Carbon disulfide">carbon disulfide</a>' is often classed as an <i>organic</i> solvent).  Halides of carbon without hydrogen (e.g., CF<sub>4</sub> and CClF<sub>3</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Phosgene" title="Phosgene">phosgene</a> (COCl<sub>2</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Carborane" title="Carborane">carboranes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metal_carbonyl" title="Metal carbonyl">metal carbonyls</a> (e.g., nickel carbonyl), <a href="/wiki/Mellitic_anhydride" title="Mellitic anhydride">mellitic anhydride</a> (C<sub>12</sub>O<sub>9</sub>), and other exotic <a href="/wiki/Oxocarbon" title="Oxocarbon">oxocarbons</a> are also considered inorganic by some authorities.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Nickel_tetracarbonyl" title="Nickel tetracarbonyl">Nickel carbonyl</a> (Ni(CO)<sub>4</sub>) and other metal carbonyls present an interesting case.  They are often volatile liquids, like many organic compounds, yet they contain only carbon bonded to a transition metal and to oxygen and are often prepared directly from metal and carbon monoxide.  Nickel carbonyl is frequently considered to be <i>organometallic</i>.  Although many organometallic chemists employ a broad definition, in which any compound containing a carbon-metal covalent bond is considered <a href="/wiki/Organometallic_chemistry" title="Organometallic chemistry">organometallic</a>, it is debatable whether organometallic compounds form a subset of organic compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Metal complexes with organic ligands but no carbon-metal bonds (e.g., Cu(OAc)<sub>2</sub>) are not considered organometallic; instead they are classed as <i>metalorganic</i>.  Likewise, it is also unclear whether metalorganic compounds should automatically be considered organic.
</p><p>The relatively narrow definition of organic compounds as those containing C-H bonds excludes compounds that are (historically and practically) considered organic. Neither urea nor oxalic acid is organic by this definition, yet they were two key compounds in the vitalism debate. The <a href="/wiki/Nomenclature_of_Organic_Chemistry" title="Nomenclature of Organic Chemistry">IUPAC Blue Book</a> on organic nomenclature specifically mentions urea<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and oxalic acid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Other compounds lacking C-H bonds but traditionally considered organic include <a href="/wiki/Benzenehexol" title="Benzenehexol">benzenehexol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesoxalic_acid" title="Mesoxalic acid">mesoxalic acid</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride" title="Carbon tetrachloride">carbon tetrachloride</a>. <a href="/wiki/Mellitic_acid" title="Mellitic acid">Mellitic acid</a>, which contains no C-H bonds, is considered a possible organic substance in <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Martian</a> soil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>  Terrestrially, it, and its anhydride, mellitic anhydride, are associated with the mineral <a href="/wiki/Mellite" title="Mellite">mellite</a> (Al<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>(COO)<sub>6</sub>·16H<sub>2</sub>O).
</p><p>A slightly broader definition of organic compound includes all compounds bearing C-H or C-C bonds.  This would still exclude urea.  Moreover, this definition still leads to somewhat arbitrary divisions in sets of carbon-halogen compounds.  For example, <a href="/wiki/Tetrafluoromethane" title="Tetrafluoromethane">CF<sub>4</sub></a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride" title="Carbon tetrachloride">CCl<sub>4</sub></a> would be considered by this rule to be "inorganic", whereas <a href="/wiki/Fluoroform" title="Fluoroform">CF<sub>3</sub>H</a>  and <a href="/wiki/Chloroform" title="Chloroform">CHCl<sub>3</sub></a> would be organic, though these compounds share many physical and chemical properties.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry#Classification_of_organic_compounds" title="Organic chemistry">Organic chemistry § Classification of organic compounds</a></div>
<p>Organic compounds may be classified in a variety of ways. One major distinction is between natural and synthetic compounds. Organic compounds can also be classified or subdivided by the presence of <a href="/wiki/Heteroatom" title="Heteroatom">heteroatoms</a>, e.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organometallic_compound" title="Organometallic compound">organometallic compounds</a>, which feature bonds between carbon and a <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Organophosphorus_compound" title="Organophosphorus compound">organophosphorus compounds</a>, which feature bonds between carbon and a <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>.
</p><p>Another distinction, based on the size of organic compounds, distinguishes between <a href="/wiki/Small_molecule" title="Small molecule">small molecules</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymers</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_compounds">Natural compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Natural compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_compound" title="Natural compound">Natural compounds</a> refer to those that are produced by plants or animals. Many of these are still extracted from natural sources because they would be more expensive to produce artificially. Examples include most <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugars</a>, some <a href="/wiki/Alkaloid" title="Alkaloid">alkaloids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Terpenoid" title="Terpenoid">terpenoids</a>, certain nutrients such as <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_B12" title="Vitamin B12">vitamin B<sub>12</sub></a>, and, in general, those natural products with large or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stereoisomer" title="Stereoisomer">stereoisometrically</a> complicated molecules present in reasonable concentrations in living organisms.
</p><p>Further compounds of prime importance in <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemistry</a> are <a href="/wiki/Antigen" title="Antigen">antigens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">hormones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fatty_acid" title="Fatty acid">fatty acids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neurotransmitter" title="Neurotransmitter">neurotransmitters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid" title="Nucleic acid">nucleic acids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peptide" title="Peptide">peptides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lectin" title="Lectin">lectins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vitamin" title="Vitamin">vitamins</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fats_and_oils" title="Fats and oils">fats and oils</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthetic_compounds">Synthetic compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Synthetic compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Compounds that are prepared by reaction of other compounds are known as "synthetic". They may be either compounds that already are found in plants or animals or those that do not occur naturally.
</p><p>Most <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymers</a> (a category that includes all <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastics</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubber" title="Rubber">rubbers</a>), are organic synthetic or semi-synthetic compounds.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biotechnology">Biotechnology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Biotechnology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many organic compounds—two examples are <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Insulin" title="Insulin">insulin</a>—are manufactured industrially using organisms such as bacteria and yeast. Typically, the <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> of an organism is altered to express  compounds  not ordinarily produced by the organism. Many such <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">biotechnology</a>-engineered compounds did not previously exist in nature.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Databases">Databases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organic_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Databases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Chemical_Abstracts_Service" title="Chemical Abstracts Service">CAS</a></i> database is the most comprehensive repository for data on organic compounds. The search tool <i><a href="/wiki/Chemical_Abstracts_Service#SciFinder" title="Chemical Abstracts Service">SciFinder</a></i> is offered.</li>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/Beilstein_database" title="Beilstein database">Beilstein database</a></i> contains information on 9.8 million substances, covers the scientific literature from 1771 to the present, and is today accessible via <a href="/wiki/Reaxys" title="Reaxys">Reaxys</a>. Structures and a large diversity of physical and chemical properties is available for each substance, with reference to original literature.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/PubChem" title="PubChem">PubChem</a></i> contains 18.4 million entries on compounds and especially covers the field of <a href="/wiki/Medicinal_chemistry" title="Medicinal chemistry">medicinal chemistry</a>.</li></ul>
<p>A great number of more specialized databases exist for diverse branches of organic chemistry.
</p>
