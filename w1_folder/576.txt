<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Compressed natural gas</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"CNG" redirects here. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/CNG_(disambiguation)" title="CNG (disambiguation)">CNG (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CNGlogo.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="110" data-file-width="142" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/CNGlogo.svg/220px-CNGlogo.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/CNGlogo.svg/330px-CNGlogo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/CNGlogo.svg/440px-CNGlogo.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CNGlogo.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Blue diamond symbol used on CNG-powered vehicles in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CNG_logo_China.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="291" data-file-width="404" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/CNG_logo_China.svg/220px-CNG_logo_China.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/CNG_logo_China.svg/330px-CNG_logo_China.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/CNG_logo_China.svg/440px-CNG_logo_China.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CNG_logo_China.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Green bordered white diamond symbol used on CNG-powered vehicles in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a></div></div></div>
<p><b>Compressed natural gas</b> (<b>CNG</b>) (methane stored at high pressure) is a fuel which can be used in place of <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> , <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel fuel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a>/<a href="/wiki/Liquefied_petroleum_gas" title="Liquefied petroleum gas">LPG</a>. CNG combustion produces fewer undesirable gases than the fuels mentioned above. It is safer than other fuels in the event of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuel_spill" title="Fuel spill">spill</a>, because natural gas is lighter than air and disperses quickly when released. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biomethane" title="Biomethane">Biomethane</a> - cleaned up <a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">biogas</a> from <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_digestion" title="Anaerobic digestion">anaerobic digestion</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfills" title="Landfills">landfills</a> - can be used.
</p><p>CNG is made by compressing <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, (which is mainly composed of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, CH<sub>4</sub>), to less than 1 percent of the volume it occupies at <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure#Standard_atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">standard atmospheric pressure</a>. It is stored and distributed in hard containers at a pressure of 20–25 MPa (2,900–3,600 psi), usually in <a href="/wiki/Gas_cylinder" title="Gas cylinder">cylindrical</a> or spherical shapes.
</p><p>CNG is used in traditional gasoline/<a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engine</a> automobiles that have been modified or in vehicles which were manufactured for CNG use, either alone ('dedicated'), with a segregated gasoline system to extend range (dual fuel) or in conjunction with another fuel such as diesel (bi-fuel). <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas_vehicle" title="Natural gas vehicle">Natural gas vehicles</a> are increasingly used in <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-highest_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-highest-1">[1]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Asia-Pacific" title="Asia-Pacific">Asia-Pacific</a> region, Indian capital of <a href="/wiki/Delhi" title="Delhi">Delhi</a>, and other large cities like <a href="/wiki/Ahmedabad" title="Ahmedabad">Ahmedabad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">Mumbai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pune" title="Pune">Pune</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kolkata" title="Kolkata">Kolkata</a>—as well as cities such as Lucknow, Kanpur, etc. Its use is also increasing in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> because of rising gasoline prices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IANGV_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-IANGV-2">[2]</a></sup> In response to high fuel prices and environmental concerns, CNG is starting to be used also in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuk-tuk" title="Tuk-tuk">tuk-tuks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pickup_truck" title="Pickup truck">pickup trucks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transit_bus" title="Transit bus">transit</a> and <a href="/wiki/School_bus" title="School bus">school buses</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Train" title="Train">trains</a>.
</p><p>The cost and placement of fuel storage tanks is the major barrier to wider/quicker adoption of CNG as a fuel.  It is also why municipal government, public transportation vehicles were the most visible early adopters of it, as they can more quickly amortize the money invested in the new (and usually cheaper) fuel. In spite of these circumstances, the number of vehicles in the world using CNG has grown steadily (30 percent per year).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Now, as a result of the industry's steady growth, the cost of such fuel storage tanks has been brought down to a much more acceptable level. Especially for the CNG Type 1 and Type 2 tanks, many countries are able to make reliable and cost effective tanks for conversion need.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>CNG's volumetric <a href="/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">energy density</a> is estimated to be 42 percent that of <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_natural_gas" title="Liquefied natural gas">liquefied natural gas</a> (because it is not <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/liquified" title="wikt:liquified">liquefied</a>), and 25 percent that of diesel fuel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Users">Users</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Compressed_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Users">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Motor_vehicles">Motor vehicles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Compressed_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Motor vehicles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Posto_GNV_01_2009_485_CWB.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="1200" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Posto_GNV_01_2009_485_CWB.jpg/220px-Posto_GNV_01_2009_485_CWB.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Posto_GNV_01_2009_485_CWB.jpg/330px-Posto_GNV_01_2009_485_CWB.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Posto_GNV_01_2009_485_CWB.jpg/440px-Posto_GNV_01_2009_485_CWB.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Posto_GNV_01_2009_485_CWB.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>CNG pumps at a Brazilian fueling station</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas_vehicle" title="Natural gas vehicle">Natural gas vehicle</a></div>
<p>Worldwide, there were 14.8 million natural gas vehicles by 2011, led by <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> with 2.86 million, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> (2.85 million), <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> (2.07 million), <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> (1.7 million) and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> (1.1 million).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ngvc.org_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ngvc.org-6">[6]</a></sup> with the Asia-Pacific region leading with 5.7 million NGVs, followed by Latin America with almost four million vehicles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IANGV_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-IANGV-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Several manufacturers (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiat" title="Fiat">Fiat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Opel" title="Opel">Opel</a>/<a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peugeot" title="Peugeot">Peugeot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen" title="Volkswagen">Volkswagen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toyota" title="Toyota">Toyota</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honda" title="Honda">Honda</a> and others) sell bi-fuel cars. In 2006, Fiat introduced the Siena Tetrafuel in the Brazilian market, equipped with a 1.4L FIRE engine that runs on E100, E25 (Standard Brazilian Gasoline), Ethanol and CNG.
</p><p>Any existing gasoline vehicle can be converted to a dual-fuel (gasoline/CNG) vehicle. Authorized shops can do the retrofitting and involves installing a CNG cylinder, plumbing, a CNG injection system and the electronics. The cost of installing a CNG conversion kit<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  can often reach $8,000 on passenger cars and light trucks and is usually reserved for vehicles that travel many miles each year. CNG costs about 50% less than gasoline and emits up to 90% fewer emissions than gasoline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Locomotives">Locomotives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Compressed_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Locomotives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CNG locomotives are operated by several railroads.  The <a href="/wiki/Napa_Valley_Wine_Train" title="Napa Valley Wine Train">Napa Valley Wine Train</a> successfully retrofit a diesel locomotive to run on compressed natural gas before 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>  This converted locomotive was upgraded to utilize a computer controlled fuel injection system in May 1190, and is now the Napa Valley Wine Train's primary locomotive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ferrocarril_Central_Andino" title="Ferrocarril Central Andino">Ferrocarril Central Andino</a> in Peru, has run a CNG locomotive on a freight line since 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> CNG locomotives are usually diesel locomotives that have been converted to use compressed natural gas generators instead of diesel generators to generate the electricity that drives the traction motors.  Some CNG locomotives are able to fire their cylinders only when there is a demand for power, which, theoretically, gives them a higher fuel efficiency than conventional diesel engines.
CNG is also cheaper than petrol or diesel.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages">Advantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Compressed_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Advantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Emt-gas-2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="600" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Emt-gas-2.jpg/220px-Emt-gas-2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Emt-gas-2.jpg/330px-Emt-gas-2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Emt-gas-2.jpg/440px-Emt-gas-2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Emt-gas-2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>CNG powered bus in Madrid, Spain being refueled.</div></div></div>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Natural_gas_vehicle" title="Natural gas vehicle">Natural gas vehicle</a> have lower maintenance costs than other hydrocarbon-fuel-powered vehicles.</li>
<li>CNG fuel systems are sealed, preventing fuel losses from spills or evaporation.</li>
<li>Increased life of lubricating oils, as CNG does not contaminate and dilute the crankcase oil.</li>
<li>Being a gaseous fuel, CNG mixes easily and evenly in air.</li>
<li>CNG is less likely to ignite on hot surfaces, since it has a high auto-ignition temperature (540 °C), and a narrow range (5–15 percent) of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flammability" title="Flammability">flammability</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GAIL_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-GAIL-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>CNG-powered vehicles are considered to be safer than gasoline-powered vehicles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Less pollution and more efficiency:</i>
<ul><li>CNG emits significantly less pollution directly than gasoline or oil when combusted (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Unburned_hydrocarbon" title="Unburned hydrocarbon">unburned hydrocarbons</a> (UHC), <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> (CO), <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxides</a> (NO<sub>x</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_oxide" title="Sulfur oxide">sulfur oxides</a> (SO<sub>x</sub>) and PM (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">particulate matter</a>)). For example, an engine running on petrol for 100 km emits 22 kilograms of CO<sub>2</sub>, while covering the same distance on CNG emits only 16.3 kilograms of CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li>Due to lower carbon dioxide emissions, switching to CNG can help mitigate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emission" title="Greenhouse gas emission">greenhouse gas emissions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GAIL_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-GAIL-12">[12]</a></sup>  However, natural gas leaks (both in the direct use and in the production and delivery of the fuel) represent an increase in greenhouse gas emissions. The ability of CNG to reduce greenhouse gas emissions over the entire fuel lifecycle will depend on the source of the natural gas and the fuel it is replacing.</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_cycle_assessment" title="Life cycle assessment">lifecycle</a> greenhouse gas emissions for CNG compressed from California's pipeline natural gas is given a value of 67.70 grams of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_equivalent" title="Carbon dioxide equivalent">CO<sub>2</sub>-equivalent</a> per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megajoule" title="Megajoule">megajoule</a> (gCO<sub>2</sub>e/MJ) by CARB (the <a href="/wiki/California_Air_Resources_Board" title="California Air Resources Board">California Air Resources Board</a>), approximately 28 percent lower than the average gasoline fuel in that market (95.86 gCO<sub>2</sub>e/MJ).</li>
<li>CNG produced from landfill biogas was found by CARB to have the lowest greenhouse gas emissions of any fuel analyzed, with a value of 11.26 gCO<sub>2</sub>e/MJ (more than 88 percent lower than conventional gasoline) in the <a href="/wiki/Low-carbon_fuel_standard" title="Low-carbon fuel standard">low-carbon fuel standard</a> that went into effect on January 12, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li></ul></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Drawbacks">Drawbacks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Compressed_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Drawbacks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Compressed natural gas vehicles require a greater amount of space for fuel storage than conventional gasoline powered vehicles. Since it is a compressed gas, rather than a liquid like gasoline, CNG takes up more space for each GGE (<a href="/wiki/Gasoline_gallon_equivalent" title="Gasoline gallon equivalent">gasoline gallon equivalent</a>). However, the tanks used to store the CNG take up space in the trunk of a car or bed of a pickup truck which has been modified to additionally run on CNG. This problem is solved in factory-built CNG vehicles that install the tanks under the body of the vehicle, leaving the trunk free (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Fiat_Multipla" title="Fiat Multipla">Fiat Multipla</a>, New <a href="/wiki/Fiat_Panda" title="Fiat Panda">Fiat Panda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Touran" title="Volkswagen Touran">Volkswagen Touran</a> Ecofuel, <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Caddy" title="Volkswagen Caddy">Volkswagen Caddy</a> Ecofuel, Chevy Taxi - which sold in countries such as Peru). Another option is installation on roof (typical on buses), requiring, however, solution of structural strength issues. In 2014, a test (by the Danish Technological Institute) of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euro6" title="Euro6">Euro6</a> heavy vehicles on CNG and diesel showed that CNG had higher consumption, but same noise and pollution of CO2 and particulates, however NOx emission was lower.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Codes_and_standards">Codes and standards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Compressed_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Codes and standards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The lack of harmonized codes and standards across international jurisdictions is an additional barrier to NGV market penetration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oee.nrcan.gc.ca_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-oee.nrcan.gc.ca-19">[19]</a></sup> The International Organization for Standardization has an active technical committee working on a standard for natural gas fuelling stations for vehicles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Despite the lack of harmonized international codes, natural gas vehicles have an excellent global safety record.  Existing international standards include ISO 14469-2:2007 which applies to CNG vehicle nozzles and receptacle<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and ISO 15500-9:2012 specifies tests and requirements for the pressure regulator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>NFPA-52 covers natural gas vehicle safety standards in the US.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_with_other_natural_gas_fuels">Comparison with other natural gas fuels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Compressed_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Comparison with other natural gas fuels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Compressed natural gas is often confused with LNG (<a href="/wiki/Liquefied_natural_gas" title="Liquefied natural gas">liquefied natural gas</a>). Both are stored forms of natural gas. The main difference is that CNG is stored at ambient temperature and high pressure, while LNG is stored at low temperature and nearly ambient pressure. In their respective storage conditions, LNG is a <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a> and CNG is a <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_fluid" title="Supercritical fluid">supercritical fluid</a>. CNG has a lower cost of production and storage compared to LNG as it does not require an expensive cooling process and cryogenic tanks. However, CNG requires a much larger volume to store the energy equivalent of gasoline or petrol and the use of very high pressures (3000 to 4000 psi, or 205 to 275 bar). As a consequence of this, LNG is often used for transporting natural gas over large distances, in ships, trains or pipelines, where the gas is converted into CNG before distribution to the end user.
</p><p>CNG is being experimentally stored at lower pressure in a form known as an ANG (<a href="/wiki/Adsorbed_natural_gas" title="Adsorbed natural gas">adsorbed natural gas</a>) tank, where it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adsorbed" title="Adsorbed">adsorbed</a> at 35 bar (500 psi, the pressure of gas in natural gas pipelines) in various sponge like materials, such as <a href="/wiki/Activated_carbon" title="Activated carbon">activated carbon</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and MOFs (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal-organic_framework" title="Metal-organic framework">metal-organic frameworks</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The fuel is stored at similar or greater energy density than CNG. This means that vehicles can be refueled from the natural gas network without extra gas compression, the fuel tanks can be slimmed down and made of lighter, weaker materials.
</p><p>Compressed natural gas is sometimes mixed with hydrogen (<a href="/wiki/HCNG" title="HCNG">HCNG</a>) which increases the H/C ratio (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen/carbon_ratio&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hydrogen/carbon ratio (page does not exist)">hydrogen/carbon ratio</a>) of the fuel and gives it a <a href="/wiki/Flame_speed" title="Flame speed">flame speed</a> up to eight times higher than CNG.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_America">South America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Compressed_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: South America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Shell_station_in_Rosario.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1728" data-file-width="2304" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Shell_station_in_Rosario.jpg/220px-Shell_station_in_Rosario.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Shell_station_in_Rosario.jpg/330px-Shell_station_in_Rosario.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Shell_station_in_Rosario.jpg/440px-Shell_station_in_Rosario.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Shell_station_in_Rosario.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>CNG station in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rosario" title="Rosario">Rosario</a>, Argentina.</div></div></div>
<p>CNG vehicles are commonly used in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, where these vehicles are mainly used as taxicabs in main cities of Argentina and Brazil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LatinGNV_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-LatinGNV-28">[28]</a></sup> Normally, standard gasoline vehicles are retrofitted in specialized shops, which involve installing the gas cylinder in the trunk and the CNG injection system and electronics. Argentina and Brazil are the two countries with the largest fleets of CNG vehicles,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LatinGNV_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-LatinGNV-28">[28]</a></sup> with a combined total fleet of more than 3.4 million vehicles by 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IANGV_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-IANGV-2">[2]</a></sup> Conversion has been facilitated by a substantial price differential with liquid fuels, locally produced conversion equipment and a growing CNG-delivery infrastructure.
</p><p>As of 2009 Argentina had 1,807,186 NGV's with 1,851 refueling stations across the nation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IANGV_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-IANGV-2">[2]</a></sup> or 15 percent of all vehicles;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LatinGNV_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-LatinGNV-28">[28]</a></sup> and Brazil had 1,632,101 vehicles and 1,704 refueling stations,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IANGV_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-IANGV-2">[2]</a></sup> with a higher concentration in the cities of <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a> and <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="São Paulo">São Paulo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LatinGNV_28-3"><a href="#cite_note-LatinGNV-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GNVNews_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-GNVNews-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a> had an NGV fleet of 300,000 vehicles, and 460 refueling stations, as of 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IANGV_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-IANGV-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a> has increased its fleet from 10,000 in 2003 to 121,908 units in 2009, with 128 refueling stations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IANGV_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-IANGV-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> had 81,024 NGVs and 94 fueling stations as 2009,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IANGV_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-IANGV-2">[2]</a></sup> but that number is expected to skyrocket as Peru sits on South America's largest gas reserves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LatinGNV_28-4"><a href="#cite_note-LatinGNV-28">[28]</a></sup>  In Peru several factory-built NGVs have the tanks installed under the body of the vehicle, leaving the trunk free. Among the models built with this feature are the Fiat Multipla, the new Fiat Panda, the Volkswagen Touran Ecofuel, the Volkswagen Caddy Ecofuel and the Chevy Taxi. Other countries with significant NGV fleets are <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a> (15,000) and <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> (8,064) as of 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IANGV_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-IANGV-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Compressed_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Volvo_B10BLE_SBS_Transit_SBS2988J.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Volvo_B10BLE_SBS_Transit_SBS2988J.jpg/220px-Volvo_B10BLE_SBS_Transit_SBS2988J.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Volvo_B10BLE_SBS_Transit_SBS2988J.jpg/330px-Volvo_B10BLE_SBS_Transit_SBS2988J.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Volvo_B10BLE_SBS_Transit_SBS2988J.jpg/440px-Volvo_B10BLE_SBS_Transit_SBS2988J.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Volvo_B10BLE_SBS_Transit_SBS2988J.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A CNG powered <a href="/wiki/Volvo_B10BLE" title="Volvo B10BLE">Volvo B10BLE</a> bus, operated by <a href="/wiki/SBS_Transit" title="SBS Transit">SBS Transit</a> in <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hino_510_ngv.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="1920" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Hino_510_ngv.jpg/220px-Hino_510_ngv.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Hino_510_ngv.jpg/330px-Hino_510_ngv.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Hino_510_ngv.jpg/440px-Hino_510_ngv.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hino_510_ngv.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A CNG powered <a href="/wiki/Hino_Motors" title="Hino Motors">Hino</a> bus, operated by <a href="/wiki/Bangkok_Mass_Transit_Authority" title="Bangkok Mass Transit Authority">BMTA</a> in <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>In Singapore, CNG is increasingly being used by public transport vehicles like buses and taxis, as well as goods vehicles. However, according to Channel NewsAsia on April 18, 2008, more owners of private cars in this country are converting their petrol-driven vehicles to also run on CNG – motivated no doubt by rising petrol prices. The initial cost of converting a regular vehicle to dual fuel at the German conversion workshop of C. Melchers, for example, is around S$3,800; with the promise of real cost-savings that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dual-fuel_vehicle" title="Dual-fuel vehicle">dual-fuel vehicles</a> bring over the long term.
</p><p>Singapore currently has five operating filling stations for natural gas. SembCorp Gas Pte Ltd. runs the station on Jurong Island and, jointly with <a href="/wiki/Singapore_Petroleum_Company" title="Singapore Petroleum Company">Singapore Petroleum Company</a>, the filling station at Jalan Buroh. Both these stations are in the western part of the country. Another station on the mainland is in Mandai Link to the north and is operated by SMART Energy. SMART also own a second station on Serangoon North Ave 5 which was set up end of March 2009; The fifth and largest station in the world was opened by the UNION Group in September 2009. This station is recognized by the Guniness World Records as being the largest in the world with 46 refuelling hoses. This station is located in Toh Tuck. The Union Group, which operates 1000 CNG Toyota Wish taxis plan to introduce another three daughter stations and increase the CNG taxi fleet to 8000 units.
</p><p>As a key incentive for using this eco-friendly fuel Singapore has a green vehicle rebate for users of CNG technology. First introduced in January 2001, the GVR grants a 40 percent discount on the OMV (open market value) cost of newly registered green passenger vehicles. This initiative will end at the end of 2012 as the government believes the 'critical mass' of CNG vehicles would then have been built up.
</p><p>The Ministry of Transport of <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a> passed a law in 2005 which required that all public transport vehicles - buses, trucks and taxis, be converted to run on CNG. The Government permitted several private companies to handle the conversion of existing diesel and petrol cars, and also to begin importing CNG variants of buses and taxis. Accidents and rumours of accidents, partly fueled by Myanmar's position in local hydrocarbon politics,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> has discouraged citizens from using CNG vehicles, although now almost every taxi and public bus in Yangon, Myanmar's largest city, run on CNG. CNG stations have been set up around Yangon and other cities, but electricity shortages mean that vehicles may have to queue up for hours to fill their gas containers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The Burmese opposition movements are against the conversion to CNG, as they accuse the companies as being proxies of the junta, and also that the petrodollars earned by the regime would go towards the defense sector, rather than towards improving the infrastructure or welfare of the people.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, the use of CNG was originally introduced for taxicabs and airport limousines during the late-1990s, when new taxis were launched with CNG engines while taxicab operators were encouraged to send in existing taxis for full engine conversions. The practice of using CNG remained largely confined to taxicabs predominantly in the <a href="/wiki/Klang_Valley" title="Klang Valley">Klang Valley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Penang" title="Penang">Penang</a> due to a lack of interest. No incentives were offered for those besides taxicab owners to use CNG engines, while government subsidies on petrol and diesel made conventional road vehicles cheaper to use in the eyes of the consumers. <a href="/wiki/Petronas" title="Petronas">Petronas</a>, Malaysia's state-owned oil company, also monopolises the provision of CNG to road users. As of July 2008, Petronas only operates about 150 CNG refueling stations, most of which are concentrated in the Klang Valley. At the same time, another 50 were expected by the end of 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MY_CNG_station_no_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-MY_CNG_station_no-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>As fuel subsidies were gradually removed in Malaysia starting June 5, 2008, the subsequent 41 percent price hike on petrol and diesel led to a 500 percent increase in the number of new CNG tanks installed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MY_rush_1_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-MY_rush_1-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MY_rush_2_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-MY_rush_2-34">[34]</a></sup> National car maker <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proton_(carmaker)" title="Proton (carmaker)">Proton</a> considered fitting its <a href="/wiki/Proton_Waja" title="Proton Waja">Waja</a>, <a href="/wiki/Proton_Saga" title="Proton Saga">Saga</a> and <a href="/wiki/Proton_Persona" title="Proton Persona">Persona</a> models with CNG kits from Prins Autogassystemen by the end of 2008,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MY_Potong_CNG_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-MY_Potong_CNG-35">[35]</a></sup> while a local distributor of locally assembled <a href="/wiki/Hyundai_Motor_Company" title="Hyundai Motor Company">Hyundai</a> cars offers new models with CNG kits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MY_Hyundai_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-MY_Hyundai-36">[36]</a></sup> Conversion centres, which also benefited from the rush for lower running costs, also perform partial conversions to existing road vehicles, allowing them to run on both petrol or diesel and CNG with a cost varying between RM3,500 to RM5,000 for passenger cars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MY_rush_1_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-MY_rush_1-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MY_CNG_conversion_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-MY_CNG_conversion-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, companies such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sino-Energy" title="Sino-Energy">Sino-Energy</a> are active in expanding the footprint of CNG filling stations in medium-size cities across the interior of the country, where at least two natural gas pipelines are operational.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, the Karachi government under the order of <a href="/wiki/Supreme_court" title="Supreme court">Supreme Court</a> in 2004 made it mandatory for all city buses and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auto_rickshaws" title="Auto rickshaws">auto rickshaws</a> to run on CNG with the intention of reducing air pollution.
</p><p><br/>
In <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> in 2012, the federal government announced plans to gradually phase out CNG over a period of approximately three years given natural gas shortages which have been negatively affecting the manufacturing sector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Aside from limiting electricity generation capacity, gas shortages in Pakistan have also raised the costs of business for key industries including the fertilizer, cement and textile sectors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> has one of the largest fleets of CNG vehicles and CNG distribution networks in the world. There are 2335 CNG fueling stations, with a total of 13,534 CNG nozzles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The number of CNG burning vehicles in Iran exceeds 3.5 million. CNG consumption by Iran’s transportation sector is around 20 million cubic meters per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Compressed_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> is amongst the top 10 countries in CNG adoption, with 128,754 CNG vehicles and 124 CNG fueling stations. Egypt was also the first nation in Africa and the Middle East to open a public CNG fueling station in January 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>The vast majority 780000 have been produced as dual fuel vehicles by the auto manufacturer in the last two years, and the remainder have been converted utilizing after market conversion kits in workshops.  There are 750 active refueling stations country wide with an additional 660 refueling stations under construction and expected to come on stream.  Currently the major problem facing the industry as a whole is the building of refueling stations that is lagging behind dual fuel vehicle production, forcing many to use petrol instead.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a> CNG started with a pilot project in Benin City <a href="/wiki/Edo_State" title="Edo State">Edo State</a> in 2010 by Green Gas Limited. Green Gas Limited is a Joint Venture Company of NGC (Nigerian Gas Company Ltd.) &amp; NIPCO PLC. As at October 2012 about seven CNG stations have been built in Benin City <a href="/wiki/Edo_State" title="Edo State">Edo State</a>, with about 1,000 cars running on CNG in Benin City <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edo_state" title="Edo state">Edo state</a>. In Benin City <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edo_state" title="Edo state">Edo state</a>, major companies such as Coca-Cola are using CNG to power their fork-lifts/trucks while Edo City Transport Ltd (ECTS) is also running some of its buses on CNG.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Compressed_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brescia_Trasporti_Irisbus_CityClass_663_Desenzano_20120618.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2100" data-file-width="2800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Brescia_Trasporti_Irisbus_CityClass_663_Desenzano_20120618.JPG/220px-Brescia_Trasporti_Irisbus_CityClass_663_Desenzano_20120618.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Brescia_Trasporti_Irisbus_CityClass_663_Desenzano_20120618.JPG/330px-Brescia_Trasporti_Irisbus_CityClass_663_Desenzano_20120618.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Brescia_Trasporti_Irisbus_CityClass_663_Desenzano_20120618.JPG/440px-Brescia_Trasporti_Irisbus_CityClass_663_Desenzano_20120618.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brescia_Trasporti_Irisbus_CityClass_663_Desenzano_20120618.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>CNG powered bus in Italy</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, there are more than 1173 CNG stations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The use of methane for vehicles, started in the 1930s and has continued off and on until today. Since 2008 there have been a large market expansion for natural gas vehicles (CNG and LPG) caused by the rise of gasoline prices and by the need to reduce air pollution emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Before 1995 the only way to have a CNG-powered car was by having it retrofitted with an after-market kit. A large producer was <a href="/wiki/Landi_Renzo" title="Landi Renzo">Landi Renzo</a>, Tartarini Auto, Prins Autogassystemen, OMVL, BiGAs,... and AeB for electronic parts used by the most part of kit producer. Landi Renzo and Tartarini selling vehicles in Asia and South America. After 1995 bi-fuel cars (gasoline/CNG) became available from several major manufacturers. Currently <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiat" title="Fiat">Fiat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Opel" title="Opel">Opel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen" title="Volkswagen">Volkswagen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Citro%C3%ABn" title="Citroën">Citroën</a>, <a href="/wiki/Renault" title="Renault">Renault</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volvo" title="Volvo">Volvo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz" title="Mercedes-Benz">Mercedes</a> sell various car models and small trucks that are gasoline/CNG powered. Usually CNG parts used by major car manufacturers are actually produced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automotive_aftermarket" title="Automotive aftermarket">automotive aftermarket</a> kit manufacturers, e.g. Fiat use Tartarini Auto components, Volkswagen use Teleflex GFI<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> and Landi Renzo components.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> CNG is a very new fuel. At the beginning of 2014 there were only 17 refuelling stations, all of them in <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a>, but the number is now increasing rapidly. At the beginning of 2015 there were 29 refueling stations in Belgium, all of them in Flanders.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> As of January 2017, there are 76 active refueling stations in Belgium, most of them being in Flanders since only 7 of them are in <a href="/wiki/Wallonia" title="Wallonia">Wallonia</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brussels_Capital_Region" title="Brussels Capital Region">Brussels</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>    As a fuel and compared to petrol, CNG has an advantageous fiscal treatment with lower excises duties (although VAT is always paid). Since CNG, as a car fuel, is not totally exempted of excise duties, CNG cars do not pay a prime road tax to partially compensate the State for the loss of revenue. Instead LPG cars pay a prime road tax in Belgium, because LPG is totally exempted from excise duties. Since CNG is not totally exempted of excise duties, in Belgium it is allowed to connect a car to the home network of natural gas and to refuel the car from home. The purchase of CNG cars is not subsidised by the government, but by the Belgian producers and distributors of natural gas. Fiat and Volkswagen sell factory-equipped CNG-cars in Belgium. At the end of 2013 there were only 344 cars running with CNG in Belgium.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stadtbus_N%C3%BCrnberg_Bus_553_Btf._F%C3%BCrth.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1012" data-file-width="1630" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Stadtbus_N%C3%BCrnberg_Bus_553_Btf._F%C3%BCrth.jpg/220px-Stadtbus_N%C3%BCrnberg_Bus_553_Btf._F%C3%BCrth.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Stadtbus_N%C3%BCrnberg_Bus_553_Btf._F%C3%BCrth.jpg/330px-Stadtbus_N%C3%BCrnberg_Bus_553_Btf._F%C3%BCrth.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Stadtbus_N%C3%BCrnberg_Bus_553_Btf._F%C3%BCrth.jpg/440px-Stadtbus_N%C3%BCrnberg_Bus_553_Btf._F%C3%BCrth.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Stadtbus_N%C3%BCrnberg_Bus_553_Btf._F%C3%BCrth.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>CNG powered bus in Germany</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, CNG-generated vehicles are expected to increase to two million units of motor-transport by the year 2020. The cost for CNG fuel is between 1/3 and 1/2 compared to other fossil fuels in Europe.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 2016 there are around 900 CNG stations in Germany<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> and major German car manufacturers like Volkswagen, Mercedes, Opel, Audi offer CNG engines on most of their models. Augsburg is one of the few cities that only run CNG operated public buses since 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ankara" title="Ankara">Ankara</a> municipality is increasingly using CNG buses, where numbers have reached 1090 by 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a> has started in 2014 with an order of 110 buses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Konya" title="Konya">Konya</a> also added 60 buses to its fleet the same year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> there are 9 CNG refueling stations as of September 25, 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> there are four public CNG refueling stations in the cities <a href="/wiki/Budapest" title="Budapest">Budapest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Szeged" title="Szeged">Szeged</a>, <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A9cs" title="Pécs">Pécs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gy%C5%91r" title="Győr">Győr</a>. The public transportation company of <a href="/wiki/Szeged" title="Szeged">Szeged</a> runs buses mainly on CNG.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>, there are 96 CNG refueling stations as of July 2011. One can be found in most of Bulgaria's big towns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> In the capital <a href="/wiki/Sofia" title="Sofia">Sofia</a> there are 22 CNG stations making it possibly the city with the most publicly available CNG stations in Europe. There are also quite a few in <a href="/wiki/Plovdiv" title="Plovdiv">Plovdiv</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ruse,_Bulgaria" title="Ruse, Bulgaria">Ruse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stara_Zagora" title="Stara Zagora">Stara Zagora</a> and <a href="/wiki/Veliko_Tarnovo" title="Veliko Tarnovo">Veliko Tarnovo</a> as well as in the towns on the Black Sea – <a href="/wiki/Varna" title="Varna">Varna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burgas" title="Burgas">Burgas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nesebar" title="Nesebar">Nesebar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kavarna" title="Kavarna">Kavarna</a>. CNG vehicles are becoming more and more popular in the country. The fuel is mostly used by taxi drivers because of its much lower price compared to petrol. Currently (as of July, 2015) the city of Sofia is rapidly renewing its public transport fleet with <a href="/wiki/MAN_Lion%27s_City" title="MAN Lion's City">MAN Lion's City</a> buses running on CNG.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Also, many companies switch to CNG cargo vans and even heavy trucks for their daily operations within city limits.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Macedonia" title="Republic of Macedonia">Macedonia</a>, there is one CNG station located in the capital Skopje, but it is not for public use. Only twenty buses of the local Public Transport Company have been fitted to use a mixture of diesel and CNG. The first commercial CNG station in Skopje is in the advanced stage of development and is expected to start operation in July 2011.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>, there are several public CNG refuelling stations two of which are in the capital <a href="/wiki/Belgrade" title="Belgrade">Belgrade</a> and the rest in the towns of <a href="/wiki/Novi_Sad" title="Novi Sad">Novi Sad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pan%C4%8Devo" title="Pančevo">Pančevo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kru%C5%A1evac" title="Kruševac">Kruševac</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C4%8Ca%C4%8Dak" title="Čačak">Čačak</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenia</a>, there are two public CNG refuelling station in the capital <a href="/wiki/Ljubljana" title="Ljubljana">Ljubljana</a> and in Maribor.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a>, there are two public CNG refuelling stations situated close to the center of <a href="/wiki/Zagreb" title="Zagreb">Zagreb</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Rijeka" title="Rijeka">Rijeka</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> At least 60 CNG buses are in use as a form of a public transport (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ZET" title="ZET">Zagreb public transport services</a>).
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a>, there are 11 public CNG refuelling stations - 4 in the country's capital <a href="/wiki/Tallinn" title="Tallinn">Tallinn</a> and the others one in <a href="/wiki/Tartu" title="Tartu">Tartu</a>, <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A4rnu" title="Pärnu">Pärnu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Viljandi" title="Viljandi">Viljandi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rakvere" title="Rakvere">Rakvere</a>, <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B5hvi" title="Jõhvi">Jõhvi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Narva" title="Narva">Narva</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> From 2011, Tartu has five Scania manufactured CNG buses operating its inner-city routes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> there are currently 90 CNG filling stations available to the public (as compared to about 10 LPG filling stations), primarily located in the southern and western parts of the country as well the Mälardalen region<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Another 70-80 CNG filling stations are under construction or in a late stage of planning (completions 2009-2010). Several of the planned filling stations are located in the northern parts of the country, which will greatly improve the infrastructure for CNG car users.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> There are approx. 14,500 CNG vehicles in Sweden (2007), of which approx. 13,500 are passenger cars and the remainder includes buses and trucks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> In Stockholm, the public transportation company SL currently operates 50 CNG buses but have a capacity to operate 500.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> The Swedish government recently prolonged its subsidies for the development of CNG filling stations, from 2009-12-31 to 2010-12-31.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> CNG is a very new fuel and the refueling network is being developed. In Madrid, the EMT, uses 672 buses running with CNG. At the beginning of 2015 there were 35 CNG refueling stations in Spain. Several car brands sell brand-new cars running with CNG, including Fiat, Volkswagen, Seat and Skoda among others.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>As of 2013, there are 47 public CNG filling stations in the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>, mainly in the big cities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Local bus manufacturers SOR Libchavy and Tedom produce CNG versions of their vehicles, with roof-mounted tanks.
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Compressed_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Natural gas has been used as a motor fuel in Canada for over 20 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> With assistance from federal and provincial research programs, demonstration projects and NGV market deployment programs during the 1980s and 1990s, the population of light-duty NGVs grew to over 35,000 by the early 1990s. This assistance resulted in a significant adoption of natural gas transit buses as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iangv.org_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-iangv.org-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p><p>The NGV market started to decline after 1995, eventually reaching today’s vehicle population of about 12,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iangv.org_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-iangv.org-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p><p>This figure includes 150 urban transit buses, 45 school buses, 9,450 light-duty cars and trucks, and 2,400 forklifts and ice-resurfacers. The total fuel use in all NGV markets in Canada was 1.9 PJs (petajoules) in 2007 (or 54.6 million liters of gasoline liters equivalent), down from 2.6 PJs in 1997. Public CNG refueling stations have declined in quantity from 
134 in 1997 to 72 today. There are 22 in British Columbia, 12 in Alberta, 10 in Saskatchewan, 27 in 
Ontario and two in Québec. There are only 12 private fleet stations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oee.nrcan.gc.ca_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-oee.nrcan.gc.ca-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Canadian industry has developed CNG-fueled truck and bus engines, CNG-fueled transit buses, and light trucks and taxis.
</p><p>Fuelmaker Corporation of Toronto, the Honda-owned manufacturer of CNG auto refueling units, was forced into bankruptcy by parent Honda USA for an unspecified reason in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>  The various assets of Fuelmaker were subsequently acquired by Fuel Systems Corporation of Santa Ana, California.<b></b>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Compressed_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>Similar to Canada, the United States has implemented various NGV initiatives and programs since 1980, but has had limited success in sustaining the market. There were 105,000 NGVs in operation in 2000; this figure peaked at 121,000 in 2004, and decreased to 110,000 in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iangv.org_66-2"><a href="#cite_note-iangv.org-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States, federal tax credits are available for buying a new CNG vehicle. Use of CNG varies from state to state; only 34 states have at least one CNG fueling site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p><p>In Texas, Railroad Commissioner David Porter launched his Texas Natural Gas Initiative in October 2013 to encourage the adoption of natural gas fuel in the transportation and exploration and production sectors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> As of 2015 Texas is rapidly becoming a leader in natural gas infrastructure in the US with 137 natural gas fueling stations (private and public).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> Nine months into FY2015 Commissioner Porter reports Texas CNG, LNG Sales Show 78 Percent Increase Over FY 2014 year to date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rrc.texas.gov_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-rrc.texas.gov-71">[71]</a></sup>  Per Commissioner Porter<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rrc.texas.gov_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-rrc.texas.gov-71">[71]</a></sup> in June 2015: “Natural gas vehicles are becoming mainstream faster than expected. These collections are nearly double the amount collected last year at this time.  At 15 cents per gallon equivalent, $3,033,600 of motor fuel tax equates to the sale of 20,224,000 gallon equivalents of natural gas.”  The $3 million in Texas natural gas tax receipts is for both CNG and LNG for FY2015 through the May 2015.  The Texas fiscal year starts Sept 1 so 9 months tax collections are represented.
</p><p>In Athens, Ala., the city and its Gas Department installed a public CNG station on the Interstate 65 Corridor, making it the only public CNG station between Birmingham and Nashville as of February 2014. The city's larger fleet vehicles such as garbage trucks also use this public station for fueling. The city also has two slow-fill non-public CNG stations for its fleet. Athens has added CNG/gasoline Tahoes for police and fire, a CNG Honda Civic, CNG Heil garbage trucks, and CNG/gasoline Dodge pickup trucks to its fleet.
</p><p>In California, CNG is used extensively in local city and county fleets, as well as public transportation (city/school buses). There are 90 public fueling stations in southern California alone, and travel from San Diego so the Bay Area to Las Vegas and Utah is routine with the advent of online station maps such as www.cngprices.com.  Compressed natural gas is typically available for 30-60 percent less than the cost of gasoline in much of California.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GCT_Orion_7.01_029_on_the_35_bus_on_P%27tree_Corners.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="4608" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/GCT_Orion_7.01_029_on_the_35_bus_on_P%27tree_Corners.jpg/220px-GCT_Orion_7.01_029_on_the_35_bus_on_P%27tree_Corners.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/GCT_Orion_7.01_029_on_the_35_bus_on_P%27tree_Corners.jpg/330px-GCT_Orion_7.01_029_on_the_35_bus_on_P%27tree_Corners.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/GCT_Orion_7.01_029_on_the_35_bus_on_P%27tree_Corners.jpg/440px-GCT_Orion_7.01_029_on_the_35_bus_on_P%27tree_Corners.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GCT_Orion_7.01_029_on_the_35_bus_on_P%27tree_Corners.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Gwinnett County Transit Orion VII CNG 029 on the 35 Bus on Peachtree Corners.</div></div></div>
<p>The 28 buses running the <a href="/wiki/Gwinnett_County_Transit" title="Gwinnett County Transit">Gwinnett County Transit</a> local routes run on 100 percent CNG. Additionally, about half of the <a href="/wiki/Georgia_Regional_Transportation_Authority" title="Georgia Regional Transportation Authority">Georgia Regional Transportation Authority</a> express fleet, which runs and refuels out of the Gwinnett County Transit facility, uses CNG.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Bay_Transportation_Authority" title="Massachusetts Bay Transportation Authority">Massachusetts Bay Transportation Authority</a> was running 360 CNG buses as early as in 2007, and is the largest user in the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Transportation_Authority" title="Metropolitan Transportation Authority">Metropolitan Transportation Authority</a> (MTA) of New York City currently has over 900 buses powered by compressed natural gas with CNG bus depots located in <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Bronx" title="The Bronx">The Bronx</a> and <a href="/wiki/Queens" title="Queens">Queens</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Nassau_Inter-County_Express" title="Nassau Inter-County Express">Nassau Inter-County Express</a> (or NICE Bus/formerly New York MTA Long Island Bus) runs a 100% Orion CNG-fueled bus fleet for fixed route service consisting of 360 buses for service in Nassau County, parts of Queens, New York, and the western sections of Suffolk County.
</p><p>The City of Harrisburg, Pennsylvania has switched some of the city's vehicles to compressed natural gas in an effort to save money on fuel costs. Trucks used by the city's street and water, sewer and gas departments have been converted from gasoline to CNG.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup>
</p><p>Personal use of CNG is a small niche market currently, though with current tax incentives and a growing number of public fueling stations available, it is experiencing unprecedented growth. The state of Utah offers a subsidised statewide network of CNG filling stations at a rate of $1.57/gge,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> while gasoline is above $4.00/gal.
</p><p>Elsewhere in the nation, retail prices average around $2.50/gge, with home refueling units compressing gas from residential gas lines for under $1/gge. Other than aftermarket conversions, and government used vehicle auctions, the only currently<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (October 2012)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> produced CNG vehicle in the United States is the <a href="/wiki/Honda_Civic_GX" title="Honda Civic GX">Honda Civic GX</a> sedan, which is made in limited numbers and available only in states with retail fueling outlets.
</p><p>An initiative, known as <a href="/wiki/Pickens_Plan" title="Pickens Plan">Pickens Plan</a>, calls for the expansion of the use of CNG as a standard fuel for heavy vehicles has been recently started by oilman and entrepreneur <a href="/wiki/T._Boone_Pickens" title="T. Boone Pickens">T. Boone Pickens</a>.  California voters defeated <a href="/wiki/California_Proposition_10_(2008)" title="California Proposition 10 (2008)">Proposition 10</a> in the 2008 General Election by a significant (59.8 percent to 40.2 percent) margin.  Proposition 10 was a $5 billion bond measure that, among other things, would have given rebates to state residents that purchase CNG vehicles.
</p><p>On February 21, 2013, <a href="/wiki/T._Boone_Pickens" title="T. Boone Pickens">T. Boone Pickens</a> and New York Mayor, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Bloomberg" title="Michael Bloomberg">Michael Bloomberg</a> unveiled a CNG powered mobile pizzeria. The company, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Neapolitan_Express&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Neapolitan Express (page does not exist)">Neapolitan Express</a> uses alternative energy to run the truck as well as 100 percent recycled and compostable materials for their carryout boxes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup>
</p><p>Congress has encouraged conversion of cars to CNG with a tax credits of up to 50 percent of the auto conversion cost and the CNG home filling station cost. However, while CNG is much cleaner fuel, the conversion requires a type certificate from the EPA. Meeting the requirements of a type certificate can cost up to $50,000.  Other non-EPA approved kits are available.  A complete and safe aftermarket conversion using a non-EPA approved kit can be achieved for as little as $400 without the cylinder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceania">Oceania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Compressed_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Oceania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the 1970s and 1980s, CNG was commonly used in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> in the wake of the oil crises, but fell into decline after petrol prices receded. At the peak of natural gas use, 10 percent of New Zealand's cars were converted, around 110,000 vehicles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TwoBillion_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-TwoBillion-78">[78]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>For a period of time, <a href="/wiki/Brisbane_Transport" title="Brisbane Transport">Brisbane Transport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>, Australia adopted a policy of purchasing only CNG buses. Brisbane Transport has <a class="external text" href="http://www.btbuses.info/?GoTo=fleetspecs&amp;find=Scania%20L94UB%20%5BVolgren%5D&amp;searching=yes" rel="nofollow">215</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scania_L94UB" title="Scania L94UB">Scania L94UB</a> and <a class="external text" href="http://www.btbuses.info/?GoTo=fleetspecs&amp;find=MAN%2018.310%20(CNG)%20%5BVolgren%5D&amp;searching=yes" rel="nofollow">324</a> MAN 18.310 models as well as <a class="external text" href="http://www.btbuses.info/?GoTo=fleetspecs&amp;find=MAN%20NG313%20artic%20%5BCustom%20Coaches%5D&amp;searching=yes" rel="nofollow">30</a> MAN NG 313 articulated CNG buses. The <a href="/wiki/State_Transit_Authority" title="State Transit Authority">State Transit Authority</a> purchased 100 <a href="/wiki/Scania_L113" title="Scania L113">Scania L113</a>CRB, 283 <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_O405" title="Mercedes-Benz O405">Mercedes-Benz O405</a>NH and 254 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euro_5" title="Euro 5">Euro 5</a>-compliant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_OC500LE" title="Mercedes-Benz OC500LE">Mercedes-Benz OC500LE</a> buses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1990s <a href="/wiki/CDC_Geelong" title="CDC Geelong">Benders Busways</a> of <a href="/wiki/Geelong" title="Geelong">Geelong</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victoria,_Australia" title="Victoria, Australia">Victoria</a> trialled CNG buses for the Energy Research and Development Corporation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup>
</p><p>Martin Ferguson, Ollie Clark and Noel Childs featured on <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_7:30_Report" title="The 7:30 Report">The 7:30 Report</a></i> raised the issue of CNG as an overlooked transport fuel option in Australia, highlighting the large volumes of LNG currently being exported from the North West Shelf in light of the cost of importing crude oil to Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Deployments">Deployments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Compressed_natural_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Deployments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/AT%26T" title="AT&amp;T">AT&amp;T</a> ordered 1,200 CNG-powered cargo vans from <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a> in 2012. It is the largest-ever order of CNG vehicles from <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a> to date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> AT&amp;T has announced its intention to invest up to $565 million to deploy approximately 15,000 alternative fuel vehicles over a 10-year period through 2018, will use the vans to provide and maintain communications, high-speed Internet and television services for AT&amp;T customers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p>
