<p><b>Carsharing</b> or <b>car sharing</b> (AU, NZ, CA, &amp; US) or <b>car clubs</b> (UK) is a model of <a href="/wiki/Car_rental" title="Car rental">car rental</a> where people rent cars for short periods of time, often by the hour. They are attractive to customers who make only occasional use of a vehicle, as well as others who would like occasional access to a vehicle of a different type than they use day-to-day. The organization renting the cars may be a commercial business or the users may be organized as a <a href="/wiki/Company" title="Company">company</a>, public agency, <a href="/wiki/Cooperative" title="Cooperative">cooperative</a>, or <i>ad hoc</i> grouping. Carsharing is part of a larger trend of <a href="/wiki/Shared_mobility" title="Shared mobility">shared mobility</a>. Shared mobility includes all modes of travel that offer short-term access to transportation on an on-needed basis either for personal transportation or goods delivery<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>. 
</p><p>Carsharing services are available in over 1,000 cities in several countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of  December 2012<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carsharing&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there were an estimated 1.7 million car-sharing members in 27 countries, including so-called peer-to-peer services, according to the Transportation Sustainability Research Center at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.C._Berkeley" title="U.C. Berkeley">U.C. Berkeley</a>. Of these, 800,000 were car-sharing members in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of July 2017, car2go is the largest carsharing company in the world with 2,500,000 registered members and a fleet of nearly 14,000 vehicles in 26 locations in North America, Europe and Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PR_Newswire_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-PR_Newswire-4">[4]</a></sup>, followed by Zipcar with 767,000 members and 11,000 vehicles <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Members_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Members-5">[5]</a></sup>.
</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/Navigant_Consulting" title="Navigant Consulting">Navigant Consulting</a>, global carsharing services revenue will approach <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$1</span> billion in 2013 and grow to <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$6.2</span> billion by 2020, with over 12 million members worldwide. The main factors driving the growth of carsharing are the rising levels of congestion faced by city dwellers; shifting generational mindsets about car ownership; the increasing costs of personal vehicle ownership; and a convergence of business models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Carsharing contributes to <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_transport" title="Sustainable transport">sustainable transport</a> because it is a less car intensive means of urban transport, and according to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i>, carsharing can reduce car ownership at an estimated rate of one rental car replacing 15 owned vehicles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_terminology">International terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carsharing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: International terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hertz_On_Demand_Vehicles_in_Park_Ridge,_NJ.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="566" data-file-width="700" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Hertz_On_Demand_Vehicles_in_Park_Ridge%2C_NJ.jpg/220px-Hertz_On_Demand_Vehicles_in_Park_Ridge%2C_NJ.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Hertz_On_Demand_Vehicles_in_Park_Ridge%2C_NJ.jpg/330px-Hertz_On_Demand_Vehicles_in_Park_Ridge%2C_NJ.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Hertz_On_Demand_Vehicles_in_Park_Ridge%2C_NJ.jpg/440px-Hertz_On_Demand_Vehicles_in_Park_Ridge%2C_NJ.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hertz_On_Demand_Vehicles_in_Park_Ridge,_NJ.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/The_Hertz_Corporation" title="The Hertz Corporation">Hertz</a> on Demand vehicles in designated parking area in Park Ridge, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a></div></div></div>
<p>In the United Kingdom, where it is a recent development, the term "car clubs" is used for what in the United States is called "carsharing", "car sharing" or "car-sharing".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In the UK, "car sharing" refers to what is called "<a href="/wiki/Carpool" title="Carpool">carpooling</a>" or "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ride_sharing" title="Ride sharing">ride sharing</a>" in the US, namely the shared use of a car for a specific journey, in particular for commuting to work, often by people who each have a car but travel together to save costs;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> in South Africa, this is called a "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lift_scheme" title="wikt:lift scheme">lift scheme</a>". In the UK, a "car pool" refers to a fleet of cars made available by an organization to its employees (which is usually referred to as a "motor pool" in the US), for example to travel to customers or between different office locations.
</p><p>In India, in contrast to the vast majority of the Indian car rental market that is defined by chauffeured-service, Indian people refer to car-sharing as "self-drive."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Carsharing benefits individuals who can gain the benefits of private cars without having costs and responsibilities associated with car ownership.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shaheen1_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-shaheen1-12">[12]</a></sup> Instead a household accesses a fleet of vehicles on an as-needed basis. Carsharing may be thought of as organized short-term car rental.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shaheen1_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-shaheen1-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Carsharing has sprung up in different parts of the world and operations are organized in many different ways in different places. Sizes of organizations vary from one shared car, and only a handful of sharers to organizations that serve a complete urban area.
</p><p>Generally, carsharing programs can be qualified as one of four sharing types: Roundtrip, one-way, peer-to-peer, and fractional. In roundtrip carsharing, members begin and end their trip, often paying by the hour, mile, or both. One-way carsharing, on the other hand, enables members to begin and end their trip at different locations through free floating zones or station-based models with designated parking locations<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shaheen2015_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shaheen2015-13">[13]</a></sup>. <a href="/wiki/Peer-to-peer_carsharing" title="Peer-to-peer carsharing">Peer-to-peer carsharing</a> (sometimes referred to as Personal Vehicle Sharing<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shaheen2015_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shaheen2015-13">[13]</a></sup>) operates similarly to roundtrip carsharing in trip and payment type; however, the vehicles themselves are typically privately owned or leased with the sharing system operated by a third-party. The fractional ownership model allows users to co-own a vehicle and share its costs and use<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Car2go_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Car2go-14">[14]</a></sup>. 
</p><p>Carsharing differs from traditional car rentals in the following ways:
</p>
<dl><dd><ul><li>Carsharing is not limited by office hours</li>
<li>Reservation, pickup, and return is all self-service</li>
<li>Vehicles can be rented by the minute, by the hour, as well as by the day</li>
<li>Users are members and have been pre-approved to drive (background driving checks have been performed and a payment mechanism has been established)</li>
<li>Vehicle locations are distributed throughout the service area, and often located for access by <a href="/wiki/Public_transport" title="Public transport">public transport</a>.</li>
<li>Insurance: state minimum liability insurance (only $5000 in some states), comprehensive and collision insurance. They do not provide uninsured, under-insured or personal injury protection insurance.</li>
<li>Fuel costs are included in the rates.</li>
<li>Vehicles are not serviced (cleaning, fueling) after each use, although certain programs (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Car2Go" title="Car2Go">Car2Go</a> or <a href="/wiki/GoGet" title="GoGet">GoGet</a>) continuously clean and fuel their fleet</li></ul></dd></dl>
<p>Some carshare operations (CSOs) cooperate with local car rental firms, in particular in situations wherein classic rental may be the cheaper option.
</p><p>Carsharing can provide numerous transportation, land use, environmental, and social benefits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Neighborhood carsharing is often promoted as an alternative to owning a car where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_transit" title="Public transit">public transit</a>, walking, and <a href="/wiki/Cycling" title="Cycling">cycling</a> can be used most of the time and a car is only necessary for out-of-town trips, moving large items, or special occasions. It can also be an alternative to owning multiple cars for households with more than one driver. A long-term study of <a href="/wiki/City_CarShare" title="City CarShare">City CarShare</a> members found that 30 percent of households that joined sold a car; others delayed purchasing one. Transit use, bicycling, and walking also increased among members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/City_CarShare" title="City CarShare">City CarShare</a>'s early adopters were found to be mostly non-car-owners, resulting in increased car-miles traveled one-year into the program <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>; two years into the program, car-sharing began to enter the mainstream of San Francisco's traveling public, prompting many families to give up second cars<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>, and by year five, nearly a third of <a href="/wiki/City_CarShare" title="City CarShare">City CarShare</a>'s member households had shed a private motorized vehicle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> A study of driving behavior of members from major carsharing organizations found an average decline in 27% of annual vehicle kilometres travelled (VKT).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> That said, it is important to note that the success of carsharing programs depends on if it provides the consumers with "better mobility or sufficient mobility at reduced cost." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Carsharing is generally not cost-effective for commuting to a full-time job on a regular basis. Most carsharing advocates, operators, and cooperating public agencies believe that those who do not drive daily or who drive less than 10,000 kilometers (about 6,200 statute miles) annually may find carsharing to be more cost-effective than car ownership.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> But variations of 50% on this figure are reported by operators and others depending on local context.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> If occasional use of a shared vehicle costs significantly less than car ownership, then carsharing makes automobile use more accessible to low-income households.
</p><p>Carsharing can also help reduce congestion and pollution. It is noted as a tool for achieving VMT and GHG reduction targets in the California Transport Plan (CTP) 2040<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-21">[21]</a></sup>. Replacing private automobiles with shared ones directly reduces demand for parking spaces. The fact that only a certain number of cars can be in use at any one time may reduce <a href="/wiki/Traffic_congestion" title="Traffic congestion">traffic congestion</a> at <a href="/wiki/Rush_hour" title="Rush hour">peak times</a>. Even more important for congestion, the strong metering of costs provides a cost incentive to drive less. With owned automobiles many expenses are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunk_costs" title="Sunk costs">sunk costs</a> and thus independent of how much the car is driven (such as original purchase, insurance, registration, and some maintenance).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Successful carsharing development has tended to be associated mainly with densely populated areas, such as city centers and more recently university and other campuses. There are some programs (mostly in Europe) for providing services in lower density and rural areas.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Low-density areas are considered more difficult to serve with carsharing because of the lack of alternative modes of transportation and the potentially larger distance that users must travel to reach the cars.
</p><p>People who have joined carsharing tend to sell either their primary, secondary, or another off-hand car, after using the service. This reduces the cost of transportation per month by an average of $135 – $435, based on University of Berkeley's Research in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carsharing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The technology of CSOs varies enormously, from simple manual systems using key boxes and log books to increasingly complex computer-based systems (e.g. partially automated and fully automated systems) with supporting <a href="/wiki/Software_suite" title="Software suite">software packages</a> that handle a growing array of back office functions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The simplest CSOs have only one or two pick-up points, but more advanced systems allow cars to be picked up and dropped off at any available public parking space within a designated operating area.
</p><p>While differing markedly in their objectives, size, business models, levels of ambition, technology and target markets, these programs do share many features. The more established operations usually require a check of past driving records and a monthly or annual fee in order to become a member. The cost and maximum time a car may be used also varies.
</p><p>To make a reservation, one can either make a reservation online, by phone, or by text messages depending on the company’s flexibility. There is a higher chance of availability the earlier the reservation is. If a reservation is cancelled however, one may still be charged.
</p><p>Once the reservations are completed and confirmed, the car will then be delivered at the time and place scheduled. There will be a small card reader mounted on the windshield. Once the customer places their membership card on the reader, it will use what is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blink_technology" title="Blink technology">blink technology</a> to activate the time and unlock the car. The reader will not work until it is time for that specific reservation. The keys can then be found somewhere inside the car such as the glove compartment. Depending on the company, the customer may be provided with a key to a lock box that contains the ignition key itself. Once the customer is set, they are off to their next destination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>
Although members are often responsible for cleaning the car and filling up the tank when low, the carsharing company is generally responsible for the long-term maintenance of the vehicles. Members have to make sure that when they are finished, the car is ready for the next user to move on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ShattuckBackMM1144C.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="375" data-file-width="500" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/ShattuckBackMM1144C.jpg/220px-ShattuckBackMM1144C.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/ShattuckBackMM1144C.jpg/330px-ShattuckBackMM1144C.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/ShattuckBackMM1144C.jpg/440px-ShattuckBackMM1144C.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ShattuckBackMM1144C.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Uhaul_Car_Share" title="Uhaul Car Share">Uhaul Car Share</a> off-street drop/pick up area on <a href="/wiki/Shattuck_Avenue" title="Shattuck Avenue">Shattuck Avenue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berkeley,_California" title="Berkeley, California">Berkely</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a></div></div></div>

<p>The first reference to carsharing in print identifies the <i>Selbstfahrergenossenschaft</i> carshare program in a <a href="/wiki/Housing_cooperative" title="Housing cooperative">housing cooperative</a> that got underway in <a href="/wiki/Z%C3%BCrich" title="Zürich">Zürich</a> in 1948,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> but there was no known formal development of the concept in the next few years. By the 1960s, as innovators, industrialists, cities, and public authorities studied the possibility of high-technology transportation—mainly computer-based small vehicle systems (almost all of them on separate guideways)—it was possible to spot some early precursors to present-day service ideas and control technologies.
</p><p>The early 1970s saw the first whole-system carshare projects. The ProcoTip system in France lasted only about two years. A much more ambitious project called the <a href="/wiki/Witkar" title="Witkar">Witkar</a> was launched in Amsterdam by the founders of the 1965 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_bicycles" title="White bicycles">white bicycles</a> project. A sophisticated project based on small electric vehicles, electronic controls for reservations and return, and plans for a large number of stations covering the entire city, the project endured into the mid-1980s before finally being abandoned.
</p><p>In July, 1977, the first official British experiment in carsharing started in Suffolk. An office in Ipswich provided a <i>Share-a-Car</i> service for "putting motorists who are interested in sharing car journeys in touch with each other."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In 1978, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agricultural_Research_Council" title="Agricultural Research Council">Agricultural Research Council</a> granted the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Leeds" title="University of Leeds">University of Leeds</a> £16,577 "for an investigation and simulation of carsharing".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The scheme was not intended for different drivers of a single car but for a driver offering seats in his car (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real-time_ridesharing" title="Real-time ridesharing">Real-time ridesharing</a>).
</p><p>The 1980s and first half of the 1990s was a "<a href="/wiki/Coming_of_age" title="Coming of age">coming of age</a>" period for carsharing, with continued slow growth, mainly of smaller non-profit systems, many in Switzerland and Germany<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> but also on a smaller scale in Canada, the Netherlands, Sweden, and the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>Carsharing in NA was founded in Quebec City in 1994 by Benoît Robert, with a company called Communauto that is still a leader in carsharing globally. Cycling advocate and environmentalist Claire Morissette (1950–2007) played a major role in its evolution starting in 1995, when Communauto established itself in Montreal as a private company. The company goal is to provide a convenient and economical alternative to owning a car.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Zipcar" title="Zipcar">Zipcar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flexcar" title="Flexcar">Flexcar</a> (bought by Zipcar in 2007), and <a href="/wiki/City_Car_Club" title="City Car Club">City Car Club</a> were all started in 2000. Several <a href="/wiki/Car_rental" title="Car rental">car rental</a> companies launched their own carsharing services beginning in 2008, including Avis On Location by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avis_Rent_a_Car_System" title="Avis Rent a Car System">Avis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hertz_on_Demand" title="Hertz on Demand">Hertz on Demand</a> (formerly known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Connect_by_Hertz" title="Connect by Hertz">Connect by Hertz</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>), operating in the U.S. and Europe; <a href="/wiki/Uhaul_Car_Share" title="Uhaul Car Share">Uhaul Car Share</a> owned by <a href="/wiki/U-Haul" title="U-Haul">U-Haul</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WeCar" title="WeCar">WeCar</a> by <a href="/wiki/Enterprise_Holdings" title="Enterprise Holdings">Enterprise Rent-A-Car</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT0910_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT0910-35">[35]</a></sup> By 2010, when various <a href="/wiki/Peer-to-peer_carsharing" title="Peer-to-peer carsharing">peer-to-peer carsharing</a> systems were introduced. As of  September 2012<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carsharing&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Members_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Members-5">[5]</a></sup> Zipcar accounted for 80% of the U.S carsharing market<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT0910_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT0910-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> and half of all car-sharers worldwide<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> with 730,000 members sharing 11,000 vehicles.
</p><p>Carsharing has also spread to the developing world (Argentina, Brazil, China, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zoom_(Car-Sharing)" title="Zoom (Car-Sharing)">India</a>, Mexico, and Turkey) because population density is often a critical determinant of success for carsharing, and developing nations often have dense urban populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many building developers are now incorporating share-cars into their developments as an added value to tenants, and municipal government bodies around the world are starting to stipulate the implementation of a carsharing service in new buildings, as a sustainability initiative. These trends have created a demand for a new model of carsharing - residential, private-access share-cars that are typically underwritten by the <a href="/wiki/Homeowner_association" title="Homeowner association">Homeowner association</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Handicapped_accessibility">Handicapped accessibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carsharing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Handicapped accessibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Adapting carsharing vehicles to persons with <a href="/wiki/Physical_disability" title="Physical disability">physical disabilities</a> presents special challenges not faced by traditional <a href="/wiki/Car_rental" title="Car rental">car rental</a>. With carsharing no mechanic is present to install or adjust adaptive equipment, and that equipment is left unattended after each use. In 2008, City CarShare introduced the first wheelchair carrying car share vehicle, the <i>Access Mobile</i>, specifically designed as a fleet vehicle shared with, not segregated from, non-wheelchair users.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Innovations">Innovations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carsharing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Innovations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carsharing operators are increasingly opting to brand parts of their fleets with third-party advertising in order to increase revenue and improve competitiveness. <a href="/wiki/Transit_media" title="Transit media">Transit media</a>, as this <a href="/wiki/Out-of-home_advertising" title="Out-of-home advertising">out-of-home advertising</a> medium is referred to, is a strategy currently (or soon to be) employed by larger carsharing operators such as Canada's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AutoShare" title="AutoShare">AutoShare</a> and the UK's <a href="/wiki/City_Car_Club" title="City Car Club">City Car Club</a>.
</p><p>For future applications, many carsharing companies are now investing in plug-in hybrid electric vehicles (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PHEV" title="PHEV">PHEV</a>). With the use of these types of vehicles, cost of gas consumption can be greatly reduced. Since most customers do not need the vehicle for long amounts of time or distance, it gives the carsharing company time to collect and recharge these vehicles for additional use. This application can greatly reduce carbon emissions and improve city environments. Another innovation is to calculate and compensate all emissions on behalf of your drivers according to the Kyoto protocol, e.g. via reforestation schemes. The world´s first certified carbon neutral carsharing service is Respiro carsharing in Madrid<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and is also done by Australian p2p car sharing platform <a class="external text" href="https://www.carnextdoor.com.au/carbon-offset" rel="nofollow">Car Next Door</a>.
</p><p>Also, carsharing can be considered as an example of a technological change in consumption, which is a ‘process of mutual adjustment between innovation and its socio-economic environment.’ This type of innovation poses much potential due to the current state of technological change based on today’s passenger transportation capabilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other countries are already moving into designing concept cars that is solely based as an urban public vehicle. The Phiaro P70t Conch, Japan’s new concept vehicle, is a completely battery-powered, three-seater vehicle which was designed for the purpose of carsharing. The vehicle was made to be small and compact enough to be driven around urban environments without sacrificing parking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The promotion of these kinds of concept vehicles have caught the attention of automotive companies worldwide.
</p><p>In Germany a pilot project has been started by the semiconductor manufacturer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infineon" title="Infineon">Infineon</a> to replace regular pool vehicles with a corporate carsharing system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-How_to_Cut_Costs_Through_Carsharing_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-How_to_Cut_Costs_Through_Carsharing-42">[42]</a></sup> Corporate carsharing is a more economical solution than company cars in regard to administration, travel and running expenses and maintenance.
</p><p>Along with EVs, one of the most important technological innovations to affect the carsharing market is self-driving cars. It is expected that most self-driving vehicles won't be owned by individuals, but will rather be shared. Some companies, like Ernst and Young, have also started to use blockchain technology to record ownership, usage of shared vehicles and insurance information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Insurance">Insurance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carsharing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Insurance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The amount of insurance provided greatly varies among companies, but all carsharing firms provide insurance that at least meets the legal minimum requirements for the given region of operation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, critics such as Rob Lieber of <i>The New York Times</i> has criticized firms such as Zipcar for the paltry coverage afforded carsharing drivers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
