<p><b>Accounting</b> or <b>accountancy</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Measurement" title="Measurement">measurement</a>, processing, and <a href="/wiki/Communication" title="Communication">communication</a> of financial information about <a href="/wiki/Economic_entity" title="Economic entity">economic entities</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NP_2013_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NP_2013-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> such as <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">businesses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">corporations</a>. The modern field was established by the <a href="/wiki/Italians" title="Italians">Italian</a> mathematician <a href="/wiki/Luca_Pacioli" title="Luca Pacioli">Luca Pacioli</a> in 1494.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jkdiwan_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-jkdiwan-3">[3]</a></sup>  Accounting, which has been called the "language of business",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wharton-language_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-wharton-language-4">[4]</a></sup> measures the results of an organization's economic activities and conveys this information to a variety of users, including <a href="/wiki/Investor" title="Investor">investors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Creditor" title="Creditor">creditors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Management" title="Management">management</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Regulatory_agency" title="Regulatory agency">regulators</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UW_Dept_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-UW_Dept-5">[5]</a></sup>  Practitioners of accounting are known as <a href="/wiki/Accountant" title="Accountant">accountants</a>. The terms "accounting" and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Financial_Reporting" title="Financial Reporting">financial reporting</a>" are often used as synonyms.
</p><p>Accounting can be divided into several fields including <a href="/wiki/Financial_accounting" title="Financial accounting">financial accounting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Management_accounting" title="Management accounting">management accounting</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auditing" title="Auditing">external auditing</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tax_accounting" title="Tax accounting">tax accounting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cost_accounting" title="Cost accounting">cost accounting</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WC_1981_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-WC_1981-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Accounting_information_system" title="Accounting information system">Accounting information systems</a> are designed to support accounting functions and related activities. Financial accounting focuses on the reporting of an organization's financial information, including the preparation of <a href="/wiki/Financial_statement" title="Financial statement">financial statements</a>, to the external users of the information, such as <a href="/wiki/Investor" title="Investor">investors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Regulatory_agency" title="Regulatory agency">regulators</a> and <a href="/wiki/Supply_chain" title="Supply chain">suppliers</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDF_2006_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-HDF_2006-8">[8]</a></sup> and management accounting focuses on the measurement, analysis and reporting of information for internal use by management.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NP_2013_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NP_2013-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDF_2006_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-HDF_2006-8">[8]</a></sup> The recording of financial transactions, so that summaries of the financials may be presented in financial reports, is known as <a href="/wiki/Bookkeeping" title="Bookkeeping">bookkeeping</a>, of which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double-entry_bookkeeping" title="Double-entry bookkeeping">double-entry bookkeeping</a> is the most common system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L_2009_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-L_2009-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Accounting is facilitated by <a href="/wiki/Category:Accounting_organizations" title="Category:Accounting organizations">accounting organizations</a> such as standard-setters, <a href="/wiki/Accounting_networks_and_associations" title="Accounting networks and associations">accounting firms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Professional_accounting_body" title="Professional accounting body">professional bodies</a>. <a href="/wiki/Financial_statement" title="Financial statement">Financial statements</a> are usually audited by <a href="/wiki/Accounting_networks_and_associations" title="Accounting networks and associations">accounting firms</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Parliament_Auditors_1_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Parliament_Auditors_1-10">[10]</a></sup> and are prepared in accordance with <a href="/wiki/Accounting_standard" title="Accounting standard">generally accepted accounting principles</a> (GAAP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDF_2006_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-HDF_2006-8">[8]</a></sup> GAAP is set by various standard-setting organizations such as the <a href="/wiki/Financial_Accounting_Standards_Board" title="Financial Accounting Standards Board">Financial Accounting Standards Board</a> (FASB) in the United States<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NP_2013_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-NP_2013-1">[1]</a></sup> and the Financial Reporting Council in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. As of 2012, "all major economies" have plans to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convergence_(accounting)" title="Convergence (accounting)">converge</a> towards or adopt the <a href="/wiki/International_Financial_Reporting_Standards" title="International Financial Reporting Standards">International Financial Reporting Standards</a> (IFRS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-globalcon_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-globalcon-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_accounting" title="History of accounting">History of accounting</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pacioli.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1250" data-file-width="1500" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Pacioli.jpg/260px-Pacioli.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Pacioli.jpg/390px-Pacioli.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Pacioli.jpg/520px-Pacioli.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pacioli.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Portrait_of_Luca_Pacioli" title="Portrait of Luca Pacioli">Portrait of Luca Pacioli</a></i>, painted by <a href="/wiki/Jacopo_de%27_Barbari" title="Jacopo de' Barbari">Jacopo de' Barbari</a>, 1495, (<a href="/wiki/Museo_di_Capodimonte" title="Museo di Capodimonte">Museo di Capodimonte</a>).</div></div></div>
<p>The history of accounting is thousands of years old and can be traced to <a href="/wiki/Ancient_history" title="Ancient history">ancient</a> <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilizations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-R_1992_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-R_1992-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYSSCPA_2003_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYSSCPA_2003-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UniSA_2013_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-UniSA_2013-14">[14]</a></sup> The early development of accounting dates back to ancient <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>, and is closely related to developments in <a href="/wiki/Writing" title="Writing">writing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Counting" title="Counting">counting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Money" title="Money">money</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-R_1992_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-R_1992-12">[12]</a></sup> there is also evidence for early forms of <a href="/wiki/Bookkeeping" title="Bookkeeping">bookkeeping</a> in ancient <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G_1980_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-G_1980-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OD_2008_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-OD_2008-16">[16]</a></sup> and early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auditing" title="Auditing">auditing</a> systems by the ancient <a href="/wiki/Egyptians" title="Egyptians">Egyptians</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Babylonians" title="Babylonians">Babylonians</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYSSCPA_2003_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYSSCPA_2003-13">[13]</a></sup> By the time of the Emperor <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Roman_Government" title="Ancient Roman Government">Roman government</a> had access to detailed financial information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O_1995_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-O_1995-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double-entry_bookkeeping" title="Double-entry bookkeeping">Double-entry bookkeeping</a> was pioneered in the Jewish community of the early-medieval Middle East<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and further refined in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval_Europe" title="Medieval Europe">medieval Europe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-logica.ugent.be_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-logica.ugent.be-20">[20]</a></sup> With the development of <a href="/wiki/Joint-stock_company" title="Joint-stock company">joint-stock companies</a>, accounting split into <a href="/wiki/Financial_accounting" title="Financial accounting">financial accounting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Management_accounting" title="Management accounting">management accounting</a>. 
</p><p>The first work on a <a href="/wiki/Double-entry_bookkeeping_system" title="Double-entry bookkeeping system">double-entry bookkeeping system</a> was published in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, by <a href="/wiki/Luca_Pacioli" title="Luca Pacioli">Luca Pacioli</a> ("Father of Accounting").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LW_1994_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-LW_1994-22">[22]</a></sup> Accounting began to transition into an organized profession in the nineteenth century,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICAEW_2013_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-ICAEW_2013-23">[23]</a></sup> with local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Professional_bodies" title="Professional bodies">professional bodies</a> in England merging to form the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Chartered_Accountants_in_England_and_Wales" title="Institute of Chartered Accountants in England and Wales">Institute of Chartered Accountants in England and Wales</a> in 1880.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Perks16_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Perks16-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hauptbuch_Hochstetter_vor_1828.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1464" data-file-width="2788" height="89" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Hauptbuch_Hochstetter_vor_1828.jpg/170px-Hauptbuch_Hochstetter_vor_1828.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Hauptbuch_Hochstetter_vor_1828.jpg/255px-Hauptbuch_Hochstetter_vor_1828.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Hauptbuch_Hochstetter_vor_1828.jpg/340px-Hauptbuch_Hochstetter_vor_1828.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hauptbuch_Hochstetter_vor_1828.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Early 19th-century <a href="/wiki/Ledger" title="Ledger">ledger</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Both the words accounting and accountancy were in use in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> by the mid-1800s, and are derived from the words <i>accompting</i> and <i>accountantship</i> used in the 18th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LN_2009_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-LN_2009-25">[25]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> (used roughly between the 12th and the late 15th century) the verb "to account" had the form <i>accounten</i>, which was derived from the Old French word <i>aconter</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-B_1984_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-B_1984-26">[26]</a></sup> which is in turn related to the <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">Vulgar Latin</a> word <i>computare</i>, meaning "to reckon". The base of <i>computare</i> is <i>putare</i>, which "variously meant to prune, to purify, to correct an account, hence, to count or calculate, as well as to think."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-B_1984_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-B_1984-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>The word "<a href="/wiki/Accountant" title="Accountant">accountant</a>" is derived from the French word <i lang="fr" title="French language text">compter</i>, which is also derived from the Italian and <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> word <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">computare</i>. The word was formerly written in English as "accomptant", but in process of time the word, which was always pronounced by dropping the "p", became gradually changed both in <a href="/wiki/Pronunciation" title="Pronunciation">pronunciation</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">orthography</a> to its present form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accounting_and_accountancy">Accounting and accountancy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Accounting and accountancy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Accounting</b> has variously been defined as the keeping or preparation of the financial records of an entity, the analysis, verification and reporting of such records and "the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generally_accepted_accounting_principles" title="Generally accepted accounting principles">principles</a> and procedures of accounting"; it also refers to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Job_(role)" title="Job (role)">job</a> of being an <a href="/wiki/Accountant" title="Accountant">accountant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_Business_Accounting_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge_Business_Accounting-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_British_Accounting_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge_British_Accounting-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MW_2013_accounting_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-MW_2013_accounting-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Accountancy</b> refers to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Job_(role)" title="Job (role)">occupation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Profession" title="Profession">profession</a> of an accountant,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MW_2013_accountancy_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-MW_2013_accountancy-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_Business_Accountancy_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge_Business_Accountancy-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_British_Accountancy_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge_British_Accountancy-33">[33]</a></sup> particularly in <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_Business_Accounting_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge_Business_Accounting-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_British_Accounting_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge_British_Accounting-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Topics">Topics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Topics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Accounting has several subfields or subject areas, including <a href="/wiki/Financial_accounting" title="Financial accounting">financial accounting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Management_accounting" title="Management accounting">management accounting</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auditing" title="Auditing">auditing</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taxation" title="Taxation">taxation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Accounting_information_system" title="Accounting information system">accounting information systems</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WC_1981_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-WC_1981-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Financial_accounting">Financial accounting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Financial accounting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Financial_accounting" title="Financial accounting">Financial accounting</a></div>
<p>Financial accounting focuses on the reporting of an organization's financial information to external users of the information, such as investors, potential investors and creditors. It calculates and records business transactions and prepares <a href="/wiki/Financial_statement" title="Financial statement">financial statements</a> for the external users in accordance with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generally_accepted_accounting_principles" title="Generally accepted accounting principles">generally accepted accounting principles</a> (GAAP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDF_2006_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-HDF_2006-8">[8]</a></sup> GAAP, in turn, arises from the wide agreement between <a href="/wiki/Accounting_research" title="Accounting research">accounting theory</a> and practice, and change over time to meet the needs of decision-makers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NP_2013_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-NP_2013-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Financial accounting produces past-oriented reports—for example the financial statements prepared in 2006 reports on performance in 2005—on an <a href="/wiki/Annual_report" title="Annual report">annual</a> or quarterly basis, generally about the organization as a whole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDF_2006_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-HDF_2006-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>This branch of accounting is also studied as part of the board exams for qualifying as an actuary. These two types of professionals, accountants and actuaries, have created a culture of being archrivals.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Management_accounting">Management accounting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Management accounting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Management_accounting" title="Management accounting">Management accounting</a></div>
<p>Management accounting focuses on the measurement, analysis and reporting of information that can help managers in making decisions to fulfill the goals of an organization. In management accounting, internal measures and reports are based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cost-benefit_analysis" title="Cost-benefit analysis">cost-benefit analysis</a>, and are not required to follow the generally accepted accounting principle (GAAP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDF_2006_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-HDF_2006-8">[8]</a></sup> In 2014 CIMA created the <a class="external text" href="http://www.cgma.org/Resources/Reports/Pages/GlobalManagementAccountingPrinciples.aspx" rel="nofollow">Global Management Accounting Principles (GMAPs)</a>. The result of research from across 20 countries in five continents, the principles aim to guide best practice in the discipline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FT_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FT-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Management accounting produces future-oriented reports—for example the budget for 2006 is prepared in 2005—and the time span of reports varies widely. Such reports may include both financial and non financial information, and may, for example, focus on specific products and departments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HDF_2006_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-HDF_2006-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Auditing">Auditing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Auditing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Financial_audit" title="Financial audit">Financial audit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Internal_audit" title="Internal audit">Internal audit</a></div>
<p>Auditing is the verification of assertions made by others regarding a payoff,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-B_1979_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-B_1979-35">[35]</a></sup> and in the context of accounting it is the "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/unbiased" title="wiktionary:unbiased">unbiased</a> examination and evaluation of the financial statements of an organization".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-I_2013_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-I_2013-36">[36]</a></sup> Audit is a professional service that is systematic and conventional.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p><br/>
An audit of financial statements aims to express or disclaim an opinion on the financial statements. The auditor expresses an opinion on the fairness with which the financial statements presents the financial position, results of operations, and cash flows of an entity, in accordance with the  generally acceptable accounting principle  (GAAP) and "in all material respects". An auditor is also required to identify circumstances in which the generally acceptable accounting principles (GAAP) has not been consistently observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAS_1_Responsibilities_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-SAS_1_Responsibilities-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accounting_information_systems">Accounting information systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Accounting information systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Accounting_information_system" title="Accounting information system">Accounting information system</a></div>
<p>An accounting information system is a part of an organization's <a href="/wiki/Information_system" title="Information system">information system</a> that focuses on processing accounting data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OpenLearn_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-OpenLearn-39">[39]</a></sup>
Many corporations use artificial intelligence-based information systems. Banking and finance industry is using AI as fraud detection. Retail industry is using AI for customer services. AI is also used in cybersecurity industry. It involves computer hardware and software systems and using statistics and modeling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tax_accounting">Tax accounting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Tax accounting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tax_accounting" title="Tax accounting">Tax accounting</a></div>
<p>Tax accounting in the United States concentrates on the preparation, analysis and presentation of tax payments and tax returns. The U.S. tax  system requires the use of specialised accounting principles for tax purposes which can differ from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generally_accepted_accounting_principles" title="Generally accepted accounting principles">generally accepted accounting principles</a> (GAAP) for financial reporting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2010_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2010-41">[41]</a></sup> U.S. tax law covers four basic forms of business ownership: <a href="/wiki/Sole_proprietorship" title="Sole proprietorship">sole proprietorship</a>, <a href="/wiki/Partnership" title="Partnership">partnership</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">corporation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Limited_liability_company" title="Limited liability company">limited liability company</a>. <a href="/wiki/Corporate_tax_in_the_United_States" title="Corporate tax in the United States">Corporate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Income_tax_in_the_United_States" title="Income tax in the United States">personal</a> income are taxed at different rates, both varying according to income levels and including varying marginal rates (taxed on each additional dollar of income) and average rates (set as a percentage of overall income).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2010_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2010-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_bodies">Professional bodies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Professional bodies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Professional_accounting_body" title="Professional accounting body">Professional accounting body</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Professional_accounting_bodies" title="Professional accounting bodies">Professional accounting bodies</a> include the <a href="/wiki/American_Institute_of_Certified_Public_Accountants" title="American Institute of Certified Public Accountants">American Institute of Certified Public Accountants</a> (AICPA) and the other 179 members of the <a href="/wiki/International_Federation_of_Accountants" title="International Federation of Accountants">International Federation of Accountants</a> (IFAC),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ifac_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-ifac-42">[42]</a></sup> including <a href="/wiki/CPA_Australia" title="CPA Australia">CPA Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Chartered_Certified_Accountants" title="Association of Chartered Certified Accountants">Association of Chartered Certified Accountants</a> (ACCA) and <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Chartered_Accountants_in_England_and_Wales" title="Institute of Chartered Accountants in England and Wales">Institute of Chartered Accountants in England and Wales</a> (ICAEW). Professional bodies for subfields of the accounting professions also exist, for example the <a href="/wiki/Chartered_Institute_of_Management_Accountants" title="Chartered Institute of Management Accountants">Chartered Institute of Management Accountants</a> (CIMA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prince_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prince-43">[43]</a></sup> Many of these professional bodies offer education and training including qualification and administration for various accounting designations, such as certified public accountant and <a href="/wiki/Chartered_accountant" title="Chartered accountant">chartered accountant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AICPA_CPA_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-AICPA_CPA-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICAEW_ACA_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-ICAEW_ACA-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accounting_firms">Accounting firms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Accounting firms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Accounting_networks_and_associations" title="Accounting networks and associations">Accounting networks and associations</a></div>
<p>Depending on its size, a company may be legally required to have their <a href="/wiki/Financial_statement" title="Financial statement">financial statements</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auditing" title="Auditing">audited</a> by a qualified auditor, and audits are usually carried out by <a href="/wiki/Accounting_networks_and_associations" title="Accounting networks and associations">accounting firms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Parliament_Auditors_1_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Parliament_Auditors_1-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Accounting firms grew in the United States and Europe in the late nineteenth and early twentieth century, and through several mergers there were large international accounting firms by the mid-twentieth century. Further large mergers in the late twentieth century led to the dominance of the auditing market by the "Big Five" accounting firms: <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Andersen" title="Arthur Andersen">Arthur Andersen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deloitte" title="Deloitte">Deloitte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ernst_%26_Young" title="Ernst &amp; Young">Ernst &amp; Young</a>, <a href="/wiki/KPMG" title="KPMG">KPMG</a> and <a href="/wiki/PricewaterhouseCoopers" title="PricewaterhouseCoopers">PricewaterhouseCoopers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Parliament_Auditors_2_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Parliament_Auditors_2-46">[46]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Andersen#Demise" title="Arthur Andersen">demise of Arthur Andersen</a> following the <a href="/wiki/Enron_scandal" title="Enron scandal">Enron scandal</a> reduced the Big Five to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_Four_(audit_firms)" title="Big Four (audit firms)">Big Four</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FT_Big_Four_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-FT_Big_Four-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Standard-setters">Standard-setters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Standard-setters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accounting_standards" title="Accounting standards">Accounting standards</a> and <a href="/wiki/Convergence_of_accounting_standards" title="Convergence of accounting standards">Convergence of accounting standards</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generally_accepted_accounting_principles" title="Generally accepted accounting principles">Generally accepted accounting principles</a> (GAAP) are accounting standards issued by national regulatory bodies. In addition, the <a href="/wiki/International_Accounting_Standards_Board" title="International Accounting Standards Board">International Accounting Standards Board</a> (IASB) issues the <a href="/wiki/International_Financial_Reporting_Standards" title="International Financial Reporting Standards">International Financial Reporting Standards</a> (IFRS) implemented by 147 countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NP_2013_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-NP_2013-1">[1]</a></sup> While standards for international audit and assurance, ethics, education, and public sector accounting are all set by independent standard settings boards supported by IFAC. The International Auditing and Assurance Standards Board sets international standards for auditing, assurance, and quality control; the <a href="/wiki/International_Ethics_Standards_Board_for_Accountants" title="International Ethics Standards Board for Accountants">International Ethics Standards Board for Accountants</a> (IESBA) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ifac2_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-ifac2-48">[48]</a></sup> sets the internationally appropriate principles- based <i>Code of Ethics for Professional Accounts</i> the <a href="/wiki/International_Accounting_Education_Standards_Board" title="International Accounting Education Standards Board">International Accounting Education Standards Board</a> (IAESB) sets professional accounting education standards;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ifac3_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-ifac3-49">[49]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/International_Public_Sector_Accounting_Standards" title="International Public Sector Accounting Standards">International Public Sector Accounting Standards</a> Board (IPSASB) sets accrual-based international public sector accounting standards <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ifac4_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-ifac4-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>Organizations in individual countries may issue accounting standards unique to the countries. For example, in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> the <a href="/wiki/Financial_Accounting_Standards_Board" title="Financial Accounting Standards Board">Financial Accounting Standards Board</a> (FASB) issues the Statements of Financial Accounting Standards, which form the basis of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_GAAP" title="US GAAP">US GAAP</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NP_2013_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-NP_2013-1">[1]</a></sup> and in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> the <a href="/wiki/Financial_Reporting_Council" title="Financial Reporting Council">Financial Reporting Council</a> (FRC) sets accounting standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICAEW_Standards_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-ICAEW_Standards-51">[51]</a></sup> However, as of 2012 "all major economies" have plans to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convergence_(accounting)" title="Convergence (accounting)">converge</a> towards or adopt the IFRS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-globalcon_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-globalcon-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_qualifications">Education and qualifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Education and qualifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accounting_degrees">Accounting degrees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Accounting degrees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At least a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor's degree">bachelor's degree</a> in accounting or a related field is required for most accountant and <a href="/wiki/Auditor" title="Auditor">auditor</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Job_(role)" title="Job (role)">job positions</a>, and some employers prefer applicants with a <a href="/wiki/Master%27s_degree" title="Master's degree">master's degree</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLS_2012_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-BLS_2012-52">[52]</a></sup> A degree in accounting may also be required for, or may be used to fulfill the requirements for, membership to professional accounting bodies. For example, the education during an accounting degree can be used to fulfill the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AICPA" title="AICPA">American Institute of CPA's</a> (AICPA) 150 semester hour requirement,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AICPA_150_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-AICPA_150-53">[53]</a></sup> and associate membership with the <a href="/wiki/Certified_Public_Accountants_Association" title="Certified Public Accountants Association">Certified Public Accountants Association</a> of the UK is available after gaining a degree in finance or accounting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACPA_Criteria_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-ACPA_Criteria-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">doctorate</a> is required in order to pursue a career in accounting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academia" title="Academia">academia</a>, for example to work as a university <a href="/wiki/Professor" title="Professor">professor</a> in accounting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AICPA_Academia_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-AICPA_Academia-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BYU_Prep_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-BYU_Prep-56">[56]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Doctor of Philosophy</a> (PhD) and the <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Business_Administration" title="Doctor of Business Administration">Doctor of Business Administration</a> (DBA) are the most popular degrees. The PhD is the most common degree for those wishing to pursue a career in academia, while DBA programs generally focus on equipping <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Business_executives" title="Business executives">business executives</a> for business or public careers requiring research skills and qualifications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AICPA_Academia_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-AICPA_Academia-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_qualifications">Professional qualifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Professional qualifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chartered_Accountant" title="Chartered Accountant">Chartered Accountant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Certified_Public_Accountant" title="Certified Public Accountant">Certified Public Accountant</a></div>
<p>Professional accounting qualifications include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chartered_Accountant" title="Chartered Accountant">Chartered Accountant</a> designations and other qualifications including certificates and diplomas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACCA_Qualifications_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-ACCA_Qualifications-57">[57]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, chartered accountants of the ICAEW undergo annual training, and are bound by the ICAEW's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Code_of_ethics" title="Code of ethics">code of ethics</a> and subject to its disciplinary procedures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICAEW_Chartered_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-ICAEW_Chartered-58">[58]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the requirements for joining the AICPA as a <a href="/wiki/Certified_Public_Accountant" title="Certified Public Accountant">Certified Public Accountant</a> are set by the Board of Accountancy of each <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">state</a>, and members agree to abide by the AICPA's <a href="/wiki/Code_of_conduct" title="Code of conduct">Code of Professional Conduct</a> and Bylaws. In India the Apex Accounting body constituted by parliament of India is "<a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Chartered_Accountants_of_India" title="Institute of Chartered Accountants of India">Institute of Chartered Accountants of India</a>" (ICAI) was known for its rigorous training and study methodology for granting the Qualification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AICPA_CPA_FAQ_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-AICPA_CPA_FAQ-59">[59]</a></sup> The ACCA is the largest global accountancy body with over 320,000 members and the organisation provides an ‘IFRS stream’ and a ‘UK stream’. Students must pass a total of 14 exams, which are arranged across three papers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accounting_research">Accounting research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Accounting research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Accounting_research" title="Accounting research">Accounting research</a></div>
<p>Accounting research is <a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">research</a> in the effects of economic events on the process of accounting, and the effects of reported information on economic events. It encompasses a broad range of research areas including <a href="/wiki/Financial_accounting" title="Financial accounting">financial accounting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Management_accounting" title="Management accounting">management accounting</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auditing" title="Auditing">auditing</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taxation" title="Taxation">taxation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OOS_2010_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-OOS_2010-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p><p>Accounting research is carried out both by academic researchers and practicing accountants. <a href="/wiki/Methodology" title="Methodology">Methodologies</a> in academic accounting research can be classified into archival research, which examines "objective data collected from <a href="/wiki/Information_repository" title="Information repository">repositories</a>"; experimental research, which examines data "the researcher gathered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Controlled_experiments" title="Controlled experiments">administering treatments to subjects</a>"; and analytical research, which is "based on the act of <a href="/wiki/Scientific_modelling" title="Scientific modelling">formally modeling</a> <a href="/wiki/Theory" title="Theory">theories</a> or substantiating ideas in mathematical terms". This classification is not exhaustive; other possible methodologies include the use of <a href="/wiki/Case_study" title="Case study">case studies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer_simulation" title="Computer simulation">computer simulations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Field_research" title="Field research">field research</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSWW_2010_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-CSWW_2010-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p><p>Empirical studies document that leading <a href="/wiki/List_of_accounting_journals" title="List of accounting journals">accounting journals</a> publish in total fewer research articles than comparable journals in economics and other business disciplines<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JAE_2002_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-JAE_2002-63">[63]</a></sup>, and consequently, accounting scholars<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> are relatively less successful in <a href="/wiki/Academic_publishing" title="Academic publishing">academic publishing</a> than their <a href="/wiki/Business_school" title="Business school">business school</a> peers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CAR_2004_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-CAR_2004-65">[65]</a></sup> Due to different publication rates between accounting and other business disciplines, a recent study based on academic author rankings concludes that the competitive value of a single publication in a top-ranked journal is highest in accounting and lowest in marketing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JBR_2018_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-JBR_2018-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accounting_information_system">Accounting information system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Accounting information system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-style ambox-No_footnotes" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" data-file-height="48" data-file-width="48" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Text_document_with_red_question_mark.svg/40px-Text_document_with_red_question_mark.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Text_document_with_red_question_mark.svg/60px-Text_document_with_red_question_mark.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Text_document_with_red_question_mark.svg/80px-Text_document_with_red_question_mark.svg.png 2x" width="40"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section includes a <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">list of references</a>, related reading or <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:External_links" title="Wikipedia:External links">external links</a>, <b>but its sources remain unclear because it lacks <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#Inline_citations" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">inline citations</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help to <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:WikiProject_Fact_and_Reference_Check" title="Wikipedia:WikiProject Fact and Reference Check">improve</a> this section by <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:When_to_cite" title="Wikipedia:When to cite">introducing</a> more precise citations.</span> <small><i>(January 2017)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Accounting_information_system" title="Accounting information system">Accounting information system</a></div>
<p>Many accounting practices have been simplified with the help of accounting computer-based software. An <a href="/wiki/Enterprise_resource_planning" title="Enterprise resource planning">Enterprise resource planning</a> (ERP) system is commonly used for a large organisation and it provides a comprehensive, centralized, integrated source of information that companies can use to manage all major business processes, from purchasing to manufacturing to human resources.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Accounting_information_system" title="Accounting information system">Accounting information systems</a> have reduced the cost of accumulating, storing, and reporting managerial accounting information and have made it possible to produce a more detailed account of all data that is entered into any given system.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accounting_scandals">Accounting scandals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Accounting scandals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Accounting_scandals" title="Accounting scandals">Accounting scandals</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Accounting_ethics" title="Accounting ethics">Accounting ethics</a></div>
<p>The year 2001 witnessed a series of financial information frauds involving <a href="/wiki/Enron" title="Enron">Enron</a>, auditing firm <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Andersen" title="Arthur Andersen">Arthur Andersen</a>, the telecommunications company <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WorldCom" title="WorldCom">WorldCom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Qwest" title="Qwest">Qwest</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jarden" title="Jarden">Sunbeam</a>, among other well-known corporations. These problems highlighted the need to review the effectiveness of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_accounting_practice" title="Standard accounting practice">accounting standards</a>, auditing regulations and <a href="/wiki/Corporate_governance" title="Corporate governance">corporate governance</a> principles. In some cases, management manipulated the figures shown in financial reports to indicate a better economic performance. In others, tax and regulatory incentives encouraged over-leveraging of companies and decisions to bear extraordinary and unjustified risk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mba.ufm.edu.gt_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-mba.ufm.edu.gt-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Enron_scandal" title="Enron scandal">Enron scandal</a> deeply influenced the development of new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regulations" title="Regulations">regulations</a> to improve the reliability of financial reporting, and increased public awareness about the importance of having accounting standards that show the financial reality of companies and the objectivity and independence of auditing firms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mba.ufm.edu.gt_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-mba.ufm.edu.gt-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition to being the largest <a href="/wiki/Bankruptcy" title="Bankruptcy">bankruptcy</a> reorganization in American history, the <a href="/wiki/Enron_scandal" title="Enron scandal">Enron scandal</a> undoubtedly is the biggest audit failure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> It involved a <a href="/wiki/Accounting_scandals" title="Accounting scandals">financial scandal</a> of <a href="/wiki/Enron" title="Enron">Enron Corporation</a> and their auditors <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Andersen" title="Arthur Andersen">Arthur Andersen</a>, which was revealed in late 2001. The scandal caused the dissolution of <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Andersen" title="Arthur Andersen">Arthur Andersen</a>, which at the time was one of the five largest accounting firms in the world. After a series of revelations involving irregular accounting procedures conducted throughout the 1990s, Enron filed for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chapter_11" title="Chapter 11">Chapter 11</a> bankruptcy protection in December 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>One consequence of these events was the passage of <a href="/wiki/Sarbanes%E2%80%93Oxley_Act" title="Sarbanes–Oxley Act">Sarbanes–Oxley Act</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> 2002, as a result of the first admissions of fraudulent behavior made by Enron. The act significantly raises criminal penalties for <a href="/wiki/Securities_fraud" title="Securities fraud">securities fraud</a>, for destroying, altering or fabricating records in federal investigations or any scheme or attempt to defraud shareholders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p>
