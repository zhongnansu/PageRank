<p><b>Fiber</b> or <b>fibre</b> (see <a href="/wiki/American_and_British_English_spelling_differences#-re,_-er" title="American and British English spelling differences">spelling differences</a>, from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> fibra<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) is a <a href="/wiki/Natural_fiber" title="Natural fiber">natural</a> or <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_fiber" title="Synthetic fiber">synthetic</a> substance that is significantly longer than it is wide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> Fibers are often used in the manufacture of other materials. The strongest engineering materials often incorporate fibers, for example <a href="/wiki/Carbon_fibers" title="Carbon fibers">carbon fiber</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ultra-high-molecular-weight_polyethylene" title="Ultra-high-molecular-weight polyethylene">ultra-high-molecular-weight polyethylene</a>.
</p><p>Synthetic fibers can often be produced very cheaply and in large amounts compared to natural fibers, but for clothing natural fibers can give some benefits, such as comfort, over their synthetic counterparts.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_fibers">Natural fibers</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Natural_fiber" title="Natural fiber">Natural fiber</a></div>
<p>Natural fibers develop or occur in the fiber shape, and include those produced by plants, animals, and geological processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> They can be classified according to their origin:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Fiber_crop" title="Fiber crop">Vegetable fibers</a> are generally based on arrangements of <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a>, often with <a href="/wiki/Lignin" title="Lignin">lignin</a>: examples include <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_sativa" title="Cannabis sativa">hemp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jute" title="Jute">jute</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flax" title="Flax">flax</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ramie" title="Ramie">ramie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sisal" title="Sisal">sisal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bagasse" title="Bagasse">bagasse</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Banana#Fiber" title="Banana">banana</a>. Plant fibers are employed in the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">textile</a> (cloth), and <a href="/wiki/Dietary_fiber" title="Dietary fiber">dietary fiber</a> is an important component of human nutrition.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wood_fibre" title="Wood fibre">Wood fiber</a>, distinguished from vegetable fiber, is from tree sources. Forms include <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Groundwood&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Groundwood (page does not exist)">groundwood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lagetta_lagetto" title="Lagetta lagetto">lacebark</a>, thermomechanical pulp (TMP), and bleached or unbleached <a href="/wiki/Kraft_process" title="Kraft process">kraft</a> or sulfite pulps. Kraft and sulfite (also called sulphite) refer to the type of pulping process used to remove the lignin bonding the original wood structure, thus freeing the fibers for use in paper and <a href="/wiki/Engineered_wood" title="Engineered wood">engineered wood</a> products such as <a href="/wiki/Fiberboard" title="Fiberboard">fiberboard</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Animal_fiber" title="Animal fiber">Animal fibers</a> consist largely of particular proteins. Instances are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silkworm" title="Silkworm">silkworm</a> <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spider_silk" title="Spider silk">spider silk</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinew" title="Sinew">sinew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catgut" title="Catgut">catgut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sea_silk" title="Sea silk">sea silk</a> and hair such as <a href="/wiki/Cashmere_wool" title="Cashmere wool">cashmere wool</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mohair" title="Mohair">mohair</a> and <a href="/wiki/Angora_wool" title="Angora wool">angora</a>, fur such as sheepskin, rabbit, mink, fox, beaver, etc.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mineral_fiber" title="Mineral fiber">Mineral fibers</a> include the <a href="/wiki/Asbestos" title="Asbestos">asbestos</a> group. Asbestos is the only naturally occurring long <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> fiber. Six minerals have been classified as "asbestos" including <a href="/wiki/Chrysotile" title="Chrysotile">chrysotile</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serpentine_group" title="Serpentine group">serpentine</a> class and those belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Amphibole" title="Amphibole">amphibole</a> class: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amosite" title="Amosite">amosite</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crocidolite" title="Crocidolite">crocidolite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tremolite" title="Tremolite">tremolite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthophyllite" title="Anthophyllite">anthophyllite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Actinolite" title="Actinolite">actinolite</a>. Short, fiber-like minerals include <a href="/wiki/Wollastonite" title="Wollastonite">wollastonite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palygorskite" title="Palygorskite">palygorskite</a>.</li>
<li>Biological fibers also known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fibrous_proteins" title="Fibrous proteins">fibrous proteins</a> or <a href="/wiki/Protein_filament" title="Protein filament">protein filaments</a> consist largely of biologically relevant and biologically very important proteins, mutations or other genetic defects can lead to <a href="/wiki/Collagen#Diseases" title="Collagen">severe diseases</a>. Instances are <a href="/wiki/Collagen" title="Collagen">collagen</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saad_1994_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Saad_1994-3">[3]</a></sup> family of proteins, <a href="/wiki/Tendon" title="Tendon">tendon</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muscle_proteins" title="Muscle proteins">muscle proteins</a> like <a href="/wiki/Actin" title="Actin">actin</a>, cell proteins like <a href="/wiki/Microtubule" title="Microtubule">microtubules</a> and many others, <a href="/wiki/Spider_silk" title="Spider silk">spider silk</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinew" title="Sinew">sinew</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hair" title="Hair">hair</a> etc.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human-made_fibers">Human-made fibers<span id="Chemical"></span></span></h2>
<p>Human-made or chemical fibers are fibers whose chemical composition, structure, and properties are significantly modified during the manufacturing process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Man-made fibers consist of regenerated fibers and synthetic fibers.
</p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fibre_modification" title="Fibre modification">fibre modification</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Semi-synthetic_fibers">Semi-synthetic fibers</span></h3>
<p>Semi-synthetic fibers are made from raw materials with naturally long-chain polymer structure and are only modified and partially degraded by chemical processes, in contrast to completely synthetic fibers such as <a href="/wiki/Nylon" title="Nylon">nylon</a> (polyamide) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dacron" title="Dacron">dacron</a> (polyester), which the chemist synthesizes from low-molecular weight compounds by polymerization (chain-building) reactions. The earliest semi-synthetic fiber is the cellulose regenerated fiber, <a href="/wiki/Rayon" title="Rayon">rayon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Most semi-synthetic fibers are cellulose regenerated fibers.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cellulose_regenerated_fibers">Cellulose regenerated fibers<span id="Regenerated"></span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cellulose_fiber" title="Cellulose fiber">Cellulose fibers</a> are a subset of man-made fibers, regenerated from natural <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a>. The cellulose comes from various sources: rayon from tree wood fiber, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modal_(textile)" title="Modal (textile)">Modal</a> from beech trees, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bamboo_fiber" title="Bamboo fiber">bamboo fiber</a> from bamboo, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Seacell&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Seacell (page does not exist)">seacell</a> from <a href="/wiki/Seaweed" title="Seaweed">seaweed</a>, etc. In the production of these fibers, the cellulose is reduced to a fairly pure form as a viscous mass and formed into fibers by extrusion through spinnerets. Therefore, the manufacturing process leaves few characteristics distinctive of the natural source material in the finished products.
</p><p>Some examples of this fiber type are:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Rayon" title="Rayon">rayon</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bamboo_textiles" title="Bamboo textiles">bamboo fiber</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lyocell" title="Lyocell">Lyocell</a>, a brand of rayon</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modal_(textile)" title="Modal (textile)">Modal</a>, using beech trees as input</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cellulose_diacetate" title="Cellulose diacetate">diacetate fiber</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cellulose_triacetate" title="Cellulose triacetate">triacetate fiber</a>.</li></ul>
<p>Historically, cellulose diacetate and -triacetate were classified under the term rayon, but are now considered distinct materials.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthetic_fibers">Synthetic fibers</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_fiber" title="Synthetic fiber">Synthetic fiber</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Synthetic_fiber" title="Synthetic fiber">Synthetic</a> come entirely from synthetic materials such as <a href="/wiki/Petrochemical" title="Petrochemical">petrochemicals</a>, unlike those man-made fibers derived from such natural substances as cellulose or protein.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fiber classification in reinforced plastics falls into two classes: (i) short fibers, also known as discontinuous fibers, with a general aspect ratio (defined as the ratio of fiber length to diameter) between 20 and 60, and (ii) long fibers, also known as continuous fibers, the general aspect ratio is between 200 and 500.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Metallic_fibers">Metallic fibers</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Metallic_fiber" title="Metallic fiber">Metallic fiber</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Metallic_fiber" title="Metallic fiber">Metallic fibers</a> can be drawn from ductile metals such as copper, gold or silver and extruded or deposited from more brittle ones, such as nickel, aluminum or iron.
See also <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel_fiber" title="Stainless steel fiber">Stainless steel fibers</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_fiber">Carbon fiber</span></h4>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_(fiber)" title="Carbon (fiber)">Carbon fibers</a> are often based on oxidized and via <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">pyrolysis</a> carbonized polymers like <a href="/wiki/Polyacrylonitrile" title="Polyacrylonitrile">PAN</a>, but the end product is almost pure carbon.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Silicon_carbide_fiber">Silicon carbide fiber</span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Silicon_carbide" title="Silicon carbide">Silicon carbide</a> fibers, where the basic polymers are not <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a> but polymers, where about 50% of the carbon atoms are replaced by silicon atoms, so-called poly-carbo-<a href="/wiki/Silane" title="Silane">silanes</a>. The pyrolysis yields an amorphous silicon carbide, including mostly other elements like oxygen, titanium, or aluminium, but with mechanical properties very similar to those of carbon fibers.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fiberglass">Fiberglass</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Glass#Fiberglass" title="Glass">Glass § Fiberglass</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fiberglass" title="Fiberglass">Fiberglass</a>, made from specific glass, and <a href="/wiki/Optical_fiber" title="Optical fiber">optical fiber</a>, made from purified natural <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a>, are also man-made fibers that come from natural raw materials, <a href="/wiki/Silica_fiber" title="Silica fiber">silica fiber</a>, made from <a href="/wiki/Sodium_silicate" title="Sodium silicate">sodium silicate</a> (water glass) and <a href="/wiki/Basalt_fiber" title="Basalt fiber">basalt fiber</a> made from melted basalt.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mineral_fibers">Mineral fibers</span></h4>
<p>Mineral fibers can be particularly strong because they are formed with a low number of surface defects, <a href="/wiki/Asbestos" title="Asbestos">asbestos</a> is a common one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Polymer_fibers">Polymer fibers</span></h4>
<ul><li>Polymer fibers are a subset of man-made fibers, which are based on synthetic chemicals (often from <a href="/wiki/Petrochemical" title="Petrochemical">petrochemical</a> sources) rather than arising from natural materials by a purely physical process. These fibers are made from:
<ul><li>polyamide <a href="/wiki/Nylon" title="Nylon">nylon</a></li>
<li>PET or PBT <a href="/wiki/Polyester" title="Polyester">polyester</a></li>
<li>phenol-formaldehyde (PF)</li>
<li>polyvinyl chloride fiber (PVC) <a href="/wiki/Vinyon" title="Vinyon">vinyon</a></li>
<li>polyolefins (PP and PE) <a href="/wiki/Olefin_fiber" title="Olefin fiber">olefin fiber</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Acrylic_fiber" title="Acrylic fiber">acrylic</a> polyesters, pure <a href="/wiki/Polyester" title="Polyester">polyester</a> PAN fibers are used to make <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_fiber" title="Carbon fiber">carbon fiber</a> by roasting them in a low oxygen environment. Traditional acrylic fiber is used more often as a synthetic replacement for wool. Carbon fibers and PF fibers are noted as two resin-based fibers that are not thermoplastic, most others can be melted.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aramid" title="Aramid">aromatic polyamids</a> (aramids) such as <a href="/wiki/Twaron" title="Twaron">Twaron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kevlar" title="Kevlar">Kevlar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nomex" title="Nomex">Nomex</a> thermally degrade at high temperatures and do not melt. These fibers have strong bonding between polymer chains</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">polyethylene</a> (PE), eventually with extremely long chains / <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultra_high_molecular_weight_polyethylene" title="Ultra high molecular weight polyethylene">HMPE</a> (e.g. Dyneema or Spectra).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Elastomer" title="Elastomer">Elastomers</a> can even be used, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Spandex" title="Spandex">spandex</a> although urethane fibers are starting to replace spandex technology.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Polyurethane" title="Polyurethane">polyurethane</a> fiber</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Elastolefin" title="Elastolefin">Elastolefin</a></li></ul></li>
<li>Coextruded fibers have two distinct polymers forming the fiber, usually as a core-sheath or side-by-side. Coated fibers exist such as nickel-coated to provide static elimination, silver-coated to provide anti-bacterial properties and aluminum-coated to provide RF deflection for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chaff_(radar_countermeasure)" title="Chaff (radar countermeasure)">radar chaff</a>. Radar chaff is actually a spool of continuous glass tow that has been aluminum coated. An aircraft-mounted high speed cutter chops it up as it spews from a moving aircraft to confuse radar signals.</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Microfibers">Microfibers</span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Microfiber" title="Microfiber">Microfibers</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Textiles" title="Textiles">textiles</a> refer to sub-denier fiber (such as <a href="/wiki/Polyester" title="Polyester">polyester</a> drawn to 0.5 denier). <a href="/wiki/Units_of_textile_measurement#Denier" title="Units of textile measurement">Denier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Units_of_textile_measurement#Tex" title="Units of textile measurement">Dtex</a> are two measurements of fiber yield based on weight and length. If the fiber density is known, you also have a fiber diameter, otherwise it is simpler to measure diameters in micrometers. Microfibers in technical fibers refer to ultra fine fibers (glass or meltblown <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermoplastics" title="Thermoplastics">thermoplastics</a>) often used in filtration. Newer fiber designs include extruding fiber that splits into multiple finer fibers. Most synthetic fibers are round in cross-section, but special designs can be hollow, oval, star-shaped or trilobal. The latter design provides more optically reflective properties. Synthetic textile fibers are often crimped to provide bulk in a woven, non woven or knitted structure. Fiber surfaces can also be dull or bright. Dull surfaces reflect more light while bright tends to transmit light and make the fiber more transparent.
</p><p>Very short and/or irregular fibers have been called fibrils. Natural <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a> or bleached <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kraft" title="Kraft">kraft</a>, show smaller fibrils jutting out and away from the main fiber structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b1_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-b1-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
