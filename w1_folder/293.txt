<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Kolbe–Schmitt reaction</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>Kolbe–Schmitt reaction</b> or <b>Kolbe process</b> (named after <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Kolbe" title="Hermann Kolbe">Hermann Kolbe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Schmitt" title="Rudolf Schmitt">Rudolf Schmitt</a>) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carboxyl" title="Carboxyl">carboxylation</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reaction</a> that proceeds by heating sodium phenoxide (the <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> salt of <a href="/wiki/Phenol" title="Phenol">phenol</a>) with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> under pressure (100 <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atm</a>, 125 °C), then treating the product with <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>. The final product is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aromatic" title="Aromatic">aromatic</a> hydroxy <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acid</a> which is also known as <a href="/wiki/Salicylic_acid" title="Salicylic acid">salicylic acid</a> (the precursor to <a href="/wiki/Aspirin" title="Aspirin">aspirin</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kolbe-Schmitt.png" title="The Kolbe–Schmitt reaction"><img alt="The Kolbe–Schmitt reaction" data-file-height="279" data-file-width="1380" height="101" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Kolbe-Schmitt.png/500px-Kolbe-Schmitt.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Kolbe-Schmitt.png/750px-Kolbe-Schmitt.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Kolbe-Schmitt.png/1000px-Kolbe-Schmitt.png 2x" width="500"/></a></div></div>
<p>By using <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">potassium hydroxide</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/4-hydroxybenzoic_acid" title="4-hydroxybenzoic acid">4-hydroxybenzoic acid</a> is accessible, an important precursor for the versatile <a href="/wiki/Paraben" title="Paraben">paraben</a> class of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biocides" title="Biocides">biocides</a> used e.g. in personal care products.
</p><p>The methodology is also used in the industrial synthesis of <a href="/wiki/3-Hydroxy-2-naphthoic_acid" title="3-Hydroxy-2-naphthoic acid">3-hydroxy-2-naphthoic acid</a>.  The regiochemistry of the carboxylation in this case is sensitive to temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction_mechanism">Reaction mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kolbe%E2%80%93Schmitt_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Reaction mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Kolbe–Schmitt reaction proceeds via the nucleophile addition of a phenoxide, classically <a href="/wiki/Sodium_phenoxide" title="Sodium phenoxide">sodium phenoxide</a> (NaOC<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>), to carbon dioxide to give the salicylate.
The final step is reaction of the salicylate with acid to form the desired salicylic acid.
</p>
