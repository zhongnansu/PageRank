<p><b>Fluor Corporation</b> is a multinational engineering and construction firm headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Irving,_Texas" title="Irving, Texas">Irving, Texas</a>. It is a holding company that provides services through its subsidiaries in the following areas: oil and gas, industrial and infrastructure, government and power. It is the largest engineering &amp; construction company in the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> rankings and is listed as 153rd overall.
</p><p>Fluor was founded in 1912 by John Simon Fluor as Fluor Construction Company. It grew quickly, predominantly by building oil refineries, pipelines and other facilities for the oil and gas industry, at first in California, and then in the Middle East and globally. In the late 1960s, it began diversifying into oil drilling, coal mining and other raw materials like lead. A global recession in the oil and gas industry and losses from its mining operation led to restructuring and layoffs in the 1980s. Fluor sold its oil operations and diversified its construction work into a broader range of services and industries.
</p><p>In the 1990s, Fluor introduced new services like equipment rentals and staffing. Nuclear waste cleanup projects and other environmental work became a significant portion of Fluor's revenues. The company also did projects related to the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">Manhattan Project</a>, rebuilding after the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a>, recovering from <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a> and building the <a href="/wiki/Trans-Alaska_Pipeline_System" title="Trans-Alaska Pipeline System">Trans-Alaska Pipeline System</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_history">Corporate history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fluor_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Corporate history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fluor_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fluor_Bros_Construction_headquarters_early_1900s.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="525" data-file-width="800" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Fluor_Bros_Construction_headquarters_early_1900s.jpg/220px-Fluor_Bros_Construction_headquarters_early_1900s.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Fluor_Bros_Construction_headquarters_early_1900s.jpg/330px-Fluor_Bros_Construction_headquarters_early_1900s.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Fluor_Bros_Construction_headquarters_early_1900s.jpg/440px-Fluor_Bros_Construction_headquarters_early_1900s.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fluor_Bros_Construction_headquarters_early_1900s.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Headquarters of Fluor Bros Construction in the early 1900s</div></div></div><p>Fluor Corporation's predecessor, <b>Rudolph Fluor &amp; Brother</b>, was founded in 1890 by John Simon Fluor<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onthology_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-onthology-2">[2]</a></sup> and his two brothers in Oshkosh, Wisconsin<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levy1993_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Levy1993-3">[3]</a></sup> as a saw and paper mill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onthology_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-onthology-2">[2]</a></sup> John Fluor acted as its president<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onthology_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-onthology-2">[2]</a></sup> and contributed $100 in personal savings to help the business get started.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-three_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-three-4">[4]</a></sup> The company was renamed Fluor Bros. Construction Co. in 1903.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onthology_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-onthology-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1912 John Fluor moved to Santa Ana, California for health reasons without his brothers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-two_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-two-5">[5]</a></sup> and founded Fluor Corporation out of his garage<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-one-6">[6]</a></sup> under the name <b>Fluor Construction Company</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-two_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-two-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-five_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-five-7">[7]</a></sup> By 1924 the business had annual revenues of $100,000 ($1.36 million in 2013 dollars)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onthology_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-onthology-2">[2]</a></sup> and a staff of 100 employees. John Fluor delegated most of the company's operations to his sons, Peter and Simon Fluor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pederson2000_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pederson2000-8">[8]</a></sup> A $100,000 capital investment was made that year and it was incorporated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onthology_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-onthology-2">[2]</a></sup> John's eldest son Peter served as head of sales and grew the company to $1.5 million ($20.4 million in 2013 dollars) in revenues by 1929.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pederson2000_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pederson2000-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1929 the company re-incorporated as Fluor Corporation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onthology_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-onthology-2">[2]</a></sup> By the 1930s, Fluor had operations in Europe, the Middle East and Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-three_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-three-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-four_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-four-9">[9]</a></sup> Business declined rapidly during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, but picked up again during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. During the war Fluor manufactured <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_rubber" title="Synthetic rubber">synthetic rubber</a> and was responsible for a substantial portion of high-octane gasoline production in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-three_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-three-4">[4]</a></sup> A Gas-Gasoline division of Fluor was created in Houston in 1948.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onthology_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-onthology-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fluor's headquarters were moved to <a href="/wiki/Alhambra,_California" title="Alhambra, California">Alhambra</a>, an inner suburb of Los Angeles, in 1940 in order to be closer to its oil and gas clients, before moving again to <a href="/wiki/Orange_County,_California" title="Orange County, California">Orange County</a>, California in the 1960s due to concerns about the cost of living and traffic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-one-6">[6]</a></sup> John Simon Fluor died in 1944. He was succeeded by his son Peter Fluor, who died three years later. Peter was followed by Shirley Meserve (1947) and Donald Darnell (1949), then John Simon "Si" Fluor Jr. in 1952 and J. Robert (Bob) Fluor in 1962.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onthology_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-onthology-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fluorbook_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-fluorbook-10">[10]</a></sup> Fluor was listed on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> in the 1950s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fortunearch_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-fortunearch-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1961 Fluor acquired an interest in construction, design and contracting firm William J. Moran.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diversification_and_restructuring">Diversification and restructuring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fluor_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Diversification and restructuring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fluor diversified its business more extensively in 1967, when five companies were merged into a division called Coral Drilling and it started a deep-water oil exploration business in Houston called Deep Oil Technology. It also created Fluor Ocean Services in Houston in 1968<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onthology_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-onthology-2">[2]</a></sup> and acquired an interest in other fossil fuel operations in the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fortunearch_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-fortunearch-11">[11]</a></sup> Fluor acquired a construction company, Pike Corp. of America<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onthology_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-onthology-2">[2]</a></sup> and the engineering division of its prior partner in Australia, Utah Construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fluorbook_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-fluorbook-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1972, Fluor bought land in <a href="/wiki/Irvine,_California" title="Irvine, California">Irvine, California</a> and started building its new headquarters on it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-one-6">[6]</a></sup> The following year, the company's oil and gas operations were consolidated under a new entity, Fluor Oil and Gas Corp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onthology_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-onthology-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1977, Fluor acquired Daniel International Corporation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henriques_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Henriques-14">[14]</a></sup> Fluor's business had become predominantly international, while Daniel International's $1 billion construction business was mostly domestic. The acquisition allowed the company to use union labor at Fluor, or non-union labor at Daniel, for each client.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fortunearch_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-fortunearch-11">[11]</a></sup> Fluor  made a $2.9 billion acquisition of a zinc, gold, lead and coal mining operation, St. Joe Minerals, in 1981<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cole_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cole-15">[15]</a></sup> after a bidding competition for the business with <a href="/wiki/Seagram" title="Seagram">Seagram</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Madrick2003_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Madrick2003-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>By the 1980s, Fluor's primary business was building large refineries, petrochemical plants, oil pipelines and other facilities for the gas and oil industry,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eighteen_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-eighteen-18">[18]</a></sup> especially in the Middle East.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eighteen_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-eighteen-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twentyone_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-twentyone-19">[19]</a></sup> By 1981, Fluor's staff had grown to 29,000 and revenue, backlog, and profits had each increased more than 30 percent over the prior year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HollieTimes_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-HollieTimes-20">[20]</a></sup> However, by 1984 the mining operation was causing heavy losses<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bwbw_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-bwbw-21">[21]</a></sup> and the oil and gas industry Fluor served was in a worldwide recession<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nine_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-nine-22">[22]</a></sup> due to declining oil prices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eighteen_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-eighteen-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-“bar_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-“bar-23">[23]</a></sup> From 1981 to 1984, Fluor's backlog went from $16 billion to $4 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eighteen_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-eighteen-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1985 it reported $633 million in losses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>  David Tappan took Bob Fluor's place as CEO in 1984 after Bob died from cancer<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eighteen_18-4"><a href="#cite_note-eighteen-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-six_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-six-26">[26]</a></sup> and led a difficult restructuring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seven_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-seven-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>The company sold $750 million in assets, including Fluor's headquarters in Irvine, in order to pay $1 billion in debt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-six_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-six-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seven_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-seven-27">[27]</a></sup> Staff were reduced from 32,000 to 14,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eighteen_18-5"><a href="#cite_note-eighteen-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1986 Fluor sold all of its oil assets and some of its gold mining operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onthology_2-12"><a href="#cite_note-onthology-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Times_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Times-28">[28]</a></sup> Fluor Engineers, Inc. and Daniel International were merged, forming Fluor Daniel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onthology_2-13"><a href="#cite_note-onthology-2">[2]</a></sup> By 1987, Fluor had returned to profitability with $26.6 million in profits<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eleven_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-eleven-29">[29]</a></sup> and $108.5 million by 1989.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-six_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-six-26">[26]</a></sup> By the end of the restructuring, Fluor had three major divisions: Fluor Daniel, Fluor Construction International and St. Joe Minerals Corp. Each division had its own smaller subsidiaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onthology_2-14"><a href="#cite_note-onthology-2">[2]</a></sup> Fluor started being named by <i>Engineering News</i> as the largest construction and engineering company in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eighteen_18-6"><a href="#cite_note-eighteen-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nine_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-nine-22">[22]</a></sup> Fluor's international revenues rebounded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fourteen_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-fourteen-30">[30]</a></sup> Having postponed his retirement to help Fluor, Tappan stepped down at the end of 1989 and was replaced by Leslie McCraw.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nine_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-nine-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cuff_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cuff-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_history">Recent history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fluor_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Recent history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the restructuring, Fluor's core construction and engineering work was diversified into 30 industries including food, paper manufacturers, prisons and others to reduce its vulnerability to market changes in the oil and gas market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eighteen_18-7"><a href="#cite_note-eighteen-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nine_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-nine-22">[22]</a></sup> In the 1990s, the company tried to change its image, calling itself a "diversified technical services" firm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eighteen_18-8"><a href="#cite_note-eighteen-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twenty_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-twenty-32">[32]</a></sup> It started offering equipment rentals, staffing services, and financing for construction projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eighteen_18-9"><a href="#cite_note-eighteen-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twenty_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-twenty-32">[32]</a></sup> The company began offering environmental cleanup and pollution control services, which grew to half of its new business by 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bwbw_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-bwbw-21">[21]</a></sup> Fluor's mining business grew from $300 million in 1990 to $1 billion in 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thirteen_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-thirteen-33">[33]</a></sup> The US government passed environmental regulations in 1995 that led to growth for the Massey Coal Co. business, because it had large reserves of low-sulfur coal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-respect_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-respect-34">[34]</a></sup> In 1992, Fluor sold its ownership of Doe Run Company, the world's largest producer of refined lead, which was losing money at the time due to declining lead prices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> By 1993, Fluor had revenues of $4.17 billion and 22,000 staff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onthology_2-15"><a href="#cite_note-onthology-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1997, Fluor's revenues fell almost 50 percent, in part due to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_financial_crisis" title="Asian financial crisis">Asian financial crisis</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twelve_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-twelve-36">[36]</a></sup> and a decrease in overseas business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fourteen_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-fourteen-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pollack_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pollack-37">[37]</a></sup> Additionally, it suffered losses from an over-budget power plant project<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> in Rabigh, Saudi Arabia. Fluor was a sub-contractor to <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a> for the project. Fluor's subsidiaries sued GE alleging that it misrepresented the complexity of the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Though revenues declined further the following year, profits were increasing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onthology_2-16"><a href="#cite_note-onthology-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1999, nearly 5,000 workers were laid off from Fluor Daniel and 15 offices were closed. Fluor Daniel was re-structured into four business groups: an engineering and construction firm called Fluor Daniel; an equipment rental, staffing and telecommunications division called Fluor Global Services; a coal-mining business called A.T. Massey Coal Co. and an administrative and support division called Fluor Signature Services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twelve_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-twelve-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>In January 1998 McCraw (age 63) resigned after being diagnosed with bladder cancer<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> and was replaced by former Shell President, Philip J. Carroll.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lkjl_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-lkjl-43">[43]</a></sup> That same year, IT Group purchased a 54 percent interest in Fluor Daniel GTI, Fluor's environmental division, for $36.3 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onthology_2-17"><a href="#cite_note-onthology-2">[2]</a></sup> Two years later, the coal mining operation under the  A.T. Massey Coal Co. name (part of St. Joe) was spun off into its own business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twelve_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-twelve-36">[36]</a></sup> In 2001, Fluor's four primary subsidiaries were consolidated into a single Fluor Corporation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fluorbook_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-fluorbook-10">[10]</a></sup> In 2002 Alan Boeckmann was appointed as the CEO, followed by David Seaton in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fluorbook_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-fluorbook-10">[10]</a></sup> In 2005, Fluor's headquarters were moved to the <a href="/wiki/Las_Colinas" title="Las Colinas">Las Colinas</a> area in <a href="/wiki/Irving,_Texas" title="Irving, Texas">Irving, Texas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-four_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-four-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>In December 2015 Fluor announced that it would take over Dutch industrial services company <a href="/wiki/Stork_B.V." title="Stork B.V.">Stork</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reuters_2015_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters_2015-44">[44]</a></sup> The acquisition of this company, which modifies and maintains large power plants, was completed in March 2016, in a stock purchase worth $755 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization">Organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fluor_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fluor was ranked 153rd in Fortune 500 companies for the year 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fortune_2017_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fortune_2017-46">[46]</a></sup> It has offices in 25 countries. Many of Fluor's operations are located near natural resources, such as uranium in Canada, oil reserves in the Middle East and mines in Australia. About 30 percent of Fluor's revenues are based in the United States as of 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swot_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-swot-47">[47]</a></sup> The Fluor family owns a 3.5 percent interest in the company, while employees own six percent through its profit-sharing program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fortunearch_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-fortunearch-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fluor's Board of Directors has 12 "Independent Directors" and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chief_Executive_Officer" title="Chief Executive Officer">CEO</a>. The Independent Directors elect a Lead Independent Director every three years that acts as a liaison to the CEO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boardind_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-boardind-48">[48]</a></sup> Each Independent Director is on two or more of four committees: audit, executive, governance, and organization &amp; compensation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Directors serve a three-year term and are elected by shareholders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boardind_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-boardind-48">[48]</a></sup> Corporate officers, such as the CEO, are elected each year by a vote of the board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fluor received an "A" ranking in <a href="/wiki/Transparency_International" title="Transparency International">Transparency International</a>'s 2012 anti-corruption study.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The company hosts online and in-person anti-corruption training sessions for staff and operates an ethics hotline. Former CEO Alan Boeckmann helped create the Partnering Against Corruption Initiative (PACI), whereby companies agree to a set of ethics principles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> A MarketLine SWOT analysis said Fluor's environmental work "enhances the company's brand image," while often lengthy and unpredictable legal disputes "tarnish the company's brand image and will erode customer confidence."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swot_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-swot-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to the <i>Los Angeles Times</i>, Fluor is a "major corporate citizen" that supports local charities and civic groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lkjl_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-lkjl-43">[43]</a></sup> It started the Fluor Foundation for its charitable work in 1952 and Fluor Cares in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fluorbook_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-fluorbook-10">[10]</a></sup> The company started the largest employer-sponsored apprenticeship program in California with a four-year program for designers in 1982.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Fluor operates a virtual college for employees called Fluor University.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Services">Services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fluor_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fluor_construction_site_Shuaiba,_Kuwait.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3600" data-file-width="5400" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Fluor_construction_site_Shuaiba%2C_Kuwait.jpg/220px-Fluor_construction_site_Shuaiba%2C_Kuwait.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Fluor_construction_site_Shuaiba%2C_Kuwait.jpg/330px-Fluor_construction_site_Shuaiba%2C_Kuwait.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Fluor_construction_site_Shuaiba%2C_Kuwait.jpg/440px-Fluor_construction_site_Shuaiba%2C_Kuwait.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fluor_construction_site_Shuaiba,_Kuwait.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Fluor construction site in Shuaiba, Kuwait</div></div></div><p>Fluor is a holding company that provides services through its subsidiaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swot_47-2"><a href="#cite_note-swot-47">[47]</a></sup> Its subsidiaries provide engineering, procurement, construction, maintenance and project management services. The company has also developed pollution control products, such as the Econamine lineup of carbon capture products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swot_47-3"><a href="#cite_note-swot-47">[47]</a></sup> According to Fluor's 2014 annual report, 67 percent of its work backlog is from the oil and gas industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-annual_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-annual-55">[55]</a></sup> According to the company's website, Fluor's work includes designing and building power plants, petrochemical factories, mining facilities, roads and bridges, government buildings, and manufacturing facilities. The company also performs nuclear cleanup and other services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>Separate teams of experts, procurement staff, project managers and workers are provided for large projects that are supported by a centralized administrative staff. Fluor has trained more than 100,000 craft workers in Indonesia, the Philippines, Korea, Pakistan, Kuwait and other countries, where the needed labor skills weren't available locally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fortunearch_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-fortunearch-11">[11]</a></sup> It may also serve clients through a joint venture with another construction firm when a local infrastructure or niche expertise is needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swot_47-4"><a href="#cite_note-swot-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fluor acquired shares of Genetech Inc. in 1981<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> and it bought a 10 percent interest in a smelter and refinery facility in Gresik, Indonesia in 1995 for $550 million. In 1994, it invested $650 million with the Beacon Group Energy Investment fund to finance energy projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-respect_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-respect-34">[34]</a></sup> Fluor also has a majority interest in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NuScale" title="NuScale">NuScale</a> LLC., which is developing a new type of 45-megawatt nuclear reactor called a small modular reactor (SMR).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_projects">Notable projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fluor_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Notable projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fluor_water_cooling_tower_(buddha_tower)_from_the_early_1900s.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="531" data-file-width="800" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Fluor_water_cooling_tower_%28buddha_tower%29_from_the_early_1900s.jpg/220px-Fluor_water_cooling_tower_%28buddha_tower%29_from_the_early_1900s.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Fluor_water_cooling_tower_%28buddha_tower%29_from_the_early_1900s.jpg/330px-Fluor_water_cooling_tower_%28buddha_tower%29_from_the_early_1900s.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Fluor_water_cooling_tower_%28buddha_tower%29_from_the_early_1900s.jpg/440px-Fluor_water_cooling_tower_%28buddha_tower%29_from_the_early_1900s.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fluor_water_cooling_tower_(buddha_tower)_from_the_early_1900s.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Fluor "Buddha Tower" water cooling tower from the early 1900s</div></div></div>
<p>Fluor's first projects were in constructing and grading roads, but by the 1920s it was known for building public facilities, industrial complexes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-five_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-five-7">[7]</a></sup> and serving a growing California oil and gas industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-three_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-three-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-one-6">[6]</a></sup> It started building office and meter manufacturing facilities for the <a href="/wiki/Southern_California_Gas_Company" title="Southern California Gas Company">Southern California Gas Company</a> in 1915, as well as a compressor station for the Industrial Fuel Supply Company in 1919.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pederson2000_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pederson2000-8">[8]</a></sup> Fluor built the first "Buddha Tower" in 1921<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-five_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-five-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pederson2000_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Pederson2000-8">[8]</a></sup> in Signal Hill, California, for the Industrial Fuel Supply Company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fluorbook_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-fluorbook-10">[10]</a></sup> The Buddha Tower was a design of water-cooling tower named after the Buddha temples they resemble.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-five_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-five-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pederson2000_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-Pederson2000-8">[8]</a></sup> The following year Fluor was awarded a contract by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richfield_Oil" title="Richfield Oil">Richfield Oil</a> to build a 10,000-gallon-per-day gasoline plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fortunearch_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-fortunearch-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Against his father's wishes, Peter Fluor expanded Fluor's business outside of California in the 1930s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pederson2000_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-Pederson2000-8">[8]</a></sup> It built refineries in Texas,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fortunearch_11-6"><a href="#cite_note-fortunearch-11">[11]</a></sup> as well as oil pipelines and compressor stations from Panhandle, Texas, to Indianapolis, Indiana, for the Panhandle Eastern Pipeline Company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pederson2000_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-Pederson2000-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fluorbook_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-fluorbook-10">[10]</a></sup> Fluor constructed the <a href="/wiki/Escondida" title="Escondida">Escondida</a> in Chile, which is the second-largest copper mine in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thirteen_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-thirteen-33">[33]</a></sup> In 1942, Fluor constructed cooling towers and other facilities in Hanford, Washington, for the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">Manhattan Project</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-five_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-five-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-four_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-four-9">[9]</a></sup> It built an expansion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dhahran_Airfield" title="Dhahran Airfield">Dhahran Airfield</a> in Saudi Arabia for the United States Army in the 1950s<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-five_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-five-7">[7]</a></sup> and accepted its first international project for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ARAMCO" title="ARAMCO">ARAMCO</a> in the Middle East.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fortunearch_11-7"><a href="#cite_note-fortunearch-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1960s and 1970s, Fluor built the first all-hydrogen refinery in Kuwait and the first exclusively offshore power plant for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_Richfield_Company" title="Atlantic Richfield Company">Atlantic Richfield Company</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-five_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-five-7">[7]</a></sup> It also constructed pumps and ports<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fortunearch_11-8"><a href="#cite_note-fortunearch-11">[11]</a></sup> for the <a href="/wiki/Trans-Alaska_Pipeline_System" title="Trans-Alaska Pipeline System">Trans-Alaska Pipeline System</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-five_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-five-7">[7]</a></sup> which traversed 800 miles from northern Alaska to <a href="/wiki/Valdez,_Alaska" title="Valdez, Alaska">Valdez, Alaska</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fluorbook_10-7"><a href="#cite_note-fluorbook-10">[10]</a></sup> and the world's largest offshore facility for natural gas on the island of <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a> in <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onthology_2-18"><a href="#cite_note-onthology-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1976, it was awarded a $5 billion project for ARAMCO in Saudi Arabia, to design facilities that capture <a href="/wiki/Sour_gas" title="Sour gas">sour gas</a>, which is expelled from oil wells as waste, in order to refine it into fuel. That same year a partially completed copper and cobalt mine in Africa was cancelled due to a war in the neighboring region of Angola and declining copper prices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fortunearch_11-9"><a href="#cite_note-fortunearch-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1979, Fluor had 13 projects for building United States power plants and had served more than half of the world's government-owned oil companies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fortunearch_11-10"><a href="#cite_note-fortunearch-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Radiation_cleanup.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="900" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Radiation_cleanup.jpg/220px-Radiation_cleanup.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Radiation_cleanup.jpg/330px-Radiation_cleanup.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Radiation_cleanup.jpg/440px-Radiation_cleanup.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Radiation_cleanup.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fluor workers at a radiation cleanup site in Ohio</div></div></div>
<p>Fluor has been working on the cleanup and shutdown of atomic energy plants in Ohio and Washington since the 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-one-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1992, Fluor won a contract with the United States Energy Department to clean up nuclear waste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bwbw_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-bwbw-21">[21]</a></sup> By 1996 Hanford was the most contaminated nuclear site in the US and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Department_of_Energy" title="US Department of Energy">US Department of Energy</a> was conducting a $50 billion to $60 billion cleanup of the site. Fluor Hanford Inc. replaced Westinghouse Hanford Co. on the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dhiun_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-dhiun-60">[60]</a></sup> After a chemical explosion in 1997, 11 workers filed a lawsuit alleging they were denied appropriate medical attention and protective gear. Fluor and the workers disagreed on whether the explosion resulted in any injuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> In 2005 the US Department of Energy fined Fluor for safety violations<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dhiun_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-dhiun-60">[60]</a></sup> and that same year a jury awarded $4.7 million in damages to 11 pipe fitters that claimed they were fired after complaining that a valve rated for 1,975 pounds-per-inch was being used where 2,235 were needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fluor built the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aladdin_Hotel_%26_Casino" title="Aladdin Hotel &amp; Casino">Aladdin Hotel &amp; Casino</a> in <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas" title="Las Vegas">Las Vegas</a> in 2001 for $1.4 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-one-6">[6]</a></sup> In 2004, the company was awarded a $1.1 billion project with AMEC to help rebuild the water, power and civic infrastructure of Iraq after the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-four_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-four-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bwbw_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-bwbw-21">[21]</a></sup> Fluor has also built a rail line in Europe and missile sites in Arizona. The company provided disaster recovery services in Louisiana after <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-two_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-two-5">[5]</a></sup> In 2010 Fluor provided workers to clean up oil tar on beaches in Florida and Alabama after the <a href="/wiki/Deepwater_Horizon_oil_spill" title="Deepwater Horizon oil spill"><i>Deepwater Horizon</i> oil spill</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> In December 2012, Fluor was awarded a $3.14 billion contract to build a new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Tappan_Zee_Bridge" title="New Tappan Zee Bridge">Tappan Zee Bridge</a> over the Hudson River.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inc._AB._2012_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-Inc._AB._2012-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p>
