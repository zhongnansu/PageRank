<p>The <b>knot</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is a unit of <a href="/wiki/Speed" title="Speed">speed</a> equal to one <a href="/wiki/Nautical_mile" title="Nautical mile">nautical mile</a> per hour, exactly <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6999514444444444444♠</span>1.852 km/h</span> (approximately <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6999514444691199999♠</span>1.150<span style="margin-left:.25em;">78</span> mph</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/ISO_80000-3" title="ISO 80000-3">ISO standard</a> symbol for the knot is <em>kn</em>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The same symbol is preferred by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IEEE" title="IEEE">Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IEEE" title="IEEE">IEEE</a>); <em>kt</em> is also common. The knot is a non-<a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">SI</a> unit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nonSi_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-nonSi-3">[3]</a></sup> Worldwide, the knot is used in <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, and in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shipping" title="Shipping">maritime</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aviation" title="Aviation">air</a> navigation—for example, a vessel travelling at 1 knot along a <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(geography)" title="Meridian (geography)">meridian</a> travels approximately one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minute_of_arc" title="Minute of arc">minute</a> of geographic <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> in one hour.
</p><p>Etymologically, the term derives from counting the number of <a href="/wiki/Knot" title="Knot">knots</a> in the line that unspooled from the reel of a <a href="/wiki/Chip_log" title="Chip log">chip log</a> in a specific time.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knot_(unit)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl><dt>1 international knot =</dt>
<dd>1 <a href="/wiki/Nautical_mile" title="Nautical mile">nautical mile</a> per <a href="/wiki/Hour" title="Hour">hour</a> (by definition),</dd>
<dd>1.852 kilometres per hour (exactly),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nonSi_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-nonSi-3">[3]</a></sup></dd>
<dd>0.51444 metres per second (approximately),</dd>
<dd>1.15078 miles per hour (approximately),</dd>
<dd>20.25372 inches per second (approximately)</dd>
<dd>1.68781 feet per second (approximately).</dd></dl>
<p><span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003185200000000000♠</span>1852 m</span> is the length of the internationally agreed nautical mile. The US adopted the international definition in 1954, having previously used the US nautical mile (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003185324800000000♠</span>1<span style="margin-left:.25em;">853</span>.248 m</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usnm_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-usnm-4">[4]</a></sup> The UK adopted the international nautical mile definition in 1970, having previously used the UK <a href="/wiki/Admiralty" title="Admiralty">Admiralty</a> nautical mile (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003185318400000000♠</span>6080 ft</span> or <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003185318400000000♠</span>1<span style="margin-left:.25em;">853</span>.184 m</span>).
</p>

</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knot_(unit)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The speeds of vessels relative to the <a href="/wiki/Fluid" title="Fluid">fluids</a> in which they travel (boat speeds and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_speed" title="Air speed">air speeds</a>) are measured in knots. For consistency, the speeds of navigational fluids (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_science" title="Marine science">tidal streams</a>, river currents and <a href="/wiki/Wind_speed" title="Wind speed">wind speeds</a>) are also measured in knots. Thus, speed over the ground (SOG) (<a href="/wiki/Ground_speed" title="Ground speed">ground speed</a> (GS) in aircraft) and rate of progress towards a distant point ("<a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a> made good", VMG) are also given in knots.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knot_(unit)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Until the mid-19th century, vessel speed at sea was measured using a <a href="/wiki/Chip_log" title="Chip log">chip log</a>. This consisted of a wooden panel, attached by line to a reel, and weighted on one edge to float perpendicularly to the water surface and thus present substantial resistance to the water moving around it. The chip log was cast over the stern of the moving vessel and the line allowed to pay out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abbott1858_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Abbott1858-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Knot" title="Knot">Knots</a> placed at a distance of 47 <a href="/wiki/Foot_(unit)" title="Foot (unit)">feet</a> 3 <a href="/wiki/Inch" title="Inch">inches</a> (14.4018 m) from each other, passed through a sailor's fingers, while another sailor used a 30-second <a href="/wiki/Hourglass" title="Hourglass">sand-glass</a> (28-second sand-glass is the currently accepted timing) to time the operation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The knot count would be reported and used in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sailing_master" title="Sailing master">sailing master</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Dead_reckoning" title="Dead reckoning">dead reckoning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Navigation" title="Navigation">navigation</a>. This method gives a value for the knot of 20.25 in/s, or 1.85166 km/h. The difference from the modern definition is less than 0.02%.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_use">Modern use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knot_(unit)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Modern use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:World_Scale_from_DMA_Series_1150_map.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="890" data-file-width="3301" height="59" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/World_Scale_from_DMA_Series_1150_map.png/220px-World_Scale_from_DMA_Series_1150_map.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/World_Scale_from_DMA_Series_1150_map.png/330px-World_Scale_from_DMA_Series_1150_map.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/World_Scale_from_DMA_Series_1150_map.png/440px-World_Scale_from_DMA_Series_1150_map.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:World_Scale_from_DMA_Series_1150_map.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Graphic scale from a Mercator projection world map, showing the change with latitude</div></div></div>
<p>Although the unit <i>knot</i> does not fit within the SI system, its retention for nautical and aviation use is important because the length of a <a href="/wiki/Nautical_mile" title="Nautical mile">nautical mile</a>, upon which the knot is based, is closely related to the size of the Earth. As a result, nautical miles and knots are convenient units to use when navigating an aircraft or ship.
</p><p>Standard nautical charts are on the <a href="/wiki/Mercator_projection" title="Mercator projection">Mercator projection</a> and the horizontal (East-West) scale varies with latitude. On a chart of the North Atlantic, the scale varies by a factor of two from Florida to Greenland. A single <a href="/wiki/Linear_scale" title="Linear scale">graphic scale</a>, of the sort on many maps, would therefore be useless on such a chart. Since the length of a nautical mile, for practical purposes, is equivalent to about a minute of latitude, a distance in nautical miles on a chart can easily be measured by using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caliper#Divider_caliper" title="Caliper">dividers</a> and the latitude scales on the sides of the chart. Recent British <a href="/wiki/Admiralty_chart" title="Admiralty chart">Admiralty charts</a> have a latitude scale down the middle to make this even easier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Speed is sometimes incorrectly expressed as "knots per hour",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kipling_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kipling-8">[8]</a></sup> which is in fact a measure of acceleration.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aeronautical_terms">Aeronautical terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Knot_(unit)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Aeronautical terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Prior to 1969, airworthiness standards for civil aircraft in the United States <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Regulations" title="Federal Aviation Regulations">Federal Aviation Regulations</a> specified that distances were to be in statute miles, and speeds in miles per hour. In 1969, these standards were progressively amended to specify that distances were to be in nautical miles, and speeds in knots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The following abbreviations are used to distinguish between various measurements of <a href="/wiki/Airspeed" title="Airspeed">airspeed</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>KTAS is "knots <a href="/wiki/True_airspeed" title="True airspeed">true airspeed</a>", the airspeed of an aircraft relative to undisturbed air</li>
<li>KIAS is "knots <a href="/wiki/Indicated_airspeed" title="Indicated airspeed">indicated airspeed</a>", the speed shown on an aircraft's <a href="/wiki/Pitot-static_system" title="Pitot-static system">pitot-static</a> <a href="/wiki/Airspeed_indicator" title="Airspeed indicator">airspeed indicator</a></li>
<li>KCAS is "knots <a href="/wiki/Calibrated_airspeed" title="Calibrated airspeed">calibrated airspeed</a>", the indicated airspeed corrected for <a href="/wiki/Position_error" title="Position error">position error</a> and instrument error</li>
<li>KEAS is "knots <a href="/wiki/Equivalent_airspeed" title="Equivalent airspeed">equivalent airspeed</a>", the calibrated airspeed corrected for <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_process" title="Adiabatic process">adiabatic</a> <a href="/wiki/Compressible_flow" title="Compressible flow">compressible flow</a> for the particular altitude</li></ul>
<p>The indicated airspeed is close to the true airspeed only at sea level in standard conditions and at low speeds. At <span style="white-space: nowrap">11<span style="margin-left: 0.25em">000</span></span> m (<span style="white-space: nowrap">36<span style="margin-left: 0.25em">000</span></span> ft), an indicated airspeed of 300 kn may correspond to a true airspeed of 500 kn in standard conditions.
</p>
