<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Oxide</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><dl><dd><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rutile-unit-cell-3D-balls.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="784" data-file-width="1100" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Rutile-unit-cell-3D-balls.png/220px-Rutile-unit-cell-3D-balls.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Rutile-unit-cell-3D-balls.png/330px-Rutile-unit-cell-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Rutile-unit-cell-3D-balls.png/440px-Rutile-unit-cell-3D-balls.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rutile-unit-cell-3D-balls.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Crystal_structure#Unit_cell" title="Crystal structure">unit cell</a> of rutile. Ti(IV) centers are grey; oxide centers are red. Notice that oxide forms three bonds to titanium and titanium forms six bonds to oxide.</div></div></div></dd></dl>
<p>An <b>oxide</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/aɪ/: 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span></span>/</a></span></span> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> that contains at least one <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> atom and one other element<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> in its <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formula</a>. "Oxide" itself is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dianion" title="Dianion">dianion</a> of oxygen, an O<sup>2–</sup> atom. <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">Metal</a> oxides thus typically contain an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">anion</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a> of −2. Most of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_crust" title="Earth's crust">Earth's crust</a> consists of solid oxides, the result of elements being oxidized by the oxygen in air or in water. <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">Hydrocarbon</a> combustion affords the two principal <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> oxides: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>.  Even materials considered pure elements often develop an oxide coating.  For example, <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_foil" title="Aluminium foil">aluminium foil</a> develops a thin skin of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alumina" title="Alumina">Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></a> (called a <a href="/wiki/Passivation_(chemistry)" title="Passivation (chemistry)">passivation layer</a>) that protects the foil from further <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-2">[2]</a></sup> Individual elements can often form multiple oxides, each containing different amounts of the element and oxygen.  In some cases these are distinguished by specifying the number of atoms as in carbon monoxide and carbon dioxide, and in other cases by specifying the element's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation_number" title="Oxidation number">oxidation number</a>, as in <a href="/wiki/Iron(II)_oxide" title="Iron(II) oxide">iron(II) oxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iron(III)_oxide" title="Iron(III) oxide">iron(III) oxide</a>.  Certain elements can form many different oxides, such as <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">those of nitrogen</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a></div>
<p>Due to its <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">electronegativity</a>, oxygen forms stable <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">chemical bonds</a> with almost all elements to give the corresponding oxides. <a href="/wiki/Noble_metal" title="Noble metal">Noble metals</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> or <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a>) are prized because they resist direct chemical combination with oxygen, and substances like <a href="/wiki/Gold(III)_oxide" title="Gold(III) oxide">gold(III) oxide</a> must be generated by indirect routes.
</p><p>Two independent pathways for corrosion of elements are hydrolysis and oxidation by oxygen.  The combination of water and oxygen is even more corrosive. Virtually all elements burn in an atmosphere of oxygen, or an oxygen rich environment.  In the presence of water and oxygen (or simply air), some elements— <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>—react rapidly, to give the hydroxides.  In part for this reason, alkali and alkaline earth metals are not found in nature in their metallic, i.e., native, form.  Caesium is so reactive with oxygen that it is used as a <a href="/wiki/Getter" title="Getter">getter</a> in <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_tube" title="Vacuum tube">vacuum tubes</a>, and solutions of potassium and sodium, so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NaK" title="NaK">NaK</a> are used to deoxygenate and dehydrate some organic solvents. The surface of most metals consists of oxides and hydroxides in the presence of air.  A well-known example is <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_foil" title="Aluminium foil">aluminium foil</a>, which is coated with a thin film of <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_oxide" title="Aluminium oxide">aluminium oxide</a> that <a href="/wiki/Passivation_(chemistry)" title="Passivation (chemistry)">passivates</a> the metal, slowing further <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a>. The aluminium oxide layer can be built to greater thickness by the process of <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolytic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anodising" title="Anodising">anodising</a>. Though solid magnesium and aluminium react slowly with oxygen at <a href="/wiki/Standard_conditions_for_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard conditions for temperature and pressure">STP</a>—they, like most metals, burn in air, generating very high temperatures.  Finely grained powders of most metals can be dangerously explosive in air.  Consequently, they are often used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid-fuel_rocket" title="Solid-fuel rocket">solid-fuel rockets</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rust_screw.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Rust_screw.jpg/220px-Rust_screw.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Rust_screw.jpg/330px-Rust_screw.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Rust_screw.jpg/440px-Rust_screw.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rust_screw.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Oxides, such as <a href="/wiki/Iron(III)_oxide" title="Iron(III) oxide">iron(III) oxide</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rust" title="Rust">rust</a>, which consists of hydrated <a href="/wiki/Iron(III)_oxide" title="Iron(III) oxide">iron(III) oxides</a> Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>·<i>n</i>H<sub>2</sub>O and <a href="/wiki/Iron(III)_oxide-hydroxide" title="Iron(III) oxide-hydroxide">iron(III) oxide-hydroxide</a> (FeO(OH), Fe(OH)<sub>3</sub>), form when oxygen combines with other elements</div></div></div>
<p>In dry oxygen, <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> readily forms <a href="/wiki/Iron(II)_oxide" title="Iron(II) oxide">iron(II) oxide</a>, but the formation of the hydrated ferric oxides, Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3−<i>x</i></sub>(OH)<sub>2<i>x</i></sub>, that mainly comprise rust, typically requires oxygen <i>and</i> water.  Free oxygen production by <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> some 3.5 billion years ago <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitate" title="Precipitate">precipitated</a> iron out of solution in the oceans as Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> in the economically important <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Banded_iron_formations" title="Banded iron formations">iron ore</a> <a href="/wiki/Hematite" title="Hematite">hematite</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Oxides have a range of different structures, from individual molecules to <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymeric</a> and crystalline structures.  At standard conditions, oxides may range from solids to gases.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxides_of_metals">Oxides of metals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Oxides of metals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Oxides of most metals adopt <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymeric</a> structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>  The oxide typically links three metal atoms (e.g., rutile structure) or six metal atoms (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carborundum" title="Carborundum">carborundum</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rock_salt" title="Rock salt">rock salt</a> structures). Because the M-O bonds are typically strong and these compounds are crosslinked <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymers</a>, the solids tend to be insoluble in solvents, though they are attacked by acids and bases. The formulas are often deceptively simple.  Many are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonstoichiometric_compound" title="Nonstoichiometric compound">nonstoichiometric compounds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Although most metal oxides are <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymeric</a>, some oxides are molecules.  Examples of molecular oxides are <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>. All simple oxides of nitrogen are molecular, e.g., NO, N<sub>2</sub>O, NO<sub>2</sub> and N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>. <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus_pentoxide" title="Phosphorus pentoxide">Phosphorus pentoxide</a> is a more complex molecular oxide with a deceptive name, the real formula being P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>10</sub>.  Some <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymeric</a> oxides <a href="/wiki/Cracking_(chemistry)" title="Cracking (chemistry)">depolymerize</a> when heated to give molecules, examples being <a href="/wiki/Selenium_dioxide" title="Selenium dioxide">selenium dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_trioxide" title="Sulfur trioxide">sulfur trioxide</a>.  Tetroxides are rare.  The more common examples: <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium_tetroxide" title="Ruthenium tetroxide">ruthenium tetroxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Osmium_tetroxide" title="Osmium tetroxide">osmium tetroxide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Xenon_tetroxide" title="Xenon tetroxide">xenon tetroxide</a>.
</p><p>Many oxyanions are known, such as <a href="/wiki/Polyphosphate" title="Polyphosphate">polyphosphates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polyoxometalate" title="Polyoxometalate">polyoxometalates</a>.  Oxycations are rarer, some examples being <a href="/wiki/Nitrosonium" title="Nitrosonium">nitrosonium</a> (NO<sup>+</sup>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vanadyl" title="Vanadyl">vanadyl</a> (VO<sup>2+</sup>), and <a href="/wiki/Uranyl" title="Uranyl">uranyl</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">UO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>).  Of course many compounds are known with both oxides and other groups.  In <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a>, these include <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">ketones</a> and many related <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonyl" title="Carbonyl">carbonyl</a> compounds.  For the transition metals, many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxo_ligand" title="Oxo ligand">oxo complexes</a> are known as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxyhalide" title="Oxyhalide">oxyhalides</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reduction">Reduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Conversion of a metal oxide to the metal is called reduction. Reduction can be induced with many reagents.  Many metal oxides convert to metals simply by heating.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reduction_by_carbon">Reduction by carbon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reduction by carbon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbothermic_reduction" title="Carbothermic reduction">carbothermic reduction</a></div>
<p>Metals are "won" from their oxides by chemical reduction, i.e. by the addition of a chemical reagent.  A common and cheap reducing agent is carbon in the form of <a href="/wiki/Coke_(fuel)" title="Coke (fuel)">coke</a>.  The most prominent example is that of <a href="/wiki/Iron#Industrial_production" title="Iron">iron ore smelting</a>. Many reactions are involved, but the simplified equation is usually shown as:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 3 C → 4 Fe + 3 CO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Metal oxides can be reduced by organic compounds.  This redox process is the basis for many important transformations in chemistry, such as the detoxification of drugs by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P450" title="P450">P450</a> enzymes and the production of <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_oxide" title="Ethylene oxide">ethylene oxide</a>, which is converted to antifreeze.  In such systems the metal centre transfers an oxide ligand to the organic compound followed by regeneration of the metal oxide, often by oxygen in air.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reduction_by_heating">Reduction by heating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reduction by heating">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Thermal_decomposition" title="Thermal decomposition">thermal decomposition</a></div>
<p>Metals that are lower in the <a href="/wiki/Reactivity_series" title="Reactivity series">reactivity series</a> can be reduced by heating alone. For example, <a href="/wiki/Silver_oxide" title="Silver oxide">silver oxide</a> decomposes at 200 °C:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Ag<sub>2</sub>O → 4 Ag + O<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reduction_by_displacement">Reduction by displacement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reduction by displacement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Single_displacement_reaction" title="Single displacement reaction">single displacement reaction</a></div>
<p>Metals that are more <a href="/wiki/Reactivity_series" title="Reactivity series">reactive</a> displace the oxide of the metals that are less reactive. For example, <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> is more reactive than <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, so it displaces <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copper_(II)_oxide" title="Copper (II) oxide">copper (II) oxide</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Zinc_oxide" title="Zinc oxide">zinc oxide</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>Zn + CuO → ZnO + Cu</dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reduction_by_hydrogen">Reduction by hydrogen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Reduction by hydrogen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Single_displacement_reaction" title="Single displacement reaction">single displacement reaction</a></div>
<p>Apart from metals, hydrogen can also displace metal oxides to form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_oxide" title="Hydrogen oxide">hydrogen oxide</a>, also known as water:
</p>
<dl><dd>H<sub>2</sub> + CuO → Cu + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reduction_by_electrolysis">Reduction by electrolysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Reduction by electrolysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a></div>
<p>Since metals that are reactive form oxides that are stable, some metal oxides must be electrolyzed to be reduced. This includes <a href="/wiki/Sodium_oxide" title="Sodium oxide">sodium oxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium_oxide" title="Potassium oxide">potassium oxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">calcium oxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_oxide" title="Magnesium oxide">magnesium oxide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_oxide" title="Aluminium oxide">aluminium oxide</a>. The oxides must be molten before immersing graphite electrodes in them:
</p>
<dl><dd>2Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> → 4Al + 3O<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrolysis_and_dissolution">Hydrolysis and dissolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Hydrolysis and dissolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Oxides typically react with <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a> or <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">bases</a>, sometimes both. Those reacting only with acids are labeled basic oxides.  Those reacting only by bases are called "acidic oxides". Oxides that react with both are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoteric" title="Amphoteric">amphoteric</a>. Metals tend to form basic oxides, non-metals tend to form acidic oxides, and amphoteric oxides are formed by elements near the boundary between metals and non-metals (<a href="/wiki/Metalloid" title="Metalloid">metalloids</a>).  This reactivity is the basis of many practical processes, such as the extraction of some metals from their ores in the process called <a href="/wiki/Hydrometallurgy" title="Hydrometallurgy">hydrometallurgy</a>.
</p><p>Oxides of more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electropositive" title="Electropositive">electropositive</a> elements tend to be basic. They are called <i>basic anhydrides</i>. Exposed to water, they may form basic <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxides</a>. For example, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_oxide" title="Sodium oxide">sodium oxide</a> is basic—when hydrated, it forms <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a>. Oxides of more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronegative" title="Electronegative">electronegative</a> elements tend to be acidic. They are called "acid anhydrides"; adding water, they form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxoacid" title="Oxoacid">oxoacids</a>. For example, <a href="/wiki/Dichlorine_heptoxide" title="Dichlorine heptoxide">dichlorine heptoxide</a> is an acid anhydride; <a href="/wiki/Perchloric_acid" title="Perchloric acid">perchloric acid</a> is its fully hydrated form. Some oxides can act as both acid and base. They are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoteric" title="Amphoteric">amphoteric</a>. An example is <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_oxide" title="Aluminium oxide">aluminium oxide</a>. Some oxides do not show behavior as either acid or base.
</p><p>The oxide <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ion</a> has the formula O<sup>2−</sup>. It is the <a href="/wiki/Conjugate_acid" title="Conjugate acid">conjugate acid</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxide</a> ion, OH<sup>−</sup>, and is encountered in <a href="/wiki/Ionic_compound" title="Ionic compound">ionic</a> solids such as <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">calcium oxide</a>.  O<sup>2−</sup> is unstable in <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">aqueous</a> <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solution</a> − its affinity for H<sup>+</sup> is so great (p<i>K</i><sub>b</sub> ~ −38) that it abstracts a <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a> from a solvent H<sub>2</sub>O molecule:
</p>
<dl><dd>O<sup>2−</sup>  +  H<sub>2</sub>O  →  2 OH<sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<p>The equilibrium constant of aforesaid reactions is pK<sub>eq</sub> ~ −22
</p><p>In the 18th century, oxides were named <b>calxes</b> or <b>calces</b> after the <a href="/wiki/Calcination" title="Calcination">calcination</a> process used to produce oxides.  <i>Calx</i> was later replaced by <i>oxyd.</i>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reductive_dissolution">Reductive dissolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Reductive dissolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <b>reductive dissolution</b> of a <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metal</a> oxide occurs when dissolution is coupled to a <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">redox</a> event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CornellSchwertmann2003-306_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-CornellSchwertmann2003-306-5">[5]</a></sup> For example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferric_Oxide" title="Ferric Oxide">ferric oxides</a> dissolve in the presence of reductants, which can include organic compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SulzbergerSuter1989_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-SulzbergerSuter1989-6">[6]</a></sup> or bacteria<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roden2008_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roden2008-7">[7]</a></sup> Reductive dissolution is integral to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geochemical" title="Geochemical">geochemical</a> phenomena such as the <a href="/wiki/Iron_cycle" title="Iron cycle">iron cycle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CornellSchwertmann2003-323_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-CornellSchwertmann2003-323-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Reductive dissolution does not necessarily occur at the site where the reductant adsorbs. Instead, the added electron travel through the particle, causing reductive dissolution elsewhere on the particle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YaninaRosso2008_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-YaninaRosso2008-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChatmanZarzycki2015_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ChatmanZarzycki2015-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature_and_formulas">Nomenclature and formulas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Nomenclature and formulas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sometimes, metal-oxygen ratios are used to name oxides. Thus, NbO would be called niobium monoxide and TiO<sub>2</sub> is titanium dioxide.  This naming follows the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Numerical_prefix" title="Numerical prefix">numerical prefixes</a>.  In the older literature and continuing in industry, oxides are named by adding the suffix <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-a#Etymology_4" title="wikt:-a">-a</a></i> to the element's name.  Hence alumina, magnesia and chromia, are, respectively, Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, MgO and Cr<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>.
</p><p>Special types of oxides are <a href="/wiki/Peroxide" title="Peroxide">peroxide</a>, O<sub>2</sub><sup>2−</sup>, and <a href="/wiki/Superoxide" title="Superoxide">superoxide</a>, O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup>.  In such species, oxygen is assigned higher <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation states</a> than oxide. 
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formulas</a> of the oxides of the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical elements</a> in their highest <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a> are predictable and are derived from the number of <a href="/wiki/Valence_electron" title="Valence electron">valence electrons</a> for that element. Even the chemical formula of O<sub>4</sub>, <a href="/wiki/Tetraoxygen" title="Tetraoxygen">tetraoxygen</a>, is predictable as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Group_16_element" title="Group 16 element">group 16 element</a>. One exception is <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, for which the highest oxidation state oxide is <a href="/wiki/Copper(II)_oxide" title="Copper(II) oxide">copper(II) oxide</a> and not <a href="/wiki/Copper(I)_oxide" title="Copper(I) oxide">copper(I) oxide</a>. Another exception is <a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">fluoride</a>, which does not exist as one might expect—as F<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>—but as <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_difluoride" title="Oxygen difluoride">OF<sub>2</sub></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since fluorine is more <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">electronegative</a> than oxygen, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_difluoride" title="Oxygen difluoride">oxygen difluoride</a> (OF<sub>2</sub>) does not represent an oxide of fluorine, but instead represents a <a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">fluoride</a> of oxygen.
</p>
