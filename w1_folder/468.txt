<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Cave of Dogs</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lago_di_Agnano_e_Grotta_del_Cane_-_acquaforte_aquerellata_Sieur_de_Rogissart.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="520" data-file-width="958" height="119" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Lago_di_Agnano_e_Grotta_del_Cane_-_acquaforte_aquerellata_Sieur_de_Rogissart.jpg/220px-Lago_di_Agnano_e_Grotta_del_Cane_-_acquaforte_aquerellata_Sieur_de_Rogissart.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Lago_di_Agnano_e_Grotta_del_Cane_-_acquaforte_aquerellata_Sieur_de_Rogissart.jpg/330px-Lago_di_Agnano_e_Grotta_del_Cane_-_acquaforte_aquerellata_Sieur_de_Rogissart.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Lago_di_Agnano_e_Grotta_del_Cane_-_acquaforte_aquerellata_Sieur_de_Rogissart.jpg/440px-Lago_di_Agnano_e_Grotta_del_Cane_-_acquaforte_aquerellata_Sieur_de_Rogissart.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lago_di_Agnano_e_Grotta_del_Cane_-_acquaforte_aquerellata_Sieur_de_Rogissart.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><b>Lake Agnano and the Cave of Dogs in their heyday.</b> Etching by the Sieur de Rogissart, 1706.</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Cave of Dogs</b> (in Italian <i>Grotta del Cane</i>, literally "Cave of the Dog") is a small <a href="/wiki/Cave" title="Cave">cave</a> on the eastern side of the <a href="/wiki/Phlegraean_Fields" title="Phlegraean Fields">Phlegraean Fields</a> near <a href="/wiki/Pozzuoli" title="Pozzuoli">Pozzuoli</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Inside the cave is a <a href="/wiki/Fumarole" title="Fumarole">fumarole</a> that releases <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic" title="Volcanic">volcanic</a> origin.  It was a famous, if gruesome, <a href="/wiki/Tourist_attraction" title="Tourist attraction">tourist attraction</a> for travellers on the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Tour" title="Grand Tour">Grand Tour</a>.  The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CO2" title="CO2">CO<sub>2</sub></a> gas, being denser than air, tends to accumulate in the deeper parts of the cave.  Local guides, for a fee, would suspend small animals inside it—usually dogs—until they became unconscious.  Because humans inhaled air from a higher level they were not affected.  The dogs might be revived by submerging them in the cold waters of the nearby <a href="/wiki/Lake_Agnano" title="Lake Agnano">Lake Agnano</a>.  Famous tourists who came to see this attraction included <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goethe" title="Goethe">Goethe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexandre_Dumas" title="Alexandre Dumas">Alexandre Dumas</a> père, and <a href="/wiki/Mark_Twain" title="Mark Twain">Mark Twain</a>. The lake became polluted and it was drained in 1870; the spectacle fell into disuse and the cave was closed.  However the area is now being restored by volunteers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:From_Taylor,_1832.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="476" data-file-width="750" height="140" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/From_Taylor%2C_1832.png/220px-From_Taylor%2C_1832.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/From_Taylor%2C_1832.png/330px-From_Taylor%2C_1832.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/From_Taylor%2C_1832.png/440px-From_Taylor%2C_1832.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:From_Taylor,_1832.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><b>The principle of the Cave of Dogs</b> sketched by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Swaine_Taylor" title="Alfred Swaine Taylor">Alfred Swaine Taylor</a>, 1832</div></div></div>
<p>The cave was often described in nineteenth century science textbooks to illustrate the <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> and toxicity of carbon dioxide,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and its reputation has given rise to a popular <a href="/wiki/Scientific_demonstration" title="Scientific demonstration">scientific demonstration</a> of the same name.  Stepped candles are successively extinguished by tipping carbon dioxide into a transparent container.
</p><p>The cave was recently investigated by Italian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Speleologists" title="Speleologists">speleologists</a> including Rosario Varriale who interpreted it as a man-made cavity constructed in <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">antiquity</a>, possibly as a <a href="/wiki/Sudatorium" title="Sudatorium">sudatorium</a>. The carbon dioxide level was measured at 9.9%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>  According to the Australian speleologist Garry K. Smith, a concentration of 5-10% produces in humans “Violent panting and fatigue to the point of exhaustion merely from respiration &amp; severe headache. Prolonged exposure at 5% could result in irreversible effects to health. Prolonged exposure at &gt; 6% could result in unconsciousness and death.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
