<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Donna Haraway</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Donna J. Haraway</b> (born September 6, 1944) is a Distinguished  American Professor <a href="/wiki/Emeritus" title="Emeritus">Emerita</a> in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_Consciousness" title="History of Consciousness">History of Consciousness</a> Department and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feminist_studies" title="Feminist studies">Feminist Studies</a> Department at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Santa_Cruz" title="University of California, Santa Cruz">University of California, Santa Cruz</a>, United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> She is a prominent scholar in the field of <a href="/wiki/Science_and_technology_studies" title="Science and technology studies">science and technology studies</a>, described in the early 1990s as a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feminist" title="Feminist">feminist</a>, rather loosely a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postmodernist" title="Postmodernist">postmodernist</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Haraway is the author of numerous foundational books and essays that bring together questions of science and feminism, such as "<a href="/wiki/A_Cyborg_Manifesto" title="A Cyborg Manifesto">A Cyborg Manifesto</a>: Science, Technology, and Socialist-Feminism in the Late Twentieth Century" (1985) and "Situated Knowledges: The Science Question in Feminism and the Privilege of Partial Perspective" (1988).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cyborg_Manifesto_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cyborg_Manifesto-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Situated_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Situated-4">[4]</a></sup> She is also a leading scholar in contemporary <a href="/wiki/Ecofeminism" title="Ecofeminism">ecofeminism</a>, associated with <a href="/wiki/Posthumanism" title="Posthumanism">post-humanism</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_materialism" title="New materialism">new materialism</a> movements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Her work criticizes <a href="/wiki/Anthropocentrism" title="Anthropocentrism">anthropocentrism</a>, emphasizes the self-organizing powers of nonhuman processes, and explores dissonant relations between those processes and cultural practices, rethinking sources of ethics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Haraway has taught <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Women%27s_Studies" title="Women's Studies">Women's Studies</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Science" title="History of Science">History of Science</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Hawaii" title="University of Hawaii">University of Hawaii</a> and <a href="/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_University" title="Johns Hopkins University">Johns Hopkins University</a>. Haraway's works have contributed to the study of both human-machine and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal_Studies" title="Animal Studies">human-animal</a> relations. Her works have sparked debate in <a href="/wiki/Primatology" title="Primatology">primatology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Developmental_biology" title="Developmental biology">developmental biology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Haraway participated in a collaborative exchange with the feminist theorist <a href="/wiki/Lynn_Randolph" title="Lynn Randolph">Lynn Randolph</a> from 1990 to 1996. Their engagement with specific ideas relating to feminism, technoscience, political consciousness, and other social issues, formed the images and narrative of Haraway's book <i>Modest_Witness</i> for which she received the <a href="/wiki/Society_for_Social_Studies_of_Science" title="Society for Social Studies of Science">Society for Social Studies of Science</a>'s (4S) <a href="/wiki/Ludwik_Fleck_Prize" title="Ludwik Fleck Prize">Ludwik Fleck Prize</a> in 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In September 2000, Haraway was awarded the Society for Social Studies of Science's highest honor, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J._D._Bernal" title="J. D. Bernal">J. D. Bernal</a> Award, for her "distinguished contributions" to the field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Haraway serves on the advisory board for numerous academic journals, including <i><a href="/wiki/Differences_(journal)" title="Differences (journal)">differences</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Signs_(journal)" title="Signs (journal)"><i>Signs</i>: <i>Journal of Women in Culture and Society</i></a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Contemporary_Women%27s_Writing" title="Contemporary Women's Writing">Contemporary Women's Writing</a></i>, and <i>Environmental Humanities</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donna_Haraway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Donna Jeanne Haraway was born in 1944 in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denver,_Colorado" title="Denver, Colorado">Denver, Colorado</a>.  Haraway's father was a sportswriter for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Denver_Post" title="The Denver Post">The Denver Post</a></i> and her mother, who came from a heavily Irish Catholic background, died when Haraway was 16 years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>  Haraway attended high school at <a href="/wiki/St._Mary%27s_Academy_(Cherry_Hills_Village)" title="St. Mary's Academy (Cherry Hills Village)">St. Mary’s Academy</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cherry_Hills_Village,_Colorado" title="Cherry Hills Village, Colorado">Cherry Hills Village, Colorado</a>.  Haraway triple majored in zoology, philosophy and literature at the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_College" title="Colorado College">Colorado College</a>, on the full-tuition <a href="/wiki/Boettcher_Scholarship" title="Boettcher Scholarship">Boettcher Scholarship</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> After college, Haraway moved to <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> and studied evolutionary philosophy and theology at the Fondation Teilhard de Chardin on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fulbright_scholarship" title="Fulbright scholarship">Fulbright scholarship</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>   She completed her Ph.D. in <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a> at <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale</a> in 1970 writing a dissertation about the use of metaphor in shaping experiments in experimental biology titled <i>The Search for Organizing Relations: An Organismic Paradigm in Twentieth-Century Developmental Biology</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> later edited into a book and published under the title <i>Crystals, Fabrics, and Fields: Metaphors of Organicism in Twentieth-Century Developmental Biology</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Haraway was the recipient of a number of scholarships, to which she wittily accepted (alluding to the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> and post-war American hegemony) saying, “...people like me became national resources in the national science efforts.  So, there was money available for educating even Irish Catholic girls’ brains."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_themes">Major themes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donna_Haraway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Major themes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id=".22A_Cyborg_Manifesto.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id='"A_Cyborg_Manifesto"'>"A Cyborg Manifesto"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donna_Haraway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title='Edit section: "A Cyborg Manifesto"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/A_Cyborg_Manifesto" title="A Cyborg Manifesto">A Cyborg Manifesto</a></div>
<p>In 1985, Haraway published the essay "Manifesto for <a href="/wiki/Cyborg" title="Cyborg">Cyborgs</a>: Science, Technology, and Socialist-Feminism in the 1980s" in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socialist_Review_(US)" title="Socialist Review (US)">Socialist Review</a></i>. Although most of Haraway's earlier work was focused on emphasizing the masculine bias in scientific culture, she has also contributed greatly to feminist narratives of the twentieth century. For Haraway, the Manifesto offered a response to the rising conservatism during the 1980s in the United States at a critical juncture at which feminists, in order to have any real-world significance, had to acknowledge their situatedness within what she terms the "informatics of domination."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cyborg_Manifesto_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cyborg_Manifesto-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Women were no longer on the outside along a hierarchy of privileged binaries but rather deeply imbued, exploited by and complicit within networked hegemony, and had to form their politics as such.
</p><p>According to Haraway's "Manifesto", "there is nothing about being female that naturally binds women together into a unified category. There is not even such a state as '<a href="/wiki/Being" title="Being">being</a>' female, itself a highly complex category constructed in contested sexual scientific discourses and other social practices".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cyborg_Manifesto_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cyborg_Manifesto-3">[3]</a></sup> A cyborg does not require a stable, essentialist identity, argues Haraway, and feminists should consider creating coalitions based on "affinity" instead of identity. To ground her argument, Haraway analyzes the phrase "women of color", suggesting it as one possible example of affinity politics. Using a term coined by theorist Chela Sandoval, Haraway writes that "oppositional consciousness" is comparable with a cyborg politics, because rather than identity it stresses how affinity comes as a result of "otherness, difference, and specificity".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cyborg_Manifesto_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Cyborg_Manifesto-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Haraway's cyborg is a set of ideals of a genderless, race-less, more collective and peaceful civilization with the caveat of being utterly connected to the machine. Her new versions of beings reject Western humanist conceptions of personhood and promote a disembodied world of information and the withering of subjectivity. The collective consciousness of the beings and their limitless access to information provide the tools with which to create a world of immense socio-political change through altruism and affinity, not biological unity. In her essay Haraway challenges the liberal human subject and its lack of concern for collective desires which leaves the possibility for wide corruption and inequality in the world. Furthermore, the cyborg's importance lays in its coalition of consciousness not in the physical body that carries the information/consciousness. A world of beings with a type of shared knowledge could create a powerful political force towards positive change. Cyborgs can see "from both perspectives at once."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cyborg_Manifesto_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Cyborg_Manifesto-3">[3]</a></sup> In addition, Haraway writes that the cyborg has an imbued nature towards the collective good.
</p><p>Haraway explains that her "Manifesto" is "an effort to build an ironic political myth faithful to feminism, socialism, and materialism."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cyborg_Manifesto_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-Cyborg_Manifesto-3">[3]</a></sup> She adds that "Cyborg imagery can suggest a way out of the maze of dualisms in which we have explained our bodies and our tools to ourselves."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cyborg_Manifesto_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-Cyborg_Manifesto-3">[3]</a></sup> Haraway is serious about finding future ways towards equality and ending dominating behavior; however, the cyborg itself is not as serious of an endeavor for her as the idea of it is. Haraway creates an analogy using current technologies and information to imagine a world with a collective coalition that had the capabilities to create grand socio-political change. Haraway's "Manifesto" is a thought experiment, defining what people think is most important about being and what the future holds for increased artificial intelligence.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cyborg_feminism">Cyborg feminism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donna_Haraway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cyborg feminism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In her updated essay "A Cyborg Manifesto: Science, Technology, and Socialist-Feminism in the Late Twentieth Century", in her book <i>Simians, Cyborgs and Women: The Reinvention of Nature</i> (1991), Haraway uses the cyborg metaphor to explain how fundamental contradictions in feminist theory and identity should be conjoined, rather than resolved, similar to the fusion of machine and organism in cyborgs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cyborg_Manifesto_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-Cyborg_Manifesto-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Haraway's "Manifesto" has considerably influenced the fields of feminism, science studies, and critical theory since its original publication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The manifesto is also an important feminist critique of capitalism.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="According to whom? (April 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span id=".22Situated_Knowledges.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id='"Situated_Knowledges"'>"Situated Knowledges"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donna_Haraway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title='Edit section: "Situated Knowledges"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Situated Knowledges: The Science Question in Feminism and the Privilege of Partial Perspective</i> sheds light on Haraway's vision for a feminist science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Situated_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Situated-4">[4]</a></sup> The essay originated as a commentary on <a href="/wiki/Sandra_Harding" title="Sandra Harding">Sandra Harding</a>'s <i>The Science Question in Feminism</i> (1986) and is a reply to Harding's <i>"successor science"</i>. Haraway offers a critique of the feminist intervention into masculinized traditions of scientific rhetoric and the concept of <a href="/wiki/Objectivity_(science)" title="Objectivity (science)">objectivity</a>. The essay identifies the metaphor that gives shape to the traditional feminist critique as a polarization. At one end lies those who would assert that science is a rhetorical practice and, as such, all <i>"science is a contestable text and a power field"</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> At the other are those interested in a feminist version of objectivity, a position Haraway describes as a "feminist empiricism".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Primate_Visions"><i>Primate Visions</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donna_Haraway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Primate Visions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Haraway also writes about the <a href="/wiki/History_of_science" title="History of science">history of science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>. In <i>Primate Visions: Gender, Race, and Nature in the World of Modern Science</i> (1990), she focused on the metaphors and narratives that direct the science of <a href="/wiki/Primatology" title="Primatology">primatology</a>. She asserted that there is a tendency to masculinize the stories about "reproductive competition and sex between aggressive males and receptive females [that] facilitate some and preclude other types of conclusions".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> She contended that female primatologists focus on different observations that require more communication and basic survival activities, offering very different perspectives of the origins of nature and culture than the currently accepted ones. Drawing on examples of Western <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Narratives" title="Narratives">narratives</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ideologies" title="Ideologies">ideologies</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gender" title="Gender">gender</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(classification_of_human_beings)" title="Race (classification of human beings)">race</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">class</a>, Haraway questioned the most fundamental constructions of scientific human nature stories based on primates. In <i>Primate Visions</i>, she wrote:
</p>
<blockquote><p>"My hope has been that the always oblique and sometimes perverse focusing would facilitate revisionings of fundamental, persistent western narratives about difference, especially racial and sexual difference; about reproduction, especially in terms of the multiplicities of generators and offspring; and about survival, especially about survival imagined in the boundary conditions of both the origins and ends of history, as told within western traditions of that complex genre".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Haraway's aim for science is "to reveal the limits and impossibility of its '<a href="/wiki/Objectivity_(science)" title="Objectivity (science)">objectivity</a>' and to consider some recent revisions offered by feminist primatologists".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Haraway presents an alternative perspective to the accepted ideologies that continue to shape the way scientific human nature stories are created.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
Haraway urges feminists to be more involved in the world of technoscience and to be credited for that involvement. In a 1997 publication, she remarked:
</p>
<blockquote><p>I want feminists to be enrolled more tightly in the meaning-making processes of technoscientific world-building. I also want feminist—activists, cultural producers, scientists, engineers, and scholars (all overlapping categories) — to be recognized for the articulations and enrollment we have been making all along within technoscience, in spite of the ignorance of most "mainstream" scholars in their characterization (or lack of characterizations) of feminism in relation to both technoscientific practice and technoscience studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> </p></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Donna_Haraway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Haraway's work has been criticized for being "methodologically vague"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and using noticeably opaque language that is "sometimes concealing in an apparently deliberate way".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onlinelibrary.wiley.com_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-onlinelibrary.wiley.com-32">[32]</a></sup> Several reviewers have argued that her understanding of the scientific method is questionable, and that her explorations of epistemology at times leave her texts virtually meaning-free.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onlinelibrary.wiley.com_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-onlinelibrary.wiley.com-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-springerlink.com_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-springerlink.com-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 1991 review of Haraway's <i>Primate Visions</i>, published in the <i><a href="/wiki/International_Journal_of_Primatology" title="International Journal of Primatology">International Journal of Primatology</a></i>, provides examples of some of the most common critiques of her view of science:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-springerlink.com_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-springerlink.com-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote><p>This is a book that contradicts itself a hundred times; but that is not a criticism of it, because its author thinks contradictions are a sign of intellectual ferment and vitality. This is a book that systematically distorts and selects historical evidence; but that is not a criticism, because its author thinks that all interpretations are biased, and she regards it as her duty to pick and choose her facts to favor her own brand of politics. This is a book full of vaporous, French-intellectual prose that makes Teilhard de Chardin sound like Ernest Hemingway by comparison; but that is not a criticism, because the author likes that sort of prose and has taken lessons in how to write it, and she thinks that plain, homely speech is part of a conspiracy to oppress the poor.
This is a book that clatters around in a dark closet of irrelevancies for 450 pages before it bumps accidentally into its index and stops; but that is not a criticism, either, because its author finds it gratifying and refreshing to bang unrelated facts together as a rebuke to stuffy minds. This book infuriated me; but that is not a defect in it, because it is supposed to infuriate people like me, and the author would have been happier still if I had blown out an artery. In short, this book is flawless, because all its deficiencies are deliberate products of art. Given its assumptions, there is nothing here to criticize. The only course open to a reviewer who dislikes this book as much as I do is to question its author’s fundamental assumptions—which are big-ticket items involving the nature and relationships of language, knowledge, and science.</p></blockquote>
<p>Another review of the same book, appearing in a 1990 issue of the <i><a href="/wiki/American_Journal_of_Primatology" title="American Journal of Primatology">American Journal of Primatology</a></i>, offers a similar criticism of Haraway's literary style and scholarly methods:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onlinelibrary.wiley.com_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-onlinelibrary.wiley.com-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote><p>There are many places where an editorial hand appears absent altogether. Neologisms are continually coined, and sentences are paragraph-long and convoluted. Biography, history, propaganda, science, science fiction, and cinema are intertwined in the most confusing way. Perhaps the idea is to induce a slightly dissociated state, so that readers can be lulled into belief. If one did not already possess some background, this book would give no lucid history of anthropology or primatology.</p></blockquote>
<p>However, a review in the <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_of_the_History_of_Biology" title="Journal of the History of Biology">Journal of the History of Biology</a></i> disagrees:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fausto-Sterling_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fausto-Sterling-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote><p><i>Primate Visions</i> is one of the most important books to come along in the last twenty years. Historians of science have begun to write more externalist histories, acknowledging the possibilities of a science profoundly integrated with ongoing social agenda. Haraway's history of primatology in the twentieth century sets new standards for this approach, standards that will not be surpassed for some time to come. The book is important to students of science, feminists, historians, and anyone else interested in how the complex systems of race, gender, and science intertwine to produce supposedly objective versions of the "truth." This analysis of primatology is at once a complex, interdisciplinary, and deeply scholarly history and an imaginative, provocative analysis of the working of science in late twentieth-century Euro-America.</p></blockquote>
