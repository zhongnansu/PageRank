<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Aerobic methane production</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Methane-2D-dimensions.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="750" data-file-width="1000" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Methane-2D-dimensions.svg/220px-Methane-2D-dimensions.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Methane-2D-dimensions.svg/330px-Methane-2D-dimensions.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Methane-2D-dimensions.svg/440px-Methane-2D-dimensions.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Methane-2D-dimensions.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Methane</div></div></div>
<p><b>Aerobic methane production</b> is a potential <a href="/wiki/Biological_pathway" title="Biological pathway">biological pathway</a> for atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> (CH<sub>4</sub>) production under <a href="/wiki/Oxygenation_(environmental)" title="Oxygenation (environmental)">oxygenated</a> conditions. The existence of this pathway was first theorized in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keppler2006_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Keppler2006-1">[1]</a></sup> While significant evidence suggests the existence of this pathway,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keppler2006_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Keppler2006-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vigano_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vigano-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keppler2008_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Keppler2008-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wang_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wang-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bloom_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bloom-5">[5]</a></sup> it remains poorly understood and its existence is controversial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vigano_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vigano-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nisbet_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nisbet-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dueck_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dueck-7">[7]</a></sup> Naturally occurring methane is mainly produced by the process of <a href="/wiki/Methanogenesis" title="Methanogenesis">methanogenesis</a>, a form of <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_respiration" title="Anaerobic respiration">anaerobic respiration</a> used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microorganisms" title="Microorganisms">microorganisms</a> as an energy source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Methanogenesis usually only occurs under <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(environmental)" title="Hypoxia (environmental)">anoxic</a> conditions. By contrast, <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/aerobic" title="wikt:aerobic">aerobic</a> methane production is thought to occur in oxygenated environments under near-<a href="/wiki/Standard_conditions_for_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard conditions for temperature and pressure">ambient conditions</a>. The process involves non-microbial methane generation from <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_plant" title="Terrestrial plant">terrestrial plant-matter</a>. Temperature and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_light" title="Ultraviolet light">ultraviolet light</a> are thought to be key factors in this process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keppler2006_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Keppler2006-1">[1]</a></sup> Methane may also be produced under aerobic conditions in near-surface ocean water, a process which likely involves the degradation of methylphosphonate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karl_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Karl-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="From_terrestrial_plants">From terrestrial plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerobic_methane_production&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: From terrestrial plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Initial_discovery">Initial discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerobic_methane_production&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Initial discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AtmosphericMethane.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="633" data-file-width="540" height="258" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/AtmosphericMethane.png/220px-AtmosphericMethane.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/AtmosphericMethane.png/330px-AtmosphericMethane.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/AtmosphericMethane.png/440px-AtmosphericMethane.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AtmosphericMethane.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Global atmospheric methane distribution</div></div></div>
<p>In 2005, Frankenberg <i>et al.</i> published the findings of a global methane distribution study in which they used space-borne <a href="/wiki/Near-infrared_spectroscopy" title="Near-infrared spectroscopy">near-infrared absorption spectroscopy</a>. The study identified significantly elevated CH<sub>4</sub> <a href="/wiki/Mixing_ratio" title="Mixing ratio">mixing ratios</a> in tropical regions above evergreen forests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frankenberg_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frankenberg-10">[10]</a></sup> The data indicated an additional tropical source of 30–40 Tg<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frankenberg_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Frankenberg-10">[10]</a></sup> over the time period of the investigation (August–November). This contribution could not be adequately explained within the currently accepted global budget of CH<sub>4</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frankenberg_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Frankenberg-10">[10]</a></sup> These findings prompted Keppler <i>et al.</i> to conduct their study to investigate the possibility of methane formation by plant material. Their study included glass vial incubation experiments with detached leaves and Plexiglas chamber experiments with intact plants. In both cases the material was sealed in a controlled environment with CH<sub>4</sub>-free air in order to analyze the production of CH<sub>4</sub>. Since the tests were conducted under aerobic conditions it was unlikely that any CH<sub>4</sub> produced would be related to methanogenic bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keppler2006_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Keppler2006-1">[1]</a></sup> This possibility was further excluded by measuring CH<sub>4</sub> production by leaf tissue sterilized with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%93_radiation" title="Γ radiation">γ-radiation</a>. They theorized that "the structural component <a href="/wiki/Pectin" title="Pectin">pectin</a> plays a prominent role in the <a href="/wiki/In_situ" title="In situ">in situ</a> formation of CH<sub>4</sub> in plants"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keppler2006_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Keppler2006-1">[1]</a></sup> but were unable to identify a <a href="/wiki/Reaction_mechanism" title="Reaction mechanism">chemical mechanism</a> for this CH<sub>4</sub> production.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_study">Further study</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerobic_methane_production&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Further study">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Wang <i>et al.</i> (2008) found that <a href="/wiki/Methane_emissions" title="Methane emissions">methane emissions</a> varied greatly by plant species, noting that <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrub</a> species were much more likely to produce methane than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbaceous" title="Herbaceous">herbaceous</a> species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wang_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wang-4">[4]</a></sup> They also noted that among herbaceous species which they tested, those that emitted methane did so from stems, but not from detached leaves, while shrub species typically emitted higher methane concentrations from detached leaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wang_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wang-4">[4]</a></sup> 
A follow-up study by Keppler <i>et al.</i> reconfirmed their earlier findings and found "unambiguous <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a> evidence that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methoxyl" title="Methoxyl">methoxyl</a> groups of pectin can act as a source of atmospheric CH<sub>4</sub> under aerobic conditions",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keppler2008_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Keppler2008-3">[3]</a></sup> but again failed to identify the chemical mechanism.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_of_temperature_and_light">Influence of temperature and light</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerobic_methane_production&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Influence of temperature and light">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Keppler <i>et al</i>. observed that the release of CH<sub>4</sub> was "very temperature sensitive—concentrations approximately doubled with every 10 <sup>o</sup>C increase over the range 30–70 <sup>o</sup>C suggesting a non-enzymic rather than an <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzyme</a>-mediated process".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keppler2006_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Keppler2006-1">[1]</a></sup> They also remarked that "emission rates were found to increase dramatically, by a factor of 3–5 (up to 870 ng per g (dry weight) h<sup>−1</sup>), when chambers were exposed to natural sunlight".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keppler2006_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Keppler2006-1">[1]</a></sup> Vigano <i>et al</i>. found that "emissions from UV irradiation are almost instantaneous, indicating a direct <a href="/wiki/Photochemistry" title="Photochemistry">photochemical process</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vigano_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Vigano-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Potential_environmental_significance">Potential environmental significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerobic_methane_production&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Potential environmental significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Keppler <i>et al</i>. calculated a "first estimate" for the newly established CH<sub>4</sub> source. Their calculations were based on broad assumptions, which they admitted neglected "the complexity of <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_ecosystem" title="Terrestrial ecosystem">terrestrial ecosystems</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keppler2006_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-Keppler2006-1">[1]</a></sup> 
They estimated methane released by living vegetation to be in the range 62–236 Tg yr<sup>−1</sup> (average 149 Tg yr<sup>−1</sup>) with the main contribution assigned to tropical forests and grasslands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keppler2006_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-Keppler2006-1">[1]</a></sup> They believed that "the detection of an additional source of this magnitude, some 10-30% of the present annual source strength, would necessitate reconsideration of the global CH<sub>4</sub> budget".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keppler2006_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-Keppler2006-1">[1]</a></sup> 
Later estimates, using Keppler <i>et al.</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s data as well as data produced by later studies suggested a lesser global significance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keppler2008_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Keppler2008-3">[3]</a></sup> One study suggested that the maximum global emissions of methane from terrestrial plants might only be on the order of 0.2–1.0 Tg CH<sub>4</sub> yr<sup>−1</sup> compared with total global emissions of 550 Tg CH<sub>4</sub> yr<sup>−1</sup>, a significantly smaller contribution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bloom_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bloom-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_and_conflicting_data">Criticism and conflicting data</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerobic_methane_production&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Criticism and conflicting data">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following the publication of Keppler <i>et al.</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s (2006) findings, there was a substantial response from the scientific community. Many questioned the findings, pointing to flaws in Keppler <i>et al.</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s methodology. In particular, their up-scaling method for calculating global estimates for methane emissions by terrestrial plants was criticized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vigano_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Vigano-2">[2]</a></sup> A number of follow-up publications presented conflicting data, generating significant uncertainty in the role of terrestrial plants to the global methane budget.
</p><p>Dueck <i>et al.</i> conducted similar experiments to the intact-plant chamber experiments conducted by Keppler <i>et al</i>. They found "no evidence for substantial methane emissions from terrestrial plants".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dueck_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dueck-7">[7]</a></sup> They suggested that the supposed emissions observed by Keppler <i>et al.</i> may have been related to "ambient methane concentrations in inter-cellular air spaces and air spaces in the soil system".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dueck_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dueck-7">[7]</a></sup> Vigano <i>et al.</i> later responded to this criticism by suggesting that, if UV light is in fact an important factor in aerobic methane emissions, "then it is not surprising that no emissions were found by Dueck <i>et al</i>. (2007), who used metal halide HPI-T lamps and glass chambers for their measurements".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vigano_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Vigano-2">[2]</a></sup> Other studies suggested that the detected methane emissions were related to transport of dissolved methane from the soil in water, or to the spontaneous breakdown of plant matter under certain stress conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nisbet_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nisbet-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_ocean">In the ocean</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aerobic_methane_production&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In the ocean">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Methane#Geological_routes" title="Methane">Methane § Geological routes</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Supersaturation" title="Supersaturation">Supersaturation</a> of methane in oxygenated, near-surface ocean water is a phenomenon which has been widely observed, but which is still poorly understood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Valle_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Valle-11">[11]</a></sup> Methane is often 10–75% supersaturated in the oxygenated surface <a href="/wiki/Mixed_layer" title="Mixed layer">mixed-layer</a>, causing the ocean to contribute methane to the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Valle_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Valle-11">[11]</a></sup> One possible source for this supersaturated methane is the degradation of dissolved <a href="/wiki/Water_column" title="Water column">water-column</a> methylphosphonate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karl_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Karl-9">[9]</a></sup> The importance of the degradation of methylphosphonate in the production of CH<sub>4</sub> in the ocean is likely variable and may be related to the availability of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">Fe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">N</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">P</a> in the water column.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Valle_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Valle-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
