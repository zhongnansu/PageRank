<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Abiogenic petroleum origin</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Abiogenic petroleum origin</b> is a term used to describe a number of different hypotheses which propose that <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> are formed by inorganic means rather than by the decomposition of organisms. The two principal abiogenic petroleum hypotheses, proposing that all petroleum is abiogenic, the <i>deep gas hypothesis</i><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag needs further explanation. (January 2018)">further explanation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Gold" title="Thomas Gold">Thomas Gold</a> and the <i>deep abiotic petroleum hypothesis</i>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag needs further explanation. (January 2018)">further explanation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> have been scientifically reviewed and largely rejected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glasby2006_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-glasby2006-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hook_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hook-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title=" (January 2018)">why?</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Earlier studies of mantle-derived rocks from many places have shown that hydrocarbons from the mantle region can be found widely around the globe. However, the content of such hydrocarbons in such rocks are extremely low and seem to lack accumulation in concentrations that would render them feasible for exploitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Scientific consensus on the <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel#Origin" title="Fossil fuel">origin of oil and gas</a> is that all natural oil and gas deposits on Earth are fossil fuels and are, therefore, biogenic. Globally significant amounts of abiotic oil in the crust can even be ruled out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherwood2002_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sherwood2002-4">[4]</a></sup> Yet, abiogenesis of small quantities of oil and gas remains an area of ongoing research in limited, mostly just laboratory scale.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview_hypotheses">Overview hypotheses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiogenic_petroleum_origin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview hypotheses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some abiogenic hypotheses have proposed that oil and gas did not originate from fossil deposits, but have instead originated from deep carbon deposits, present since the <a href="/wiki/Formation_and_evolution_of_the_Solar_System" title="Formation and evolution of the Solar System">formation of the Earth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gold1999_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-gold1999-5">[5]</a></sup> Additionally, it has been suggested that hydrocarbons may have arrived on Earth from solid bodies such as <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroids</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Late_Heavy_Bombardment" title="Late Heavy Bombardment">late formation</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, carrying hydrocarbons with them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scott2004_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-scott2004-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stachel_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stachel-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Certain abiogenic hypotheses gained some popularity among geologists over the past several centuries. Scientists in the former <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> widely held that significant petroleum deposits could be attributed to abiogenic origin, though this view fell out of favor toward the end of the 20th century because they did not make useful predictions for the discovery of oil deposits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glasby2006_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-glasby2006-1">[1]</a></sup> It has now been generally accepted that the theory of abiogenic formation of petroleum has insufficient scientific support and that oil and gas fuels on Earth are formed almost exclusively from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_material" title="Organic material">organic material</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The abiogenic hypothesis regained some support in 2009 when researchers at the Royal Institute of Technology (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KTH" title="KTH">KTH</a>) in Stockholm reported they believed they had proven that fossils from animals and plants are not necessary for crude oil and natural gas to be generated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ScienceDaily_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ScienceDaily-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In his 2014 publication <i>Chemistry of the Climate System</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climate_System_p_10_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Climate_System_p_10-11">[11]</a></sup> German chemist Detlev Moller documents sufficient reliable evidence to show that both processes can be shown to co-exist, that they're not mutually exclusive.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiogenic_petroleum_origin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An abiogenic hypothesis was first proposed by <a href="/wiki/Georgius_Agricola" title="Georgius Agricola">Georgius Agricola</a> in the 16th century and various additional abiogenic hypotheses were proposed in the 19th century, most notably by Prussian geographer <a href="/wiki/Alexander_von_Humboldt" title="Alexander von Humboldt">Alexander von Humboldt</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (November 2014)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> the Russian chemist <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Mendeleev" title="Dmitri Mendeleev">Dmitri Mendeleev</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (November 2014)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> and the French chemist <a href="/wiki/Marcellin_Berthelot" title="Marcellin Berthelot">Marcellin Berthelot</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (November 2014)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> Abiogenic hypotheses were revived in the last half of the 20th century by Soviet scientists who had little influence outside the Soviet Union because most of their research was published in Russian. The hypothesis was re-defined and made popular in the West by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Gold" title="Thomas Gold">Thomas Gold</a> who developed his theories from 1979 to 1998, and published his research in English.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glasby2006_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-glasby2006-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Abraham_Gottlob_Werner" title="Abraham Gottlob Werner">Abraham Gottlob Werner</a> and the proponents of <a href="/wiki/Neptunism" title="Neptunism">neptunism</a> in the 18th century regarded <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basaltic</a> <a href="/wiki/Sill_(geology)" title="Sill (geology)">sills</a> as solidified oils or bitumen. While these notions proved unfounded, the basic idea of an association between petroleum and magmatism persisted. <a href="/wiki/Alexander_von_Humboldt" title="Alexander von Humboldt">Alexander von Humboldt</a> proposed an inorganic abiogenic hypothesis for petroleum formation after he observed petroleum springs in the Bay of Cumaux (<a href="/wiki/Cuman%C3%A1" title="Cumaná">Cumaná</a>) on the northeast coast of <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
He is quoted as saying in 1804, "the petroleum is the product of a distillation from great depth and issues from the primitive rocks beneath which the forces of all volcanic action lie".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  Other early prominent proponents of what would become the generalized abiogenic hypothesis included <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Mendeleev" title="Dmitri Mendeleev">Dmitri Mendeleev</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Marcellin_Berthelot" title="Marcellin Berthelot">Berthelot</a>.
</p><p>In 1951, the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> geologist <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Kudryavtsev" title="Nikolai Kudryavtsev">Nikolai Alexandrovitch Kudryavtsev</a> proposed the modern abiotic hypothesis of petroleum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kudryavtsev_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kudryavtsev-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kudryavtsev2_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kudryavtsev2-15">[15]</a></sup> On the basis of his analysis of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athabasca_Oil_Sands" title="Athabasca Oil Sands">Athabasca Oil Sands</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alberta,_Canada" title="Alberta, Canada">Alberta, Canada</a>, he concluded that no <a href="/wiki/Source_rock" title="Source rock">"source rocks"</a> could form the enormous volume of hydrocarbons, and therefore offered abiotic deep petroleum as the most plausible explanation. (Humic coals have since been proposed for the source rocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>)  Others who continued Kudryavtsev's work included <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petr_N._Kropotkin" title="Petr N. Kropotkin">Petr N. Kropotkin</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vladimir_B._Porfir%27ev" title="Vladimir B. Porfir'ev">Vladimir B. </p><p>Astronomer <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Gold" title="Thomas Gold">Thomas Gold</a> was a prominent proponent of the abiogenic hypothesis in the West until his death in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glasby2006_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-glasby2006-1">[1]</a></sup> More recently, Jack Kenney of Gas Resources Corporation has come to prominence,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kenney2_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-kenney2-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kenney2001_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-kenney2001-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kenney2002_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-kenney2002-19">[19]</a></sup> supported by studies by researchers at the Royal Institute of Technology in Stockholm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ScienceDaily_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-ScienceDaily-9">[9]</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="State_of_current_research">State of current research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiogenic_petroleum_origin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: State of current research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Petroleum#Formation" title="Petroleum">Petroleum § Formation</a></div>
<p>The weight of evidence currently shows that petroleum is derived from ancient <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> However, it still has to be established conclusively, which means that abiogenic alternative theories of petroleum formation cannot be dismissed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-speight_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-speight-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>A 2006 review article by Geoffrey Glasby presented arguments against the abiogenic origin of petroleum<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> on a number of counts;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glasby2006_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-glasby2006-1">[1]</a></sup> on the other hand several recent studies demonstrate that hydrocarbons heavier than methane can be produced by abiogenic processes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ScienceDaily_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-ScienceDaily-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Climate_System_p_10_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Climate_System_p_10-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Foundations_of_abiogenic_hypotheses">Foundations of abiogenic hypotheses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiogenic_petroleum_origin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Foundations of abiogenic hypotheses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Within the mantle, carbon may exist as <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a>—chiefly <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>—and as elemental carbon, carbon dioxide, and carbonates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kenney2002_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-kenney2002-19">[19]</a></sup> The abiotic hypothesis is that the full suite of hydrocarbons found in petroleum can either be generated in the mantle by abiogenic processes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kenney2002_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-kenney2002-19">[19]</a></sup> or by biological processing of those abiogenic hydrocarbons, and that the source-hydrocarbons of abiogenic origin can migrate out of the mantle into the <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a> until they escape to the surface or are trapped by impermeable strata, forming petroleum reservoirs.
</p><p>Abiogenic hypotheses generally reject the supposition that certain molecules found within petroleum, known as <a href="/wiki/Biomarker_(petroleum)" title="Biomarker (petroleum)">biomarkers</a>, are indicative of the biological origin of petroleum.  They contend that these molecules mostly come from microbes feeding on petroleum in its upward migration through the crust, that some of them are found in meteorites, which have presumably never contacted living material, and that some can be generated abiogenically by plausible reactions in petroleum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kenney2001_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-kenney2001-18">[18]</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Primordial_deposits">Primordial deposits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiogenic_petroleum_origin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Primordial deposits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Thomas Gold's work was focused on hydrocarbon deposits of primordial origin.  Meteorites are believed to represent the major composition of material from which the Earth was formed.  Some meteorites, such as <a href="/wiki/Carbonaceous_chondrite" title="Carbonaceous chondrite">carbonaceous chondrites</a>, contain carbonaceous material.  If a large amount of this material is still within the Earth, it could have been leaking upward for billions of years.  The thermodynamic conditions within the mantle would allow many hydrocarbon molecules to be at equilibrium under high pressure and high temperature.  Although molecules in these conditions may disassociate, resulting fragments would be reformed due to the pressure.  An average equilibrium of various molecules would exist depending upon conditions and the carbon-hydrogen ratio of the material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldusgs_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-goldusgs-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Creation_within_the_mantle">Creation within the mantle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiogenic_petroleum_origin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Creation within the mantle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Russian researchers concluded that hydrocarbon mixes would be created within the mantle.  Experiments under high temperatures and pressures produced many hydrocarbons—including n-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkanes" title="Alkanes">alkanes</a> through C<sub>10</sub>H<sub>22</sub>—from <a href="/wiki/Iron_oxide" title="Iron oxide">iron oxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>, and water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kenney2002_19-4"><a href="#cite_note-kenney2002-19">[19]</a></sup>  Because such materials are in the mantle and in <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subducted</a> crust, there is no requirement that all hydrocarbons be produced from primordial deposits.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrogen_generation">Hydrogen generation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiogenic_petroleum_origin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Hydrogen generation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hydrogen gas and water have been found more than 6,000 metres (20,000 ft) deep in the upper crust in the <a href="/wiki/Siljan_Ring" title="Siljan Ring">Siljan Ring</a> <a href="/wiki/Borehole" title="Borehole">boreholes</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kola_Superdeep_Borehole" title="Kola Superdeep Borehole">Kola Superdeep Borehole</a>.  Data from the western United States suggests that <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifers</a> from near the surface may extend to depths of 10,000 metres (33,000 ft) to 20,000 metres (66,000 ft). Hydrogen gas can be created by water reacting with <a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">silicates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Feldspar" title="Feldspar">feldspar</a> at temperatures in the range of 25 °C (77 °F) to 270 °C (518 °F).  These minerals are common in crustal rocks such as <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a>. Hydrogen may react with dissolved carbon compounds in water to form methane and higher carbon compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>One reaction not involving silicates which can create hydrogen is:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>Ferrous oxide + water → magnetite + hydrogen</dd>
<dd>3FeO + H<sub>2</sub>O → Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> + H<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>The above reaction operates best at low pressures. At pressures greater than 5 gigapascals (49,000 atm) almost no hydrogen is created.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scott2004_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-scott2004-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Thomas Gold reported that hydrocarbons were found in the Siljan Ring borehole and in general increased with depth, although the venture was not a commercial success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, several geologists analysed the results and said that no hydrocarbon was found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glasby2006_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-glasby2006-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mello2005_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-mello2005-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kerr_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-kerr-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jeffrey_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-jeffrey-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-castano_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-castano-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jeffrey2_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-jeffrey2-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Serpentinite_mechanism">Serpentinite mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiogenic_petroleum_origin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Serpentinite mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1967, the <a href="/wiki/Ukrainians" title="Ukrainians">Ukrainian</a> scientist <a href="/wiki/Emmanuil_B._Chekaliuk" title="Emmanuil B. Chekaliuk">Emmanuil B. Chekaliuk</a> proposed that petroleum could be formed at high temperatures and pressures from inorganic carbon in the form of carbon dioxide, hydrogen and/or methane.
</p><p>This mechanism is supported by several lines of evidence which are accepted by modern scientific literature. This involves synthesis of oil within the crust via catalysis by chemically reductive rocks. A proposed mechanism for the formation of inorganic hydrocarbons<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-keith2005_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-keith2005-41">[41]</a></sup> is via natural analogs of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fischer-Tropsch_process" title="Fischer-Tropsch process">Fischer-Tropsch process</a> known as the <i>serpentinite mechanism</i> or the serpentinite process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-szatmari_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-szatmari-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-charlou2005_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-charlou2005-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Serpentinites are ideal rocks to host this process as they are formed from <a href="/wiki/Peridotite" title="Peridotite">peridotites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dunite" title="Dunite">dunites</a>, rocks which contain greater than 80% <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">olivine</a> and usually a percentage of Fe-Ti spinel minerals. Most olivines also contain high nickel concentrations (up to several percent) and may also contain chromite or chromium as a contaminant in olivine, providing the needed transition metals.
</p><p>However, serpentinite synthesis and spinel cracking reactions require <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_alteration" title="Hydrothermal alteration">hydrothermal alteration</a> of pristine peridotite-dunite, which is a finite process intrinsically related to metamorphism, and further, requires significant addition of water. Serpentinite is unstable at mantle temperatures and is readily dehydrated to <a href="/wiki/Granulite" title="Granulite">granulite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amphibolite" title="Amphibolite">amphibolite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Talc" title="Talc">talc</a>–<a href="/wiki/Schist" title="Schist">schist</a> and even <a href="/wiki/Eclogite" title="Eclogite">eclogite</a>. This suggests that methanogenesis in the presence of serpentinites is restricted in space and time to mid-ocean ridges and upper levels of subduction zones.  However, water has been found as deep as 12,000 metres (39,000 ft),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> so water-based reactions are dependent upon the local conditions. Oil being created by this process in intracratonic regions is limited by the materials and temperature.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Serpentinite_synthesis">Serpentinite synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiogenic_petroleum_origin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Serpentinite synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A chemical basis for the abiotic petroleum process is the <a href="/wiki/Serpentinite" title="Serpentinite">serpentinization</a> of <a href="/wiki/Peridotite" title="Peridotite">peridotite</a>, beginning with methanogenesis via hydrolysis of olivine into serpentine in the presence of carbon dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-charlou2005_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-charlou2005-42">[42]</a></sup> Olivine, composed of Forsterite and Fayalite metamorphoses into serpentine, magnetite and silica by the following reactions, with silica from fayalite decomposition (reaction 1a) feeding into the forsterite reaction (1b).

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spinel_polymerization_mechanism">Spinel polymerization mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiogenic_petroleum_origin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Spinel polymerization mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evidence_of_abiogenic_mechanisms">Evidence of abiogenic mechanisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiogenic_petroleum_origin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Evidence of abiogenic mechanisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Theoretical calculations by J.F. Kenney using scaled particle theory (a statistical mechanical model) for a simplified perturbed hard-chain predict that methane compressed to 30,000 bars (3.0 GPa) or 40,000 bars (4.0 GPa) kbar at 1,000 °C (1,830 °F) (conditions in the mantle) is relatively unstable in relation to higher hydrocarbons. However, these calculations do not include methane pyrolysis yielding amorphous carbon and hydrogen, which is recognized as the prevalent reaction at high temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kenney2001_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-kenney2001-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kenney2002_19-5"><a href="#cite_note-kenney2002-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>Experiments in diamond anvil high pressure cells have resulted in partial conversion of methane and inorganic carbonates into light hydrocarbons.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sharma_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-sharma-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kolesnikov_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kolesnikov-45">[45]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span id="Biotic_.28microbial.29_hydrocarbons"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Biotic_(microbial)_hydrocarbons">Biotic (microbial) hydrocarbons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiogenic_petroleum_origin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Biotic (microbial) hydrocarbons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The "deep biotic petroleum hypothesis", similar to the abiogenic petroleum origin hypothesis, holds that not all <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> deposits within the Earth's rocks can be explained purely according to the orthodox view of <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_geology" title="Petroleum geology">petroleum geology</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Gold" title="Thomas Gold">Thomas Gold</a> used the term <i>the deep hot biosphere</i> to describe the microbes which live underground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gold1999_5-10"><a href="#cite_note-gold1999-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>This hypothesis is different from biogenic oil in that the role of deep-dwelling microbes is a biological source for oil which is not of a sedimentary origin and is not sourced from surface carbon. Deep microbial life is only a contaminant of primordial hydrocarbons. Parts of microbes yield molecules as biomarkers.
</p><p>Deep biotic oil is considered to be formed as a byproduct of the life cycle of deep microbes.
Shallow biotic oil is considered to be formed as a byproduct of the life cycles of shallow microbes.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Microbial_biomarkers">Microbial biomarkers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiogenic_petroleum_origin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Microbial biomarkers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Thomas_Gold" title="Thomas Gold">Thomas Gold</a>, in a 1999 book, cited the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Thermophile" title="Thermophile">thermophile</a> bacteria in the Earth's crust as new support for the postulate that these bacteria could explain the existence of certain <a href="/wiki/Biomarker_(petroleum)" title="Biomarker (petroleum)">biomarkers</a> in extracted petroleum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gold1999_5-11"><a href="#cite_note-gold1999-5">[5]</a></sup> A rebuttal of biogenic origins based on biomarkers has been offered by Kenney, et al. (2001).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glasby2006_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-glasby2006-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kenney2001_18-4"><a href="#cite_note-kenney2001-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopic_evidence">Isotopic evidence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiogenic_petroleum_origin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Isotopic evidence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">Methane</a> is ubiquitous in crustal fluid and gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lollar2006_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-lollar2006-32">[32]</a></sup> Research continues to attempt to characterise crustal sources of methane as biogenic or abiogenic using carbon isotope fractionation of observed gases (Lollar &amp; Sherwood 2006). There are few clear examples of abiogenic methane-ethane-butane, as the same processes favor enrichment of light isotopes in all chemical reactions, whether organic or inorganic. δ<sup>13</sup>C of methane overlaps that of inorganic carbonate and graphite in the crust, which are heavily depleted in <sup>12</sup>C, and attain this by isotopic fractionation during metamorphic reactions.
</p><p>One argument for abiogenic oil cites the high carbon depletion of methane as stemming from the observed carbon isotope depletion with depth in the crust. However, diamonds, which are definitively of mantle origin, are not as depleted as methane, which implies that methane carbon isotope fractionation is not controlled by mantle values.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mello2005_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-mello2005-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>Commercially extractable concentrations of <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> (greater than 0.3%) are present in natural gas from the Panhandle-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hugoton_Natural_Gas_Area" title="Hugoton Natural Gas Area">Hugoton</a> fields in the USA, as well as from some Algerian and Russian gas fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>Helium trapped within most petroleum occurrences, such as the occurrence in Texas, is of a distinctly crustal character with an <i>Ra</i> ratio of less than 0.0001 that of the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weinlich_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weinlich-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Polyak_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Polyak-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Chimaera gas seep, near Antalya (SW Turkey), new and thorough molecular and isotopic analyses including methane (~87% v/v; D13C1 from -7.9 to -12.3 ‰; D13D1 from -119 to -124 ‰), light alkanes (C2+C3+C4+C5 = 0.5%; C6+: 0.07%; D13C2 from -24.2 to -26.5 ‰; D13C3 from -25.5 to -27 ‰), hydrogen (7.5 to 11%), carbon dioxide (0.01-0.07%; D13CCO2: -15 ‰), helium (~80 ppmv; R/Ra: 0.41) and nitrogen (2-4.9%; D15N from -2 to -2.8 ‰) converge to indicate that the seep releases a mixture of organic thermogenic gas, related to mature Type III kerogen occurring in Paleozoic and Mesozoic organic rich sedimentary rocks, and abiogenic gas produced by low temperature serpentinization in the Tekirova ophiolitic unit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoşgörmez,_H._2008_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hoşgörmez,_H._2008-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biomarker_chemicals">Biomarker chemicals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiogenic_petroleum_origin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Biomarker chemicals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Certain chemicals found in naturally occurring petroleum contain chemical and structural similarities to compounds found within many living organisms. These include <a href="/wiki/Terpenoid" title="Terpenoid">terpenoids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Terpene" title="Terpene">terpenes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pristane" title="Pristane">pristane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phytane" title="Phytane">phytane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cholestane" title="Cholestane">cholestane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chlorin" title="Chlorin">chlorins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Porphyrin" title="Porphyrin">porphyrins</a>, which are large, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chelate" title="Chelate">chelating</a> molecules in the same family as <a href="/wiki/Heme" title="Heme">heme</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chlorophyll" title="Chlorophyll">chlorophyll</a>. Materials which suggest certain biological processes include <span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">tetracyclic diterpane and oleanane.</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The presence of these chemicals in crude oil is a result of the inclusion of biological material in the oil; these chemicals are released by <a href="/wiki/Kerogen" title="Kerogen">kerogen</a> during the production of hydrocarbon oils, as these are chemicals highly resistant to degradation and plausible chemical paths have been studied. Abiotic defenders state that biomarkers get into oil during its way up as it gets in touch with ancient fossils. However a more plausible explanation is that biomarkers are traces of biological molecules from bacteria (archaea) that feed on primordial hydrocarbons and die in that environment. For example, hopanoids are just parts of the bacterial cell wall present in oil as contaminant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gold1999_5-12"><a href="#cite_note-gold1999-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trace_metals">Trace metals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiogenic_petroleum_origin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Trace metals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">Nickel</a> (Ni), <a href="/wiki/Vanadium" title="Vanadium">vanadium</a> (V), <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> (Pb), <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a> (As), <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium</a> (Cd), <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> (Hg) and others metals frequently occur in oils. Some heavy crude oils, such as Venezuelan heavy crude have up to 45% <a href="/wiki/Vanadium" title="Vanadium">vanadium</a> pentoxide content in their ash, high enough that it is a commercial source for vanadium. Abiotic supporters argue that these metals are common in Earth's mantle, but relatively high contents of nickel, vanadium, lead and arsenic can be usually found in almost all marine sediments.
</p><p>Analysis of 22 trace elements in oils correlate significantly better with <a href="/wiki/Chondrite" title="Chondrite">chondrite</a>, serpentinized fertile mantle peridotite, and the primitive mantle than with oceanic or continental crust, and shows no correlation with seawater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-szatmari_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-szatmari-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reduced_carbon">Reduced carbon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiogenic_petroleum_origin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Reduced carbon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sir <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Robinson_(organic_chemist)" title="Robert Robinson (organic chemist)">Robert Robinson</a> studied the chemical makeup of natural petroleum oils in great detail, and concluded that they were mostly far too hydrogen-rich to be a likely product of the decay of plant debris, assuming a dual origin for Earth hydrocarbons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldusgs_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-goldusgs-33">[33]</a></sup>   However, several processes which generate hydrogen could supply kerogen hydrogenation which is compatible with the conventional explanation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olefin" title="Olefin">Olefins</a>, the unsaturated hydrocarbons, would have been expected to predominate by far in any material that was derived in that way. He also wrote: "Petroleum ... [seems to be] a primordial hydrocarbon mixture into which bio-products have been added."
</p><p>This has however been demonstrated later to be a misunderstanding by Robinson, related to the fact that only short duration experiments were available to him.  Olefins are thermally very unstable (that is why natural petroleum normally does not contain such compounds) and in laboratory experiments that last more than a few hours, the olefins are no longer present.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The presence of low-oxygen and hydroxyl-poor hydrocarbons in natural living media is supported by the presence of natural waxes (n=30+), oils (n=20+) and lipids in both plant matter and animal matter, for instance fats in phytoplankton, zooplankton and so on. These oils and waxes, however, occur in quantities too small to significantly affect the overall hydrogen/carbon ratio of biological materials.
However, after the discovery of highly aliphatic biopolymers in algae, and that oil generating kerogen essentially represent concentrates of such materials, no theoretical problem exists anymore.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  Also, the millions of source rock samples that have been analyzed for petroleum yield by the petroleum industry have confirmed the large quantities of petroleum found in sedimentary basins.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Empirical_evidence">Empirical evidence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiogenic_petroleum_origin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Empirical evidence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Occurrences of abiotic petroleum in commercial amounts in the oil wells in offshore Vietnam are sometimes cited, as well as in the <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Island_block_330_oil_field" title="Eugene Island block 330 oil field">Eugene Island block 330 oil field</a>, and the Dnieper-Donets Basin.  However, the origins of all these wells can also be explained with the biotic theory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glasby2006_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-glasby2006-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hook_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hook-2">[2]</a></sup> Modern geologists think that commercially profitable deposits of abiotic petroleum <i>could</i> be found, but no current deposit has convincing evidence that it originated from abiotic sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hook_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hook-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Soviet school saw evidence of their<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (December 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> hypothesis in the fact that some oil reservoirs exist in non-sedimentary rocks such as granite, metamorphic or porous volcanic rocks. However, opponents noted that non-sedimentary rocks served as reservoirs for biologically originated oil expelled from nearby sedimentary source rock through common migration or re-migration mechanisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hook_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Hook-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The following observations have been commonly used to argue for the abiogenic hypothesis, however each observation of actual petroleum can also be fully explained by biotic origin:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hook_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Hook-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lost_City_hydrothermal_vent_field">Lost City hydrothermal vent field</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiogenic_petroleum_origin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Lost City hydrothermal vent field">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lost_City_(hydrothermal_field)" title="Lost City (hydrothermal field)">Lost City</a> hydrothermal field was determined to have abiogenic hydrocarbon production.  Proskurowski et al. wrote, "Radiocarbon evidence rules out seawater bicarbonate as the carbon source for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fisher-Tropsch_process" title="Fisher-Tropsch process">FTT reactions</a>, suggesting that a mantle-derived inorganic carbon source is leached from the host rocks. Our findings illustrate that the abiotic synthesis of hydrocarbons in nature may occur in the presence of ultramafic rocks, water, and moderate amounts of heat."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Siljan_Ring_crater">Siljan Ring crater</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiogenic_petroleum_origin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Siljan Ring crater">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Siljan_Ring" title="Siljan Ring">Siljan Ring</a> meteorite crater, Sweden, was proposed by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Gold" title="Thomas Gold">Thomas Gold</a> as the most likely place to test the hypothesis because it was one of the few places in the world where the granite basement was cracked sufficiently (by meteorite impact) to allow oil to seep up from the mantle; furthermore it is infilled with a relatively thin veneer of sediment, which was sufficient to trap any abiogenic oil, but was modelled as not having been subjected to the heat and pressure conditions (known as the "oil window") normally required to create biogenic oil.  However, some geochemists concluded by geochemical analysis that the oil in the seeps came from the organic-rich <a href="/wiki/Ordovician" title="Ordovician">Ordovician</a> Tretaspis shale, where it was heated by the meteorite impact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1986–1990 The Gravberg-1 borehole was drilled through the deepest rock in the Siljan Ring in which proponents had hoped to find hydrocarbon reservoirs. It stopped at the depth of 6,800 metres (22,300 ft) due to drilling problems, after private investors spent $40 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kerr_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-kerr-37">[37]</a></sup> Some eighty barrels of magnetite paste and hydrocarbon-bearing sludge were recovered from the well; Gold maintained that the hydrocarbons were chemically different from, and not derived from, those added to the borehole, but analyses showed that the hydrocarbons were derived from the diesel fuel-based drilling fluid used in the drilling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kerr_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-kerr-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jeffrey_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-jeffrey-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-castano_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-castano-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jeffrey2_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-jeffrey2-40">[40]</a></sup> This well also sampled over 13,000 feet (4,000 m) of methane-bearing inclusions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1991–1992, a second borehole, Stenberg-1, was drilled a few miles away to a depth of 6,500 metres (21,300 ft), finding similar results. Again, no abiotic hydrocarbons were found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glasby2006_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-glasby2006-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mello2005_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-mello2005-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bacterial_mats">Bacterial mats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiogenic_petroleum_origin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Bacterial mats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Direct observation of bacterial mats and fracture-fill carbonate and humin of bacterial origin in deep boreholes in Australia are also taken as evidence for the abiogenic origin of petroleum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Example_proposed_abiogenic_methane_deposits">Example proposed abiogenic methane deposits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiogenic_petroleum_origin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Example proposed abiogenic methane deposits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Panhandle-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hugoton_Natural_Gas_Area" title="Hugoton Natural Gas Area">Hugoton field</a> (<a href="/wiki/Anadarko_Basin" title="Anadarko Basin">Anadarko Basin</a>) in the south-central United States is the most important gas field with commercial helium content. Some abiogenic proponents interpret this as evidence that both the helium and the natural gas came from the mantle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weinlich_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-Weinlich-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Polyak_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-Polyak-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lloyd_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lloyd-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/B%E1%BA%A1ch_H%E1%BB%95_oil_field" title="Bạch Hổ oil field">Bạch Hổ oil field</a> in <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> has been proposed as an example of abiogenic oil because it is 4,000 m of fractured basement granite, at a depth of 5,000 m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> However, others argue that it contains biogenic oil which leaked into the basement horst from conventional source rocks within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuu_Long" title="Cuu Long">Cuu Long</a> basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brown2005_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-brown2005-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p><p>A major component of mantle-derived carbon is indicated in commercial gas reservoirs in the <a href="/wiki/Pannonian_Basin" title="Pannonian Basin">Pannonian</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vienna_basin" title="Vienna basin">Vienna basins</a> of Hungary and Austria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p><p>Natural gas pools interpreted as being mantle-derived are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shengli_Field" title="Shengli Field">Shengli Field</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> and Songliao Basin, northeastern China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Chimaera gas seep, near <a href="/wiki/%C3%87%C4%B1ral%C4%B1" title="Çıralı">Çıralı</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antalya_Province" title="Antalya Province">Antalya</a> (southwest Turkey), has been continuously active for millennia and it is known to be the source of the first Olympic fire in the Hellenistic period. On the basis of chemical composition and isotopic analysis, the Chimaera gas is said to be about half biogenic and half abiogenic gas, the largest emission of biogenic methane discovered; deep and pressurized gas accumulations necessary to sustain the gas flow for millennia, posited to be from an inorganic source, may be present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hoşgörmez,_H._2008_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hoşgörmez,_H._2008-52">[52]</a></sup> Local geology of Chimaera flames, at exact position of flames, reveals contact between serpentinized ophiolite and carbonate rocks.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
Fischer-Tropsch process can be suitable reaction to form hydrocarbon gases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glasby2006_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-glasby2006-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geological_arguments">Geological arguments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiogenic_petroleum_origin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Geological arguments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Incidental_arguments_for_abiogenic_oil">Incidental arguments for abiogenic oil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiogenic_petroleum_origin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Incidental arguments for abiogenic oil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Given the known occurrence of methane and the probable catalysis of methane into higher atomic weight hydrocarbon molecules, various abiogenic theories consider the following to be key observations in support of abiogenic hypotheses:
</p>
<ul><li>the serpentinite synthesis, graphite synthesis and spinel catalysation models prove the process is viable<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-szatmari_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-szatmari-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-charlou2005_42-3"><a href="#cite_note-charlou2005-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li>the likelihood that abiogenic oil seeping up from the mantle is trapped beneath sediments which effectively seal mantle-tapping faults<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-keith2005_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-keith2005-41">[41]</a></sup></li>
<li>outdated<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> mass-balance calculations<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (December 2014)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> for supergiant oilfields which argued that the calculated source rock could not have supplied the reservoir with the known accumulation of oil, implying deep recharge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kudryavtsev_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kudryavtsev-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kudryavtsev2_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kudryavtsev2-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>the presence of hydrocarbons encapsulated in diamonds <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leung_et_al.,2005_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leung_et_al.,2005-66">[66]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The proponents of abiogenic oil also use several arguments which draw on a variety of natural phenomena in order to support the hypothesis:
</p>
<ul><li>the modeling of some researchers shows the Earth was accreted at relatively low temperature, thereby perhaps preserving primordial carbon deposits within the mantle, to drive abiogenic hydrocarbon production<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>the presence of methane within the gases and fluids of mid-ocean ridge spreading centre <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrothermal" title="Hydrothermal">hydrothermal</a> fields<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>the presence of diamond within <a href="/wiki/Kimberlite" title="Kimberlite">kimberlites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lamproite" title="Lamproite">lamproites</a> which sample the mantle depths proposed as being the source region of mantle methane (by Gold et al.).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldusgs_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-goldusgs-33">[33]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Incidental_arguments_against_abiogenic_oil">Incidental arguments against abiogenic oil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiogenic_petroleum_origin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Incidental arguments against abiogenic oil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:USGS_world_oil_endowment.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="551" data-file-width="1047" height="116" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/USGS_world_oil_endowment.png/220px-USGS_world_oil_endowment.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/USGS_world_oil_endowment.png/330px-USGS_world_oil_endowment.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/USGS_world_oil_endowment.png/440px-USGS_world_oil_endowment.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:USGS_world_oil_endowment.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Oil deposits are not directly associated with tectonic structures.</div></div></div>
<p>Arguments against chemical reactions, such as the serpentinite mechanism, being a source of hydrocarbon deposits within the crust include:
</p>
<ul><li><ul><li>this<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> is contradicted by numerous studies which have documented the existence of hydrologic systems operating over a range of scales and at all depths in the continental crust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></li></ul></li>
<li>the lack of any hydrocarbon within the crystalline shield<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> areas of the major <a href="/wiki/Craton" title="Craton">cratons</a>, especially around key deep seated structures which are predicted to host oil by the abiogenic hypothesis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mello2005_36-3"><a href="#cite_note-mello2005-36">[36]</a></sup> See <a href="/wiki/Siljan_(lake)#Deep_drilling_project" title="Siljan (lake)">Siljan Lake</a>.</li>
<li>lack of conclusive proof<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (December 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> that carbon isotope fractionation observed in crustal methane sources is entirely of abiogenic origin (Lollar et al. 2006)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lollar2006_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-lollar2006-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
<li>drilling of the Siljan Ring failed to find commercial quantities of oil,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mello2005_36-4"><a href="#cite_note-mello2005-36">[36]</a></sup> thus providing a counter example to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kudryavtsev%27s_Rule" title="Kudryavtsev's Rule">Kudryavtsev's Rule</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kerr_37-3"><a href="#cite_note-kerr-37">[37]</a></sup> and failing to locate the predicted abiogenic oil.</li>
<li>helium in the Siljan Gravberg-1 well was depleted in <a href="/wiki/Helium-3" title="Helium-3"><sup>3</sup>He</a> and not consistent with a mantle origin<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
<ul><li>The Gravberg-1 well only produced 84 barrels (13.4 m<sup>3</sup>) of oil, which later was shown to derive from organic additives, lubricants and mud used in the drilling process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kerr_37-4"><a href="#cite_note-kerr-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jeffrey_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-jeffrey-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-castano_39-2"><a href="#cite_note-castano-39">[39]</a></sup></li></ul></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kudryavtsev%27s_Rule" title="Kudryavtsev's Rule">Kudryavtsev's Rule</a> has been explained for oil and gas (not coal)—gas deposits which are below oil deposits can be created from that oil or its source rocks.  Because natural gas is less dense than oil, as kerogen and hydrocarbons are generating gas the gas fills the top of the available space.  Oil is forced down, and can reach the spill point where oil leaks around the edge(s) of the formation and flows upward.  If the original formation becomes completely filled with gas then all the oil will have leaked above the original location.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup></li>
<li>ubiquitous <a href="/wiki/Diamondoid" title="Diamondoid">diamondoids</a> in natural hydrocarbons such as oil, gas and condensates are composed of carbon from biological sources, unlike the carbon found in normal diamonds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraterrestrial_argument">Extraterrestrial argument</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiogenic_petroleum_origin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Extraterrestrial argument">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The presence of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> on Saturn's moon Titan and in the atmospheres of Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus and Neptune is cited as evidence of the formation of hydrocarbons without biological intermediate forms,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-glasby2006_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-glasby2006-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hook_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Hook-2">[2]</a></sup> for example by Thomas Gold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gold1999_5-13"><a href="#cite_note-gold1999-5">[5]</a></sup>  (Terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> is composed primarily of methane).  Some comets contain massive amounts of organic compounds, the equivalent of cubic kilometers of such mixed with other material;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IdahoNationalLab_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-IdahoNationalLab-71">[71]</a></sup> for instance, corresponding hydrocarbons were detected during a probe flyby through the tail of Comet Halley in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huebner1990_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-Huebner1990-72">[72]</a></sup>
Drill samples from the surface of <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> taken in 2015 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curiosity_Rover" title="Curiosity Rover"><i>Curiosity Rover's</i></a> <a href="/wiki/Mars_Science_Laboratory" title="Mars Science Laboratory">Mars Science Laboratory</a> have found organic molecules of <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a> in 3 billion year old rock samples in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gale_Crater" title="Gale Crater">Gale Crater</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt20180607_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt20180607-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p>
