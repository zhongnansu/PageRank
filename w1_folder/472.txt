<p><b>Lake Kivu</b> is one of the <a href="/wiki/African_Great_Lakes" title="African Great Lakes">African Great Lakes</a>. It lies on the border between the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rwanda" title="Rwanda">Rwanda</a>, and is in the <a href="/wiki/Albertine_Rift" title="Albertine Rift">Albertine Rift</a>, the western branch of the <a href="/wiki/East_African_Rift" title="East African Rift">East African Rift</a>. Lake Kivu empties into the <a href="/wiki/Ruzizi_River" title="Ruzizi River">Ruzizi River</a>, which flows southwards into <a href="/wiki/Lake_Tanganyika" title="Lake Tanganyika">Lake Tanganyika</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Kivu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lake Kivu is approximately 90 km (56 mi) long and 50 km (31 mi) at its widest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-readersnatural_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-readersnatural-2">[2]</a></sup> Its irregular shape makes measuring its precise surface area difficult; it has been estimated to cover a total surface area of some 2,700 km<sup>2</sup> (1,040 sq mi).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The surface of the lake sits at a height of 1,460 metres (4,790 ft) above sea level.This lake has a chance of erupting every 1000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-readersnatural_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-readersnatural-2">[2]</a></sup> The lake has a maximum depth of 475 m (1,558 ft) and a mean depth of 220 m (722 ft), making it the world's eighteenth <a href="/wiki/List_of_lakes_by_depth" title="List of lakes by depth">deepest lake</a> by maximum depth, and the ninth deepest by mean depth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-readersnatural_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-readersnatural-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some 1,370 km<sup>2</sup> or 58 percent of the lake's waters lie within DRC borders.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The lake bed sits upon a <a href="/wiki/Rift_valley" title="Rift valley">rift valley</a> that is slowly being pulled apart, causing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic_activity" title="Volcanic activity">volcanic activity</a> in the area. 
</p><p>The world's tenth-largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_islands_in_lakes" title="List of islands in lakes">island on a lake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Idjwi" title="Idjwi">Idjwi</a>, lies in Lake Kivu,  within the boundaries of <a href="/wiki/Virunga_National_Park" title="Virunga National Park">Virunga National Park</a>. Settlements on the lake's shore include <a href="/wiki/Bukavu" title="Bukavu">Bukavu</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kabare" title="Kabare">Kabare</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kalehe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kalehe (page does not exist)">Kalehe</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sake,_DRC" title="Sake, DRC">Sake</a> and <a href="/wiki/Goma" title="Goma">Goma</a> in Congo, and <a href="/wiki/Gisenyi" title="Gisenyi">Gisenyi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kibuye,_Rwanda" title="Kibuye, Rwanda">Kibuye</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cyangugu" title="Cyangugu">Cyangugu</a> in Rwanda.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Kivu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lake Kivu is a <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">fresh water</a> lake and, along with Cameroonian <a href="/wiki/Lake_Nyos" title="Lake Nyos">Lake Nyos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Monoun" title="Lake Monoun">Lake Monoun</a>, is one of three that are known to undergo <a href="/wiki/Limnic_eruption" title="Limnic eruption">limnic eruptions</a>. Around the lake, geologists found evidence of massive local extinctions about every thousand years, presumably caused by outgassing events. The trigger for lake overturns is unknown in Lake Kivu's case, but volcanic activity is suspected. The gaseous chemical composition of exploding lakes is unique to each lake, in Lake Kivu's case, <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> and carbon dioxide due to lake water interaction with a volcano.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Physiochemical_characteristics_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Physiochemical_characteristics-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The amount of methane is estimated to be 65 cubic kilometres. If burnt over one year, it would give an average <a href="/wiki/Power_(physics)" title="Power (physics)">power</a> of about 100 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigawatt" title="Gigawatt">gigawatts</a> for the whole period. There is also an estimated 256 cubic kilometers of carbon dioxide. The water temperature is 24 °C, and the <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> level is about 8.6.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Physiochemical_characteristics_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Physiochemical_characteristics-3">[3]</a></sup> The methane is reported to be produced by microbial reduction of the volcanic CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> A future overturn and gas release from the deep waters of Lake Kivu would result in catastrophe, dwarfing the historically documented lake overturns at Lakes Nyos and Monoun. The lives of the approximately two million people who live in the lake basin area would be in jeopardy.
</p><p>Cores from the Bukavu Bay area of the lake reveal that the bottom has layered deposits of the rare mineral <a href="/wiki/Monohydrocalcite" title="Monohydrocalcite">monohydrocalcite</a> interlain with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diatoms" title="Diatoms">diatoms</a>, on top of <a href="/wiki/Sapropel" title="Sapropel">sapropelic</a> sediments with high <a href="/wiki/Pyrite" title="Pyrite">pyrite</a> content. These are found at three different intervals. The sapropelic layers are believed to be related to hydrothermal discharge and the diatoms to a bloom which reduced the carbon dioxide levels low enough to precipitiate monohydrocalcite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Scientists hypothesise that sufficient volcanic interaction with the lake's bottom water that has high gas concentrations would heat water, force the methane out of the water, spark a methane explosion, and trigger a nearly simultaneous release of carbon dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solidarites_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-solidarites-7">[7]</a></sup> The carbon dioxide would then suffocate large numbers of people in the lake basin as the gases roll off the lake surface. It is also possible that the lake could spawn <a href="/wiki/Tsunamis_in_lakes" title="Tsunamis in lakes">lake tsunamis</a> as gas explodes out of it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rwanda_and_DRC_Sign_Agreement_Over_L._Kivu_Methane_Gas_Exploration_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rwanda_and_DRC_Sign_Agreement_Over_L._Kivu_Methane_Gas_Exploration-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Killer_Lakes_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Killer_Lakes-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The risk posed by Lake Kivu began to be understood during the analysis of more recent events at Lake Nyos. Lake Kivu's methane was originally thought to be merely a cheap natural resource for export, and for the generation of cheap power. Once the mechanisms that caused lake overturns began to be understood, so did awareness of the risk the lake posed to the local population.
</p><p>An experimental vent pipe was installed at Lake Nyos in 2001 to remove gas from the deep water, but such a solution for the much larger Lake Kivu would be considerably more expensive. No plan has been initiated to reduce the risk posed by Lake Kivu.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (January 2013)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata"> – <a href="/wiki/Talk:Lake_Kivu#Dubious" title="Talk:Lake Kivu">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> The approximately 500 million tonnes of carbon dioxide in the lake is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_in_the_Earth%27s_atmosphere#Concentration" title="Carbon dioxide in the Earth's atmosphere">a little under 2 percent</a> of the amount released annually by human fossil fuel burning. Therefore, the process of releasing it could potentially have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_trade" title="Carbon trade">costs</a> beyond building and operating the system.
</p><p>This problem associated with the prevalence of methane is that of <a href="/wiki/Mazuku" title="Mazuku">mazuku</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Swahili_language" title="Swahili language">Swahili</a> term "evil wind" for the outgassing of methane and carbon dioxide that kills people and animals, and can even kill vegetation when in high enough concentration.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Methane_extraction">Methane extraction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Kivu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Methane extraction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Methane_extraction_platform_at_Lake_Kivu.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="589" data-file-width="640" height="202" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Methane_extraction_platform_at_Lake_Kivu.jpg/220px-Methane_extraction_platform_at_Lake_Kivu.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Methane_extraction_platform_at_Lake_Kivu.jpg/330px-Methane_extraction_platform_at_Lake_Kivu.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Methane_extraction_platform_at_Lake_Kivu.jpg/440px-Methane_extraction_platform_at_Lake_Kivu.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Methane_extraction_platform_at_Lake_Kivu.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A methane extraction platform</div></div></div>
<p>Lake Kivu has recently been found to contain approximately 55 billion cubic metres (1.94 trillion cubic feet) of dissolved <a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">biogas</a> at a depth of 300 metres (1,000 ft). Until 2004, extraction of the gas was done on a small scale, with the extracted gas being used to run boilers at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bralirwa" title="Bralirwa">Bralirwa brewery</a> in <a href="/wiki/Gisenyi" title="Gisenyi">Gisenyi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-powerhouse_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-powerhouse-12">[12]</a></sup> As far as large-scale exploitation of this resource is concerned, the Rwandan government has negotiated with a number of parties to produce methane from the lake.
</p><p>In 2011 ContourGlobal, a UK-based energy company focused on emerging markets, secured project financing to initiate a large-scale methane extraction project. The project is run through a local Rwandan entity called <a href="/wiki/KivuWatt_Power_Station" title="KivuWatt Power Station">KivuWatt</a>, using an offshore barge platform to extract, separate, and clean the gasses obtained from the lake bed before pumping purified methane via an underwater pipeline to on-shore <a href="/wiki/Gas_engine" title="Gas engine">gas engines</a>. Stage one of the project, powering three "gensets" along the lake shore and supplying 26 <a href="/wiki/Watt#Megawatt" title="Watt">MW</a> of electricity to the local grid, has since been completed. The next phase aims to deploy nine additional "gensets" at 75 MW to create a total capacity of over 100 MW.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-contourglobal_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-contourglobal-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition, Symbion Power Lake Kivu Limited was awarded a Concession and Power Producing Agreement (PPA) in 2015, to produce 50MW of power using the Lake Kivu methane resource.  The project is expected to commence construction in 2019, with first power (Phase 1 - 14MW) to be produced in first quarter 2020. The plant will be fully operational in 2021.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Symbion Power has purchased another concession for a further 25MW and are currently negotiating a PPA with the Rwanda Electricity Group which may see 8MW of power despatched to the Grid six months after the PPA has been signed.  This concession is on the site of the original pilot plant known as KP1. 
</p><p>In addition to managing gas extraction, KivuWatt will also manage the electrical generation plants and on-sell the electrical power to the Rwandan government under the terms of a long-term Power Purchase Agreement (PPA). This allows KivuWatt to control a vertically integrated energy offering from point of extraction to point of sale into the local grid. Extraction is said to be cost-effective and relatively simple because once the gas-rich water is pumped up, the dissolved gases (primarily carbon dioxide, hydrogen sulphide and methane) begin to bubble out as the water pressure gets lower. This project is expected to increase Rwanda's energy generation capability by as much as 20 times, and will enable Rwanda to sell electricity to neighbouring African countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-powerhouse_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-powerhouse-12">[12]</a></sup> The firm was awarded the 2011 Africa Power deal of the year for innovation in the financing arrangements it obtained from various sources for the KivuWatt project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The $200 million power plant was operating at 26 MW in 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology_and_fisheries">Biology and fisheries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Kivu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Biology and fisheries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lake_Kivu,_boats.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1288" data-file-width="1936" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Lake_Kivu%2C_boats.jpg/300px-Lake_Kivu%2C_boats.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Lake_Kivu%2C_boats.jpg/450px-Lake_Kivu%2C_boats.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Lake_Kivu%2C_boats.jpg/600px-Lake_Kivu%2C_boats.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lake_Kivu,_boats.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fishing boats on Lake Kivu, 2009</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:View_of_the_sky_and_its_reflection_on_Lake_Kivu.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3744" data-file-width="5616" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/View_of_the_sky_and_its_reflection_on_Lake_Kivu.jpg/300px-View_of_the_sky_and_its_reflection_on_Lake_Kivu.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/View_of_the_sky_and_its_reflection_on_Lake_Kivu.jpg/450px-View_of_the_sky_and_its_reflection_on_Lake_Kivu.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/View_of_the_sky_and_its_reflection_on_Lake_Kivu.jpg/600px-View_of_the_sky_and_its_reflection_on_Lake_Kivu.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:View_of_the_sky_and_its_reflection_on_Lake_Kivu.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The sky reflected on Lake Kivu</div></div></div>
<p>The fish fauna in Lake Kivu is relatively poor with 28 described species, including four <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">introduced species</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FishSnoeks_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FishSnoeks-18">[18]</a></sup> The natives are the <a href="/wiki/Lake_Rukwa_minnow" title="Lake Rukwa minnow">Lake Rukwa minnow</a> (<i>Raiamas moorii</i>), four species of barb (<a href="/wiki/Ripon_barbel" title="Ripon barbel">Ripon barbel</a>, <i>Barbus altianalis</i>, <a href="/wiki/East_African_red-finned_barb" title="East African red-finned barb">East African red-finned barb</a>, <i>Enteromius. apleurogramma</i>, <a href="/wiki/Redspot_barb" title="Redspot barb">Redspot barb</a>, <i>Enteromius kerstenii</i> and <a href="/wiki/Pellegrin%27s_barb" title="Pellegrin's barb">Pellegrin's barb</a>, <i>Enteromius pellegrini</i>), an <i><a href="/wiki/Amphilius" title="Amphilius">Amphilius</a></i> catfish, two <i><a href="/wiki/Clarias" title="Clarias">Clarias</a></i> catfish (<i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Clarias_liocephalus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Clarias liocephalus (page does not exist)">C. liocephalus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Clarias_gariepinus" title="Clarias gariepinus">C. gariepinus</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Nile_tilapia" title="Nile tilapia">Nile tilapia</a> (<i>Oreochromis niloticus</i>) and 15 endemic <i><a href="/wiki/Haplochromis" title="Haplochromis">Haplochromis</a></i> cichlids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FishSnoeks_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-FishSnoeks-18">[18]</a></sup> Another c. 20 possibly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Undescribed_species" title="Undescribed species">undescribed species</a> of cichlids are known from the lake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The introduced species are three cichlids, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Longfin_tilapia" title="Longfin tilapia">longfin tilapia</a> (<i>Oreochromis macrochir</i>), <i><a href="/wiki/Oreochromis_leucostictus" title="Oreochromis leucostictus">O. leucostictus</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Redbreast_tilapia" title="Redbreast tilapia">redbreast tilapia</a> (<i>Coptodon rendalli</i>), and a clupeid, the <a href="/wiki/Lake_Tanganyika_sardine" title="Lake Tanganyika sardine">Lake Tanganyika sardine</a>, <i>Limnothrissa miodon</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FishSnoeks_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-FishSnoeks-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>The exploitable stock of the Lake Tanganyika sardine was estimated at 2000–4000 tons per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> It was introduced to Lake Kivu in the late 1959 by a Belgian Engineer A. Collart. At present, Lake Kivu is the sole natural lake in which <i>L. miodon</i>, a sardine originally restricted to Lake Tanganyika, has been introduced initially to fill an empty niche. Prior to the introduction, no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planktivorous" title="Planktivorous">planktivorous</a> fish was present in the pelagic waters of Lake Kivu. In the early 1990s, the number of fishers on the lake was 6,563, of which 3,027 were associated with the pelagic fishery and 3,536 with the traditional fishery. Widespread armed conflict in the surrounding region from the mid-1990s resulted in a decline in the fisheries harvest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Following this introduction, the sardine has gained substantial economic and nutritional importance for the lakeside human population but from an ecosystem standpoint, the introduction of planktivorous fish may result in important modifications of plankton community structure. Recent observations showed the disappearance during the last decades of a large grazer, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Daphnia_curvirostris&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Daphnia curvirostris (page does not exist)">Daphnia curvirostris</a></i>, and the dominance of mesozooplankton community by three species of cyclopoid copepod: <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thermocyclops_consimilis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thermocyclops consimilis (page does not exist)">Thermocyclops consimilis</a></i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mesocyclops_aequatorialis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mesocyclops aequatorialis (page does not exist)">Mesocyclops aequatorialis</a></i> and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tropocyclops_confinis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tropocyclops confinis (page does not exist)">Tropocyclops confinis</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first comprehensive <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a> survey was released in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sarmento_2006_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sarmento_2006-24">[24]</a></sup> With an annual average chlorophyll a in the mixed layer of 2.2 mg m<sup>−3</sup> and low nutrient levels in the euphotic zone, the lake is clearly <a href="/wiki/Trophic_state_index" title="Trophic state index">oligotrophic</a>. Diatoms are the dominant group in the lake, particularly during the dry season episodes of deep mixing. During the rainy season, the stratified water column, with high light and lower nutrient availability, favour dominance of cyanobacteria with high numbers of phototrophic picoplankton.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sarmento_2006_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sarmento_2006-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The actual primary production is 0.71 g C m<sup>−2</sup> d<sup>−1</sup> (≈ 260 g C m<sup>−2</sup> a<sup>−1</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>A study of evolutionary genetics showed that the cichlids from lakes in northern Virunga (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Lake_Edward" title="Lake Edward">Edward</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake_George_(Uganda)" title="Lake George (Uganda)">George</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Victoria" title="Lake Victoria">Victoria</a>) would have evolved in a "proto-lake Kivu", much older than the intense volcanic activity (20,000-25,000 years ago) which cut the connection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The elevation of the mountains west of the lake (which is currently the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kahuzi-Biega_National_Park" title="Kahuzi-Biega National Park">Kahuzi-Biega National Park</a>, one of the largest reserves of eastern lowland (or Grauer's) gorillas in the world), combined with the elevation of the eastern rift (located in eastern Rwanda) would be responsible for drainage of water from central Rwanda in the actual Lake Kivu. This concept of "proto-lake Kivu" was challenged by lack of consistent geological evidence,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> although the cichlid's molecular clock suggests the existence of a lake much older than the commonly cited 15,000 years.
</p><p>Lake Kivu is the home of four species of <a href="/wiki/Freshwater_crab" title="Freshwater crab">freshwater crab</a>, including two non-endemics (<i><a href="/wiki/Potamonautes_lirrangensis" title="Potamonautes lirrangensis">Potamonautes lirrangensis</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Potamonautes_mutandensis" title="Potamonautes mutandensis">P. mutandensis</a></i>) and two endemics (<i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Potamonautes_bourgaultae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Potamonautes bourgaultae (page does not exist)">P. bourgaultae</a></i> and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Potamonautes_idjwiensis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Potamonautes idjwiensis (page does not exist)">P. idjwiensis</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crabs_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-crabs-31">[31]</a></sup> Among <a href="/wiki/Rift_Valley_lakes" title="Rift Valley lakes">Rift Valley lakes</a>, Lake Tanganyika is the only other with endemic freshwater crabs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crabs_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-crabs-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
