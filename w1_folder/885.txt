<p><b>Boundary Dam Power Station</b> is the largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_power_plant" title="Fossil fuel power plant">coal fired station</a> owned by <a href="/wiki/SaskPower" title="SaskPower">SaskPower</a>, located near <a href="/wiki/Estevan" title="Estevan">Estevan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boundary_Dam_Power_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Boundary Dam Power Station consists of<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> two 62 net MW units (commissioned in 1959); two 139 net MW units (commissioned in 1970), one of which—Unit 3—was decommissioned and replaced with a new 160 MW unit in 2013;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> one 139 net MW unit (commissioned in 1973); and one 273 net MW unit (commissioned in 1978).
</p><p>The boilers are supplied by  <a href="/wiki/Babcock_%26_Wilcox" title="Babcock &amp; Wilcox">Babcock &amp; Wilcox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Combustion_Engineering" title="Combustion Engineering">Combustion Engineering</a> while the turbines/generator are supplied by <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hitachi_Canadian_Industries" title="Hitachi Canadian Industries">Hitachi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
There is also  <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Boundary_Dam_(Saskatchewan)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Boundary Dam (Saskatchewan) (page does not exist)">Boundary Dam</a> (constructed in 1957) located next to the station. It is an earth fill dam, which created the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Boundary_Reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Boundary Reservoir (page does not exist)">Boundary Reservoir</a> on <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Long_Creek_(Saskatchewan)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Long Creek (Saskatchewan) (page does not exist)">Long Creek</a> a few kilometres west of the river's mouth on the <a href="/wiki/Souris_River" title="Souris River">Souris River</a>. The station uses water from the <a href="/wiki/Reservoir" title="Reservoir">reservoir</a> for <a href="/wiki/Coolant" title="Coolant">coolant</a>, which is why Boundary Reservoir is the only body of water in Saskatchewan that doesn't freeze over during the winter months and supports largemouth bass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The lake's <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> species include <a href="/wiki/Walleye" title="Walleye">walleye</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yellow_perch" title="Yellow perch">yellow perch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_pike" title="Northern pike">northern pike</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burbot" title="Burbot">burbot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Largemouth_bass" title="Largemouth bass">largemouth bass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Smallmouth_bass" title="Smallmouth bass">smallmouth bass</a> and <a href="/wiki/White_sucker" title="White sucker">white sucker</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The plant has five 300 ft (91.44 m) tall smokestacks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> SaskPower has advised that the project has a 30-year life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_Capture_and_Storage_Demonstration_Project">Carbon Capture and Storage Demonstration Project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boundary_Dam_Power_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Carbon Capture and Storage Demonstration Project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="external text" href="http://www.saskpowerccs.com/The" rel="nofollow">Boundary Dam Integrated Carbon Capture and Storage Demonstration</a> is a project to retrofit the <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a>-fired Unit #3 with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture</a> and an <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">enhanced oil recovery</a> system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It consists of two distinct parts: Repowering of the existing Unit #3 and the installation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_Dioxide" title="Carbon Dioxide">Carbon Dioxide</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulphur_Dioxide" title="Sulphur Dioxide">Sulphur Dioxide</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxides" title="Nitrogen oxides">NOx</a> capture facility. Its primary purpose is to provide a low-cost source of carbon dioxide, to the mature <a href="/wiki/Weyburn-Midale_Carbon_Dioxide_Project" title="Weyburn-Midale Carbon Dioxide Project">Weyburn Oil Field</a>, for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enhanced_Oil_Recovery" title="Enhanced Oil Recovery">Enhanced Oil Recovery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It is expected to result in a 90 percent (1 million tonnes/year) reduction in CO2 emissions and will also reduce the output of Unit 3 from 139 MW to 110 MW.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbc.ca_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-cbc.ca-10">[10]</a></sup> However, critics point out that the 90% figure refers to the percentage of total CO2 emissions captured, and that only about a half of this CO2 will be actually be permanently stored. The remainder is released into the atmosphere during capturing, and the processing in the oil field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grist.org_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-grist.org-11">[11]</a></sup> The retrofitted unit was officially opened in October 2014,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and is the world's first commercial-scale, <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a>-fired power plant equipped with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a> technology<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2015, internal documents from SaskPower revealed that there were "serious design issues" in the carbon capture system, resulting in  regular breakdowns and maintenance problems that led the unit to only be operational 40% of the time. SNC-Lavalin had been contracted to engineer, procure, and build the facility, and the documents asserted that it "has neither the will or the ability to fix some of these fundamental flaws."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leo2015a_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leo2015a-15">[15]</a></sup> The low productivity of the plant had in turn meant that SaskPower was only able to sell half of the 800,000 tonnes of captured carbon dioxide that it had contracted to sell to <a href="/wiki/Cenovus_Energy" title="Cenovus Energy">Cenovus Energy</a> for use in <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">enhanced oil recovery</a> at a cost of $25 per tonne. In addition to the lost sales, this meant that SaskPower had been forced to pay Cenovus $12 million in penalties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leo2015b_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leo2015b-16">[16]</a></sup> In June 2016, and to avoid paying a $91-million penalty, SaskPower renegotiated its CO2 supply contract with Cenovus. That renegotiation means annual revenues are reduced from $25-million to "$16-17-million". Over the 30-year project life this represents reduced revenue of $240-million to $270-million and will further weaken the project economics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Cenovus sold its Saskatchewan interests in 2017 to Whitecap Resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whitecap_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whitecap-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Several major publications have noted what they deem poor economics of the project, among them <i>The Economist</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <i>The Financial Times</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> <i>The MIT Technology Review</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and <i>The New York Times</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> An April 2016 Parliamentary Budget Office report found that CCS at Boundary Dam doubles the price of electricity. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Financial concerns appear to fall into three main areas: 
</p>
<ul><li>negative <a href="/wiki/Earnings_before_interest,_taxes,_depreciation,_and_amortization" title="Earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization">earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization</a>, in part due to the pioneering nature of the project;.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li>the existence of cheaper alternatives, such as wind turbines, which urgently need to be developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grist.org_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-grist.org-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li>the potential for substantial losses for initial investors (Saskatchewan taxpayers and SaskPower ratepayers), in excess of $1-billion <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grist.org_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-grist.org-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Among environmental concerns, critics note that while 90% of CO2 smokestack emissions are indeed <i>captured</i>, only a portion is actually <i>stored.</i> Almost half of captured emissions end up being released in the atmosphere by processing in the oil field and the capturing process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saskwind.ca_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-saskwind.ca-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Among other concerns, critics point to the following: 
</p>
<ul><li>The foremost goal of the CCS project is to keep the aging plant from becoming a <a href="/wiki/Stranded_asset" title="Stranded asset">stranded asset</a>. "Unless SaskPower outfits the boilers of the Boundary Dam station with CCS, they have to shut down by 2020"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grist.org_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-grist.org-11">[11]</a></sup> due to Canadian federal regulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li>The unfair sharing of the costs, risks and benefits: Energy consumers are said to be saddled with a "very expensive carbon tax", while Saskatchewan's aging oil industry, namely Weyburn Consortium (formerly headed by Cenovus Energy), which owned the Weyburn oil field until it was sold in 2017<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whitecap_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Whitecap-18">[18]</a></sup> receives a "backdoor subsidy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grist.org_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-grist.org-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>All of these concerns are compounded by a lack of transparency: As of 2015 the project operators had not presented any financial or environmental performance numbers.
</p><p>In July 2018, SaskPower announced that it would not retrofit Units 4 and 5 with CCS, with minister responsible <a href="/wiki/Dustin_Duncan" title="Dustin Duncan">Dustin Duncan</a> saying that the units were approaching their mandated shut down in 2024 and that natural gas is a cheaper option.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Capital_cost">Capital cost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boundary_Dam_Power_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Capital cost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In February 2008 the <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a> government announced that it would proceed with a 7-year reconstruction and repowering of Boundary Dam's Unit 3. The initial project cost was <a href="/wiki/Canadian_dollar" title="Canadian dollar">$C</a> 1.24 billion<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> out of which $C 240-million was from the federal government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbc.ca_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-cbc.ca-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Budget over-runs have led to the plant having a total cost of some $C 1.5 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Revenue">Revenue</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boundary_Dam_Power_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Revenue">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Other than the generation of electricity, the plant will have several by-products which will generate revenue.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dry_Ice_Pellets_Subliming.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="523" data-file-width="609" height="249" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Dry_Ice_Pellets_Subliming.jpg/290px-Dry_Ice_Pellets_Subliming.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Dry_Ice_Pellets_Subliming.jpg/435px-Dry_Ice_Pellets_Subliming.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Dry_Ice_Pellets_Subliming.jpg/580px-Dry_Ice_Pellets_Subliming.jpg 2x" width="290"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dry_Ice_Pellets_Subliming.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sample of solid carbon dioxide or "dry ice" pellets</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_dioxide">Carbon dioxide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boundary_Dam_Power_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Carbon dioxide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The facility will capture 90 percent of total carbon dioxide emissions from Unit 3<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SaskPower_Boundary_Dam_Project_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-SaskPower_Boundary_Dam_Project-37">[37]</a></sup> or one million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tonnes" title="Tonnes">tonnes</a> annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>Most of the total annual carbon dioxide capture volume, at least for the first ten years of the project, was sold to Alberta-based Cenovus Energy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> the operator of the Weyburn Oil Field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> SaskPower has not publicly disclosed the sale price but <a href="/wiki/Bill_Boyd_(Canadian_politician)" title="Bill Boyd (Canadian politician)">Minister Boyd</a> has stated that it is "in the range of" $25 per tonne.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Annual revenue from carbon dioxide sales is thus estimated at $25 million. <a href="/wiki/Cenovus_Energy" title="Cenovus Energy">Cenovus Energy</a> used the carbon dioxide mainly for field injection work to increase <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a> production before its Saskatchewan operations were sold to Whitecap Resources<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whitecap_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Whitecap-18">[18]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sulphuric_acid">Sulphuric acid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boundary_Dam_Power_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sulphuric acid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulphur" title="Sulphur">sulphur</a> emissions from the power station (predominantly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulphur_dioxide" title="Sulphur dioxide">sulphur dioxide</a>) will be captured,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SaskPower_Boundary_Dam_Project_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-SaskPower_Boundary_Dam_Project-37">[37]</a></sup> converted to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulphuric_acid" title="Sulphuric acid">sulphuric acid</a> and sold. SaskPower sulphur dioxide emissions are approximately 5 tonnes per gigawatt hour of system generation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Given annual electricity generation of 1,010 gigawatt hours, it follows that total annual sulphur capture will be 5,050 tonnes.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fly_ash">Fly ash</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boundary_Dam_Power_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fly ash">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fly_ash" title="Fly ash">Fly ash</a>, also known as flue ash, is one of the residues generated following the combustion of coal and lignite. In the past it was simply released into the atmosphere through the smoke stack but pollution control requirements have put an end to that practice. It is now captured by electrostatic precipitators and stored at the point of generation, placed in landfills or recycled - where it will typically replace <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portland_Cement" title="Portland Cement">Portland Cement</a> in <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a> production. Typical fly ash recovery rates, for Saskatchewan lignite-fired power stations, are 31 kilograms per megawatt hour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Fly ash, depending on its use, can attract a range of prices. The best arises from concrete-quality ash which fetches $22–$49 per tonne.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Expenses">Expenses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boundary_Dam_Power_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Expenses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There appear to be two main expense items: Operations and maintenance, and <a href="/wiki/Parasitic_load" title="Parasitic load">parasitic load</a>. In the latter, carbon and sulphur dioxide <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amine</a>-based capture units, such as the one at Boundary Dam, consume significant amounts of electricity and heat in operation and the largest single component is associated with solvent regeneration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Significant electrical load is also used to run additional emission control components (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxides" title="Nitrogen oxides">NOx</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">Mercury</a> capture) as well as associated pumps and other equipment.
</p>
