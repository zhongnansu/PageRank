<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Partial pressure</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none"> Pressure attributed to a component gas in a mixture </div>
<p>In a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gases</a>, each gas has a <b>partial pressure</b> which is the notional <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> of that gas if it alone occupied the entire <a href="/wiki/Volume" title="Volume">volume</a> of the original mixture at the same <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The total pressure of an <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas" title="Ideal gas">ideal gas</a> mixture is the sum of the partial pressures of the gases in the mixture.
</p><p>The partial pressure of a gas is a measure of thermodynamic activity of the gas's <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a>.  Gases dissolve, diffuse, and react according to their partial pressures, and not according to their <a href="/wiki/Concentration" title="Concentration">concentrations</a> in gas mixtures or liquids. This general property of gases is also true in chemical reactions of gases in biology. For example, the necessary amount of oxygen for human respiration, and the amount that is toxic, is set by the partial pressure of oxygen alone. This is true across a very wide range of different concentrations of oxygen present in various inhaled breathing gases or dissolved in blood.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The partial pressures of oxygen and carbon dioxide are important parameters in tests of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arterial_blood_gas" title="Arterial blood gas">arterial blood gases</a>, but can also be measured in, for example, <a href="/wiki/Cerebrospinal_fluid" title="Cerebrospinal fluid">cerebrospinal fluid</a>.
</p>

<h2><span id="Dalton.27s_law_of_partial_pressures"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Dalton's_law_of_partial_pressures">Dalton's law of partial pressures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Partial_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dalton's law of partial pressures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dalton%27s_law" title="Dalton's law">Dalton's law</a></div>
<p>Dalton's law expresses the fact that the total pressure of a mixture of gases is equal to the sum of the partial pressures of the individual gases in the mixture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This equality arises from the fact that in an ideal gas the molecules are so far apart that they do not interact with each other. Most actual real-world gases come very close to this ideal. <div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vapor_pressure_chart.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="512" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Vapor_pressure_chart.svg/220px-Vapor_pressure_chart.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Vapor_pressure_chart.svg/330px-Vapor_pressure_chart.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Vapor_pressure_chart.svg/440px-Vapor_pressure_chart.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vapor_pressure_chart.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A log-lin vapor pressure chart for various liquids</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Vapor_pressure" title="Vapor pressure">Vapor pressure</a> is the pressure of a <a href="/wiki/Vapor" title="Vapor">vapor</a> in equilibrium with its non-vapor phases (i.e., liquid or solid). Most often the term is used to describe a <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a>'s tendency to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evaporate" title="Evaporate">evaporate</a>.  It is a measure of the tendency of <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> to escape from a liquid or a <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solid</a>. A liquid's atmospheric pressure boiling point corresponds to the temperature at which its vapor pressure is equal to the surrounding atmospheric pressure and it is often called the <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Normal_boiling_point" title="Normal boiling point">normal boiling point</a></b>.
</p><p>The higher the vapor pressure of a liquid at a given temperature, the lower the normal boiling point of the liquid.
</p><p>The vapor pressure chart displayed has graphs of the vapor pressures versus temperatures for a variety of liquids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> As can be seen in the chart, the liquids with the highest vapor pressures have the lowest normal boiling points.
</p><p>For example, at any given temperature, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methyl_chloride" title="Methyl chloride">methyl chloride</a> has the highest vapor pressure of any of the liquids in the chart. It also has the lowest normal boiling point (-24.2 °C), which is where the vapor pressure curve of methyl chloride (the blue line) intersects the horizontal pressure line of one atmosphere (<a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">atm</a>) of absolute vapor pressure. Note that at higher altitudes, the atmospheric pressure is less than that at sea level, so boiling points of liquids are reduced. At the top of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">Mount Everest</a>, the atmospheric pressure is approximately 0.333 atm, so by using the graph, the boiling point of <a href="/wiki/Diethyl_ether" title="Diethyl ether">Diethyl ether</a> would be approximately 7.5 °C versus 34.6 °C at sea level (1 atm).
</p>

<p>For reversible reactions, changes in the total pressure, temperature or reactant concentrations will shift the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_equilibrium" title="Chemical equilibrium">equilibrium</a> so as to favor either the right or left side of the reaction in accordance with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Le_Chatelier%27s_Principle" title="Le Chatelier's Principle">Le Chatelier's Principle</a>. However, the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_kinetics" title="Chemical kinetics">reaction kinetics</a> may either oppose or enhance the equilibrium shift. In some cases, the reaction kinetics may be the overriding factor to consider.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_diving_breathing_gases">In diving breathing gases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Partial_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In diving breathing gases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Underwater_diving" title="Underwater diving">underwater diving</a> the physiological effects of individual component gases of <a href="/wiki/Breathing_gas" title="Breathing gas">breathing gases</a> is a function of partial pressure.
</p><p>Using diving terms, partial pressure is calculated as:
</p>
<dl><dd><b>partial pressure = (total absolute pressure) × (volume fraction of gas component)</b></dd></dl>
<p>For the component gas "i":
</p>
<dl><dd><b>pp<sub>i</sub> = P × F<sub>i</sub></b></dd></dl>
<p>For example, at 50 metres (164 ft) underwater, the total absolute pressure is 6 bar (600 kPa) (i.e., 1 bar of <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> + 5 bar of water pressure) and the partial pressures of the main components of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">air</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> 21% by volume and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> approximately 79% by volume are:
</p>
<dl><dd><b>ppN<sub>2</sub></b> = 6 bar × 0.79 = 4.7 bar absolute</dd>
<dd><b>ppO<sub>2</sub></b> = 6 bar × 0.21 = 1.3 bar absolute</dd></dl>

<p>The minimum safe lower limit for the partial pressures of oxygen in a gas mixture is 0.16 bars (16 kPa) absolute. <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(medical)" title="Hypoxia (medical)">Hypoxia</a> and sudden unconsciousness becomes a problem with an oxygen partial pressure of less than 0.16 bar absolute.  <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_toxicity" title="Oxygen toxicity">Oxygen toxicity</a>, involving convulsions, becomes a problem when oxygen partial pressure is too high.  The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">NOAA</a> Diving Manual recommends a maximum single exposure of 45 minutes at 1.6 bar absolute, of 120 minutes at 1.5 bar absolute, of 150 minutes at 1.4 bar absolute, of 180 minutes at 1.3 bar absolute and of 210 minutes at 1.2 bar absolute. Oxygen toxicity becomes a risk when these oxygen partial pressures and exposures are exceeded. The partial pressure of oxygen determines the <a href="/wiki/Maximum_operating_depth" title="Maximum operating depth">maximum operating depth</a> of a gas mixture.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_narcosis" title="Nitrogen narcosis">Narcosis</a> is a problem when breathing gases at high pressure. Typically, the maximum total partial pressure of narcotic gases used when planning for <a href="/wiki/Technical_diving" title="Technical diving">technical diving</a> may be around 4.5 bar absolute, based on an <a href="/wiki/Equivalent_narcotic_depth" title="Equivalent narcotic depth">equivalent narcotic depth</a> of 35 metres (115 ft).
</p><p>The effect of a toxic contaminant such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> in breathing gas is also related to the partial pressure when breathed. A mixture which may be relatively safe at the surface could be dangerously toxic at the maximum depth of a dive, or a tolerable level of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> in the breathing loop of a diving <a href="/wiki/Rebreather" title="Rebreather">rebreather</a> may be come intolerable within seconds during descent when the partial pressure rapidly increases, and could lead to panic or incapacitation of the diver.
</p>
