<p>The <b>American Physical Society</b> (<b>APS</b>) is the world's second largest organization of physicists. The Society publishes more than a dozen scientific journals, including the prestigious <i><a href="/wiki/Physical_Review" title="Physical Review">Physical Review</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Physical_Review_Letters" title="Physical Review Letters">Physical Review Letters</a></i>, and organizes more than twenty science meetings each year. APS is a member society of the <a href="/wiki/American_Institute_of_Physics" title="American Institute of Physics">American Institute of Physics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Physical_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The American Physical Society was founded on May 20, 1899, when thirty-six physicists gathered at <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a> for that purpose. They proclaimed the mission of the new Society to be "to advance and diffuse the knowledge of physics", and in one way or another the APS has been at that task ever since. In the early years, virtually the sole activity of the APS was to hold scientific meetings, initially four per year. In 1913, the APS took over the operation of the <i><a href="/wiki/Physical_Review" title="Physical Review">Physical Review</a>,</i> which had been founded in 1893 at <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Academic_journal" title="Academic journal">journal</a> publication became its second major activity. The <i>Physical Review</i> was followed by <i><a href="/wiki/Reviews_of_Modern_Physics" title="Reviews of Modern Physics">Reviews of Modern Physics</a></i> in 1929 and by <i><a href="/wiki/Physical_Review_Letters" title="Physical Review Letters">Physical Review Letters</a></i> in 1958. Over the years, <i>Phys. Rev.</i> has subdivided into five separate sections as the fields of physics proliferated and the number of submissions grew.
</p><p>In more recent years, the activities of the Society have broadened considerably. Stimulated by the increase in Federal funding in the period after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, and even more by the increased public involvement of scientists in the 1960s, the APS is active in public and governmental affairs, and in the international physics community. In addition, the Society conducts extensive programs in education, <a href="/wiki/Science_outreach" title="Science outreach">science outreach</a> (specifically <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physics_Outreach" title="Physics Outreach">Physics Outreach</a>), and media relations. APS has 14 divisions and 11 topical groups covering all areas of physics research. There are 6 forums that reflect the interest of its 50,000 members<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> in broader issues, and 9 sections organized by geographical region.
</p><p>In 1999, APS Physics celebrated its centennial with the biggest-ever physics meeting in <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>. In 2005, APS took the lead role in United States participation in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Year_of_Physics" title="World Year of Physics">World Year of Physics</a>, initiating several programs to broadly publicize physics during the 100th anniversary of <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Annus_mirabilis" title="Annus mirabilis">annus mirabilis</a>. <a href="/wiki/Einstein@Home" title="Einstein@Home">Einstein@Home</a>, one of the projects APS initiated during World Year of Physics, is an ongoing and popular <a href="/wiki/Distributed_computing" title="Distributed computing">distributed computing</a> project.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Name_change_proposal">Name change proposal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Physical_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Name change proposal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the summer of 2005, the society conducted an electronic poll, in which the majority of APS members preferred the name <i>American Physics Society</i>. The poll became the motivation for a proposal of a name change promised in the leadership election that year. However, because of legal issues, the planned name change was eventually abandoned by the APS Executive Board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>To promote public recognition of APS as a physics society, while retaining the name American Physical Society, the APS Executive Board adopted a new logo incorporating the phrase "<i>APS Physics</i>."  General use of <i>APS Physics</i> to refer to APS or the American Physical Society is encouraged. The new <i>APS Physics</i> logo was designed by <a href="/wiki/Kerry_G._Johnson" title="Kerry G. Johnson">Kerry G. Johnson</a>. Marvin Cohen, who was then APS President, said, "<i>I like the logo.  At least now when you are in an elevator at an APS meeting and someone looks at your badge, they won't ask you about sports.</i>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="APS_journals">APS journals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Physical_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: APS journals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main category: <a href="/wiki/Category:American_Physical_Society_publications" title="Category:American Physical Society publications">American Physical Society publications</a></div>
<p>The American Physical Society publishes 13 international research journals and an open-access on-line news and commentary website <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physics_(American_Physical_Society_journal)" title="Physics (American Physical Society journal)">Physics</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Physical_Review_Letters" title="Physical Review Letters">Physical Review Letters</a></i> (PRL)</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Reviews_of_Modern_Physics" title="Reviews of Modern Physics">Reviews of Modern Physics</a></i> (RMP)</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Physical_Review_A" title="Physical Review A">Physical Review A</a></i> (PRA): Atomic, molecular and optical physics.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Physical_Review_B" title="Physical Review B">Physical Review B</a></i> (PRB): Condensed matter and materials physics.</li>
<li><i>Physical Review C</i> (PRC): Nuclear physics.</li>
<li><i>Physical Review D</i> (PRD): Particles, fields, gravitation, and cosmology.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Physical_Review_E" title="Physical Review E">Physical Review E</a></i> (PRE): Statistical, nonlinear, and soft matter physics.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Physical_Review_X" title="Physical Review X">Physical Review X</a></i> (PRX): <a href="/wiki/Open_access" title="Open access">Open access</a>; pure, applied, and interdisciplinary physics.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Physical_Review_Applied" title="Physical Review Applied">Physical Review Applied</a></i> (PRApplied): Experimental and theoretical applications of physics.</li>
<li><i>Physical Review Fluids</i> (PRFluids): Fluid dynamics.</li>
<li><i>Physical Review Accelerators and Beams</i> (PRAB): Open access; accelerator science and technology.</li>
<li><i>Physical Review Physics Education Research</i> (PRPER): Open access; experimental and theoretical research on physics education.</li>
<li><i>Physical Review Materials</i> (PRMaterials): A broad-scope international journal for the multidisciplinary community engaged in research on materials.</li></ul>
<p>All members of APS receive the monthly publication <i><a href="/wiki/Physics_Today" title="Physics Today">Physics Today</a></i>, published by the <a href="/wiki/American_Institute_of_Physics" title="American Institute of Physics">American Institute of Physics</a> (AIP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="APS_units">APS units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Physical_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: APS units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The American Physical Society has 47 units (divisions, forums, topical groups and sections) that represent the wide range of interests of the physics community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-APS_Units_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-APS_Units-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Divisions">Divisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Physical_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Divisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Astrophysics (DAP)</li>
<li>Atomic, Molecular &amp; Optical Physics (DAMOP): The objective of the division is the promotion of the fundamental research on atoms, simple molecules, electrons and light, and their interactions. Historically, this is the oldest division of the American Physical Society. It was created in 1943. The division manages a number of prestigious awards for AMO scientists at various stages of their careers, such as the Davisson-Germer Prize in Atomic or Surface Physics, Rabi Prize in AMO Physics, Outstanding Doctoral Thesis Research in AMO Physics, Herbert P. Broida Prize, etc. It also organizes annual DAMOP Meetings attended by many leading AMO researchers, both from the United States and abroad.</li>
<li>Biological Physics (DBIO): With over 2,000 members,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MEMBERS_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-MEMBERS-8">[8]</a></sup> the division is the second largest learned society in the world devoted to biological physics, following the <a href="/wiki/Biophysical_Society" title="Biophysical Society">Biophysical Society</a>. The objective of the division is the advancement and dissemination of knowledge on the broad interface of <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>. This includes studying biological phenomena with physics tools and identifying new physics questions within biological contexts. The division supports a program of over 600 (as of 2016) presentations at the annual March Meeting of the APS, on topics ranging from protein biophysics, to neuroscience and evolution. It promotes research and development of biological physics, enhanced the standing of the field, and recognizes important contributions to the field, such as by awarding the <a href="/wiki/Max_Delbruck_Prize" title="Max Delbruck Prize">Max Delbruck Prize</a> in biological physics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DBIO_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-DBIO-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>Chemical Physics (DCP)</li>
<li>Computational Physics (DCOMP): The division has more than 2,000 members, and the objective of the division is the advancement and dissemination of knowledge regarding the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computers" title="Computers">computers</a> in <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> research and education. This division provides to its members an opportunity for coordination and a forum for discussion and communication. In addition, the division promotes research and development in computational physics; enhances prestige and professional standing of its members; encourages scholarly publication; and promotes international cooperation in these activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DCOMP_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-DCOMP-10">[10]</a></sup></li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Programs">Programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Physical_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physics_Teacher_Education_Coalition">Physics Teacher Education Coalition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Physical_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Physics Teacher Education Coalition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>Physics Teacher Education Coalition (PhysTEC)</i> is a joint project of the American Physical Society and the <i><a href="/wiki/American_Association_of_Physics_Teachers" title="American Association of Physics Teachers">American Association of Physics Teachers</a></i>, which helps universities transform their physics teacher education programs into national models. PhysTEC Supported Sites develop their physics teacher preparation programs by implementing a set of Key Components that project leaders have identified as critical to success in physics teacher preparation. The broader Coalition is a national network of institutions committed to developing and promoting excellence in physics and physical science teacher preparation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="APS_Bridge_Program">APS Bridge Program</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Physical_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: APS Bridge Program">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>APS Bridge Program</i> aims to increase the number of underrepresented minority students that earn doctoral degrees in physics. The program names doctoral and master's degree-granting institutions as Bridge Sites and awards them <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a> funding to prepare post-baccalaureate students for doctoral studies through additional coursework, mentoring, research, application coaching, and GRE preparation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_APS_Scholarship_for_Minority_Undergraduate_Physics_Majors">The APS Scholarship for Minority Undergraduate Physics Majors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Physical_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: The APS Scholarship for Minority Undergraduate Physics Majors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Formerly called the APS Corporate Sponsored Scholarship Program for Minority Undergraduate Students Who Major in Physics, this scholarship was established in 1980 with the goal of increasing the number of underrepresented minorities receiving bachelor's degrees in physics. The program provides funding and mentoring to talented students.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conferences_for_Undergraduate_Women_in_Physics">Conferences for Undergraduate Women in Physics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Physical_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Conferences for Undergraduate Women in Physics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>APS Conferences for Undergraduate Women in Physics (APS CUWiP)</i> are three-day regional conferences for undergraduate physics majors.  The conferences aim to help undergraduate women continue in physics by providing them with the opportunity to experience a professional conference, information about graduate school and professions in physics, and access to other women in physics of all ages with whom they can share experiences, advice, and ideas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Career_Center">Career Center</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Physical_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Career Center">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>APS Careers in Physics</i> website is a gateway for physicists, students, and physics enthusiasts to obtain information about physics jobs and careers.  APS Careers in Physics has an award-winning job board, offers professional development advice through its website and blog, and provides links to workshops, grants, and career resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Faculty_Workshop">New Faculty Workshop</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Physical_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: New Faculty Workshop">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>APS co-sponsors a set of workshops for new physics and astronomy faculty with the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_of_Physics_Teachers" title="American Association of Physics Teachers">American Association of Physics Teachers</a> and the <a href="/wiki/American_Astronomical_Society" title="American Astronomical Society">American Astronomical Society</a>. These workshops reach nearly half of all new physics and astronomy faculty, and introduce them to current pedagogical practices, results of physics education research, and time management skills to help them begin and improve their academic careers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="CSWP.2FCOM_site_visits"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="CSWP/COM_site_visits">CSWP/COM site visits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Physical_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: CSWP/COM site visits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The APS has had a long-standing interest in improving the climate in physics departments for underrepresented minorities and women. The Committee on the Status of Women in Physics (CSWP) and the Committee on Minorities (COM) both sponsor site visit programs to universities as well as national labs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_conferences">Education conferences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Physical_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Education conferences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>APS is a leading voice for physics education and the society sponsors a variety of conferences dedicating to helping physics education leaders stay on top of the trends in the field.  Conferences include the annual Physics Department Chair Conference, a Graduate Education in Physics Conference, and a Distance Education &amp; Online Learning in Physics Workshop.
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
