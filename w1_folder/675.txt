<p><b>Space.com</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">space</a> and <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a> news website. Its stories are often syndicated to other <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">media</a> outlets, including <a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">CNN</a>, <a href="/wiki/MSNBC" title="MSNBC">MSNBC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!" title="Yahoo!">Yahoo!</a>, and <a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a>.
</p><p>Space.com was founded by former CNN anchor <a href="/wiki/Lou_Dobbs" title="Lou Dobbs">Lou Dobbs</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rich_Zahradnik&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rich Zahradnik (page does not exist)">Rich Zahradnik</a>, in July 1999. At that time, Dobbs owned a sizeable share of the company, and, in an unexpected move, left CNN later that year to become Space.com's <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">chief executive officer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  The move came as a surprise to many and was the source of intense media speculation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The company struggled to turn a profit in its early days, and when the <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_bubble" title="Dot-com bubble">dot-com bubble</a> burst in 2000, many felt that it would collapse. Co-founder Rich Zahradnik had left his position as president less than two months after the start of the company; former <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">astronaut</a> <a href="/wiki/Sally_Ride" title="Sally Ride">Sally Ride</a> took his place but then stepped down in September 2000. As it expanded, it acquired other web sites such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Starport.com&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Starport.com (page does not exist)">Starport.com</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Explorezone.com&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Explorezone.com (page does not exist)">Explorezone.com</a>. It also acquired <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sienna_Software&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sienna Software (page does not exist)">Sienna Software</a> (the company which produced the <a href="/wiki/Starry_Night_(planetarium_software)" title="Starry Night (planetarium software)">Starry Night</a> software) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_News" title="Space News">Space News</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Despite some growth, Space.com was never able to achieve what Dobbs had hoped for and in 2001, he returned to CNN.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He remains on its board and still owns a minority stake.
</p><p>Space.com has enjoyed the participation of several key space-related public figures, <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">Neil Armstrong</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexei_Leonov" title="Alexei Leonov">Alexei Leonov</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eugene_A._Cernan" title="Eugene A. Cernan">Eugene A. Cernan</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Patten_Stafford" title="Thomas Patten Stafford">Thomas Stafford</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> 
In 2003, for its coverage of the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Columbia" title="Space Shuttle Columbia">Space Shuttle <i>Columbia</i></a> <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Columbia_disaster" title="Space Shuttle Columbia disaster">disaster</a>, it received the Online Journalism Award for Breaking News by the <a href="/wiki/Online_News_Association" title="Online News Association">Online News Association</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In May 2004, Space.com's parent company changed its name from Space.com to <a href="/wiki/Imaginova" title="Imaginova">Imaginova</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and in 2009 sold Space.com and other properties to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Purch" title="Purch">Purch</a>, an online publishing company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
