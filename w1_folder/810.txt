<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Sugarcane</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>

<p><b>Sugarcane</b>, or <b>sugar cane</b>, are several species of tall <a href="/wiki/Perennial_plant" title="Perennial plant">perennial</a> <a href="/wiki/Poaceae" title="Poaceae">true grasses</a> of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharum" title="Saccharum">Saccharum</a></i>, tribe <a href="/wiki/Andropogoneae" title="Andropogoneae">Andropogoneae</a>, native to the warm temperate to tropical regions of <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polynesia" title="Polynesia">Polynesia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Melanesia" title="Melanesia">Melanesia</a>, and used for <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a> <a href="/wiki/Sugar_industry" title="Sugar industry">production</a>. It has stout, jointed, fibrous stalks that are rich in the <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a> <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a>, which accumulates in the <a href="/wiki/Plant_stem" title="Plant stem">stalk internodes</a>. The plant is two to six metres (six to twenty feet)  tall. All sugar cane species can interbreed and the major commercial <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivars</a> are complex <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrids</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>. Sugarcane belongs to the grass family <a href="/wiki/Poaceae" title="Poaceae">Poaceae</a>, an economically important seed plant family that includes maize, wheat, rice, and <a href="/wiki/Sorghum" title="Sorghum">sorghum</a>, and many <a href="/wiki/Forage" title="Forage">forage</a> crops.
</p><p>Sucrose, extracted and purified in specialized mill factories, is used as raw material in the food industry or is fermented to produce <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>. Sugarcane is the world's largest crop by production quantity, with 1.9 billion <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> produced in 2016, and Brazil accounting for 41% of the world total (ethanol being produced on a large scale by the Brazilian sugarcane industry). In 2012, The <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">Food and Agriculture Organization</a> estimated it was cultivated on about 26 million hectares (64 million acres), in more than 90 countries.
</p><p>The global demand for sugar is the primary driver of sugarcane agriculture. Cane accounts for 80% of sugar produced; most of the rest is made from <a href="/wiki/Sugar_beet" title="Sugar beet">sugar beets</a>. Sugarcane predominantly grows in the tropical and subtropical regions (sugar beets grow in colder temperate regions). Other than sugar, products derived from sugarcane include <a href="/wiki/Falernum" title="Falernum">falernum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Molasses" title="Molasses">molasses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rum" title="Rum">rum</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Cacha%C3%A7a" title="Cachaça">cachaça</a></i> (a traditional spirit from Brazil), <a href="/wiki/Bagasse" title="Bagasse">bagasse</a>, and ethanol. In some regions, people use sugarcane reeds to make pens, mats, screens, and thatch. The young, unexpanded <a href="/wiki/Inflorescence" title="Inflorescence">inflorescence</a> of <a href="/wiki/Saccharum_edule" title="Saccharum edule">Saccharum edule</a> (<i>duruka</i> or <i>tebu telor</i>) is eaten raw, steamed, or toasted, and prepared in various ways in Southeast Asia, including <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a> and certain island communities of Indonesia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Persians, followed by the Greeks, encountered the famous "reeds that produce honey without bees." in India between the 6th and 4th centuries BC. They adopted and then spread sugarcane agriculture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-agrisugar1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-agrisugar1-3">[3]</a></sup> Merchants began to trade in sugar from India, which was considered a luxury and an expensive spice. In the 18th century AD, sugarcane plantations   began in Caribbean, South American, Indian Ocean and Pacific island nations and the need for laborers became a major driver of large human migrations, including <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">slave labor</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Indentured_servitude" title="Indentured servitude">indentured servants</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nationalarchives1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-nationalarchives1-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sugarcane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sugarcane is a tropical,  perennial grass that forms lateral shoots at the base to produce multiple stems, typically three to four m (10 to 13 ft) high and about 5 cm (2 in) in diameter. The stems grow into cane stalk, which when mature constitutes around 75% of the entire plant. A mature stalk is typically composed of 11–16% fiber, 12–16% soluble sugars, 2–3% nonsugars, and 63–73% water. A sugarcane crop is sensitive to the climate, soil type, irrigation, fertilizers, insects, disease control, varieties, and the harvest period. The average yield of cane stalk is 60–70 tonnes per hectare (24–28 long ton/acre; 27–31 short ton/acre) per year. However, this figure can vary between 30 and 180 tonnes per hectare depending on knowledge and crop management approach used in sugarcane cultivation. Sugarcane is a <a href="/wiki/Cash_crop" title="Cash crop">cash crop</a>, but it is also used as livestock fodder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sugarcane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_sugar" title="History of sugar">History of sugar</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Spread_sugarcane.JPG"><img alt="Map showing sugar cane India as the first sugar cane country, followed by small areas in Africa, and then smaller areas on Atlantic Islands west of Africa" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="248" data-file-width="483" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Spread_sugarcane.JPG/350px-Spread_sugarcane.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3d/Spread_sugarcane.JPG 1.5x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Spread_sugarcane.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The westward diffusion of sugarcane in pre-Islamic times (shown in red), in the medieval <a href="/wiki/Muslim_world" title="Muslim world">Muslim world</a> (green), and in the 15th century by the Portuguese on the Madeira archipelago, and by the Spanish on the Canary Islands archipelago (islands west of Africa, circled by violet lines)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Sugarcane is indigenous to tropical parts of <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sharpe_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sharpe-8">[8]</a></sup> Different species likely originated in different locations, with <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharum_barberi" title="Saccharum barberi">Saccharum barberi</a></i> originating in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <i>S. edule</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharum_officinarum" title="Saccharum officinarum">S. officinarum</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sharpe_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sharpe-8">[8]</a></sup>  The earliest known production of crystalline sugar began in northern India. The exact date of the first cane sugar production is unclear.  The earliest evidence of sugar production comes from ancient Sanskrit and Pali texts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Around the 8th century, Muslim and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab" title="Arab">Arab</a> traders introduced sugar from <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> to the other parts of the <a href="/wiki/Abbasid_Caliphate" title="Abbasid Caliphate">Abbasid Caliphate</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">Andalusia</a>. By the 10th century, sources state that every village in <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a> grew sugarcane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watson_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Watson-10">[10]</a></sup> It was among the early crops brought to the Americas by the Spanish, mainly Andalusians, from their fields in the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>, and the Portuguese from their fields in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Madeira_Islands" title="Madeira Islands">Madeira Islands</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> first brought sugarcane to the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> during his second voyage to the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>; initially to the island of <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a> (modern day <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>). In colonial times, sugar formed one side of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triangle_trade" title="Triangle trade">triangle trade</a> of New World raw materials, along with European manufactured goods, and African <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">slaves</a>. Sugar (often in the form of molasses) was shipped from the Caribbean to Europe or New England, where it was used to make rum. The profits from the sale of sugar were then used to purchase manufactured goods, which were then shipped to West Africa, where they were bartered for slaves. The slaves were then brought back to the Caribbean to be sold to sugar planters. The profits from the sale of the slaves were then used to buy more sugar, which was shipped to Europe.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Mill_Yard_-_Ten_Views_in_the_Island_of_Antigua_(1823),_plate_V_-_BL.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1241" data-file-width="1784" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/The_Mill_Yard_-_Ten_Views_in_the_Island_of_Antigua_%281823%29%2C_plate_V_-_BL.jpg/220px-The_Mill_Yard_-_Ten_Views_in_the_Island_of_Antigua_%281823%29%2C_plate_V_-_BL.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/The_Mill_Yard_-_Ten_Views_in_the_Island_of_Antigua_%281823%29%2C_plate_V_-_BL.jpg/330px-The_Mill_Yard_-_Ten_Views_in_the_Island_of_Antigua_%281823%29%2C_plate_V_-_BL.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/The_Mill_Yard_-_Ten_Views_in_the_Island_of_Antigua_%281823%29%2C_plate_V_-_BL.jpg/440px-The_Mill_Yard_-_Ten_Views_in_the_Island_of_Antigua_%281823%29%2C_plate_V_-_BL.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Mill_Yard_-_Ten_Views_in_the_Island_of_Antigua_(1823),_plate_V_-_BL.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Sugar_plantations_in_the_Caribbean" title="Sugar plantations in the Caribbean">Sugar plantation</a> in the British colony of <a href="/wiki/Antigua" title="Antigua">Antigua</a>, 1823</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> found its sugarcane islands so valuable that it effectively traded its <a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">portion of Canada</a>, famously dubbed "<a href="/wiki/A_few_acres_of_snow" title="A few acres of snow">a few acres of snow</a>", to Britain for their return of <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lucia" title="Saint Lucia">St. Lucia</a> at the end of the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years' War">Seven Years' War</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> similarly kept <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>, a sugar colony in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, instead of seeking the return of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Netherlands" title="New Netherlands">New Netherlands</a> (New York).
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boiling_house" title="Boiling house">Boiling houses</a> in the 17th through 19th centuries converted <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane_juice" title="Sugarcane juice">sugarcane juice</a> into raw sugar. These houses were attached to sugar plantations in the Western colonies. Slaves often ran the boiling process under very poor conditions. Rectangular boxes of brick or stone served as furnaces, with an opening at the bottom to stoke the fire and remove ashes. At the top of each furnace were up to seven copper kettles or boilers, each one smaller and hotter than the previous one. The cane juice began in the largest kettle. The juice was then heated and lime added to remove impurities. The juice was skimmed and then channeled to successively smaller kettles. The last kettle, the "teache", was where the cane juice became syrup. The next step was a cooling trough, where the sugar crystals hardened around a sticky core of molasses. This raw sugar was then shoveled from the cooling trough into <a href="/wiki/Hogshead" title="Hogshead">hogsheads</a> (wooden barrels), and from there into the curing house.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cannes-sucre-Georgi.jpg"><img alt="Black-and-white photo of sugar cane standing in field" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="693" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Cannes-sucre-Georgi.jpg/220px-Cannes-sucre-Georgi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Cannes-sucre-Georgi.jpg/330px-Cannes-sucre-Georgi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Cannes-sucre-Georgi.jpg/440px-Cannes-sucre-Georgi.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cannes-sucre-Georgi.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A sugar plantation on the island of <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union" title="Réunion">Réunion</a> in the late 19th century</div></div></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>, slaves were liberated after 1833 and many would no longer work on sugarcane plantations when they had a choice. British owners of sugarcane plantations therefore needed new workers, and they found cheap labour in China, Portugal and India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The people were subject to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indentured_servant" title="Indentured servant">indenture</a>, a long-established form of contract which bound them to forced labour for a fixed term; apart from the fixed term of servitude, this resembled slavery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The first ships carrying indentured labourers from India left in 1836.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britain1_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-britain1-14">[14]</a></sup> The migrations to serve sugarcane plantations led to a significant number of ethnic Indians, southeast Asians and Chinese settling in various parts of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In some islands and countries, the <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asian</a> migrants now constitute between 10 and 50 percent of the population. Sugarcane plantations and Asian ethnic groups continue to thrive in countries such as <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colony_of_Natal" title="Colony of Natal">Natal</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burma" title="Burma">Burma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Guiana" title="British Guiana">British Guiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grenada" title="Grenada">Grenada</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Lucia" title="St. Lucia">St. Lucia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Vincent_(island)" title="Saint Vincent (island)">St. Vincent</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Kitts" title="St. Kitts">St. Kitts</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Croix" title="St. Croix">St. Croix</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nevis" title="Nevis">Nevis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britain1_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-britain1-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Old-fashioned_Indian_Sugar%3Dcane_Press.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="464" data-file-width="597" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Old-fashioned_Indian_Sugar%3Dcane_Press.jpg/220px-Old-fashioned_Indian_Sugar%3Dcane_Press.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Old-fashioned_Indian_Sugar%3Dcane_Press.jpg/330px-Old-fashioned_Indian_Sugar%3Dcane_Press.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Old-fashioned_Indian_Sugar%3Dcane_Press.jpg/440px-Old-fashioned_Indian_Sugar%3Dcane_Press.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Old-fashioned_Indian_Sugar%3Dcane_Press.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Old-fashioned Indian Sugarcane Press, c. 1905</div></div></div>
<p>Between 1863 and 1900, traders and plantation owners from the British colony of <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a> (now a state of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>) brought between 55,000 and 62,500 people from the South <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Islands" title="Pacific Islands">Pacific Islands</a> to work on sugarcane plantations. It is estimated that one-third of these workers were coerced or kidnapped into slavery (known as <a href="/wiki/Blackbirding" title="Blackbirding">blackbirding</a>). Many others were paid very low wages. Between 1904 and 1908, most of the 10,000 remaining workers were deported in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_Australia_Policy" title="White Australia Policy">an effort to keep Australia racially pure</a> and protect white workers from cheap foreign labour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AHRC_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-AHRC-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuban</a> sugar derived from sugarcane was exported to the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">USSR</a>, where it received price supports and was ensured a guaranteed market. The <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Dissolution of the Soviet Union">1991 dissolution of the Soviet state</a> forced the closure of most of Cuba's sugar industry.
</p><p>Sugarcane remains an important part of the economy of <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>, along with the
<a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, and other islands.
</p><p>About 70% of the sugar produced globally comes from <i>S. officinarum</i> and hybrids using this species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kew2_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-kew2-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>

Slave workers toil during the harvest. To the left is a flat-bottomed vessel for cane transportation.</div></div></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation">Cultivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sugarcane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cultivation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sugarcane_plantation_in_Mauritius_(reduced_colour_saturation).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Sugarcane_plantation_in_Mauritius_%28reduced_colour_saturation%29.jpg/250px-Sugarcane_plantation_in_Mauritius_%28reduced_colour_saturation%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Sugarcane_plantation_in_Mauritius_%28reduced_colour_saturation%29.jpg/375px-Sugarcane_plantation_in_Mauritius_%28reduced_colour_saturation%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Sugarcane_plantation_in_Mauritius_%28reduced_colour_saturation%29.jpg/500px-Sugarcane_plantation_in_Mauritius_%28reduced_colour_saturation%29.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sugarcane_plantation_in_Mauritius_(reduced_colour_saturation).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sugarcane plantation, <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a></div></div></div>
<p>Sugarcane cultivation requires a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">tropical</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtropical" title="Subtropical">subtropical</a> climate, with a minimum of 60 cm (24 in) of annual moisture. It is one of the most <a href="/wiki/Photosynthetic_efficiency" title="Photosynthetic efficiency">efficient</a> <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesizers</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plant_kingdom" title="Plant kingdom">plant kingdom</a>. It is a <a href="/wiki/C4_carbon_fixation" title="C4 carbon fixation">C<sub>4</sub> plant</a>, able to convert up to 1% of incident solar energy into biomass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In prime growing regions, such as <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">El Salvador</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>, sugarcane crops can produce over 15 kg/m<sup>2</sup> of cane. Once a major crop of the southeastern region of the United States, sugarcane cultivation has declined there in recent decades, and is now primarily confined to <a href="/wiki/Sugar_Cane_Growers_Cooperative_of_Florida" title="Sugar Cane Growers Cooperative of Florida">Florida</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_Texas" title="South Texas">South Texas</a>.
</p><p>Sugarcane is cultivated in the tropics and subtropics in areas with a plentiful supply of water for a continuous period of more than six to seven months each year, either from natural rainfall or through irrigation. The crop does not tolerate severe frosts. Therefore, most of the world's sugarcane is grown between <a href="/wiki/22nd_parallel_north" title="22nd parallel north">22°N</a> and <a href="/wiki/22nd_parallel_south" title="22nd parallel south">22°S</a>, and some up to <a href="/wiki/33rd_parallel_north" title="33rd parallel north">33°N</a> and <a href="/wiki/33rd_parallel_south" title="33rd parallel south">33°S</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> When sugarcane crop is found outside this range, such as the <a href="/wiki/KwaZulu-Natal" title="KwaZulu-Natal">Natal</a> region of South Africa, it is normally due to anomalous climatic conditions in the region, such as warm ocean currents that sweep down the coast. In terms of altitude, sugarcane crop is found up to 1,600 metres or 5,200 feet close to the equator in countries such as <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sugarcane can be grown on many soils ranging from highly fertile well-drained <a href="/wiki/Mollisol" title="Mollisol">mollisols</a>, through heavy cracking <a href="/wiki/Vertisol" title="Vertisol">vertisols</a>, infertile acid <a href="/wiki/Oxisol" title="Oxisol">oxisols</a>, peaty <a href="/wiki/Histosol" title="Histosol">histosols</a>, to rocky <a href="/wiki/Andisol" title="Andisol">andisols</a>. Both plentiful sunshine and water supplies increase cane production. This has made desert countries with good irrigation facilities such as Egypt some of the highest-yielding sugarcane-cultivating regions.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sugar_cane_flower.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2734" data-file-width="1823" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Sugar_cane_flower.jpg/170px-Sugar_cane_flower.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Sugar_cane_flower.jpg/255px-Sugar_cane_flower.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Sugar_cane_flower.jpg/340px-Sugar_cane_flower.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sugar_cane_flower.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sugarcane flower, <a href="/wiki/Dominica" title="Dominica">Dominica</a></div></div></div>
<p>Although some sugarcanes produce seeds, modern stem cutting has become the most common reproduction method.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Each cutting must contain at least one bud, and the cuttings are sometimes hand-planted. In more technologically advanced countries like the United States and Australia, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Billet_planting&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Billet planting (page does not exist)">billet planting</a> is common. Billets (stalks or stalk sections) harvested by a mechanical harvester are planted by a machine that opens and recloses the ground. Once planted, a stand can be harvested several times; after each harvest, the cane sends up new stalks, called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ratoon" title="Ratoon">ratoons</a>. Successive harvests give decreasing yields, eventually justifying replanting. Two to 10 harvests are usually made depending on the type of culture. In a country with a mechanical agriculture looking for a high production of large fields, like in North America, sugar canes are replanted after two or three harvests to avoid a lowering in yields. In countries with a more traditional type of agriculture with smaller fields and hand harvesting, like in the French island <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_R%C3%A9union" title="La Réunion">la Réunion</a>, sugar cane is often harvested up to 10 years before replanting.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Caminh%C3%A3o_Carregado.jpg"><img alt="Photo of trailer trucks filled with plant cane" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Caminh%C3%A3o_Carregado.jpg/220px-Caminh%C3%A3o_Carregado.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Caminh%C3%A3o_Carregado.jpg/330px-Caminh%C3%A3o_Carregado.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Caminh%C3%A3o_Carregado.jpg/440px-Caminh%C3%A3o_Carregado.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Caminh%C3%A3o_Carregado.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sugarcane mechanical harvest, <a href="/wiki/Jaboticabal" title="Jaboticabal">Jaboticabal</a>, <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo_(state)" title="São Paulo (state)">São Paulo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a></div></div></div>
<p>Sugarcane is harvested by hand and mechanically. Hand harvesting accounts for more than half of production, and is dominant in the developing world. In hand harvesting, the field is first set on fire. The fire burns dry leaves, and chases away or kills any lurking venomous snakes, without harming the stalks and roots. Harvesters then cut the cane just above ground-level using <a href="/wiki/Cane_knife" title="Cane knife">cane knives</a> or <a href="/wiki/Machete" title="Machete">machetes</a>. A skilled harvester can cut 500 kg (1,100 lb) of sugarcane per hour.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Mechanical harvesting uses a <a href="/wiki/Combine_harvester" title="Combine harvester">combine</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane_harvester" title="Sugarcane harvester">sugarcane harvester</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The Austoft 7000 series, the original modern harvester design, has now been copied by other companies, including Cameco / <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deere_%26_Company" title="Deere &amp; Company">John Deere</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The machine cuts the cane at the base of the stalk, strips the leaves, chops the cane into consistent lengths and deposits it into a transporter following alongside. The harvester then blows the trash back onto the field. Such machines can harvest 100 long tons (100 t) each hour; however, harvested cane must be rapidly processed. Once cut, sugarcane begins to lose its sugar content, and damage to the cane during mechanical harvesting accelerates this decline. This decline is offset because a modern chopper harvester can complete the harvest faster and more efficiently than hand cutting and loading. Austoft also developed a series of hydraulic high-lift infield transporters to work alongside their harvesters to allow even more rapid transfer of cane to, for example, the nearest railway siding. This mechanical harvesting doesn't require the field to be set on fire; the remains left in the field by the machine consist of the top of the sugar cane and the dead leaves, which act as mulch for the next round of planting.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pests">Pests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sugarcane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cane_beetle" title="Cane beetle">cane beetle</a> (also known as cane grub) can substantially reduce crop yield by eating roots; it can be controlled with <a href="/wiki/Imidacloprid" title="Imidacloprid">imidacloprid</a> (Confidor) or <a href="/wiki/Chlorpyrifos" title="Chlorpyrifos">chlorpyrifos</a> (Lorsban). Other important pests are the <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> of some <a href="/wiki/Lepidoptera" title="Lepidoptera">butterfly/moth</a> species, including the <a href="/wiki/Turnip_moth" title="Turnip moth">turnip moth</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sugarcane_borer" title="Sugarcane borer">sugarcane borer</a> (<i>Diatraea saccharalis</i>), the <a href="/wiki/Eldana" title="Eldana">African sugarcane borer</a> (<i>Eldana saccharina</i>), the Mexican rice borer (<i><a href="/wiki/Eoreuma" title="Eoreuma">Eoreuma</a> loftini</i>), the <a href="/wiki/African_armyworm" title="African armyworm">African armyworm</a> (<i>Spodoptera exempta</i>), leaf-cutting ants, <a href="/wiki/Termite" title="Termite">termites</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spittlebug" title="Spittlebug">spittlebugs</a> (especially <i>Mahanarva fimbriolata</i> and <i>Deois flavopicta</i>), and the <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetle</a> <i>Migdolus fryanus</i>. The <a href="/wiki/Planthopper" title="Planthopper">planthopper</a> insect <i><a href="/wiki/Eumetopina_flavipes" title="Eumetopina flavipes">Eumetopina flavipes</a></i> acts as a <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">virus</a> vector, which causes the sugarcane disease <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ramu_stunt" title="Ramu stunt">ramu stunt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pathogens">Pathogens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sugarcane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pathogens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_sugarcane_diseases" title="List of sugarcane diseases">List of sugarcane diseases</a></div>
<p>Numerous pathogens infect sugarcane, such as sugarcane grassy shoot disease caused by <i><a href="/wiki/Phytoplasma" title="Phytoplasma">Phytoplasma</a></i>, whiptail disease or <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane_smut" title="Sugarcane smut">sugarcane smut</a>, <i>pokkah boeng</i> caused by <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fusarium_moniliforme" title="Fusarium moniliforme">Fusarium moniliforme</a></i>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xanthomonas_axonopodis" title="Xanthomonas axonopodis">Xanthomonas axonopodis</a> bacteria causes <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gumming_Disease&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gumming Disease (page does not exist)">Gumming Disease</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Red_rot" title="Red rot">red rot</a> disease caused by <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colletotrichum_falcatum" title="Colletotrichum falcatum">Colletotrichum falcatum</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">Viral</a> diseases affecting sugarcane include <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane_mosaic_virus" title="Sugarcane mosaic virus">sugarcane mosaic virus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maize_streak_virus" title="Maize streak virus">maize streak virus</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sugarcane_yellow_leaf_virus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sugarcane yellow leaf virus (page does not exist)">sugarcane yellow leaf virus</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nitrogen_fixation">Nitrogen fixation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sugarcane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nitrogen fixation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some sugarcane varieties are capable of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation" title="Nitrogen fixation">fixing atmospheric nitrogen</a> in association with the bacterium <i><a href="/wiki/Gluconacetobacter_diazotrophicus" title="Gluconacetobacter diazotrophicus">Gluconacetobacter diazotrophicus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Unlike <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">legumes</a> and other nitrogen-fixing plants that form <a href="/wiki/Root_nodule" title="Root nodule">root nodules</a> in the soil in association with bacteria, <i>G. diazotrophicus</i> lives within the intercellular spaces of the sugarcane's stem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Coating seeds with the bacteria is a newly developed technology that can enable every crop species to fix nitrogen for its own use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conditions_for_sugarcane_workers">Conditions for sugarcane workers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sugarcane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Conditions for sugarcane workers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At least 20,000 people are estimated to have died of chronic kidney disease (CKD) in Central America in the past two decades – most of them sugar cane workers along the Pacific coast. This may be due to working long hours in the heat without adequate fluid intake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Processing">Processing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sugarcane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Processing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Traditionally, sugarcane processing requires two stages. Mills extract raw sugar from freshly harvested cane and "mill-white” sugar is sometimes produced immediately after the first stage at sugar-extraction mills, intended for local consumption. Sugar crystals appear naturally white in color during the crystallization process. Sulfur dioxide is added to inhibit the formation of color-inducing molecules as well as to stabilize the sugar juices during evaporation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Refineries, often located nearer to consumers in North America, Europe, and Japan, then produce refined white sugar, which is 99 percent sucrose. These two stages are slowly merging. Increasing affluence in the sugar-producing tropics increased demand for refined sugar products, driving a trend toward combined milling and refining.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Milling">Milling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sugarcane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Milling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sugar_cane_mill" title="Sugar cane mill">Sugar cane mill</a></div>

<p>Sugarcane processing produces <b>cane sugar</b> (sucrose) from sugarcane. Other products of the processing include bagasse, molasses, and filtercake.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bagasse" title="Bagasse">Bagasse</a>, the residual dry fiber of the cane after cane juice has been extracted, is used for several purposes:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa2005_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-epa2005-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>fuel for the boilers and kilns,</li>
<li>production of paper, paperboard products, and reconstituted panelboard,</li>
<li>agricultural mulch, and more,</li>
<li>as a raw material for production of chemicals.</li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:UsinaSantaElisa.jpg"><img alt="Photo of shorter building with smoke coming out of smokestack next to five-story office building" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="789" data-file-width="1271" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/UsinaSantaElisa.jpg/200px-UsinaSantaElisa.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/UsinaSantaElisa.jpg/300px-UsinaSantaElisa.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/UsinaSantaElisa.jpg/400px-UsinaSantaElisa.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:UsinaSantaElisa.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Santa Elisa sugarcane processing plant in <a href="/wiki/Sert%C3%A3ozinho" title="Sertãozinho">Sertãozinho</a>, one of the largest and oldest in Brazil</div></div></div>
<p>The primary use of bagasse and bagasse residue is as a fuel source for the boilers in the generation of process steam in sugar plants. Dried filtercake is used as an animal feed supplement, fertilizer, and source of sugarcane wax.
</p><p>Molasses is produced in two forms: <a href="/wiki/Molasses#Blackstrap_Molasses" title="Molasses">Blackstrap</a>, which has a characteristic strong flavor, and a purer <a href="/wiki/Molasses" title="Molasses">molasses</a> syrup. Blackstrap molasses is sold as a food and dietary supplement. It is also a common ingredient in animal feed, is used to produce <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rum" title="Rum">rum</a>, and in the manufacturing of <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid" title="Citric acid">citric acid</a>. Purer molasses syrups are sold as molasses, and may also be blended with <a href="/wiki/Maple_syrup" title="Maple syrup">maple syrup</a>, invert sugars, or <a href="/wiki/Corn_syrup" title="Corn syrup">corn syrup</a>. Both forms of molasses are used in baking.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Refining">Refining</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sugarcane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Refining">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sugar_refinery" title="Sugar refinery">Sugar refining</a> further purifies the raw sugar. It is first mixed with heavy syrup and then centrifuged in a process called "affination". Its purpose is to wash away the sugar crystals' outer coating, which is less pure than the crystal interior. The remaining sugar is then dissolved to make a syrup, about 60 percent solids by weight.
</p><p>The sugar solution is clarified by the addition of <a href="/wiki/Phosphoric_acid" title="Phosphoric acid">phosphoric acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calcium_hydroxide" title="Calcium hydroxide">calcium hydroxide</a>, which combine to precipitate <a href="/wiki/Calcium_phosphate" title="Calcium phosphate">calcium phosphate</a>. The calcium phosphate particles entrap some impurities and absorb others, and then float to the top of the tank, where they can be skimmed off. An alternative to this "phosphatation" technique is "<a href="/wiki/Carbonatation" title="Carbonatation">carbonatation</a>", which is similar, but uses <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and calcium hydroxide to produce a <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> precipitate.
</p><p>After filtering any remaining solids, the clarified syrup is decolorized by filtration through <a href="/wiki/Activated_carbon" title="Activated carbon">activated carbon</a>. <a href="/wiki/Bone_char" title="Bone char">Bone char</a> or coal-based activated carbon is traditionally used in this role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VegetarianJournal_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-VegetarianJournal-34">[34]</a></sup> Some remaining color-forming impurities are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adsorb" title="Adsorb">adsorbed</a> by the carbon. The purified syrup is then concentrated to supersaturation and repeatedly crystallized in a vacuum, to produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_refined_sugar" title="White refined sugar">white refined sugar</a>. As in a sugar mill, the sugar crystals are separated from the molasses by centrifuging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Additional sugar is recovered by blending the remaining syrup with the washings from affination and again crystallizing to produce <a href="/wiki/Brown_sugar" title="Brown sugar">brown sugar</a>. When no more sugar can be economically recovered, the final molasses still contains 20–30 percent sucrose and 15–25 percent glucose and fructose.
</p><p>To produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Granulated_sugar" title="Granulated sugar">granulated sugar</a>, in which individual grains do not clump, sugar must be dried, first by heating in a rotary dryer, and then by blowing cool air through it for several days.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ribbon_cane_syrup">Ribbon cane syrup</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sugarcane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ribbon cane syrup">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ribbon cane is a subtropical type that was once widely grown in the southern United States, as far north as coastal <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>. The juice was extracted with horse or mule-powered crushers; the juice was boiled, like <a href="/wiki/Maple_syrup" title="Maple syrup">maple syrup</a>, in a flat pan, and then used in the syrup form as a food sweetener.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> It is not currently a commercial crop, but a few growers find ready sales for their product.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pollution_from_sugarcane_processing">Pollution from sugarcane processing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sugarcane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Pollution from sugarcane processing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Particulate matter, combustion products, and volatile organic compounds are the primary pollutants emitted during the sugarcane processing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa2005_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-epa2005-33">[33]</a></sup> Combustion products include nitrogen oxides (NO<sub>X</sub>), carbon monoxide (CO), CO<sub>2</sub>, and sulfur oxides (SO<sub>X</sub>). Potential emission sources include the sugar granulators, sugar conveying and packaging equipment, bulk loadout operations, boilers, granular carbon and char regeneration kilns, regenerated adsorbent transport systems, kilns and handling equipment (at some facilities), carbonation tanks, multi-effect evaporator stations, and vacuum boiling pans. Modern pollution prevention technologies are capable of addressing all of these potential pollutants.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<p>Global production of sugarcane in 2016 was 1.9 billion tonnes, with Brazil producing 41% of the world total and India 18% (table). The average worldwide yield of sugarcane crops in 2016 was 70.6 tonnes per hectare, led by <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> with 112 tonnes per hectare and <a href="/wiki/Zambia" title="Zambia">Zambia</a> with 103.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faostat-sc16_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-faostat-sc16-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>The theoretical possible yield for sugar cane is about 280 tonnes per hectare per year, and small experimental plots in Brazil have demonstrated yields of 236–280 tonnes of cane per hectare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Other promising regions for high-yield sugarcane production are in sun-drenched, irrigated farms of northern Africa, and other deserts with plentiful water from nearby rivers or irrigation canals.
</p><p>In the United States, sugarcane is grown commercially in <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethanol">Ethanol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sugarcane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Ethanol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fuel" title="Ethanol fuel">Ethanol fuel</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">Biofuel</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sao_Paulo_ethanol_pump_04_2008_74_zoom.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="900" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Sao_Paulo_ethanol_pump_04_2008_74_zoom.jpg/220px-Sao_Paulo_ethanol_pump_04_2008_74_zoom.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Sao_Paulo_ethanol_pump_04_2008_74_zoom.jpg/330px-Sao_Paulo_ethanol_pump_04_2008_74_zoom.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Sao_Paulo_ethanol_pump_04_2008_74_zoom.jpg/440px-Sao_Paulo_ethanol_pump_04_2008_74_zoom.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sao_Paulo_ethanol_pump_04_2008_74_zoom.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A fuel pump in Brazil, offering cane ethanol (A) and gasoline (G).</div></div></div>
<p>Ethanol is generally available as a byproduct of sugar production. It can be used as a biofuel alternative to gasoline, and is widely used in cars in Brazil. It is an alternative to gasoline, and may become the primary product of sugarcane processing, rather than sugar.
</p><p>In Brazil, gasoline is required to contain at least 22 percent bioethanol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iea1_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-iea1-41">[41]</a></sup> This bioethanol is sourced from Brazil's large sugarcane crop.
</p><p>The production of ethanol from sugar cane is more energy efficient than from corn or sugar beets or palm/vegetable oils, particularly if cane bagasse is used to produce heat and power for the process. Furthermore, if biofuels are used for crop production and transport, the fossil energy input needed for each ethanol energy unit can be very low. EIA estimates that with an integrated sugar cane to ethanol technology, the well-to-wheels CO<sub>2</sub> emissions can be 90 percent lower than conventional gasoline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iea1_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-iea1-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>A textbook on renewable energy<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> describes the energy transformation:
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Presently, 75 tons of raw sugar cane are produced annually per hectare in Brazil. The cane delivered to the processing plant is called burned and cropped (b&amp;c), and represents 77% of the mass of the raw cane. The reason for this reduction is that the stalks are separated from the leaves (which are burned and whose ashes are left in the field as fertilizer), and from the roots that remain in the ground to sprout for the next crop. Average cane production is, therefore, 58 tons of b&amp;c per hectare per year.
</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>Each ton of b&amp;c yields 740 kg of juice (135 kg of sucrose and 605 kg of water) and 260 kg of moist bagasse (130 kg of dry bagasse). Since the lower heating value of sucrose is 16.5 M<a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">J</a>/kg, and that of the bagasse is 19.2 MJ/kg, the total heating value of a ton of b&amp;c is 4.7 GJ of which 2.2 GJ come from the sucrose and 2.5 from the bagasse.
</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>Per hectare per year, the biomass produced corresponds to 0.27 TJ. This is equivalent to 0.86 W per square meter. Assuming an average insolation of 225 W per square meter, the photosynthetic efficiency of sugar cane is 0.38%.
</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>The 135 kg of sucrose found in 1 ton of b&amp;c are transformed into 70 litres of ethanol with a combustion energy of 1.7 GJ. The practical sucrose-ethanol conversion efficiency is, therefore, 76% (compare with the theoretical 97%).
</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>One hectare of sugar cane yields 4,000 litres of ethanol per year (without any additional energy input, because the bagasse produced exceeds the amount needed to distill the final product). This, however, does not include the energy used in tilling, transportation, and so on. Thus, the solar energy-to-ethanol conversion efficiency is 0.13%.
</p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bagasse_applications">Bagasse applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sugarcane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Bagasse applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sugarcane is a major crop in many countries. It is one of the plants with the highest bioconversion efficiency. Sugarcane crop is able to efficiently fix solar energy, yielding some 55 tonnes of dry matter per hectare of land annually. After harvest, the crop produces sugar juice and bagasse, the fibrous dry matter. This dry matter is biomass with potential as fuel for energy production. Bagasse can also be used as an alternative source of pulp for paper production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sugarcane bagasse is a potentially abundant source of energy for large producers of sugarcane, such as Brazil, India and China. According to one report, with use of latest technologies, bagasse produced annually in Brazil has the potential of meeting 20 percent of Brazil’s energy consumption by 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bw2009_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-bw2009-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electricity_production">Electricity production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sugarcane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Electricity production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A number of countries, in particular those devoid of any fossil fuel, have implemented energy conservation and efficiency measures to minimize energy used in cane processing and furthermore export any excess electricity to the grid. Bagasse is usually burned to produce steam, which in turn creates electricity. Current technologies, such as those in use in <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a>, produce over 100 kWh of electricity per tonne of bagasse. With a total world harvest of over 1 billion tonnes of sugar cane per year, the global energy potential from bagasse is over 100,000 GWh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Using Mauritius as a reference, an annual potential of 10,000 GWh of additional electricity could be produced throughout <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Electrical generation from bagasse could become quite important, particularly to the rural populations of sugarcane producing nations.
</p><p>Recent cogeneration technology plants are being designed to produce from 200 to over 300 kWh of electricity per tonne of bagasse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> As sugarcane is a seasonal crop, shortly after harvest the supply of bagasse would peak, requiring power generation plants to strategically manage the storage of bagasse.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biogas_production">Biogas production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sugarcane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Biogas production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A greener alternative to burning bagasse for the production of electricity is to convert bagasse into <a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">biogas</a>. Technologies are being developed to use <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> to transform bagasse into advanced <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuel</a> and biogas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bw2009_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-bw2009-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In most countries where sugarcane is cultivated, there are several foods and popular dishes derived directly from it, such as:
</p>
<ul><li>Raw sugarcane: chewed to extract the juice</li>
<li><i>Sayur nganten</i>: an <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_cuisine" title="Indonesian cuisine">Indonesian</a> soup made with the stem of trubuk (<i>Saccharum edule</i>), a type of sugarcane.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sugarcane_juice" title="Sugarcane juice">Sugarcane juice</a>: a combination of fresh juice, extracted by hand or small mills, with a touch of lemon and ice to make a popular drink, known variously as <i>air tebu</i>, <i>usacha rass</i>, <i>guarab</i>, <i>guarapa,</i> <i>guarapo,</i> <i>papelón</i>, <i>aseer asab</i>, <i>ganna sharbat</i>, <i>mosto</i>, <i>caldo de cana</i>, <i>nước miá</i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Syrup" title="Syrup">Syrup</a>: a traditional sweetener in soft drinks, now largely supplanted in the US by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_fructose_corn_syrup" title="High fructose corn syrup">high fructose corn syrup</a>, which is less expensive because of corn subsidies and sugar tariffs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Molasses" title="Molasses">Molasses</a>: used as a <a href="/wiki/Sugar_substitute" title="Sugar substitute">sweetener</a> and a syrup accompanying other foods, such as cheese or cookies</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jaggery" title="Jaggery">Jaggery</a>: a solidified molasses, known as <i>gur</i> or <i>gud</i> or <i>gul</i> in India, is traditionally produced by evaporating juice to make a thick sludge, and then cooling and molding it in buckets. Modern production partially freeze dries the juice to reduce caramelization and lighten its color. It is used as sweetener in cooking traditional entrees, sweets and desserts.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Falernum" title="Falernum">Falernum</a>: a sweet, and slightly alcoholic drink made from sugarcane juice</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Cacha%C3%A7a" title="Cachaça">Cachaça</a></i>: the most popular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distilled" title="Distilled">distilled</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcoholic beverage</a> in Brazil; a liquor made of the distillation of sugarcane juice.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rum" title="Rum">Rum</a>: is a liquor made from sugarcane products, typically molasses but sometimes also cane juice. It is most commonly produced in the Caribbean and environs.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Basi" title="Basi">Basi</a>: is a fermented alcoholic beverage made from sugarcane juice produced in the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a>.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Panela" title="Panela">Panela</a></i>: solid pieces of sucrose and <a href="/wiki/Fructose" title="Fructose">fructose</a> obtained from the boiling and evaporation of sugarcane juice; a food staple in Colombia and other countries in South and Central America</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapadura" title="Rapadura">Rapadura</a></i>: a sweet flour that is one of the simplest refinings of sugarcane juice, common in Latin American countries such as Brazil, Argentina and Venezuela (where it is known as <i>papelón</i>) and the Caribbean.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rock_candy" title="Rock candy">Rock candy</a>: crystallized cane juice</li>
