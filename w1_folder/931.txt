<p>A <b>seismometer</b> is an instrument that measures motion of the ground, caused by, for example, an <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquake</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Types_of_volcanic_eruptions" title="Types of volcanic eruptions">volcanic eruption</a>, or the use of <a href="/wiki/Explosive" title="Explosive">explosives</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Records of seismic waves allow seismologists to map the interior of the Earth and to locate and measure the size of events like these.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_principles">Basic principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seismometer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Basic principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A simple seismometer, sensitive to up-down motions of the Earth, is like a weight hanging from a spring, both suspended from a frame that moves along with any motion detected. The relative motion between the weight (called the mass) and the frame provides a measurement of the vertical ground motion. A rotating drum is attached to the frame and a pen is attached to the weight, thus recording any ground motion in a <a href="/wiki/Seismogram" title="Seismogram">seismogram</a>.
</p><p>Any movement of the ground moves the frame.  The mass tends not to move because of its <a href="/wiki/Inertia" title="Inertia">inertia</a>, and by measuring the movement between the frame and the mass, the motion of the ground can be determined.
</p><p>Early seismometers used optical levers or mechanical linkages to amplify the small motions involved, recording on soot-covered paper or photographic paper. Modern instruments use electronics.  In some systems, the mass is held nearly motionless relative to the frame by an electronic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PID_loop" title="PID loop">negative feedback loop</a>.  The motion of the mass relative to the frame is measured, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feedback_loop" title="Feedback loop">feedback loop</a> applies a magnetic or electrostatic force to keep the mass nearly motionless. The voltage needed to produce this force is the output of the seismometer, which is recorded digitally.
</p><p>In other systems the weight is allowed to move, and its motion produces an electrical charge in a coil attached to the mass which voltage moves through the magnetic field of a magnet attached to the frame. This design is often used in a <a href="/wiki/Geophone" title="Geophone">geophone</a>, which is used in exploration for oil and gas.
</p><p>Seismic observatories usually have instruments measuring three axes: north-south (y-axis), east-west (x-axis), and vertical (z-axis). If only one axis is measured, it is usually the vertical because it is less noisy and gives better records of some seismic waves.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The foundation of a seismic station is critical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A professional station is sometimes mounted on <a href="/wiki/Bedrock" title="Bedrock">bedrock</a>.  The best mountings may be in deep boreholes, which avoid thermal effects, ground noise and tilting from weather and tides.  Other instruments are often mounted in insulated enclosures on small buried piers of unreinforced concrete. Reinforcing rods and aggregates would distort the pier as the temperature changes.  A site is always surveyed for ground noise with a temporary installation before pouring the pier and laying conduit. Originally, European seismographs were placed in a particular area after a destructive earthquake. Today, they are spread to provide appropriate coverage (in the case of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Weak-motion_seismology&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Weak-motion seismology (page does not exist)">weak-motion seismology</a>) or concentrated in high-risk regions (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Strong-motion_seismology&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Strong-motion seismology (page does not exist)">strong-motion seismology</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reitherman_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reitherman-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature">Nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seismometer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word derives from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> σεισμός, <i>seismós</i>, a shaking or quake, from the verb σείω, <i>seíō</i>, to shake; and μέτρον, <i>métron</i>, to measure, and was coined by <a href="/wiki/David_Milne-Home" title="David Milne-Home">David Milne-Home</a> in 1841, to describe an instrument designed by Scottish physicist <a href="/wiki/James_David_Forbes" title="James David Forbes">James David Forbes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ben-Menahem_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ben-Menahem-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Seismograph</i> is another Greek term from <i>seismós</i> and γράφω, <i>gráphō</i>, to draw.  It is often used to mean <i>seismometer</i>, though it is more applicable to the older instruments in which the measuring and recording of ground motion were combined, than to modern systems, in which these functions are separated.
Both types provide a continuous record of ground motion; this record distinguishes them from <b>seismoscopes</b>, which merely indicate that motion has occurred, perhaps with some simple measure of how large it was.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The technical discipline concerning such devices is called <b>seismometry</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LeeJennings2002_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-LeeJennings2002-6">[6]</a></sup> a branch of <a href="/wiki/Seismology" title="Seismology">seismology</a>.
</p><p>The concept of measuring the "shaking" of something means that the word "seismograph" might be used in a more general sense.  For example, a monitoring station that tracks changes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electromagnetic" title="Electromagnetic">electromagnetic</a> noise affecting <a href="/wiki/Amateur_radio" title="Amateur radio">amateur radio</a> waves presents an <i>rf seismograph</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  And <a href="/wiki/Helioseismology" title="Helioseismology">Helioseismology</a> studies the "quakes" on the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seismometer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first seismometer was made in China during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2nd_Century" title="2nd Century">2nd Century</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sivin_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-sivin-9">[9]</a></sup> The first Western description of the device comes from the French physicist and priest <a href="/wiki/Jean_de_Hautefeuille" title="Jean de Hautefeuille">Jean de Hautefeuille</a> in 1703.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Needham_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Needham-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The modern seismometer was developed in the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reitherman_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reitherman-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_era">Ancient era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seismometer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ancient era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EastHanSeismograph.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="552" data-file-width="480" height="253" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/EastHanSeismograph.JPG/220px-EastHanSeismograph.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/EastHanSeismograph.JPG/330px-EastHanSeismograph.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/EastHanSeismograph.JPG/440px-EastHanSeismograph.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EastHanSeismograph.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Replica of <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Heng" title="Zhang Heng">Zhang Heng</a>'s seismoscope <i>Houfeng Didong Yi</i></div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Chinese_inventions" title="List of Chinese inventions">List of Chinese inventions</a></div>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AD_132" title="AD 132">AD 132</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Heng" title="Zhang Heng">Zhang Heng</a> of China's <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han dynasty</a> invented the first seismoscope (by the definition above), which was called <i>Houfeng Didong Yi</i> (translated as, "instrument for measuring the seasonal winds and the movements of the Earth"). The description we have, from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Book_of_Later_Han" title="Book of Later Han">History of the Later Han Dynasty</a>, says that it was a large bronze vessel, about 2 meters in diameter; at eight points around the top were dragon's heads holding bronze balls.  When there was an earthquake, one of the dragons' mouths would open and drop its ball into a bronze toad at the base, making a sound and supposedly showing the direction of the earthquake. On at least one occasion, probably at the time of a large earthquake in <a href="/wiki/Gansu" title="Gansu">Gansu</a> in AD 143, the seismoscope indicated an earthquake even though one was not felt. The available text says that inside the vessel was a central column that could move along eight tracks; this is thought to refer to a pendulum, though it is not known exactly how this was linked to a mechanism that would open only one dragon's mouth. The first earthquake recorded by this seismoscope was supposedly "somewhere in the east". Days later, a rider from the east reported this earthquake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sivin_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-sivin-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-needham_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-needham-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_designs">Modern designs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seismometer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Modern designs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Milne_Horizontal_Pendulum_Seismograph.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1304" data-file-width="2316" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Milne_Horizontal_Pendulum_Seismograph.jpg/220px-Milne_Horizontal_Pendulum_Seismograph.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Milne_Horizontal_Pendulum_Seismograph.jpg/330px-Milne_Horizontal_Pendulum_Seismograph.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Milne_Horizontal_Pendulum_Seismograph.jpg/440px-Milne_Horizontal_Pendulum_Seismograph.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Milne_Horizontal_Pendulum_Seismograph.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Milne horizontal pendulum seismometer. One of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Important_Cultural_Properties_of_Japan" title="Important Cultural Properties of Japan">Important Cultural Properties of Japan</a>. Exhibit in the <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_Nature_and_Science" title="National Museum of Nature and Science">National Museum of Nature and Science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>By the 13th century, seismographic devices existed in the <a href="/wiki/Maragheh_observatory" title="Maragheh observatory">Maragheh observatory</a> in Persia. French physicist and priest <a href="/wiki/Jean_de_Hautefeuille" title="Jean de Hautefeuille">Jean de Hautefeuille</a> built one in 1703.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Needham_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Needham-10">[10]</a></sup> After 1880, most seismometers were descended from those developed by the team of <a href="/wiki/John_Milne" title="John Milne">John Milne</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Alfred_Ewing" title="James Alfred Ewing">James Alfred Ewing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Lomar_Gray" title="Thomas Lomar Gray">Thomas Gray</a>, who worked as <a href="/wiki/Foreign_government_advisors_in_Meiji_Japan" title="Foreign government advisors in Meiji Japan">foreign-government advisors</a> in Japan from 1880 to 1895.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reitherman_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Reitherman-3">[3]</a></sup>  These seismometers used damped horizontal pendulums.  After World War II, these were adapted into the widely used <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Press-Ewing_seismometer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Press-Ewing seismometer (page does not exist)">Press-Ewing seismometer</a>.
</p><p>An early special-purpose seismometer consisted of a large, stationary <a href="/wiki/Pendulum" title="Pendulum">pendulum</a>, with a <a href="/wiki/Stylus" title="Stylus">stylus</a> on the bottom.  As the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">earth</a> started to move, the heavy mass of the pendulum had the <a href="/wiki/Inertia" title="Inertia">inertia</a> to stay still within the <a href="/wiki/Frame_of_reference" title="Frame of reference">frame</a>.  The result is that the stylus scratched a pattern corresponding with the Earth's movement. This type of strong-motion seismometer recorded upon a <a href="/wiki/Smoked_glass" title="Smoked glass">smoked glass</a> (glass with carbon <a href="/wiki/Soot" title="Soot">soot</a>). While not sensitive enough to detect distant earthquakes, this instrument could indicate the direction of the pressure waves and thus help find the epicenter of a local quake. Such instruments were useful in the analysis of the <a href="/wiki/1906_San_Francisco_earthquake" title="1906 San Francisco earthquake">1906 San Francisco earthquake</a>. Further analysis was performed in the 1980s, using these early recordings, enabling a more precise determination of the initial fault break location in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marin_county" title="Marin county">Marin county</a> and its subsequent progression, mostly to the south.
</p><p>Later, professional suites of instruments for the worldwide standard seismographic network had one set of instruments tuned to oscillate at fifteen seconds, and the other at ninety seconds, each set measuring in three directions.  Amateurs or observatories with limited means tuned their smaller, less sensitive instruments to ten seconds.
The basic damped horizontal pendulum seismometer swings like the gate of a fence.  A heavy weight is mounted on the point of a long (from 10 cm to several meters) triangle, hinged at its vertical edge.  As the ground moves, the weight stays unmoving, swinging the "gate" on the hinge.
</p><p>The advantage of a horizontal pendulum is that it achieves very low frequencies of oscillation in a compact instrument.  The "gate" is slightly tilted, so the weight tends to slowly return to a central position.  The pendulum is adjusted (before the damping is installed) to oscillate once per three seconds, or once per thirty seconds.  The general-purpose instruments of small stations or amateurs usually oscillate once per ten seconds.  A pan of oil is placed under the arm, and a small sheet of metal mounted on the underside of the arm drags in the oil to damp oscillations. The level of oil, position on the arm, and angle and size of sheet is adjusted until the damping is "critical", that is, almost having oscillation.  The hinge is very low friction, often torsion wires, so the only friction is the internal friction of the wire.  Small seismographs with low proof masses are placed in a vacuum to reduce disturbances from air currents.
</p><p>Zollner described torsionally suspended horizontal pendulums as early as 1869, but developed them for gravimetry rather than seismometry.
</p><p>Early seismometers had an arrangement of levers on jeweled bearings, to scratch smoked glass or paper.  Later, mirrors reflected a light beam to a direct-recording plate or roll of photographic paper.  Briefly, some designs returned to mechanical movements to save money.  In mid-twentieth-century systems, the light was reflected to a pair of differential electronic photosensors called a photomultiplier.  The voltage generated in the photomultiplier was used to drive galvanometers which had a small mirror mounted on the axis.  The moving reflected light beam would strike the surface of the turning drum, which was covered with photo-sensitive paper.  The expense of developing photo sensitive paper caused many seismic observatories to switch to ink or thermal-sensitive paper.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_instruments">Modern instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seismometer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Modern instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CMG-40T_Triaxial_Broadband_Seismometer.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3024" data-file-width="4032" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/CMG-40T_Triaxial_Broadband_Seismometer.JPG/220px-CMG-40T_Triaxial_Broadband_Seismometer.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/CMG-40T_Triaxial_Broadband_Seismometer.JPG/330px-CMG-40T_Triaxial_Broadband_Seismometer.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/CMG-40T_Triaxial_Broadband_Seismometer.JPG/440px-CMG-40T_Triaxial_Broadband_Seismometer.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CMG-40T_Triaxial_Broadband_Seismometer.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>CMG-40T triaxial broadband seismometer</div></div></div>
<p>Modern instruments use electronic sensors, amplifiers, and recording devices. Most are broadband covering a wide range of frequencies. Some seismometers can measure motions with frequencies from 500 Hz to 0.00118 Hz (1/500 = 0.002 seconds per cycle, to 1/0.00118 = 850 seconds per cycle).  The mechanical suspension for horizontal instruments remains the garden-gate described above. Vertical instruments use some kind of constant-force suspension, such as the LaCoste suspension. The LaCoste suspension uses a <a href="/wiki/Spring_(device)#Zero-length_springs" title="Spring (device)">zero-length spring</a> to provide a long period (high sensitivity).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Some modern instruments use a "triaxial" design, in which three identical motion sensors are set at the same angle to the vertical but 120 degrees apart on the horizontal. Vertical and horizontal motions can be computed from the outputs of the three sensors.
</p><p>Seismometers unavoidably introduce some distortion into the signals they measure, but professionally designed systems have carefully characterized frequency transforms.
</p><p>Modern sensitivities come in three broad ranges: <a href="/wiki/Geophone" title="Geophone">geophones</a>, 50 to 750 <a href="/wiki/Volt" title="Volt">V</a>/m; local geologic seismographs, about 1,500 V/m; and teleseismographs, used for world survey, about 20,000 V/m. Instruments come in three main varieties: short period, long period and broadband.  The short and long period measure velocity and are very sensitive, however they 'clip' the signal or go off-scale for ground motion that is strong enough to be felt by people.  A 24-bit analog-to-digital conversion channel is commonplace.  Practical devices are linear to roughly one part per million.
</p><p>Delivered seismometers come with two styles of output: analog and digital. Analog seismographs require analog recording equipment, possibly including an analog-to-digital converter.  The output of a digital seismograph can be simply input to a computer.  It presents the data in a standard digital format (often "SE2" over <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Teleseismometers">Teleseismometers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seismometer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Teleseismometers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seismometer_kum_hg.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1798" data-file-width="1798" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Seismometer_kum_hg.jpg/220px-Seismometer_kum_hg.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Seismometer_kum_hg.jpg/330px-Seismometer_kum_hg.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Seismometer_kum_hg.jpg/440px-Seismometer_kum_hg.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Seismometer_kum_hg.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A low-frequency 3-direction <a href="/wiki/Ocean-bottom_seismometer" title="Ocean-bottom seismometer">ocean-bottom seismometer</a> (cover removed). Two masses for x- and y-direction can be seen, the third one for z-direction is below.  This model is a CMG-40TOBS, manufactured by Güralp Systems Ltd and is part of the <a class="external text" href="http://www.mbari.org/mars/default.html" rel="nofollow">Monterey Accelerated Research System</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The modern broadband seismograph can record a very broad range of <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequencies</a>.  It consists of a small "proof mass", confined by electrical forces, driven by sophisticated <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a>.  As the earth moves, the electronics attempt to hold the mass steady through a <a href="/wiki/Feedback" title="Feedback">feedback</a> circuit.  The amount of force necessary to achieve this is then recorded.
</p><p>In most designs the electronics holds a mass motionless relative to the frame.  This device is called a "force balance accelerometer".  It measures  <a href="/wiki/Peak_ground_acceleration" title="Peak ground acceleration">acceleration</a> instead of velocity of ground movement.  Basically, the distance between the mass and some part of the frame is measured very precisely, by a <a href="/wiki/Linear_variable_differential_transformer" title="Linear variable differential transformer">linear variable differential transformer</a>. Some instruments use a <a href="/wiki/Variable_capacitor" title="Variable capacitor">linear variable differential capacitor</a>.
</p><p>That measurement is then amplified by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_amplifier" title="Electronic amplifier">electronic amplifiers</a> attached to parts of an electronic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PID_loop" title="PID loop">negative feedback loop</a>.  One of the amplified currents from the negative feedback loop drives a coil very like a <a href="/wiki/Loudspeaker" title="Loudspeaker">loudspeaker</a>, except that the coil is attached to the mass, and the magnet is mounted on the frame. The result is that the mass stays nearly motionless.
</p><p>Most instruments measure directly the ground motion using the distance sensor. The voltage generated in a sense coil on the mass by the magnet directly measures the instantaneous velocity of the ground. The current to the drive coil provides a sensitive, accurate measurement of the force between the mass and frame, thus measuring directly the ground's acceleration (using f=ma where f=force, m=mass, a=acceleration).
</p><p>One of the continuing problems with sensitive vertical seismographs is the buoyancy of their masses.  The uneven changes in pressure caused by wind blowing on an open window can easily change the density of the air in a room enough to cause a vertical seismograph to show spurious signals.  Therefore, most professional seismographs are sealed in rigid gas-tight enclosures.  For example, this is why a common Streckeisen model has a thick glass base that must be glued to its pier without bubbles in the glue.
</p><p>It might seem logical to make the heavy magnet serve as a mass, but that subjects the seismograph to errors when the Earth's magnetic field moves.  This is also why seismograph's moving parts are constructed from a material that interacts minimally with magnetic fields.  A seismograph is also sensitive to changes in temperature so many instruments are constructed from low expansion materials such as nonmagnetic <a href="/wiki/Invar" title="Invar">invar</a>.
</p><p>The hinges on a seismograph are usually patented, and by the time the patent has expired, the design has been improved.  The most successful public domain designs use thin foil hinges in a clamp.
</p><p>Another issue is that the <a href="/wiki/Transfer_function" title="Transfer function">transfer function</a> of a seismograph must be accurately characterized, so that its frequency response is known.  This is often the crucial difference between professional and amateur instruments.  Most instruments are characterized on a variable frequency shaking table.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strong-motion_seismometers">Strong-motion seismometers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seismometer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Strong-motion seismometers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another type of seismometer is a digital strong-motion seismometer, or <a href="/wiki/Accelerograph" title="Accelerograph">accelerograph</a>. The data from such an instrument is essential to understand how an earthquake affects man-made structures, through <a href="/wiki/Earthquake_engineering" title="Earthquake engineering">earthquake engineering</a>. The recordings of such instruments are crucial for the assessment of <a href="/wiki/Seismic_hazard" title="Seismic hazard">seismic hazard</a>, through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Engineering_Seismology" title="Engineering Seismology">engineering seismology</a>.
</p><p>A strong-motion seismometer measures acceleration. This can be mathematically <a href="/wiki/Integral" title="Integral">integrated</a> later to give velocity and position. Strong-motion seismometers are not as sensitive to ground motions as teleseismic instruments but they stay on scale during the strongest seismic shaking.
</p><p>Strong motion sensors are used for intensity meter applications.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_forms">Other forms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seismometer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other forms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kinemetrics_seismograph.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Kinemetrics_seismograph.jpg/220px-Kinemetrics_seismograph.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Kinemetrics_seismograph.jpg/330px-Kinemetrics_seismograph.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Kinemetrics_seismograph.jpg/440px-Kinemetrics_seismograph.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kinemetrics_seismograph.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Kinemetrics seismograph, formerly used by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Interior" title="United States Department of the Interior">United States Department of the Interior</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Accelerographs and <a href="/wiki/Geophone" title="Geophone">geophones</a> are often heavy cylindrical magnets with a spring-mounted coil inside.  As case moves, the coil tends to stay stationary, so the magnetic field cuts the wires, inducing current in the output wires.  They receive frequencies from several hundred hertz down to 1 Hz.  Some have electronic damping, a low-budget way to get some of the performance of the closed-loop wide-band geologic seismographs.
</p><p>Strain-beam accelerometers constructed as integrated circuits are too insensitive for geologic seismographs (2002), but are widely used in geophones.
</p><p>Some other sensitive designs measure the current generated by the flow of a non-corrosive ionic fluid through an <a href="/wiki/Electret" title="Electret">electret</a> sponge or a conductive fluid through a <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interconnected_seismometers">Interconnected seismometers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seismometer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Interconnected seismometers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Seismometers spaced in an array can also be used to precisely locate, in three dimensions, the source of an earthquake, using the time it takes for <a href="/wiki/Seismic_wave" title="Seismic wave">seismic waves</a> to propagate away from the <a href="/wiki/Hypocenter" title="Hypocenter">hypocenter</a>, the initiating point of <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)" title="Fault (geology)">fault</a> rupture (See also <a href="/wiki/Earthquake_location" title="Earthquake location">Earthquake location</a>).  Interconnected seismometers are also used, as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Monitoring_System" title="International Monitoring System">International Monitoring System</a> to detect underground <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_test" title="Nuclear test">nuclear test</a> explosions, as well as for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earthquake_early_warning" title="Earthquake early warning">Earthquake early warning</a> systems. These seismometers are often used as part of a large scale governmental or scientific project, but some organizations such as the <a href="/wiki/Quake-Catcher_Network" title="Quake-Catcher Network">Quake-Catcher Network</a>, can use residential size detectors built into computers to detect earthquakes as well.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Reflection_seismology" title="Reflection seismology">reflection seismology</a>, an array of seismometers image sub-surface features.  The data are reduced to images using algorithms similar to <a href="/wiki/Tomographic_reconstruction" title="Tomographic reconstruction">tomography</a>.  The data reduction methods resemble those of computer-aided tomographic medical imaging X-ray machines (CAT-scans), or imaging <a href="/wiki/Sonar" title="Sonar">sonars</a>.
</p><p>A worldwide array of seismometers can actually image the interior of the Earth in wave-speed and transmissivity.  This type of system uses events such as earthquakes, <a href="/wiki/Impact_event" title="Impact event">impact events</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_explosion" title="Nuclear explosion">nuclear explosions</a> as wave sources.  The first efforts at this method used manual data reduction from paper seismograph charts.  Modern digital seismograph records are better adapted to direct computer use.  With inexpensive seismometer designs and internet access, amateurs and small institutions have even formed a "public seismograph network".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Seismographic systems used for petroleum or other mineral exploration historically used an explosive and a wireline of <a href="/wiki/Geophone" title="Geophone">geophones</a> unrolled behind a truck.  Now most short-range systems use "thumpers" that hit the ground, and some small commercial systems have such good digital signal processing that a few sledgehammer strikes provide enough signal for short-distance refractive surveys.  Exotic cross or two-dimensional arrays of geophones are sometimes used to perform three-dimensional reflective imaging of subsurface features.  Basic linear refractive geomapping software (once a black art) is available off-the-shelf, running on laptop computers, using strings as small as three geophones.  Some systems now come in an 18" (0.5 m) plastic field case with a computer, display and printer in the cover.
</p><p>Small seismic imaging systems are now sufficiently inexpensive to be used by civil engineers to survey foundation sites, locate bedrock, and find subsurface water.
</p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Seismogram" title="Seismogram">Seismogram</a></div>
<p>Today, the most common recorder is a computer with an analog-to-digital converter, a disk drive and an internet connection; for amateurs, a PC with a sound card and associated software is adequate. Most systems record continuously, but some record only when a signal is detected,
as shown by a short-term increase in the variation of the signal, compared to its long-term
average (which can vary slowly because of changes in seismic noise)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, also known as a STA/LTA trigger.
</p><p>Prior to the availability of digital processing of seismic data in the late 1970s, the records were done in a few different forms on different types of media. A "Helicorder" drum was a device used to record data into photographic paper or in the form of paper and ink. A "Develocorder" was a machine that record data from up to 20 channels into a 16-mm film. The recorded film can be viewed by a machine. The reading and measuring from these types of media can be done by hand. After the digital processing has been used, the archives of the seismic data were recorded in magnetic tapes. Due to the deterioration of older magnetic tape medias, large number of waveforms from the archives are not recoverable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
