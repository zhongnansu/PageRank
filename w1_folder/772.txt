<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Product (business)</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>In marketing, a <b>product</b> is anything that can be offered to a <a href="/wiki/Market_(economics)" title="Market (economics)">market</a> that might satisfy a want or need.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retailing" title="Retailing">retailing</a>, products are called <i><a href="/wiki/Merchandising" title="Merchandising">merchandise</a></i>. In <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a>, products are bought as <a href="/wiki/Raw_material" title="Raw material">raw materials</a> and sold as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finished_goods" title="Finished goods">finished goods</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Service_(economics)" title="Service (economics)">service</a> is another common product type.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Commodity" title="Commodity">Commodities</a> are usually raw materials such as metals and agricultural products, but a commodity can also be anything widely available in the open market. In <a href="/wiki/Project_management" title="Project management">project management</a>, products are the formal definition of the <a href="/wiki/Product_breakdown_structure" title="Product breakdown structure">project deliverables</a> that make up or contribute to delivering the objectives of the project. In insurance, the policies are considered products offered for sale by the insurance company that created the contract. In <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Commerce" title="Commerce">commerce</a>, products belong to a broader category of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Good_(economics)" title="Good (economics)">goods</a>. The economic meaning of product was first used by political economist <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>A related concept is that of a sub-product, a secondary but useful result of a <a href="/wiki/Production_(economics)" title="Production (economics)">production</a> process.
</p><p>Dangerous products, particularly physical ones, that cause injuries to consumers or bystanders may be subject to <a href="/wiki/Product_liability" title="Product liability">product liability</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Product_classification">Product classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Product_(business)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Product classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A product can be classified as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tangible_asset" title="Tangible asset">tangible</a> or <a href="/wiki/Intangible_asset" title="Intangible asset">intangible</a>. A tangible product is a physical object that can be perceived by touch such as a building, vehicle, gadget, or clothing. An intangible product is a product that can only be perceived indirectly such as an insurance policy. <a href="/wiki/Service_(economics)" title="Service (economics)">Services</a> can be broadly classified under intangible products which can be durable or non durable.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_use">By use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Product_(business)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: By use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In its online product catalog, retailer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sears,_Roebuck_and_Company" title="Sears, Roebuck and Company">Sears, Roebuck and Company</a> divides its products into "departments", then presents products to potential shoppers according to (1) function or (2) brand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Each product has a Sears item-number and a manufacturer's model-number. Sears uses the departments and product groupings with the intention of helping customers browse products by function or brand within a traditional <a href="/wiki/Department_store" title="Department store">department-store</a> structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_association">By association</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Product_(business)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: By association">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A product line is "a group of products that are closely related, either because they function in a similar manner, are sold to the same customer groups, are marketed through the same types of outlets, or fall within given price ranges."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
Many businesses offer a range of product lines which may be unique to a single organization or may be common across the business's industry. In 2002 the US Census compiled revenue figures for the finance and insurance industry by various product lines such as "accident, health and medical insurance premiums" and "income from secured consumer loans".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Within the <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a> industry, product lines are indicated by the type of risk coverage, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auto_insurance" title="Auto insurance">auto insurance</a>, commercial <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Life_insurance" title="Life insurance">life insurance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_and_international_product_classifications">National and international product classifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Product_(business)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: National and international product classifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Various classification systems for products have been developed for economic statistical purposes. The NAFTA signatories are working on a system that classifies products called NAPCS as a companion to <a href="/wiki/North_American_Industry_Classification_System" title="North American Industry Classification System">North American Industry Classification System</a> (NAICS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The European Union uses a "Classification of Products by Activity" among other product classifications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The United Nations also classifies products for international economic activity reporting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <b>Aspinwall Classification System</b> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> classifies and rates products based on five variables:
</p>
<ol><li>Replacement rate (How frequently is the product repurchased?)</li>
<li>Gross margin (How much profit is obtained from each product?)</li>
<li>Buyer goal adjustment (How flexible are the buyers' purchasing habits with regard to this product?)</li>
<li>Duration of product satisfaction (How long will the product produce benefits for the user?)</li>
<li>Duration of buyer search behavior (How long will consumers shop for the product?)</li></ol>
<p>The National Institute of Governmental Purchasing (NIGP)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> developed a commodity and services classification system for use by state and local governments, the <a href="/wiki/NIGP_Code" title="NIGP Code">NIGP Code</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>  The NIGP Code is used by 33 states within the United States as well as thousands of cities, counties and political subdivisions.  The NIGP Code is a hierarchical schema consisting of a 3 digit class, 5 digit class-item, 7 digit class-item-group and an 11 digit class-item-group-detail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>  Applications of the NIGP Code include vendor registration, inventory item identification, contract item management, spend analysis and strategic sourcing.
</p><p><span id="Product_Model"></span>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Product_model">Product model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Product_(business)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Product model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A manufacturer usually provides an identifier for each particular type of product they make, known as a <b>model</b>, <b>model variant</b>, or <b>model number</b> (often abbreviated as <b>MN</b>, <b>M/N</b> or <b>model no.</b>). For example, <a href="/wiki/Dyson_(company)" title="Dyson (company)">Dyson Ltd</a>, a manufacturer of appliances (mainly vacuum cleaners), requires customers to identify their model in the support section of the website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Brand" title="Brand">Brand</a> and model can be used together to identify products in the market. The model number is not necessarily the same as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manufacturer_part_number" title="Manufacturer part number">manufacturer part number</a> (MPN).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Because of the huge amount of similar products in the <a href="/wiki/Automotive_industry" title="Automotive industry">automotive industry</a>, there is a special kind of defining a car with options (marks, attributes), that represent the characteristics features of the vehicle. A model of a car is defined by some basic options like body, engine, gear box and axles. The variants of a model are built by some additional options like color, seats, wheels, mirrors, trims, entertainment and assistant systems etc. Options, that exclude each other (pairwise) build an option-family. That means, that you can choose only one option by each family and you have to choose exactly one option. This kind of product definition fulfill the requirements of an ideal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boolean_Algebra" title="Boolean Algebra">Boolean Algebra</a> and can be helpful to construct a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Product_configurator" title="Product configurator">product configurator</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Sometimes, a set of options (car features) are combined to an automotive package and are offered by a lower price. A consistent car definition is essential for the <a href="/wiki/Production_planning" title="Production planning">production planning</a> and control in the automotive industry, to generate a <a href="/wiki/Master_production_schedule" title="Master production schedule">master production schedule</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> which is the fundamental for the <a href="/wiki/Enterprise_resource_planning" title="Enterprise resource planning">enterprise resource planning</a>.
</p><p>In addition, a specific unit of a product is usually (and has to be) identified by a <a href="/wiki/Serial_number" title="Serial number">serial number</a>, which is necessary to distinguish products with the same product definition. In the case of automotive products it's called the Vehicle Identification Number <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VIN" title="VIN">VIN</a>, an international standardized format.
</p>
