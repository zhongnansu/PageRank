<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Virtual water</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Virtual water trade</b> (also known as trade in <b>embedded</b> or <b>embodied water</b>) refers to the hidden flow of water if food or other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commodities" title="Commodities">commodities</a> are <a href="/wiki/Trade" title="Trade">traded</a> from one place to another.  For instance, it takes 1,340 cubic meters of water (based on the world average) to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crop_production" title="Crop production">produce</a> one metric tonne of <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>. The precise volume can be more or less depending on climatic conditions and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agricultural" title="Agricultural">agricultural</a> practice. Hoekstra and Chapagain have defined the virtual-water content of a product (a commodity, good or service) as "the volume of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a> used to produce the product, measured at the place where the product was actually produced".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It refers to the sum of the water use in the various steps of the production chain.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/King%27s_College_London" title="King's College London">King's College London</a> and the <a href="/wiki/SOAS,_University_of_London" title="SOAS, University of London">SOAS, University of London</a> introduced the virtual water concept,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> to support his argument that countries in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> can save their scarce water resources by relying more on import of food. For his contributions he was awarded the 2008 <a href="/wiki/Stockholm_Water_Prize" title="Stockholm Water Prize">Stockholm Water Prize</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Allan stated: "The water is said to be virtual because once the wheat is grown, the real water used to grow it is no longer actually contained in the wheat. The concept of virtual water helps us realize how much water is needed to produce different goods and services.  In semi-arid and arid areas, knowing the virtual water value of a good or service can be useful towards determining how best to use the scarce water available."
</p><p>There are, however, significant deficiencies with the concept of virtual water that mean there is a significant risk in relying on these measures to guide policy conclusions. Accordingly, Australia's National Water Commission considers that the measurement of virtual water has little practical value in decision making regarding the best allocation of scarce water resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trade">Trade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Trade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Virtual water trade</b> refers to the idea that when goods and services are exchanged, so is virtual water.  When a country imports one tonne of wheat instead of producing it domestically, it is saving about 1,300 cubic meters of real indigenous water.  If this country is <a href="/wiki/Water_scarcity" title="Water scarcity">water-scarce</a>, the water that is 'saved' can be used towards other ends.  If the exporting country is water-scarce, however, it has exported 1,300 cubic meters of virtual water since the real water used to grow the wheat will no longer be available for other purposes. This has obvious strategic implications for countries that are water-constrained such as those found in the Southern African Development Community (SADC) area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Water-scarce countries like <a href="/wiki/State_of_Palestine" title="State of Palestine">Palestine</a> discourage the export of oranges (relatively heavy water guzzlers) precisely to prevent large quantities of water being exported to different parts of the world.
</p><p>In recent years, the concept of virtual water trade has gained weight both in the scientific as well as in the political debate. The notion of the concept is ambiguous. It changes between an analytical, descriptive concept and a political induced strategy.  As an analytical concept, virtual water trade represents an instrument which allows the identification and assessment of policy options not only in the scientific but also in the political discourse.  As a politically induced strategy the question is, whether virtual water trade can be implemented in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainable" title="Sustainable">sustainable</a> way, whether the implementation can be managed in a social, economical and ecological fashion, and for which countries the concept offers a meaningful option.
</p><p>The data that underlie the concept of virtual water can readily be used to construct water satellite accounts, and brought into economic models of international trade such as the <a href="/wiki/GTAP" title="GTAP">GTAP</a> Computable General Equilibrium Model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Such a model can be used to study the economic implications of changes in water supply or water policy, as well as the water resource implications of economic development and trade liberalisation.
</p><p>In sum, virtual water trade allows a new, amplified perspective on water problems: In the framework of recent developments from a supply-oriented to a demand-oriented management of water resources it opens up new fields of <a href="/wiki/Governance" title="Governance">governance</a> and facilitates a differentiation and balancing of different perspectives, basic conditions and interests.  Analytically, the concept enables one to distinguish between global, regional and local levels and their linkages. This means, that water resource problems have to be solved in <i>problemsheds</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> if they cannot be successfully addressed in the local or regional watershed. Virtual water trade can thus overcome the hydro-centricity of a narrow watershed view. According to the proceedings of a 2006 conference in <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a>, Germany, it seems reasonable to link the new concept with the approach of <a href="/wiki/Integrated_water_resources_management" title="Integrated water resources management">integrated water resources management</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations_of_the_virtual_water_measure">Limitations of the virtual water measure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Limitations of the virtual water measure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Key shortcomings of virtual water measures are that the concept:
</p>
<ol><li>It relies on an assumption that all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_source" title="Water source">sources of water</a>, whether in the form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainfall" title="Rainfall">rainfall</a> or provided through an <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> system, are of equal value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dpi_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-dpi-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>It implicitly assumes that water that would be released by reducing a high water use activity would necessarily be available for use in a less water-intensive activity. For example, the implicit assumption is that water used in rangeland beef production would be available to be used to produce an alternative, less water-intensive activity. As a practical matter this may not be the case, nor might the alternatives be economic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dpi_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-dpi-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>It fails as an indicator of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_harm" title="Environmental harm">environmental harm</a> nor does it provide any indication of whether <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_resource" title="Water resource">water resources</a> are being used within sustainable extraction limits. The use of virtual water estimates therefore offer no guidance for policy makers seeking to ensure that environmental objectives are being met.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dpi_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-dpi-11">[11]</a></sup></li></ol>
<p>The deficiencies with the concept of virtual water mean that there is a significant risk in relying on these measures to guide policy conclusions. Accordingly, Australia's National Water Commission considers that the measurement of virtual water has little practical value in decision making regarding the best allocation of scarce water resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nwc_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-nwc-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other limitations more specific to the MENA (Middle East &amp; <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>) region include:
</p>
<ol><li>In MENA rural societies, farmers are by tradition politically influential and would prohibit new policies for water allocation. Reallocating the water resources adds a huge burden on the farmers especially when a large portion of those farmers use their land for their own food consumption which happens to be their only source of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_supply" title="Food supply">food supply</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>Importing food could pose the risk of further political dependence. The notion of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self_sufficiency" title="Self sufficiency">self sufficiency</a>" has always been the pride of the MENA region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-osd_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-osd-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>The use of virtual water lies in the religious regulations for charging for water. According to Al-Bukhari, Prophet Mohammad's teachings, the Prophet said: "People are partners in three: Water, Herbs and Fire" (referring to basic energy resources). Therefore, and because farmers are generally poor and rain water, rivers and lakes are like a gift from God, the MENA countries might find it difficult to charge the farmers the full cost for water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-osd_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-osd-14">[14]</a></sup></li></ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_footprint">Water footprint</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Water footprint">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The concept of virtual water trade was introduced to refer to the idea that countries can save domestic water by importing food. Imported food, however, comes from somewhere. In 2002, professor Arjen Y. Hoekstra, at the time working for UNESCO-IHE, now at University of Twente, the Netherlands, introduced the concept of <a href="/wiki/Water_footprint" title="Water footprint">water footprint</a>. The water footprint shows the link between consumer goods or a consumption pattern and water use and pollution. Virtual water trade and water footprint can be seen as part of a bigger story: the globalization of water.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Embodied_energy">Embodied energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Embodied energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some researchers have attempted to use the methods of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_analysis" title="Energy analysis">energy analysis</a>, which aim to produce <a href="/wiki/Embodied_energy" title="Embodied energy">embodied energy</a> estimates, to derive virtual, or embodied water estimates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lenzen_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-lenzen-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
