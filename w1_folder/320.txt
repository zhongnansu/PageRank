<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Air gun</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Air_gun_(disambiguation)" title="Air gun (disambiguation)">Air gun (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>An <b>air gun</b> (or <b>airgun</b>) is any kind of <a href="/wiki/Gun" title="Gun">gun</a> that launches <a href="/wiki/Projectile" title="Projectile">projectiles</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pneumatic" title="Pneumatic">pneumatically</a> with <a href="/wiki/Compressed_air" title="Compressed air">compressed air</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gases</a> that are pressurized <i>mechanically</i> without involving any <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reactions</a>, in contrast to a <a href="/wiki/Firearm" title="Firearm">firearm</a>, which pressurizes gases <i>chemically</i> via an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidation</a> (<a href="/wiki/Deflagration" title="Deflagration">deflagration</a>) of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combustible" title="Combustible">combustible</a> <a href="/wiki/Propellant" title="Propellant">propellants</a> which generates propulsive energy by breaking <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_bond" title="Molecular bond">molecular bonds</a>.  Both the <a href="/wiki/Long_gun" title="Long gun">long gun</a> and <a href="/wiki/Handgun" title="Handgun">handgun</a> forms (<b>air rifle</b> and <b>air pistol</b>) typically propel metallic projectiles, that are either <a href="/wiki/Diabolo" title="Diabolo">diabolo</a>-shaped <a href="/wiki/Pellet_(air_gun)" title="Pellet (air gun)">pellets</a>, or spherical <a href="/wiki/Shot_(pellet)" title="Shot (pellet)">shots</a> called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BB_(ammunition)" title="BB (ammunition)">BBs</a>.  Certain types of air guns (usually rifles) may also propel <a href="/wiki/Flechette" title="Flechette">darts</a> or <a href="/wiki/Arrow" title="Arrow">arrows</a>.
</p><p>The first air guns were developed as early as the 1500s.  They have been used in hunting, sporting and warfare.  Modern air guns use one of three types of power source depending on the design: spring-piston, pneumatic, and <a href="/wiki/Bottled_gas" title="Bottled gas">bottled</a> compressed gas (most commonly <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Air_gun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:KunitomoAirGun.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="629" data-file-width="2500" height="55" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/KunitomoAirGun.jpg/220px-KunitomoAirGun.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/KunitomoAirGun.jpg/330px-KunitomoAirGun.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/KunitomoAirGun.jpg/440px-KunitomoAirGun.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:KunitomoAirGun.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Kunitomo_air_gun" title="Kunitomo air gun">Kunitomo air gun</a> developed by the Japanese inventor <a href="/wiki/Kunitomo_Ikkansai" title="Kunitomo Ikkansai">Kunitomo Ikkansai</a>, circa 1820–1830</div></div></div>
<p>Air guns represent the oldest <a href="/wiki/Pneumatics" title="Pneumatics">pneumatic</a> technology.  The oldest existing mechanical air gun, a bellows air gun dating back to about 1580, is in the <a href="/wiki/Livrustkammaren" title="Livrustkammaren">Livrustkammaren</a> Museum in <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a>.  This is the time most historians recognize as the beginning of the modern air gun.
</p><p>Throughout 17th to 19th century, air guns in <a href="/wiki/Caliber" title="Caliber">calibers</a> .30–.51, were used to hunt big-game <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">deer</a> and wild <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boar" title="Boar">boar</a>. These air rifles were charged using a pump to fill an air reservoir and gave velocities from 650 to 1,000 feet per second (200–300 m/s).  They were also used in warfare, the most recognized example being the <a href="/wiki/Girandoni_air_rifle" title="Girandoni air rifle">Girandoni air rifle</a>.
</p><p>At that time, they had compelling advantages over the primitive firearms of the day.  For example, air guns could be discharged in wet weather and rain (unlike both <a href="/wiki/Matchlock" title="Matchlock">matchlock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flintlock" title="Flintlock">flintlock</a> <a href="/wiki/Musket" title="Musket">muskets</a>), and discharged much faster than <a href="/wiki/Muzzleloader" title="Muzzleloader">muzzle-loading guns</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Philip_2006_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Philip_2006-1">[1]</a></sup> Moreover, they were quieter than a firearm of similar caliber, had no muzzle flash, and were smokeless. Thus, they did not disclose the shooter's position or obscure the shooter's view, unlike the black powder muskets of the 18th and 19th centuries.
</p><p>In the hands of skilled soldiers, they gave the military a distinct advantage. France, Austria and other nations had special <a href="/wiki/Sniper" title="Sniper">sniper</a> detachments using air rifles. The Austrian 1770 model was named <i>Windbüchse</i> (literally "wind rifle" in German). The gun was developed in 1768 or 1769<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> by the <a href="/wiki/German_Tyrol" title="German Tyrol">Tyrolean</a> watchmaker, mechanic and gunsmith Bartholomäus Girandoni (1744–1799) and is sometimes referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Girandoni_air_rifle" title="Girandoni air rifle">Girandoni air rifle</a> or Girandoni air gun in literature (the name is also spelled "Girandony," "Giradoni"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> or "Girardoni".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>) The <i>Windbüchse</i> was about 4 ft (1.2 m) long and weighed 10 pounds (4.5 kg), about the same size and mass as a conventional <a href="/wiki/Musket" title="Musket">musket</a>. The air reservoir was a removable, club-shaped, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stock_(firearm)" title="Stock (firearm)">butt</a>. The <i>Windbüchse</i> carried twenty-two .51 <a href="/wiki/Caliber" title="Caliber">caliber</a> (13 mm) lead balls in a tubular <a href="/wiki/Magazine_(firearms)" title="Magazine (firearms)">magazine</a>. A skilled shooter could fire off one magazine in about thirty seconds. A shot from this air gun could penetrate an inch thick wooden board at a hundred paces, an effect roughly equal to that of a modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/9%C3%9719mm" title="9×19mm">9×19mm</a> or <a href="/wiki/.45_ACP" title=".45 ACP">.45 ACP</a> caliber pistol.
</p><p>Circa 1820, the Japanese inventor <a href="/wiki/Kunitomo_Ikkansai" title="Kunitomo Ikkansai">Kunitomo Ikkansai</a> developed various manufacturing methods for guns, and also created an air gun based on the study of Western knowledge ("<a href="/wiki/Rangaku" title="Rangaku">rangaku</a>") acquired from the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dejima" title="Dejima">Dejima</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:KunitomoAirGunMechanism.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1152" data-file-width="1536" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/KunitomoAirGunMechanism.jpg/220px-KunitomoAirGunMechanism.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/KunitomoAirGunMechanism.jpg/330px-KunitomoAirGunMechanism.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/KunitomoAirGunMechanism.jpg/440px-KunitomoAirGunMechanism.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:KunitomoAirGunMechanism.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Kunitomo air gun trigger mechanism</div></div></div>
<p>The celebrated <a href="/wiki/Lewis_and_Clark_Expedition" title="Lewis and Clark Expedition">Lewis and Clark Expedition</a> (1804) carried a reservoir air gun. It held 22 .46 caliber round balls in a tubular magazine mounted on the side of the <a href="/wiki/Gun_barrel" title="Gun barrel">barrel</a>. The butt served as the air reservoir and had a working pressure of 800 psi (55 bar). The rifle was said to be capable of 22 aimed shots per minute and had a rifled bore of 0.452 in (11.5 mm) and a groove diameter 0.462 in (11.7 mm).
</p><p>One of the first commercially successful and mass-produced air guns was manufactured by the William F. Markham's Markham Air Rifle Company in <a href="/wiki/Plymouth,_Michigan" title="Plymouth, Michigan">Plymouth, Michigan</a>.  Their first model air gun was the wooden <i>Challenger</i> and marketed in 1886.  In response, Clarence Hamilton from the neighboring Plymouth Air Rifle Company (later renamed to <a href="/wiki/Daisy_Outdoor_Products" title="Daisy Outdoor Products">Daisy Manufacturing Company</a> in 1895) marketed their all-metal Daisy BB Gun in early 1888, which prompted Markham to respond with their <i>Chicago</i> model in 1888 followed by the <i>King</i> model in 1890.  The <i>Chicago</i> model was sold by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sears,_Roebuck" title="Sears, Roebuck">Sears, Roebuck</a> for 73 cents in its catalog.  In 1928 the name of the Markham company was changed to King Air Rifle Company after the company was purchased by Daisy in 1916 after decades of intense competition,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and continued to manufacture the "King" model air rifle until 1935 before ceasing operation all together in the 1940s.
</p><p>During the 1890s, air rifles were used in <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a>, England, for competitive <a href="/wiki/Shooting_sports" title="Shooting sports">target shooting</a>. Matches were held in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_house" title="Public house">public houses</a>, which sponsored shooting teams. Prizes, such as a leg of <a href="/wiki/Lamb_and_mutton" title="Lamb and mutton">mutton</a> for the winning team, were paid for by the losing team. The sport became so popular that in 1899, the <a href="/wiki/National_Smallbore_Rifle_Association" title="National Smallbore Rifle Association">National Smallbore Rifle Association</a> was created. During this time over 4,000 air rifle clubs and associations existed across <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, many of them in Birmingham. During this time, the air gun was associated with <a href="/wiki/Poaching" title="Poaching">poaching</a> because it could deliver a shot without a significant report.
</p><p>In terms of power modern air guns are capable of delivering high levels of energy, the recently showcased Umarex Hammer fires a .50 caliber air bullet delivering over 700 ft lbs of energy (950 Joules).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use">Use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Air_gun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Air guns are used for <a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">hunting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pest_control" title="Pest control">pest control</a>, recreational <a href="/wiki/Shooting" title="Shooting">shooting</a> (commonly known as <a href="/wiki/Plinking" title="Plinking">plinking</a>), and competitive sports, such as the <a href="/wiki/Shooting_at_the_Summer_Olympics" title="Shooting at the Summer Olympics">Olympic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/10_m_Air_Rifle" title="10 m Air Rifle">10 m Air Rifle</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/10_m_Air_Pistol" title="10 m Air Pistol">10 m Air Pistol</a> events. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Field_Target" title="Field Target">Field Target</a> (FT) is a competitive form of target shooting in which the targets are knock-down metal silhouettes of animals, with a 'kill zone' cut out of the steel plate. <a href="/wiki/Hunter_Field_Target" title="Hunter Field Target">Hunter Field Target</a> (HFT) is a variation, using identical equipment, but with differing rules. The distances FT and HFT competitions are shot at range between 7.3 and 41.1 metres (24 and 135 ft) for HFT &amp; 7.3 and 50.29 metres (24.0 and 165.0 ft)for FT, with varying sizes of 'reducers' being used to increase or decrease the size of the kill zone. In the UK, competition power limits are set at the legal maximum for an unlicensed air rifle, i.e. 12 <a href="/wiki/Foot-pound_(energy)" title="Foot-pound (energy)">ft⋅lbf</a> (16 J).
</p><p>The increasing affordability of higher-power PCP rifles has allowed large projectiles and further target distance for competition purposes. For instance, the Extreme Benchrest competition held annually in <a href="/wiki/Green_Valley,_Arizona" title="Green Valley, Arizona">Green Valley, Arizona</a> allows calibers up to .35 inches (8.9 mm) and targets at 75 and 100 yards (69 and 91 m), while the Big Bore Benchrest arm of the same competition engages targets at 35 to 300 yards (32 to 274 m).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Powerplant">Powerplant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Air_gun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Powerplant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The different methods of powering an air gun can be broadly divided into three groups: spring-piston, pneumatic, and <a href="/wiki/Compressed_fluid" title="Compressed fluid">compressed</a> CO<sub>2</sub>.  These methods are used in both air rifles and air pistols.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-airguntypes_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-airguntypes-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spring-piston">Spring-piston</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Air_gun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Spring-piston">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Spring-piston air guns</b> (or simply "spring guns" or "springers") operate by means of a <a href="/wiki/Coil_spring" title="Coil spring">coil spring</a>-loaded <a href="/wiki/Piston_pump" title="Piston pump">piston pump</a> assembly contained within a compression chamber separate from the <a href="/wiki/Gun_barrel" title="Gun barrel">gun barrel</a>.  Before shooting, the user need to manually cock the gun by flexing a lever connected to the pump assembly.  The cocking drives the pump piston backwards and compress the spring until its rear engages the <a href="/wiki/Sear_(firearm)" title="Sear (firearm)">sear</a>.  The act of pulling the <a href="/wiki/Trigger_(firearms)" title="Trigger (firearms)">trigger</a> disengages the sear, allows the spring to decompress and release the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elastic_potential_energy" title="Elastic potential energy">elastic potential energy</a> stored within it, and pushes the piston forward thereby compressing the air in the pump cylinder.  Because the pump outlet (located to the front of the pump) is directly behind the pellet sitting in the barrel <a href="/wiki/Chamber_(firearms)" title="Chamber (firearms)">chamber</a>, once the air pressure has risen enough to overcome any static friction and/or barrel restriction holding back the pellet, the pellet is propelled forward by an expanding column of pressurized air.  All this takes place in a fraction of a second, during which the air undergoes <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_process#Adiabatic_heating_and_cooling" title="Adiabatic process">adiabatic heating</a> to several hundred degrees and then cools as the air expands.  This can also cause a phenomenon referred as "dieseling", where flammable substances in the compression chamber (e.g. petroleum-based lubricant) can be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compression_ignition" title="Compression ignition">ignited by the compression heat</a> like in a <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">diesel engine</a>, and lead to an <a href="/wiki/Afterburner" title="Afterburner">afterburner</a> effect with (often unpredictable) additional thrusts, as well as combustion smoke coming out of the muzzle upon firing.  Most spring-piston guns are <a href="/wiki/Single-shot" title="Single-shot">single-shot</a> <a href="/wiki/Breechloader" title="Breechloader">breechloaders</a> by nature, but multiple-shot <a href="/wiki/Repeating_rifle" title="Repeating rifle">repeaters</a> with <a href="/wiki/Magazine_(firearms)" title="Magazine (firearms)">magazine</a> feeders have become more common in recent years.
</p><p>Spring-piston guns, especially the high-powered "magnum" guns, are able to achieve muzzle velocities near or exceeding the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">speed of sound</a>.  The effort required for the cocking stroke is usually related to the designed power of the gun, with higher muzzle velocities requiring a stiffer spring and hence a greater cocking effort.  Spring-piston guns have a practical upper limit of 1,250 ft/s (380 m/s) for .177 cal (4.5 mm) pellets, as higher velocities cause unstable pellet flight and loss of accuracy.  This is due to the extreme <a href="/wiki/Aeroelasticity#Buffeting" title="Aeroelasticity">buffeting</a> caused when the pellet reaches and surpasses <a href="/wiki/Transonic" title="Transonic">transonic</a> speed, then slows back down and goes through <a href="/wiki/Sound_barrier" title="Sound barrier">sound barrier</a> again, which is more than enough to destabilize the pellet's flight.  Shortly after leaving the barrel, the supersonic pellet falls back below the speed of sound and the shock wave overtakes the pellet, causing its flight stability to be disrupted.  Drag increases rapidly as pellets are pushed past the speed of sound, so it is generally better to increase pellet weight to keep velocities subsonic in high-powered guns.  Sonic crack from the pellet as it moves with supersonic speed also makes the shot louder sometimes making it possible to be mistaken for firearm discharge.  Many shooters have found that velocities in the 800–900 ft/s (240–270 m/s) range offer an ideal balance between power and pellet stability.
</p><p>Spring guns are typically cocked by one of the following mechanisms:
</p>
<ul><li>Break-barrel — like a <a href="/wiki/Break_action" title="Break action">break action</a> firearm, the barrel is hinged at the junction with the <a href="/wiki/Receiver_(firearms)" title="Receiver (firearms)">receiver</a> and can be flexed downwards to expose the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breech_(firearms)" title="Breech (firearms)">breech</a> and also serve as a cocking lever itself</li>
<li>Fixed-barrel — the barrel is fixed to the receiver, and the cocking is done with an additional cocking lever
<ul><li>Underlever — the cocking lever is located underneath the barrel and is flexed downwards during cocking</li>
<li>Sidelever — the cocking lever is located to the side (usually the right side) of the receiver and is flexed sideways during cocking</li>
<li>Overlever — the cocking lever is located above the barrel and is flexed upwards during cocking, seen in some air pistols</li></ul></li>
<li>Motorized cocking powered by a rechargeable battery</li></ul>
<p>Spring-piston guns, especially high-powered ("magnum") models, do still <a href="/wiki/Recoil" title="Recoil">recoil</a> as a result of the forward motion of the piston.  Although the recoil is less than that of some <a href="/wiki/Cartridge_(firearms)" title="Cartridge (firearms)">cartridge</a> firearms, it can make the gun difficult to shoot accurately as the recoil forces are in effect whilst the pellet is still within the barrel.  Spring gun recoil also has a sharp forward component, caused by the piston hitting the front end of the pump chamber when the spring has fully decompressed.  These two rapid jerking movements are known to damage <a href="/wiki/Telescopic_sight" title="Telescopic sight">scopes</a> not rated for spring gun usage.  In addition, the spring vibrations are often unpredictable and can also cause accuracy to suffer.  These vibrations can be controlled by adding features like close-fitting spring guides or by aftermarket tuning done by "airgunsmiths" who specialize in air gun modifications, a common one being the addition of viscous <a href="/wiki/Silicone_grease" title="Silicone grease">silicone grease</a> to the spring, which both lubricates it and dampens vibration.  Some shooters also often hold the gun in a very loose grip (the "artillery hold" as coined by famous American airgunner Tom Gaylord) that allows the gun to vibrate in a natural and consistent manner.  There are also newer models with a floating receiver design that allows the <a href="/wiki/Action_(firearms)" title="Action (firearms)">action</a> to slide over the <a href="/wiki/Stock_(firearms)" title="Stock (firearms)">stock</a> autonomously, thus making the gun less hold-sensitive.
</p><p>The better quality spring guns can have very long service lives, being simple to maintain and repair.  Because they deliver the same mechanical energy output on each shot, the external ballistic is quite consistent.  Most <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympic</a> air gun competitions through the 1970s and into the 1980s were shot with spring-piston guns, often of the opposing-piston recoil-eliminating type.  Beginning in the 1980s, guns powered by compressed/liquefied <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> began to dominate the competition.  Today, the guns used at the highest levels of competition are powered by <a href="/wiki/Compressed_air" title="Compressed air">compressed air</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_spring">Gas spring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Air_gun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Gas spring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Some makes of air guns incorporate a <a href="/wiki/Gas_spring" title="Gas spring">gas spring</a> (commonly referred to as a <i>gas piston</i>, <i>gas ram</i>, <i>gas strut</i> or <i>nitro piston</i>) instead of a mechanical coil spring.  The spring itself is essentially a stand-alone enclosed <a href="/wiki/Piston_pump" title="Piston pump">piston pump</a> without outlet and with pressurized air or <a href="/wiki/Inert_gas" title="Inert gas">inert gas</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>) held tightly sealed within the <a href="/wiki/Cylinder_(engine)" title="Cylinder (engine)">cylinder</a>.  When the gun is cocked, the gas inside the cylinder gets further compressed by the piston, stores <a href="/wiki/Potential_energy" title="Potential energy">potential energy</a> and acts in effect as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pneumatic" title="Pneumatic">pneumatic</a> <a href="/wiki/Accumulator_(energy)" title="Accumulator (energy)">accumulator</a>.  Gas spring units require higher precision to manufacture, since they require a low-friction sliding seal that can withstand the high pressures when cocked.  The advantages of the gas spring include the ability to keep the gun cocked and ready to fire for extended periods of time without long-term spring <a href="/wiki/Fatigue_(material)" title="Fatigue (material)">fatigue</a>, smoother recoil pattern and lower vibration, and faster "lock time" (the time between pulling the trigger and the pellet being discharged) which results in better accuracy.  Gas spring air guns are also usually less "hold-sensitive" and hence easier to achieve consistent <a href="/wiki/Shot_grouping" title="Shot grouping">shot groupings</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pneumatic">Pneumatic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Air_gun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pneumatic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Walther_LGR_Match_Universal.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="160" data-file-width="622" height="57" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/ae/Walther_LGR_Match_Universal.jpg/220px-Walther_LGR_Match_Universal.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/ae/Walther_LGR_Match_Universal.jpg/330px-Walther_LGR_Match_Universal.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/ae/Walther_LGR_Match_Universal.jpg/440px-Walther_LGR_Match_Universal.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Walther_LGR_Match_Universal.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Walther_LGR" title="Walther LGR">Walther LGR</a> single-stroke pneumatic match air rifle</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:New_Benelli_Kite.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1547" data-file-width="3740" height="91" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/New_Benelli_Kite.jpg/220px-New_Benelli_Kite.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/New_Benelli_Kite.jpg/330px-New_Benelli_Kite.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/New_Benelli_Kite.jpg/440px-New_Benelli_Kite.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:New_Benelli_Kite.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An example of a <a href="/wiki/Benelli_Kite" title="Benelli Kite">Benelli Kite</a> pre-charged pneumatic air pistol, as used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/10_m_Air_Pistol" title="10 m Air Pistol">10 metre air pistol</a> <a href="/wiki/ISSF_shooting_events" title="ISSF shooting events">ISSF shooting events</a></div></div></div>
<p>Pneumatic air guns propel the projectiles by utilizing the pneumatic potential energy within <a href="/wiki/Compressed_air" title="Compressed air">compressed air</a>, which is pressurized beforehand and stored inside the gun, and then released through valves during shooting.  Single-stroke and multi-stroke pump guns utilize an on-board pump to pressurize air in an internal reservoir, while pre-charged pneumatic guns' reservoirs are filled from an external source using either a high-pressure <a href="/wiki/Hand_pump" title="Hand pump">hand pump</a> or by decanting air from a <a href="/wiki/Diving_cylinder" title="Diving cylinder">diving cylinder</a>.  
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Pump_pneumatic">Pump pneumatic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Air_gun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Pump pneumatic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Pump pneumatc air guns, or pump guns, use a lever-operated onboard air pump to pressurize an internal reservoir, which then discharge the stored compressed air during shooting. Depend on the design, pump guns can be either single-stroke or mult-stroke.
</p>
<ul><li>In <i>single-stroke</i> pneumatic air guns, a single motion of the cocking lever is all that is required to mechanically compress the air.  The single-pump system has always dominated the casual plinking market, and is usually found in target rifles and pistols, where the higher muzzle energy of a multi-stroke pumping system is not required.  Single-stroke pneumatic rifles dominated the national and international <a href="/wiki/International_Shooting_Sport_Federation" title="International Shooting Sport Federation">ISSF</a> <a href="/wiki/ISSF_shooting_events" title="ISSF shooting events">10 metre air rifle shooting events</a> from the 1970s up to the 1990s.</li></ul>
<ul><li><i>Multi-stroke</i> pneumatic air guns use multiple pumpings to achieve variable power levels in order to adapt for both long or short-range shooting.  This air gun are usually single-shot, which each shot requires approximately 5 strokes.  However, up to five shots are possible, usually requiring around 10 to 20 strokes as long as the air reservoir is enough to store higher pressure.  For safety reasons, most multi-stroke guns are usually designed to have its pump lever jammed when the reservoir has reached its maximum pressure limit, which the user can no longer pump the gun until it's discharged.  The maximum pressure limit for the reservoir is approximately 20 to 30 strokes.</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-charged_pneumatic">Pre-charged pneumatic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Air_gun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pre-charged pneumatic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:20ozCO2Condor.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="1600" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/20ozCO2Condor.JPG/220px-20ozCO2Condor.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/20ozCO2Condor.JPG/330px-20ozCO2Condor.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/20ozCO2Condor.JPG/440px-20ozCO2Condor.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:20ozCO2Condor.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Airforce Condor, one of the most powerful PCP air rifles on the market</div></div></div>
<p>Pre-charged pneumatic (PCP) air guns have their internal reservoir pre-filled from an external air source (such as a <a href="/wiki/Diving_cylinder" title="Diving cylinder">diving cylinder</a>, or by charging with a <a href="/wiki/Hand_pump" title="Hand pump">hand pump</a>), and remains pressurized until depleted after repeated shooting.  During shooting, the hammer strikes the reservior's release valve, allowing a set volume of the pressurized air to be discharged into the chamber and propel the projectile.  Depending on the release valve design, PCP air guns can be categorized into two types — <i>unregulated</i> and <i>regulated</i> (which has either a mechanical or electronic <a href="/wiki/Pressure_regulator" title="Pressure regulator">regulator</a> valve).  Because of the need for cylinders or charging systems, PCP guns have higher initial costs but much lower operating costs when compared to CO<sub>2</sub> rifles, and have superior performance over ordinary pump guns.  Having no significant movement of heavy mechanical parts during the discharge cycle, PCP airgun designs produce lower <a href="/wiki/Recoil" title="Recoil">recoil</a>, and can shoot as many as 100 shots per charge depending on the tank/reservoir size.
</p><p>The ready supply of air has allowed the development of semi- and fully automatic air guns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> PCP guns are very popular in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK" title="UK">UK</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> because of their accuracy and ease of use.  They are widely utilized in <a href="/wiki/ISSF_shooting_events" title="ISSF shooting events">ISSF 10 metre air pistol and rifle shooting events</a>, the sport of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Field_Target" title="Field Target">Field Target</a> shooting,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and are usually fitted with <a href="/wiki/Telescopic_sight" title="Telescopic sight">telescopic sights</a>.
</p><p>Early hand-pump designs encountered problems of fatigue (both human and mechanical), temperature warping, and condensation — none of which are beneficial to accurate shooting or the airguns' longevity.  Modern hand pumps have built-in air filtration systems and have overcome many of these problems.  Using scuba-quality air decanted from a <a href="/wiki/Diving_cylinder" title="Diving cylinder">scuba cylinder</a> provides consistently clean, dry, high-pressure air.
</p><p>During the typical PCP's discharge cycle, the hammer of the rifle is released by the sear to strike the <a href="/wiki/Bash_valve" title="Bash valve">bash valve</a>.  The hammer may move rearwards or forwards, unlike firearms where the hammer almost always moves forward.  The valve is held closed by a spring and the pressure of the air in the reservoir.  The pressure of the spring is constant, and the pressure of the air released (which is also known as the <i>working pressure</i>) decreases with each successive shot.  As a result, when the reservoir pressure is high, the valve opens less fully and closes faster than when the reservoir pressure is lower, resulting in a similar total volume of air flowing past the valve with each shot.  This results in a degree of partial self-regulation that gives a greater consistency of velocity from shot to shot, which corresponds to the middle "plateau" phase of the gun's shot-to-shot <a href="/wiki/Muzzle_velocity" title="Muzzle velocity">muzzle velocity</a> profile (also known as the <i>power curve</i> ).  A well-designed PCP will display good shot-to-shot consistency over longer period as the air reservoir is being depleted.
</p><p>Other PCP rifles and pistols are regulated, i.e. the firing valve operates within a secondary chamber separated from the main air reservoir by the regulator body. The regulator maintains the pressure within this secondary chamber at a set pressure (lower than the main reservoir's) until the main reservoir's pressure drops to the point where it can no longer do so.  As a result, shot-to-shot consistency is maintained for longer than in an unregulated rifle, and the gun can also output more shots due to reduced waste of reservoir pressure.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CO2">CO<sub>2</sub></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Air_gun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: CO2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BB_gun_with_CO2_and_BBs.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1773" data-file-width="1977" height="179" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/BB_gun_with_CO2_and_BBs.jpg/200px-BB_gun_with_CO2_and_BBs.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/BB_gun_with_CO2_and_BBs.jpg/300px-BB_gun_with_CO2_and_BBs.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/BB_gun_with_CO2_and_BBs.jpg/400px-BB_gun_with_CO2_and_BBs.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BB_gun_with_CO2_and_BBs.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>CO<sub>2</sub> pistol and disposable Powerlet cylinders</div></div></div>
<p>Originally called <i>CG guns</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compressed_gas" title="Compressed gas">compressed gas</a> guns),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> air guns utilizing prefilled removable <a href="/wiki/Gas_cylinder" title="Gas cylinder">gas cylinders</a> as power source have now become known as <i>CO<sub>2</sub> guns</i> due to the ubiquitous commercial use of <a href="/wiki/Bottled_gas" title="Bottled gas">bottled</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> gas.  Most CO<sub>2</sub> guns use a disposable cylinder called a "<a href="/wiki/Powerlet" title="Powerlet">Powerlet</a>", that is often purchased with 12 grams (0.42 oz) of pressurized CO<sub>2</sub> gas, although some, usually more expensive models, use larger refillable CO<sub>2</sub> reservoirs like those typically used with <a href="/wiki/Paintball_marker" title="Paintball marker">paintball markers</a>.
</p><p>CO<sub>2</sub> guns, like other pneumatic guns using compressed air, offer power for repeated shots in a compact package without the need for complex cocking or filling mechanisms.  The ability to store power for repeated shots also means that repeating arms are possible. There are many replica revolvers and semi-automatic pistols on the market that use CO<sub>2</sub> power.  These guns are popular for training, as the guns and ammunition are inexpensive, relatively safe to use, and no specialized facilities are needed for safety.  In addition, they can be purchased and owned in areas where firearms possession is either strictly controlled or banned outright.  Most CO<sub>2</sub> powered guns are relatively inexpensive, and there are a few precision target guns available that use CO<sub>2</sub>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ammunition">Ammunition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Air_gun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ammunition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pellet">Pellet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Air_gun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Pellet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pellet_(air_gun)" title="Pellet (air gun)">Pellet (air gun)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:177cal.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="426" data-file-width="568" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/177cal.jpg/250px-177cal.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/177cal.jpg/375px-177cal.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/177cal.jpg/500px-177cal.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:177cal.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A .177 (4.5mm) caliber "Wadcutter" pellet next to a stick of chewing gum</div></div></div>
<p>The most popular ammunition used in rifled air guns is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wasp-waist" title="Wasp-waist">wasp-waisted</a> <b><a href="/wiki/Diabolo" title="Diabolo">diabolo</a> pellet</b>, which has two sections — a solid front portion called the "head", which contains the <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">center of mass</a> and is available in a variety of shapes and styles such as flat (<a href="/wiki/Wadcutter" title="Wadcutter">wadcutter</a>), round (domed), cone-shaped (pointed) and pitted (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hollow_point" title="Hollow point">hollow point</a>); and a hollowed, thin-walled rear portion called the "skirt", which expands and fully engages the bore to provide a good <a href="/wiki/Seal_(mechanical)" title="Seal (mechanical)">seal</a>, thus allows maximal efficiency in pellet propulsion during shooting.  In flight, the skirt has greater <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_drag" title="Air drag">drag</a>-to-weight ratio than the head, and provides drag-stabilization similar to that of a <a href="/wiki/Shuttlecock" title="Shuttlecock">shuttlecock</a>.  This shape design also means that the overall pellet will have poor <a href="/wiki/Ballistic_coefficient" title="Ballistic coefficient">ballistic coefficient</a> and tends to be more unstable than some other projectile shapes, especially in the <a href="/wiki/External_ballistics#The_transonic_problem" title="External ballistics">transonic region</a> (272–408 m/s ~ 893–1340 ft/s).  Diabolo pellets are traditionally made from <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, but can also be manufactured from <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>, or a combination of materials such as <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> alloys with <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a> tips.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Slug">Slug</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Air_gun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Slug">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Some manufacturers also have recently introduced the more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cylindrical" title="Cylindrical">cylindrical</a>-shaped "<a href="/wiki/Slug_(projectile)" title="Slug (projectile)">slug</a>" pellets for the more powerful modern PCP air rifles.  Compared to the commonly used diabolo pellets, these slug pellets resemble <a href="/wiki/Mini%C3%A9_ball" title="Minié ball">Minié balls</a> and have more contact surface with the bore and hence needs greater propelling force to overcome friction, but have better <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamics" title="Aerodynamics">aerodynamics</a> and longer effective ranges due to the more similar shape to firearm <a href="/wiki/Bullet" title="Bullet">bullets</a>, however also require a fully <a href="/wiki/Rifling" title="Rifling">rifled</a> barrel for spin stabilization in flight.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="BB">BB</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Air_gun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: BB">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BB_copper_and_nickel_plated.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1773" data-file-width="2064" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/BB_copper_and_nickel_plated.jpg/200px-BB_copper_and_nickel_plated.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/BB_copper_and_nickel_plated.jpg/300px-BB_copper_and_nickel_plated.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/BB_copper_and_nickel_plated.jpg/400px-BB_copper_and_nickel_plated.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BB_copper_and_nickel_plated.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Steel BBs coated with copper and nickel</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/BB_gun" title="BB gun">BB gun</a></div>
<p>The BB was once the most common airgun ammunition in the USA, thanks to the popularity of <a href="/wiki/Daisy_Outdoor_Products" title="Daisy Outdoor Products">Daisy</a>’s line of BB airguns. A BB is a small metallic ball, typically made of steel (with a copper or zinc coating) or lead, in 4.5 mm/.177" diameter.  Originally called the “round shot”, the contemporary name came from the “BB”- size lead birdshot used in shotguns, which the first BB gun invented in 1886 was designed to shoot.  Steel BBs can be acceptably accurate at short distances when fired from properly designed <a href="/wiki/BB_gun" title="BB gun">BB guns</a> with <a href="/wiki/Smoothbore" title="Smoothbore">smoothbore</a> barrels.  Lead number 3 buckshot pellets can be used in .25" caliber airguns as if they were large BBs.
</p><p>Due to the hardness of the steel, they can not "take" to rifled barrels, which is why they are undersized (4.4 against 4.5 mm) to allow them to be used in .177" rifled barrels, which when used in this configuration can in effect be considered <a href="/wiki/Smoothbore" title="Smoothbore">smoothbore</a>, but with a poorer gas-seal. Were they 4.5 mm diameter, they would jam in the bore. Therefore, BB's lack the spin stabilization required for long-range accuracy, and usage in any but the cheapest rifled guns is discouraged.
</p><p>Typically BBs are used for indoor practice, casual outdoor plinking, training children, or for air gun enthusiasts who like to practice, but cannot afford high-powered air gun systems that use pellets. Some shotgunners use sightless BB rifles to train in instinctive shooting. Similar guns were also used briefly by the United States Army in a Vietnam-era instinctive shooting program called "<a href="/wiki/Point_shooting#Rifle_Quick_Kill" title="Point shooting">Quick Kill</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Darts_and_arrows">Darts and arrows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Air_gun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Darts and arrows">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 18th and 19th centuries air gun darts were popular, largely because they could be reused. Although less popular now, several different types of darts are made to be used in air guns. But it is not recommended that darts be used in air guns with <a href="/wiki/Rifling" title="Rifling">rifled</a> bores or in spring-powered air guns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Air guns that shoot darts are sometimes called <i>dart guns</i>, and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tranquilizer_gun" title="Tranquilizer gun">tranquilizer guns</a></i> if the darts used are loaded with <a href="/wiki/Anesthetic" title="Anesthetic">anesthetic</a> (<a href="/wiki/Tranquilizer" title="Tranquilizer">tranquilizer</a>) compounds.
</p><p>Some modern air guns are designed to discharge arrows and are called <i>arrow guns</i> or sometimes <i>airbows</i>. These arrows are designed with a hollow shaft that is open in the rear where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nock_(arrow)" title="Nock (arrow)">nock</a> would normally be. When loaded, the hollow arrow shaft is slid rearwards over a barrel whose external diameter is only fractionally smaller than the shaft's interior diameter, providing a close enough fitting that minimizes rattling and gives reasonable enveloping <a href="/wiki/Seal_(mechanical)" title="Seal (mechanical)">seal</a> without causing too much <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a>. During shooting, the trigger releases high-pressure air from the barrel to the front portion of the hollow arrow shaft, pushing the arrow forward.  Such air guns can shoot arrows at launch velocities rivalling or even exceeding high-end <a href="/wiki/Crossbow" title="Crossbow">crossbows</a>, while retaining consistency of precision unaffected by <a href="/wiki/Archer%27s_paradox" title="Archer's paradox">archer's paradox</a>, but they are also more expensive to setup and maintain.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Calibers">Calibers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Air_gun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Calibers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The most common air gun <a href="/wiki/Caliber" title="Caliber">calibers</a> are
</p>
<ul><li>.177 (4.5 mm): the most common caliber. Mandated by the ISSF for use in international target shooting competition at 10m, up to Olympic level in both rifle and pistol events. It has also been adopted by most National Governing Bodies for domestic use in similar target shooting events. It has the flattest trajectory of all the calibers for a given energy level, making accuracy simpler. At suitable energy levels it can be used effectively for hunting.</li>
<li>.22 (5.5 mm &amp; 5.6 mm): for hunting and general use. In recent years air rifles and pistols in .22" (and some other calibers) have been allowed for use in both domestic and international target shooting in events not controlled by the ISSF. Most notably in FT/HFT and Smallbore Benchrest competitions. These events often allow the use of any caliber air gun, up to a maximum which is often .22", rather than a fixed caliber.</li></ul>
<p>Other less common traditional calibers include:
</p>
<ul><li>.20 (5 mm): initially proprietary to the Sheridan multi-pump pneumatic air rifle, later more widely used.</li>
<li>.25 (6.35 mm): the largest commonly available caliber for most of the 20th century.</li>
<li>.30 (7.62 mm): the current largest available for non-PCP powered airguns</li></ul>
<p>Larger caliber air rifles suitable for hunting large animals are offered by major manufacturers. These are usually PCP guns. The major calibers available are:
</p>
<ul><li>.357</li>
<li>.45 (11.43 mm)</li>
<li>.50 (12.7 mm)</li>
<li>.58 (14.5 mm)</li></ul>
<p>Custom air guns are available in even larger calibers such as 20 mm (0.79") or .87 (22.1 mm).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legislation">Legislation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Air_gun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Legislation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Air_gun_laws" title="Air gun laws">Air gun laws</a></div>
<p>While in some countries air guns are not subject to any specific regulation, in most there are laws, which differ widely. Each jurisdiction has its own definition of an air gun; and regulations may vary for weapons of different bore, <a href="/wiki/Muzzle_energy" title="Muzzle energy">muzzle energy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Muzzle_velocity" title="Muzzle velocity">velocity</a>, or material of ammunition, with guns designed to fire <a href="/wiki/Pellet_(air_gun)" title="Pellet (air gun)">metal pellets</a> often more tightly controlled than <a href="/wiki/Airsoft" title="Airsoft">airsoft</a> weapons. There may be minimum ages for possession, and sales of both air guns and ammunition may be restricted. Some areas require permits and background checks similar to those required for proper firearms.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_and_misuse">Safety and misuse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Air_gun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Safety and misuse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While historical air guns have been made specifically for warfare, modern air guns can also be deadly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stuff.co.nz_3916775_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stuff.co.nz_3916775-12">[12]</a></sup> In medical literature, modern air guns have been noted as a cause of death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> This has been the case for guns of caliber .177 and .22 that are within the legal muzzle energy of air guns in the United Kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
