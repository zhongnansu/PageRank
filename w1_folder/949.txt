<p><b>Occidental Petroleum Corporation</b> (often abbreviated <b>Oxy</b> in reference to its <a href="/wiki/Ticker_symbol" title="Ticker symbol">ticker symbol</a>) is an American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multinational_company" title="Multinational company">multinational</a> petroleum and natural gas exploration and production company incorporated in <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a> and headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a>, Texas with operations in the United States, the Middle East, and Latin America. The company is ranked 722nd on the <a href="/wiki/Forbes_Global_2000" title="Forbes Global 2000">Forbes Global 2000</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and 278th on the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2017, the company was the 3rd largest petroleum producer and 21st largest natural gas producer in <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foundation">Foundation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Foundation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Occidental Petroleum was founded in 1920 in California. In 1957, <a href="/wiki/Armand_Hammer" title="Armand Hammer">Armand Hammer</a> was elected president and chief executive officer of the company after he acquired a controlling stake in the firm for tax reasons. In 1961, the company discovered the Lathrop Gas Field in <a href="/wiki/Lathrop,_California" title="Lathrop, California">Lathrop, California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moscow_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-moscow-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1960s, the company expanded internationally with operations in <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a>, and the United Kingdom. Occidental won exploration rights in Libya in 1965<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moscow_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-moscow-5">[5]</a></sup> and operated there until all activities were suspended in 1986 after the United States imposed economic sanctions on Libya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-libya_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-libya-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1968, the company entered the chemical business with the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Hooker_Chemical_Company" title="Hooker Chemical Company">Hooker Chemical Company</a>, 26 years after the contamination at <a href="/wiki/Love_Canal" title="Love Canal">Love Canal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-love_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-love-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1971, the company received permission to develop an <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">oil refinery</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canvey_Island" title="Canvey Island">Canvey Island</a> in Essex. The company began construction but stopped in 1975 as a result of the <a href="/wiki/1970s_energy_crisis" title="1970s energy crisis">1970s energy crisis</a>. The site remained derelict; the tanks and the chimney were subsequently demolished. Only some concrete foundations and the river jetty remain extant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1972, the company was one of the first companies to research developing <a href="/wiki/Oil_shale" title="Oil shale">oil shale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1981, the company acquired <a href="/wiki/IBP,_Inc." title="IBP, Inc.">IBP, Inc.</a>, one of largest producers of beef and pork products in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ibp_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ibp-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1983, the company and Ecopetrol, the state-owned oil company, discovered the giant <a href="/wiki/Ca%C3%B1o_Lim%C3%B3n_oilfield" title="Caño Limón oilfield">Caño Limón oilfield</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>In April 1988, the company acquired <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cain_Chemical" title="Cain Chemical">Cain Chemical</a> for $2 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>On July 6, 1988, an explosion and subsequent inferno on the company's <a href="/wiki/Piper_Alpha" title="Piper Alpha">Piper Alpha</a> platform in the Scottish North Sea, resulted in 167 fatalities in what remains the world's most deadly offshore disaster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-piper_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-piper-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>In September 1988, the company formed a joint venture with <a href="/wiki/Church_%26_Dwight" title="Church &amp; Dwight">Church &amp; Dwight</a>, which makes <a href="/wiki/Arm_%26_Hammer" title="Arm &amp; Hammer">Arm &amp; Hammer</a> products, for a potassium carbonate plant at <a href="/wiki/Muscle_Shoals,_Alabama" title="Muscle Shoals, Alabama">Muscle Shoals, Alabama</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1990, Armand Hammer died and Dr. <a href="/wiki/Ray_R._Irani" title="Ray R. Irani">Ray R. Irani</a> became chairman and chief executive officer of the company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1991, the company sold its stake in <a href="/wiki/IBP,_Inc." title="IBP, Inc.">IBP, Inc.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ibp_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-ibp-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1993, the company sold its remaining coal operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coal_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-coal-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 1996, the company announced layoffs of 450 people in its chemical division.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 1996, the company sold its interest in 3 oilfields in the Congo to the Congolese government for $215 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1997, the company paid $3.65 billion to acquire the <a href="/wiki/Elk_Hills_Oil_Field" title="Elk Hills Oil Field">Elk Hills Oil Field</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2005, the company and its partner, Liwa, won 8 out of 15 exploration spots on the EPSA-4 auction, making both companies among the first to enter the Libyan market since the United States lifted its embargo on Libya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2005, the company acquired Vintage Petroleum for $3.8 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2006, the government of Ecuador seized the company's interest in block 15 of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazon_Rainforest" title="Amazon Rainforest">Amazon Rainforest</a> in Ecuador, forcing the company to take a $306 million after-tax charge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In 2016, Ecuador agreed to pay $980 million in restitution to the company, down from the original award of $1.77 billion. The agreement was based on a 2012 arbitration award from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Center_for_Settlement_of_Investment_Disputes" title="International Center for Settlement of Investment Disputes">International Center for Settlement of Investment Disputes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2007, the company's compensation policies came under scrutiny after it was announced that Irani received $460 million in compensation in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In May 2011, Irani retired as CEO after <a href="/wiki/CalSTRS" title="CalSTRS">CalSTRS</a> and Relational Investors, two major shareholders, objected to the company's compensation policies for top executives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> President Stephen I. Chazen was named CEO to replace Irani and in 2013, shareholders ousted Irani as chairman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Despite his outlandish compensation, during Irani's tenure, the company grew from a collection of unrelated businesses to one that focuses on oil and gas and the <a href="/wiki/Market_capitalization" title="Market capitalization">market capitalization</a> of the company went from $5.5 billion to $80 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2008, the company acquired a 10% stake in <a href="/wiki/Plains_All_American_Pipeline" title="Plains All American Pipeline">Plains All American Pipeline</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The company also acquired assets from <a href="/wiki/Plains_Exploration_%26_Production" title="Plains Exploration &amp; Production">Plains Exploration &amp; Production</a> for $1.3 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2009, the company closed its Louisville OxyVinyl <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">polyvinyl chloride</a> production plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2009, the company acquired <a href="/wiki/Citigroup" title="Citigroup">Citigroup</a>'s controversial <a href="/wiki/Phibro" title="Phibro">Phibro</a> energy-trading business, for its net asset value of approximately $250 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The unit was managed by <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Hall_(hedge_fund_manager)" title="Andrew Hall (hedge fund manager)">Andrew J. Hall</a>, who received compensation of approximately $100 million per year in 2007 and 2008. After the acquisition, the division reported its first losses since the 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> In 2016, Phibro was wound down and sold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>In December 2010, the company acquired shale oil properties in the <a href="/wiki/Williston_Basin" title="Williston Basin">Williston Basin</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_Dakota" title="North Dakota">North Dakota</a> for $1.4 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> These assets, as well as other assets acquired by Oxy in the Williston Basin, were sold in 2015 for $600 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>In December 2010, the company sold its proven and probable reserves of 393 million 393 million <a href="/wiki/Barrel_of_oil_equivalent" title="Barrel of oil equivalent">barrels of oil equivalent</a> (2.40<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>9</sup> GJ) in Argentina to <a href="/wiki/Sinopec" title="Sinopec">Sinopec</a>, a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/China_Petrochemical_Corporation" title="China Petrochemical Corporation">China Petrochemical Corporation</a>. It also acquired properties in South Texas and North Dakota for $3.2 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>In January 2011, Occidental partnered with Abu Dhabi's state oil company in developing the Shah Field, one of the largest natural gas fields in the Middle East, through a joint venture known as Al Hosn Gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Al Hosn Gas became operational in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2013, Oxychem sold its investment in <a href="/wiki/Unipar_Carbocloro" title="Unipar Carbocloro">Unipar Carbocloro</a> for R$550 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>In September 2014, the company moved its headquarters to Houston, Texas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 2014, the company sold its 50% interest in BridgeTex Pipeline Company, owner of a 300,000 barrel-per-day crude oil pipeline system that extends from <a href="/wiki/Colorado_City,_Texas" title="Colorado City, Texas">Colorado City, Texas</a> to <a href="/wiki/Texas_City,_Texas" title="Texas City, Texas">Texas City, Texas</a>, for $1.075 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>In December 2014, the company distributed 80.5% of its shares in California Resources Corporation, the largest producer of oil and natural gas on a gross-operated barrels of oil equivalent basis in California, to Occidental shareholders<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> and distributed its remaining stake to shareholders in March 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2015, the company completed the first phase of a $500 million carbon dioxide flooding project in <a href="/wiki/Hobbs,_New_Mexico" title="Hobbs, New Mexico">Hobbs, New Mexico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>In January 2016, the company sold an office tower in <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas</a>, Texas for $95 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2016, <a href="/wiki/Vicki_Hollub" title="Vicki Hollub">Vicki Hollub</a>, who had worked at Occidental since 1981 and joined the board in 2015, became the chief executive officer of the company, the first female to serve as chief executive officer of a major U.S. oil and gas company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2017, the company and its 50/50 joint venture partner <a href="/wiki/Mexichem" title="Mexichem">Mexichem</a> began operations of a 1.2-billion-pound per year capacity <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">ethylene</a> cracker at the OxyChem plant in <a href="/wiki/Ingleside,_Texas" title="Ingleside, Texas">Ingleside, Texas</a>, along with pipelines and storage at <a href="/wiki/Markham,_Texas" title="Markham, Texas">Markham, Texas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2017, Christopher Stavros, the chief financial officer of the company, retired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2017, the company sold land in the Permian Basin for $600 million and used the proceeds to acquire other assets in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_operations">Current operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Current operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oil_and_gas">Oil and gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Oil and gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The company's oil and gas operations are concentrated in three geographic areas: the United States, Latin America, and the Middle East. As of December 31, 2017, Occidental had 2.598 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1000000000_(number)" title="1000000000 (number)">billion</a> <a href="/wiki/Barrel_of_oil_equivalent" title="Barrel of oil equivalent">barrels of oil equivalent</a> (1.589<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>10</sup> GJ) of oil equivalent net proved reserves, of which 58% was petroleum, 17% was natural gas liquids, and 25% was natural gas. In 2017, the company had production of 603 thousand <a href="/wiki/Barrel_of_oil_equivalent" title="Barrel of oil equivalent">barrels of oil equivalent</a> (3,690,000 GJ) per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10K_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 2017, the company's United States operations produced 304 thousand <a href="/wiki/Barrel_of_oil_equivalent" title="Barrel of oil equivalent">barrels of oil equivalent</a> (1,860,000 GJ) per day, representing 50% of the company's worldwide production. Substantially of this production was in the <a href="/wiki/Permian_Basin_(North_America)" title="Permian Basin (North America)">Permian Basin</a>, where Occidental is the largest operator and oil producer. The company produced 141 thousand <a href="/wiki/Barrel_of_oil_equivalent" title="Barrel of oil equivalent">barrels of oil equivalent</a> (860,000 GJ) per day from <a href="/wiki/Unconventional_oil" title="Unconventional oil">unconventional oil</a> via Permian Resources and 150 thousand <a href="/wiki/Barrel_of_oil_equivalent" title="Barrel of oil equivalent">barrels of oil equivalent</a> (920,000 GJ) per day using a technique called <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">enhanced oil recovery</a>, whereby <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and water are injected into underground formations to extract the oil and gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10K_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_East">Middle East</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Middle East">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The company's oil and gas operations in the Middle East are in <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Qatar" title="Qatar">Qatar</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a> and are via <a href="/wiki/Production_sharing_agreement" title="Production sharing agreement">production sharing agreements</a>. The region produced 268 thousand <a href="/wiki/Barrel_of_oil_equivalent" title="Barrel of oil equivalent">barrels of oil equivalent</a> (1,640,000 GJ) per day, representing approximately 44% of 2017 total worldwide production. The region also held 28% of the company's proved reserves in 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10K_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The company is the largest independent oil producer in Oman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> In Qatar, the company is the second-largest oil producer offshore and is a partial owner in the <a href="/wiki/Dolphin_Gas_Project" title="Dolphin Gas Project">Dolphin Gas Project</a>, which delivers gas to Oman and the United Arab Emirates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Latin_America">Latin America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Latin America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Latin America, which accounted for 31 thousand <a href="/wiki/Barrel_of_oil_equivalent" title="Barrel of oil equivalent">barrels of oil equivalent</a> (190,000 GJ) per day of production, or 5% of total production in 2017, the company operates in <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, where it operates the <a href="/wiki/Ca%C3%B1o_Lim%C3%B3n_oilfield" title="Caño Limón oilfield">Caño Limón oilfield</a>. Occidental's share of production from its Latin America assets was approximately 34 thousand <a href="/wiki/Barrel_of_oil_equivalent" title="Barrel of oil equivalent">barrels of oil equivalent</a> (210,000 GJ) per day in 2017, which included 33 thousand <a href="/wiki/Barrel_of_oil_equivalent" title="Barrel of oil equivalent">barrels of oil equivalent</a> (200,000 GJ) per day from Colombia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10K_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical">Chemical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Chemical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>OxyChem manufactures <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">polyvinyl chloride</a> (PVC) resins, <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> (caustic soda) used in plastics, pharmaceuticals and water treatment chemicals. Other products manufactured by the company include caustic <a href="/wiki/Potash" title="Potash">potash</a>, chlorinated organics, sodium silicates, chlorinated <a href="/wiki/Cyanuric_acid" title="Cyanuric acid">cyanuric acid</a> (isocyanurate), and <a href="/wiki/Calcium_chloride" title="Calcium chloride">calcium chloride</a>. OxyChem has manufacturing facilities in the United States, Canada and Chile. In a joint venture with <a href="/wiki/Church_%26_Dwight" title="Church &amp; Dwight">Church &amp; Dwight</a>, OxyChem owns Armand Products Company, which sells <a href="/wiki/Potassium_carbonate" title="Potassium carbonate">potassium carbonate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Potassium_bicarbonate" title="Potassium bicarbonate">potassium bicarbonate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10K_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lobbying_to_do_business_in_Libya">Lobbying to do business in Libya</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Lobbying to do business in Libya">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The company began operations in <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a> in 1965 and operated there until <a href="/wiki/Economic_sanctions" title="Economic sanctions">economic sanctions</a> were imposed in 1986 by the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-libya_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-libya-6">[6]</a></sup> The company was one of the first American companies to resume negotiations in Libya after the sanctions were lifted in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> In 2008, the company, along with 5 other oil companies, was criticized for hiring <a href="/wiki/Hogan_Lovells" title="Hogan Lovells">Hogan Lovells</a> to lobby to exempt Libya from a law written by U.S. Senator <a href="/wiki/Frank_Lautenberg" title="Frank Lautenberg">Frank Lautenberg</a> (D-NJ) to assist American terror victims in seizing assets of countries found culpable in terror attacks, such as the Libyan bombing of <a href="/wiki/Pan_Am_Flight_103" title="Pan Am Flight 103">Pan Am Flight 103</a> over <a href="/wiki/Lockerbie" title="Lockerbie">Lockerbie</a> in 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> and to remove a provision in the <a href="/wiki/Dodd%E2%80%93Frank_Wall_Street_Reform_and_Consumer_Protection_Act" title="Dodd–Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act">Dodd–Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act</a> that requires disclosure of payments to foreign governments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> In early 2011, the company ceased exploration activities and production operations in Libya due to the growing civil unrest in the country and U.S. sanctions. In June 2011, the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission">U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission</a> and United Kingdom prosecutors requested information from the company, <a href="/wiki/ExxonMobil" title="ExxonMobil">ExxonMobil</a> and <a href="/wiki/ConocoPhillips" title="ConocoPhillips">ConocoPhillips</a> related to the <a href="/wiki/Libyan_Investment_Authority" title="Libyan Investment Authority">Libyan Investment Authority</a> (LIA), an investment firm controlled by Libyan leader <a href="/wiki/Muammar_Gaddafi" title="Muammar Gaddafi">Muammar Gaddafi</a>, to determine if there were any violations of international bribery laws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ruffel_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ruffel-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> The Libyan Investment Authority's investments were frozen by the U.S. government in early 2011 following the Gaddafi regime's attacks on Libyan civilians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ruffel_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ruffel-66">[66]</a></sup> In 2016, the company ceased operations in Libya.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_record">Environmental record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Environmental record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2017, the company was ranked 55th on the Carbon Majors Report, a list of the Top 100 producers and their cumulative greenhouse gas emissions from 1988-2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2017, 25% of the company's production was from a method of oil extraction called <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage#Sequestration" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon dioxide (CO2) flood technology</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">carbon sequestration</a> for <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">enhanced oil recovery</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10K_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">[1]</a></sup> which is a more environmental-friendly method of oil extraction because it could stall the accumulation of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> in the atmosphere by instead injecting it into the ground. The company has stated that this is one way in which it reduces emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cleanup_of_the_Copper_Basin">Cleanup of the Copper Basin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Cleanup of the Copper Basin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1982, the company acquired land in the Copper Basin in <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a>, formerly the site of the <a href="/wiki/Burra_Burra_Mine_(Tennessee)" title="Burra Burra Mine (Tennessee)">Burra Burra Mine</a>, where copper and sulfur had been mined in the 1800s. In 2016, Occidental agreed to spend $50 million to clean up the Copper Basin and restore the water quality of its creeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Love_Canal">Love Canal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Love Canal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Love_Canal" title="Love Canal">Love Canal</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Abandoned_parking_lot_in_Love_Canal.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3584" height="159" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Abandoned_parking_lot_in_Love_Canal.jpg/220px-Abandoned_parking_lot_in_Love_Canal.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Abandoned_parking_lot_in_Love_Canal.jpg/330px-Abandoned_parking_lot_in_Love_Canal.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Abandoned_parking_lot_in_Love_Canal.jpg/440px-Abandoned_parking_lot_in_Love_Canal.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Abandoned_parking_lot_in_Love_Canal.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An abandoned parking lot near <a href="/wiki/Love_Canal" title="Love Canal">Love Canal</a></div></div></div>
<p>Since the 1920, several companies and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Armed_Forces" title="United States Armed Forces">United States Armed Forces</a> used the <a href="/wiki/Love_Canal" title="Love Canal">Love Canal</a> as a chemical disposal site. In 1942, <a href="/wiki/Hooker_Chemical_Company" title="Hooker Chemical Company">Hooker Chemical Company</a> began disposing chemical waste on the site and, in 1947, it became the sole owner and user of the land. In 1952, the site was filled to capacity and closed off. The company leased the land to the school board in 1953. Later in the 1950s, the local school board requested that the company sell the land, and threatened to use <a href="/wiki/Eminent_domain" title="Eminent domain">eminent domain</a>. The school board intended to build a school on an unused area of the dump.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p><p>A school was built on the site, and later a middle-class residential district was built upon the land adjacent to the site. The construction broke through the 4-foot (1.2 m) clay seal containing the waste. In 1968, Hooker Chemical was purchased by Occidental. In 1978, residents became concerned about unusual health issues in the region, including high rates of cancer and <a href="/wiki/Birth_defect" title="Birth defect">birth defects</a>. This subsequently became a national news story, and in 1980, president <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">Jimmy Carter</a> declared a federal emergency in the area. Residents were eventually relocated, and the company paid $129 million in restitution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-love_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-love-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Oleum_spill">Oleum spill</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Oleum spill">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On Saturday, October 11, 2008, an accidental spill of <a href="/wiki/Oleum" title="Oleum">oleum</a>, a chemical mixture of <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>, occurred at a facility in <a href="/wiki/Petrolia,_Pennsylvania" title="Petrolia, Pennsylvania">Petrolia, Pennsylvania</a> belonging to Indspec, an affiliate of Occidental Chemical Corporation. The accident caused contamination of the ventilation system and a cloud of toxic gas in the sky and forced the evacuation of over 2,000 residents of the area for the day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> The spill was determined to be caused by the use of an auxiliary pump <a href="/wiki/Power_supply" title="Power supply">power supply</a> that lacked safety interlocks to prevent tank overfilling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-love_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-love-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Colombia">Colombia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Colombia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>From 1992 to 2001, the company tried to drill for oil in the territory of the <a href="/wiki/U%27wa_people" title="U'wa people">U'wa people</a>, in northeast <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>. The locals resisted, concerned about <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">environmental degradation</a> and fears that development would bring strangers and be a target for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guerilla_warfare" title="Guerilla warfare">guerilla warfare</a>. There also were tribal beliefs that oil is the "blood of the earth" and should not be removed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> In 2002, after years of <a href="/wiki/Shareholder_resolution" title="Shareholder resolution">shareholder resolutions</a>, legal battles, protests, and a failed test well, the company abandoned the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Repsol" title="Repsol">Repsol</a> took over the project.
</p>
<h4><span id="Ca.C3.B1o_Lim.C3.B3n"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Caño_Limón">Caño Limón</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Caño Limón">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ca%C3%B1o_Lim%C3%B3n_%E2%80%93_Cove%C3%B1as_pipeline" title="Caño Limón – Coveñas pipeline">Caño Limón – Coveñas pipeline</a></div>
<p>On December 13, 1998, 17 civilians, including 7 children, were killed when the <a href="/wiki/Colombian_Air_Force" title="Colombian Air Force">Colombian Air Force</a> (CAF) dropped a cluster bomb in the hamlet of Santo Domingo, Colombia, after <a href="/wiki/AirScan" title="AirScan">AirScan</a>, Occidental's security contractor, misidentified it as a hostile guerrilla target. Groups such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FARC" title="FARC">FARC</a> and the <a href="/wiki/National_Liberation_Army_(Colombia)" title="National Liberation Army (Colombia)">National Liberation Army</a> were active in the area. Three employees of AirScan were flying the Skymaster plane from which they provided the Colombian military with the coordinates to drop the bombs. The operation had been planned by the CAF and AirScan at Occidental's complex in Caño Limón. In April 2003, Luis Alberto Galvis Mujica, a witness and survivor of the accident, sued Occidental.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> The courts ruled that Occidental was not liable for the incident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Maynas_Carijano_v._Occidental_Petroleum">Maynas Carijano v. Occidental Petroleum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Maynas Carijano v. Occidental Petroleum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On May 10, 2007, a group of 25 <a href="/wiki/Achuar" title="Achuar">Achuar</a> <a href="/wiki/Peruvians" title="Peruvians">Peruvians</a>, a group of <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">Indigenous peoples</a>, filed suit against the company, demanding <a href="/wiki/Environmental_remediation" title="Environmental remediation">environmental remediation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reparation_(legal)" title="Reparation (legal)">reparations</a> for <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">environmental degradation</a> allegedly caused by the company between 1971 and 2000, when it drilled in Block 1-AB in Peru. The plaintiffs claimed that the company violated <a href="/wiki/Technical_standard" title="Technical standard">technical standards</a> and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_law" title="Environmental law">environmental law</a> when it dumped a total of 9 billion barrels (1.4<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>9</sup> m<sup>3</sup>) of toxic oil <a href="/wiki/By-product" title="By-product">by-products</a>, such as cadmium, lead, and <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">drainage basins</a> used by the Achuar people to fish, drink, and bathe. This environmental damage was alleged to have caused premature deaths and <a href="/wiki/Birth_defect" title="Birth defect">birth defects</a>. A 2006 study by the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Health_(Peru)" title="Ministry of Health (Peru)">Ministry of Health of Peru</a>, found that all but 2 of the 199 people tested had levels of <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium</a> in their blood above safe levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Achuar were represented by EarthRights International and the law firm Schonbrun DeSimone Seplow Harris &amp; Hoffman LLP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-payout_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-payout-84">[84]</a></sup>
</p><p>On March 3, 2010, EarthRights International argued to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Ninth_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit">United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit</a> that the case should be litigated in Los Angeles, where the company was headquartered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> The court agreed with a trial in the United States, overturning the decision of the lower courts, and, in 2013, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Supreme_Court" title="United States Supreme Court">United States Supreme Court</a> refused to hear the company's appeal. In March 2015, the company made a <a href="/wiki/Settlement_(litigation)" title="Settlement (litigation)">settlement</a> for an undisclosed amount, with the funds to be used for health, education, and nutrition projects in 5 Achuar communities in the <a href="/wiki/Corrientes_River" title="Corrientes River">Corrientes River</a> basin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-payout_84-1"><a href="#cite_note-payout-84">[84]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_record">Political record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Political record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Contributions">Contributions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Contributions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Occidental has disclosed its contributions to <a href="/wiki/Political_action_committee" title="Political action committee">political action committees</a>, lobbyists, and <a href="/wiki/Trade_association" title="Trade association">trade associations</a> on its website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2005, the company was among 53 entities which contributed the maximum of $250,000 to the <a href="/wiki/Second_inauguration_of_George_W._Bush" title="Second inauguration of George W. Bush">Second inauguration of George W. Bush</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup>
</p><p>The company also donated between $10,000 and $25,000 to the <a href="/wiki/Clinton_Foundation" title="Clinton Foundation">Clinton Foundation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gore_family">Gore family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Gore family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Former CEO Armand Hammer was a long time friend of former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Senator" title="U.S. Senator">U.S. Senator</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albert_Gore,_Sr." title="Albert Gore, Sr.">Albert Gore, Sr.</a> and Gore was a member of the board of directors of the company. In 1972, after he lost an election for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a>, Gore became the head of Island Creek Coal Company, an Occidental subsidiary. Much of the company's coal and phosphate production was in <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a>, the state Gore represented in the Senate, and Gore owned shares in the company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> The company liquidated its coal assets in 1993 after Hammer died.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coal_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-coal-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Former Vice President of the United States <a href="/wiki/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">Al Gore</a> was criticized by environmentalists when he inherited shares in the company after the death of his father in 1998; however, the shares were immediately sold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goreties_93-0"><a href="#cite_note-goreties-93">[93]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1998, the U.S. government sold the <a href="/wiki/Elk_Hills_Oil_Field" title="Elk Hills Oil Field">Elk Hills Oil Field</a> to Occidental for $3.65 billion after an auction process that involved selling the field in segments and offering it to multiple bidders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> However, critics cited the Gore family's involvement with the company as evidence of <a href="/wiki/Graft_(politics)" title="Graft (politics)">graft</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goreties_93-1"><a href="#cite_note-goreties-93">[93]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_record">Safety record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Safety record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1999, OxyChem achieved Star Status under OSHA's Voluntary Protection Programs as being among the safest work sites in the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Piper_Alpha">Piper Alpha</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Piper Alpha">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Piper_Alpha" title="Piper Alpha">Piper Alpha</a></div>
<p>On July 6, 1988, the company's <a href="/wiki/Piper_Alpha" title="Piper Alpha">Piper Alpha</a> offshore production platform in the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> was destroyed when an out of service gas <a href="/wiki/Condensate_pump" title="Condensate pump">condensate pump</a> was started with its pressure <a href="/wiki/Safety_valve" title="Safety valve">safety valve</a> removed. The subsequent <a href="/wiki/Gas_leak" title="Gas leak">gas leak</a>, explosion and fire resulted in the deaths of 167 workers in what remains the world's deadliest offshore disaster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-piper_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-piper-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenmail">Greenmail</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Occidental_Petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Greenmail">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1984, billionaire <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Murdock" title="David Murdock">David Murdock</a> owned about 5% of the company and was a member of its board of directors, after the company acquired <a href="/wiki/IBP,_Inc." title="IBP, Inc.">IBP, Inc.</a>, of which Murdock owned 19%. After disagreements between Murdock and then-CEO Armand Hammer, the company paid <a href="/wiki/Greenmail" title="Greenmail">greenmail</a> to buy Murdock's shares at $40.09 each, while the market price was $28.75.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup>
</p>
