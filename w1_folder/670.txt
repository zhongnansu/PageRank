<p><b>Thomas Gold</b> (May 22, 1920 – June 22, 2004)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frs_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-frs-2">[2]</a></sup> was an <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austrian</a>-born <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">astrophysicist</a>, a professor of <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a> at <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a>, a member of the U.S. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Society">Fellow of the Royal Society</a> (London).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frs_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-frs-2">[2]</a></sup>  Gold was one of three young <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">Cambridge</a> scientists who in 1948 proposed the now mostly abandoned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steady_state_theory" title="Steady state theory">'steady state'</a> hypothesis of the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a>. Gold's work crossed academic and scientific boundaries, into <a href="/wiki/Biophysics" title="Biophysics">biophysics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_engineering" title="Aerospace engineering">aerospace engineering</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">geophysics</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Gold&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gold was born on May 22, 1920 in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> to Max Gold, a wealthy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> industrialist (pre-war) who ran one of Austria's largest mining and metal fabrication companies, and German former actress Josefine Martin. Following the economic downfall of the European mining industry in the late 1920s, Max Gold moved his family to Berlin, where he had taken a job as director of a metal trading company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODNB_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ODNB-3">[3]</a></sup> Following the start of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> leader <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a>'s anti-Jewish campaigns  in 1933, Gold and his family left Germany because of his father's heritage. The family travelled through Europe for the next few years. Gold attended <a href="/wiki/Boarding_school" title="Boarding school">boarding school</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Lyceum_Alpinum_Zuoz" title="Lyceum Alpinum Zuoz">Lyceum Alpinum Zuoz</a> in <a href="/wiki/Zuoz" title="Zuoz">Zuoz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, where he quickly proved to be a clever, competitive and physically and mentally aggressive individual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BMNAS3_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-BMNAS3-4">[4]</a></sup> Gold finished his schooling at Zuoz in 1938, and fled with his family to <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Anschluss" title="Anschluss">German invasion of Austria</a> in early 1938. Gold entered <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Cambridge" title="Trinity College, Cambridge">Trinity College, Cambridge</a> in 1939 and began studying mechanical sciences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In May 1940, just as Hitler was commencing his advance in <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> and <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, Gold was sent into <a href="/wiki/Internment" title="Internment">internment</a> as an <a href="/wiki/Enemy_alien" title="Enemy alien">enemy alien</a> by the British government. It was on the first night of internment, at an army barracks in <a href="/wiki/Bury_St_Edmunds" title="Bury St Edmunds">Bury St Edmunds</a>, that he met his future collaborator and close friend, <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Bondi" title="Hermann Bondi">Hermann Bondi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burbidge4_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burbidge4-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Gold spent most of his nearly 15 months of internment in a camp in Canada, after which he returned to England and reentered Cambridge University, where he abandoned his study of mechanical sciences for <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burbidge4_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Burbidge4-6">[6]</a></sup> After graduating with a <a href="/wiki/British_undergraduate_degree_classification" title="British undergraduate degree classification">pass (Ordinary) degree</a> in June 1942, Gold worked briefly as an agricultural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labourer" title="Labourer">labourer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lumberjack" title="Lumberjack">lumberjack</a> in northern England before joining Bondi and <a href="/wiki/Fred_Hoyle" title="Fred Hoyle">Fred Hoyle</a> on naval research into radar ground clutter near <a href="/wiki/Dunsfold" title="Dunsfold">Dunsfold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a>. The three men would spend their off-duty hours in "intense and wide-ranging scientific discussion" on topics such as <a href="/wiki/Cosmology" title="Cosmology">cosmology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">astrophysics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tucker_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tucker-8">[8]</a></sup> Within months, Gold was placed in charge of constructing new radar systems. Gold determined how <a href="/wiki/Landing_craft" title="Landing craft">landing craft</a> could use radar to navigate to the appropriate landing spot on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Normandy_Landings" title="Normandy Landings">D-Day</a> and also discovered that the German navy had fitted <a href="/wiki/Submarine_snorkel" title="Submarine snorkel">snorkels</a> to its <a href="/wiki/U-boat" title="U-boat">U-boats</a>, making them operable underwater while still taking in air from above the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODNB_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ODNB-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Work_at_Cambridge">Work at Cambridge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Gold&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Work at Cambridge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Immediately after the war, Hoyle and Bondi returned to Cambridge, while Gold stayed with naval research until 1947. He then began  working at Cambridge's <a href="/wiki/Cavendish_Laboratory" title="Cavendish Laboratory">Cavendish Laboratory</a> to help construct the world's largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetron" title="Magnetron">magnetron</a>, a device invented by two British scientists in 1940 that generated intense <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">microwaves</a> for radar. Soon after, Gold joined R. J. Pumphrey, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zoologist" title="Zoologist">zoologist</a> at the Cambridge Zoology Laboratory who had served as the deputy head of radar naval research during the war, to study the effect of <a href="/wiki/Resonance" title="Resonance">resonance</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_ear" title="Human ear">human ear</a>. He found that the degree of resonance observed in the <a href="/wiki/Cochlea" title="Cochlea">cochlea</a> was not in accordance with the level of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Damping" title="Damping">damping</a> that would be expected from the <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> of the watery liquid that fills the inner ear. In 1948, Gold hypothesized that the ear operates by "<a href="/wiki/Otoacoustic_emission" title="Otoacoustic emission">regeneration</a>", in that electromechanical action occurs when electrical energy is used to counteract the effects of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Damping" title="Damping">damping</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burbidge5_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burbidge5-9">[9]</a></sup> Although Gold won a prize fellowship from Trinity College for his thesis on the regeneration and obtained a junior lectureship at the Cavendish Laboratory, his theory was widely ignored by ear specialists and physiologists, such as future <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prize</a> winner <a href="/wiki/Georg_von_B%C3%A9k%C3%A9sy" title="Georg von Békésy">Georg von Békésy</a>, who did not believe the cochlea operated under a feedback system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In the 1970s, researchers discovered that Gold's hypothesis had been correct – the ear contained microscopic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hair_cells" title="Hair cells">hair cells</a> that operated on a feedback mechanism to generate resonance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burbidge5_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Burbidge5-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Steady_State_theory">Steady State theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Gold&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Steady State theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steady_State_theory" title="Steady State theory">Steady State theory</a></div>
<p>Gold began discussing problems in physics with Hoyle and Bondi again, centering on the issues over <a href="/wiki/Redshift" title="Redshift">redshift</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hubble%27s_law" title="Hubble's law">Hubble's law</a>. This led the three to all start questioning the <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a> theory originally proposed by <a href="/wiki/Georges_Lema%C3%AEtre" title="Georges Lemaître">Georges Lemaître</a> in 1931 and later advanced by <a href="/wiki/George_Gamow" title="George Gamow">George Gamow</a>, which suggested that the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a> expanded from an extremely dense and hot state and continues to expand today. As recounted in a 1978 interview with physicist and historian <a href="/wiki/Spencer_R._Weart" title="Spencer R. Weart">Spencer R. Weart</a>, Gold believed that there was reason to think that the creation of matter was "done all the time and then none of the problems about fleeting moments arise. It can be just in a steady state with the expansion taking things apart as fast as new matter comes into being and condenses into new galaxies".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burbidge6_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burbidge6-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Two papers were published in 1948 discussing the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steady_State_theory" title="Steady State theory">Steady State theory</a>" as an <a href="/wiki/Non-standard_cosmology" title="Non-standard cosmology">alternative</a> to the Big Bang: one by Gold and Bondi, the other by Hoyle. In their seminal paper, Gold and Bondi asserted that although the universe is expanding, it nevertheless does not change its look over time; it has no beginning and no end.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burbidge6_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Burbidge6-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silk_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Silk-12">[12]</a></sup> They proposed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perfect_cosmological_principle" title="Perfect cosmological principle">perfect cosmological principle</a> as the underpinning of their theory, which held that the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a> is <a href="/wiki/Homogeneity_(physics)" title="Homogeneity (physics)">homogeneous</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isotropy" title="Isotropy">isotropic</a> in space and time. On the large scale, they argued that there "is nothing outstanding about any place in the universe, and that those differences which do exist are only of local significance; that seen on a large scale the universe is homogeneous."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> However, since the universe was not characterized by a lack of evolution, distinguishing features or recognizable direction of time, they postulated that there had to be large-scale motions in the universe. They highlighted two possible types of motion: large-scale expansion and its reverse, large-scale contraction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> They estimated that within the expanding universe, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> atoms were being created out of a <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a> at a rate of one atom per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cubic_meter" title="Cubic meter">cubic meter</a> per 10<sup>9</sup> years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> This creation of matter would keep the density of the universe constant as it expanded. Gold and Bondi also stated that the issues with <a href="/wiki/Age_of_the_universe" title="Age of the universe">time scale</a> that had plagued other cosmological theories – such as the discrepancy between the age of the universe as calculated by Hubble and dating of radioactive decay in terrestrial rocks – were absent for the Steady State theory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>It was not until the 1960s that major problems with the Steady State theory began to emerge, when observations apparently supported the idea that the universe was in fact changing: <a href="/wiki/Quasar" title="Quasar">quasars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radio_galaxy" title="Radio galaxy">radio galaxies</a> were found only at large distances (therefore existing only in the distant past), not in closer galaxies. Whereas the Big Bang theory predicted as much, Steady State predicted that such objects would be found everywhere, including close to our own galaxy, since evolution would be more evenly distributed, not observed only at great distances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silk_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Silk-12">[12]</a></sup> In addition, proponents of the theory predicted that in addition to hydrogen atoms, <a href="/wiki/Antimatter" title="Antimatter">antimatter</a> would also be produced, as with cosmic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma_rays" title="Gamma rays">gamma ray background</a> from the annihilation of <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antiproton" title="Antiproton">antiprotons</a> and <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> emitting gas from the creation of <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silk_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Silk-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>For most cosmologists, the refutation of the steady-state theory came with the discovery of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation" title="Cosmic microwave background radiation">cosmic microwave background radiation</a> in 1965, which was predicted by the Big Bang theory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burbidge7_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burbidge7-17">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Hawking" title="Stephen Hawking">Stephen Hawking</a> said that the fact that microwave radiation had been found, and that it was thought to be left over from the Big Bang, was "the final nail in the coffin of the steady-state theory."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Bondi conceded that the theory had been disproved, but Hoyle and Gold remained unconvinced for a number of years. Gold even supported Hoyle's modified Steady State theory, however, by 1998, he started to express some doubts about the theory, but maintained that despite its faults, the theory helped improve understanding regarding the origin of the universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accidental_panspermia">Accidental panspermia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Gold&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Accidental panspermia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gold suggested a "garbage theory" for the origin of life which was an accidental <a href="/wiki/Panspermia" title="Panspermia">panspermia</a>; the theory says that life on Earth might have spread from a pile of <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">waste</a> products accidentally dumped on Earth long ago by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extraterrestrials" title="Extraterrestrials">extraterrestrials</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Astrophysics_research">Astrophysics research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Gold&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Astrophysics research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arecibo_naic_big.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="477" data-file-width="640" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Arecibo_naic_big.png/220px-Arecibo_naic_big.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Arecibo_naic_big.png/330px-Arecibo_naic_big.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Arecibo_naic_big.png/440px-Arecibo_naic_big.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arecibo_naic_big.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Arecibo_Observatory" title="Arecibo Observatory">Arecibo Observatory</a>, established by <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a> and the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a> during Gold's tenure as director of the Center for Radiophysics and Space Research</div></div></div>
<p>In 1951, at a meeting of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Astronomical_Society" title="Royal Astronomical Society">Royal Astronomical Society</a>, Gold proposed that the source of recent radio signals detected from space was outside the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a> galaxy, much to the derision of radio astronomer <a href="/wiki/Martin_Ryle" title="Martin Ryle">Martin Ryle</a> and several mathematical cosmologists. However, a year later, a distant source was identified and Gold announced at an International
Astronomical Union meeting in Rome that his theory had been proven. Ryle would later take Gold's argument as proof of extragalactic evolution, claiming that it invalidated the Steady State theory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Gold left Cambridge in 1952 to become the chief assistant to <a href="/wiki/Astronomer_Royal" title="Astronomer Royal">Astronomer Royal</a> <a href="/wiki/Harold_Spencer_Jones" title="Harold Spencer Jones">Harold Spencer Jones</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Greenwich_Observatory" title="Royal Greenwich Observatory">Royal Greenwich Observatory</a> in <a href="/wiki/Herstmonceux" title="Herstmonceux">Herstmonceux</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sussex" title="Sussex">Sussex</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. While there, Gold attracted some controversy by suggesting that the interaction between charged particles from the Sun with the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a> in creating magnetic storms in the upper atmosphere was an example of a collisionless <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">shock wave</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilford_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wilford-21">[21]</a></sup> The theory was widely disputed, until American scientists in 1957 discovered that Gold's theory held up to mathematical scrutiny by conducting a simulation using a <a href="/wiki/Shock_tube" title="Shock tube">shock tube</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Gold resigned from the Royal Observatory following Spencer-Jones's retirement and moved to the United States in 1956, where he served as Professor of Astronomy (1957–1958) and Robert Wheeler Wilson Professor of Applied Astronomy (1958–1959) at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burbidge7_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Burbidge7-17">[17]</a></sup> In early 1959, he accepted an appointment at <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a>, which had offered him the opportunity to set up an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interdisciplinary" title="Interdisciplinary">interdisciplinary</a> unit for <a href="/wiki/Radiophysics" title="Radiophysics">radiophysics</a> and space research, and take charge of the Department of Astronomy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bondi_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bondi-22">[22]</a></sup> At the time, there was only one other faculty member in the department. Gold would serve as director of the Center for Radiophysics and Space Research until 1981, establishing Cornell as a leading hub of scientific research. During his tenure, Gold hired famed astronomers <a href="/wiki/Carl_Sagan" title="Carl Sagan">Carl Sagan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frank_Drake" title="Frank Drake">Frank Drake</a>, helped establish the world's largest <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">radio telescope</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Arecibo_Observatory" title="Arecibo Observatory">Arecibo Observatory</a> in Puerto Rico and the Cornell-Sydney University Astronomy Center with <a href="/wiki/Harry_Messel" title="Harry Messel">Harry Messel</a>. In addition, Gold served as Assistant Vice President for Research from 1969–1971 and the John L. Wetherill Professor of Astronomy from 1971 until his retirement in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearce_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pearce-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dermott1674_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dermott1674-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Independent_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chandra-crab.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2400" data-file-width="2400" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Chandra-crab.jpg/220px-Chandra-crab.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Chandra-crab.jpg/330px-Chandra-crab.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Chandra-crab.jpg/440px-Chandra-crab.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chandra-crab.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The discovery of a pulsar with 0.033 second period in the <a href="/wiki/Crab_Nebula" title="Crab Nebula">Crab Nebula</a> led to the acceptance of Gold's theory on pulsars.</div></div></div>
<p>In 1959, Gold expanded on his previous prediction of a collisionless shock wave, arguing that <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">solar flares</a> would eject material into magnetic clouds to produce a shock front that would result in geomagnetic storms. He also coined the term "<a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a>" in his paper "Motions in the Magnetosphere of the Earth" to describe "the region above the ionosphere in which the magnetic field of the Earth has a dominant control over the motions of gas and fast charged particles ... [which was] known to extend out to a distance of the order of 10 Earth radii".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In 1960, Gold collaborated again with Fred Hoyle to show that magnetic energy fueled solar flares and that flares were triggered when opposite magnetic loops interact and release their stored energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1968, a Cambridge <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">radio astronomy</a> <a href="/wiki/Postgraduate_education" title="Postgraduate education">postgraduate student</a> <a href="/wiki/Jocelyn_Bell_Burnell" title="Jocelyn Bell Burnell">Jocelyn Bell Burnell</a> and her doctoral adviser <a href="/wiki/Antony_Hewish" title="Antony Hewish">Antony Hewish</a> discovered a <a href="/wiki/Pulse_(signal_processing)" title="Pulse (signal processing)">pulsing</a> radio source with a <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">period</a> of 1.33 seconds. The source – which was termed "<a href="/wiki/Pulsar" title="Pulsar">pulsar</a>" – emitted beams of <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> at a very short and consistent interval. Gold proposed that these objects were rapidly rotating <a href="/wiki/Neutron_star" title="Neutron star">neutron stars</a>. Gold argued that due to their strong <a href="/wiki/Magnetism" title="Magnetism">magnetic</a> fields and high rotational speed, pulsars would emit radiation similar to a rotating beacon. Gold's conclusion was initially not well received by the scientific community; in fact, he was refused permission to present his theory at the first international conference on pulsars. However, Gold's theory became widely accepted following the discovery of a pulsar in the <a href="/wiki/Crab_Nebula" title="Crab Nebula">Crab Nebula</a> using the Arecibo radio telescope, opening the door for future advancements in <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_physics" title="Solid-state physics">solid-state physics</a> and astronomy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Anthony Tucker of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> remarked that Gold's discovery paved the way for <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Hawking" title="Stephen Hawking">Stephen Hawking</a>'s groundbreaking research into <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">black holes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tucker_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tucker-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_NASA">Relationship with NASA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Gold&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Relationship with NASA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Apollo_11_bootprint.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2363" data-file-width="2349" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Apollo_11_bootprint.jpg/220px-Apollo_11_bootprint.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Apollo_11_bootprint.jpg/330px-Apollo_11_bootprint.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Apollo_11_bootprint.jpg/440px-Apollo_11_bootprint.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Apollo_11_bootprint.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bootprint of Lunar Module Pilot <a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">Buzz Aldrin</a> on the surface of the Moon. Aldrin photographed this bootprint on July 20, 1969, as part of investigations into the soil mechanics of the lunar surface.</div></div></div>
<p>From the 1950s, Gold served as a consultant to <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> and held positions on several national space committees, including the <a href="/wiki/President%27s_Science_Advisory_Committee" title="President's Science Advisory Committee">President's Science Advisory Committee</a>, as the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> tried to develop its space program. At the time, scientists were engaged in a heated debate over the physical properties of the moon's surface. In 1955, he predicted that the Moon was covered by a layer of fine rock powder stemming from "the ceaseless bombardment of its surface by Solar System debris".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bondi_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bondi-22">[22]</a></sup> This led to the dust being jokingly referred to as "Gold dust"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> or "Gold's dust".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashingtonPost_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-WashingtonPost-31">[31]</a></sup> Gold initially suggested that astronauts would sink into the dust, but upon later analysis of impact craters and electrostatic fields, he determined that the astronauts' boots would sink only three centimeters into the Moon's surface. In any case, NASA sent unmanned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surveyor_Program" title="Surveyor Program">Surveyors</a> to analyze the conditions on the surface of the Moon. Gold was ridiculed by fellow scientists, not only for his hypothesis, but for the approach he took in communicating NASA's concerns to the American public; in particular, some experts were infuriated with his usage of the term "moon dust" in reference to lunar <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">regolith</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burbidge8_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burbidge8-32">[32]</a></sup> When the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">Apollo 11</a> crew landed on the Moon in 1969 and brought back the first samples of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_rock" title="Lunar rock">lunar rocks</a>, researchers found that lunar soil was in fact powdery. Gold said the findings were consistent with his hypothesis, noting that "in one area as they walked along, they sank in between five and eight inches". However, Gold received little credit for his correct prediction, and was even criticized for his original prediction of a deep layer of lunar dust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bondi_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bondi-22">[22]</a></sup> Gold had also contributed to the Apollo program by designing the Apollo Lunar Surface Closeup Camera (ALSCC) (a kind of <a href="/wiki/Stereo_camera" title="Stereo camera">stereo camera</a>) used on the Apollo 11, 12, and 14 missions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bondi_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-Bondi-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1970s and 1980s, Gold was a vocal critic of NASA's <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> program, deriding claims that the agency could fly 50 missions a year or that it could have low budget costs. NASA officials warned Gold that if he testified his concerns before Congress, his research proposals would lose their support from NASA. Gold ignored the warning and testified before a Congressional committee headed by Senator <a href="/wiki/Walter_Mondale" title="Walter Mondale">Walter Mondale</a>. In a letter to NASA administrator <a href="/wiki/James_C._Fletcher" title="James C. Fletcher">James C. Fletcher</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Low" title="George Low">George Low</a> wrote that "Gold should realize that being funded by the Government and NASA is a privilege, and that it would make little sense for us to fund him as long as his views are what they are now".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burbidge9_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burbidge9-34">[34]</a></sup> Gold recalled the aftermath of his testimony in a 1983 interview with astronomy historian <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=David_H._DeVorkin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="David H. DeVorkin (page does not exist)">David H. DeVorkin</a>:
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>I had a very hard time with NASA, year after year. I got some more money, but eventually it fizzled out, after three years or so after this event. My applications, which previously each year had always gone through very smoothly, were turned down. I would then have to go to Washington, discuss it with them. and I then would get a certain fraction of it resurrected. For several years running this happened, and then eventually it fizzled permanently, and I've not tried to get any money out of NASA since.<br/>
...
<br/>
I was certainly regarded as persona non grata with NASA after that. I had a very hard time. Shortly after that <a href="/wiki/Noel_Hinners" title="Noel Hinners">Noel Hinners</a> became the Space Science administrator, and he used to joke about it and say, "Oh. Tommy's got to come to his annual pilgrimage to Washington," and regarded it as very funny, but then he'd always give me some money. But always clearly as a persona non grata.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_of_petroleum">Origins of petroleum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Gold&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Origins of petroleum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Abiogenic_petroleum_origin" title="Abiogenic petroleum origin">Abiogenic petroleum origin</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nur04512.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1824" data-file-width="1184" height="339" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Nur04512.jpg/220px-Nur04512.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Nur04512.jpg/330px-Nur04512.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Nur04512.jpg/440px-Nur04512.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nur04512.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Siboglinidae" title="Siboglinidae">Tube worms</a> feeding at base of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_smoker" title="Black smoker">black smoker</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent" title="Hydrothermal vent">hydrothermal vent</a></div></div></div>
<p>Gold first became interested in the origins of <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> in the 1950s, postulating a theory on the <a href="/wiki/Abiogenic_petroleum_origin" title="Abiogenic petroleum origin">abiogenic formation of fossil fuels</a>. Gold engaged in thorough discussion on the matter with Fred Hoyle, who even included a chapter on "Gold's Pore Theory" in his 1955 book <i>Frontiers in Astronomy</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dermott1674_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dermott1674-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In the late 1970s, just as the United States faced another major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1979_energy_crisis" title="1979 energy crisis">energy crisis</a>, Gold resurrected his work on petroleum. In 1977, a research submarine near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galapagos_Islands" title="Galapagos Islands">Galapagos Islands</a> discovered a number of thriving ecosystems down on the ocean floor, living alongside <a href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent" title="Hydrothermal vent">hydrothermal vents</a>. Later expeditions found that these vents were host to a number of organisms, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_tube_worm" title="Giant tube worm">giant tube worms</a> and albino <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a>, that survived off heat-loving <a href="/wiki/Chemosynthesis" title="Chemosynthesis">chemosynthetic microbes</a>. The discovery of life in this adverse environment led Gold to reconsider the established interpretation of <a href="/wiki/Petroleum#Formation" title="Petroleum">biogenic petroleum formation</a>. Gold believed that "biology is just a branch of thermodynamics" and that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_life" title="History of life">history of life</a> is just "a gradual systematic development toward more efficient ways of degrading energy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ringle_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ringle-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>He began his investigation by studying how <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a> facilitated the migration of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> gas from the deep Earth to the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glasby89_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Glasby89-38">[38]</a></sup> He speculated that a large enough earthquake would fracture the ground, thus opening up an "escape route" for gas. Gold believed that this would explain the number of unusual phenomena associated with earthquakes, such as fires, flares, <a href="/wiki/Earthquake_light" title="Earthquake light">Earthquake lights</a> and gas emissions. With his colleague <a href="/wiki/Steven_Soter" title="Steven Soter">Steven Soter</a>, Gold constructed a map of the world depicting major oil-producing regions and areas with historical seismic activity. Several oil-rich regions, such as Alaska, Texas, the Caribbean, Mexico, Venezuela, the Persian Gulf, the Urals, Siberia, and Southeast Asia, were found to be lying on major earthquake belts. Gold and Soter suggested that these belts may explain the upward migration of gases through the ground, and subsequently, the production of oil and gas fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glasby89_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Glasby89-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>Gold theorized that since petroleum and its component hydrocarbons were present across the entire universe, there was no reason to believe "that on Earth they must be biological in origin".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ringle_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ringle-37">[37]</a></sup> Gold proposed that fossil fuels were trapped inside the core of the Earth in randomized molecular form nearly 4.5 billion years ago. Over time, the extreme heat of the core "sweated" the rocks that contained these molecules, pushing them up through the porous layers of the Earth. As they move up toward the surface, the hydrocarbons fueled the development of large microbial colonies, which served as the basis for life on Earth. The migrating fossil fuels collect biological remnants before becoming trapped in deep underground reservoirs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ringle_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ringle-37">[37]</a></sup> Soon after Gold started publishing his theories, researchers discovered a number of ecosystems functioning under "conditions of heat and pressure once thought impossible to sustain life". In addition, Gold discovered that the location of major oil-producing regions in the Middle East and southeast Asia was defined by large scale patterns in surface geology and topography, such as deep fault lines. He also pointed to the abundance of <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> in oil and gas reserves as evidence for "a deep source of the hydrocarbons".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glasby89_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-Glasby89-38">[38]</a></sup> Moreover, a few <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_reserve" title="Oil reserve">oil reserves</a> thought to have been exhausted were suddenly generating vast amounts of crude oil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EugeneIsland_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-EugeneIsland-40">[40]</a></sup>  From this, Gold proposed that the Earth may possess a virtually endless supply – suggesting as much as "at least 500 million years' worth of gas" – of fossil fuels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WorthExploring_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-WorthExploring-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>Gold was accused of plagiarizing the abiogenic theory from <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> geologists who first published it in the 1950s, but the accusations were refuted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MetaResearch_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-MetaResearch-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plagiarism_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Plagiarism-44">[44]</a></sup> After first publishing his views on abiogenic petroleum in 1979, Gold began finding the papers on the subject by Soviet geologists and had them translated.  He was both disappointed (that his ideas were not original) and delighted (because such independent formulation of these ideas added weight to the hypothesis). He always credited the Soviet work once he knew about it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frs_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-frs-2">[2]</a></sup> His 1987 book <i>Power from the Earth</i> devoted five pages to describing important Russian contributions to the field, including those by Mendeleev, Sokoloff, Vernadsky, Kudryavtsev, Beskrovny, Porfir'ev, Kravtsov, Kropotkin, Valyaev, Voronoy, and Chekaliuk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MetaResearch_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-MetaResearch-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Drilling_in_Siljan">Drilling in Siljan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Gold&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Drilling in Siljan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Siljan_WorldWind.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="640" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Siljan_WorldWind.jpg/220px-Siljan_WorldWind.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Siljan_WorldWind.jpg/330px-Siljan_WorldWind.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Siljan_WorldWind.jpg/440px-Siljan_WorldWind.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Siljan_WorldWind.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Siljan" title="Lake Siljan">Lake Siljan</a> is a large lake created from an eroded impact crater, the <a href="/wiki/Siljan_Ring" title="Siljan Ring">Siljan Ring</a>, that was formed by a <a href="/wiki/Impact_event" title="Impact event">meteorite impact</a> about 370 million years ago. It was at this lake that Gold proposed as the most likely place to test the hypothesis on the origin of petroleum because it was one of the few places in the world where the granite basement was cracked sufficiently to allow oil to seep up from the mantle.</div></div></div>
<p>Gold began testing his theory in 1986 when, with the backing of a group of investors, <a href="/wiki/Vattenfall" title="Vattenfall">Vattenfall</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_Research_Institute" title="Gas Research Institute">Gas Research Institute</a>, he commenced efforts to drill a deep <a href="/wiki/Borehole" title="Borehole">borehole</a> – named Gravberg-1 – into the earth near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Siljan" title="Lake Siljan">Lake Siljan</a> in search of abiogenic gas in the <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a>. The region was the site of a large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteor_crater" title="Meteor crater">meteor crater</a>, which would have "opened channels deep enough for the methane to migrate upward" and formed deposits in <a href="/wiki/Caprock#Petroleum" title="Caprock">caprock</a> just a few miles beneath the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> He estimated that the <a href="/wiki/Fracture_(geology)" title="Fracture (geology)">fractures</a> near Lake Siljan reached down nearly 40 <a href="/wiki/Kilometre" title="Kilometre">kilometres</a> (25 <a href="/wiki/Mile" title="Mile">mi</a>) into the earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shiry_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shiry-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1987, approximately 900 barrels (140 m<sup>3</sup>) of <a href="/wiki/Drilling_fluid" title="Drilling fluid">drilling lubricant</a> disappeared nearly 20,000 <a href="/wiki/Foot_(unit)" title="Foot (unit)">feet</a> (6,100 <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">m</a>) into the ground, leading Gold to believe that the lubricant had fallen into a methane reservoir.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Soon after, the team brought up nearly 100 liters of black oily sludge to the surface. Gold claimed that the sludge contained both oil and remnants of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaebacteria" title="Archaebacteria">archaebacteria</a>. He argued that "it suggests there is an enormous sphere of life, of biology, at deeper levels in the ground than we have had any knowledge of previously" and that this evidence would "destroy the orthodox argument that since oil contains biological molecules, oil reserves must have derived from biological material". The announcement of Gold's findings was met with mixed reactions, ranging from "furious incredulity" to "deep skepticism".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Geochemist Geoffrey P. Glasby speculated that the sludge could have been formed from the <a href="/wiki/Fischer%E2%80%93Tropsch_process" title="Fischer–Tropsch process">Fischer–Tropsch process</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyzed</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemical reaction</a> in which <a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">synthesis gas</a>, a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, is converted into liquid <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glasby90_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Glasby90-49">[49]</a></sup> Critics also dismissed Gold's archaebacteria finding, stating that "since micro-organisms cannot survive at such depth, the bacteria prove that the well has been contaminated from the surface".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cole748_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cole748-50">[50]</a></sup> Geochemist Paul Philp analyzed the sludge and concluded that he could not differentiate between the samples of sludge and <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_seep" title="Petroleum seep">oil seep</a> found in sedimentary <a href="/wiki/Shale" title="Shale">shale</a> rocks near the surface. He reasoned that oil had migrated from the shale down to the granite deep in the ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Gold disputed Philp's finding, believing that the oil and gas could have just as easily migrated up to the surface: "They would have it that the oil and gas we found down there was from the five feet of sediments on the top – had seeped all the way down six kilometres down into the granite. I mean, such complete absurdity: you can imagine sitting there with five feet of soil and six kilometres underneath of dense granitic rock, and that methane produced up there has crawled all the way down in preference to water. Absolute nonsense."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cole748_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cole748-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>In light of the controversy surrounding the sludge and possible drill contamination, Gold abandoned the project at Gravberg-1, calling it a "complete fiasco", and redesigned the experiment by replacing his oil-based drilling lubricant with a water-based one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>The drill hit oil in the spring of 1989, but only collected about 80 barrels (13 m<sup>3</sup>). Gold stated, "It was not coming up at a rate at which you could sell it, but it showed there was oil down there." The drill then ran into technical problems and was stopped at a depth of 6.8 <a href="/wiki/Kilometre" title="Kilometre">kilometres</a> (4.2 <a href="/wiki/Mile" title="Mile">mi</a>). The hole was closed, but a second hole was opened for drilling closer to the "center of the impact ring where there was even less sedimentary rock". By October 1991, the drill hit oil 3.8 <a href="/wiki/Kilometre" title="Kilometre">kilometres</a> (2.4 <a href="/wiki/Mile" title="Mile">mi</a>) into the ground, but many skeptics remained unconvinced of the site's prospects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shiry_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shiry-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> One skeptic, Christer Akerman, the chief geologist of the Geological Survey of Sweden, remarked, "[t]here is every reason to stay calm and await the analysis of what they have found. The point is also that they will have to find commercially viable amounts, and it may be a long time before we know if they do."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Geologist John R. Castaño concluded that there was insufficient evidence of the mantle as the hydrocarbon source and that it was unlikely that the Siljan site could be used as a commercial gas field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Some skeptics countered Gold's claims by suggesting that the oil found was actually contamination from the drilling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashingtonPost_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-WashingtonPost-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Deep_Hot_Biosphere">The Deep Hot Biosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Gold&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The Deep Hot Biosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a 1992 paper "The Deep Hot Biosphere" in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proceedings_of_the_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences">Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Gold first suggested that microbial life is widespread in the porosity of the crust of the Earth, down to depths of several kilometers, where rising temperatures finally set a limit. The subsurface life obtains its energy not from photosynthesis but from chemical sources in fluids migrating upwards through the crust. The mass of the deep biosphere may be comparable to that of the surface biosphere. Subsurface life may be widespread on other bodies in the solar system and throughout the universe, even on worlds unaccompanied by other stars.
</p><p>Gold also published a book of the same title in 1999, which expanded on the arguments in his 1992 paper and included speculations on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Origin_of_life" title="Origin of life">origin of life</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to Gold, bacteria feeding on the oil accounts for the presence of biological debris in <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">hydrocarbon fuels</a>, obviating the need to resort to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biogenic" title="Biogenic">biogenic</a> theory for the origin of the latter.  The flows of underground <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a> may also explain oddities in the concentration of other mineral deposits.
</p><p>In short, Gold said about the origin of natural hydrocarbons (petroleum and natural gas): <i>Hydrocarbons are not biology reworked by geology (as the traditional view would hold), but rather geology reworked by biology</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MetaResearch_43-2"><a href="#cite_note-MetaResearch-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_legacy">Academic legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Gold&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Academic legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carl_Sagan_Planetary_Society.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="448" data-file-width="328" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Carl_Sagan_Planetary_Society.JPG/220px-Carl_Sagan_Planetary_Society.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/be/Carl_Sagan_Planetary_Society.JPG 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Carl_Sagan_Planetary_Society.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Carl_Sagan" title="Carl Sagan">Carl Sagan</a>, hired by Gold after Sagan was denied tenure at Harvard University in 1968</div></div></div>
<p>Throughout his academic career, Gold received a number of honors and distinctions. He was a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Astronomical_Society" title="Royal Astronomical Society">Royal Astronomical Society</a> (1948), the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> (1964),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frs_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-frs-2">[2]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/American_Geophysical_Union" title="American Geophysical Union">American Geophysical Union</a> (1962), the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="American Academy of Arts and Sciences">American Academy of Arts and Sciences</a> (1974), and the <a href="/wiki/American_Astronautical_Society" title="American Astronautical Society">American Astronautical Society</a>, a member of the <a href="/wiki/American_Philosophical_Society" title="American Philosophical Society">American Philosophical Society</a> (1972), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">United States National Academy of Sciences</a> (1974) and the <a href="/wiki/International_Academy_of_Astronautics" title="International Academy of Astronautics">International Academy of Astronautics</a>, and an Honorary Fellow of Trinity College, Cambridge (1986).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AIM25_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-AIM25-58">[58]</a></sup> In addition, he served as President of the New York Astronomical Society from 1981 to 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearce_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pearce-23">[23]</a></sup> Gold won the John Frederick Lewis Prize from the American Philosophical Society in 1972 for his paper "The Nature of the Lunar Surface: Recent Evidence"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Humboldt_Prize" title="Humboldt Prize">Humboldt Prize</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Alexander_von_Humboldt_Foundation" title="Alexander von Humboldt Foundation">Alexander von Humboldt Foundation</a> in 1979.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AIM25_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-AIM25-58">[58]</a></sup> In 1985, Gold won the prestigious <a href="/wiki/Gold_Medal_of_the_Royal_Astronomical_Society" title="Gold Medal of the Royal Astronomical Society">Gold Medal of the Royal Astronomical Society</a>, an award whose recipients include Fred Hoyle, Hermann Bondi, Martin Ryle, Edwin Hubble, <a href="/wiki/James_Van_Allen" title="James Van Allen">James Van Allen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Zwicky" title="Fritz Zwicky">Fritz Zwicky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hannes_Alfv%C3%A9n" title="Hannes Alfvén">Hannes Alfvén</a> and <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Gold did not earn a <a href="/wiki/Doctorate" title="Doctorate">doctorate</a>, but received an honorary <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Science" title="Doctor of Science">Doctor of Science</a> degree from Cambridge University in 1969.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p><p>Astrophysicists <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Burbidge" title="Geoffrey Burbidge">Geoffrey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Burbidge" title="Margaret Burbidge">Margaret Burbidge</a> remarked that Gold "was one of the outstanding physicists of his time" and that his "versatility was unmatched".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burbidge11_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burbidge11-62">[62]</a></sup> In his <a href="/wiki/Foreword" title="Foreword">foreword</a> to Gold's book <i>The Deep Hot Biosphere</i>, theoretical physicist <a href="/wiki/Freeman_Dyson" title="Freeman Dyson">Freeman Dyson</a> stated, "Gold's theories are always original, always important, usually controversial – and usually right."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the journal <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Bondi" title="Hermann Bondi">Hermann Bondi</a> wrote "Tommy Gold will long be remembered as a singular scientist who stepped into any field where he thought an option was being overlooked. He was also unusual in working mainly theoretically, but using little mathematics, relying instead on his profound intuitive understanding of physics."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bondi_22-4"><a href="#cite_note-Bondi-22">[22]</a></sup> Stanley F. Dermott wrote "Tommy was a handsome, charming and generous man and a loyal colleague who formed many long-lasting friendships. A witty and articulate speaker, he was regarded by some as a scientific maverick who delighted in controversy. In reality, he was an iconoclast whose strength was in penetrating analysis of the assumptions on which some of our most important theories are based."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
Anthony Tucker of <i>The Guardian</i> said, "Throughout his life he would dive into new territory to open up problems unseen by others – in biophysics, astrophysics, space engineering, or geophysics. Controversy followed him everywhere. Possessing profound scientific intuition and open-minded rigour, he usually ended up challenging the cherished assumptions of others and, to the discomfiture of the scientific establishment, often found them wanting. His stature and influence were international."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tucker_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tucker-8">[8]</a></sup>
Harvard biologist <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Jay_Gould" title="Stephen Jay Gould">Stephen Jay Gould</a> labeled Gold as "one of America's most iconoclastic scientists".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ringle_37-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ringle-37">[37]</a></sup> Gold has been derided by geologists, such as <a href="/wiki/Harmon_Craig" title="Harmon Craig">Harmon Craig</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Hunt_(oceanographer)" title="John Hunt (oceanographer)">John Hunt</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ringle_37-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ringle-37">[37]</a></sup> who are strongly opposed to Gold's abiogenic petroleum theory. Others had even started campaigns to prevent Gold from publishing his findings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ringle_37-5"><a href="#cite_note-Ringle-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Gold&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gold married his first wife, Merle Eleanor Tuberg, an American astrophysicist who had worked with <a href="/wiki/Subrahmanyan_Chandrasekhar" title="Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar">Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar</a>, in 1947. He had three daughters with her – Linda, Lucy, and Tanya. After divorcing her, Gold married Carvel Lee Beyer in 1972.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODNB_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-ODNB-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tucker_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Tucker-8">[8]</a></sup> With her, he had a daughter Lauren.
</p><p>Thomas Gold died at the age of 84 from complications due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_disease" title="Heart disease">heart disease</a>, at Cayuga Medical Center in <a href="/wiki/Ithaca,_New_York" title="Ithaca, New York">Ithaca, New York</a>. He was buried in the Pleasant Grove Cemetery in Ithaca.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearce_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pearce-23">[23]</a></sup> He was survived by his wife, four daughters, and six grandchildren.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashingtonPost_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-WashingtonPost-31">[31]</a></sup>
