<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Carbon dioxide sensor</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>carbon dioxide sensor</b> or <b>CO<sub>2</sub> sensor</b> is an instrument for the measurement of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> gas. The most common principles for CO<sub>2</sub> sensors are infrared gas sensors (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NDIR" title="NDIR">NDIR</a>) and chemical gas sensors. Measuring carbon dioxide is important in monitoring <a href="/wiki/Indoor_air_quality" title="Indoor air quality">indoor air quality</a>, the function of the lungs in the form of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capnograph" title="Capnograph">capnograph</a> device, and many industrial processes.
</p>

<h2><span id="Nondispersive_Infrared_.28NDIR.29_CO2_Sensors"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Nondispersive_Infrared_(NDIR)_CO2_Sensors">Nondispersive Infrared (NDIR) CO<sub>2</sub> Sensors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_sensor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nondispersive Infrared (NDIR) CO2 Sensors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nondispersive_infrared_sensor" title="Nondispersive infrared sensor">Nondispersive infrared sensor</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Capnography" title="Capnography">Capnography</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NDIR" title="NDIR">NDIR</a> sensors are <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopic</a> sensors to detect CO<sub>2</sub> in a gaseous environment by its characteristic absorption. The key components are an <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> source, a <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> tube, an interference (wavelength) filter, and an infrared detector. The gas is pumped or diffuses into the light tube, and the electronics measure the absorption of the characteristic <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> of light. NDIR sensors are most often used for measuring carbon dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lang_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lang-1">[1]</a></sup> The best of these have sensitivities of 20–50 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">PPM</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lang_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lang-1">[1]</a></sup> Typical NDIR sensors cost in the (US) $100 to $1000 range.
</p><p>New developments include using <a href="/wiki/Microelectromechanical_systems" title="Microelectromechanical systems">microelectromechanical systems</a> to bring down the costs of this sensor and to create smaller devices (for example for use in <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">air conditioning</a> applications). NDIR CO<sub>2</sub> sensors are also used for dissolved CO<sub>2</sub> for applications such as beverage carbonation, pharmaceutical fermentation and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon dioxide capture and storage">CO<sub>2</sub> sequestration</a> applications. In this case they are mated to an ATR (attenuated total reflection) optic and measure the gas in situ.
</p><p>Another method (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry%27s_Law" title="Henry's Law">Henry's Law</a>) can be also be used to measure the amount of dissolved CO<sub>2</sub> in a liquid, if the amount of foreign gases is insignificant.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag needs further explanation. (August 2014)">further explanation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_CO2_sensors">Chemical CO<sub>2</sub> sensors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_sensor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Chemical CO2 sensors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chemical CO<sub>2</sub> gas sensors with sensitive layers based on polymer- or heteropolysiloxane have the principal advantage of a very low energy consumption, and can be reduced in size to fit into microelectronic-based systems. On the downside, short- and long term drift effects as well as a rather low overall lifetime are major obstacles when compared with the NDIR measurement principle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zhou_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zhou-2">[2]</a></sup> Most CO₂ sensors are fully calibrated prior to shipping from the factory. Over time, the zero point of the sensor needs to be calibrated to maintain the long term stability of the sensor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_sensor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<li>Cellar and gas stores</li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Ventilation_(architecture)" title="Ventilation (architecture)">Ventilation</a> management</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">Mining</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rebreather" title="Rebreather">Rebreathers</a> (SCUBA)</li></ul></li>
<li>For indoor human <a href="/wiki/Occupancy_sensor" title="Occupancy sensor">occupancy counting</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>For <a href="/wiki/HVAC" title="HVAC">HVAC</a> applications, CO<sub>2</sub> sensors can be used to monitor the quality of air and the tailored need for fresh air, respectively. Measuring CO<sub>2</sub> levels indirectly determines how many people are in a room, and ventilation can be adjusted accordingly. See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demand-controlled_ventilation" title="Demand-controlled ventilation">demand controlled ventilation (DCV)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>In applications where direct <a href="/wiki/Temperature_measurement" title="Temperature measurement">temperature measurement</a> is not applicable, <a href="#Nondispersive_Infrared_.28NDIR.29_CO2_Sensors">NDIR sensors</a> can be used. The sensors absorb ambient <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared radiation (IR)</a> given off by a heated surface.</li></ul>
