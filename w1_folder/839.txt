<p><b>Slow Food</b> is an organization that promotes local food and traditional cooking. It was founded by <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Petrini" title="Carlo Petrini">Carlo Petrini</a> in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> in 1986 and has since spread worldwide. Promoted as an alternative to <a href="/wiki/Fast_food" title="Fast food">fast food</a>, it strives to preserve traditional and regional <a href="/wiki/Cuisine" title="Cuisine">cuisine</a> and encourages farming of <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seed" title="Seed">seeds</a>, and livestock characteristic of the local <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a>. It was the first established part of the broader <a href="/wiki/Slow_movement_(culture)" title="Slow movement (culture)">slow movement</a>. Its goals of sustainable foods and promotion of local small businesses are paralleled by a political agenda directed against globalization of agricultural products.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization">Organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slow_Food&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SlowFoodThera06676.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/SlowFoodThera06676.JPG/220px-SlowFoodThera06676.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/SlowFoodThera06676.JPG/330px-SlowFoodThera06676.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/SlowFoodThera06676.JPG/440px-SlowFoodThera06676.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SlowFoodThera06676.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A restaurant placard, <a href="/wiki/Santorini" title="Santorini">Santorini</a>, Greece</div></div></div>
<p>Slow Food began in Italy with the founding of its forerunner organisation, Agricola, in 1986<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> to resist the opening of a <a href="/wiki/McDonald%27s" title="McDonald's">McDonald's</a> near the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Steps" title="Spanish Steps">Spanish Steps</a> in Rome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-petrini_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-petrini-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1989, the founding manifesto of the international Slow Food movement was signed in Paris, France by delegates from 15 countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>At its heart is the aim to promote local foods and traditional gastronomy and food production. Conversely, this means an opposition to <a href="/wiki/Fast_food" title="Fast food">fast food</a>, industrial food production, and globalization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anthro_2004_p170_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anthro_2004_p170-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Slow Food organisation has expanded to include over 100,000 members with branches in over 150 countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slowfood.com_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-slowfood.com-6">[6]</a></sup> Over 1,300 local <i>convivia</i> chapters exist. About 360 convivia in Italy—to which the name <i>condotta</i> (singular) / <i>condotte</i> (plural) applies—are composed of 35,000 members, along with 450 other regional chapters around the world. The organisational structure is decentralised: each convivium has a leader who is responsible for promoting local artisans, local farmers, and local flavors through regional events such as <i>Taste Workshops</i>, wine tastings, and farmers' markets.
</p><p>Offices have been opened in <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> (1995), <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> (1998), <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> (2000), <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> (2003), <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> (2005), the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>. Global headquarters are located in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bra_(CN)" title="Bra (CN)">Bra</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Turin" title="Turin">Turin</a>, Italy. Numerous publications are put out by the organisation, in several languages around the world. Recent efforts at publicity include the world's largest food and wine fair, the <a href="/wiki/Terra_Madre_Salone_del_Gusto" title="Terra Madre Salone del Gusto">Salone del Gusto</a> in Turin, a biennial cheese fair in Bra called Cheese, the <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoan</a> fish festival called SlowFish, and Turin's <a href="/wiki/Terra_Madre" title="Terra Madre">Terra Madre</a> ("Mother Earth") world meeting of food communities.
</p><p>In 2004, Slow Food opened a <a href="/wiki/University_of_Gastronomic_Sciences" title="University of Gastronomic Sciences">University of Gastronomic Sciences</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pollenzo" title="Pollenzo">Pollenzo</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Piedmont" title="Piedmont">Piedmont</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Colorno" title="Colorno">Colorno</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Emilia-Romagna" title="Emilia-Romagna">Emilia-Romagna</a>, Italy. The Colorno branch has since been closed and transferred to Pollenzo. Carlo Petrini and <a href="/wiki/Massimo_Montanari" title="Massimo Montanari">Massimo Montanari</a> are the leading figures in the creation of the university, whose goal is to promote awareness of good food and <a href="/wiki/Nutrition" title="Nutrition">nutrition</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Objectives">Objectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slow_Food&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Objectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wir_haben_es_satt_2013_demo_19.01.2013_14-40-39.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2120" data-file-width="3191" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Wir_haben_es_satt_2013_demo_19.01.2013_14-40-39.jpg/220px-Wir_haben_es_satt_2013_demo_19.01.2013_14-40-39.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Wir_haben_es_satt_2013_demo_19.01.2013_14-40-39.jpg/330px-Wir_haben_es_satt_2013_demo_19.01.2013_14-40-39.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Wir_haben_es_satt_2013_demo_19.01.2013_14-40-39.jpg/440px-Wir_haben_es_satt_2013_demo_19.01.2013_14-40-39.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wir_haben_es_satt_2013_demo_19.01.2013_14-40-39.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Slow Food Germany is among the organisers of the yearly demonstrations under the banner <i><a href="/wiki/We_are_fed_up" title="We are fed up">We are fed up!</a></i> in Berlin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Slow Food incorporates a series of objectives within its mission, including:
</p>
<ul><li>developing an "<a href="/wiki/Ark_of_Taste" title="Ark of Taste">Ark of Taste</a>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anthro_2004_p170_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Anthro_2004_p170-5">[5]</a></sup> for each <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion" title="Ecoregion">ecoregion</a>, where local culinary traditions and foods are celebrated</li>
<li>creating "Praesidia" grassroots organizations to promote slow foods to the public<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anthro_2004_p170_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Anthro_2004_p170-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>forming and sustaining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seedbank" title="Seedbank">seed banks</a> to preserve <a href="/wiki/Heirloom_plant" title="Heirloom plant">heirloom varieties</a> in cooperation with local food systems</li>
<li>preserving and promoting local and traditional food products, along with their lore and preparation</li>
<li>organizing small-scale processing (including facilities for slaughtering and short run products)</li>
<li>organizing celebrations of local cuisine within regions (for example, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Feast_of_Fields&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Feast of Fields (page does not exist)">Feast of Fields</a> held in some cities in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>)</li>
<li>promoting "taste education"</li>
<li>educating consumers about the risks of <a href="/wiki/Fast_food" title="Fast food">fast food</a></li>
<li>educating citizens about the drawbacks of commercial <a href="/wiki/Agribusiness" title="Agribusiness">agribusiness</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Factory_farm" title="Factory farm">factory farms</a></li>
<li>educating citizens about the risks of <a href="/wiki/Monoculture" title="Monoculture">monoculture</a> and reliance on too few <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genomes</a> or varieties</li>
<li>developing various political programmes to preserve <a href="/wiki/Family_farm" title="Family farm">family farms</a></li>
<li>lobbying for the inclusion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_farm" title="Organic farm">organic farming</a> concerns within <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_policy" title="Agricultural policy">agricultural policy</a></li>
<li>lobbying against government funding of <a href="/wiki/Genetic_engineering" title="Genetic engineering">genetic engineering</a></li>
<li>lobbying against the use of <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a></li>
<li>teaching <a href="/wiki/Gardening" title="Gardening">gardening</a> skills to <a href="/wiki/Student" title="Student">students</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prison" title="Prison">prisoners</a></li>
<li>encouraging <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moral_purchasing" title="Moral purchasing">ethical buying</a> in local marketplaces</li></ul>
<p>Founder and President Carlo Petrini, believes "everyone has the right to good, clean, and fair food." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Good, meaning a high quality product with a flavorful taste, clean meaning the naturalness in the way the product was produced and transported and fair, meaning adequate pricing and treatment for both the consumers and producers.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slow_Food&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SlowFoodVictoryGarden.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1224" data-file-width="1632" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b5/SlowFoodVictoryGarden.jpg/220px-SlowFoodVictoryGarden.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b5/SlowFoodVictoryGarden.jpg/330px-SlowFoodVictoryGarden.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b5/SlowFoodVictoryGarden.jpg/440px-SlowFoodVictoryGarden.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SlowFoodVictoryGarden.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Victory Garden at <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> <a href="/wiki/Civic_Center" title="Civic Center">Civic Center</a> Plaza</div></div></div>
<p>In 2008, Slow Food USA hosted its largest gathering to date dubbed <a href="/wiki/Slow_Food_Nation" title="Slow Food Nation">Slow Food Nation</a> in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt1_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt1-10">[10]</a></sup> The event reconvened in 2017 as <a class="external text" href="https://slowfoodnations.org/" rel="nofollow">Slow Food Nations</a>, the stateside equivalent to Terra Madre Salone del Gusto, and was held in Denver, Colorado. The event is scheduled to continue in 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of  2013<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Slow_Food&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, Slow Food USA has a membership of roughly 12,000, down from over 30,000 in 2008.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 2011, the organization was forced to make a series of staff layoffs and reductions and had faced a significant reduction in their income from wealthy supporters. This was partly attributed to the economic recession, but also to disagreements within the movement and a loss of several key personalities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Slow Food USA currently has 200 chapters,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> down from 225 chapters in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfusa_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-sfusa-14">[14]</a></sup>  These are locally based and governed 501(c)3 non-profit organizations that hold events and education outreach programs that benefit their communities while carrying out the message of the slow food movement and advancing the local environmental movement. The movement also encourages the creation of urban gardens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Beyond the chapters established within the cities in the United States, a number of universities are becoming recognised by Slow Food USA, including the University of Wisconsin at Madison. Slow Food-University of Wisconsin has five projects that are dedicated to the movement's efforts, including a Family Dinner Night, weekly café, and a Farm to University scheme. From then, 46 Slow Food chapters have been established on campuses of higher education.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Notable members include <a href="/wiki/Alice_Waters" title="Alice Waters">Alice Waters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eric_Schlosser" title="Eric Schlosser">Eric Schlosser</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Pollan" title="Michael Pollan">Michael Pollan</a>. The executive director is <a href="/wiki/Richard_McCarthy_(activist)" title="Richard McCarthy (activist)">Richard McCarthy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2014, the organization formed an initial 15-month partnership with fast-food chain <a href="/wiki/Chipotle_Mexican_Grill" title="Chipotle Mexican Grill">Chipotle Mexican Grill</a>, which had the company funding $500,000 toward Slow Food USA's National School Garden Program; 100 school gardens in different cities across the US would be funded in an effort to teach children where food comes from and how food is grown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slow_Food&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Slow Food UK works to raise strategic awareness about sustainability and social justice issues surrounding food and farming in Britain. In 2014, Slow Food UK devolved into Slow Food England, Slow Food Scotland, Slow Food Cymru, and Slow Food Northern Ireland. Slow Food UK as an entity provides administrative support to those nations and local groups, and the Slow Food UK Board is now made up of directors from the nations (Shane Holland, Chair &amp; Director for England; John Cooke, Director for Scotland; Illtud Dunsford, Director for Wales; and Paula McIntyre, Director for Slow Food Northern Ireland.  The numerous local groups are led by Slow Food members, who take significant grassroots action in their local communities. The many notable Slow Food UK members and supporters include <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Blanc" title="Raymond Blanc">Raymond Blanc</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Oliver" title="Jamie Oliver">Jamie Oliver</a>.
</p><p>Some of the local groups are very large, such as Slow Food London, and run programmes such as the Slow Food Global Schools Twinning Programme, which are more akin to the work of a national office. Slow Food London is also the major campaigning Slow Food body within the UK, responding to every local, national, and European consultation on food, fisheries, and agriculture, and has even been a co-signatory in judicial review against the UK government in regards to food and farming, retaining a leading firm of solicitors <i>pro bono</i> on an ongoing basis.
</p><p>Besides running national education programmes, such as Slow Food Kids, and Slow Food on Campus, Slow Food UK National Office co-ordinates fights to preserve British culinary heritage through the Chef Alliance and Forgotten Foods programmes (UK Ark of Taste).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The Chef Alliance is a network of chefs committed to protecting Britain's edible biodiversity by cooking with Forgotten Foods, or foods that are produced on a very small scale and are being lost due to commercial varieties overtaking the market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The Forgotten Foods programme is part of the Slow Food International <a href="/wiki/Ark_of_Taste" title="Ark of Taste">Ark of Taste</a>. In 2014, the Chef Alliance had over 100 members, and now over 150 Forgotten Foods are recognized.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slow_Food&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Australian slow food movement aims to increase community awareness of the value from farm to market of good, clean, local food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> A campaign is being mounted to have included in Slow Food International's <a href="/wiki/Ark_of_Taste" title="Ark of Taste">Ark of Taste</a> (nationally nominated threatened produce and food products) the following Australian foods: <a href="/wiki/Kangaroo_Island" title="Kangaroo Island">Kangaroo Island</a>'s Ligurian bee honey, the Queensland-native <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bunya_nut" title="Bunya nut">bunya nut</a>, bull-boar sausage from Victoria, and Tasmanian leatherwood honey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wine">Wine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slow_Food&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Wine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2010, Slow Food International began its independent Slow Wine project with the release of a wine guide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Prior to 2010, Slow Food worked with publisher <a href="/wiki/Gambero_Rosso" title="Gambero Rosso">Gambero Rosso</a> to release a guide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The first edition of Slow Food's first solo effort was released in 1993, with the title "Slow Food guide to the wines of the world", translated into five languages and sold in more than 50,000 copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The guide was an attempt to review not only the wines, but also the wineries and the people behind the bottle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Two editions of the guide have been published, also available in English.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slow_Food&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Slow Food's aims have been compared to the <a href="/wiki/Arts_and_Crafts_movement" title="Arts and Crafts movement">Arts and Crafts movement</a>'s response to 19th-century industrialisation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anthro_2004_p170_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Anthro_2004_p170-5">[5]</a></sup> Some of the criticisms aimed at the movement are socioeconomic. For example, without significantly altering the working day of the masses, slow food preparation can be an additional burden to whoever prepares food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anthro_2004_p170_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Anthro_2004_p170-5">[5]</a></sup> In contrast, the more affluent society can afford the time and expense of developing "taste", "knowledge", and "discernment". Slow Food's stated aim of preserving itself from the "contagion of the multitude" can be seen as elitist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anthro_2004_p170_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-Anthro_2004_p170-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1989, Petrini visited Venezuela and began to recognize the socioeconomic barriers that many faced with regard to the slow food movement. To address this, he adjusted the slow food agenda to include an alternative food approach that favored healthy, local, community-based food consumption and production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> While this made the slow food movement more accessible for many, it did not eliminate all of the socioeconomic barriers faced within the movement.
</p>
