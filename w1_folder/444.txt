<p>The <b>domestic canary</b>, often simply known as the <b>canary</b> (<i>Serinus canaria</i> forma <i>domestica</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>), is a <a href="/wiki/Domestication" title="Domestication">domesticated</a> form of the wild <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_canary" title="Atlantic canary">canary</a>, a small <a href="/wiki/Songbird" title="Songbird">songbird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Finch" title="Finch">finch</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> originating from the <a href="/wiki/Macaronesia" title="Macaronesia">Macaronesian Islands</a> (The <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>).
</p><p>Canaries were first bred in <a href="/wiki/Captivity_(animal)" title="Captivity (animal)">captivity</a> in the 17th century. They were brought over by Spanish <a href="/wiki/Sailor" title="Sailor">sailors</a> to Europe. This bird became expensive and fashionable to breed in courts of Spanish and English kings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Monks started breeding them and only sold the males (which sing).  This kept the birds in short supply and drove the price up.  Eventually, Italians obtained hens and were able to breed the birds.  This made them very popular, resulted in many breeds arising, and the birds being bred all over Europe.
</p><p>The same occurred in England.  First the birds were only owned by the rich, but eventually the local citizens started to breed them and, again, they became very popular.  Many breeds arose through selective breeding, and they are still very popular today for their voices.
</p><p>From the eighteenth up to twentieth centuries, canaries and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finches" title="Finches">finches</a> were used in the UK, Canada and USA in the <a href="/wiki/Coal_mining" title="Coal mining">coal mining</a> industry, to detect <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>. In the UK, this practice ceased in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Typically, the domestic canary is kept as a popular <a href="/wiki/Birdcage" title="Birdcage">cage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aviary" title="Aviary">aviary</a> bird. Given proper housing and care, a canary's lifespan ranges from 10 to 15 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Domestic_canary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The birds are named after <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a>, which derive their name from the Latin <i>Insula Canaria</i> (after one of the larger islands, <a href="/wiki/Gran_Canaria" title="Gran Canaria">Gran Canaria</a>), meaning "island of dogs", due to its "vast multitudes of dogs of very large size".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tiner2009_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tiner2009-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pliny_the_Elder_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pliny_the_Elder-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Varieties">Varieties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Domestic_canary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Varieties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Canaries are generally divided into three main groups:
</p>
<ul><li>Colour-bred canaries (bred for their many colour <a href="/wiki/Mutation" title="Mutation">mutations</a> – ino, eumo, satinette, bronze, ivory, onyx, mosaic, brown and <a href="/wiki/Red_factor_canary" title="Red factor canary">red factor</a> etc.)</li>
<li>Type canaries (bred for their shape and conformation – <a href="/wiki/Australian_plainhead" title="Australian plainhead">Australian plainhead</a>, Berner, Border, Fife, Gibber Italicus, Gloster, Lancashire, Raza Española, Yorkshire, etc.)</li>
<li>Song canaries (bred for their unique and specific song patterns – Spanish Timbrado, German Roller (also known as <a href="/wiki/Harz_Roller" title="Harz Roller">Harz Roller</a>), Waterslager (also known as "Malinois"), American Singer, Russian Singer, Persian Singer).</li></ul>
<p>While wild canaries are a yellowish-green colour, domestic canaries have been selectively bred for a wide variety of colours, such as yellow, orange, brown, black, white, and red. (The colour red was introduced to the domesticated canary through <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridisation</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Red_siskin" title="Red siskin">red siskin</a>, a type of South American finch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>)
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Competitions">Competitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Domestic_canary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Competitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Canaries are judged in competitions following the annual <a href="/wiki/Moulting#In_birds" title="Moulting">molt</a> in the summer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> This means that in the Northern Hemisphere the show season generally begins in October or November and runs through December or January. Birds can only be shown by the persons who raised them. A show bird must have a unique band on its leg indicating the year of birth, the band number, and the club to which the breeder belongs.
</p><p>There are many canary shows all over the world. The world show (C.O.M. - Confederation Ornithologique Mondiale) is held in Europe each year and attracts thousands of breeders.  As many as 20,000 birds are brought together for this competition.
</p>
<h2><span id="Miner.27s_canary"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Miner's_canary">Miner's canary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Domestic_canary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Miner's canary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Canaries were once regularly used in <a href="/wiki/Coal_mining" title="Coal mining">coal mining</a> as an early warning system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toxic" title="Toxic">Toxic</a> <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gases</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> or <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> in the mine would kill the bird before affecting the miners. Signs of distress from the bird indicated to the miners that conditions were unsafe. The use of miners' canaries in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">British</a> mines was phased out in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>The phrase "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/canary_in_a_coal_mine" title="wikt:canary in a coal mine">canary in a coal mine</a>" is frequently used to refer to a person or thing which serves as an early warning of a coming crisis. By analogy, the term "climate canary" is used to refer to a species that is affected by an environmental danger prior to other species, thus serving as an early warning system for the other species with regard to the danger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> (See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indicator_species" title="Indicator species">indicator species</a>.)
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_research">Use in research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Domestic_canary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Use in research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Canaries have been extensively used in research to study <a href="/wiki/Neurogenesis" title="Neurogenesis">neurogenesis</a>, or the birth of new neurons in the adult brain, and also for basic research in order to understand how songbirds encode and produce song. Thus, canaries have served as model species for discovering how the vertebrate brain learns, consolidates memories, and recalls coordinated motor movements.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Fernando_Nottebohm" title="Fernando Nottebohm">Fernando Nottebohm</a>, a professor at the <a href="/wiki/Rockefeller_University" title="Rockefeller University">Rockefeller University</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, detailed the brain structures and pathways that are involved in the production of bird song.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Domestic_canary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In organized crime, the canary symbolizes an informant who "sings to the police."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Canaries have been depicted in <a href="/wiki/Cartoon" title="Cartoon">cartoons</a> from the mid-20th century as being harassed by domestic <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cats</a>; the most famous cartoon canary is <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros." title="Warner Bros.">Warner Bros.</a>' "<a href="/wiki/Tweety" title="Tweety">Tweety</a>".
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Norwich_City_F.C." title="Norwich City F.C.">Norwich City</a>, an <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Football_(soccer)" title="Football (soccer)">football</a> team, is nicknamed "The Canaries" due to the city once being a famous centre for breeding and export of the birds. The club adopted the colours of yellow and green in homage. Jacob Mackley, of Norwich, won many prizes with birds of the local variety and shipped about 10,000 from Norwich to New York every year. In the early 1900s, he opened his aviaries to the public for three days and 10,000 people turned up.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A number of other sports teams worldwide use variations of the name "Canaries", such as <a href="/wiki/Frosinone_Calcio" title="Frosinone Calcio">Frosinone</a> (Italy), <a href="/wiki/FC_Koper" title="FC Koper">Koper</a> (Slovenia), <a href="/wiki/FK_Novi_Sad" title="FK Novi Sad">FC Novi Sad</a> (Serbia), <a href="/wiki/Fenerbah%C3%A7e_S.K._(football)" title="Fenerbahçe S.K. (football)">Fenerbahçe</a> (Turkey), <a href="/wiki/Kedah_FA" title="Kedah FA">Kedah FA</a> (Malaysia), the <a href="/wiki/Brazil_national_football_team" title="Brazil national football team">Brazil national football team</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Brazil_women%27s_national_football_team" title="Brazil women's national football team">Brazil women's national football team</a>.
</p>
