<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Medication</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>medication</b> (also referred to as <b>medicine</b>, <b>pharmaceutical drug</b>, or simply <b>drug</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Drug" title="Drug">drug</a> used to <a href="/wiki/Medical_diagnosis" title="Medical diagnosis">diagnose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cure" title="Cure">cure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Therapy" title="Therapy">treat</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preventive_medicine" title="Preventive medicine">prevent</a> <a href="/wiki/Disease" title="Disease">disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Drug therapy (<a href="/wiki/Pharmacotherapy" title="Pharmacotherapy">pharmacotherapy</a>) is an important part of the <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medical field</a> and relies on the science of <a href="/wiki/Pharmacology" title="Pharmacology">pharmacology</a> for continual advancement and on <a href="/wiki/Pharmacy" title="Pharmacy">pharmacy</a> for appropriate management.
</p><p>Drugs are <a href="/wiki/Drug_class" title="Drug class">classified</a> in various ways. One of the key divisions is by level of <a href="/wiki/Controlled_substance" title="Controlled substance">control</a>, which distinguishes <a href="/wiki/Prescription_drug" title="Prescription drug">prescription drugs</a> (those that a <a href="/wiki/Pharmacist" title="Pharmacist">pharmacist</a> dispenses only on the <a href="/wiki/Medical_prescription" title="Medical prescription">order</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">physician</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physician_assistant" title="Physician assistant">physician assistant</a>, or qualified <a href="/wiki/Nursing" title="Nursing">nurse</a>) from <a href="/wiki/Over-the-counter_drug" title="Over-the-counter drug">over-the-counter drugs</a> (those that consumers can order for themselves). Another key distinction is between traditional <a href="/wiki/Small_molecule" title="Small molecule">small-molecule</a> drugs, usually derived from <a href="/wiki/Chemical_synthesis" title="Chemical synthesis">chemical synthesis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Biopharmaceutical" title="Biopharmaceutical">biopharmaceuticals</a>, which include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recombinant_proteins" title="Recombinant proteins">recombinant proteins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vaccine" title="Vaccine">vaccines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blood_product" title="Blood product">blood products</a> used <a href="/wiki/Therapy" title="Therapy">therapeutically</a> (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IVIG" title="IVIG">IVIG</a>), <a href="/wiki/Gene_therapy" title="Gene therapy">gene therapy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monoclonal_antibody" title="Monoclonal antibody">monoclonal antibodies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cell_therapy" title="Cell therapy">cell therapy</a> (for instance, <a href="/wiki/Stem_cell" title="Stem cell">stem-cell</a> therapies). Other ways to classify medicines are by mode of action, <a href="/wiki/Route_of_administration" title="Route of administration">route of administration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biological_system" title="Biological system">biological system</a> affected, or <a href="/wiki/Therapeutic_effect" title="Therapeutic effect">therapeutic effects</a>. An elaborate and widely used classification system is the <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_Therapeutic_Chemical_Classification_System" title="Anatomical Therapeutic Chemical Classification System">Anatomical Therapeutic Chemical Classification System</a> (ATC system). The <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> keeps a list of <a href="/wiki/Essential_medicines" title="Essential medicines">essential medicines</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Drug_discovery" title="Drug discovery">Drug discovery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drug_development" title="Drug development">drug development</a> are complex and expensive endeavors undertaken by <a href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_industry" title="Pharmaceutical industry">pharmaceutical companies</a>, academic scientists, and governments. As a result of this complex path from discovery to commercialization, partnering has become a standard practice for advancing drug candidates through development pipelines.  Governments generally regulate what drugs can be marketed, how drugs are marketed, and in some jurisdictions, drug pricing.   Controversies have arisen over drug pricing and disposal of used drugs.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medication&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Europe, the term is "medicinal product", and it is defined by EU law as:
"(a) Any substance or combination of substances presented as having properties for treating or preventing disease in human beings; or
</p><p>(b) Any substance or combination of substances which may be used in or administered to human beings either with a view to restoring, correcting or modifying physiological functions by exerting a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmacological" title="Pharmacological">pharmacological</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immunological" title="Immunological">immunological</a> or metabolic action, or to making a <a href="/wiki/Medical_diagnosis" title="Medical diagnosis">medical diagnosis</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>36</span></sup>
</p><p>In the US, a "drug" is:
</p>
<ul><li>A substance recognized by an official pharmacopoeia or formulary.</li>
<li>A substance intended for use in the diagnosis, cure, mitigation, treatment, or prevention of disease.</li>
<li>A substance (other than food) intended to affect the structure or any function of the body.</li>
<li>A substance intended for use as a component of a medicine but not a device or a component, part or accessory of a device.</li>
<li>Biological products are included within this definition and are generally covered by the same laws and regulations, but differences exist regarding their manufacturing processes (chemical process versus biological process).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medication&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Drug use among elderly Americans has been studied; in a group of 2377 people with average age of 71 surveyed between 2005 and 2006, 84% took at least one prescription drug, 44% took at least one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Over-the-counter" title="Over-the-counter">over-the-counter</a> (OTC) drug, and 52% took at least one <a href="/wiki/Dietary_supplement" title="Dietary supplement">dietary supplement</a>; in a group of 2245 elderly Americans (average age of 71) surveyed over the period 2010 – 2011, those percentages were 88%, 38%, and 64%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medication&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Drug_class" title="Drug class">Drug class</a></div>
<p>One of the key classifications is between traditional <a href="/wiki/Small_molecule" title="Small molecule">small molecule</a> drugs; usually derived from chemical synthesis, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biologic_medical_product" title="Biologic medical product">biologic medical products</a>; which include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recombinant_proteins" title="Recombinant proteins">recombinant proteins</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vaccines" title="Vaccines">vaccines</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_products" title="Blood products">blood products</a> used therapeutically (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IVIG" title="IVIG">IVIG</a>), <a href="/wiki/Gene_therapy" title="Gene therapy">gene therapy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cell_therapy" title="Cell therapy">cell therapy</a> (for instance, <a href="/wiki/Stem_cell" title="Stem cell">stem cell</a> therapies).
</p><p>Pharmaceutical or drug or medicines are classified in various other groups besides their origin on the basis of pharmacological properties like mode of action and their pharmacological action or activity,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> such as by <a href="/wiki/Chemical_property" title="Chemical property">chemical properties</a>, mode or <a href="/wiki/Route_of_administration" title="Route of administration">route of administration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biological_system" title="Biological system">biological system</a> affected, or <a href="/wiki/Therapeutic_effect" title="Therapeutic effect">therapeutic effects</a>. An elaborate and widely used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classification_system" title="Classification system">classification system</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_Therapeutic_Chemical_Classification_System" title="Anatomical Therapeutic Chemical Classification System">Anatomical Therapeutic Chemical Classification System</a> (ATC system). The <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> keeps a list of <a href="/wiki/Essential_medicines" title="Essential medicines">essential medicines</a>.
</p><p>A sampling of classes of medicine includes:
</p>
<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Antipyretic" title="Antipyretic">Antipyretics</a>: reducing <a href="/wiki/Fever" title="Fever">fever</a> (pyrexia/pyresis)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Analgesic" title="Analgesic">Analgesics</a>: reducing <a href="/wiki/Pain" title="Pain">pain</a> (painkillers)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antimalarial_drug" title="Antimalarial drug">Antimalarial drugs</a>: treating <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Antibiotic" title="Antibiotic">Antibiotics</a>: inhibiting <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">germ</a> growth</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Antiseptic" title="Antiseptic">Antiseptics</a>: prevention of germ growth near <a href="/wiki/Burn" title="Burn">burns</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cut_(wound)" title="Cut (wound)">cuts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wound" title="Wound">wounds</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mood_stabilizer" title="Mood stabilizer">Mood stabilizers</a>: <a href="/wiki/Lithium_(medication)" title="Lithium (medication)">lithium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Valpromide" title="Valpromide">valpromide</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hormone_replacement" title="Hormone replacement">Hormone replacements</a>: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Premarin" title="Premarin">Premarin</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oral_contraceptive" title="Oral contraceptive">Oral contraceptives</a>: Enovid, "biphasic" pill, and "triphasic" pill</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Stimulant" title="Stimulant">Stimulants</a>: <a href="/wiki/Methylphenidate" title="Methylphenidate">methylphenidate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amphetamine" title="Amphetamine">amphetamine</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tranquilizer" title="Tranquilizer">Tranquilizers</a>: <a href="/wiki/Meprobamate" title="Meprobamate">meprobamate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chlorpromazine" title="Chlorpromazine">chlorpromazine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reserpine" title="Reserpine">reserpine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chlordiazepoxide" title="Chlordiazepoxide">chlordiazepoxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diazepam" title="Diazepam">diazepam</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alprazolam" title="Alprazolam">alprazolam</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Statin" title="Statin">Statins</a>: <a href="/wiki/Lovastatin" title="Lovastatin">lovastatin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pravastatin" title="Pravastatin">pravastatin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Simvastatin" title="Simvastatin">simvastatin</a></li></ol>
<p>Pharmaceuticals may also be described as "specialty", independent of other classifications, which is an ill-defined class of drugs that might be difficult to administer, require special handling during administration, require patient monitoring during and immediately after administration, have particular regulatory requirements restricting their use, and are generally expensive relative to other drugs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Administration">Administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medication&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Route_of_administration" title="Route of administration">Route of administration</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Drawing_by_Marguerite_Martyn_of_a_visiting_nurse_with_medicine_and_four_babies,_1918.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1941" data-file-width="3000" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/0b/Drawing_by_Marguerite_Martyn_of_a_visiting_nurse_with_medicine_and_four_babies%2C_1918.jpg/220px-Drawing_by_Marguerite_Martyn_of_a_visiting_nurse_with_medicine_and_four_babies%2C_1918.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/0b/Drawing_by_Marguerite_Martyn_of_a_visiting_nurse_with_medicine_and_four_babies%2C_1918.jpg/330px-Drawing_by_Marguerite_Martyn_of_a_visiting_nurse_with_medicine_and_four_babies%2C_1918.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/0b/Drawing_by_Marguerite_Martyn_of_a_visiting_nurse_with_medicine_and_four_babies%2C_1918.jpg/440px-Drawing_by_Marguerite_Martyn_of_a_visiting_nurse_with_medicine_and_four_babies%2C_1918.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Drawing_by_Marguerite_Martyn_of_a_visiting_nurse_with_medicine_and_four_babies,_1918.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>February 1918 drawing by <a href="/wiki/Marguerite_Martyn" title="Marguerite Martyn">Marguerite Martyn</a> of a visiting nurse in St. Louis, Missouri, with medicine and babies</div></div></div>
<p>Administration is the process by which a patient takes a medicine. There are three major categories of drug administration; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enteral" title="Enteral">enteral</a> (by mouth), <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/parenteral" title="wikt:parenteral">parenteral</a> (into the blood stream), and other (which includes giving a drug through intranasal, topical, inhalation, and rectal means).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>It can be performed in various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dosage_forms" title="Dosage forms">dosage forms</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pill_(pharmacy)" title="Pill (pharmacy)">pills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tablet_(pharmacy)" title="Tablet (pharmacy)">tablets</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Capsule_(pharmacy)" title="Capsule (pharmacy)">capsules</a>.
</p><p>There are many variations in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Routes_of_administration" title="Routes of administration">routes of administration</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Intravenous_therapy" title="Intravenous therapy">intravenous</a> (into the blood through a vein) and <a href="/wiki/Oral_administration" title="Oral administration">oral administration</a> (through the mouth).
</p><p>They can be administered all at once as a <a href="/wiki/Bolus_(medicine)" title="Bolus (medicine)">bolus</a>, at frequent intervals or continuously. Frequencies are often abbreviated from Latin, such as <i>every 8 hours</i> reading Q8H from <i>Quaque VIII Hora</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Drug_discovery">Drug discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medication&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Drug discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Drug_discovery" title="Drug discovery">Drug discovery</a></div>
<p>In the fields of <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">biotechnology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pharmacology" title="Pharmacology">pharmacology</a>, drug discovery is the process by which new drugs are discovered.
</p><p>Historically, drugs were discovered through identifying the active ingredient from traditional remedies or by <a href="/wiki/Serendipity" title="Serendipity">serendipitous</a> discovery.  Later <a href="/wiki/Chemical_library" title="Chemical library">chemical libraries</a> of synthetic <a href="/wiki/Small_molecule" title="Small molecule">small molecules</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_product" title="Natural product">natural products</a> or <a href="/wiki/Extract" title="Extract">extracts</a> were screened in intact cells or whole organisms to identify substances that have a desirable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Therapeutic" title="Therapeutic">therapeutic</a> effect in a process known as
<a href="/wiki/Classical_pharmacology" title="Classical pharmacology">classical pharmacology</a>.  Since <a href="/wiki/Human_Genome_Project" title="Human Genome Project">sequencing</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Human_genome" title="Human genome">human genome</a> which allowed rapid cloning and synthesis of large quantities of purified proteins, it has become common practice to use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_throughput_screening" title="High throughput screening">high throughput screening</a> of large compounds libraries against isolated <a href="/wiki/Biological_target" title="Biological target">biological targets</a> which are hypothesized to be disease modifying in a process known as <a href="/wiki/Reverse_pharmacology" title="Reverse pharmacology">reverse pharmacology</a>.  Hits from these screens are then tested in cells and then in animals for <a href="/wiki/Efficacy" title="Efficacy">efficacy</a>.  Even more recently, scientists have been able to understand the shape of biological molecules at the atomic level, and to use that knowledge to design (see <a href="/wiki/Drug_design" title="Drug design">drug design</a>) drug candidates.
</p><p>Modern drug discovery involves the identification of screening hits, <a href="/wiki/Medicinal_chemistry" title="Medicinal chemistry">medicinal chemistry</a> and optimization of those hits to increase the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Affinity_(pharmacology)#Protein-ligand_binding" title="Affinity (pharmacology)">affinity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Binding_selectivity" title="Binding selectivity">selectivity</a> (to reduce the potential of side effects), efficacy/<a href="/wiki/Potency_(pharmacology)" title="Potency (pharmacology)">potency</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolic</a> stability (to increase the <a href="/wiki/Biological_half-life" title="Biological half-life">half-life</a>), and oral <a href="/wiki/Bioavailability" title="Bioavailability">bioavailability</a>. Once a compound that fulfills all of these requirements has been identified, it will begin the process of <a href="/wiki/Drug_development" title="Drug development">drug development</a> prior to <a href="/wiki/Clinical_trial" title="Clinical trial">clinical trials</a>. One or more of these steps may, but not necessarily, involve <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer-aided_drug_design" title="Computer-aided drug design">computer-aided drug design</a>.
</p><p>Despite advances in technology and understanding of biological systems, drug discovery is still a lengthy, "expensive, difficult, and inefficient process" with low rate of new therapeutic discovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anson2009_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Anson2009-10">[10]</a></sup> In 2010, the research and development cost of each <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_molecular_entity" title="New molecular entity">new molecular entity</a> (NME) was approximately US$1.8 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Drug discovery is done by pharmaceutical companies, with research assistance from universities. The "final product" of drug discovery is a <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a> on the potential drug. The drug requires very expensive Phase I, II and III clinical trials, and most of them fail. Small companies have a critical role, often then selling the rights to larger companies that have the resources to run the clinical trials.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medication&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Drug_development" title="Drug development">Drug development</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Drug_development" title="Drug development">Drug development</a> is the process of bringing a new drug to the market once a <a href="/wiki/Lead_compound" title="Lead compound">lead compound</a> has been identified through the process of <a href="/wiki/Drug_discovery" title="Drug discovery">drug discovery</a>. It includes pre-clinical research (microorganisms/animals) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clinical_trials" title="Clinical trials">clinical trials</a> (on humans) and may include the step of obtaining regulatory approval to market the drug.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation">Regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medication&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Regulation_of_therapeutic_goods" title="Regulation of therapeutic goods">Regulation of therapeutic goods</a></div>
<p>The regulation of drugs varies by jurisdiction. In some countries, such as the United States, they are regulated at the national level by a single agency. In other jurisdictions they are regulated at the state level, or at both state and national levels by various bodies, as is the case in Australia.  The role of therapeutic goods regulation is designed mainly to protect the health and safety of the population. Regulation is aimed at ensuring the safety, quality, and efficacy of the therapeutic goods which are covered under the scope of the regulation. In most jurisdictions, therapeutic goods must be registered before they are allowed to be marketed. There is usually some degree of restriction of the availability of certain therapeutic goods depending on their risk to consumers.
</p><p>Depending upon the <a href="/wiki/Jurisdiction" title="Jurisdiction">jurisdiction</a>, drugs may be divided into <a href="/wiki/Over-the-counter_drug" title="Over-the-counter drug">over-the-counter drugs</a> (OTC) which may be available without special restrictions, and <a href="/wiki/Prescription_drug" title="Prescription drug">prescription drugs</a>, which must be <a href="/wiki/Medical_prescription" title="Medical prescription">prescribed</a> by a licensed medical practitioner. The precise distinction between OTC and prescription depends on the legal <a href="/wiki/Jurisdiction" title="Jurisdiction">jurisdiction</a>. A third category, "behind-the-counter" drugs, is implemented in some jurisdictions. These do not require a prescription, but must be kept in the <a href="/wiki/Dispensary" title="Dispensary">dispensary</a>, not visible to the public, and only be sold by a <a href="/wiki/Pharmacist" title="Pharmacist">pharmacist</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pharmacy_technician" title="Pharmacy technician">pharmacy technician</a>. Doctors may also prescribe prescription drugs for <a href="/wiki/Off-label_use" title="Off-label use">off-label use</a> – purposes which the drugs were not originally approved for by the regulatory agency. The <a href="/wiki/Classification_of_Pharmaco-Therapeutic_Referrals" title="Classification of Pharmaco-Therapeutic Referrals">Classification of Pharmaco-Therapeutic Referrals</a> helps guide the referral process between pharmacists and doctors.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Narcotics_Control_Board" title="International Narcotics Control Board">International Narcotics Control Board</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> imposes a world law of <a href="/wiki/Prohibition" title="Prohibition">prohibition</a> of certain drugs. They publish a lengthy list of chemicals and plants whose trade and consumption (where applicable) is forbidden. OTC drugs are sold without restriction as they are considered safe enough that most people will not hurt themselves accidentally by taking it as instructed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Many countries, such as the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> have a third category of "pharmacy medicines", which can only be sold in registered <a href="/wiki/Pharmacy" title="Pharmacy">pharmacies</a> by or under the supervision of a <a href="/wiki/Pharmacist" title="Pharmacist">pharmacist</a>.
</p>

<p>In many jurisdictions <a href="/wiki/Prescription_costs" title="Prescription costs">drug prices</a> are regulated.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medication&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the UK the Pharmaceutical Price Regulation Scheme is intended to ensure that the <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service" title="National Health Service">National Health Service</a> is able to purchase drugs at reasonable prices. The prices are negotiated between the Department of Health, acting with the authority of Northern Ireland and the UK Government, and the representatives of the Pharmaceutical industry brands, the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_the_British_Pharmaceutical_Industry" title="Association of the British Pharmaceutical Industry">Association of the British Pharmaceutical Industry</a> (ABPI). For 2017 this payment percentage set by the PPRS will be 4,75%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medication&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Canada, the Patented Medicine Prices Review Board examines drug pricing and determines if a price is excessive or not. In these circumstances, drug manufacturers must submit a proposed price to the appropriate regulatory agency. Furthermore, "the International Therapeutic Class Comparison Test is responsible for comparing the National Average Transaction Price of the patented drug product under review"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-price-comparison_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-price-comparison-16">[16]</a></sup> different countries that the prices are being compared to are the following: France, Germany, Italy, Sweden, Switzerland, the United Kingdom, and the United States<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-price-comparison_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-price-comparison-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazil">Brazil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medication&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Brazil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Brazil, the prices are regulated through a legislation under the name of <i>Medicamento Genérico</i> (<a href="/wiki/Generic_drug" title="Generic drug">generic drugs</a>) since 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medication&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In India, drug prices are regulated by the <a href="/wiki/National_Pharmaceutical_Pricing_Authority" title="National Pharmaceutical Pricing Authority">National Pharmaceutical Pricing Authority</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medication&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Prescription_drug_prices_in_the_United_States" title="Prescription drug prices in the United States">Prescription drug prices in the United States</a></div>
<p>In the United States, drug costs are unregulated, but instead are the result of negotiations between drug companies and insurance companies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>High prices have been attributed to monopolies given to manufacturers by the government and a lack of ability for organizations to negotiate prices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Blockbuster_drug">Blockbuster drug</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medication&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Blockbuster drug">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_largest_selling_pharmaceutical_products" title="List of largest selling pharmaceutical products">List of largest selling pharmaceutical products</a></div>
<p>A blockbuster drug is a drug generating more than $1 billion of revenue for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_company" title="Pharmaceutical company">pharmaceutical company</a> that sells it each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cimetidine" title="Cimetidine">Cimetidine</a> was the first drug ever to reach more than $1 billion a year in sales, thus making it the first blockbuster drug.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>"In the pharmaceutical industry, a blockbuster drug is one that achieves acceptance by prescribing physicians as a therapeutic standard for, most commonly, a highly prevalent chronic (rather than acute) condition. Patients often take the medicines for long periods."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p>Interestingly, despite the enormous  advances in science and technology, the number of new blockbuster drugs has halved roughly every 9 years since 1950. This was ascribed to the fact that every new drug competes in effectiveness with every other drugs known so far, other economic factors and ever-tightening regulations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medication&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_pharmacy" title="History of pharmacy">History of pharmacy</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prescription_drug_history">Prescription drug history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medication&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Prescription drug history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Antibiotics first arrived on the medical scene in 1932 thanks to Gerhard Domagk;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and were coined the "wonder drugs". The introduction of the sulfa drugs led to the mortality rate from pneumonia in the U.S. to drop from 0.2% each year to 0.05% by 1939.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Antibiotics inhibit the growth or the metabolic activities of bacteria and other microorganisms by a chemical substance of microbial origin. Penicillin, introduced a few years later, provided a broader spectrum of activity compared to sulfa drugs and reduced side effects. Streptomycin, found in 1942, proved to be the first drug effective against the cause of tuberculosis and also came to be the best known of a long series of important antibiotics. A second generation of antibiotics was introduced in the 1940s: aureomycin and chloramphenicol. Aureomycin was the best known of the second generation.
</p><p>Lithium was discovered in the 19th century for nervous disorders and its possible mood-stabilizing or prophylactic effect; it was cheap and easily produced. As lithium fell out of favor in France, valpromide came into play. This antibiotic was the origin of the drug that eventually created the mood stabilizer category. Valpromide had distinct psychotrophic effects that were of benefit in both the treatment of acute manic states and in the maintenance treatment of manic depression illness. Psychotropics can either be sedative or stimulant; sedatives aim at damping down the extremes of behavior. Stimulants aim at restoring normality by increasing tone. Soon arose the notion of a tranquilizer which was quite different from any sedative or stimulant. The term tranquilizer took over the notions of sedatives and became the dominant term in the West through the 1980s. In Japan, during this time, the term tranquilizer produced the notion of a psyche-stabilizer and the term mood stabilizer vanished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ToneAndrea_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-ToneAndrea-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Premarin (conjugated estrogens, introduced in 1942) and Prempro (a combination estrogen-progestin pill, introduced in 1995) dominated the hormone replacement therapy (HRT) during the 1990s. HRT is not a life-saving drug, nor does it cure any disease. HRT has been prescribed to improve one's quality of life. Doctors prescribe estrogen for their older female patients both to treat short-term menopausal symptoms and to prevent long-term diseases. In the 1960s and early 1970s more and more physicians began to prescribe estrogen for their female patients. between 1991 and 1999, Premarin was listed as the most popular prescription and best-selling drug in America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ToneAndrea_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-ToneAndrea-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first oral contraceptive, Enovid, was approved by FDA in 1960. Oral contraceptives inhibit ovulation and so prevent conception. Enovid was known to be much more effective than alternatives including the condom and the diaphragm. As early as 1960, oral contraceptives were available in several different strengths by every manufacturer. In the 1980s and 1990s an increasing number of options arose including, most recently, a new <a href="/wiki/Drug_delivery" title="Drug delivery">delivery system</a> for the oral contraceptive via a transdermal patch. In 1982, a new version of the Pill was introduced, known as the "biphasic" pill. By 1985, a new triphasic pill was approved. Physicians began to think of the Pill as an excellent means of birth control for young women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ToneAndrea_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-ToneAndrea-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Stimulants such as Ritalin (methylphenidate) came to be pervasive tools for behavior management and modification in young children. Ritalin was first marketed in 1955 for narcolepsy; its potential users were middle-aged and the elderly. It wasn't until some time in the 1980s along with hyperactivity in children that Ritalin came onto the market. Medical use of methlyphenidate is predominately for symptoms of attention deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD). Consumption of methylphenidate in the U.S. out-paced all other countries between 1991 and 1999. Significant growth in consumption was also evident in Canada, New Zealand, Australia, and Norway. Currently, 85% of the world's methylphanidate is consumed in America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ToneAndrea_26-3"><a href="#cite_note-ToneAndrea-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first minor tranquilizer was Meprobamate. Only fourteen months after it was made available, meprobamate had become the country's largest-selling prescription drug. By 1957, meprobamate had become the fastest-growing drug in history. The popularity of meprobamate paved the way for Librium and Valium, two minor tranquilizers that belonged to a new chemical class of drugs called the benzodiazepines. These were drugs that worked chiefly as anti-anxiety agents and muscle relaxants. The first benzodiazepine was Librium. Three months after it was approved, Librium had become the most prescribed tranquilizer in the nation. Three years later, Valium hit the shelves and was ten times more effective as a muscle relaxant and anti-convulsant. Valium was the most versatile of the minor tranquilizers. Later came the widespread adoption of major tranquilizers such as chlorpromazine and the drug reserpine. In 1970 sales began to decline for Valium and Librium, but sales of new and improved tranquilizers, such as Xanax, introduced in 1981 for the newly created diagnosis of panic disorder, soared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ToneAndrea_26-4"><a href="#cite_note-ToneAndrea-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mevacor (lovastatin) is the first and most influential statin in the American market. The 1991 launch of Pravachol (pravastatin), the second available in the United States, and the release of Zocor (simvastatin) made Mevacor no longer the only statin on the market.
In 1998, Viagra was released as a treatment for erectile dysfunction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ToneAndrea_26-5"><a href="#cite_note-ToneAndrea-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_pharmacology">Ancient pharmacology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medication&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Ancient pharmacology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Using plants and plant substances to treat all kinds of diseases and medical conditions is believed to date back to <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_medicine" title="Prehistoric medicine">prehistoric medicine</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Kahun_Gynaecological_Papyrus" title="Kahun Gynaecological Papyrus">Kahun Gynaecological Papyrus</a>, the oldest known medical text of any kind, dates to about 1800 BC and represents the first documented use of any kind of drug.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> It and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_papyri" title="Medical papyri">medical papyri</a> describe <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_medicine" title="Ancient Egyptian medicine">Ancient Egyptian medical practices</a>, such as using <a href="/wiki/Honey" title="Honey">honey</a> to treat infections and the legs of bee-eaters to treat neck pains.
</p><p>Ancient <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Babylonian_medicine" title="Babylonian medicine">Babylonian medicine</a> demonstrate the use of <a href="/wiki/Prescription_drug" title="Prescription drug">prescriptions</a> in the first half of the <a href="/wiki/2nd_millennium_BC" title="2nd millennium BC">2nd millennium BC</a>. <a href="/wiki/Cream_(pharmaceutical)" title="Cream (pharmaceutical)">Medicinal creams</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pill_(pharmacy)" title="Pill (pharmacy)">pills</a> were employed as treatments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stol-99_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stol-99-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>On the Indian subcontinent, the <a href="/wiki/Atharvaveda" title="Atharvaveda">Atharvaveda</a>, a sacred text of <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a> whose core dates from the 2nd millennium BC, although the hymns recorded in it are believed to be older, is the first Indic text dealing with <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>. It describes plant-based drugs to counter diseases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The earliest foundations of <a href="/wiki/Ayurveda" title="Ayurveda">ayurveda</a> were built on a synthesis of selected ancient herbal practices, together with a massive addition of theoretical conceptualizations, new <a href="/wiki/Nosology" title="Nosology">nosologies</a> and new therapies dating from about 400 BC onwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The student of Āyurveda was expected to know ten arts that were indispensable in the preparation and application of his medicines: distillation, operative skills, cooking, horticulture, metallurgy, sugar manufacture, pharmacy, analysis and separation of minerals, compounding of metals, and preparation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkalis" title="Alkalis">alkalis</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Hippocratic_Oath" title="Hippocratic Oath">Hippocratic Oath</a> for physicians, attributed to 5th century BC Greece, refers to the existence of "deadly drugs", and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medicine_in_ancient_Greece" title="Medicine in ancient Greece">ancient Greek physicians</a> imported drugs from Egypt and elsewhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_pharmacology">Medieval pharmacology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medication&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: Medieval pharmacology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Al-Kindi" title="Al-Kindi">Al-Kindi</a>'s 9th century AD book, <i><a href="/wiki/De_Gradibus" title="De Gradibus">De Gradibus</a></i> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ibn_Sina" title="Ibn Sina">Ibn Sina</a> (Avicenna)'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Canon_of_Medicine" title="The Canon of Medicine">The Canon of Medicine</a></i> cover a range of drugs known to <a href="/wiki/Medicine_in_the_medieval_Islamic_world" title="Medicine in the medieval Islamic world">Medicine in the medieval Islamic world</a>.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval_medicine" title="Medieval medicine">Medieval medicine</a> saw advances in surgery, but few truly effective drugs existed, beyond <a href="/wiki/Opium" title="Opium">opium</a> (found in such extremely popular drugs as the "Great Rest" of the <a href="/wiki/Antidotarium_Nicolai" title="Antidotarium Nicolai">Antidotarium Nicolai</a> at the time)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Quinine" title="Quinine">quinine</a>. Folklore cures and potentially poisonous metal-based compounds were popular treatments. <a href="/wiki/Theodoric_Borgognoni" title="Theodoric Borgognoni">Theodoric Borgognoni</a>, (1205–1296), one of the most significant surgeons of the medieval period, responsible for introducing and promoting important surgical advances including basic <a href="/wiki/Antiseptic" title="Antiseptic">antiseptic</a> practice and the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anaesthetic" title="Anaesthetic">anaesthetics</a>. <a href="/wiki/Garcia_de_Orta" title="Garcia de Orta">Garcia de Orta</a> described some herbal treatments that were used.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_pharmacology">Modern pharmacology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medication&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Modern pharmacology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For most of the 19th century, drugs were not highly effective, leading <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oliver_Wendell_Holmes,_Sr." title="Oliver Wendell Holmes, Sr.">Oliver Wendell Holmes, Sr.</a> to famously comment in 1842 that "if all medicines in the world were thrown into the sea, it would be all the better for mankind and all the worse for the fishes".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reasonable_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reasonable-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>21</span></sup>
</p><p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">First World War</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexis_Carrel" title="Alexis Carrel">Alexis Carrel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henry_Drysdale_Dakin" title="Henry Drysdale Dakin">Henry Dakin</a> developed the Carrel-Dakin method of treating wounds with an irrigation, Dakin's solution, a germicide which helped prevent gangrene.
</p><p>In the inter-war period, the first anti-bacterial agents such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulpha" title="Sulpha">sulpha</a> antibiotics were developed. The Second World War saw the introduction of widespread and effective antimicrobial therapy with the development and mass production of <a href="/wiki/Penicillin" title="Penicillin">penicillin</a> antibiotics, made possible by the pressures of the war and the collaboration of British scientists with the American <a href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_industry" title="Pharmaceutical industry">pharmaceutical industry</a>.
</p><p>Medicines commonly used by the late 1920s included <a href="/wiki/Aspirin" title="Aspirin">aspirin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Codeine" title="Codeine">codeine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Morphine" title="Morphine">morphine</a> for pain; <a href="/wiki/Digoxin" title="Digoxin">digitalis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitroglycerin" title="Nitroglycerin">nitroglycerin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Quinine" title="Quinine">quinine</a> for heart disorders, and <a href="/wiki/Insulin" title="Insulin">insulin</a> for diabetes. Other drugs included <a href="/wiki/Antitoxin" title="Antitoxin">antitoxins</a>, a few biological vaccines, and a few synthetic drugs. In the 1930s antibiotics emerged: first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulfa_drug" title="Sulfa drug">sulfa drugs</a>, then <a href="/wiki/Penicillin" title="Penicillin">penicillin</a> and other antibiotics. Drugs increasingly became "the center of medical practice".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reasonable_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reasonable-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>22</span></sup> In the 1950s other drugs emerged including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corticosteroids" title="Corticosteroids">corticosteroids</a> for <a href="/wiki/Inflammation" title="Inflammation">inflammation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rauvolfia#Chemical_constituents" title="Rauvolfia">rauvolfia alkaloids</a> as tranqulizers and antihypertensives, <a href="/wiki/Antihistamine" title="Antihistamine">antihistamines</a> for nasal allergies, <a href="/wiki/Xanthine" title="Xanthine">xanthines</a> for asthma, and typical <a href="/wiki/Antipsychotic" title="Antipsychotic">antipsychotics</a> for psychosis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reasonable_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-Reasonable-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>23–24</span></sup> As of 2007, thousands of approved drugs have been <a href="/wiki/Drug_development" title="Drug development">developed</a>. Increasingly, <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">biotechnology</a> is used to discover <a href="/wiki/Biopharmaceutical" title="Biopharmaceutical">biopharmaceuticals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reasonable_34-3"><a href="#cite_note-Reasonable-34">[34]</a></sup> Recently, multi-disciplinary approaches have yielded a wealth of new data on the development of novel antibiotics and antibacterials and on the use of biological agents for antibacterial therapy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MillerAAMillerPF_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-MillerAAMillerPF-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1950s new psychiatric drugs, notably the antipsychotic <a href="/wiki/Chlorpromazine" title="Chlorpromazine">chlorpromazine</a>, were designed in laboratories and slowly came into preferred use. Although often accepted as an advance in some ways, there was some opposition, due to serious adverse effects such as <a href="/wiki/Tardive_dyskinesia" title="Tardive dyskinesia">tardive dyskinesia</a>. Patients often opposed psychiatry and refused or stopped taking the drugs when not subject to psychiatric control.
</p><p>Governments have been heavily involved in the regulation of drug development and drug sales. In the U.S., the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elixir_Sulfanilamide_disaster" title="Elixir Sulfanilamide disaster">Elixir Sulfanilamide disaster</a> led to the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a>, and the 1938 Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act required manufacturers to file new drugs with the FDA. The 1951 Humphrey-Durham Amendment required certain drugs to be sold by prescription. In 1962 a subsequent amendment required new drugs to be tested for efficacy and safety in <a href="/wiki/Clinical_trial" title="Clinical trial">clinical trials</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reasonable_34-4"><a href="#cite_note-Reasonable-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>24–26</span></sup>
</p><p>Until the 1970s, drug prices were not a major concern for doctors and patients. As more drugs became prescribed for chronic illnesses, however, costs became burdensome, and by the 1970s nearly every U.S. state required or encouraged the substitution of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generic_drugs" title="Generic drugs">generic drugs</a> for higher-priced brand names. This also led to the 2006 U.S. law, <a href="/wiki/Medicare_Part_D" title="Medicare Part D">Medicare Part D</a>, which offers Medicare coverage for drugs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reasonable_34-5"><a href="#cite_note-Reasonable-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>28–29</span></sup>
</p><p>As of 2008, the United States is the leader in <a href="/wiki/Medical_research" title="Medical research">medical research</a>, including pharmaceutical development. U.S. drug prices are among the highest in the world, and drug innovation is correspondingly high. In 2000 U.S.-based firms developed 29 of the 75 top-selling drugs; firms from the second-largest market, Japan, developed eight, and the United Kingdom contributed 10. France, which imposes price controls, developed three. Throughout the 1990s outcomes were similar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reasonable_34-6"><a href="#cite_note-Reasonable-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>30–31</span></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medication&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Controversies concerning pharmaceutical drugs include patient access to drugs under development and not yet approved, pricing, and environmental issues.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Access_to_unapproved_drugs">Access to unapproved drugs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medication&amp;action=edit&amp;section=42" title="Edit section: Access to unapproved drugs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Named_patient_programs" title="Named patient programs">Named patient programs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Expanded_access" title="Expanded access">Expanded access</a></div>
<p>Governments worldwide have created provisions for granting access to drugs prior to approval for patients who have exhausted all alternative treatment options and do not match clinical trial entry criteria. Often grouped under the labels of compassionate use, <a href="/wiki/Expanded_access" title="Expanded access">expanded access</a>, or named patient supply, these programs are governed by rules which vary by country defining access criteria, data collection, promotion, and control of drug distribution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>Within the United States, pre-approval demand is generally met through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treatment_IND" title="Treatment IND">treatment IND</a> (investigational new drug) applications (INDs), or single-patient INDs. These mechanisms, which fall under the label of expanded access programs, provide access to drugs for groups of patients or individuals residing in the US. Outside the US, Named Patient Programs provide controlled, pre-approval access to drugs in response to requests by physicians on behalf of specific, or "named", patients before those medicines are licensed in the patient's home country.  Through these programs, patients are able to access drugs in late-stage clinical trials or approved in other countries for a genuine, unmet medical need, before those drugs have been licensed in the patient's home country.
</p><p>Patients who have not been able to get access to drugs in development have organized and advocated for greater access.  In the United States, <a href="/wiki/ACT_UP" title="ACT UP">ACT UP</a> formed in the 1980s, and eventually formed its <a href="/wiki/Treatment_Action_Group" title="Treatment Action Group">Treatment Action Group</a> in part to pressure the US government to put more resources into discovering treatments for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AIDS" title="AIDS">AIDS</a> and then to speed release of drugs that were under development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abigail_Alliance" title="Abigail Alliance">Abigail Alliance</a> was established in November 2001 by Frank Burroughs in memory of his daughter, Abigail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> The Alliance seeks broader availability of investigational drugs on behalf of terminally ill patients.
</p><p>In 2013, <a href="/wiki/BioMarin_Pharmaceutical" title="BioMarin Pharmaceutical">BioMarin Pharmaceutical</a> was at the center of a high-profile debate regarding expanded access of cancer patients to experimental drugs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Access_to_medicines_and_drug_pricing">Access to medicines and drug pricing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medication&amp;action=edit&amp;section=43" title="Edit section: Access to medicines and drug pricing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Essential_medicines" title="Essential medicines">Essential medicines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Societal_views_on_patents" title="Societal views on patents">Societal views on patents</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Essential_medicines" title="Essential medicines">Essential medicines</a> as defined by the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> (WHO) are "those drugs that satisfy the health care needs of the majority of the population; they should therefore be available at all times in adequate amounts and in appropriate dosage forms, at a price the community can afford."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>  Recent studies have found that most of the medicines on the WHO essential medicines list, outside of the field of HIV drugs, are not patented in the developing world, and that lack of widespread <a href="/wiki/Access_to_medicines" title="Access to medicines">access to these medicines</a> arise from issues fundamental to economic development – lack of infrastructure and poverty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9decins_Sans_Fronti%C3%A8res" title="Médecins Sans Frontières">Médecins Sans Frontières</a> also runs a <a href="/wiki/Campaign_for_Access_to_Essential_Medicines" title="Campaign for Access to Essential Medicines">Campaign for Access to Essential Medicines</a> campaign, which includes advocacy for greater resources to be devoted to currently untreatable diseases that primarily occur in the developing world.   The <a href="/wiki/Access_to_Medicine_Index" title="Access to Medicine Index">Access to Medicine Index</a> tracks how well pharmaceutical companies make their products available in the developing world.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Organization" title="World Trade Organization">World Trade Organization</a> negotiations in the 1990s, including the <a href="/wiki/TRIPS_Agreement" title="TRIPS Agreement">TRIPS Agreement</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Doha_Declaration" title="Doha Declaration">Doha Declaration</a>, have centered on issues at the intersection of international trade in pharmaceuticals and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intellectual_property_rights" title="Intellectual property rights">intellectual property rights</a>, with developed world nations seeking strong intellectual property rights to protect investments made to develop new drugs, and developing world nations seeking to promote their generic pharmaceuticals industries and their ability to make medicine available to their people via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compulsory_licenses" title="Compulsory licenses">compulsory licenses</a>.
</p><p>Some have raised ethical objections specifically with respect to pharmaceutical patents and the high prices for drugs that they enable their proprietors to charge, which poor people in the developed world, and developing world, cannot afford.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Banta,_D.H._2001_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Banta,_D.H._2001-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Critics also question the rationale that exclusive patent rights and the resulting high prices are required for pharmaceutical companies to recoup the large investments needed for research and development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Banta,_D.H._2001_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Banta,_D.H._2001-43">[43]</a></sup> One study concluded that marketing expenditures for new drugs often doubled the amount that was allocated for research and development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Other critics claim that patent settlements would be costly for consumers, the health care system, and state and federal governments because it would result in delaying access to lower cost generic medicines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Novartis" title="Novartis">Novartis</a> fought a protracted battle with the government of India over the patenting of its drug, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gleevec" title="Gleevec">Gleevec</a>, in India, which ended up in India's Supreme Court in a case known as <a href="/wiki/Novartis_v._Union_of_India_%26_Others" title="Novartis v. Union of India &amp; Others">Novartis v. Union of India &amp; Others</a>.  The Supreme Court ruled narrowly against Novartis, but opponents of patenting drugs claimed it as a major victory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_issues">Environmental issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medication&amp;action=edit&amp;section=44" title="Edit section: Environmental issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_pharmaceuticals_and_personal_care_products" title="Environmental impact of pharmaceuticals and personal care products">Environmental impact of pharmaceuticals and personal care products</a></div>
<p>The environmental impact of pharmaceuticals and personal care products is controversial. PPCPs are substances used by individuals for personal health or <a href="/wiki/Cosmetics" title="Cosmetics">cosmetic</a> reasons and the products used by <a href="/wiki/Agribusiness" title="Agribusiness">agribusiness</a> to boost growth or health of livestock.  PPCPs comprise a diverse collection of thousands of chemical substances, including prescription and over-the-counter therapeutic drugs, veterinary drugs, fragrances, and cosmetics. PPCPs have been detected in water bodies throughout the world and ones that persist in the environment are called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_Persistent_Pharmaceutical_Pollutant" title="Environmental Persistent Pharmaceutical Pollutant">Environmental Persistent Pharmaceutical Pollutants</a>.  The effects of these chemicals on humans and the environment are not yet known, but to date there is no scientific evidence that they affect human health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-epa-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
