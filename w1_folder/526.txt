<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Room temperature</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Room_temperature_(disambiguation)" title="Room temperature (disambiguation)">Room temperature (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mercury_Thermometer.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1990" data-file-width="922" height="367" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Mercury_Thermometer.jpg/170px-Mercury_Thermometer.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Mercury_Thermometer.jpg/255px-Mercury_Thermometer.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Mercury_Thermometer.jpg/340px-Mercury_Thermometer.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mercury_Thermometer.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">Mercury</a>-in-glass <a href="/wiki/Thermometer" title="Thermometer">thermometer</a> showing an ambient temperature within the range of room temperature</div></div></div>
<p>Colloquially, <b>room temperature</b> is the range of air temperatures that most people prefer for indoor settings, which feel comfortable when wearing typical indoor clothing. As a medical definition, the range generally considered suitable for human occupancy is between 15 degrees <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">Celsius</a> (59 degrees <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">Fahrenheit</a>) and 25 °C (77 °F),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> though human comfort can extend beyond this range depending on humidity, air circulation and other factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In certain fields, like science and engineering, and within a particular context, room temperature can mean different agreed-on ranges. In contrast, <b>ambient temperature</b> is the actual temperature of the air in any particular place, as measured by a thermometer. It may be very different from usual room temperature, for example an unheated room in winter.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comfort_levels">Comfort levels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Room_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Comfort levels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Thermal_comfort" title="Thermal comfort">Thermal comfort</a></div>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_American_Heritage_Dictionary_of_the_English_Language" title="The American Heritage Dictionary of the English Language">The American Heritage Dictionary of the English Language</a></i> identifies room temperature as around 20 to 22 °C (68 to 72 °F),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and the Oxford English Dictionary claims that it is "conventionally taken as about 20 °C (68 °F)".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Owing to variations in humidity and likely clothing, recommendations for summer and winter may vary; a suggested typical range for summer is 23 to 25.5 °C (73 to 78 °F), with that for winter being 20 to 23.5 °C (68 to 74 °F),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-airQuality_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-airQuality-5">[5]</a></sup> although by other considerations the maximum should be below 24 °C (75 °F) – and to avoid <a href="/wiki/Sick_building_syndrome" title="Sick building syndrome">sick building syndrome</a>, below 22 °C (72 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-airQuality_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-airQuality-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some studies have suggested that thermal comfort preferences of men and women may differ significantly, with women on average preferring higher ambient temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary,_secondary_and_tertiary_sources" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable secondary sources. (August 2018)">non-primary source needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Health_Organisation" title="World Health Organisation">World Health Organisation</a>'s standard for comfortable warmth is 18 °C (64 °F) for normal, healthy adults who are appropriately dressed. For those with respiratory problems or allergies, they recommend no less than 16 °C (61 °F), and for the sick, disabled, very old or very young, a minimum of 20 °C (68 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions_in_science_and_industry">Definitions in science and industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Room_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definitions in science and industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Temperature ranges are defined as <i>room temperature</i> for certain products and processes in industry, science, and consumer goods. For instance, for the shipping and storage of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceuticals" title="Pharmaceuticals">pharmaceuticals</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Pharmacopeia" title="United States Pharmacopeia">United States Pharmacopeia</a>-National Formulary (USP-NF) defines <i>controlled room temperature</i> as between 20 to 25 °C (68 to 77 °F), with excursions between 15 to 30 °C (59 to 86 °F) allowed, provided the mean kinetic temperature does not exceed 25 °C (77 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USP_2017_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-USP_2017-10">[10]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/European_Pharmacopoeia" title="European Pharmacopoeia">European Pharmacopoeia</a> defines it as being simply 15 to 25 °C (59 to 77 °F), and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_Pharmacopeia" title="Japanese Pharmacopeia">Japanese Pharmacopeia</a> defines "ordinary temperature" as 15 to 25 °C (59 to 77 °F), with room temperature being 1 to 30 °C (34 to 86 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ECA_2017_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ECA_2017-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chow2007a_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chow2007a-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Serving_temperature_of_red_wine">Serving temperature of red wine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Room_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Serving temperature of red wine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>People traditionally serve red wine at room temperature. This practice dates from before central heating, when room temperature in wine-drinking countries was considerably lower than it is today, usually in the range between 15 °C (59 °F) and 18 °C (64 °F). The advice is therefore to serve the wine at, at most, about 18 °C (64 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
