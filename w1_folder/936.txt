<p><b>Magnesium carbonate</b>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">Mg</a><a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">CO<sub>3</sub></a> (archaic name magnesia alba), is an inorganic salt that is a white solid. Several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrated" title="Hydrated">hydrated</a> and <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">basic</a> forms of magnesium carbonate also exist as <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forms">Forms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnesium_carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Forms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The most common magnesium carbonate forms are the <a href="/wiki/Anhydrous" title="Anhydrous">anhydrous</a> salt called <i><a href="/wiki/Magnesite" title="Magnesite">magnesite</a></i> (MgCO<sub>3</sub>) and the di, tri, and pentahydrates known as <i>barringtonite</i> (MgCO<sub>3</sub>·2 H<sub>2</sub>O), <i>nesquehonite</i> (MgCO<sub>3</sub>·3 H<sub>2</sub>O), and <i>lansfordite</i> (MgCO<sub>3</sub>·5 H<sub>2</sub>O), respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ullmann_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ullmann-5">[5]</a></sup> Some basic forms such as <i><a href="/wiki/Artinite" title="Artinite">artinite</a></i> (MgCO<sub>3</sub>·Mg(OH)<sub>2</sub>·3 H<sub>2</sub>O), <i><a href="/wiki/Hydromagnesite" title="Hydromagnesite">hydromagnesite</a></i> (4 MgCO<sub>3</sub>·Mg(OH)<sub>2</sub>·4 H<sub>2</sub>O), and <i><a href="/wiki/Dypingite" title="Dypingite">dypingite</a></i> (4 MgCO<sub>3</sub>· Mg(OH)<sub>2</sub>·5 H<sub>2</sub>O) also occur as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minerals" title="Minerals">minerals</a>.
</p><p>Magnesite consists of white <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trigonal" title="Trigonal">trigonal</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystals" title="Crystals">crystals</a>. The anhydrous salt is practically <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insoluble" title="Insoluble">insoluble</a> in <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acetone" title="Acetone">acetone</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>. All forms of magnesium carbonate react in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acids" title="Acids">acids</a>. Magnesium carbonate crystallizes in the <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a> structure where in <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">Mg<sup>2+</sup></a> is surrounded by six <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> atoms. The dihydrate one has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triclinic" title="Triclinic">triclinic</a> structure, while the trihydrate has a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monoclinic" title="Monoclinic">monoclinic</a> structure.
</p><p>References to 'light' and 'heavy' magnesium carbonates actually refer to the magnesium hydroxy carbonates <a href="/wiki/Hydromagnesite" title="Hydromagnesite">hydromagnesite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dypingite" title="Dypingite">dypingite</a> (respectively).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnesium_carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Magnesium carbonate is ordinarily obtained by mining the mineral <a href="/wiki/Magnesite" title="Magnesite">magnesite</a>. Seventy percent of the world's supply is mined and prepared in China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Magnesium carbonate can be prepared in laboratory by reaction between any soluble magnesium salt and sodium bicarbonate:
</p>
<dl><dd>MgCl<sub>2</sub>(aq) + 2NaHCO<sub>3</sub>(aq) → MgCO<sub>3</sub>(s) + 2NaCl(aq) + H<sub>2</sub>O(l) + CO<sub>2</sub>(g)</dd></dl>
<p>If magnesium chloride (or sulfate) is treated with aqueous sodium carbonate, a precipitate of basic magnesium carbonate—a <a href="/wiki/Hydrate" title="Hydrate">hydrated</a> complex of magnesium carbonate and <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_hydroxide" title="Magnesium hydroxide">magnesium hydroxide</a>—rather than magnesium carbonate itself is formed:
</p>
<dl><dd>5MgCl<sub>2</sub>(aq) + 5Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>(aq) + 5H<sub>2</sub>O(l) → Mg(OH)<sub>2</sub>·3MgCO<sub>3</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O(s) + Mg(HCO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>(aq) + 10NaCl(aq)</dd></dl>
<p>High purity industrial routes include a path through <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_bicarbonate" title="Magnesium bicarbonate">magnesium bicarbonate</a>, which can be formed by combining a <a href="/wiki/Slurry" title="Slurry">slurry</a> of magnesium hydroxide and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> at high pressure and moderate temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ullmann_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-ullmann-5">[5]</a></sup> The bicarbonate is then vacuum dried, causing it to lose carbon dioxide and a molecule of water:
</p>
<dl><dd>Mg(OH)<sub>2</sub> + 2 CO<sub>2</sub> → Mg(HCO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub></dd>
<dd>Mg(HCO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub> → MgCO<sub>3</sub> + CO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnesium_carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="With_acids">With acids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnesium_carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: With acids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like many common group 2 metal carbonates, magnesium carbonate reacts with aqueous acids to release <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>MgCO<sub>3</sub> + 2 HCl → MgCl<sub>2</sub> + CO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd>
<dd>MgCO<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> → MgSO<sub>4</sub> + CO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decomposition">Decomposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnesium_carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Decomposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At high temperatures MgCO<sub>3</sub> decomposes to <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_oxide" title="Magnesium oxide">magnesium oxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. This process is important in the production of magnesium oxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ullmann_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-ullmann-5">[5]</a></sup> This process is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calcining" title="Calcining">calcining</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>MgCO<sub>3</sub> → MgO + CO<sub>2</sub> (ΔH = +118 kJ/mol)</dd></dl>
<p>The decomposition temperature is given as 350 °C (662 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
However, calcination to the oxide is generally not considered complete below 900 °C due to interfering readsorption of liberated carbon dioxide.
</p><p>The hydrates of the salts lose water at different temperatures during decomposition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> For example in the trihydrate, which molecular formula may be written as Mg(HCO<sub>3</sub>)(OH)•2(H<sub>2</sub>O), the dehydration steps occur at 157 °C and 179 °C as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>Mg(HCO<sub>3</sub>)(OH)•2(H<sub>2</sub>O) → Mg(HCO<sub>3</sub>)(OH)•(H<sub>2</sub>O) + H<sub>2</sub>O at 157 °C</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>Mg(HCO<sub>3</sub>)(OH)•(H<sub>2</sub>O) → Mg(HCO<sub>3</sub>)(OH) + H<sub>2</sub>O at 179 °C</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnesium_carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The primary use of magnesium carbonate is the production of <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_oxide" title="Magnesium oxide">magnesium oxide</a> by calcining. Magnesite and <a href="/wiki/Dolomite" title="Dolomite">dolomite</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minerals" title="Minerals">minerals</a> are used to produce refractory bricks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ullmann_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-ullmann-5">[5]</a></sup>  MgCO<sub>3</sub> is also used in flooring, fireproofing, fire extinguishing compositions, cosmetics, dusting powder, and toothpaste.  Other applications are as filler material, smoke suppressant in plastics, a reinforcing agent in <a href="/wiki/Neoprene" title="Neoprene">neoprene</a> rubber, a drying agent, a laxative to loosen the bowels, and color retention in foods. In addition, high purity magnesium carbonate is used as <a href="/wiki/Antacid" title="Antacid">antacid</a> and as an additive in table salt to keep it free flowing. Magnesium carbonate can do this because it doesn't dissolve in water, only acid, where it will effervesce (bubble).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Because of its low solubility in water and <a href="/wiki/Hygroscopy" title="Hygroscopy">hygroscopic</a> properties, MgCO<sub>3</sub> was first added to salt in 1911 to make it flow more freely. The <a href="/wiki/Morton_Salt" title="Morton Salt">Morton Salt</a> company adopted the slogan "When it rains it pours" with reference to the fact that its MgCO<sub>3</sub>-containing salt would not stick together in humid weather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Magnesium carbonate, most often referred to as 'chalk', is also used as a drying agent on athletes' palms in <a href="/wiki/Rock_climbing" title="Rock climbing">rock climbing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gymnastics" title="Gymnastics">gymnastics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Weight_training" title="Weight training">weight lifting</a>.
</p><p>As a food additive magnesium carbonate is known as E504, for which the only known side effect is that it may work as a <a href="/wiki/Laxative" title="Laxative">laxative</a> in high concentrations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Magnesium carbonate is also used in taxidermy for whitening skulls. It can be mixed with hydrogen peroxide to create a paste, which is then spread on the skull to give it a white finish.
</p><p>As a matte white coating for projection screens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
