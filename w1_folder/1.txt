<p>A <b>carbon footprint</b> is historically defined as the total emissions caused by an individual, event, organisation, or product, expressed as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_equivalent" title="Carbon dioxide equivalent">carbon dioxide equivalent</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In most cases, the total carbon footprint cannot be exactly calculated because of inadequate knowledge of and data about the complex interactions between contributing processes, especially which including the influence on natural processes storing or releasing carbon dioxide. For this reason, Wright, Kemp, and Williams, have suggested to define the carbon footprint as:
</p>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>A measure of the total amount of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> (CH<sub>4</sub>) emissions of a defined population, system or activity, considering all relevant sources, sinks and storage within the spatial and temporal boundary of the population, system or activity of interest. Calculated as carbon dioxide equivalent using the relevant 100-year <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_potential" title="Global warming potential">global warming potential</a> (GWP100).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p>Greenhouse gases (GHGs) can be emitted through land clearance and the production and consumption of food, fuels, manufactured goods, materials, wood, roads, buildings, transportation and other services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> For simplicity of reporting, it is often expressed in terms of the amount of carbon dioxide, or its equivalent of other GHGs, emitted.
</p><p>Most of the carbon footprint emissions for the average U.S. household come from "indirect" sources, e.g. fuel burned to produce goods far away from the final consumer. These are distinguished from emissions which come from burning fuel directly in one's car or stove, commonly referred to as "direct" sources of the consumer's carbon footprint.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The concept name of the carbon footprint originates from <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a>, discussion,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> which was developed by Rees and Wackernagel in the 1990s which estimates the number of "earths" that would theoretically be required if everyone on the planet consumed resources at the same level as the person calculating their ecological footprint. However, given that ecological footprints are a measure of failure, Anindita Mitra (CREA, Seattle) chose the more easily calculated "carbon footprint" to easily measure use of carbon, as an indicator of unsustainable energy use. In 2007, carbon footprint was used as a measure of carbon emissions to develop the energy plan for City of Lynnwood, Washington. Carbon footprints are much more specific than ecological footprints since they measure direct emissions of gases that cause climate change into the atmosphere.
</p><p>Carbon footprint is one of a family of footprint indicators, which also includes <a href="/wiki/Water_footprint" title="Water footprint">water footprint</a> and <a href="/wiki/Land_footprint" title="Land footprint">land footprint</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measuring_carbon_footprints">Measuring carbon footprints</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Measuring carbon footprints">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An individual's, nation's, or organization's carbon footprint can be measured by undertaking a GHG emissions assessment or other calculative activities denoted as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_accounting" title="Carbon accounting">carbon accounting</a>. Once the size of a carbon footprint is known, a strategy can be devised to reduce it, e.g. by technological developments, better process and product management, changed Green Public or Private Procurement (GPP), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_capture" title="Carbon capture">carbon capture</a>, consumption strategies, carbon offsetting and others.
</p><p>Several free online carbon footprint calculators exist,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> including a few supported by publicly available peer-reviewed data and calculations including the University of California, Berkeley's CoolClimate Network research consortium and CarbonStory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> These websites ask you to answer more or less detailed questions about your diet, transportation choices, home size, shopping and recreational activities, usage of electricity, heating, and heavy appliances such as dryers and refrigerators, and so on. The website then estimates your carbon footprint based on your answers to these questions. A systematic literature review was conducted to objectively determine the best way to calculate individual/household carbon footprints. This review identified 13 calculation principles and subsequently used the same principles to evaluate the 15 most popular online carbon footprint calculators. A recent study’s results by Carnegie Mellon's Christopher Weber found that the calculation of carbon footprints for products is often filled with large uncertainties. The variables of owning electronic goods such as the production, shipment, and previous technology used to make that product, can make it difficult to create an accurate carbon footprint. It is important to question, and address the accuracy of Carbon Footprint techniques, especially due to its overwhelming popularity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Carbon Footprints can be reduced through the development of alternative projects, such as solar and wind energy, which are environment friendly, renewable resources, or reforestation, the restocking of existing forests or woodlands that have previously been depleted. These examples are known as Carbon Offsetting, the counteracting of carbon dioxide emissions with an equivalent reduction of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The main influences on carbon footprints include population, economic output, and energy and carbon intensity of the economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown,_Marilyn_A._2008_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown,_Marilyn_A._2008-13">[13]</a></sup> These factors are the main targets of individuals and businesses in order to decrease carbon footprints. Production creates a large carbon footprint, scholars suggest that decreasing the amount of energy needed for production would be one of the most effective ways to decrease a carbon footprint. This is due to the fact that Electricity is responsible for roughly 37% of Carbon Dioxide emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Coal production has been refined to greatly reduce carbon emissions; since the 1980s, the amount of energy used to produce a ton of steel has decreased by 50%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Average_carbon_footprint_per_person_by_country">Average carbon footprint per person by country</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Average carbon footprint per person by country">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The global average carbon footprint in 2007 was around 5.7 tons CO<sub>2</sub>e/cap. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EU" title="EU">EU</a> average for this time was about 13.8 tons CO<sub>2</sub>e/cap, whereas for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S." title="U.S.">U.S.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> it was over 25 tons CO<sub>2</sub>e/cap. The footprints per capita of countries in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> were well below average. To set this numbers into context, assuming a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_population" title="Global population">global population</a> around 9-10 billion by 2050 a carbon footprint of about 2 - 2.5 tons CO<sub>2</sub>e per capita is needed to stay within a 2 °C target. The carbon footprint calculations are based on a consumption based approach using a Multi-Regional <a href="/wiki/Input%E2%80%93output_model" title="Input–output model">Input-Output</a> database, which accounts for all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_Gas" title="Greenhouse Gas">Greenhouse Gas</a> (GHG) emissions in the global supply chain and allocates them to the final consumer of the purchased commodities. GHG emissions related to <a href="/wiki/Land_use,_land-use_change,_and_forestry" title="Land use, land-use change, and forestry">land use cover change</a> are not included.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mobility (driving, flying &amp; small amount from public transit), shelter (electricity, heating, construction) and food are the most important consumption categories determining the carbon footprint of a person. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EU" title="EU">EU</a>, the carbon footprint of mobility is evenly split between direct emissions (e.g. from driving private cars) and emissions embodied in purchased products related to mobility (air transport service, emissions occurring during the production of cars and during the extraction of fuel).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>The carbon footprint of U.S. households is about 5 times greater than the global average. For most U.S. households the single most important action to reduce their carbon footprint is driving less or switching to a more efficient vehicle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>

</p>

</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight">Flight</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Flight">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_aviation" title="Environmental impact of aviation">Environmental impact of aviation</a></div>
<p>Some representative figures for CO<sub>2</sub> emissions are provided by LIPASTO's survey of average direct emissions (not accounting for high-altitude radiative effects) of airliners expressed as CO<sub>2</sub> and CO<sub>2</sub> equivalent per passenger kilometre:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Domestic, short distance, less than 463 km (288 mi): 257 g/km CO<sub>2</sub> or 259 g/km (14.7 oz/mile) CO<sub>2</sub>e</li>
<li>Long distance flights: 113 g/km CO<sub>2</sub> or 114 g/km (6.5 oz/mile) CO<sub>2</sub>e</li></ul>
<p>However, emissions per unit distance traveled is not necessarily the best indicator for the carbon footprint of air travel, because the distances covered are commonly longer than by other modes of travel. It is the total emissions for a trip that matters for a carbon footprint, not the merely rate of emissions. For example, a greatly more distant holiday destination may be chosen than if another mode of travel were used, because air travel makes the longer distance feasible in the limited time available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rail">Rail</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Rail">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 2005, the US company Amtrak's carbon dioxide equivalent emissions per passenger kilometre were 0.116 kg,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> about twice as high as the UK rail average (where much more of the system is electrified),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and about eight times a Finnish electric intercity train.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sea">Sea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Average carbon dioxide emissions by ferries per passenger-kilometre seem to be 0.12 kg (4.2 oz).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> However, 18-<a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">knot</a> ferries between Finland and Sweden produce 0.221 kg (7.8 oz) of CO<sub>2</sub>, with total emissions equalling a CO<sub>2</sub> equivalent of 0.223 kg (7.9 oz), while 24–27-knot ferries between Finland and Estonia produce 0.396 kg (14.0 oz) of CO<sub>2</sub> with total emissions equalling a CO<sub>2</sub> equivalent of 0.4 kg (14 oz).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Indirect_carbon_emissions:_the_carbon_footprints_of_products">Indirect carbon emissions: the carbon footprints of products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Indirect carbon emissions: the carbon footprints of products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several organizations offer footprint calculators for public and corporate use,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-6">[6]</a></sup> and several organizations have calculated carbon footprints of products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The US Environmental Protection Agency has addressed paper, plastic (candy wrappers), glass, cans, computers, carpet and tires. Australia has addressed lumber and other building materials. Academics in Australia, Korea and the US have addressed paved roads. Companies, nonprofits and academics have addressed mailing letters and packages. Carnegie Mellon University has estimated the CO<sub>2</sub> footprints of 46 large sectors of the economy in each of eight countries. Carnegie Mellon, Sweden and the Carbon Trust have addressed foods at home and in restaurants.
</p><p>The Carbon Trust has worked with UK manufacturers on foods, shirts and detergents, introducing a <a href="/wiki/Carbon_emission_label" title="Carbon emission label">CO<sub>2</sub> label</a> in March 2007. The label is intended to comply with a new British <a href="/wiki/Publicly_Available_Specification" title="Publicly Available Specification">Publicly Available Specification</a> (i.e. not a standard), PAS 2050,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> and is being actively piloted by The Carbon Trust and various industrial partners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>  As of August 2012 The Carbon Trust state they have measured 27,000 certifiable product carbon footprints.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>Evaluating the package of some products is key to figuring out the carbon footprint.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The key way to determine a carbon footprint is to look at the materials used to make the item. For example, a juice carton is made of an aseptic carton, a beer can is made of aluminum, and some water bottles either made of glass or plastic. The larger the size, the larger the footprint will be.<b></b>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food">Food</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Food">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_carbon_diet" title="Low carbon diet">Low carbon diet</a></div>
<p>In a 2014 study by Scarborough et al., the real-life diets of British people were surveyed and their dietary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GHG_footprint" title="GHG footprint">greenhouse gas footprints</a> estimated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Average dietary greenhouse-gas emissions per day (in kilograms of carbon dioxide equivalent) were:
</p>
<ul><li>7.19 for high meat-eaters</li>
<li>5.63 for medium meat-eaters</li>
<li>4.67 for low meat-eaters</li>
<li>3.91 for fish-eaters</li>
<li>3.81 for vegetarians</li>
<li>2.89 for vegans</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Textiles">Textiles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Textiles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The precise carbon footprint of different textiles varies considerably according to a wide range of factors. However, studies of textile production in Europe suggest the following carbon dioxide equivalent emissions footprints per kilo of textile at the point of purchase by a consumer:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Cotton: 8</li>
<li>Nylon: 5.43</li>
<li>PET (e.g. synthetic fleece): 5.55</li>
<li>Wool: 5.48</li></ul>
<p>Accounting for durability and energy required to wash and dry textile products, synthetic fabrics generally have a substantially lower carbon footprint than natural ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Materials">Materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The carbon footprint of materials (also known as embodied carbon) varies widely. The carbon footprint of many common materials can be found in the Inventory of Carbon &amp; Energy database,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICE_Database_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-ICE_Database-44">[44]</a></sup> the GREET databases and models,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GREET_databases_and_models_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-GREET_databases_and_models-45">[45]</a></sup> and LCA databases via openLCA Nexus<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LCA_databases_via_openLCA_Nexus_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-LCA_databases_via_openLCA_Nexus-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cement">Cement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Cement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">Cement</a> production and carbon footprint resulting from soil sealing was 8.0 Mg person<sup>−1</sup> of total per capita CO<sub>2</sub> emissions (Italy, year 2003); the balance between C loss due to soil sealing and C stocked in man-made infrastructures resulted in a net loss to the atmosphere, -0.6 Mg C ha<sup>−1</sup> y<sup>−1</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Schemes_to_reduce_carbon_emissions:_Kyoto_Protocol.2C_carbon_offsetting.2C_and_certificates"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Schemes_to_reduce_carbon_emissions:_Kyoto_Protocol,_carbon_offsetting,_and_certificates">Schemes to reduce carbon emissions: Kyoto Protocol, carbon offsetting, and certificates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Schemes to reduce carbon emissions: Kyoto Protocol, carbon offsetting, and certificates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carbon dioxide emissions into the atmosphere, and the emissions of other GHGs, are often associated with the burning of fossil fuels, like natural gas, crude oil and coal. While this is harmful to the environment, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_offset" title="Carbon offset">carbon offsets</a> can be purchased in an attempt to make up for these harmful effects.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> defines legally binding targets and timetables for cutting the GHG emissions of industrialized countries that ratified the Kyoto Protocol. Accordingly, from an economic or market perspective, one has to distinguish between a <i>mandatory market</i> and a <i>voluntary market</i>. Typical for both markets is the trade with emission certificates:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Certified_Emission_Reduction" title="Certified Emission Reduction">Certified Emission Reduction</a> (CER)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Emission_Reduction_Unit" title="Emission Reduction Unit">Emission Reduction Unit</a> (ERU)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verified_Emission_Reduction" title="Verified Emission Reduction">Verified Emission Reduction</a> (VER)</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mandatory_market_mechanisms">Mandatory market mechanisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Mandatory market mechanisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To reach the goals defined in the Kyoto Protocol, with the least economical costs, the following <a href="/wiki/Flexible_Mechanisms" title="Flexible Mechanisms">flexible mechanisms</a> were introduced for the mandatory market:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Clean_Development_Mechanism" title="Clean Development Mechanism">Clean Development Mechanism</a> (CDM)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Joint_Implementation" title="Joint Implementation">Joint Implementation</a> (JI)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Emissions_trading" title="Emissions trading">Emissions trading</a></li></ul>
<p>The CDM and JI mechanisms requirements for projects which create a supply of emission reduction instruments, while <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emissions_Trading" title="Emissions Trading">Emissions Trading</a> allows those instruments to be sold on international markets.
</p><p>- Projects which are compliant with the requirements of the CDM mechanism generate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Certified_Emissions_Reduction" title="Certified Emissions Reduction">Certified Emissions Reductions</a> (CERs).<br/>  - Projects which are compliant with the requirements of the JI mechanism generate <a href="/wiki/Emission_Reduction_Unit" title="Emission Reduction Unit">Emission Reduction Units</a> (ERUs).
</p><p>The CERs and ERUs can then be sold through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emissions_Trading" title="Emissions Trading">Emissions Trading</a>. The demand for the CERs and ERUs being traded is driven by:
</p><p>- Shortfalls in national emission reduction obligations under the Kyoto Protocol.<br/>
- Shortfalls amongst entities obligated under local emissions reduction schemes.
</p><p>Nations which have failed to deliver their Kyoto emissions reductions obligations can enter <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emissions_Trading" title="Emissions Trading">Emissions Trading</a> to purchase CERs and ERUs to cover their treaty shortfalls.  Nations and groups of nations can also create local emission reduction schemes which place mandatory carbon dioxide emission targets on entities within their national boundaries.  If the rules of a scheme allow, the obligated entities may be able to cover all or some of any reduction shortfalls by purchasing CERs and ERUs through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emissions_Trading" title="Emissions Trading">Emissions Trading</a>.  While local emissions reduction schemes have no status under the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> itself, they play a prominent role in creating the demand for CERs and ERUs, stimulating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emissions_Trading" title="Emissions Trading">Emissions Trading</a> and setting a <a href="/wiki/Market_price" title="Market price">market price</a> for emissions.
</p><p>A well-known mandatory local emissions trading scheme is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EU_Emissions_Trading_Scheme" title="EU Emissions Trading Scheme">EU Emissions Trading Scheme</a> (EU ETS).
</p><p>New changes are being made to the trading schemes. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EU_Emissions_Trading_Scheme" title="EU Emissions Trading Scheme">EU Emissions Trading Scheme</a> is set to make some new changes within the next year. The new changes will target the emissions produced by flight travel in and out of the European Union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Callick,_Rowan_2011_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Callick,_Rowan_2011-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other nations are scheduled to start participating in Emissions Trading Schemes within the next few year. These nations include China, India and the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Callick,_Rowan_2011_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-Callick,_Rowan_2011-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voluntary_market_mechanisms">Voluntary market mechanisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Voluntary market mechanisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In contrast to the strict rules set out for the mandatory market, the voluntary market provides companies with different options to acquire emissions reductions. A solution, comparable with those developed for the mandatory market, has been developed for the voluntary market, the Verified Emission Reductions (VER). This measure has the great advantage that the projects/activities are managed according to the quality standards set out for CDM/JI projects but the certificates provided are not registered by the governments of the host countries or the Executive Board of the UNO. As such, high quality VERs can be acquired at lower costs for the same project quality. However, at present VERs can not be used in the mandatory market.
</p><p>The voluntary market in North America is divided between members of the Chicago Climate Exchange and the Over The Counter (OTC) market. The <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Climate_Exchange" title="Chicago Climate Exchange">Chicago Climate Exchange</a> is a voluntary yet legally binding <a href="/wiki/Emissions_trading" title="Emissions trading">cap-and-trade emission scheme</a> whereby members commit to the capped emission reductions and must purchase allowances from other members or offset excess emissions. The OTC market does not involve a legally binding scheme and a wide array of buyers from the public and private spheres, as well as special events that want to go <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_neutral" title="Carbon neutral">carbon neutral</a>. Being carbon neutral refers to achieving net zero carbon emissions by balancing a measured amount of carbon released with an equivalent amount sequestered or offset, or buying enough carbon credits to make up the difference.
</p><p>There are project developers, wholesalers, brokers, and retailers, as well as carbon funds, in the voluntary market. Some businesses and nonprofits in the voluntary market encompass more than just one of the activities listed above. A report by Ecosystem Marketplace shows that carbon offset prices increase as it moves along the supply chain—from project developer to retailer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ecosysmarket_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-ecosysmarket-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>While some mandatory emission reduction schemes exclude forest projects, these projects flourish in the voluntary markets. A major criticism concerns the imprecise nature of GHG sequestration quantification methodologies for forestry projects. However, others note the community co-benefits that <a href="/wiki/Forestry" title="Forestry">forestry</a> projects foster.
Project types in the voluntary market range from avoided <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, afforestation/reforestation, industrial gas <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_sink" title="Carbon dioxide sink">sequestration</a>, increased <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">energy efficiency</a>, fuel switching, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">methane capture</a> from coal plants and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_agriculture#Livestock" title="Climate change and agriculture">livestock</a>, and even <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a>. Renewable Energy Certificates (RECs) sold on the voluntary market are quite controversial due to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_credit#Additionality_and_its_importance" title="Carbon credit">additionality</a> concerns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Industrial Gas projects receive criticism because such projects only apply to large industrial plants that already have high fixed costs. Siphoning off industrial gas for sequestration is considered picking the low hanging fruit; which is why credits generated from industrial gas projects are the cheapest in the voluntary market.
</p><p>The size and activity of the voluntary carbon market is difficult to measure. The most comprehensive report on the voluntary carbon markets to date was released by Ecosystem Marketplace and New Carbon Finance in July 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ecosysmarket_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-ecosysmarket-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%86ON" title="ÆON">ÆON</a> of Japan is firstly approved by Japanese authority to indicate carbon footprint on three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Private_brand" title="Private brand">private brand</a> goods in October 2009.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ways_to_reduce_carbon_footprint">Ways to reduce carbon footprint</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Ways to reduce carbon footprint">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The most common way to reduce the carbon footprint of humans is to <a href="/wiki/Waste_hierarchy" title="Waste hierarchy">Reduce, Reuse, Recycle, Refuse</a>. In manufacturing this can be done by recycling the packing materials, by selling the obsolete inventory of one industry to the industry who is looking to buy unused items at lesser price to become competitive. Nothing should be disposed off into the soil, all the ferrous materials which are prone to degrade or oxidize with time should be sold as early as possible at reduced price.
</p><p>This can also be done by using reusable items such as thermoses for daily coffee or plastic containers for water and other cold beverages rather than disposable ones. If that option isn't available, it is best to properly recycle the disposable items after use. When one household recycles at least half of their household waste, they can save 1.2 tons of carbon dioxide annually<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-environment.about.com_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-environment.about.com-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (December 2012)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>]</sup>.
</p><p>Another easy option is to drive less. By walking or biking to the destination rather than driving, not only is a person going to save money on gas, but they will be burning less fuel and releasing fewer emissions into the atmosphere. However, if walking is not an option, one can look into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carpooling" title="Carpooling">carpooling</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_transportation" title="Mass transportation">mass transportation</a> options in their area.
</p><p>Yet another option for reducing the <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> footprint of humans is to use less air conditioning and heating in the home. By adding insulation to the walls and attic of one's home, and installing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_stripping" title="Weather stripping">weather stripping</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caulking" title="Caulking">caulking</a> around doors and windows one can lower their heating costs more than 25 percent. Similarly, one can very inexpensively upgrade the "insulation" (clothing) worn by residents of the home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aquarianonline.com_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-aquarianonline.com-52">[52]</a></sup> For example, it's estimated that wearing a base layer of long underwear (top and bottom) made from a lightweight, super insulating fabric like microfleece (<i>aka</i> Polartec®, Capilene®) can conserve as much body heat as a full set of clothing, allowing a person to remain warm with the thermostat lowered by over 5 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aquarianonline.com_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-aquarianonline.com-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> These measures all help because they reduce the amount of energy needed to heat and cool the house. One can also turn down the heat while sleeping at night or away during the day, and keep temperatures moderate at all times. Setting the thermostat just 2 degrees lower in winter and higher in summer could save about 1 ton of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-environment.about.com_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-environment.about.com-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (December 2012)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Choice of diet is a major influence on a person's carbon footprint. Animal sources of protein (especially red meat), rice (typically produced in high methane-emitting paddies), foods transported long distance and/or via fuel-inefficient transport (e.g., highly perishable produce flown long distance) and heavily processed and packaged foods are among the major contributors to a high carbon diet. Scientists at the University of Chicago have estimated<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> "that the average American diet – which derives 28% of its calories from animal foods – is responsible for approximately one and a half more tonnes of greenhouse gasses – as CO<sub>2</sub> equivalents – per person, per year than a fully plant-based, or vegan, diet."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Their calculations suggest that even replacing one third of the animal protein in the average American's diet with plant protein (e.g., beans, grains) can reduce the diet's carbon footprint by half a tonne. Exchanging two thirds of the animal protein with plant protein is roughly equivalent to switching from a Toyota Camry to a Prius. Finally, throwing food out not only adds its associated carbon emissions to a person or household's footprint, it adds the emissions of transporting the wasted food to the garbage dump and the emissions of food decomposition, mostly in the form of the highly potent greenhouse gas, methane.
</p><p>The carbon handprint movement emphasizes individual forms of carbon offsetting, like using more public transportation or planting trees in deforested regions, to reduce one's carbon footprint and increase their "handprint."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>Furthermore, the <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> footprint in the <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> industry can be reduced by optimizing the <a href="/wiki/Supply_chain" title="Supply chain">supply chain</a>. A life cycle or supply chain <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> footprint  study can provide useful data which  will help the business to identify critical areas for improvement and provides a  focus. Such studies also demonstrate a company’s commitment to reducing  carbon footprint now ahead of other  competitors as well as preparing  companies for potential regulation. In addition to increased market advantage  and differentiation eco-efficiency can also help to reduce costs where <a href="/wiki/Alternative_energy" title="Alternative energy">alternative energy</a> systems are implemented.
</p><p>A July 2017 study published in <i><a href="/wiki/Environmental_Research_Letters" title="Environmental Research Letters">Environmental Research Letters</a></i> argued that the most significant way individuals could mitigate their own carbon footprint is to have fewer children, followed by living without a vehicle, forgoing air travel and adopting a plant-based diet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">
