<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Coal gasification</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Coal gasification</b> is the process of producing <a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">syngas</a>–a mixture consisting primarily of  <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> (CO), <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> (H<sub>2</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> (CH<sub>4</sub>), and water vapour (H<sub>2</sub>O)–from <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, air and/or oxygen. 
</p><p>Historically, coal was gasified using early technology to produce <a href="/wiki/Coal_gas" title="Coal gas">coal gas</a> (also known as "town gas"), which is a combustible gas traditionally used for municipal lighting and heating before the advent of industrial-scale production of natural gas. 
</p><p>In current practice, large-scale instances of coal gasification are primarily for electricity generation, such as in <a href="/wiki/Integrated_gasification_combined_cycle" title="Integrated gasification combined cycle">integrated gasification combined cycle</a> power plants, for production of chemical feedstocks, or for production of synthetic natural gas. The hydrogen obtained from coal <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">gasification</a> can be used for <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen#Applications" title="Hydrogen">various purposes</a> such as making <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>, powering a <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_economy" title="Hydrogen economy">hydrogen economy</a>, or upgrading fossil fuels. 
</p><p>Alternatively, coal-derived syngas can be converted into transportation fuels such as <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel</a> through additional treatment via the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fischer-Tropsch_process" title="Fischer-Tropsch process">Fischer-Tropsch process</a> or into <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a> which itself can be used as transportation fuel or fuel additive, or which can be converted into gasoline by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methanol_to_gasoline" title="Methanol to gasoline">methanol to gasoline</a> process. Methane from coal gasification can be converted into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LNG" title="LNG">LNG</a> for use as a fuel in the transport sector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_gasification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the past, coal was converted to make coal gas, which was piped to customers to burn for illumination, heating, and cooking. High prices of oil and natural gas are leading to increased interest in "BTU Conversion" technologies such as <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">gasification</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methanation" title="Methanation">methanation</a> and liquefaction. The <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_Fuels_Corporation" title="Synthetic Fuels Corporation">Synthetic Fuels Corporation</a> was a U.S. government-funded corporation established in 1980 to create a market for alternatives to imported fossil fuels (such as coal gasification). The corporation was discontinued in 1985.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history_of_coal_gas_production_by_carbonization">Early history of coal gas production by carbonization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_gasification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history of coal gas production by carbonization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Coal_gasification&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Latarnia_gazowa_przyMoscieTumskim.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="288" height="611" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Latarnia_gazowa_przyMoscieTumskim.jpg/220px-Latarnia_gazowa_przyMoscieTumskim.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7a/Latarnia_gazowa_przyMoscieTumskim.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Latarnia_gazowa_przyMoscieTumskim.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Gas lighting in historical center of <a href="/wiki/Wroc%C5%82aw" title="Wrocław">Wrocław</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a></div></div></div>
<p>The Flemish scientist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jan_Baptista_van_Helmont" title="Jan Baptista van Helmont">Jan Baptista van Helmont</a> used the name "gas" in his <i>Origins of Medicine</i> (<i>c</i>. 1609) to describe his discovery of a "wild spirit" which escaped from heated wood and coal, and which "differed little from the <a href="/wiki/Chaos_(cosmogony)" title="Chaos (cosmogony)">chaos</a> of the ancients". Similar experiments were carried out in 1681 by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Johann_Becker_(scientist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Johann Becker (scientist) (page does not exist)">Johann Becker</a> of <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> and in 1684 by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=John_Clayton_(scientist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="John Clayton (scientist) (page does not exist)">John Clayton</a> of <a href="/wiki/Wigan" title="Wigan">Wigan</a>, England.  The latter called it "Spirit of the Coal". <a href="/wiki/William_Murdoch" title="William Murdoch">William Murdoch</a> (later known as Murdock) discovered new ways of making, purifying and storing gas. Among others, he illuminated his house at <a href="/wiki/Redruth" title="Redruth">Redruth</a> and his cottage at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soho,_Birmingham" title="Soho, Birmingham">Soho, Birmingham</a> in 1792, the entrance to the <a href="/wiki/Manchester_(ancient_township)" title="Manchester (ancient township)">Manchester Police Commissioners</a> premises in 1797, the exterior of the factory of <a href="/wiki/Boulton_and_Watt" title="Boulton and Watt">Boulton and Watt</a> in <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a>, and a large <a href="/wiki/Cotton_mill" title="Cotton mill">cotton mill</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salford,_Greater_Manchester" title="Salford, Greater Manchester">Salford</a>, Lancashire in 1805.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-basichandbook_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-basichandbook-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Professor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jan_Pieter_Minckeleers" title="Jan Pieter Minckeleers">Jan Pieter Minckeleers</a> lit his lecture room at the <a href="/wiki/Old_University_of_Leuven" title="Old University of Leuven">University of Louvain</a> in 1783 and <a href="/wiki/Archibald_Cochrane,_9th_Earl_of_Dundonald" title="Archibald Cochrane, 9th Earl of Dundonald">Lord Dundonald</a> lit his house at <a href="/wiki/Culross" title="Culross">Culross</a>, Scotland, in 1787, the gas being carried in sealed vessels from the local tar works. In France, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philippe_le_Bon" title="Philippe le Bon">Philippe le Bon</a> patented a gas fire in 1799 and demonstrated street lighting in 1801. Other demonstrations followed in France and in the United States, but, it is generally recognized that the first commercial gas works was built by the <a href="/wiki/Gas_Light_and_Coke_Company" title="Gas Light and Coke Company">London and Westminster Gas Light and Coke Company</a> in Great Peter Street in 1812 laying wooden pipes to illuminate <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Bridge" title="Westminster Bridge">Westminster Bridge</a> with <a href="/wiki/Gas_lighting" title="Gas lighting">gas lights</a> on New Year's Eve in 1813. In 1816, <a href="/wiki/Rembrandt_Peale" title="Rembrandt Peale">Rembrandt Peale</a> and four others established the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_Light_Company_of_Baltimore" title="Gas Light Company of Baltimore">Gas Light Company of Baltimore</a>, the first manufactured gas company in America. In 1821, <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> was being used commercially in <a href="/wiki/Fredonia,_New_York" title="Fredonia, New York">Fredonia, New York</a>. The first German gas works was built in Hannover in 1825 and by 1870 there were 340 gas works in Germany making town gas from coal, wood, peat and other materials.
</p><p>Working conditions in the <a href="/wiki/Gas_Light_and_Coke_Company" title="Gas Light and Coke Company">Gas Light and Coke Company</a>'s Horseferry Road Works, London, in the 1830s were described by a French visitor, Flora Tristan, in her <i>Promenades Dans Londres</i>:
</p>
<blockquote><p>Two rows of furnaces on each side were fired up; the effect was not unlike the description of <a href="/wiki/Vulcan_(mythology)" title="Vulcan (mythology)">Vulcan</a>'s forge, except that the <a href="/wiki/Cyclops" title="Cyclops">Cyclops</a> were animated with a divine spark, whereas the dusky servants of the English furnaces were joyless, silent and benumbed.... The foreman told me that stokers were selected from among the strongest, but that nevertheless they all became consumptive after seven or eight years of toil and died of pulmonary consumption. That explained the sadness and apathy in the faces and every movement of the hapless men.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tristan_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tristan-3">[3]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>The first public piped gas supply was to 13 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_lamps" title="Gas lamps">gas lamps</a>, each with three glass globes along the length of <a href="/wiki/Pall_Mall,_London" title="Pall Mall, London">Pall Mall</a>, London in 1807. The credit for this goes to the inventor and entrepreneur <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Albert_Winsor" title="Frederick Albert Winsor">Fredrick Winsor</a> and the plumber <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Sugg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thomas Sugg (page does not exist)">Thomas Sugg</a>, who made and laid the pipes. Digging up streets to lay pipes <a href="/wiki/Easement" title="Easement">required legislation</a> and this delayed the development of street lighting and gas for domestic use. Meanwhile, <a href="/wiki/William_Murdoch" title="William Murdoch">William Murdoch</a> and his pupil <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Clegg" title="Samuel Clegg">Samuel Clegg</a> were installing gas lighting in factories and work places, encountering no such impediments.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history_of_coal_gas_production_by_gasification">Early history of coal gas production by gasification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_gasification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early history of coal gas production by gasification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span> <small><i>(August 2014)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>In the 1850s every small to medium-sized town and city had a gas plant to provide for street lighting. Subscribing customers could also have piped lines to their houses. By this era, gas lighting became accepted. Gaslight trickled down to the middle class and later came gas cookers and stoves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The 1860s were the golden age of coal gas development. Scientists like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friedrich_August_Kekul%C3%A9_von_Stradonitz" title="Friedrich August Kekulé von Stradonitz">Kekulé</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Perkin" title="William Henry Perkin">Perkin</a> cracked the secrets of organic chemistry to reveal how gas is made and its composition. From this came better gas plants and Perkin's purple dyes, such as <a href="/wiki/Mauveine" title="Mauveine">Mauveine</a>. In the 1850s, processes for making <a href="/wiki/Producer_gas" title="Producer gas">Producer gas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_gas" title="Water gas">Water gas</a> from coke were developed. Unenriched water gas may be described as Blue water gas (BWG).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mond_gas" title="Mond gas">Mond gas</a>, developed in the 1850s by <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Mond" title="Ludwig Mond">Ludwig Mond</a>, was producer gas made from coal instead of coke. It contained ammonia and coal tar and was processed to recover these valuable compounds.
</p><p>Blue water gas (BWG) burns with a non-luminous flame which makes it unsuitable for lighting purposes. Carburetted Water Gas (CWG), developed in the 1860s, is BWG enriched with gases obtained by spraying oil into a hot retort. It has a higher calorific value and burns with a luminous flame.
</p><p>The carburetted water gas process was improved by <a href="/wiki/Thaddeus_S._C._Lowe" title="Thaddeus S. C. Lowe">Thaddeus S. C. Lowe</a> in 1875. The gas oil was fixed into the BWG via thermocracking in the carburettor and superheater of the CWG generating set. CWG was the dominant technology in the USA from the 1880s until the 1950s, replacing coal gasification. CWG has a CV of 20 MJ/m³ i.e. slightly more than half that of natural gas.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_of_the_coal_gas_industry_in_the_UK">Development of the coal gas industry in the UK</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_gasification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Development of the coal gas industry in the UK">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Coal_gasification&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span> <small><i>(August 2014)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The advent of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incandescent_gas_lighting" title="Incandescent gas lighting">incandescent gas lighting</a> in factories, homes and in the streets, replacing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_lamps" title="Oil lamps">oil lamps</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Candles" title="Candles">candles</a> with steady clear light, almost matching <a href="/wiki/Daylight" title="Daylight">daylight</a> in its colour, turned night into day for many—making night <a href="/wiki/Shift_work" title="Shift work">shift work</a> possible in industries where light was all important—in <a href="/wiki/Spinning_(textiles)" title="Spinning (textiles)">spinning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Weaving" title="Weaving">weaving</a> and making up garments etc. The social significance of this change is difficult for generations brought up with lighting after dark available at the touch of a switch to appreciate. Not only was industrial production accelerated, but streets were made safe, social intercourse facilitated and reading and writing made more widespread. Gas works were built in almost every town, main streets were brightly illuminated and gas was piped in the streets to the majority of urban households. The invention of the <a href="/wiki/Gas_meter" title="Gas meter">gas meter</a> and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pre-payment_meter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pre-payment meter (page does not exist)">pre-payment meter</a> in the late 1880s played an important role in selling town gas to domestic and commercial customers.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gas_cooker_1934_arp.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1413" data-file-width="1252" height="248" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Gas_cooker_1934_arp.jpg/220px-Gas_cooker_1934_arp.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Gas_cooker_1934_arp.jpg/330px-Gas_cooker_1934_arp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Gas_cooker_1934_arp.jpg/440px-Gas_cooker_1934_arp.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gas_cooker_1934_arp.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1934 gas cooker in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a></div></div></div>
<p>The education and training of the large workforce, the attempts to standardise manufacturing and commercial practices and the moderating of commercial rivalry between supply companies prompted the founding of associations of gas managers, first in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> in 1861. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Association_of_Gas_Managers" title="British Association of Gas Managers">British Association of Gas Managers</a> was formed in 1863 in <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> and this, after a turbulent history, became the foundation of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Institute_of_Gas_Engineers&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Institute of Gas Engineers (page does not exist)">Institute of Gas Engineers</a> (IGE). In 1903, the reconstructed <a href="/wiki/Institution_of_Civil_Engineers" title="Institution of Civil Engineers">Institution of Civil Engineers</a> (ICE) initiated courses for students of gas manufacture in the <a href="/wiki/City_and_Guilds_of_London_Institute" title="City and Guilds of London Institute">City and Guilds of London Institute</a>. The IGE was granted the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Charter" title="Royal Charter">Royal Charter</a> in 1929. Universities were slow to respond to the needs of the industry and it was not until 1908 that the first Professorship of Coal Gas and Fuel Industries was founded at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Leeds" title="University of Leeds">University of Leeds</a>. In 1926, the <a href="/wiki/Gas_Light_and_Coke_Company" title="Gas Light and Coke Company">Gas Light and Coke Company</a> opened <i>Watson House</i> adjacent to <a href="/wiki/Nine_Elms" title="Nine Elms">Nine Elms</a> Gas Works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-everard-xx_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-everard-xx-5">[5]</a></sup> At first, this was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_laboratory" title="Scientific laboratory">scientific laboratory</a>. Later it included a centre for training <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apprentices" title="Apprentices">apprentices</a> but its major contribution to the industry was its gas appliance testing facilities, which were made available to the whole industry, including gas appliance manufacturers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-everard-xx_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-everard-xx-5">[5]</a></sup> Using this facility, the industry established not only safety but also performance standards for both the manufacture of gas appliances and their servicing in customers' homes and commercial premises.
</p><p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, the gas industry's by-products, <a href="/wiki/Phenol" title="Phenol">phenol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toluene" title="Toluene">toluene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> and sulphurous compounds were valuable ingredients for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explosives" title="Explosives">explosives</a>. Much <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> for the gas works was shipped by sea and was vulnerable to enemy attack. The gas industry was a large employer of clerks, mainly male before the war. But the advent of the <a href="/wiki/Typewriter" title="Typewriter">typewriter</a> and the female <a href="/wiki/Copy_typist" title="Copy typist">typist</a> made another important social change that was, unlike the employment of women in war-time industry, to have long-lasting effects.
</p><p>The inter-war years were marked by the development of the continuous <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vertical_retort&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vertical retort (page does not exist)">vertical retort</a> which replaced many of the batch fed horizontal retorts. There were improvements in storage, especially the waterless <a href="/wiki/Gas_holder" title="Gas holder">gas holder</a>, and distribution with the advent of 2–4 inch steel pipes to convey gas at up to 50 psi (340 kPa) as feeder mains compared to the traditional <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron_pipe" title="Cast iron pipe">cast iron pipes</a> working at an average of 2–3 inches <a href="/wiki/Pressure#Units" title="Pressure">water gauge</a> (500–750 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">Pa</a>). <a href="/wiki/Benzole" title="Benzole">Benzole</a> as a vehicle fuel and <a href="/wiki/Coal_tar" title="Coal tar">coal tar</a> as the main feedstock for the emerging <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Organic_chemical_industry&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Organic chemical industry (page does not exist)">organic chemical industry</a> provided the gas industry with substantial revenues. <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">Petroleum</a> supplanted coal tar as the primary feedstock of the organic chemical industry after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and the loss of this market contributed to the economic problems of the gas industry after the war.
</p><p>A wide variety of appliances and uses for gas developed over the years. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_fire" title="Gas fire">Gas fires</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_cooker" title="Gas cooker">gas cookers</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Refrigerators" title="Refrigerators">refrigerators</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washing_machines" title="Washing machines">washing machines</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron_(appliance)" title="Iron (appliance)">hand irons</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fireplace_poker" title="Fireplace poker">pokers</a> for lighting coal fires, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gas-heated_bath&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gas-heated bath (page does not exist)">gas-heated baths</a>, remotely controlled clusters of <a href="/wiki/Gas_lighting" title="Gas lighting">gas lights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gas_engine" title="Gas engine">gas engines</a> of various types and, in later years, gas warm air and hot water <a href="/wiki/Central_heating" title="Central heating">central heating</a> and <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">air conditioning</a>, all of which made immense contributions to the improvement of the quality of life in cities and towns worldwide. The evolution of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_lighting" title="Electric lighting">electric lighting</a> made available from public supply extinguished the gas light, except where colour matching was practised as in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haberdashery" title="Haberdashery">haberdashery</a> shops.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Process">Process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_gasification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Coal_gasification&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lurgi_Druckvergaser.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="939" data-file-width="673" height="307" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Lurgi_Druckvergaser.svg/220px-Lurgi_Druckvergaser.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Lurgi_Druckvergaser.svg/330px-Lurgi_Druckvergaser.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Lurgi_Druckvergaser.svg/440px-Lurgi_Druckvergaser.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lurgi_Druckvergaser.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Scheme of a Lurgi gasifier</div></div></div>
<p>During gasification, the coal is blown through with <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> and steam (water vapor) while also being heated (and in some cases pressurized). If the coal is heated by external heat sources the process is called "allothermal", while "autothermal" process assumes heating of the coal via exothermal chemical reactions occurring inside the gasifier itself. It is essential that the oxidizer supplied is insufficient for complete oxidizing (combustion) of the fuel. During the reactions mentioned, oxygen and water molecules <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidize" title="Oxidize">oxidize</a> the coal and produce a gaseous mixture of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> (CO), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_vapour" title="Water vapour">water vapour</a> (H<sub>2</sub>O), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_hydrogen" title="Molecular hydrogen">molecular hydrogen</a> (H<sub>2</sub>). (Some by-products like tar, phenols, etc. are also possible end products, depending on the specific gasification technology utilized.) This process has been conducted in-situ within natural coal seams (referred to as <a href="/wiki/Underground_coal_gasification" title="Underground coal gasification">underground coal gasification</a>) and in coal refineries. The desired end product is usually syngas (i.e., a combination of H<sub>2</sub> + CO), but the produced coal gas may also be further refined to produce additional quantities of H<sub>2</sub>:
</p>
<dl><dd>3C (i.e., coal) + O<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O → H<sub>2</sub> + 3CO</dd></dl>
<p>If the refiner wants to produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkanes" title="Alkanes">alkanes</a> (i.e., hydrocarbons present in <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel fuel</a>), the coal gas is collected at this state and routed to a Fischer-Tropsch reactor. If, however, hydrogen is the desired end-product, the coal gas (primarily the CO product) undergoes the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_gas_shift_reaction" title="Water gas shift reaction">water gas shift reaction</a> where more hydrogen is produced by additional reaction with water vapor:
</p>
<dl><dd>CO + H<sub>2</sub>O → CO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Although other technologies for coal gasification currently exist, all employ, in general, the same chemical processes. For low-grade coals (i.e., "brown coals") which contain significant amounts of water, there are technologies in which no steam is required during the reaction, with coal (carbon) and oxygen being the only reactants. As well, some coal gasification technologies do not require high pressures. Some utilize pulverized coal as fuel while others work with relatively large fractions of coal. Gasification technologies also vary in the way the blowing is supplied.
</p><p><b>"Direct blowing"</b> assumes the coal and the oxidizer being supplied towards each other from the opposite sides of the reactor channel. In this case the oxidizer passes through coke and (more likely) ashes to the reaction zone where it interacts with coal. The hot gas produced then passes fresh fuel and heats it while absorbing some products of thermal destruction of the fuel, such as tars and phenols. Thus, the gas requires significant refining before being used in the Fischer-Tropsch reaction. Products of the refinement are highly toxic and require special facilities for their utilization. As a result, the plant utilizing the described technologies has to be very large to be economically efficient. One of such plants called SASOL is situated in the Republic of South Africa (RSA). It was built due to embargo applied to the country preventing it from importing oil and natural gas. RSA is rich in Bituminous coal and Anthracite and was able to arrange the use of the well known high pressure "Lurgi" gasification process developed in Germany in the first half of 20th century.
</p><p><b>"Reversed blowing"</b> (as compared to the previous type described which was invented first) assumes the coal and the oxidizer being supplied from the same side of the reactor. In this case there is no chemical interaction between coal and oxidizer before the reaction zone. The gas produced in the reaction zone passes solid products of gasification (coke and ashes), and CO<sub>2</sub> and H<sub>2</sub>O contained in the gas are additionally chemically restored to CO and H<sub>2</sub>. As compared to the "direct blowing" technology, no toxic by-products are present in the gas: those are disabled in the reaction zone. This type of gasification has been developed in the first half of 20th century, along with the "direct blowing", but the rate of gas production in it is significantly lower than that in "direct blowing" and there were no further efforts of developing the "reversed blowing" processes until 1980-s when a Soviet research facility KATEKNIIUgol' (R&amp;D Institute for developing Kansk-Achinsk coal field) began R&amp;D activities to produce the technology now known as "TERMOKOKS-S" process. The reason for reviving the interest to this type of gasification process is that it is ecologically clean and able to produce two types of useful products (simultaneously or separately): gas (either combustible or syngas) and middle-temperature coke. The former may be used as a fuel for gas boilers and diesel-generators or as syngas for producing gasoline, etc., the latter - as a technological fuel in metallurgy, as a chemical absorbent or as raw material for household fuel briquettes. Combustion of the product gas in gas boilers is ecologically cleaner than combustion of initial coal. Thus, a plant utilizing gasification technology with the "reversed blowing" is able to produce two valuable products of which one has relatively zero production cost since the latter is covered by competitive market price of the other. As the Soviet Union and its KATEKNIIUgol' ceased to exist, the technology was adopted by the individual scientists who originally developed it and is now being further researched in Russia and commercially distributed worldwide. Industrial plants utilizing it are now known to function in Ulaan-Baatar (Mongolia) and Krasnoyarsk (Russia).
</p><p>Pressurized airflow bed gasification technology created through the joint development between <a href="/wiki/Wison_Group" title="Wison Group">Wison Group</a> and Shell (Hybrid). For example: Hybrid is an advanced pulverized coal gasification technology, this technology combined with the existing advantages of Shell SCGP waste heat boiler, includes more than just a conveying system, pulverized coal pressurized gasification burner arrangement, lateral jet burner membrane type water wall, and the intermittent discharge has been fully validated in the existing SCGP plant such as mature and reliable technology, at the same time, it removed the existing process complications and  in the syngas cooler (waste pan) and [fly ash] filters which easily failed, and combined the current existing gasification technology that is widely used in synthetic gas quench process. It not only retains the original Shell SCGP waste heat boiler of coal characteristics of strong adaptability, and ability to scale up easily, but also absorb the advantages of the existing quench technology.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Underground_coal_gasification">Underground coal gasification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_gasification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Underground coal gasification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Underground_coal_gasification" title="Underground coal gasification">Underground coal gasification</a></div>
<p>Underground coal gasification is an industrial gasification process, which is carried out in non-mined coal seams using injection of a gaseous <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_agent" title="Oxidizing agent">oxidizing agent</a>, usually oxygen or air, and bringing the resulting product gas to surface through production wells drilled from the surface. The product gas could to be used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical" title="Chemical">chemical</a> feedstock or as <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_generation" title="Power generation">power generation</a>. The technique can be applied to resources that are otherwise not economical to extract and also offers an alternative to conventional <a href="/wiki/Coal_mining" title="Coal mining">coal mining</a> methods for some resources. Compared to traditional coal mining and gasification, UCG has less environmental and social impact, though some concerns including potential for aquifer contamination are known.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_capture_technology">Carbon capture technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_gasification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Carbon capture technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Carbon capture, utilization, and sequestration (or storage) is increasingly being utilized in modern coal gasification projects to address the greenhouse gas emissions concern associated with the use of coal and carbonaceous fuels. In this respect, gasification has a significant advantage over conventional coal combustion, in which CO<sub>2</sub> resulting from combustion is considerably diluted by nitrogen and residual oxygen in the near-ambient pressure combustion exhaust, making it relatively difficult, energy-intensive, and expensive to capture the CO<sub>2</sub> (this is known as “post-combustion” CO<sub>2</sub> capture).
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">gasification</a>, on the other hand, oxygen is normally supplied to the gasifiers and just enough fuel is combusted to provide the heat to gasify the rest; moreover, gasification is often performed at elevated pressure. The resulting syngas is typically at higher pressure and not diluted by nitrogen, allowing for much easier, efficient, and less costly removal of CO<sub>2</sub>. Gasification and integrated gasification combined cycle’s unique ability to easily remove CO<sub>2</sub> from the syngas prior to its combustion in a gas turbine (called "pre-combustion" CO<sub>2</sub> capture) or its use in fuels or chemicals synthesis is one of its significant advantages over conventional coal utilization systems.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="CO2_capture_technology_options">CO<sub>2</sub> capture technology options</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_gasification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: CO2 capture technology options">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Coal_gasification&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span> <small><i>(August 2014)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>All coal gasification-based conversion processes require removal of hydrogen sulfide (H<sub>2</sub>S; an acid gas) from the syngas as part of the overall plant configuration. Typical acid gas removal (AGR) processes employed for gasification design are either a chemical solvent system (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Amine_gas_treating" title="Amine gas treating">amine gas treating</a> systems based on MDEA, for example) or a physical solvent system (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Rectisol" title="Rectisol">Rectisol</a> or <a href="/wiki/Selexol" title="Selexol">Selexol</a>). Process selection is mostly dependent on the syngas cleanup requirement and costs. Conventional chemical/physical AGR processes using MDEA, Rectisol or Selexol are commercially proven technologies and can be designed for selective removal of CO<sub>2</sub> in addition to H<sub>2</sub>S from a syngas stream. For significant capture of CO<sub>2</sub> from a gasification plant (e.g., &gt; 80%) the CO in the syngas must first be converted to CO<sub>2</sub> and hydrogen (H<sub>2</sub>) via a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water-gas-shift" title="Water-gas-shift">water-gas-shift</a> (WGS) step upstream of the AGR plant.
</p><p>For gasification applications, or IGCC, the plant modifications required to add the ability to capture CO<sub>2</sub> are minimal. The syngas produced by the gasifiers needs to be treated through various processes for the removal of impurities already in the gas stream, so all that is required to remove CO<sub>2</sub> is to add the necessary equipment, an absorber and regenerator, to this process train. In combustion applications, modifications must be done to the exhaust stack and because of the lower concentrations of CO<sub>2</sub> present in the exhaust, much larger volumes of total gas require processing, necessitating larger and more expensive equipment.
</p>
<h4><span id="IGCC-based_projects_in_the_United_States_with_CO2_capture_and_use.2Fstorage"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="IGCC-based_projects_in_the_United_States_with_CO2_capture_and_use/storage">IGCC-based projects in the United States with CO<sub>2</sub> capture and use/storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_gasification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: IGCC-based projects in the United States with CO2 capture and use/storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Mississippi Power’s <a href="/wiki/Kemper_Project" title="Kemper Project">Kemper Project</a> is in late stages of construction. It will be a lignite-fuel IGCC plant, generating a net 524 MW of power from syngas, while capturing over 65% of CO<sub>2</sub> generated using the <a href="/wiki/Selexol" title="Selexol">Selexol</a> process. The technology at the Kemper facility, Transport-Integrated Gasification (TRIG), was developed and is licensed by KBR. The CO<sub>2</sub> will be sent by pipeline to depleted oil fields in Mississippi for <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">enhanced oil recovery</a> operations.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_Energy_California" title="Hydrogen Energy California">Hydrogen Energy California</a> (HECA) will be a 300MW net, coal and petroleum coke-fueled IGCC polygeneration plant (producing hydrogen for both power generation and fertilizer manufacture). Ninety percent of the CO<sub>2</sub> produced will be captured (using <a href="/wiki/Rectisol" title="Rectisol">Rectisol</a>) and transported to Elk Hills Oil Field for EOR, enabling recovery of 5 million additional barrels of domestic oil per year.
</p><p>Summit’s <a href="/wiki/Texas_Clean_Energy_Project" title="Texas Clean Energy Project">Texas Clean Energy Project</a> (TCEP) will be a coal-fueled, IGCC-based 400MW power/polygeneration project (also producing urea fertilizer), which will capture 90% of its CO<sub>2</sub> in pre-combustion capture using the <a href="/wiki/Rectisol" title="Rectisol">Rectisol</a> process. The CO<sub>2</sub> not used in fertilizer manufacture will be used for enhanced oil recovery in the West Texas Permian Basin.
</p><p>Plants such as the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Clean_Energy_Project" title="Texas Clean Energy Project">Texas Clean Energy Project</a> which employ <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a> have been touted as a partial, or interim, solution to climate change issues if they can be made economically viable by improved design and mass production. There was opposition by utility regulators and ratepayers due to increased cost and by some environmentalists such as <a href="/wiki/Bill_McKibben" title="Bill McKibben">Bill McKibben</a> who view any continued use of fossil fuels as counterproductive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT031513_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT031513-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="By-products">By-products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_gasification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: By-products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The by-products of coal gas manufacture included <a href="/wiki/Coke_(fuel)" title="Coke (fuel)">coke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coal_tar" title="Coal tar">coal tar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>; all useful products. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dyes" title="Dyes">Dyes</a>, medicines, including sulfa drugs, <a href="/wiki/Saccharin" title="Saccharin">saccharin</a> and many organic compounds are therefore derived from coal gas.
</p><p>Coke is used as a smokeless fuel and for the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Water_gas" title="Water gas">water gas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Producer_gas" title="Producer gas">producer gas</a>. Coal tar is subjected to <a href="/wiki/Fractional_distillation" title="Fractional distillation">fractional distillation</a> to recover various products, including
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Tar" title="Tar">tar</a>, for roads</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Benzole" title="Benzole">benzole</a>, a motor fuel</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Creosote" title="Creosote">creosote</a>, a wood preservative</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Phenol" title="Phenol">phenol</a>, used in the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastics</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cresol" title="Cresol">cresols</a>, disinfectants</li></ul>
<p>Sulfur is used in the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> and ammonia is used in the manufacture of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertiliser" title="Fertiliser">fertilisers</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercialization">Commercialization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_gasification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Commercialization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Coal_gasification_commercialization" title="Coal gasification commercialization">Coal gasification commercialization</a></div>
<p>According to the Gasification and Syngas Technologies Council, a trade association, there are globally 272 operating gasification plants with 686 gasifiers and 74 plants with 238 gasifiers under construction. Most of them use coal as feedstock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gsic_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-gsic-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of 2017 large scale expansion of the coal gasification industry was occurring only in China where local governments and energy companies promote the industry for the sake of jobs and a market for coal. The central government is aware of the conflict with environmental goals. For the most part the plants are located in remote coal rich areas. In addition to producing a great deal of carbon dioxide the plants use a great deal of water in areas where water is scarce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT2717_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT2717-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impact">Environmental impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_gasification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Environmental impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impact_of_manufactured_coal_gas_industry">Environmental impact of manufactured coal gas industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_gasification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Environmental impact of manufactured coal gas industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gasometer_in_East_London.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Gasometer_in_East_London.jpg/220px-Gasometer_in_East_London.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Gasometer_in_East_London.jpg/330px-Gasometer_in_East_London.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Gasometer_in_East_London.jpg/440px-Gasometer_in_East_London.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gasometer_in_East_London.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Gas_holder" title="Gas holder">Gasometer</a> at <a href="/wiki/West_Ham" title="West Ham">West Ham</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a></div></div></div>
<p>From its original development until the wide-scale adoption of natural gas, more than 50,000 manufactured gas plants were in existence in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> alone. The process of manufacturing gas usually produced a number of by-products that contaminated the <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> in and around the manufacturing plant, so many former town gas plants are a serious <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environmental</a> concern, and cleanup and remediation costs are often high. Manufactured gas plants (MGPs) were typically sited near or adjacent to waterways that were used to transport in coal and for the discharge of wastewater contaminated with tar, ammonia and/or drip oils, as well as outright waste tars and tar-water emulsions.
</p><p>In the earliest days of MGP operations, coal tar was considered a waste and often disposed into the environment in and around the plant locations. While uses for coal tar developed by the late-19th century, the market for tar varied and plants that could not sell tar at a given time could store tar for future use, attempt to burn it as fuel for the boilers, or dump the tar as waste. Commonly, waste tars were disposed of in old gas holders, adits or even mine shafts (if present). Over time, the waste tars degrade with <a href="/wiki/Phenols" title="Phenols">phenols</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a> (and other mono-aromatics – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BTEX" title="BTEX">BTEX</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbons" title="Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons">polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons</a> released as pollutant plumes that can escape into the surrounding environment. Other wastes included "<a href="/wiki/Blue_billy" title="Blue billy">blue billy</a>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> which is a ferroferricyanide compound—the blue colour is from <a href="/wiki/Prussian_blue" title="Prussian blue">Prussian blue</a>, which was commercially used as a <a href="/wiki/Dye" title="Dye">dye</a>. Blue billy is typically a granular material and was sometimes sold locally with the strap line "guaranteed weed free drives". The presence of blue billy can give gas works waste a characteristic musty/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bitter_almonds" title="Bitter almonds">bitter almonds</a> or <a href="/wiki/Marzipan" title="Marzipan">marzipan</a> smell which is associated with <a href="/wiki/Cyanide" title="Cyanide">cyanide</a> gas.
</p><p>The shift to the CWG process initially resulted in a reduced output of water gas tar as compared to the volume of coal tars. The advent of automobiles reduced the availability of naphtha for carburetion oil, as that fraction was desirable as motor fuel. MGPs that shifted to heavier grades of oil often experienced problems with the production of tar-water emulsions, which were difficult, time consuming, and costly to break. (The cause of tar change
water emulsions is complex and was related to several factors, including free carbon in the carburetion oil and the substitution of bituminous coal as a feedstock instead of coke.) The production of large volumes of tar-water emulsions quickly filled up available storage capacity at MGPs and plant management often dumped the emulsions in pits, from which they may or may not have been later reclaimed. Even if the emulsions were reclaimed, the environmental damage from placing tars in unlined pits remained. The dumping of emulsions (and other tarry residues such as tar sludges, tank bottoms, and off-spec tars) into the soil and waters around MGPs is a significant factor in the pollution found at FMGPs today.
</p><p>Commonly associated with former manufactured gas plants (known as "FMGPs" in <a href="/wiki/Environmental_remediation" title="Environmental remediation">environmental remediation</a>) are contaminants including:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BTEX" title="BTEX">BTEX</a>
<ul><li>Diffused out from deposits of coal/gas tars</li>
<li>Leaks of carburetting oil/light oil</li>
<li>Leaks from drip pots, that collected condensible hydrocarbons from the gas</li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Coal_tar" title="Coal tar">Coal tar</a> waste/sludge
<ul><li>Typically found in sumps of gas holders and decanting ponds.</li>
<li>Coal tar sludge has no resale value and so was always dumped.</li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound" title="Volatile organic compound">Volatile organic compounds</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbons" title="Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons">Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons</a> (PAHs)
<ul><li>Present in coal tar, gas tar, and pitch at significant concentrations.</li></ul></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_(chemistry)" title="Heavy metal (chemistry)">Heavy metals</a>
<ul><li>Leaded solder for gas mains, lead piping, coal ashes.</li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cyanide" title="Cyanide">Cyanide</a>
<ul><li>Purifier waste has large amounts of complex ferrocyanides in it.</li></ul></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lampblack" title="Lampblack">Lampblack</a>
<ul><li>Only found where crude oil was used as gasification feedstock.</li></ul></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tar_emulsion&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tar emulsion (page does not exist)">Tar emulsions</a></li></ul>
<p>Coal tar and coal tar sludges are frequently denser than water and are present in the environment as a <a href="/wiki/Dense_non-aqueous_phase_liquid" title="Dense non-aqueous phase liquid">dense non-aqueous phase liquid</a>.
</p><p>In the UK, former gasworks have commonly been developed over for residential and other uses (including the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Dome" title="Millennium Dome">Millennium Dome</a>), being seen as prime developable land in the confines of city boundaries. Situations such as these are now leading to problems associated with planning and the Contaminated Land Regime and have recently<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (June 2016)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> been debated in the House of Commons.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impact_of_modern_coal_gasification">Environmental impact of modern coal gasification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_gasification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Environmental impact of modern coal gasification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable plainlinks" role="note">This list is <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:WikiProject_Lists#Incomplete_lists" title="Wikipedia:WikiProject Lists">incomplete</a>; you can help by <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Coal_gasification&amp;action=edit">expanding it</a>.</div>
<p>Coal gasification processes require controls and pollution prevention measures to mitigate pollutant emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beychok1_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beychok1-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beychok2_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beychok2-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint noexcerpt Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (August 2014)">better source needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Pollutants or emissions of concern in the context of coal gasification include primarily:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Ash &amp; slag</li></ul>
<p>Non-slagging gasifiers produce dry ash similar to that produced by conventional coal combustion, which can be an environmental liability if the ash (typically containing heavy metals) is leachable or caustic, and if the ash must be stored in ash ponds. Slagging gasifiers, which are utilized at many of the major coal gasification applications worldwide, have considerable advantage in that ash components are fused into a glassy slag, capturing trace heavy metals in the non-leachable glassy matrix, rendering the material non-toxic. This non-hazardous slag has multiple beneficial uses such as aggregate in concrete, aggregate in asphalt for road construction, grit in abrasive blasting, roofing granules, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>)</li></ul>
<p>CO<sub>2</sub> is of paramount importance in global climate change. 
</p>
<ul><li>Mercury</li>
<li>Arsenic</li>
<li>Particulate matter (PM)</li></ul>
<p>Ash is formed in gasification from inorganic impurities in the coal. Some of these impurities react to form microscopic solids which can be suspended in the syngas produced by gasification.
</p>
<ul><li>Sulfur dioxide (SO<sub>2</sub>)</li></ul>
<p>Typically coal contains anywhere from 0.2 to 5 percent sulfur by dry weight, which converts to H<sub>2</sub>S and COS in the gasifiers due to the high temperatures and low oxygen levels. These "acid gases" are removed from the syngas produced by the gasifiers by acid gas removal equipment prior to the syngas being burned in the gas turbine to produce electricity, or prior to its use in fuels synthesis.
</p>
<ul><li>Nitrogen oxides (NO<sub>x</sub>)</li></ul>
<p>(NO<sub>x</sub>) refers to nitric oxide (NO) and nitrogen dioxide (NO<sub>2</sub>). Coal usually contains between 0.5 and 3 percent nitrogen on a dry weight basis, most of which converts to harmless nitrogen gas. Small levels of ammonia and hydrogen cyanide are produced, and must be removed during the syngas cooling process. In the case of power generation, NO<sub>x</sub> also can be formed downstream by the combustion of syngas in turbines.
</p>
