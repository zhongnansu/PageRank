<p><b>Precipitation</b> is the creation of a solid from a solution. When the reaction occurs in a liquid solution, the solid formed is called the 'precipitate'. The chemical that causes the solid to form is called the 'precipitant'. Without sufficient force of gravity (<a href="/wiki/Settling" title="Settling">settling</a>) to bring the solid particles together, the precipitate remains in <a href="/wiki/Suspension_(chemistry)" title="Suspension (chemistry)">suspension</a>.  After <a href="/wiki/Sedimentation" title="Sedimentation">sedimentation</a>, especially when using a <a href="/wiki/Laboratory_centrifuge" title="Laboratory centrifuge">centrifuge</a> to press it into a compact mass, the precipitate may be referred to as a 'pellet'. Precipitation can be used as a medium. The precipitate-free liquid remaining above the solid is called the 'supernate' or 'supernatant'.  Powders derived from precipitation have also <a href="/wiki/History_of_chemistry" title="History of chemistry">historically</a> been known as 'flowers'.  When the solid appears in the form of <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a> fibers which have been through chemical processing, the process is often referred to as <b>regeneration</b>.
</p><p>Sometimes the formation of a precipitate indicates the occurrence of a chemical reaction. If silver nitrate solution is poured into a solution of sodium chloride, a chemical reaction occurs forming a white precipitate of silver chloride. When potassium iodide solution reacts with lead(II) nitrate solution, a yellow precipitate of lead(II) iodide is formed.
</p><p>Precipitation may occur if the concentration of a compound exceeds its <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">solubility</a> (such as when mixing solvents or changing their temperature).  Precipitation may occur rapidly from a <a href="/wiki/Supersaturation" title="Supersaturation">supersaturated</a> solution.
</p><p>In solids, precipitation occurs if the concentration of one solid is above the solubility limit in the host solid, due to e.g. rapid quenching or
<a href="/wiki/Ion_implantation" title="Ion implantation">ion implantation</a>, and the temperature is high enough that diffusion can lead to segregation into precipitates. Precipitation in solids is routinely used to synthesize <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanoparticles" title="Nanoparticles">nanoclusters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>An important stage of the precipitation process is the onset of <a href="/wiki/Nucleation" title="Nucleation">nucleation</a>.  The creation of a hypothetical solid <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Particle" title="wiktionary:Particle">particle</a> includes the formation of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interface_(chemistry)" title="Interface (chemistry)">interface</a>, which requires some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_(chemistry)" title="Energy (chemistry)">energy</a> based on the relative <a href="/wiki/Surface_energy" title="Surface energy">surface energy</a> of the solid and the solution.  If this energy is not available, and no suitable nucleation surface is available, supersaturation occurs.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precipitation_(chemistry)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tetratolylporphyrin.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1249" data-file-width="1026" height="268" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Tetratolylporphyrin.jpg/220px-Tetratolylporphyrin.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Tetratolylporphyrin.jpg/330px-Tetratolylporphyrin.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Tetratolylporphyrin.jpg/440px-Tetratolylporphyrin.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tetratolylporphyrin.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Crystals of <i>meso</i>-tetratolylporphyrin from a <a href="/wiki/Reflux" title="Reflux">reflux</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propanoic_acid" title="Propanoic acid">propanoic acid</a> precipitate on cooling</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Precipitation_of_Silver_on_Copper_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3888" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Precipitation_of_Silver_on_Copper_1.jpg/220px-Precipitation_of_Silver_on_Copper_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Precipitation_of_Silver_on_Copper_1.jpg/330px-Precipitation_of_Silver_on_Copper_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Precipitation_of_Silver_on_Copper_1.jpg/440px-Precipitation_of_Silver_on_Copper_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Precipitation_of_Silver_on_Copper_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Copper from a wire <a href="/wiki/Single_displacement_reaction" title="Single displacement reaction">is displaced by</a> silver in a silver nitrate solution it is dipped into, and solid silver precipitates out.</div></div></div>
<p>Precipitation reactions can be used for making <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pigments" title="Pigments">pigments</a>, removing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salts" title="Salts">salts</a> from water in <a href="/wiki/Water_treatment" title="Water treatment">water treatment</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Qualitative_inorganic_analysis" title="Qualitative inorganic analysis">classical qualitative inorganic analysis</a>.
</p><p>Precipitation is also useful to isolate the products of a reaction during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Workup_(chemistry)" title="Workup (chemistry)">workup</a>. Ideally, the product of the reaction is insoluble in the reaction solvent. Thus, it precipitates as it is formed, preferably <a href="/wiki/Crystallization" title="Crystallization">forming pure crystals</a>. An example of this would be the synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Porphyrin" title="Porphyrin">porphyrins</a> in refluxing <a href="/wiki/Propionic_acid" title="Propionic acid">propionic acid</a>. By cooling the reaction mixture to room temperature, crystals of the porphyrin precipitate, and are collected by filtration:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:H2TPPsyn.png"><img alt="H2TPPsyn.png" data-file-height="435" data-file-width="1141" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/H2TPPsyn.png/400px-H2TPPsyn.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/H2TPPsyn.png/600px-H2TPPsyn.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/H2TPPsyn.png/800px-H2TPPsyn.png 2x" width="400"/></a></dd></dl>
<p>Precipitation may also occur when an <i>antisolvent</i> (a solvent in which the product is insoluble) is added, drastically reducing the solubility of the desired product. Thereafter, the precipitate may easily be separated by <a href="/wiki/Filtration" title="Filtration">filtration</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decanting" title="Decanting">decanting</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Centrifugation" title="Centrifugation">centrifugation</a>. An example would be the synthesis of chromic tetraphenylporphyrin chloride: water is added to the DMF reaction solution, and the product precipitates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Precipitation is also useful in purifying products: crude bmim-Cl is taken up in <a href="/wiki/Acetonitrile" title="Acetonitrile">acetonitrile</a>, and dropped into ethyl acetate, where it precipitates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Another important application of an antisolvent is in <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_precipitation" title="Ethanol precipitation">ethanol precipitation</a> of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Metallurgy" title="Metallurgy">metallurgy</a>, precipitation from a <a href="/wiki/Solid_solution" title="Solid solution">solid solution</a> is also a useful way to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_strengthening" title="Precipitation strengthening">strengthen alloys</a>; this process is known as <a href="/wiki/Solid_solution_strengthening" title="Solid solution strengthening">solid solution strengthening</a>.
</p>

<p>An example of a precipitation reaction: Aqueous <a href="/wiki/Silver_nitrate" title="Silver nitrate">silver nitrate</a> (AgNO<sub>3</sub>) is added to a solution containing <a href="/wiki/Potassium_chloride" title="Potassium chloride">potassium chloride</a> (KCl), the precipitation of a 
white solid, <a href="/wiki/Silver_chloride" title="Silver chloride">silver chloride</a> (AgCl), is observed. (Zumdahl, 2005)
</p>

<h2><span id="Anion.2Fcation_analysis"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Anion/cation_analysis">Anion/cation analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precipitation_(chemistry)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Anion/cation analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Qualitative_inorganic_analysis" title="Qualitative inorganic analysis">Qualitative inorganic analysis</a></div>
<p>Precipitate formation is useful in the detection of the type of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">cation</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salt</a>. To do this, an <a href="/wiki/Alkali" title="Alkali">alkali</a> first reacts with the unknown salt to produce a precipitate that is the <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxide</a> of the unknown salt. To identify the cation, the color of the precipitate and its solubility in excess are noted.  Similar processes are often used in sequence – for example, a <a href="/wiki/Barium_nitrate" title="Barium nitrate">barium nitrate</a> solution will react with <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfate</a> ions to form a solid <a href="/wiki/Barium_sulfate" title="Barium sulfate">barium sulfate</a> precipitate, indicating that it is likely that sulfate ions are present.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Digestion">Digestion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precipitation_(chemistry)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Digestion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Digestion, or <i>precipitate ageing</i>, happens when a freshly formed precipitate is left, usually at a higher <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>, in the solution from which it precipitates. It results in cleaner and bigger particles. The physico-chemical process underlying digestion is called <a href="/wiki/Ostwald_ripening" title="Ostwald ripening">Ostwald ripening</a>.
</p>
