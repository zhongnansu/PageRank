<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Abiotic component</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"><div class="flaggedrevs_short flaggedrevs_stable_synced plainlinks noprint nomobile" id="mw-fr-revisiontag"><div class="flaggedrevs_short_basic"><img alt="Changes must be reviewed before being displayed on this page." class="flaggedrevs-icon" src="/w/extensions/FlaggedRevs/frontend/modules/img/doc-magnify.png" title="Changes must be reviewed before being displayed on this page."/><img alt="show/hide details" class="fr-toggle-arrow" id="mw-fr-revisiontoggle" src="/w/extensions/FlaggedRevs/frontend/modules/img/arrow-down.png" style="display:none;"/></div>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Abiotic_components.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="384" data-file-width="512" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Abiotic_components.svg/220px-Abiotic_components.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Abiotic_components.svg/330px-Abiotic_components.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Abiotic_components.svg/440px-Abiotic_components.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Abiotic_components.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Abiotic factors are non living components found in an ecosystem which influence living things (biotic factors).</div></div></div>
<p>
In <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, <b>abiotic components</b> or <b>abiotic factors</b> are non-living chemical and <a href="/wiki/Physical_property" title="Physical property">physical</a> parts of the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environment</a> that affect living <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a> and the functioning of <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>. Abiotic factors and the phenomena associated with them underpin all biology. 
</p><p>Abiotic components include physical conditions and non-living <a href="/wiki/Resource_(biology)" title="Resource (biology)">resources</a> that affect living organisms in terms of <a href="/wiki/Developmental_biology" title="Developmental biology">growth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maintenance_of_an_organism" title="Maintenance of an organism">maintenance</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Reproduction" title="Reproduction">reproduction</a>.  Resources are distinguished as substances or objects in the environment required by one organism and consumed or otherwise made unavailable for use by other organisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Abiotic_Factors.tif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1219" data-file-width="1920" height="140" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Abiotic_Factors.tif/lossless-page1-220px-Abiotic_Factors.tif.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Abiotic_Factors.tif/lossless-page1-330px-Abiotic_Factors.tif.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Abiotic_Factors.tif/lossless-page1-440px-Abiotic_Factors.tif.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Abiotic_Factors.tif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>These are different settings on earth that are abiotic factors, which mean they are not living organisms, that contribute to the earth in many different ways.</div></div></div>
<p>Component degradation of a substance occurs by <a href="/wiki/Chemical_process" title="Chemical process">chemical</a> or <a href="/wiki/Physical_change" title="Physical change">physical processes</a>, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a>.  All non-living components of an ecosystem, such as atmospheric conditions and water resources, are called abiotic components.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Abiotic_component&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In biology, abiotic factors can include water, light, radiation, temperature, <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, and soil. The macroscopic climate often influences each of the above. Pressure and sound waves may also be considered in the context of marine or sub-terrestrial environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoEarth_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-EoEarth-4">[4]</a></sup> Abiotic factors in ocean environments also include aerial exposure, substrate, water clarity, solar energy and tides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-National_Geographic_Education_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-National_Geographic_Education-5">[5]</a></sup>
Consider the differences in the mechanics of <a href="/wiki/C3_carbon_fixation" title="C3 carbon fixation">C3</a>, <a href="/wiki/C4_carbon_fixation" title="C4 carbon fixation">C4</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Crassulacean_acid_metabolism" title="Crassulacean acid metabolism">CAM</a> plants in regulating the influx of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calvin-Benson_Cycle" title="Calvin-Benson Cycle">Calvin-Benson Cycle</a> in relation to their abiotic stressors. C3 plants have no mechanisms to manage <a href="/wiki/Photorespiration" title="Photorespiration">photorespiration</a>, whereas C4 and CAM plants utilize a separate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PEP_Carboxylase" title="PEP Carboxylase">PEP Carboxylase</a> enzyme to prevent photorespiration, thus increasing the yield of photosynthetic processes in certain high energy environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archea" title="Archea">Archea</a> require very high temperatures, pressures or unusual concentrations of chemical substances such as sulfur; this is due to their specialization into extreme conditions. In addition, fungi have also evolved to survive at the temperature, the humidity, and stability of their environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UWC_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-UWC-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>For example, there is a significant difference in access in both water and humidity between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate_rain_forest" title="Temperate rain forest">temperate rain forests</a> and <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">deserts</a>. This difference in water availability causes a diversity in the types of plants and animals that survive in these areas. These differences in abiotic components alter the species present both by creating boundaries of what species can survive within the environment, as well as influencing competition between two species. Abiotic factors such as salinity can give one species a competitive advantage over another, creating pressures that lead to speciation and alteration of a species to and from generalist and specialist competitors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
