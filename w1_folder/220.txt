<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Nucleophile</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Nucleophile</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_species" title="Chemical species">chemical species</a> that donates an <a href="/wiki/Electron_pair" title="Electron pair">electron pair</a> to an <a href="/wiki/Electrophile" title="Electrophile">electrophile</a> to form a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">chemical bond</a> in relation to a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">reaction</a>. All <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> with a free pair of electrons or at least one <a href="/wiki/Pi_bond" title="Pi bond">pi bond</a> can act as nucleophiles. Because nucleophiles donate electrons, they are by definition <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_base" title="Lewis base">Lewis bases</a>. 
<b>Nucleophilic</b> describes the affinity of a nucleophile to the nuclei. <b>Nucleophilicity</b>, sometimes referred to as  <b>nucleophile strength</b>, refers to a substance's nucleophilic character and is often used to compare the affinity of atoms. Neutral nucleophilic reactions with solvents such as <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohols</a> and water are named <a href="/wiki/Solvolysis" title="Solvolysis">solvolysis</a>. Nucleophiles may take part in <a href="/wiki/Nucleophilic_substitution" title="Nucleophilic substitution">nucleophilic substitution</a>, whereby a nucleophile becomes attracted to a full or partial positive charge.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucleophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The terms <i>nucleophile</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Electrophile" title="Electrophile">electrophile</a></i> were introduced by <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Kelk_Ingold" title="Christopher Kelk Ingold">Christopher Kelk Ingold</a> in 1933,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> replacing the terms <i>anionoid</i> and <i>cationoid</i> proposed earlier by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A._J._Lapworth" title="A. J. Lapworth">A. J. Lapworth</a> in 1925.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The word nucleophile is derived from <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">nucleus</a> and the Greek word <a href="/wiki/-phil-" title="-phil-">φιλος, philos</a> for love.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucleophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In general, in a row across the periodic table, the more basic the ion (the higher the pK<sub>a</sub> of the conjugate acid) the more reactive it is as a nucleophile. In a given group, <a href="/wiki/Polarizability" title="Polarizability">polarizability</a> is more important in the determination of the nucleophilicity. Thus, the easier it is to distort the electron cloud around an atom or molecule, the more readily it will react; e.g., the <a href="/wiki/Iodide" title="Iodide">iodide</a> ion (I<sup>−</sup>) is more nucleophilic than the <a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">fluoride</a> ion (F<sup>−</sup>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nucleophilicity">Nucleophilicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucleophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nucleophilicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many schemes attempting to quantify relative nucleophilic strength have been devised. The following <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empirical" title="Empirical">empirical</a> data have been obtained by measuring <a href="/wiki/Reaction_rate" title="Reaction rate">reaction rates</a> for a large number of reactions involving a large number of nucleophiles and electrophiles. Nucleophiles displaying the so-called <a href="/wiki/Alpha_effect" title="Alpha effect">alpha effect</a> are usually omitted in this type of treatment.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Swain-Scott_equation">Swain-Scott equation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucleophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Swain-Scott equation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>This <a href="/wiki/Free-energy_relationship" title="Free-energy relationship">free-energy relationship</a> relates the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pseudo_first_order_reaction" title="Pseudo first order reaction">pseudo first order</a> <a href="/wiki/Reaction_rate_constant" title="Reaction rate constant">reaction rate constant</a> (in water at 25 °C), <i>k</i>, of a reaction, normalized to the reaction rate, <i>k</i><sub>0</sub>, of a standard reaction with water as the nucleophile, to a nucleophilic constant <i>n</i> for a given nucleophile and a substrate constant <i>s</i> that depends on the sensitivity of a substrate to nucleophilic attack (defined as 1 for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methyl_bromide" title="Methyl bromide">methyl bromide</a>).
</p><p>This treatment results in the following values for typical nucleophilic anions: <a href="/wiki/Acetate" title="Acetate">acetate</a> 2.7, <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chloride</a> 3.0, <a href="/wiki/Azide" title="Azide">azide</a> 4.0, <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxide</a> 4.2, <a href="/wiki/Aniline" title="Aniline">aniline</a> 4.5, <a href="/wiki/Iodide" title="Iodide">iodide</a> 5.0, and <a href="/wiki/Thiosulfate" title="Thiosulfate">thiosulfate</a> 6.4. </p><p>The equation predicts that, in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nucleophilic_displacement" title="Nucleophilic displacement">nucleophilic displacement</a> on <a href="/wiki/Benzyl_chloride" title="Benzyl chloride">benzyl chloride</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Azide" title="Azide">azide</a> anion reacts 3000 times faster than water.
</p>

</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mayr-Patz_equation">Mayr-Patz equation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucleophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mayr-Patz equation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

In short, nucleophilicities towards sp<sub>2</sub> or sp<sub>3</sub> centers follow the same pattern.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucleophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Examples of nucleophiles are anions such as Cl<sup>−</sup>, or a compound with a <a href="/wiki/Lone_pair" title="Lone pair">lone pair</a> of electrons such as NH<sub>3</sub> (ammonia).
</p><p>In the example below, the <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> of the hydroxide ion donates an electron pair to form a new chemical bond with the <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> at the end of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkyl_halide" title="Alkyl halide">bromopropane</a> molecule. The bond between the carbon and the <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">bromine</a> then undergoes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterolytic_fission" title="Heterolytic fission">heterolytic fission</a>, with the bromine atom taking the donated electron and becoming the <a href="/wiki/Bromide" title="Bromide">bromide</a> ion (Br<sup>−</sup>), because a S<sub>N</sub>2 reaction occurs by backside attack. This means that the hydroxide ion attacks the carbon atom from the other side, exactly opposite the bromine ion. Because of this backside attack, S<sub>N</sub>2 reactions result in a reversal of the <a href="/wiki/Molecular_configuration" title="Molecular configuration">configuration</a> of the electrophile. If the electrophile is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chiral" title="Chiral">chiral</a>, it typically maintains its chirality, though the S<sub>N</sub>2 product's <a href="/wiki/Absolute_configuration" title="Absolute configuration">absolute configuration</a> is flipped as compared to that of the original electrophile.
</p>
<dl><dd><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hydrox_subst.png" title="Displacement of bromine by a hydroxide"><img alt="Displacement of bromine by a hydroxide" data-file-height="101" data-file-width="386" height="101" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/47/Hydrox_subst.png" width="386"/></a></dd></dl>
<p>An <b>ambident nucleophile</b> is one that can attack from two or more places, resulting in two or more products. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Thiocyanate" title="Thiocyanate">thiocyanate</a> ion (SCN<sup>−</sup>) may attack from either the <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">S</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">N</a>. For this reason, the <a href="/wiki/SN2_reaction" title="SN2 reaction">S<sub>N</sub>2 reaction</a> of an alkyl halide with SCN<sup>−</sup> often leads to a mixture of an alkyl thiocyanate (R-SCN) and an alkyl <a href="/wiki/Isothiocyanate" title="Isothiocyanate">isothiocyanate</a> (R-NCS). Similar considerations apply in the <a href="/wiki/Kolbe_nitrile_synthesis" title="Kolbe nitrile synthesis">Kolbe nitrile synthesis</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Halogens">Halogens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucleophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Halogens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halogens" title="Halogens">halogens</a> aren't nucleophilic in their diatomic form (i.e. I<sub>2</sub> is not a nucleophile), their anions are good nucleophiles. In polar, protic solvents, F<sup>−</sup> is the weakest nucleophile, and I<sup>−</sup> the strongest; this order is reversed in polar, aprotic solvents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon">Carbon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucleophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Carbon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Carbon nucleophiles are often <a href="/wiki/Organometallic_chemistry" title="Organometallic chemistry">organometallic reagents</a> such as those found in the <a href="/wiki/Grignard_reaction" title="Grignard reaction">Grignard reaction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blaise_reaction" title="Blaise reaction">Blaise reaction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reformatsky_reaction" title="Reformatsky reaction">Reformatsky reaction</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Barbier_reaction" title="Barbier reaction">Barbier reaction</a> or reactions involving <a href="/wiki/Organolithium_reagent" title="Organolithium reagent">organolithium reagents</a> and <a href="/wiki/Acetylide" title="Acetylide">acetylides</a>. These reagents are often used to perform <a href="/wiki/Nucleophilic_addition" title="Nucleophilic addition">nucleophilic additions</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Enol" title="Enol">Enols</a> are also carbon nucleophiles. The formation of an enol is catalyzed by <a href="/wiki/Acid_catalysis" title="Acid catalysis">acid</a> or <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">base</a>. Enols are <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ambident" title="wikt:ambident">ambident</a> nucleophiles, but, in general, nucleophilic at the <a href="/wiki/Alpha_and_beta_carbon" title="Alpha and beta carbon">alpha carbon</a> atom. Enols are commonly used in <a href="/wiki/Condensation_reaction" title="Condensation reaction">condensation reactions</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Claisen_condensation" title="Claisen condensation">Claisen condensation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Aldol_condensation" title="Aldol condensation">aldol condensation</a> reactions.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxygen">Oxygen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucleophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Oxygen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Examples of oxygen nucleophiles are <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> (H<sub>2</sub>O), <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxide</a> anion, <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohols</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alkoxide" title="Alkoxide">alkoxide</a> anions, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carboxylate" title="Carboxylate">carboxylate anions</a>.
Nucleophilic attack does not take place during intermolecular hydrogen bonding.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sulfur">Sulfur</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucleophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sulfur">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Of sulfur nucleophiles, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a> and its salts, <a href="/wiki/Thiol" title="Thiol">thiols</a> (RSH), thiolate anions (RS<sup>−</sup>), anions of thiolcarboxylic acids (RC(O)-S<sup>−</sup>), and anions of dithiocarbonates (RO-C(S)-S<sup>−</sup>) and dithiocarbamates (R<sub>2</sub>N-C(S)-S<sup>−</sup>) are used most often.
</p><p>In general, sulfur is very nucleophilic because of its large size, which makes it readily polarizable, and its lone pairs of electrons are readily accessible.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nitrogen">Nitrogen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucleophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Nitrogen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nitrogen nucleophiles include <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Azide" title="Azide">azide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrite" title="Nitrite">nitrites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydroxylamine" title="Hydroxylamine">hydroxylamine</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrazin" title="Hydrazin">hydrazin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbazide" title="Carbazide">carbazide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phenylhydrazine" title="Phenylhydrazine">phenylhydrazine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Semicarbazide" title="Semicarbazide">semicarbazide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Amide" title="Amide">amide</a>.
</p>
