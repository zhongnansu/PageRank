<p><b>Fresh water</b> (or <b>freshwater</b>) is  any naturally occurring <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> except seawater and <a href="/wiki/Brackish_water" title="Brackish water">brackish water</a>. Fresh water includes water in <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice_cap" title="Ice cap">ice caps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceberg" title="Iceberg">icebergs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pond" title="Pond">ponds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">streams</a>, and even underground water called <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a>. Fresh water is generally characterized by having low <a href="/wiki/Concentration" title="Concentration">concentrations</a> of dissolved <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Total_dissolved_solids" title="Total dissolved solids">total dissolved solids</a>. Though the term specifically excludes <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brackish_water" title="Brackish water">brackish water</a>, it does include mineral-rich waters such as <a href="/wiki/Chalybeate" title="Chalybeate">chalybeate</a> springs. 
</p><p>Fresh water is not the same as <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">potable water</a> (or drinking water). Much of the earth's fresh water (on the surface and groundwater) is unsuitable for drinking without some treatment. Fresh water can easily become <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">polluted by human activities</a> or due to naturally occurring processes, such as erosion.
</p><p>Water is critical to the survival of all living organisms. Some organisms can thrive on salt water, but the great majority of higher plants and most <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> need fresh water to live. 
</p><p><br/>
</p>

<p>Fresh water habitats are classified as either <a href="/wiki/Lake_ecosystem" title="Lake ecosystem">lentic systems</a>, which are the still-waters including <a href="/wiki/Pond" title="Pond">ponds</a>, lakes, <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">mires</a> ; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lotic" title="Lotic">lotic</a> which are running-water systems; or <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwaters</a> which flow in rocks and <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifers</a>. There is, in addition, a zone which bridges between groundwater and lotic systems, which is the <a href="/wiki/Hyporheic_zone" title="Hyporheic zone">hyporheic zone</a>, which underlies many larger rivers and can contain substantially more water than is seen in the open channel. It may also be in direct contact with the underlying underground water. 
</p><p>The majority of fresh water on Earth is in <a href="/wiki/Ice_cap" title="Ice cap">ice caps</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources">Sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fresh_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The source of almost all fresh water is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, in the form of <a href="/wiki/Mist" title="Mist">mist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a>. Fresh water falling as mist, rain or snow contains materials dissolved from the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> and material from the sea and land over which the rain bearing clouds have traveled.  In <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industrialized</a> areas rain is typically <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acidic" title="Acidic">acidic</a> because of dissolved oxides of <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> formed from burning of fossil fuels in cars, factories, trains and aircraft and from the atmospheric emissions of industry. In some cases this <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a> results in <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> of lakes and rivers.
</p><p>In coastal areas fresh water may contain significant concentrations of salts derived from the sea if windy conditions have lifted drops of seawater into the rain-bearing clouds. This can give rise to elevated concentrations of <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chloride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfate</a> as well as many other compounds in smaller concentrations.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a> areas, or areas with impoverished or dusty soils, rain-bearing winds can pick up <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dust" title="Dust">dust</a> and this can be deposited elsewhere in precipitation and causing the freshwater flow to be measurably contaminated both by insoluble solids but also by the soluble components of those soils. Significant quantities of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> may be transported in this way including the well-documented transfer of iron-rich rainfall falling in Brazil derived from sand-storms in the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">north Africa</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_distribution">Water distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fresh_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Water distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Water_distribution_on_Earth" title="Water distribution on Earth">Water distribution on Earth</a></div>
<p>Out of all the water on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, saline water in <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">seas</a> and saline <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> make up about 97% of it. Only 2.5–2.75% is fresh water, including 1.75–2% frozen in <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a>, 0.5–0.75% as fresh <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> moisture, and less than 0.01% of it as <a href="/wiki/Surface_water" title="Surface water">surface water</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamps</a> and <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Freshwater lakes contain about 87% of this fresh surface water, including 29% in the <a href="/wiki/African_Great_Lakes" title="African Great Lakes">African Great Lakes</a>, 22% in <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baikal" title="Lake Baikal">Lake Baikal</a> in Russia, 21% in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_American_Great_Lakes" title="North American Great Lakes">North American Great Lakes</a>, and 14% in other lakes. <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">Swamps</a> have most of the balance with only a small amount in rivers, most notably the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon River</a>. The atmosphere contains 0.04% water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In areas with no fresh water on the ground surface, fresh water derived from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> may, because of its lower density, overlie saline ground water in lenses or layers. Most of the world's fresh water is frozen in <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a>. Many areas suffer from lack of distribution of fresh water, such as deserts.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aquatic_organisms">Aquatic organisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fresh_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aquatic organisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_animal" title="Aquatic animal">Aquatic animal</a></div>
<p>Water is a critical issue for the survival of all living organisms. Some can use salt water but many organisms including the great majority of higher plants and most <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> must have access to fresh water to live. Some terrestrial mammals, especially desert <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a> appear to survive without drinking but they do generate water through the <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">cereal</a> seeds and they also have mechanisms to conserve water to the maximum degree.
</p><p>Fresh water creates a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypotonicity" title="Hypotonicity">hypotonic</a> environment for aquatic organisms. This is problematic for some organisms with pervious skins or with <a href="/wiki/Gill" title="Gill">gill</a> membranes, whose cell membranes may burst if excess water is not excreted. Some <a href="/wiki/Protist" title="Protist">protists</a> accomplish this using <a href="/wiki/Contractile_vacuole" title="Contractile vacuole">contractile vacuoles</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Freshwater_fish" title="Freshwater fish">freshwater fish</a> excrete excess water via the <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidney</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Although most aquatic organisms have a limited ability to regulate their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Osmotic" title="Osmotic">osmotic</a> balance and therefore can only live within a narrow range of salinity, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diadromous" title="Diadromous">diadromous</a> <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> have the ability to <a href="/wiki/Fish_migration" title="Fish migration">migrate</a> between fresh water and <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">saline</a> water bodies. During these migrations they undergo changes to adapt to the surroundings of the changed salinities; these processes are hormonally controlled. The <a href="/wiki/Eel" title="Eel">eel</a> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anguilla_anguilla" title="Anguilla anguilla">Anguilla anguilla</a></i>) uses the <a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">hormone</a> <a href="/wiki/Prolactin" title="Prolactin">prolactin</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> while in <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a> (<i>Salmo salar</i>) the hormone <a href="/wiki/Cortisol" title="Cortisol">cortisol</a> plays a key role during this process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_bird" title="Sea bird">sea birds</a> have special glands at the base of the bill through which excess salt is excreted. Similarly the <a href="/wiki/Marine_iguana" title="Marine iguana">marine iguanas</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Gal%C3%A1pagos_Islands" title="Galápagos Islands">Galápagos Islands</a> excrete excess salt through a nasal gland and they sneeze out a very salty excretion.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Freshwater_mollusc" title="Freshwater mollusc">Freshwater molluscs</a> include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater_snails" title="Freshwater snails">freshwater snails</a> and <a href="/wiki/Freshwater_bivalve" title="Freshwater bivalve">freshwater bivalves</a>. Freshwater crustaceans include <a href="/wiki/Freshwater_crab" title="Freshwater crab">freshwater crabs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crayfish" title="Crayfish">crayfish</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Problems">Problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fresh_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Limited_resource">Limited resource</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fresh_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Limited resource">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Water_scarcity" title="Water scarcity">Water scarcity</a></div>
<p>Fresh water is a renewable and variable, but finite natural resource.  Fresh water can only be replenished through the process of the <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">water cycle</a>, in which water from seas, lakes, forests, land, rivers, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reservoirs" title="Reservoirs">reservoirs</a> evaporates, forms clouds, and returns as precipitation. Locally however, if more fresh water is consumed through human activities than is naturally restored, this may result in reduced fresh water availability from surface and underground sources and can cause serious damage to surrounding and associated environments.
</p><p>Fresh and unpolluted water accounts for 0.003% of total water available globally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>The increase in the world population and the increase in per capita water use puts increasing strains on the finite resources availability of clean fresh water. The World Bank adds that the response by freshwater ecosystems to a changing climate can be described in terms of three interrelated components: water quality, water quantity or volume, and water timing. A change in one often leads to shifts in the others as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">Water pollution</a> and subsequent <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">eutrophication</a>  also reduces the availability of fresh water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many areas of the world are already experiencing stress on water availability (or water scarcity). Due to the accelerated pace of population growth and an increase in the amount of water a single person uses, it is expected that this situation will continue to get worse. A shortage of water in the future would be detrimental to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_population" title="Human population">human population</a> as it would affect everything from sanitation, to overall health and the production of grain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Minimum_streamflow">Minimum streamflow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fresh_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Minimum streamflow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An important concern for hydrological ecosystems is securing minimum <a href="/wiki/Streamflow" title="Streamflow">streamflow</a>, especially preserving and restoring <a href="/wiki/Instream_use" title="Instream use">instream water allocations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Fresh water is an important natural resource necessary for the survival of all <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>.  The use of water by humans for activities such as irrigation and industrial applications can have adverse impacts on down-stream ecosystems.  
</p><p>Fresh water withdrawal is the quantity of water removed from available sources for use in any purpose, excluding evaporation losses.
Water drawn off is not necessarily entirely consumed and some portion may be returned for further use downstream.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_pollution">Water pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fresh_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Water pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">Water pollution</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">Pollution</a> from human activity, including oil spills and also presents a problem for freshwater resources.  The largest <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> spill that has ever occurred in fresh water was caused by a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Dutch_Shell" title="Royal Dutch Shell">Royal Dutch Shell</a> tank ship in Magdalena, <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, on 15 January 1999, polluting the environment, drinkable water, plants and animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Chemical contamination of fresh water can also seriously damage eco-systems.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_uses">Human uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fresh_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Human uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">Agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fresh_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Changing landscape for the use of agriculture has a great effect on the flow of fresh water. Changes in landscape by the removal of trees and <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soils</a> changes the flow of fresh water in the local environment and also affects the cycle of fresh water. As a result, more fresh water is stored in the soil which benefits agriculture. However, since agriculture is the <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> activity that consumes the most fresh water,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gordon-17">[17]</a></sup> this can put a severe strain on local freshwater resources resulting in the destruction of local ecosystems. 
</p><p>In Australia,  over-abstraction of fresh water for intensive <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> activities has caused 33% of the land area to be at risk of salination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gordon-17">[17]</a></sup> With regards to agriculture, the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> targets food production and water management as an increasingly global issue that will foster debate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
