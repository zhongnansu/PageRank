<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Activated carbon</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Activated_Carbon.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1740" data-file-width="2419" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Activated_Carbon.jpg/220px-Activated_Carbon.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Activated_Carbon.jpg/330px-Activated_Carbon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Activated_Carbon.jpg/440px-Activated_Carbon.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Activated_Carbon.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Activated carbon</div></div></div>
<p><b>Activated carbon</b>, also called <b>activated charcoal</b>, is a form of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> processed to have small, low-volume pores that increase the <a href="/wiki/Surface_area" title="Surface area">surface area</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> available for <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a>   or <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reactions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <i>Activated</i> is sometimes substituted with <i>active</i>.
</p><p>Due to its high degree of microporosity, one gram of activated carbon has a surface area in excess of 3,000 m<sup>2</sup> (32,000 sq ft)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dillon_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dillon-4">[4]</a></sup> as determined by gas <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-surfacearea_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-surfacearea-5">[5]</a></sup> An activation level sufficient for useful application may be obtained solely from high surface area. Further chemical treatment often enhances adsorption properties.
</p><p>Activated carbon is usually derived from <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a> and is sometimes used as <a href="/wiki/Biochar" title="Biochar">biochar</a>. When derived from <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> or corn<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup> it is referred to as <b>activated coal</b>. <b>Activated coke</b> is derived from <a href="/wiki/Coke_(fuel)" title="Coke (fuel)">coke</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Activated carbon is used in <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> storage,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Air_purifier" title="Air purifier">air purification</a>, <a href="/wiki/Decaffeination" title="Decaffeination">decaffeination</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_in_pulp" title="Carbon in pulp">gold purification</a>, <a href="/wiki/Extractive_metallurgy" title="Extractive metallurgy">metal extraction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_purification" title="Water purification">water purification</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">sewage treatment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Air_filter" title="Air filter">air filters</a> in <a href="/wiki/Gas_mask" title="Gas mask">gas masks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Respirator" title="Respirator">respirators</a>, filters in compressed air, teeth whitening,  and many other applications.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_application">Industrial application</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Industrial application">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One major industrial application involves use of activated carbon in metal finishing for purification of electroplating solutions. For example, it is a main purification technique for removing organic impurities from bright nickel plating solutions. A variety of organic chemicals are added to plating solutions for improving their deposit qualities and for enhancing properties like brightness, smoothness, ductility, etc. Due to passage of direct current and electrolytic reactions of anodic oxidation and cathodic reduction, organic additives generate unwanted breakdown products in solution. Their excessive build up can adversely affect plating quality and physical properties of deposited metal. Activated carbon treatment removes such impurities and restores plating performance to the desired level.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_uses">Medical uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Medical uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Activated_charcoal_(medication)" title="Activated charcoal (medication)">Activated charcoal (medication)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ActivatedCharcoal.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2478" data-file-width="1135" height="371" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/ActivatedCharcoal.JPG/170px-ActivatedCharcoal.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/ActivatedCharcoal.JPG/255px-ActivatedCharcoal.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/ActivatedCharcoal.JPG/340px-ActivatedCharcoal.JPG 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ActivatedCharcoal.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Activated charcoal for medical use</div></div></div>
<p>Activated carbon is used to treat <a href="/wiki/Poison" title="Poison">poisonings</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Overdose" title="Overdose">overdoses</a> following oral <a href="/wiki/Ingestion" title="Ingestion">ingestion</a>. Tablets or capsules of activated carbon are used in many countries as an over-the-counter drug to treat <a href="/wiki/Diarrhea" title="Diarrhea">diarrhea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indigestion" title="Indigestion">indigestion</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Flatulence" title="Flatulence">flatulence</a>.
</p><p>However, it is ineffective for a number of poisonings including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strong_acids" title="Strong acids">strong acids</a> or <a href="/wiki/Alkali" title="Alkali">alkali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyanide" title="Cyanide">cyanide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">ethylene glycol</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AHFS_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-AHFS-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Incorrect application (e.g. into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lungs" title="Lungs">lungs</a>) results in <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_aspiration" title="Pulmonary aspiration">pulmonary aspiration</a>, which can sometimes be fatal if immediate medical treatment is not initiated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chest1989-Elliott_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chest1989-Elliott-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Analytical_chemistry_applications">Analytical chemistry applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Analytical chemistry applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Activated carbon, in 50% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/W/w" title="W/w">w/w</a> combination with <a href="/wiki/Diatomaceous_earth" title="Diatomaceous earth">celite</a>, is used as stationary phase in low-pressure <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chromatographic" title="Chromatographic">chromatographic</a> separation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbohydrates" title="Carbohydrates">carbohydrates</a> (mono-, di-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trisaccharides" title="Trisaccharides">trisaccharides</a>) using <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> solutions (5–50%) as <a href="/wiki/Chromatography" title="Chromatography">mobile phase</a> in analytical or preparative protocols.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_applications">Environmental applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Environmental applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Water_Filtration_Systems.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1163" data-file-width="738" height="347" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Water_Filtration_Systems.png/220px-Water_Filtration_Systems.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Water_Filtration_Systems.png/330px-Water_Filtration_Systems.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Water_Filtration_Systems.png/440px-Water_Filtration_Systems.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Water_Filtration_Systems.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Activated carbon is usually used in water filtration systems. In this illustration, the activated carbon is in the fourth level (counted from bottom).</div></div></div>
<p>Carbon <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a> has numerous applications in removing <a href="/wiki/Pollutant" title="Pollutant">pollutants</a> from air or water streams both in the field and in industrial processes such as:
</p>
<ul><li>Spill cleanup</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">Groundwater</a> <a href="/wiki/Environmental_remediation" title="Environmental remediation">remediation</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">Drinking water</a> <a href="/wiki/Filtration" title="Filtration">filtration</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Air_purifier" title="Air purifier">Air purification</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound" title="Volatile organic compound">Volatile organic compounds</a> capture from <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">painting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dry_cleaning" title="Dry cleaning">dry cleaning</a>, gasoline dispensing operations, and other processes.</li></ul>
<p>During early implementation of the 1974 Safe Drinking Water Act in the US, EPA officials developed a rule that proposed requiring drinking water treatment systems to use granular activated carbon. Because of its high cost, the so-called GAC rule encountered strong opposition across the country from the water supply industry, including the largest water utilities in California. Hence, the agency set aside the rule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Activated carbon filtration is an effective water treatment method due to its multi-functional nature. There are specific types of activated carbon filtration methods and equipment that are indicated – depending upon the contaminants involved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Activated carbon is also used for the measurement of radon concentration in air.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture_uses">Agriculture uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Agriculture uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Activated carbon (charcoal) is an allowed substance used by organic farmers in both <a href="/wiki/Animal_husbandry" title="Animal husbandry">livestock production</a> and wine making. In livestock production it is used as a pesticide, animal feed additive, processing aid, nonagricultural ingredient and disinfectant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>  In organic winemaking, activated carbon is allowed for use as a processing agent to adsorb brown color pigments from white grape concentrates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Distilled_alcoholic_beverage_purification">Distilled alcoholic beverage purification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Distilled alcoholic beverage purification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_County_Process" title="Lincoln County Process">Lincoln County Process</a></div>
<p>Activated carbon filters (AC filters) can be used to filter <a href="/wiki/Vodka" title="Vodka">vodka</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whiskey" title="Whiskey">whiskey</a> of <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic</a> impurities which can affect color, taste, and odor. Passing an organically impure vodka through an activated carbon filter at the proper flow rate will result in vodka with an identical alcohol content and significantly increased organic purity, as judged by odor and taste.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fuel_storage">Fuel storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Fuel storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Research is being done testing various activated carbons' ability to store <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup>and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_gas" title="Hydrogen gas">hydrogen gas</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup>.  The porous material acts like a sponge for different types of gases. The gas is attracted to the carbon material via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Van_der_Waals_forces" title="Van der Waals forces">Van der Waals forces</a>. Some carbons have been able to achieve bonding energies of 5–10 kJ per mol. The gas may then be desorbed when subjected to higher temperatures and either combusted to do work or in the case of hydrogen gas extracted for use in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fuel_cell" title="Hydrogen fuel cell">hydrogen fuel cell</a>. Gas storage in activated carbons is an appealing gas storage method because the gas can be stored in a low pressure, low mass, low volume environment that would be much more feasible than bulky on-board pressure tanks in vehicles. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">United States Department of Energy</a> has specified certain goals to be achieved in the area of research and development of nano-porous carbon materials. All of the goals are yet to be satisfied but numerous institutions, including the ALL-CRAFT program<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> are continuing to conduct work in this promising field.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_purification">Gas purification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Gas purification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Filters with activated carbon are usually used in compressed air and gas purification to remove <a href="/wiki/Oil" title="Oil">oil</a> vapors, odor, and other <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a> from the air. The most common designs use a 1-stage or 2 stage filtration principle in which activated carbon is embedded inside the filter media.
</p><p>Activated carbon is also used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spacesuit" title="Spacesuit">spacesuit</a> <a href="/wiki/Primary_Life_Support_System" title="Primary Life Support System">Primary Life Support Systems</a>.
</p><p>Activated carbon filters are used to retain radioactive gases within the air vacuumed from a nuclear boiling water reactor turbine condenser. The large charcoal beds adsorb these gases and retain them while they rapidly decay to non-radioactive solid species. The solids are trapped in the charcoal particles, while the filtered air passes through.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_purification">Chemical purification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Chemical purification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Activated carbon is commonly used on the laboratory scale to purify solutions of organic molecules containing unwanted colored organic impurities.
</p><p>Filtration over activated carbon is used in large scale fine chemical and pharmaceutical processes for the same purpose. The carbon is either mixed with the solution then filtered off or immobilized in a filter.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mercury_scrubbing">Mercury scrubbing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Mercury scrubbing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Activated carbon, often infused with sulfur<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> or iodine, is widely used to trap mercury emissions from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal-fired_power_station" title="Coal-fired power station">coal-fired power stations</a>, medical <a href="/wiki/Incineration" title="Incineration">incinerators</a>, and from <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> at the wellhead. This carbon is a special product costing more than US$4.00 per kg.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Since it is often not recycled, the mercury-laden activated carbon presents a disposal dilemma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> If the activated carbon contains less than 260 ppm mercury, United States federal regulations allow it to be stabilized (for example, trapped in concrete) for landfilling.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, waste containing greater than 260 ppm is considered to be in the high-mercury subcategory and is banned from landfilling (Land-Ban Rule).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This material is now accumulating in warehouses and in deep abandoned mines at an estimated rate of 1000 tons per year.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The problem of disposal of mercury-laden activated carbon is not unique to the United States. In the Netherlands, this mercury is largely recovered<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and the activated carbon is disposed of by complete burning.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Activated carbon is carbon produced from carbonaceous source materials such as bamboo, coconut husk, willow <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coir" title="Coir">coir</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(resin)" title="Pitch (resin)">petroleum pitch</a>.  It can be produced by one of the following processes:
</p>
<ol><li><b>Physical activation</b>: The source material is developed into activated carbons using hot gases. Air is then introduced to burn out the gasses, creating a graded, screened and de-dusted form of activated carbon. This is generally done by using one or a combination of the following processes:
<ul><li><i>Carbonization</i>: Material with carbon content is <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">pyrolyzed</a> at temperatures in the range 600–900 °C, usually in inert atmosphere with gases like <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a></li>
<li><i>Activation/Oxidation</i>: Raw material or <a href="/wiki/Carbonization" title="Carbonization">carbonized</a> material is exposed to oxidizing atmospheres (oxygen or steam) at temperatures above 250 °C, usually in the temperature range of 600–1200 °C.</li></ul></li>
<li><b>Chemical activation</b>: Prior to carbonization, the raw material is impregnated with certain chemicals.  The chemical is typically an <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acid</a>, strong <a href="/wiki/Alkali" title="Alkali">base</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup>, or a <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salt</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Phosphoric_acid" title="Phosphoric acid">phosphoric acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">potassium hydroxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcium_chloride" title="Calcium chloride">calcium chloride</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zinc_chloride" title="Zinc chloride">zinc chloride</a> 25%). Then, the raw material is carbonized at lower temperatures (450–900 °C). It is believed that the carbonization / activation step proceeds simultaneously with the chemical activation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Chemical activation is preferred over physical activation owing to the lower temperatures and shorter time needed for activating material.</li></ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Activated carbons are complex products which are difficult to classify on the basis of their behaviour, surface characteristics and other fundamental criteria. However, some broad classification is made for general purpose based on their size, preparation methods, and industrial applications.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Powdered_activated_carbon">Powdered activated carbon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Powdered activated carbon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Powdered_activated_carbon_treatment" title="Powdered activated carbon treatment">Powdered activated carbon treatment</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ActivatedCharcoalPowder_BrightField.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4254" data-file-width="6900" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/ActivatedCharcoalPowder_BrightField.jpg/280px-ActivatedCharcoalPowder_BrightField.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/ActivatedCharcoalPowder_BrightField.jpg/420px-ActivatedCharcoalPowder_BrightField.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/ActivatedCharcoalPowder_BrightField.jpg/560px-ActivatedCharcoalPowder_BrightField.jpg 2x" width="280"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ActivatedCharcoalPowder_BrightField.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Micrograph" title="Micrograph">micrograph</a> of activated charcoal (R 1) under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bright_field_microscopy" title="Bright field microscopy">bright field</a> illumination on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_microscope" title="Light microscope">light microscope</a>. Notice the <a href="/wiki/Fractal" title="Fractal">fractal</a>-like shape of the particles hinting at their enormous surface area. Each particle in this image, despite being only around 0.1 mm across, can have a surface area of several square centimeters. The entire image covers a region of approximately 1.1 by 0.7 mm, and the full resolution version is at a scale of 6.236 pixels/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%9Cm" title="Μm">μm</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Normally, activated carbons (R 1) are made in particulate form as powders or fine granules less than 1.0 mm in size with an average diameter between 0.15 and 0.25 mm. Thus they present a large surface to volume ratio with a small diffusion distance.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Activated carbon (R 1) is defined as the activated carbon particles retained on a 50-mesh sieve (0.297 mm).
</p><p>PAC material is finer material. PAC is made up of crushed or ground carbon particles, 95–100% of which will pass through a designated <a href="/wiki/Mesh_(scale)" title="Mesh (scale)">mesh sieve</a>. The <a href="/wiki/ASTM_International" title="ASTM International">ASTM</a> classifies particles passing through an 80-mesh sieve (0.177 mm) and smaller as PAC. It is not common to use PAC in a dedicated vessel, due to the high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Head_loss" title="Head loss">head loss</a> that would occur. Instead, PAC is generally added directly to other process units, such as raw water intakes, rapid mix basins, clarifiers, and gravity filters.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Granular_activated_carbon">Granular activated carbon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Granular activated carbon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Activated_Charcoal.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="2000" height="280" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Activated_Charcoal.jpg/280px-Activated_Charcoal.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Activated_Charcoal.jpg/420px-Activated_Charcoal.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Activated_Charcoal.jpg/560px-Activated_Charcoal.jpg 2x" width="280"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Activated_Charcoal.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Micrograph" title="Micrograph">micrograph</a> of activated charcoal <b>(GAC)</b> under <a href="/wiki/Scanning_electron_microscope" title="Scanning electron microscope">scanning electron microscope</a></div></div></div>
<p>Granular activated carbon (GAC) has a relatively larger particle size compared to powdered activated carbon and consequently, presents a smaller external surface. Diffusion of the adsorbate is thus an important factor. These carbons are suitable for <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a> of gases and vapors, because they diffuse rapidly. Granulated carbons are used for <a href="/wiki/Water_treatment" title="Water treatment">water treatment</a>, deodorization and separation of components of flow system and is also used in rapid mix basins. GAC can be either in granular or extruded form. GAC is designated by sizes such as 8×20, 20×40, or 8×30 for liquid phase applications and 4×6, 4×8 or 4×10 for vapor phase applications. A 20×40 carbon is made of particles that will pass through a U.S. Standard Mesh Size No. 20 sieve (0.84 mm) (generally specified as 85% passing) but be retained on a U.S. Standard Mesh Size No. 40 sieve (0.42 mm) (generally specified as 95% retained). AWWA (1992) B604 uses the 50-mesh sieve (0.297 mm) as the minimum GAC size. The most popular aqueous phase carbons are the 12×40 and 8×30 sizes because they have a good balance of size, surface area, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Head_loss" title="Head loss">head loss</a> characteristics.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extruded_activated_carbon">Extruded activated carbon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Extruded activated carbon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Extruded activated carbon (EAC) combines powdered activated carbon with a binder, which are fused together and extruded into a cylindrical shaped activated carbon block with diameters from 0.8 to 130 mm.  These are mainly used for gas phase applications because of their low pressure drop, high mechanical strength and low dust content. Also sold as CTO filter (Chlorine, Taste, Odor).
</p>
<h3><span id="Bead_activated_carbon_.28BAC.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Bead_activated_carbon_(BAC)">Bead activated carbon (BAC)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Bead activated carbon (BAC)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bead activated carbon (BAC) is made from petroleum pitch and supplied in diameters from approximately 0.35 to 0.80 mm.  Similar to EAC, it is also noted for its low pressure drop, high mechanical strength and low dust content, but with a smaller grain size. Its spherical shape makes it preferred for fluidized bed applications such as water filtration.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impregnated_carbon">Impregnated carbon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Impregnated carbon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Porous carbons containing several types of inorganic impregnate such as <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">cations</a> such as Al, Mn, Zn, Fe, Li, Ca have also been prepared for specific application in <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a> control especially in museums and galleries. Due to its antimicrobial and antiseptic properties, silver loaded activated carbon is used as an adsorbent for purification of domestic water. Drinking water can be obtained from natural water by treating the natural water with a mixture of activated carbon and <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_hydroxide" title="Aluminium hydroxide">Al(OH)<sub>3</sub></a>, a <a href="/wiki/Flocculation" title="Flocculation">flocculating agent</a>. Impregnated carbons are also used for the adsorption of Hydrogen Sulfide(<a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">H<sub>2</sub>S</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Thiol" title="Thiol">thiols</a>. Adsorption rates for H<sub>2</sub>S as high as 50% by weight have been reported.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polymer_coated_carbon">Polymer coated carbon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Polymer coated carbon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This is a process by which a porous carbon can be coated with a biocompatible <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a> to give a smooth and permeable coat without blocking the pores. The resulting carbon is useful for <a href="/wiki/Hemoperfusion" title="Hemoperfusion">hemoperfusion</a>. Hemoperfusion is a treatment technique in which large volumes of the patient's blood are passed over an adsorbent substance in order to remove toxic substances from the blood.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Woven_carbon">Woven carbon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Woven carbon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is a technology of processing technical rayon fiber into activated carbon cloth for <a href="/wiki/Carbon_filtering" title="Carbon filtering">carbon filtering</a>. Adsorption capacity of activated cloth is greater than that of activated charcoal (<a href="/wiki/BET_theory" title="BET theory">BET theory</a> surface area: 500–1500 m<sup>2</sup>/g, pore volume: 0.3–0.8 cm<sup>3</sup>/g). Thanks to the different forms of activated material, it can be used in a wide range of applications (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supercapacitors" title="Supercapacitors">supercapacitors</a>, odour-absorbers, <a href="/wiki/CBRN_defense" title="CBRN defense">CBRN defense</a> industry etc.).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A gram of activated carbon can have a surface area in excess of 500 m<sup>2</sup> (5,400 sq ft), with 3,000 m<sup>2</sup> (32,000 sq ft) being readily achievable<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dillon_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dillon-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-surfacearea_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-surfacearea-5">[5]</a></sup> Carbon <a href="/wiki/Aerogel" title="Aerogel">aerogels</a>, while more expensive, have even higher surface areas, and are used in special applications.
</p><p>Under an <a href="/wiki/Electron_microscope" title="Electron microscope">electron microscope</a>, the high surface-area structures of activated carbon are revealed. Individual particles are intensely convoluted and display various kinds of <a href="/wiki/Porosity" title="Porosity">porosity</a>; there may be many areas where flat surfaces of <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a>-like material run parallel to each other<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>, separated by only a few nanometers or so. These <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micropore" title="Micropore">micropores</a> provide superb conditions for <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a> to occur, since adsorbing material can interact with many surfaces simultaneously. Tests of adsorption behaviour are usually done with <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> gas at 77 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a> under high <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a>, but in everyday terms activated carbon is perfectly capable of producing the equivalent, by adsorption from its environment, liquid water from <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a> at 100 °C (212 °F) and a pressure of 1/10,000 of an <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">atmosphere</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/James_Dewar" title="James Dewar">James Dewar</a>, the scientist after whom the Dewar (<a href="/wiki/Vacuum_flask" title="Vacuum flask">vacuum flask</a>) is named, spent much time studying activated carbon and published a paper regarding its adsorption capacity with regard to gases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In this paper, he discovered that cooling the carbon to liquid nitrogen temperatures allowed it to adsorb significant quantities of numerous air gases, among others, that could then be recollected by simply allowing the carbon to warm again and that coconut based carbon was superior for the effect. He uses oxygen as an example, wherein the activated carbon would typically adsorb the atmospheric concentration (21%) under standard conditions, but release over 80% oxygen if the carbon was first cooled to low temperatures.
</p><p>Physically, activated carbon binds materials by <a href="/wiki/Van_der_Waals_force" title="Van der Waals force">van der Waals force</a> or <a href="/wiki/London_dispersion_force" title="London dispersion force">London dispersion force</a>.
</p><p>Activated carbon does not bind well to certain chemicals, including <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohols</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diol" title="Diol">diols</a>, strong <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">bases</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metals</a> and most  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inorganic" title="Inorganic">inorganics</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a>, and boric acid.
</p><p>Activated carbon adsorbs <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a> very well. The iodine capacity, mg/g, (<a href="/wiki/ASTM_International" title="ASTM International">ASTM</a> D28 Standard Method test) may be used as an indication of total surface area.
</p><p>Carbon monoxide is not well adsorbed by activated carbon. This should be of particular concern to those using the material in filters for respirators, fume hoods or other gas control systems as the gas is undetectable to the human senses, toxic to metabolism and neurotoxic.
</p><p>Substantial lists of the common industrial and agricultural gases adsorbed by activated carbon can be found online.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Activated carbon can be used as a substrate for the application of various chemicals to improve the adsorptive capacity for some inorganic (and problematic organic) compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a> (H<sub>2</sub>S), ammonia (NH<sub>3</sub>), formaldehyde (HCOH), <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> (Hg) and radioactive <a href="/wiki/Iodine-131" title="Iodine-131">iodine-131</a>(<sup>131</sup>I). This property is known as <a href="/wiki/Chemisorption" title="Chemisorption">chemisorption</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iodine_number">Iodine number</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Iodine number">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many carbons preferentially adsorb small molecules. <a href="/wiki/Iodine_value" title="Iodine value">Iodine number</a> is the most fundamental parameter used to characterize activated carbon performance.
It is a measure of activity level (higher number indicates higher degree of activation,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>) often reported in mg/g (typical range 500–1200 mg/g).
It is a measure of the micropore content of the activated carbon (0 to 20 <a href="/wiki/%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="Ångström">Å</a>, or up to 2 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanometer" title="Nanometer">nm</a>) by adsorption of iodine from solution.
It is equivalent to surface area of carbon between 900 and 1100 m<sup>2</sup>/g.
It is the standard measure for liquid-phase applications.
</p><p>Iodine number is defined as the milligrams of iodine <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorbed</a> by one gram of carbon when the iodine concentration in the residual filtrate is at a concentration of 0.02 normal (i.e. 0.02N). Basically, iodine number is a measure of the iodine adsorbed in the pores and, as such, is an indication of the pore volume available in the activated carbon of interest. Typically, water-treatment carbons have iodine numbers ranging from 600 to 1100. Frequently, this parameter is used to determine the degree of exhaustion of a carbon in use. However, this practice should be viewed with caution, as chemical interactions with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adsorbate" title="Adsorbate">adsorbate</a> may affect the iodine uptake, giving false results. Thus, the use of iodine number as a measure of the degree of exhaustion of a carbon bed can only be recommended if it has been shown to be free of chemical interactions with adsorbates and if an experimental correlation between iodine number and the degree of exhaustion has been determined for the particular application.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Molasses">Molasses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Molasses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some carbons are more adept at adsorbing large molecules.
<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Molasses_number&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Molasses number (page does not exist)">Molasses number</a> or molasses efficiency is a measure of the <a href="/wiki/Mesoporous_material" title="Mesoporous material">mesopore</a> content of the activated carbon (greater than 20 <a href="/wiki/%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="Ångström">Å</a>, or larger than 2 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanometer" title="Nanometer">nm</a>) by adsorption of molasses from solution.
A high molasses number indicates a high adsorption of big molecules (range 95–600).  Caramel dp (decolorizing performance) is similar to molasses number.  Molasses efficiency is reported as a percentage (range 40%–185%) and parallels molasses number (600 = 185%, 425 = 85%).
The European molasses number (range 525–110) is inversely related to the North American molasses number.
</p><p>Molasses Number is a measure of the degree of decolorization of a standard molasses solution that has been diluted and standardized against standardized activated carbon. Due to the size of color bodies, the molasses number represents the potential pore volume available for larger adsorbing species. As all of the pore volume may not be available for adsorption in a particular waste water application, and as some of the adsorbate may enter smaller pores, it is not a good measure of the worth of a particular activated carbon for a specific application. Frequently, this parameter is useful in evaluating a series of active carbons for their rates of adsorption. Given two active carbons with similar pore volumes for adsorption, the one having the higher molasses number will usually have larger feeder pores resulting in more efficient transfer of adsorbate into the adsorption space.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tannin">Tannin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Tannin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tannin" title="Tannin">Tannins</a> are a mixture of large and medium size molecules.
Carbons with a combination of <a href="/wiki/Macropore" title="Macropore">macropores</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mesoporous_material" title="Mesoporous material">mesopores</a> adsorb tannins.
The ability of a carbon to adsorb tannins is reported in parts per million concentration (range 200 ppm–362 ppm).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Methylene_blue">Methylene blue</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Methylene blue">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some carbons have a mesopore (20 <a href="/wiki/%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="Ångström">Å</a> to 50 Å, or 2 to 5 nm) structure which adsorbs medium size molecules, such as the dye <a href="/wiki/Methylene_blue" title="Methylene blue">methylene blue</a>.
Methylene blue adsorption is reported in g/100g (range 11–28 g/100g).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dechlorination">Dechlorination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Dechlorination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some carbons are evaluated based on the <a href="/wiki/Reductive_dechlorination" title="Reductive dechlorination">dechlorination</a> half-life length, which measures the chlorine-removal efficiency of activated carbon.  The dechlorination half-value length is the depth of carbon required to reduce the chlorine level of a flowing stream from 5 ppm to 3.5 ppm.  A lower half-value length indicates superior performance.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Apparent_density">Apparent density</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Apparent density">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The solid or skeletal density of activated carbons will typically range between 2000 and 2100 kg/m<sup>3</sup> (125–130 lbs./cubic foot). However, a large part of an activated carbon sample will consist of air space between particles, and the actual or apparent density will therefore be lower, typically 400 to 500 kg/m<sup>3</sup> (25–31 lbs./cubic foot).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Higher density provides greater volume activity and normally indicates better-quality activated carbon.
ASTM D 2854 -09 (2014) is used to determine the apparent density of activated carbon.
</p>
<h3><span id="Hardness.2Fabrasion_number"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Hardness/abrasion_number">Hardness/abrasion number</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Hardness/abrasion number">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It is a measure of the activated carbon’s resistance to attrition.
It is an important indicator of activated carbon to maintain its physical integrity and withstand frictional forces. There are large differences in the hardness of activated carbons, depending on the raw material and activity levels.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ash_content">Ash content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Ash content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ash_(analytical_chemistry)" title="Ash (analytical chemistry)">Ash</a> reduces the overall activity of activated carbon and reduces the efficiency of reactivation.
The metal oxides (Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) can leach out of activated carbon resulting in discoloration. Acid/water-soluble ash content is more significant than total ash content. Soluble ash content can be very important for aquarists, as ferric oxide can promote algal growths. A carbon with a low soluble ash content should be used for marine, freshwater fish and reef tanks to avoid heavy metal poisoning and excess plant/algal growth.
Standard method D 2866-2011 is used to determine the ash content of activated carbon.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_tetrachloride_activity">Carbon tetrachloride activity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Carbon tetrachloride activity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Measurement of the porosity of an activated carbon by the adsorption of saturated <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride" title="Carbon tetrachloride">carbon tetrachloride</a> vapour.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Particle_size_distribution">Particle size distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Particle size distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The finer the particle size of an activated carbon, the better the access to the surface area and the faster the rate of adsorption kinetics. In vapour phase systems this needs to be considered against pressure drop, which will affect energy cost. Careful consideration of particle size distribution can provide significant operating benefits.
However, in the case of using activated carbon for adsorption of minerals such as gold, the particle size should be in the range of 3.35–1.4 millimetres (0.132–0.055 in). Activated carbon with particle size less than 1 mm would not be suitable for elution (the stripping of mineral from an activated carbon).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modification_of_properties_and_reactivity">Modification of properties and reactivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Modification of properties and reactivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Acid-base, oxidation-reduction and specific adsorption characteristics are strongly dependent on the composition of the surface functional groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Philippe_Serp_2009_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Philippe_Serp_2009-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>The surface of conventional activated carbon is reactive, capable of oxidation by atmospheric oxygen and oxygen <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> steam,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and also <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-25">[25]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Oxidation in the liquid phase is caused by a wide range of reagents (HNO<sub>3</sub>, H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, KMnO<sub>4</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Through the formation of a large number of basic and acidic groups on the surface of oxidized carbon to sorption and other properties can differ significantly from the unmodified forms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Philippe_Serp_2009_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Philippe_Serp_2009-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Activated carbon can be nitrogenated by natural products or <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymers</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> or processing of carbon with nitrogenating <a href="/wiki/Reagent" title="Reagent">reagents</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>Activated carbon can interact with <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">bromine</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>Surface of activated carbon, like other carbon materials can be fluoralkylated by treatment with (per)fluoropolyether peroxide<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> in a liquid phase, or with wide range of fluoroorganic substances by CVD-method<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>. Such materials combine high hydrophobicity and chemical stability with electrical and thermal conductivity.
</p><p>Sulfonic acid functional groups can be attached to activated carbon to give "starbons" which can be used to selectively catalyse the esterification of fatty acids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Formation of such activated carbons from halogenated precursors gives a more effective catalyst which is thought to be a result of remaining halogens improving stability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some of the chemical properties of activated carbon have been attributed to presence of the surface active carbon <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkenes" title="Alkenes">double bond</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Potential_theory_of_Polanyi" title="Potential theory of Polanyi">Polyani adsorption theory</a> is a popular method for analyzing adsorption of various organic substances to their surface.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_of_adsorption">Examples of adsorption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Examples of adsorption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heterogeneous_catalysis">Heterogeneous catalysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Heterogeneous catalysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The most commonly encountered form of chemisorption in industry, occurs when a solid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a> interacts with a gaseous feedstock, the reactant/s.  The adsorption of reactant/s to the catalyst surface creates a chemical bond, altering the electron density around the reactant molecule and allowing it to undergo reactions that would not normally be available to it.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactivation_and_regeneration">Reactivation and regeneration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Reactivation and regeneration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Reactivation_Furnace_Feluy_Belgium.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="497" data-file-width="744" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Reactivation_Furnace_Feluy_Belgium.jpg/220px-Reactivation_Furnace_Feluy_Belgium.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Reactivation_Furnace_Feluy_Belgium.jpg/330px-Reactivation_Furnace_Feluy_Belgium.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Reactivation_Furnace_Feluy_Belgium.jpg/440px-Reactivation_Furnace_Feluy_Belgium.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Reactivation_Furnace_Feluy_Belgium.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>World's largest reactivation plant located in Feluy, Belgium.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Activated-carbon-reactivation-centre-Roeselare.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="427" data-file-width="640" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Activated-carbon-reactivation-centre-Roeselare.jpg/220px-Activated-carbon-reactivation-centre-Roeselare.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Activated-carbon-reactivation-centre-Roeselare.jpg/330px-Activated-carbon-reactivation-centre-Roeselare.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Activated-carbon-reactivation-centre-Roeselare.jpg/440px-Activated-carbon-reactivation-centre-Roeselare.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Activated-carbon-reactivation-centre-Roeselare.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Activated carbon reactivation center in Roeselare, Belgium.</div></div></div>
<p>The reactivation or the regeneration of activated carbons involves restoring the <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorptive capacity</a> of saturated activated carbon by desorbing adsorbed contaminants on the activated carbon surface.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal_reactivation">Thermal reactivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Thermal reactivation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The most common regeneration technique employed in industrial processes is thermal reactivation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The thermal regeneration process generally follows three steps:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sabio_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-sabio-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Adsorbent drying at approximately 105 °C (221 °F)</li>
<li>High temperature desorption and decomposition (500–900 °C (932–1,652 °F)) under an inert atmosphere</li>
<li>Residual organic gasification by a non-oxidising gas (steam or carbon dioxide) at elevated temperatures (800 °C (1,470 °F))</li></ul>
<p>The heat treatment stage utilises the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermic</a> nature of adsorption and results in desorption, partial <a href="/wiki/Cracking_(chemistry)" title="Cracking (chemistry)">cracking</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polymerization" title="Polymerization">polymerization</a> of the adsorbed organics. The final step aims to remove charred organic residue formed in the porous structure in the previous stage and re-expose the porous carbon structure regenerating its original surface characteristics. After treatment the adsorption column can be reused. Per adsorption-thermal regeneration cycle between 5–15 wt% of the carbon bed is burnt off resulting in a loss of adsorptive capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Thermal regeneration is a high energy process due to the high required temperatures making it both an energetically and commercially expensive process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sabio_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-sabio-53">[53]</a></sup> Plants that rely on thermal regeneration of activated carbon have to be of a certain size before it is economically viable to have regeneration facilities onsite. As a result, it is common for smaller waste treatment sites to ship their activated carbon cores to specialised facilities for regeneration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_regeneration_techniques">Other regeneration techniques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Activated_carbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Other regeneration techniques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Current concerns with the high energy/cost nature of thermal regeneration of activated carbon has encouraged research into alternative regeneration methods to reduce the environmental impact of such processes. Though several of the regeneration techniques cited have remained areas of purely academic research, some alternatives to thermal regeneration systems have been employed in industry. Current alternative regeneration methods are:
</p>
<ul><li>Chemical and solvent regeneration<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></li>
<li>Microbial regeneration<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Electrochemical_regeneration" title="Electrochemical regeneration">Electrochemical regeneration</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></li>
<li>Ultrasonic regeneration<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></li>
<li>Wet air oxidation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></li></ul>
