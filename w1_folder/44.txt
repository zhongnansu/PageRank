<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Flexible Mechanisms</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Flexible mechanisms</b>, also sometimes known as <b>Flexibility Mechanisms</b> or <b>Kyoto Mechanisms</b>), refers to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_emission_trading" title="Carbon emission trading">Emissions Trading</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Development_Mechanism" title="Clean Development Mechanism">Clean Development Mechanism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joint_Implementation" title="Joint Implementation">Joint Implementation</a>. These are mechanisms defined under the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> intended to lower the overall costs of achieving its emissions targets. These mechanisms enable Parties to achieve emission reductions or to remove carbon from the atmosphere cost-effectively in other countries. While the cost of limiting emissions varies considerably from region to region, the benefit for the atmosphere is in principle the same, wherever the action is taken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Much of the negotiations on the mechanisms has been concerned with ensuring their integrity. There was concern that the mechanisms do not confer a "right to emit" on <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change#Annex_I_countries" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">Annex 1</a> Parties or lead to exchanges of fictitious credits which would undermine the Protocol’s environmental goals. The negotiators of the Protocol and the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change#2001_–_COP_7,_Marrakech,_Morocco" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">Marrakesh Accords</a> therefore sought to design a system that fulfilled the cost-effectiveness promise of the mechanisms, while addressing concerns about environmental integrity and equity.
</p><p>To participate in the mechanisms, <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change#Annex_I_countries" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">Annex 1</a> Parties must meet the following eligibility requirements:
</p>
<ol><li>They must have ratified the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a>.</li>
<li>They must have calculated their assigned amount, as referred to in Articles 3.7 and 3.8 and Annex B of the Protocol in terms of tonnes of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_equivalent" title="Carbon dioxide equivalent">CO<sub>2</sub>-equivalent</a> emissions.</li>
<li>They must have in place a national system for estimating emissions and removals of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> within their territory.</li>
<li>They must have in place a national registry to record and track the creation and movement of <a href="/wiki/Emission_Reduction_Unit" title="Emission Reduction Unit">Emission Reduction Units</a>, <a href="/wiki/Certified_Emission_Reduction" title="Certified Emission Reduction">Certified Emission Reductions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Assigned_amount_units" title="Assigned amount units">Assigned amount units</a> and <a href="/wiki/Removal_Units" title="Removal Units">Removal Units</a> (RMU)s and must annually report such information to the secretariat.</li>
<li>They must annually report information on emissions and removals to the secretariat.</li></ol>

<h2><span id="Emissions_trading_.28ET.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Emissions_trading_(ET)">Emissions trading (ET)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flexible_Mechanisms&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Emissions trading (ET)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_emissions_trading" title="Carbon emissions trading">Carbon emissions trading</a></div>
<p>The Emissions Trading-mechanism allows parties to the Kyoto Protocol to buy 'Kyoto units'(emission permits for <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a>) from other countries to help meet their domestic emission reduction targets.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Project-based_mechanisms">Project-based mechanisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flexible_Mechanisms&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Project-based mechanisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Protocol defines two project-based mechanisms that allow Annex I countries to meet their GHG emission reduction commitments by acquiring GHG emission reductions "<a href="/wiki/Carbon_credit" title="Carbon credit">credits</a>." The credits are acquired by an Annex I country financing projects that reduce emissions in non-Annex I countries or other Annex I countries, or by purchasing credits from Annex I countries with excess credits. The project-based mechanisms are the Clean Development Mechanism (CDM) and Joint Implementation (JI).
</p><p>The project-based mechanisms allow Annex I countries with efficient, low GHG-emitting industries, and high prevailing <a href="/wiki/Environmental_standard" title="Environmental standard">environmental standards</a> to purchase carbon credits on the world market instead of reducing greenhouse gas emissions domestically. Annex I countries typically will want to acquire carbon credits as cheaply as possible,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (March 2010)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> while non-Annex I countries want to maximize the value of carbon credits generated from their domestic greenhouse gas reducing projects.
</p>
<h3><span id="Joint_Implementation_.28JI.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Joint_Implementation_(JI)">Joint Implementation (JI)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flexible_Mechanisms&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Joint Implementation (JI)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Joint_Implementation" title="Joint Implementation">Joint Implementation</a></div>
<p>Through the Joint Implementation, any Annex I country can invest in emission reduction projects (referred to as "Joint Implementation Projects") in any other Annex I country as an alternative to reducing emissions domestically.
</p>
<h3><span id="Clean_Development_Mechanism_.28CDM.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Clean_Development_Mechanism_(CDM)">Clean Development Mechanism (CDM)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flexible_Mechanisms&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Clean Development Mechanism (CDM)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Clean_Development_Mechanism" title="Clean Development Mechanism">Clean Development Mechanism</a></div>
<p>Through the CDM, countries can meet their domestic emission reduction targets by buying greenhouse gas reduction units from (projects in) non <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change#Annex_I_countries" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">Annex I</a> countries to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kyoto_protocol" title="Kyoto protocol">Kyoto protocol</a> (mostly developing countries). Non-Annex I countries have no GHG emission restrictions, but have financial incentives to develop GHG emission reduction projects to receive <a href="/wiki/Certified_Emission_Reduction" title="Certified Emission Reduction">Certified Emission Reductions</a> that can then be sold to Annex I countries, encouraging <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_market">Carbon market</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flexible_Mechanisms&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Carbon market">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kyoto provides for a 'cap and trade' system which imposes national caps on the emissions of annex I countries. On average, this cap requires countries to reduce their emissions by 5.2% below their 1990 baseline over the 2008 to 2012 period. Although these caps are national-level commitments, in practice, most countries will devolve their emissions targets to individual industrial entities, such as a power plant or paper factory. One example of a 'cap and trade' system is the '<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EU_ETS" title="EU ETS">EU ETS</a>'. Other schemes may follow suit in time.
</p><p>The ultimate buyers of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_credit" title="Carbon credit">credits</a> are often individual companies that expect emissions to exceed their quota, their assigned allocation units, AAUs or 'allowances' for short. Typically, they will purchase credits directly from another party with excess allowances, from a broker, from a JI/CDM developer, or on an exchange.
</p><p>National governments, some of whom may not have devolved responsibility for meeting Kyoto obligations to industry, and that have a net deficit of allowances, will buy credits for their own account, mainly from JI/CDM developers. These deals are occasionally done directly through a national fund or agency, as in the case of the Dutch government's ERUPT programme, or via collective funds such as the World Bank’s <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Prototype_Carbon_Fund&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Prototype Carbon Fund (page does not exist)">Prototype Carbon Fund</a> (PCF). The PCF, for example, represents a consortium of six governments and 17 major utility and energy companies on whose behalf it purchases credits.
</p><p>Since allowances and carbon credits are tradeable instruments with a transparent price, financial investors can buy them on the <a href="/wiki/Spot_market" title="Spot market">spot market</a> for speculation purposes, or link them to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Futures_contracts" title="Futures contracts">futures contracts</a>. A high volume of trading in this secondary market helps <a href="/wiki/Price_discovery" title="Price discovery">price discovery</a> and liquidity, and in this way helps to keep down costs and set a clear price signal in CO<sub>2</sub> which helps businesses to plan investments. This market has grown substantially, with banks, brokers, funds, arbitrageurs and private traders now participating in a market valued at about $60 billion in 2007.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Emissions Trading PLC, for example, was floated on the <a href="/wiki/London_Stock_Exchange" title="London Stock Exchange">London Stock Exchange</a>'s AIM market in 2005 with the specific remit of investing in emissions instruments.
</p><p>Although Kyoto created a framework and a set of rules for a global carbon market, there are in practice several distinct schemes or markets in operation today, with varying degrees of linkages among them.
</p><p>Kyoto enables a group of several annex I countries to create a market-within-a-market together. The EU elected to be treated as such a group, and created the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Union_Emissions_Trading_Scheme" title="European Union Emissions Trading Scheme">EU Emissions Trading Scheme</a> (ETS). The EU ETS uses EAUs (EU Allowance Units), each equivalent to a Kyoto AAU. The scheme went into operation on 1 January 2005, although a forward market has existed since 2003.
</p><p>The UK established its own learning-by-doing voluntary scheme, the UK ETS, which ran from 2002 through 2006. This market existed alongside the EU's scheme, and participants in the UK scheme have the option of applying to opt out of the first phase of the EU ETS, which lasts through 2007<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.
</p><p>The sources of Kyoto credits are the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Development_Mechanism" title="Clean Development Mechanism">Clean Development Mechanism</a> (CDM) and <a href="/wiki/Joint_Implementation" title="Joint Implementation">Joint Implementation</a> (JI) projects. The CDM allows the creation of new carbon credits by developing emission reduction projects in non-annex I countries, while JI allows project-specific credits to be converted from existing credits within annex I countries. CDM projects produce Certified Emission Reductions (CERs), and JI projects produce Emission Reduction Units (ERUs), each equivalent to one AAU. Kyoto CERs are also accepted for meeting EU ETS obligations, and ERUs will become similarly valid from 2008 for meeting ETS obligations (although individual countries may choose to limit the number and source of CER/JIs they will allow for compliance purposes starting from 2008). CERs/ERUs are overwhelmingly bought from project developers by funds or individual entities, rather than being exchange-traded like allowances.
</p><p>Since the creation of Kyoto is subject to a lengthy process of registration and certification by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNFCCC" title="UNFCCC">UNFCCC</a>, and the projects themselves require several years to develop, this market is at this point largely a forward market where purchases are made at a discount to their equivalent currency, the EUA, and are almost always subject to certification and delivery (although up-front payments are sometimes made). According to IETA, the market value of CDM/JI credits transacted in 2004 was EUR 245 m; it is estimated that more than EUR 620 m worth of credits were transacted in 2005.
</p><p>Several non-Kyoto carbon markets are in existence or being planned, and these are likely to grow in importance and numbers in the coming years. These include the <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales_Greenhouse_Gas_Abatement_Scheme" title="New South Wales Greenhouse Gas Abatement Scheme">New South Wales Greenhouse Gas Abatement Scheme</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Regional_Greenhouse_Gas_Initiative" title="Regional Greenhouse Gas Initiative">Regional Greenhouse Gas Initiative</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_Climate_Initiative" title="Western Climate Initiative">Western Climate Initiative</a> in the United States and Canada, the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Climate_Exchange" title="Chicago Climate Exchange">Chicago Climate Exchange</a> and the State of California’s <a href="/wiki/Global_Warming_Solutions_Act_of_2006" title="Global Warming Solutions Act of 2006">recent initiative</a> to reduce emissions.
</p><p>These initiatives taken together may create a series of partly linked markets, rather than a single carbon market. The common theme is the adoption of market-based mechanisms centered on carbon credits that represent a reduction of CO<sub>2</sub> emissions. The fact that some of these initiatives have similar approaches to certifying their credits makes it possible that carbon credits in one market may in the long run be tradeable in other schemes. The scheme would broaden the current carbon market far more than the current focus on the CDM/JI and EU ETS domains. An obvious precondition, however, is a realignment of penalties and fines to similar levels,since these create an effective ceiling for each market.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Views_on_the_flexibility_mechanisms">Views on the flexibility mechanisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flexible_Mechanisms&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Views on the flexibility mechanisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Views_on_the_Kyoto_Protocol" title="Views on the Kyoto Protocol">Views on the Kyoto Protocol</a></div>
<p>As stated in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flexible_mechanisms" title="Flexible mechanisms">lede</a>, one of the main arguments made in favour of the flexibility mechanisms is that of cost-effectiveness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The principle of cost-effectiveness is included in the UN Framework Convention on Climate Change (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNFCCC" title="UNFCCC">UNFCCC</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The economic basis of costs being reduced through flexibility is discussed in <a href="/wiki/Emissions_trading#Applying_the_economic_theory" title="Emissions trading">emissions trading#Applying the economic theory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Economics_of_climate_change_mitigation#Flexibility" title="Economics of climate change mitigation">economics of climate change mitigation#Flexibility</a>.
</p><p>A number of concerns were raised about flexibility in the lead-up to negotiations of the Kyoto Protocol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Two examples of issues raised were that of domestic emissions reductions in the developed countries, and the issue of developed countries effectively taking up all the low-cost emissions reductions in developing countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-banuri_joint_implementation_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-banuri_joint_implementation-7">[7]</a></sup> The idea behind the first view was that most emissions reductions should occur first in the developed countries - this would encourage the development of low-carbon energy technologies which could then be taken up later on by developing counties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-banuri_joint_implementation_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-banuri_joint_implementation-7">[7]</a></sup> The second idea was that all of the low-cost emissions reductions in developing countries would, in effect, be stolen by the developed countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-banuri_joint_implementation_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-banuri_joint_implementation-7">[7]</a></sup> Thus, when it came time for developing countries to take on their own commitments to reduce emissions, it would be more costly for them to do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-banuri_joint_implementation_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-banuri_joint_implementation-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Differing views on flexibility were summarized in the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a>'s (IPCC) <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Second_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Second Assessment Report">Second Assessment Report</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The basic economic argument in favour of flexibility was that, in principle at least, issues to do with fairness ("<a href="/wiki/Equity_(economics)" title="Equity (economics)">equity</a>" in the language of economics) could be separated from efficiency (i.e., reducing emissions most cheaply).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> From this viewpoint, flexibility through <a href="/wiki/Emissions_trading" title="Emissions trading">emissions trading</a> could promote efficiency, while arguments of equity could be partially addressed through, for example, the allocations of emissions rights between different countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>During negotiations, the US was a supporter of flexibility,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> while several other negotiating parties were in favour of uniform emissions cuts (e.g., the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_of_Small_Island_States" title="Alliance of Small Island States">Alliance of Small Island States</a>, ASIS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In the end, flexibility was incorporated into Kyoto's design, but the treaty still places an emphasis on developed countries achieving the bulk of their emissions reductions domestically, rather than in developing countries (i.e., by using the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Development_Mechanism" title="Clean Development Mechanism">Clean Development Mechanism</a>, CDM).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spash_domestic_v_international_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-spash_domestic_v_international-14">[14]</a></sup> The balance between domestic emissions reductions in developed countries and reductions through the CDM is not, however, quantified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spash_domestic_v_international_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-spash_domestic_v_international-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Issues_raised_since_implementation">Issues raised since implementation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flexible_Mechanisms&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Issues raised since implementation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since the implementation of the flexibility mechanisms, a number of other concerns have been raised. There have been various criticisms of the CDM (see <a href="/wiki/Clean_Development_Mechanism" title="Clean Development Mechanism">Clean Development Mechanism</a> for details). These include excess profits generated by CDM projects designed to reduce emissions of industrial gases,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> adverse effects of projects on local communities,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and the failure of the CDM to promote development in the poorest regions of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Criticisms have also been made of the various emissions trading schemes set-up by developed countries to meet their first-round Kyoto targets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> These criticisms are discussed in the individual articles on these trading schemes: see <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol#International_Emissions_Trading" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol#International Emissions Trading</a> for a list of these trading schemes. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_organization" title="Environmental organization">environmental organization</a> <a href="/wiki/Friends_of_the_Earth_(EWNI)" title="Friends of the Earth (EWNI)">Friends of the Earth (EWNI)</a> has called for the EU Emissions Trading System (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EU_ETS" title="EU ETS">EU ETS</a>) to be scrapped, and be replaced by other policies (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">energy efficiency</a> <a href="/wiki/Standardization" title="Standardization">standards</a>), which they argue would be more effective than the EU ETS at reducing emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>The articles referred to above also contain policy measures proposed by governments and commentators<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> to address some of these criticisms.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Future">Future</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flexible_Mechanisms&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Future">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The second commitment period of the Kyoto Protocol ends in 2020. Due to this, negotiations are underway to have the role of Flexible Mechanisms continue in some form under the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Agreement" title="Paris Agreement">Paris_Agreement</a>. All though not yet worked out (at the moment of writing; november 2016), This should be possible as both article 5 and 6 call for a similar mechanism to be created. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
