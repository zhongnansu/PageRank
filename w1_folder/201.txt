<p>The <b>picometre</b> (international <a href="/wiki/American_and_British_English_spelling_differences#-re,_-er" title="American and British English spelling differences">spelling</a> as used by the <a href="/wiki/International_Bureau_of_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Bureau of Weights and Measures">International Bureau of Weights and Measures</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI" title="SI">SI</a> symbol: <b>pm</b>) or <b>picometer</b> (<a href="/wiki/American_and_British_English_spelling_differences#-re,_-er" title="American and British English spelling differences">American spelling</a>) is a <a href="/wiki/Unit_of_length" title="Unit of length">unit of length</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">metric system</a>, equal to <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6988100000000000000♠</span>1<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>−12</sup> <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">m</a></span>, or one trillionth <span class="nowrap">(<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;"><span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7012100000000000000♠</span>1<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span></span></span></span>)</span> of a <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metre</a>, which is the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">SI</a> <a href="/wiki/SI_base_unit" title="SI base unit">base unit</a> of length.
</p><p>The picometre is one thousandth of a <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">nanometre</a>, one millionth of a <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">micrometre</a> (also known as a micron), and used to be called micromicron, stigma, or bicron.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The symbol µµ was once used for it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It is also one hundredth of an <a href="/wiki/%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="Ångström">Ångström</a>, an internationally recognised (but non-SI) unit of length.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use">Use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Picometre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The picometre's length is of an order such that its application is almost entirely confined to <a href="/wiki/Particle_physics" title="Particle physics">particle physics</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_physics" title="Quantum physics">quantum physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Acoustics" title="Acoustics">acoustics</a>. <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">Atoms</a> are between 62 and 520 pm in diameter, and the typical <a href="/wiki/Bond_length" title="Bond length">length</a> of a carbon-carbon <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">single bond</a> is 154 pm.  Smaller units still may be used to describe smaller particles (some of which are the components of atoms themselves), such as <a href="/wiki/Hadron" title="Hadron">hadrons</a> and the upper limits of possible size for <a href="/wiki/Fermion" title="Fermion">fermion</a> point particles.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Laser_Interferometer_Space_Antenna#Science" title="Laser Interferometer Space Antenna">Laser Interferometer Space Antenna</a> (LISA) probe is planned for launch in 2034 to directly detect <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_wave" title="Gravitational wave">gravitational waves</a> and will measure relative displacements with a resolution of 20 picometres over a distance of 2.5 gigametres, yielding a strain sensitivity of better than 1 part in 10<sup>20</sup>.
</p>
