<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">By-product</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">unwanted secondary product of an item or process</div>
<p>A <b>by-product</b> is a secondary product derived from a manufacturing process or chemical reaction. It is not the primary product or service being produced. In the context of <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">production</a>, a by-product is the 'output from a joint production process that is minor in quantity and/or <a href="/wiki/Net_realizable_value" title="Net realizable value">net realizable value</a> (NRV) when compared with the main products'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Because they are deemed to have no influence on reported financial results, by-products do not receive allocations of <a href="/wiki/Joint_cost" title="Joint cost">joint costs</a>. By-products also by convention are not inventoried, but the NRV from by-products is typically recognized as 'other income' or as a reduction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joint_production_process" title="Joint production process">joint production processing</a> costs when the by-product is produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A by-product can be useful and marketable or it can be considered waste. For example, <a href="/wiki/Bran" title="Bran">bran</a> is a byproduct of the <a href="/wiki/Mill_(grinding)" title="Mill (grinding)">milling</a> of refined <a href="/wiki/Flour" title="Flour">flour</a>, sometimes <a href="/wiki/Compost" title="Compost">composted</a> or burned for disposal but in other cases used as a nutritious ingredient in <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> or <a href="/wiki/Animal_feed" title="Animal feed">feed</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> was once a byproduct of <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">oil refining</a> that later became a desirable <a href="/wiki/Commodity" title="Commodity">commodity</a> as <a href="/wiki/Motor_fuel" title="Motor fuel">motor fuel</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">IEA</a> defines "by-product" in the context of <a href="/wiki/Life-cycle_assessment" title="Life-cycle assessment">life-cycle assessment</a>: "... main products, co-products (which involve similar revenues to the main product), by-products (which result in smaller revenues), and waste products (which provide little or no revenue)."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>While some chemists treat "by-product" and "side-product" as synonyms in the above sense of a generic secondary (untargeted) product, others find it useful to distinguish the two.  When the two terms are distinguished, "by-product" is used to refer to a product that is not desired but inevitably results from molecular fragments of starting materials and/or reagents that are not incorporated into the desired product, as a consequence of conservation of mass.  In contrast, "side-product" is used to refer to a product that is formed from a competitive process that could, in principle, be suppressed by optimization of reaction conditions.
</p>
