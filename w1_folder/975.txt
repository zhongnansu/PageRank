<p><b>Southern Company</b> is an American gas   and electric utility <a href="/wiki/Holding_company#Public_utility_holding_company" title="Holding company">holding company</a> based in the southern <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. It is headquartered in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlanta,_Georgia" title="Atlanta, Georgia">Atlanta, Georgia</a>, with executive offices also located in <a href="/wiki/Birmingham,_Alabama" title="Birmingham, Alabama">Birmingham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>. The company is currently the second largest utility company in the  U.S., in terms of customer base. Through its subsidiaries it serves 9 million gas and electric utility customers in nine states. Southern Company's regulated regional electric utilities serve a 120,000-square-mile (310,000 km<sup>2</sup>) territory with 27,000 miles (43,000 km) of distribution lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AboutUs_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-AboutUs-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Southern Company is one of the largest energy providers in the United States and 162nd on the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a> listing of the largest U. S. corporations. It has more than 500,000 shareholders (NYSE: SO) and has been traded since September 30, 1949.
</p><p>Southern Company subsidiaries are operating or developing renewable: solar, wind, and biomass facilities across the U.S., as well as the first new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_unit" title="Nuclear unit">nuclear units</a> in the U.S. in 30 years at <a href="/wiki/Vogtle_Electric_Generating_Plant" title="Vogtle Electric Generating Plant">Plant Vogtle</a> near <a href="/wiki/Augusta,_Georgia" title="Augusta, Georgia">Augusta, Georgia</a>.
</p><p>Four retail electric companies, <a href="/wiki/Alabama_Power" title="Alabama Power">Alabama Power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_Power" title="Georgia Power">Georgia Power</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulf_Power" title="Gulf Power">Gulf Power</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Power" title="Mississippi Power">Mississippi Power</a>, serve 120,000 square miles (310,000 km<sup>2</sup>) in four states. Southern Power serves wholesale electricity customers across the U.S. Southern Company Gas serves utility customers in seven states.
</p><p>Southern Company owns the following companies:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Alabama_Power" title="Alabama Power">Alabama Power</a> - operating company, based in <a href="/wiki/Birmingham,_Alabama" title="Birmingham, Alabama">Birmingham</a>.  Serves the southern two-thirds of Alabama.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Georgia_Power" title="Georgia Power">Georgia Power</a> - operating company, based in Atlanta.  Serves all of Georgia, except for mostly rural counties.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulf_Power" title="Gulf Power">Gulf Power</a> - operating company, based in <a href="/wiki/Pensacola,_Florida" title="Pensacola, Florida">Pensacola, Florida</a>.  Serves most of the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Florida Panhandle</a>. An agreement was reached in May 2018 to sell Gulf Power to rival utility company <a href="/wiki/NextEra_Energy" title="NextEra Energy">NextEra Energy</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Power" title="Mississippi Power">Mississippi Power</a> - operating company, based in <a href="/wiki/Gulfport,_Mississippi" title="Gulfport, Mississippi">Gulfport</a>.  Serves the Mississippi Gulf Coast.</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Southern_Company_logo.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="44" data-file-width="121" height="80" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Southern_Company_logo.svg/220px-Southern_Company_logo.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Southern_Company_logo.svg/330px-Southern_Company_logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Southern_Company_logo.svg/440px-Southern_Company_logo.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Southern_Company_logo.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Southern Company previous logo</div></div></div>
<p>Southern Company can be traced back to 1924, when Southeastern Power &amp; Light was formed as a holding company for Alabama Traction, Light and Power (formed 1906), the immediate forerunner of Alabama Power.  Later that year, it formed Mississippi Power as a subsidiary, with Gulf Power following in 1925. In 1926, it merged with Georgia Power (formed 1902). In 1930, Southeastern Power &amp; Light merged into the Commonwealth &amp; Southern Corporation. The new system included five Northern companies and six Southern companies. However, in the late 1940s Commonwealth &amp; Southern was dissolved to meet the <a href="/wiki/Public_Utility_Holding_Company_Act_of_1935" title="Public Utility Holding Company Act of 1935">Public Utility Holding Company Act of 1935</a>. Four of Commonwealth &amp; Southern's Deep South operating companies—Alabama Power, Georgia Power, Gulf Power, and Mississippi Power—were deemed to be an integrated system and thus were allowed to remain under common ownership.  A new holding company, Southern Company, was incorporated in Delaware on November 9, 1945. It commenced operation in 1949, and moved to Georgia in 1950. In 1954–55, the company was involved in the <a href="/wiki/Dixon-Yates_contract" title="Dixon-Yates contract">Dixon-Yates contract</a> with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Atomic_Energy_Commission" title="United States Atomic Energy Commission">Atomic Energy Commission</a>, and the associated political controversy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1981, Southern Company became the first electric utility holding company in 46 years to diversify its operations by forming an unregulated subsidiary. In January 1982, Southern Energy, Inc., began official operations as a global energy company, growing to serve 10 countries on four continents. On April 2, 2001, Southern Company completed the spinoff of Southern Energy as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mirant_Corporation" title="Mirant Corporation">Mirant Corporation</a>.
</p><p>Another Southern Company subsidiary—Southern Nuclear—began providing services in 1991 to the system's nuclear power plants.
</p><p>In 1996, Southern Communications Services began providing digital wireless communications services to Southern Company's subsidiaries and also began marketing these services to the public within the Southeast as Southern Linc. Southern Telecom, a telecommunications subsidiary of Southern Company, was founded in 1997. Southern Telecom provides <a href="/wiki/Colocation_(business)" title="Colocation (business)">colocation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dark_fiber" title="Dark fiber">dark fiber</a> optic lines to network businesses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>On January 9, 2001, Southern Company received final approval from the Securities and Exchange Commission to form Southern Power, a subsidiary to own, manage and finance wholesale generating assets in the Southeast. The new subsidiary targets wholesale customers.
</p><p>On July 19, 2002, Southern Company Gas received certification from the Georgia Public Service Commission to enter the retail gas market. After nearly four years of operations, the company was sold and customers transferred to Cobb EMC's newly formed affiliate, Gas South.
</p><p>In 2016, Southern Company acquired PowerSecure, a distributed energy infrastructure technologies company, and AGL Resources (which was renamed <a href="/wiki/Southern_Company_Gas" title="Southern Company Gas">Southern Company Gas</a>). As a result of the AGL Resources merger, Southern Company doubled its customer base, expanded its footprint and broadened the scope of its business by increasing its natural gas presence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plant_Vogtle">Plant Vogtle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Plant Vogtle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vogtle_NPP.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="676" data-file-width="1015" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Vogtle_NPP.jpg/220px-Vogtle_NPP.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Vogtle_NPP.jpg/330px-Vogtle_NPP.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Vogtle_NPP.jpg/440px-Vogtle_NPP.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vogtle_NPP.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plant_Vogtle" title="Plant Vogtle">Plant Vogtle</a> in Georgia. Southern Company and its partners are constructing two new units on this site by 2022.</div></div></div>
<p>Southern Company subsidiaries operate hydroelectric, gas, coal, and nuclear generation sources to generate approximately 200 terawatt-hours of electricity. In 2009, coal represented 57 percent of the company's output, followed by <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear</a> (23%) and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> (16%). Renewable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroelectric_power" title="Hydroelectric power">hydroelectric power</a> represented 4 percent of Southern's generation. Coal-based generation dropped significantly in 2009 from an average of 70% between 2005 and 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRdata_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRdata-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2010, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">United States Department of Energy</a> awarded an $8.3 billion <a href="/wiki/Loan_guarantee" title="Loan guarantee">loan guarantee</a> to facilitate the construction of two new <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactors</a> at <a href="/wiki/Vogtle_Electric_Generating_Plant" title="Vogtle Electric Generating Plant">Plant Vogtle</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Augusta,_Georgia" title="Augusta, Georgia">Augusta, Georgia</a>. A Southern Company subsidiary, Georgia Power, owns 45.7% of the current 2,430 MW facility, with co-owners <a href="/wiki/Oglethorpe_Power" title="Oglethorpe Power">Oglethorpe Power</a> (30%) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Municipal_Electric_Authority_of_Georgia" title="Municipal Electric Authority of Georgia">Municipal Electric Authority of Georgia</a> (22.7%) and the <a href="/wiki/Dalton,_Georgia" title="Dalton, Georgia">City of Dalton</a> (1.6%). The plant is operated by Georgia Power. The $14 billion construction project is scheduled to be completed by 2022 and would double the plant's capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The construction of two 1,154 MW reactors has been hailed by Energy Secretary <a href="/wiki/Steven_Chu" title="Steven Chu">Steven Chu</a> as "the first new nuclear power plant to break ground in decades". It is expected to create up to 3,500 jobs during the construction phase, and 800 once operational.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> However in March 2017 <a href="/wiki/Westinghouse_Electric_Company" title="Westinghouse Electric Company">Westinghouse Electric Company</a>, who are building the plant, filed for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chapter_11_bankruptcy" title="Chapter 11 bankruptcy">Chapter 11 bankruptcy</a> because of $9 billion of losses from its two U.S. nuclear construction projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters-20170324_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-reuters-20170324-10">[10]</a></sup> The U.S. government had given $8.3 billion of loan guarantees on the financing of the four nuclear reactors being built in the U.S., and it is expected a way forward to completing the plant can be agreed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters-20170330_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-reuters-20170330-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plant_Ratcliffe">Plant Ratcliffe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Plant Ratcliffe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In September 2013 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EPA" title="EPA">EPA</a> introduced new provisions regarding output of carbon emissions in new power facilities.  The proposed emission limit for new energy sources will be 1,100lbs of carbon dioxide per megawatt hour of electricity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Preemptively recognizing the need for these changes, Southern Company broke ground on its 21st-century clean coal facility in June 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Southern's subsidiary, <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Power" title="Mississippi Power">Mississippi Power</a> will operate the plant.  The Kemper County Energy Facility, or <a href="/wiki/Kemper_Project" title="Kemper Project">Kemper Project</a>, takes advantage of the abundant <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a>, or poor quality coal, available in Mississippi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Additionally, it employs Transport Gasifier (TRIG) technology.  TRIG technology is built on the idea of dry-feed, non-slag gasifiers, which operate at lower temperatures than other coal gasifiers.  This dry-feed is crushed, heated, and circulated in the gasifier, producing a flammable synthetic gas, syngas.  Syngas can generate electricity with fewer emissions.  Of course, other byproducts are produced, like ammonia and sulphuric acid. These particular products are sold for commercial use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EPA" title="EPA">EPA</a> considers the <a href="/wiki/Kemper_Project" title="Kemper Project">Kemper Coal Project</a> and other planned facilities like it, to be a lifeline for the coal industry in the wake of the new climate change plan. Between 2010 and 2014, approximately 150 coal plants were shut down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Guardian_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Guardian-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of April 2014, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Department_of_Energy" title="US Department of Energy">US Department of Energy</a> had invested $270m in this project. Southern Company, and its subsidiary, <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Power" title="Mississippi Power">Mississippi Power</a> anticipate that the Kemper Coal Plant will generate enough energy to serve more than 187,000 customers.  Upon opening, the Kemper Coal Project is expected to be capable of stripping out at least 65% of the carbon dioxide, significantly exceeding the EPA's proposed requirement of 40%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Partnerships">Partnerships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Partnerships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Southern Company works closely with the US <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">Department of Energy</a> on a variety of projects including transmission and distribution of infrastructure and smart grid initiatives, environmental research programs, and nuclear generation.  One of the more significant joint efforts, the DOE's National Carbon Capture Center, is managed by Southern Company and represents national efforts to reduce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emissions" title="Greenhouse gas emissions">greenhouse gas emissions</a> from coal-based power generation.  At this location, Southern Company has been working with scientists and technology developers from government, industry, and universities who are creating the next generation of carbon capture technologies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Along with the DOE, Southern Company has been working with <a href="/wiki/KBR_(company)" title="KBR (company)">KBR</a>, another technology partner, to perfect its TRIG advanced <a href="/wiki/Coal_gasification" title="Coal gasification">coal gasification</a> technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> This process of breaking down "dirty coal", or <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a>, into its chemical components is not only cleaner, but it's also less expensive and more reliable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> This technology is currently being implemented at Southern Company's Kemper County power plant, one of the few new coal facilities working to keep the US coal industry alive. This new facility will be built on a lignite seam, is expected to strip out two-thirds of carbon dioxide emissions, leaving emissions at about the same level as natural gas.  The Kemper Coal Plant is expected to fall well under new regulations implemented by the EPA, which limits coal plants to 1,100lbs of carbon dioxide emissions per megawatt hour of electricity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Guardian_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Guardian-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>In an effort to make this technology more attainable, Southern Company has partnered with China's Shenhua Group to collaborate on further research, development, and deployment of clean coal technologies in the US, China, and around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>  This partnership with Shenhua, who is currently expected to add more than 400,000 megawatts of coal-fired capacity by 2035, could lead to wide deployment of TRIG-equipped power plants across Asia.  TRIG technology has the potential to not only assist China with their growing carbon issue, but also enable the country to tap into their own low quality coal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kemper_Project_Controversies">Kemper Project Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Kemper Project Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In February 2015, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mississippi_Supreme_Court" title="Mississippi Supreme Court">Mississippi Supreme Court</a> ruled Southern Company's subsidiary <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Power" title="Mississippi Power">Mississippi Power</a> must refund 186,000 South Mississippi ratepayers for rate increases related to the <a href="/wiki/Kemper_Project" title="Kemper Project">Kemper Project</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> These fees are derived from Mississippi's Baseload Act, allowing Mississippi Power to charge ratepayers for powerplants under construction.
</p><p>In May 2016, Southern Company and its subsidiary Mississippi Power announced they were being investigated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="Securities and Exchange Commission">Securities and Exchange Commission</a> related to overruns at the Kemper Project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The project had been repeatedly delayed and costs increased from $2.88 billion to $6.58 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2016, Mississippi Power was sued by Treetop Midstream Services over cancelation of a contract to receive <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> from the Kemper Project as part of the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a> design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Treetop had contracted to buy carbon dioxide from the Kemper plant and had built a pipeline in preparation to receive the gas. Treetop alleged Mississippi Power had fraudulently and "intentionally misrepresenting and concealing the start date" for the Kemper Project, though Mississippi Power stated the suit was without merit.
</p><p>The company was also found to have unlawfully fired a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whistle-blower" title="Whistle-blower">whistle-blower</a> who had criticized alleged false statements by company management.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_concerns">Environmental concerns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Environmental concerns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a coal-burning energy company, <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions are a primary environmental concern. In response to growing public and financial community interest the company has enacted both corrective and palliative environmental measures.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Corrective_measures">Corrective measures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Corrective measures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The company recently opened the Plant Crist Test Center<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
in <a href="/wiki/Pensacola,_Florida" title="Pensacola, Florida">Pensacola, Florida</a>, where they hope to discover new ways to reduce mercury emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Additionally, the company has developed TRIG advanced <a href="/wiki/Coal_gasification" title="Coal gasification">coal gasification</a> technology which turns coal into gas that can be used to generate electricity with less emissions than traditional coal-fired power plants. Southern Company's <a class="external text" href="http://kemperproject.org" rel="nofollow">Kemper Project</a> in Mississippi will be the only <a href="/wiki/Integrated_gasification_combined_cycle" title="Integrated gasification combined cycle">Integrated gasification combined cycle</a> (IGCC) plant in the U.S. that will capture and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">store carbon dioxide</a> emissions from commercial operations starting in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Palliative_measures">Palliative measures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Palliative measures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Southern Company is building one of the largest all-biomass plants in the nation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
The company expects the 100-megawatt Nacogdoches Generating Facility to serve the city of Austin for 20 years.
</p><p>In partnership with Turner Renewable Energy, the company is building one of the largest solar photovoltaic plants in the U.S. near <a href="/wiki/Cimarron,_New_Mexico" title="Cimarron, New Mexico">Cimarron, New Mexico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
The 30-megawatt project will supply power to approximately 9,000 homes.
</p><p>The company manages and operates the National Carbon Capture Center, a focal point of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Energy" title="U.S. Department of Energy">U.S. Department of Energy</a>'s efforts to develop <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_capture" title="Carbon capture">carbon capture</a> and greenhouse gas reduction technologies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> under which various projects to test <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">geologic sequestration</a> are in progress at Plant Gorgas in Alabama, Plant Daniel in Mississippi and other company sites.
</p><p>Southern Company participates in <i>Renew Our Rivers</i>, a volunteer program to remove debris from rivers and other waterways throughout the <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_United_States" title="Southeastern United States">Southeast</a>, which claims<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> over 11 million pounds of trash removed or recycled in Renew Our Rivers events.
</p>
