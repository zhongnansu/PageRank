<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Water pricing</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Water pricing</b> is a term that covers various processes to assign a price to <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>. These processes differ greatly under different circumstances:
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bottled_water">Bottled water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_pricing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Bottled water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prices for <a href="/wiki/Bottled_water" title="Bottled water">bottled water</a> are set in the market, but must be seen not as much as a price of water, than the price of the convenience, bottle and transportation. It is comparable to other bottled cheap beverages (soda, beer, ...). Retail prices vary widely between countries, brands, bottle sizes (0.33 liter to 20 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liter" title="Liter">liters</a>) and place of sale (supermarket, fair, restaurant etc.). They range from US$ 0.05 to US$6 per liter, equivalent to US$ 50 to US$ 6,000 per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cubic_meter" title="Cubic meter">cubic meter</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tanker_trucks">Tanker trucks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_pricing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tanker trucks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prices for water sold by tanker trucks in bulk, which is common in cities of some developing countries for households without access to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_pipe" title="Water pipe">piped</a> <a href="/wiki/Water_supply" title="Water supply">water supply</a>, are set in the market. Prices for trucked water vary between about US$1 and US$6 per cubic meter.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Utility_tariffs">Utility tariffs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_pricing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Utility tariffs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prices for piped water supply provided by utilities, be they publicly or privately managed, are determined administratively (see <a href="/wiki/Water_tariff" title="Water tariff">water tariffs</a>). They vary from US$ 0.01 to almost US$ 8 per cubic meter (including sewer tariffs).
</p><p>Portland 2.17 gallons cost $0.01 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Irrigation">Irrigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_pricing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Irrigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prices for <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> water that is being provided by a public agency are also typically determined administratively, usually using a <a href="/wiki/Flat_rate" title="Flat rate">flat rate</a>, since metering is not common in agriculture in most countries of the world.
</p><p>The following pricing systems exist for irrigation:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Area-based tariffs, sometimes differentiated by type of crop grown</li>
<li>Volumetric pricing, which requires measurement</li></ul>
<p>Tariffs can be paid in the form of labor, which holds mainly in communal types of management in traditional irrigation systems, or in cash. Tariffs can also vary between seasons, with higher tariffs charged during the dry season.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_abstraction">Direct abstraction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_pricing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Direct abstraction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In most countries there is no charge for abstracting water directly from <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifers</a>. However, some countries do levy volumetric charges or fees for water abstraction rights. These charges are typically levied on industries, utilities and farmers. Fees for water abstraction and discharge exist for example in France, where revenues are significant and are re-invested in the water sector by water agencies established in major basins. In Germany abstraction fees exist only for groundwater and only in some states, and their proceeds go into the general state budget. <a href="/wiki/Water_resources_management_in_Mexico" title="Water resources management in Mexico">Mexico</a> also charges for water abstraction and returns proceeds to utilities, but not to industries. Outside the <a href="/wiki/OECD" title="OECD">OECD</a> countries few countries charge water abstraction fees. Where they are applied the level of fees tends to be nominal, such as in <a href="/wiki/Water_supply_and_sanitation_in_Morocco" title="Water supply and sanitation in Morocco">Morocco</a>, or enforcement is partial, such as for groundwater abstraction fees in Jordan. In almost all countries that have introduced abstraction fees agriculture, the major water user worldwide, is exempted from abstraction fees.  Some countries allow water rights to be traded, so that the price for water itself is formed in the market. Such <a href="/wiki/Water_trading" title="Water trading">water trading</a> exist in parts of Australia, Chile and the Southwestern United States).
</p>
