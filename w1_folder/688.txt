<p><b>Sunlight</b> is a portion of the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> given off by the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, in particular <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a>, <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">visible</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> light. On <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, sunlight is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filter_(optics)" title="Filter (optics)">filtered</a> through <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">Earth's atmosphere</a>, and is obvious as <a href="/wiki/Daylight" title="Daylight">daylight</a> when the Sun is above the <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">horizon</a>. When the direct <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a> is not blocked by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clouds" title="Clouds">clouds</a>, it is experienced as <b>sunshine</b>, a combination of bright <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiant_heat" title="Radiant heat">radiant heat</a>. When it is blocked by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clouds_(meteorology)" title="Clouds (meteorology)">clouds</a> or <a href="/wiki/Diffuse_reflection" title="Diffuse reflection">reflects off other objects</a>, it is experienced as diffused light. The <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> uses the term "<a href="/wiki/Sunshine_duration" title="Sunshine duration">sunshine duration</a>" to mean the cumulative time during which an area receives direct <a href="/wiki/Irradiance" title="Irradiance">irradiance</a> from the Sun of at least 120 <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">watts</a> per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Square_meter" title="Square meter">square meter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Other sources indicate an "Average over the entire earth" of "164 Watts per square meter over a 24 hour day".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The ultraviolet radiation in sunlight has both positive and negative health effects, as it is both a principal source of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vitamin_D3" title="Vitamin D3">vitamin D<sub>3</sub></a> and a <a href="/wiki/Mutagen" title="Mutagen">mutagen</a>.
</p><p>Sunlight takes about 8.3 minutes to reach Earth from the surface of the Sun. A photon starting at the center of the Sun and changing direction every time it encounters a <a href="/wiki/Charged_particle" title="Charged particle">charged particle</a> would take between 10,000 and 170,000 years to get to the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sunlight is a key factor in <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>, the process used by plants and other <a href="/wiki/Autotroph" title="Autotroph">autotrophic</a> organisms to convert <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_energy" title="Light energy">light energy</a>, normally from the Sun, into <a href="/wiki/Chemical_energy" title="Chemical energy">chemical energy</a> that can be used to synthesize carbohydrates and to fuel the organisms' activities.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement">Measurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunlight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Measurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Researchers can measure the intensity of sunlight using a <a href="/wiki/Sunshine_recorder" title="Sunshine recorder">sunshine recorder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pyranometer" title="Pyranometer">pyranometer</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Pyrheliometer" title="Pyrheliometer">pyrheliometer</a>. To calculate the amount of sunlight reaching the ground, both the <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">eccentricity</a> of Earth's <a href="/wiki/Elliptic_orbit" title="Elliptic orbit">elliptic orbit</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)#Atmospheric_extinction" title="Extinction (astronomy)">attenuation</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a> have to be taken into account. <p>where <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">c</span> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_extinction" title="Atmospheric extinction">atmospheric extinction</a> and <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">m</span> is the relative optical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airmass" title="Airmass">airmass</a>. The atmospheric extinction brings the number of lux down to around 100 000.
</p><p>The total amount of energy received at ground level from the Sun at the zenith depends on the distance to the Sun and thus on the time of year. It is about 3.3% higher than average in January and 3.3% lower in July (see below). If the extraterrestrial solar radiation is 1367 watts per square meter (the value when the Earth–Sun distance is 1 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">astronomical unit</a>), then the direct sunlight at Earth's surface when the Sun is at the <a href="/wiki/Zenith" title="Zenith">zenith</a> is about 1050 W/m<sup>2</sup>, but the total amount (direct and indirect from the atmosphere) hitting the ground is around 1120 W/m<sup>2</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Solar_constant_at_ground_level_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Solar_constant_at_ground_level-5">[5]</a></sup> In terms of energy, sunlight at Earth's surface is around 52 to 55 percent infrared (above 700 <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">nm</a>), 42 to 43 percent visible (400 to 700 nm), and 3 to 5 percent ultraviolet (below 400 nm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> At the top of the atmosphere, sunlight is about 30% more intense, having about 8% <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> (UV),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> with most of the extra UV consisting of biologically damaging short-wave ultraviolet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Solar_radiation_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Solar_radiation-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p><span id="Direct_sunlight">Direct sunlight</span> has a <a href="/wiki/Luminous_efficacy" title="Luminous efficacy">luminous efficacy</a> of about 93 <a href="/wiki/Lumen_(unit)" title="Lumen (unit)">lumens</a> per watt of <a href="/wiki/Radiant_flux" title="Radiant flux">radiant flux</a>. This is higher than the efficacy (of source) of most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artificial_lighting" title="Artificial lighting">artificial lighting</a> (including fluorescent), which means using sunlight for illumination heats up a room less than using most forms of artificial lighting.
</p><p>Multiplying the figure of 1050 watts per square metre by 93 lumens per watt indicates that bright sunlight provides an <a href="/wiki/Illuminance" title="Illuminance">illuminance</a> of approximately 98 000 <a href="/wiki/Lux" title="Lux">lux</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lumen_(unit)" title="Lumen (unit)">lumens</a> per square meter) on a perpendicular surface at sea level. The illumination of a horizontal surface will be considerably less than this if the Sun is not very high in the sky. Averaged over a day, the highest amount of sunlight on a horizontal surface occurs in January at the <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">South Pole</a> (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation">insolation</a>).
</p><p>Dividing the <a href="/wiki/Irradiance" title="Irradiance">irradiance</a> of 1050 W/m<sup>2</sup> by the size of the sun's disk in <a href="/wiki/Steradian" title="Steradian">steradians</a> gives an average <a href="/wiki/Radiance" title="Radiance">radiance</a> of 15.4 MW per square metre per steradian. (However, the radiance at the centre of the sun's disk is somewhat higher than the average over the whole disk due to <a href="/wiki/Limb_darkening" title="Limb darkening">limb darkening</a>.) Multiplying this by π gives an upper limit to the irradiance which can be focused on a surface using mirrors: 48.5 MW/m<sup>2</sup>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition_and_power">Composition and power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunlight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Composition and power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solar_spectrum_en.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Solar_spectrum_en.svg/220px-Solar_spectrum_en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Solar_spectrum_en.svg/330px-Solar_spectrum_en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Solar_spectrum_en.svg/440px-Solar_spectrum_en.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solar_spectrum_en.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Solar irradiance spectrum above atmosphere and at surface. Extreme UV and X-rays are produced (at left of wavelength range shown) but comprise very small amounts of the Sun's total output power.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">Ultraviolet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">Infrared</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">Light</a></div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequency_spectrum" title="Frequency spectrum">spectrum</a> of the Sun's solar radiation is close to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_body_radiation" title="Black body radiation">that of a black body</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> with a temperature of about 5,800 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The Sun emits EM radiation across most of the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">electromagnetic spectrum</a>. Although the Sun produces <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma rays</a> as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear-fusion</a> process, internal absorption and thermalization convert these super-high-energy <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a> to lower-energy photons before they reach the Sun's surface and are emitted out into space. As a result, the Sun does not emit gamma rays from this process, but it does emit gamma rays from <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">solar flares</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The Sun also emits <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xray" title="Xray">X-rays</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light">visible light</a>, <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a>, and even <a href="/wiki/Radio_wave" title="Radio wave">radio waves</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> the only direct signature of the nuclear process is the emission of <a href="/wiki/Neutrino" title="Neutrino">neutrinos</a>.
</p><p>Although the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_corona" title="Solar corona">solar corona</a> is a source of <a href="/wiki/Extreme_ultraviolet" title="Extreme ultraviolet">extreme ultraviolet</a> and X-ray radiation, these rays make up only a very small amount of the power output of the Sun (see spectrum at right). The spectrum of nearly all solar <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> striking the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">Earth's atmosphere</a> spans a range of 100 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanometer" title="Nanometer">nm</a> to about 1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millimeter" title="Millimeter">mm</a> (1,000,000 nm).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Specific information with no sourcing (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This band of significant radiation power can be divided into five regions in increasing order of <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelengths</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Ultraviolet C</b> or (UVC) range, which spans a range of 100 to 280 nm. The term <i>ultraviolet</i> refers to the fact that the radiation is at higher frequency than violet light (and, hence, also invisible to the <a href="/wiki/Human_eye" title="Human eye">human eye</a>). Due to absorption by the atmosphere very little reaches Earth's surface. This spectrum of radiation <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_germicidal_irradiation" title="Ultraviolet germicidal irradiation">has germicidal properties</a>, as used in <a href="/wiki/Germicidal_lamp" title="Germicidal lamp">germicidal lamps</a>.</li>
<li><b>Ultraviolet B</b> or (UVB) range spans 280 to 315 nm. It is also greatly absorbed by the Earth's atmosphere, and along with UVC causes the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photochemical_reaction" title="Photochemical reaction">photochemical reaction</a> leading to the production of the <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a>. It directly damages DNA and causes <a href="/wiki/Sunburn" title="Sunburn">sunburn</a>, but is also required for <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_D" title="Vitamin D">vitamin D</a> synthesis in the skin and fur of mammals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Ultraviolet A</b> or (UVA) spans 315 to 400 nm. This band was once<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (December 2016)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> held to be less damaging to <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>, and hence is used in cosmetic artificial <a href="/wiki/Sun_tanning" title="Sun tanning">sun tanning</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tanning_booth" title="Tanning booth">tanning booths</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tanning_bed" title="Tanning bed">tanning beds</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PUVA" title="PUVA">PUVA</a> therapy for <a href="/wiki/Psoriasis" title="Psoriasis">psoriasis</a>. However, UVA is now known to cause significant damage to DNA via indirect routes (formation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_radicals" title="Free radicals">free radicals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reactive_oxygen_species" title="Reactive oxygen species">reactive oxygen species</a>), and can cause cancer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light">Visible</a> range</b> or <b>light</b> spans 380 to 780 nm. As the name suggests, this range is visible to the naked eye. It is also the strongest output range of the Sun's total irradiance spectrum.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">Infrared</a></b> range that spans 700 nm to 1,000,000 nm (1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millimeter" title="Millimeter">mm</a>). It comprises an important part of the electromagnetic radiation that reaches Earth. Scientists divide the infrared range into three types on the basis of wavelength:
<ul><li>Infrared-A: 700 nm to 1,400 nm</li>
<li>Infrared-B: 1,400 nm to 3,000 nm</li>
<li>Infrared-C: 3,000 nm to 1 mm.</li></ul></li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_constant">Solar constant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunlight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Solar constant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Solar_constant" title="Solar constant">Solar constant</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solar_irradiance_spectrum_1992.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="540" data-file-width="553" height="215" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Solar_irradiance_spectrum_1992.gif/220px-Solar_irradiance_spectrum_1992.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Solar_irradiance_spectrum_1992.gif/330px-Solar_irradiance_spectrum_1992.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Solar_irradiance_spectrum_1992.gif/440px-Solar_irradiance_spectrum_1992.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solar_irradiance_spectrum_1992.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Solar irradiance spectrum at top of atmosphere, on a linear scale and plotted against <a href="/wiki/Wavenumber" title="Wavenumber">wavenumber</a></div></div></div>
<p>The <b>solar constant</b>, a measure of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flux_density" title="Flux density">flux density</a>, is the amount of incoming solar <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a> per unit area that would be incident on a plane perpendicular to the rays, at a distance of one <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">astronomical unit (AU)</a> (roughly the mean distance from the Sun to Earth). The "solar constant" includes all types of solar radiation, not just the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light">visible light</a>. Its average value was thought to be approximately 1366 W/m²,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TSI20_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-TSI20-18">[18]</a></sup> varying slightly with <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">solar activity</a>, but recent recalibrations of the relevant satellite observations indicate a value closer to 1361 W/m² is more realistic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KoppLean11_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-KoppLean11-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Total_solar_irradiance_.28TSI.29_and_spectral_solar_irradiance_.28SSI.29_upon_Earth"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Total_solar_irradiance_(TSI)_and_spectral_solar_irradiance_(SSI)_upon_Earth">Total solar irradiance (TSI) and spectral solar irradiance (SSI) upon Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunlight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Total solar irradiance (TSI) and spectral solar irradiance (SSI) upon Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span id="Total_Solar_Irradiance"></span><span id="TSI"></span>
<b>Total solar irradiance</b> (TSI) – the amount of solar radiation received at the top of Earth's atmosphere – has been measured since 1978 by a series of overlapping NASA and ESA satellite experiments to be 1.361 kilo⁠watts per square meter (kW/m²).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TSI20_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-TSI20-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TSI_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-TSI-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> TSI observations are continuing today with the <a href="/wiki/ACRIMSAT" title="ACRIMSAT">ACRIMSAT</a>/ACRIM3, <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">SOHO</a>/VIRGO and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SORCE" title="SORCE">SORCE</a>/TIM satellite experiments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Variation of TSI has been discovered on many timescales including the solar magnetic cycle <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acrim_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-acrim-24">[24]</a></sup> and many shorter periodic cycles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> TSI provides the energy that drives Earth's climate, so continuation of the TSI time series database is critical to understanding the role of solar variability in climate change.
</p><p><b>Spectral solar irradiance</b> (SSI) – the spectral distribution of the TSI – has been monitored since 2003 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SORCE" title="SORCE">SORCE</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Spectral_Irradiance_Monitor&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Spectral Irradiance Monitor (page does not exist)">Spectral Irradiance Monitor</a> (SIM). It has been found that SSI at UV (ultraviolet) wavelength corresponds in a less clear, and probably more complicated fashion, with Earth's climate responses than earlier assumed, fueling broad avenues of new research in “the connection of the Sun and stratosphere, troposphere, biosphere, ocean, and Earth’s climate”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goddard_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-goddard-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The actual brightness of sunlight that would be observed at the surface depends also on the presence and composition of an <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>. For example, <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Venus" title="Atmosphere of Venus">Venus's thick atmosphere</a> reflects more than 60% of the solar light it receives. The actual illumination of the surface is about 14,000 lux, comparable to that on Earth "in the daytime with overcast clouds".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sunlight on Mars would be more or less like daylight on Earth during a slightly overcast day, and, as can be seen in the pictures taken by the rovers, there is enough <a href="/wiki/Diffuse_sky_radiation" title="Diffuse sky radiation">diffuse sky radiation</a> that shadows would not seem particularly dark. Thus, it would give perceptions and "feel" very much like Earth daylight. The spectrum on the surface is slightly redder than that on Earth, due to scattering by reddish dust in the Martian atmosphere.
</p><p>For comparison,  sunlight on Saturn is slightly brighter than Earth sunlight at the average sunset or sunrise (see <a href="/wiki/Daylight" title="Daylight">daylight</a> for comparison table). Even on Pluto, the sunlight would still be bright enough to almost match the average living room. To see sunlight as dim as full <a href="/wiki/Moonlight" title="Moonlight">moonlight</a> on Earth, a distance of about 500 AU (~69 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light-hour" title="Light-hour">light-hours</a>) is needed; there are only a handful of objects in the Solar System known to orbit farther than such a distance, among them <a href="/wiki/90377_Sedna" title="90377 Sedna">90377 Sedna</a> and <span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/(87269)_2000_OO67" title="(87269) 2000 OO67">(87269) 2000 OO<span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;"><span style="font-size:80%;">67</span></span></a></span>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surface_illumination">Surface illumination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunlight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Surface illumination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The spectrum of surface illumination depends upon solar elevation due to atmospheric effects, with the blue spectral component dominating during twilight before and after sunrise and sunset, respectively, and red dominating during sunrise and sunset. These effects are apparent in natural light <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">photography</a> where the principal source of illumination is sunlight as mediated by the atmosphere.
</p><p>While the color of the sky is usually determined by <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">Rayleigh scattering</a>, an exception occurs at sunset and twilight. "Preferential absorption of sunlight by ozone over long horizon paths gives the zenith sky its blueness when the sun is near the horizon".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>See <a href="/wiki/Diffuse_sky_radiation" title="Diffuse sky radiation">diffuse sky radiation</a> for more details.
</p>
<h3><span id="Spectral_composition_of_sunlight_at_Earth.27s_surface"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Spectral_composition_of_sunlight_at_Earth's_surface">Spectral composition of sunlight at Earth's surface</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunlight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Spectral composition of sunlight at Earth's surface">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Sun's electromagnetic radiation which is received at the Earth's surface is predominantly light that falls within the range of wavelengths to which the visual systems of the animals that inhabit Earth's surface are sensitive. The Sun may therefore be said to <a href="/wiki/Lighting" title="Lighting">illuminate</a>, which is a measure of the light within a specific sensitivity range. Many animals (including humans) have a sensitivity range of approximately 400–700 nm,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BuserImbert1992_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-BuserImbert1992-29">[29]</a></sup> and given optimal conditions the absorption and scattering by Earth's atmosphere produces illumination that approximates an <a href="/wiki/Standard_illuminant" title="Standard illuminant">equal-energy illuminant</a> for most of this range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacEvoy_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-MacEvoy-30">[30]</a></sup> The useful range for color vision in humans, for example, is approximately 450–650 nm. Aside from effects that arise at sunset and sunrise, the spectral composition changes primarily in respect to how directly sunlight is able to illuminate. When illumination is indirect, <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">Rayleigh scattering</a> in the upper atmosphere will lead blue wavelengths to dominate. Water vapour in the lower atmosphere produces further scattering and ozone, dust and water particles will also absorb selective wavelengths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wyszecki_and_Stiles_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wyszecki_and_Stiles-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacAdam_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-MacAdam-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations_in_solar_irradiance">Variations in solar irradiance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunlight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Variations in solar irradiance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seasonal_and_orbital_variation">Seasonal and orbital variation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunlight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Seasonal and orbital variation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation">Insolation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sunshine_duration" title="Sunshine duration">Sunshine duration</a></div>
<p>On Earth, the solar radiation varies with the angle of the sun above the <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">horizon</a>, with longer sunlight duration at high latitudes during summer, varying to no sunlight at all in winter near the pertinent pole. When the direct radiation is not blocked by clouds, it is experienced as <i>sunshine</i>. The warming of the ground (and other objects) depends on the <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(electromagnetic_radiation)" title="Absorption (electromagnetic radiation)">absorption of the electromagnetic radiation</a> in the form of <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a>.
</p><p>The amount of radiation intercepted by a planetary body varies inversely with the square of the distance between the star and the planet. Earth's <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obliquity" title="Obliquity">obliquity</a> change with time (over thousands of years), sometimes forming a nearly perfect circle, and at other times stretching out to an <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">orbital eccentricity</a> of 5% (currently 1.67%). As the orbital eccentricity changes, the average distance from the sun (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semimajor_axis" title="Semimajor axis">semimajor axis</a> does not significantly vary, and so the total <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation">insolation</a> over a year remains almost constant due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kepler%27s_second_law" title="Kepler's second law">Kepler's second law</a>,

</p><p>But the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seasonal" title="Seasonal">seasonal</a> and latitudinal distribution and intensity of solar radiation received at Earth's surface does vary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effect_of_sun_angle_on_climate" title="Effect of sun angle on climate">effect of sun angle on climate</a> results in the change in solar energy in summer and winter.  For example, at <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitudes</a> of 65 degrees, this can vary by more than 25% as a result of Earth's orbital variation. Because changes in winter and summer tend to offset, the change in the annual average insolation at any given location is near zero, but the redistribution of energy between summer and winter does strongly affect the intensity of seasonal cycles. Such changes associated with the redistribution of solar energy are considered a likely cause for the coming and going of recent <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice ages</a> (see: <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">Milankovitch cycles</a>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_intensity_variation">Solar intensity variation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunlight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Solar intensity variation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_variation" title="Solar variation">Solar variation</a></div>
<p>Space-based observations of solar irradiance started in 1978. These measurements show that the solar constant is not constant. It varies on many time scales, including the 11-year sunspot solar cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acrim_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-acrim-24">[24]</a></sup> When going further back in time, one has to rely on irradiance reconstructions, using sunspots for the past 400 years or cosmogenic radionuclides for going back 10,000 years.
Such reconstructions have been done.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> These studies show that in addition to the solar irradiance variation with the solar cycle (the (Schwabe) cycle), the solar activitiy varies with longer cycles, such as the proposed 88 year (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gleisberg_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gleisberg cycle (page does not exist)">Gleisberg cycle</a>), 208 year (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=DeVries_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="DeVries cycle (page does not exist)">DeVries cycle</a>) and 1,000 year (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eddy_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eddy cycle (page does not exist)">Eddy cycle</a>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_on_Earth">Life on Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunlight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Life on Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The existence of nearly all <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">life on Earth</a> is fueled by light from the Sun. Most <a href="/wiki/Autotroph" title="Autotroph">autotrophs</a>, such as plants, use the energy of sunlight, combined with carbon dioxide and water, to produce simple sugars—a process known as <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>. These sugars are then used as building-blocks and in other synthetic pathways that allow the organism to grow.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Heterotroph" title="Heterotroph">Heterotrophs</a>, such as animals, use light from the Sun indirectly by consuming the products of autotrophs, either by consuming autotrophs, by consuming their products, or by consuming other heterotrophs. The sugars and other molecular components produced by the autotrophs are then broken down, releasing stored solar energy, and giving the heterotroph the energy required for survival. This process is known as <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">cellular respiration</a>.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Prehistory" title="Prehistory">prehistory</a>, humans began to further extend this process by putting plant and animal materials to other uses. They used animal skins for warmth, for example, or wooden weapons to hunt. These skills allowed humans to harvest more of the sunlight than was possible through glycolysis alone, and human population began to grow.
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_Revolution" title="Neolithic Revolution">Neolithic Revolution</a>, the domestication of plants and animals further increased human access to solar energy. Fields devoted to crops were enriched by inedible plant matter, providing sugars and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nutrients" title="Nutrients">nutrients</a> for future harvests. Animals that had previously provided humans with only meat and tools once they were killed were now used for labour throughout their lives, fueled by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grasses" title="Grasses">grasses</a> inedible to humans.
</p><p>The more recent discoveries of <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> are modern extensions of this trend. These <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a> are the remnants of ancient plant and animal matter, formed using energy from sunlight and then trapped within Earth for millions of years. Because the stored energy in these fossil fuels has accumulated over many millions of years, they have allowed modern humans to massively increase the production and consumption of <a href="/wiki/Primary_energy" title="Primary energy">primary energy</a>. As the amount of fossil fuel is large but finite, this cannot continue indefinitely, and various theories exist as to what will follow this stage of human civilization (e.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alternative_fuels" title="Alternative fuels">alternative fuels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malthusian_catastrophe" title="Malthusian catastrophe">Malthusian catastrophe</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_urbanism" title="New urbanism">new urbanism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peak_oil" title="Peak oil">peak oil</a>).
</p>

<p>The effect of sunlight is relevant to <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">painting</a>, evidenced for instance in works of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eduard_Manet" title="Eduard Manet">Eduard Manet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Claude_Monet" title="Claude Monet">Claude Monet</a> on outdoor scenes and landscapes.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Winter_Sunshine.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1687" data-file-width="2094" height="242" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Winter_Sunshine.jpg/300px-Winter_Sunshine.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Winter_Sunshine.jpg/450px-Winter_Sunshine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Winter_Sunshine.jpg/600px-Winter_Sunshine.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Winter_Sunshine.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Téli verőfény</i> ("Winter Sunshine") by <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A1szl%C3%B3_Medny%C3%A1nszky" title="László Mednyánszky">László Mednyánszky</a></div></div></div>
<p>Many people find direct sunlight to be too <a href="/wiki/Brightness" title="Brightness">bright</a> for comfort, especially when reading from white paper upon which the sun is directly shining. Indeed, looking directly at the sun can cause long-term vision damage. To compensate for the brightness of sunlight, many people wear <a href="/wiki/Sunglasses" title="Sunglasses">sunglasses</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">Cars</a>, many <a href="/wiki/Helmet" title="Helmet">helmets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cap" title="Cap">caps</a> are equipped with <a href="/wiki/Visor" title="Visor">visors</a> to block the sun from direct vision when the sun is at a low angle. Sunshine is often blocked from entering buildings through the use of <a href="/wiki/Wall" title="Wall">walls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Window_blind" title="Window blind">window blinds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Awning" title="Awning">awnings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Window_shutter" title="Window shutter">shutters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Curtain" title="Curtain">curtains</a>, or nearby <a href="/wiki/Shade_tree" title="Shade tree">shade trees</a>. Sunshine exposure is needed biologically for the creation of <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_D" title="Vitamin D">Vitamin D</a> in the skin, a vital compound needed to make strong bone and muscle in the body.
</p><p>In colder countries, many people prefer sunnier days and often avoid the <a href="/wiki/Shade_(shadow)" title="Shade (shadow)">shade</a>. In hotter countries, the converse is true; during the midday hours, many people prefer to stay inside to remain cool. If they do go outside, they seek shade that may be provided by trees, <a href="/wiki/Umbrella" title="Umbrella">parasols</a>, and so on.
</p><p>In many world religions, such as <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>, the sun is considered to be a god, as it is the source of life and energy on earth. It also formed the basis for religion in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egypt</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sunbathing">Sunbathing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunlight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Sunbathing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sun_tanning" title="Sun tanning">Sun tanning</a></div>

<p>Sunbathing is a popular <a href="/wiki/Leisure" title="Leisure">leisure</a> activity in which a person sits or lies in direct sunshine. People often sunbathe in comfortable places where there is ample sunlight. Some common places for sunbathing include <a href="/wiki/Beach" title="Beach">beaches</a>, open air <a href="/wiki/Swimming_pool" title="Swimming pool">swimming pools</a>, <a href="/wiki/Park" title="Park">parks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Garden" title="Garden">gardens</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sidewalk_cafe" title="Sidewalk cafe">sidewalk cafes</a>. Sunbathers typically wear limited amounts of clothing or some simply go <a href="/wiki/Nudity" title="Nudity">nude</a>. For some, an alternative to sunbathing is the use of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tanning_bed" title="Tanning bed">sunbed</a> that generates <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> light and can be used indoors regardless of weather conditions. Tanning beds have been banned in a number of states in the world.
</p><p>For many people with light skin, one purpose for sunbathing is to darken one's <a href="/wiki/Human_skin_color" title="Human skin color">skin color</a> (get a sun tan), as this is considered in some cultures to be attractive, associated with outdoor activity, <a href="/wiki/Vacation" title="Vacation">vacations/holidays</a>, and health. Some people prefer <a href="/wiki/Naturism" title="Naturism">naked</a> sunbathing so that an "all-over" or "even" tan can be obtained, sometimes as part of a specific lifestyle.
</p><p>Controlled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliotherapy" title="Heliotherapy">heliotherapy</a>, or sunbathing, has been used as a treatment for <a href="/wiki/Psoriasis" title="Psoriasis">psoriasis</a> and other maladies.
</p><p>Skin tanning is achieved by an increase in the dark pigment inside skin cells called <a href="/wiki/Melanocyte" title="Melanocyte">melanocytes</a>, and is an automatic response mechanism of the body to sufficient exposure to ultraviolet radiation from the sun or from artificial sunlamps. Thus, the tan gradually disappears with time, when one is no longer exposed to these sources.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_human_health">Effects on human health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunlight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Effects on human health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Health_effects_of_sunlight_exposure" title="Health effects of sunlight exposure">Health effects of sunlight exposure</a></div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_radiation" title="Ultraviolet radiation">ultraviolet radiation</a> in sunlight has both positive and negative health effects, as it is both a principal source of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vitamin_D3" title="Vitamin D3">vitamin D<sub>3</sub></a> and a <a href="/wiki/Mutagen" title="Mutagen">mutagen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> A dietary supplement can supply <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_D" title="Vitamin D">vitamin D</a> without this mutagenic effect,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ODS-vitamin-D-sheet_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-ODS-vitamin-D-sheet-39">[39]</a></sup> but bypasses natural mechanisms that would prevent overdoses of vitamin D generated internally from sunlight. Vitamin D has a wide range of positive health effects, which include strengthening bones<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> and possibly inhibiting the growth of some cancers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Sun exposure has also been associated with the timing of <a href="/wiki/Melatonin" title="Melatonin">melatonin</a> synthesis, maintenance of normal <a href="/wiki/Circadian_rhythm" title="Circadian rhythm">circadian rhythms</a>, and reduced risk of <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_affective_disorder" title="Seasonal affective disorder">seasonal affective disorder</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>Long-term sunlight exposure is known to be associated with the development of <a href="/wiki/Skin_cancer" title="Skin cancer">skin cancer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Photoaging" title="Photoaging">skin aging</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immune_suppression" title="Immune suppression">immune suppression</a>, and eye diseases such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cataracts" title="Cataracts">cataracts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Macular_degeneration" title="Macular degeneration">macular degeneration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Short-term overexposure is the cause of <a href="/wiki/Sunburn" title="Sunburn">sunburn</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snow_blindness" title="Snow blindness">snow blindness</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_retinopathy" title="Solar retinopathy">solar retinopathy</a>.
</p><p>UV rays, and therefore sunlight and sunlamps, are the only listed <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogens</a> that are known to have health benefits,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13th_Report_on_Carcinogens_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-13th_Report_on_Carcinogens-45">[45]</a></sup> and a number of public health organizations state that there needs to be a balance between the risks of having too much sunlight or too little.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> There is a general consensus that sunburn should always be avoided.
</p><p>Epidemiological data shows that people who have more exposure to the sun have less high blood pressure and cardiovascular-related mortality. While sunlight (and its UV rays) are a risk factor for skin cancer, "sun avoidance may carry more of a cost than benefit for over-all good health."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wellersunlight_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-wellersunlight-47">[47]</a></sup> A study found that there is no evidence that UV reduces lifespan in contrast to other risk factors like smoking, alcohol and high blood pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wellersunlight_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-wellersunlight-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_on_plant_genomes">Effect on plant genomes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunlight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Effect on plant genomes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Elevated solar <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">UV</a>-B doses increase the frequency of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> <a href="/wiki/Homologous_recombination" title="Homologous recombination">recombination</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana" title="Arabidopsis thaliana">Arabidopsis thaliana</a></i> and tobacco (<i><a href="/wiki/Nicotiana_tabacum" title="Nicotiana tabacum">Nicotiana tabacum</a></i>) plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid10894550_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid10894550-48">[48]</a></sup>  These increases are accompanied by strong induction of an enzyme with a key role in recombinational repair of DNA damage.  Thus the level of terrestrial solar UV-B radiation likely affects <a href="/wiki/Genome_instability" title="Genome instability">genome stability</a> in plants.
</p>
