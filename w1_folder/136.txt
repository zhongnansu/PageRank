Largest is 1.0 mm in diameter.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Carbonate rocks</b> are a class of <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rocks</a> composed primarily of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonate_mineral" title="Carbonate mineral">carbonate minerals</a>. The two major types are <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>, which is composed of <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a> or <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">aragonite</a> (different crystal forms of CaCO<sub>3</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Dolostone" title="Dolostone">dolostone</a>, which is composed of the mineral <a href="/wiki/Dolomite" title="Dolomite">dolomite</a> (CaMg(CO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>).
</p><p>Calcite can be either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dissolution_(chemistry)" title="Dissolution (chemistry)">dissolved</a> by <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> or <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(chemistry)" title="Precipitation (chemistry)">precipitated</a> by groundwater, depending on several factors including the water temperature, <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a>, and dissolved <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ion</a> concentrations. Calcite exhibits an unusual characteristic called <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Retrograde_solubility&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Retrograde solubility (page does not exist)">retrograde solubility</a> in which it becomes less soluble in water as the temperature increases.  
</p><p>When conditions are right for precipitation, calcite forms mineral coatings that cement the existing rock grains together or it can fill fractures. 
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karst_topography" title="Karst topography">Karst topography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cave" title="Cave">caves</a> develop in carbonate rocks because of their <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">solubility</a> in dilute <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acidic</a> groundwater.  Cooling groundwater or mixing of different groundwaters will also create conditions suitable for <a href="/wiki/Cave" title="Cave">cave</a> formation.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">Marble</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphic</a> carbonate rock. Rare <a href="/wiki/Igneous_rock" title="Igneous rock">igneous</a> carbonate rocks exist as <a href="/wiki/Intrusive_rock" title="Intrusive rock">intrusive</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbonatite" title="Carbonatite">carbonatites</a> and even rarer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic" title="Volcanic">volcanic</a> carbonate <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava</a>.
</p>
