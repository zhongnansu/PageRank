<p><b>Methyllithium</b> is the simplest <a href="/wiki/Organolithium_reagent" title="Organolithium reagent">organolithium reagent</a> with the empirical formula CH<sub>3</sub>Li. This <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S-block" title="S-block">s-block</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organometallic" title="Organometallic">organometallic</a> compound adopts an <a href="/wiki/Oligomer" title="Oligomer">oligomeric</a> structure both in solution and in the solid state. This highly reactive compound, invariably used as a solution in <a href="/wiki/Ether" title="Ether">ethers</a>, is a <a href="/wiki/Reagent" title="Reagent">reagent</a> in <a href="/wiki/Organic_synthesis" title="Organic synthesis">organic synthesis</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Organometallic_chemistry" title="Organometallic chemistry">organometallic chemistry</a>.  Operations involving methyllithium require anhydrous conditions, because the compound is highly reactive toward <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>. <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">Oxygen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> are also incompatible with MeLi. Methyllithium is usually not prepared, but purchased as a solution in various ethers.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis">Synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methyllithium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the direct synthesis, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methyl_bromide" title="Methyl bromide">methyl bromide</a> is treated with a suspension of <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Diethyl_ether" title="Diethyl ether">diethyl ether</a>.
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Li + MeBr → LiMe + <a href="/wiki/Lithium_bromide" title="Lithium bromide">LiBr</a></dd></dl>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Lithium_bromide" title="Lithium bromide">lithium bromide</a> forms a complex with the methyllithium.  Most commercially available methyllithium consists of this complex.  "Halide-free" methyllithium is prepared from methyl chloride.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lithium_chloride" title="Lithium chloride">Lithium chloride</a> precipitates from the diethyl ether since it does not form a strong complex with methyllithium.  The filtrate consists of fairly pure methyllithium.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactivity">Reactivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methyllithium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reactivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Methyllithium is both strongly <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">basic</a> and highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nucleophilic" title="Nucleophilic">nucleophilic</a> due to the partial negative charge on <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> and is therefore particularly reactive towards electron acceptors and proton donors.  In contrast to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N-BuLi" title="N-BuLi">n-BuLi</a>, MeLi reacts only very slowly with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/THF" title="THF">THF</a> at room temperature, and solutions in <a href="/wiki/Ether" title="Ether">ether</a> are indefinitely stable.  Water and alcohols react violently.  Most reactions involving methyllithium are conducted below room temperature.  Although MeLi can be used for deprotonations, <a href="/wiki/N-Butyllithium" title="N-Butyllithium"><i>n</i>-butyllithium</a> is more commonly employed since it is less expensive and more reactive.
</p><p>Methyllithium is mainly used as the synthetic equivalent of the methyl anion <a href="/wiki/Synthon" title="Synthon">synthon</a>.  For example, ketones react to give tertiary alcohols in a two-step process:
</p>
<dl><dd>Ph<sub>2</sub>CO  +  MeLi  →  Ph<sub>2</sub>C(Me)OLi</dd>
<dd>Ph<sub>2</sub>C(Me)OLi  +  H<sup>+</sup>  →  Ph<sub>2</sub>C(Me)OH  +  Li<sup>+</sup></dd></dl>
<p>Nonmetal halides are converted to methyl compounds with methyllithium:
</p>
<dl><dd>PCl<sub>3</sub>  +  3 MeLi  →  <a href="/wiki/Trimethylphosphine" title="Trimethylphosphine">PMe<sub>3</sub></a>  +  3 LiCl</dd></dl>
<p>Such reactions more commonly employ the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grignard_reagent" title="Grignard reagent">Grignard reagents</a> methylmagnesium halides, which are often equally effective, and less expensive or more easily prepared in situ.
</p><p>It also reacts with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> to give <a href="/wiki/Lithium_acetate" title="Lithium acetate">Lithium acetate</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>CH<sub>3</sub>Li + CO<sub>2</sub>  →  CH<sub>3</sub>CO<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup>Li<sup>+</sup></dd></dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">Transition metal</a> methyl compounds can be prepared by reaction of MeLi with metal halides.  Especially important are the formation of <a href="/wiki/Organocopper_compound" title="Organocopper compound">organocopper compounds</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gilman_reagent" title="Gilman reagent">Gilman reagents</a>), of which the most useful is <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lithium_dimethylcuprate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lithium dimethylcuprate (page does not exist)">lithium dimethylcuprate</a>.  This reagent is widely used for nucleophilic substitutions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epoxides" title="Epoxides">epoxides</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkyl_halide" title="Alkyl halide">alkyl halides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alkyl_sulfonate" title="Alkyl sulfonate">alkyl sulfonates</a>, as well as for conjugate additions to α,β-unsaturated carbonyl compounds by methyl anion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  Many other transition metal methyl compounds have been prepared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>ZrCl<sub>4</sub>  +  6 MeLi  →  Li<sub>2</sub>ZrMe<sub>6</sub>  +  4 LiCl</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methyllithium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Two structures have been verified by single crystal <a href="/wiki/X-ray_crystallography" title="X-ray crystallography">X-ray crystallography</a> as well as by <sup>6</sup>Li, <sup>7</sup>Li, and <sup>13</sup>C <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NMR_spectroscopy" title="NMR spectroscopy">NMR spectroscopy</a>. The tetrameric structure is a distorted <a href="/wiki/Cubane-type_cluster" title="Cubane-type cluster">cubane-type cluster</a>, with carbon and lithium atoms at alternate corners.  The Li---Li distances are 2.68 Å, almost identical with the Li-Li bond in gaseous <a href="/wiki/Dilithium" title="Dilithium">dilithium</a>.  The C-Li distances are 2.31 Å.  Carbon is bonded to three hydrogen atoms and three Li atoms.  The nonvolatility of (MeLi)<sub>4</sub> and its insolubility in alkanes results from the fact that the clusters interact via further inter-cluster <a href="/wiki/Agostic_interaction" title="Agostic interaction">agostic interactions</a>.  In contrast the bulkier cluster (tertiary-butylLi)<sub>4</sub>, where intercluster interactions are precluded by steric effects, is volatile as well as soluble in alkanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tetramer1.jpg"><img alt="Tetramer1.jpg" data-file-height="146" data-file-width="198" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8f/Tetramer1.jpg" width="198"/></a> <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Methyllithium-tetramer-1-3D-balls.png"><img alt="Methyllithium-tetramer-1-3D-balls.png" data-file-height="1100" data-file-width="1086" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Methyllithium-tetramer-1-3D-balls.png/150px-Methyllithium-tetramer-1-3D-balls.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Methyllithium-tetramer-1-3D-balls.png/225px-Methyllithium-tetramer-1-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Methyllithium-tetramer-1-3D-balls.png/300px-Methyllithium-tetramer-1-3D-balls.png 2x" width="150"/></a> <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Methyllithium-tetramer-2-3D-balls.png"><img alt="Methyllithium-tetramer-2-3D-balls.png" data-file-height="1100" data-file-width="1099" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Methyllithium-tetramer-2-3D-balls.png/150px-Methyllithium-tetramer-2-3D-balls.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Methyllithium-tetramer-2-3D-balls.png/225px-Methyllithium-tetramer-2-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Methyllithium-tetramer-2-3D-balls.png/300px-Methyllithium-tetramer-2-3D-balls.png 2x" width="150"/></a>
</p><p>Colour code: Li- purple C- black H- white
</p><p>The hexameric form features hexagonal prisms with Li and C atoms again at alternate corners.
</p><p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hexamer1.jpg"><img alt="Hexamer1.jpg" data-file-height="175" data-file-width="201" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/ca/Hexamer1.jpg" width="201"/></a> <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Methyllithium-hexamer-2-3D-balls.png"><img alt="Methyllithium-hexamer-2-3D-balls.png" data-file-height="1100" data-file-width="963" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Methyllithium-hexamer-2-3D-balls.png/150px-Methyllithium-hexamer-2-3D-balls.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Methyllithium-hexamer-2-3D-balls.png/225px-Methyllithium-hexamer-2-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Methyllithium-hexamer-2-3D-balls.png/300px-Methyllithium-hexamer-2-3D-balls.png 2x" width="150"/></a> <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Methyllithium-hexamer-3-3D-balls.png"><img alt="Methyllithium-hexamer-3-3D-balls.png" data-file-height="1100" data-file-width="1008" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Methyllithium-hexamer-3-3D-balls.png/150px-Methyllithium-hexamer-3-3D-balls.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Methyllithium-hexamer-3-3D-balls.png/225px-Methyllithium-hexamer-3-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Methyllithium-hexamer-3-3D-balls.png/300px-Methyllithium-hexamer-3-3D-balls.png 2x" width="150"/></a>
</p><p>Colour code: Li- purple C- black H- white
</p><p>The degree of aggregation, "n" for (MeLi)<sub>n</sub>, depends upon the solvent and the presence of additives (such as lithium bromide). Hydrocarbon solvents such as benzene<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown1957_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown1957-5">[5]</a></sup> favour formation of the hexamer, whereas <a href="/wiki/Ether" title="Ether">ethereal</a> solvents favour the tetramer.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bonding">Bonding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methyllithium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Bonding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>These clusters are considered "electron-deficient," that is, they do not follow the <a href="/wiki/Octet_rule" title="Octet rule">octet rule</a> because the molecules lack sufficient electrons to form four 2-centered, 2-electron bonds around each carbon atom, in contrast to most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_compounds" title="Organic compounds">organic compounds</a>. The hexamer is a 30 electron compound (30 valence electrons.) If one allocates 18 electrons for the strong C-H bonds, 12 electrons remain for Li-C and Li-Li bonding. There are six electrons for six metal-metal bonds and one electron per methyl-η<sup>3</sup> lithium interaction.
</p><p>The strength of the C-Li bond has been estimated at around 57 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilocalories" title="Kilocalories">kcal</a>/mol from <a href="/wiki/Infrared_spectroscopy" title="Infrared spectroscopy">IR</a> spectroscopic measurements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown1957_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brown1957-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
