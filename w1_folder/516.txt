<p>The <b>seabed</b> (also known as the <b>seafloor</b>, <b>sea floor</b>, or <b>ocean floor</b>) is the bottom of the <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Seabed_structure">Seabed structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seabed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Seabed structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Seafloor_spreading" title="Seafloor spreading">Seafloor spreading</a></div>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Seabed&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Oceanic_divisions.svg"><img alt="Drawing showing divisions according to depth and distance from shore" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="835" data-file-width="1114" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Oceanic_divisions.svg/300px-Oceanic_divisions.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Oceanic_divisions.svg/450px-Oceanic_divisions.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Oceanic_divisions.svg/600px-Oceanic_divisions.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Oceanic_divisions.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The major oceanic divisions</div></div></div>
<p>Most of the oceans have a common structure, created by common physical phenomena, mainly from tectonic movement, and sediment from various sources. The structure of the oceans, starting with the continents, begins usually with a <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a>, continues to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_slope" title="Continental slope">continental slope</a> – which is a steep descent into the ocean, until reaching the <a href="/wiki/Abyssal_plain" title="Abyssal plain">abyssal plain</a> – a topographic <a href="/wiki/Plain" title="Plain">plain</a>, the beginning of the seabed, and its main area. The border between the continental slope and the abyssal plain usually has a more gradual descent, and is called the <a href="/wiki/Continental_rise" title="Continental rise">continental rise</a>, which is caused by <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a> cascading down the continental slope.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Mid-ocean_ridge" title="Mid-ocean ridge">mid-ocean ridge</a>, as its name implies, is a mountainous rise through the middle of all the oceans, between the continents. Typically a <a href="/wiki/Rift" title="Rift">rift</a> runs along the edge of this ridge. Along <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic_plate" title="Tectonic plate">tectonic plate</a> edges there are typically <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_trench" title="Oceanic trench">oceanic trenches</a> – deep valleys, created by the mantle circulation movement from the mid-ocean mountain ridge to the oceanic trench.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Hotspot_(geology)" title="Hotspot (geology)">Hotspot</a> volcanic island ridges are created by volcanic activity, erupting periodically, as the tectonic plates pass over a hotspot. In areas with volcanic activity and in the oceanic trenches there are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_vents" title="Hydrothermal vents">hydrothermal vents</a> – releasing high pressure and extremely hot water and chemicals into the typically freezing water around it.
</p><p>Deep ocean water is divided into layers or zones, each with typical features of salinity, pressure, temperature and marine life, according to their depth. Lying along the top of the <a href="/wiki/Abyssal_plain" title="Abyssal plain">abyssal plain</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Abyssal_zone" title="Abyssal zone">abyssal zone</a>, whose lower boundary lies at about 6,000 m (20,000 ft). The <a href="/wiki/Hadal_zone" title="Hadal zone">hadal zone</a> – which includes the oceanic trenches, lies between 6,000–11,000 metres (20,000–36,000 ft) and is the deepest oceanic zone.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Benthos">Benthos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seabed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Benthos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Benthos" title="Benthos">Benthos</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Benthos" title="Benthos">Benthos</a> is the community of organisms which live on, in, or near the seabed, the area known as the <a href="/wiki/Benthic_zone" title="Benthic zone">benthic zone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-caml_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-caml-3">[3]</a></sup> This community lives in or near marine <a href="/wiki/Depositional_environment" title="Depositional environment">sedimentary environments</a>, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidal_pool" title="Tidal pool">tidal pools</a> along the <a href="/wiki/Intertidal_zone" title="Intertidal zone">foreshore</a>, out to the <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a>, and then down to the <a href="/wiki/Abyssal_zone" title="Abyssal zone">abyssal depths</a>. The benthic zone is the ecological region on, in and immediately above the seabed, including the sediment surface and some sub-surface layers. Benthos generally live in close relationship with the substrate bottom, and many such organisms are permanently attached to the bottom. The superficial layer of the soil lining the given body of water, the <a href="/wiki/Benthic_boundary_layer" title="Benthic boundary layer">benthic boundary layer</a>, is an integral part of the benthic zone, and greatly influences the biological activity which takes place there. Examples of contact soil layers include <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a> bottoms, rocky outcrops, <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">coral</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bay_mud" title="Bay mud">bay mud</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Seabed_features">Seabed features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seabed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Seabed features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pelagiczone.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1500" data-file-width="522" height="575" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Pelagiczone.svg/200px-Pelagiczone.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Pelagiczone.svg/300px-Pelagiczone.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Pelagiczone.svg/400px-Pelagiczone.svg.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pelagiczone.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Layers of the <a href="/wiki/Pelagic_zone" title="Pelagic zone">pelagic zone</a></div></div></div>
<p>Each area of the seabed has typical features such as common soil composition, typical topography, salinity of water layers above it, marine life, magnetic direction of rocks, and <a href="/wiki/Sedimentation" title="Sedimentation">sedimentation</a>.
</p><p>Seabed topography is flat where sedimentation is heavy and covers the tectonic features. Sediments comes from various sources:
</p>
<ul><li>Land erosion sediments, brought mainly by rivers</li>
<li>Underwater volcanic ash spreading, especially from hydrothermal vents</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Marine_microorganism" title="Marine microorganism">Microorganism</a> activity</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_current" title="Sea current">Sea currents</a> eroding the seabed itself</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Marine_life" title="Marine life">Marine life</a>: corals, fish, algae, crabs, marine plants and other biologically created sediment</li></ul>
<p>Where sedimentation is very light, such as in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_ocean" title="Atlantic ocean">Atlantic ocean</a>, especially in the northern and eastern Atlantic, the original tectonic activity can be clearly seen as straight line "cracks" or "vents" thousands of kilometers long.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (February 2017)">original research?</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Marine life is abundant in the <a href="/wiki/Deep_sea" title="Deep sea">deep sea</a>, especially around <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_vents" title="Hydrothermal vents">hydrothermal vents</a>. Large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deep_sea_communities" title="Deep sea communities">deep sea communities</a> of marine life have been discovered around <a href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent#Black_smokers_and_white_smokers" title="Hydrothermal vent">black and white smokers</a>—vents emitting chemicals toxic to humans and most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertebrates" title="Vertebrates">vertebrates</a>. This marine life receives its energy both from the extreme temperature difference (typically a drop of 150 degrees) and from <a href="/wiki/Chemosynthesis" title="Chemosynthesis">chemosynthesis</a> by <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Brine_pool" title="Brine pool">Brine pools</a> are another seabed feature,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> usually connected to <a href="/wiki/Cold_seep" title="Cold seep">cold seeps</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_exploration">History of exploration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seabed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History of exploration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Oceanography#History" title="Oceanography">Oceanography § History</a></div>
<p>The seabed has been explored by submersibles such as <a href="/wiki/DSV_Alvin" title="DSV Alvin">Alvin</a> and, to some extent, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scuba_divers" title="Scuba divers">scuba divers</a> with special equipment. The process that continually adds new material to the ocean floor is <a href="/wiki/Seafloor_spreading" title="Seafloor spreading">seafloor spreading</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_slope" title="Continental slope">continental slope</a>. In recent years satellite images show a very clear mapping of the seabed, and are used extensively in the study and exploration of the ocean floor.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Resources">Resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seabed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Seabed contains "several hundred years' worth of cobalt and nickel". In 2001-2013, <a href="/wiki/International_Seabed_Authority" title="International Seabed Authority">International Seabed Authority</a> issued 13 various licences to seabed mining.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_art_and_culture">In art and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seabed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In art and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some children's play songs include elements such as "There's a hole at the bottom of the sea", or "A sailor went to sea... but all that he could see was the bottom of the deep blue sea".
</p><p>On and under the seabed are archaeological sites of historic interest, such as shipwrecks and sunken towns. This underwater cultural heritage is protected by the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO_Convention_on_the_Protection_of_the_Underwater_Cultural_Heritage" title="UNESCO Convention on the Protection of the Underwater Cultural Heritage">UNESCO Convention on the Protection of the Underwater Cultural Heritage</a>. The convention aims at preventing looting and the destruction or loss of historic and cultural information by providing an international legal framework.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
