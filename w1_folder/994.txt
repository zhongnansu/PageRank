<p><b>BASF <a href="/wiki/Societas_Europaea" title="Societas Europaea">SE</a></b> is a German chemical company and the <a href="/wiki/List_of_largest_chemical_producers" title="List of largest chemical producers">largest chemical producer in the world</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The BASF Group comprises <a href="/wiki/Subsidiary" title="Subsidiary">subsidiaries</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joint_ventures" title="Joint ventures">joint ventures</a> in more than 80 countries and operates six integrated production sites and 390 other production sites in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Its <a href="/wiki/Headquarters" title="Headquarters">headquarters</a> is located in <a href="/wiki/Ludwigshafen" title="Ludwigshafen">Ludwigshafen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.  BASF has customers in over 190 countries and supplies products to a wide variety of industries.  Despite its size and global presence, BASF has received relatively little public attention since it abandoned manufacturing and selling BASF-branded <a href="/wiki/Consumer_electronics" title="Consumer electronics">consumer electronics</a> products in the 1990s.
</p><p>At the end of 2017, the company employed around 115,490 people, with over 52,000 in Germany alone.  In 2015, BASF posted sales of €70.4 billion and income from operations before special items of about €6.7 billion.  The company is currently expanding its international activities with a particular focus on Asia.  Between 1990 and 2005, the company invested €5.6 billion in Asia, for example in sites near <a href="/wiki/Nanjing" title="Nanjing">Nanjing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">Shanghai</a>, China and <a href="/wiki/Mangalore" title="Mangalore">Mangalore</a>, India.
</p><p>BASF is listed on the <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt_Stock_Exchange" title="Frankfurt Stock Exchange">Frankfurt Stock Exchange</a>, <a href="/wiki/London_Stock_Exchange" title="London Stock Exchange">London Stock Exchange</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zurich_Stock_Exchange" title="Zurich Stock Exchange">Zurich Stock Exchange</a>. The company delisted its <a href="/wiki/American_depositary_receipt" title="American depositary receipt">ADR</a> from the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> in September 2007. The company is a component of the <a href="/wiki/Euro_Stoxx_50" title="Euro Stoxx 50">Euro Stoxx 50</a> <a href="/wiki/Stock_market_index" title="Stock market index">stock market index</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BASF&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Basf_1865.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1465" data-file-width="2768" height="116" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Basf_1865.jpg/220px-Basf_1865.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Basf_1865.jpg/330px-Basf_1865.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Basf_1865.jpg/440px-Basf_1865.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Basf_1865.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>BASF Werk in Ludwigshafen, 1865</div></div></div>
<p>BASF (<b>B</b>adische <b>A</b>nilin und <b>S</b>oda <b>F</b>abrik, or, in English, Baden Aniline and Soda Factory) was founded on 6 April 1865 in <a href="/wiki/Mannheim" title="Mannheim">Mannheim</a>, in the German-speaking country of <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Baden" title="Grand Duchy of Baden">Baden</a> by <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Engelhorn" title="Friedrich Engelhorn">Friedrich Engelhorn</a>. It had been responsible for setting up a <a href="/wiki/Gasworks" title="Gasworks">gasworks</a> and street lighting for the town council in 1861. The gasworks produced <a href="/wiki/Tar" title="Tar">tar</a> as a byproduct, and Engelhorn used this for the production of <a href="/wiki/Dye" title="Dye">dyes</a>. BASF was set up in 1865 to produce other chemicals necessary for dye production, notably soda and acids. The plant, however, was erected on the other side of the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> river at <a href="/wiki/Ludwigshafen" title="Ludwigshafen">Ludwigshafen</a> because the town council of Mannheim was afraid that the air pollution of the chemical plant could bother the inhabitants of the town. In 1866 the dye production processes were also moved to the BASF site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ludewig_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ludewig-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dyes">Dyes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BASF&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dyes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BASF_Werk_Ludwigshafen_1881.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="650" data-file-width="1156" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/BASF_Werk_Ludwigshafen_1881.JPG/220px-BASF_Werk_Ludwigshafen_1881.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/BASF_Werk_Ludwigshafen_1881.JPG/330px-BASF_Werk_Ludwigshafen_1881.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/BASF_Werk_Ludwigshafen_1881.JPG/440px-BASF_Werk_Ludwigshafen_1881.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BASF_Werk_Ludwigshafen_1881.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>BASF Werk in Ludwigshafen, 1881</div></div></div>
<p>The discovery in 1857 by <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Perkin" title="William Henry Perkin">William Henry Perkin</a> that <a href="/wiki/Aniline" title="Aniline">aniline</a> could be used to make intense colouring agents had led to the commercial production of synthetic dyes in England from aniline extracted from coal tar. BASF recruited <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Caro" title="Heinrich Caro">Heinrich Caro</a>, a German chemist with experience of the dyestuffs industry in England, to be the first head of research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Caro developed a synthesis for <a href="/wiki/Alizarin" title="Alizarin">alizarin</a> (a natural pigment in <a href="/wiki/Rubia" title="Rubia">madder</a>), and applied for a British patent on 25 June 1869. Coincidentally Perkin applied for a virtually identical patent on 26 June 1869, and the two companies came to a mutual commercial agreement about the process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ludewig_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ludewig-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Further patents were granted for the synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Methylene_blue" title="Methylene blue">methylene blue</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eosin" title="Eosin">eosin</a>, and in 1880 research began to try to find a synthetic process for <a href="/wiki/Indigo_dye" title="Indigo dye">indigo dye</a>, though this was not successfully brought to the market until 1897. In 1901, some 80% of the BASF production was dyestuffs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ludewig_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ludewig-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soda">Soda</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BASF&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Soda">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hauptlaboratorim_BASF_1887.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="224" data-file-width="324" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Hauptlaboratorim_BASF_1887.JPG/220px-Hauptlaboratorim_BASF_1887.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d4/Hauptlaboratorim_BASF_1887.JPG 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hauptlaboratorim_BASF_1887.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>BASF main laboratory in Ludwigshafen, 1887</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">Sodium carbonate</a> (soda) was produced by the <a href="/wiki/Leblanc_process" title="Leblanc process">Leblanc process</a> until 1880 when the much cheaper <a href="/wiki/Solvay_process" title="Solvay process">Solvay process</a> became available. BASF ceased to make its own and bought it from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solvay_(company)" title="Solvay (company)">Solvay company</a> thereafter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ludewig_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ludewig-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sulfuric_acid">Sulfuric acid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BASF&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sulfuric acid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Indigoproduktion_BASF_1890.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="247" data-file-width="351" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Indigoproduktion_BASF_1890.JPG/220px-Indigoproduktion_BASF_1890.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Indigoproduktion_BASF_1890.JPG/330px-Indigoproduktion_BASF_1890.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6c/Indigoproduktion_BASF_1890.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Indigoproduktion_BASF_1890.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Indigo production at BASF in 1890</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">Sulfuric acid</a> was initially produced by the <a href="/wiki/Lead_chamber_process" title="Lead chamber process">lead chamber process</a>, but in 1890 a unit using the <a href="/wiki/Contact_process" title="Contact process">contact process</a> was brought on stream, producing the acid at higher concentration (98% instead of 80%), and at a lower cost. This followed extensive research and development by Rudolf Knietsch, for which he received the <a href="/wiki/Liebig_Medal" title="Liebig Medal">Liebig Medal</a> in 1904.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ludewig_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ludewig-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ammonia">Ammonia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BASF&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ammonia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The development of the <a href="/wiki/Haber_process" title="Haber process">Haber process</a> from 1908 to 1912 made it possible to synthesize <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> (a major industrial chemical as the primary source of nitrogen), and, after acquiring exclusive rights to the process, in 1913 BASF started a new production plant in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ludwigshafen-Oppau#Oppau" title="Ludwigshafen-Oppau">Oppau</a>, adding <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a> to its product range. BASF also acquired and began mining <a href="/wiki/Anhydrite" title="Anhydrite">anhydrite</a> for <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Kohnstein" title="Kohnstein">Kohnstein</a> in 1917.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ordway_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ordway-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="IG_Farben">IG Farben</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BASF&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: IG Farben">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1916, BASF started operations at a <a href="/wiki/Leuna_works" title="Leuna works">new site in Leuna</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Explosive" title="Explosive">explosives</a> were produced during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">First World War</a>. On 21 September 1921, an explosion occurred in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ludwigshafen-Oppau#Oppau" title="Ludwigshafen-Oppau">Oppau</a>, killing 565 people. The <a href="/wiki/Oppau_explosion" title="Oppau explosion">Oppau explosion</a> was the biggest industrial accident in German history. Under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Carl_Bosch" title="Carl Bosch">Carl Bosch</a> - a critic of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> policies - BASF founded <a href="/wiki/IG_Farben" title="IG Farben">IG Farben</a> with <a href="/wiki/Hoechst_AG" title="Hoechst AG">Hoechst</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bayer_AG" title="Bayer AG">Bayer</a>, and three other companies, thus losing its independence. BASF was the nominal survivor, as all shares were exchanged for BASF shares prior to the merger. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubber" title="Rubber">Rubber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuels</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Coating" title="Coating">coatings</a> were added to the range of products.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BASF&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following the appointment of <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> as Chancellor in 1933, IG Farben collaborated with the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi regime</a>, profiting from guaranteed volumes and prices, and from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slave_labor" title="Slave labor">slave labor</a> provided by the government's <a href="/wiki/Nazi_concentration_camps" title="Nazi concentration camps">Nazi concentration camps</a>. IG Farben also achieved notoriety owing to its production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zyklon-B" title="Zyklon-B">Zyklon-B</a>, the lethal gas used to murder prisoners in Nazi extermination camps. In 1935, IG Farben and <a href="/wiki/AEG" title="AEG">AEG</a> presented the <a href="/wiki/Magnetophon" title="Magnetophon">magnetophon</a> – the first <a href="/wiki/Tape_recorder" title="Tape recorder">tape recorder</a> – at the <a href="/wiki/Internationale_Funkausstellung_Berlin" title="Internationale Funkausstellung Berlin">Radio Exhibition</a> in <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Ludwigshafen site was almost completely destroyed during the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a> and was subsequently rebuilt. The <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">allies</a> dissolved IG Farben in November 1945.
</p><p>Both the Ludwigshafen and Oppau plants were of strategic importance for the war because the German military needed many of their products, e.g. synthetic rubber and gasoline. As a result, they were major targets for air raids. Over the course of the war, Allied bombers attacked the plants 65 times.
</p><p>Shelling took place from the autumn of 1943 and saturation bombing inflicted extensive damage. Production virtually stopped by the end of 1944.
</p><p>Due to a shortage of male workers during the war, women were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conscript" title="Conscript">conscripted</a> to work in the factories, and later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prisoners_of_war" title="Prisoners of war">prisoners of war</a> and foreign civilians. Concentration camp inmates did not work at the Ludwigshafen and Oppau plants.
</p><p>In July 1945, the American military administration confiscated the entire assets of IG Farben. That same year, the Allied Commission decreed that IG Farben should be dissolved. The sites at Ludwigshafen and Oppau were controlled by French authorities.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="BASF_refounded">BASF refounded</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BASF&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: BASF refounded">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 28 July 1948, an explosion occurred at a BASF site in <a href="/wiki/Ludwigshafen" title="Ludwigshafen">Ludwigshafen</a>, killing 207 people and injuring 3818.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1952, BASF was refounded under its own name following the efforts of former <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">Nazi Party</a> member and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_Reich" title="Third Reich">Third Reich</a> Wehrwirtschaftsführer (war economy leader) <a href="/wiki/Carl_Wurster" title="Carl Wurster">Carl Wurster</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> With the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_economic_miracle" title="German economic miracle">German economic miracle</a> in the 1950s, BASF added synthetics such as <a href="/wiki/Nylon" title="Nylon">nylon</a> to its product range. BASF developed <a href="/wiki/Polystyrene" title="Polystyrene">polystyrene</a> in the 1930s and invented Styropor in 1951.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Production_abroad">Production abroad</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BASF&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Production abroad">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 1960s, production abroad was expanded and plants were built in <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, Australia, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, France, India, Italy, Japan, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, Spain, United Kingdom and the United States. Following a change in corporate strategy in 1965, greater emphasis was placed on higher-value products such as coatings, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical" title="Pharmaceutical">pharmaceuticals</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pesticides" title="Pesticides">pesticides</a> and fertilizers. Following <a href="/wiki/German_reunification" title="German reunification">German reunification</a>, BASF acquired a site in <a href="/wiki/Schwarzheide" title="Schwarzheide">Schwarzheide</a>, eastern Germany, on 25 October 1990. It expanded to <a href="/wiki/Podolsk" title="Podolsk">Podolsk</a>, Russia, in 2012, and to <a href="/wiki/Kazan" title="Kazan">Kazan</a> in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-russia_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-russia-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Takeovers">Takeovers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BASF&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Takeovers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1968 BASF (together with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bayer_AG" title="Bayer AG">Bayer AG</a>) bought the German coatings company <a href="/wiki/Herbol" title="Herbol">Herbol</a>. BASF completely took over the Herbol branches in <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a> and <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Würzburg">Würzburg</a> in 1970. Under new management the renewal and expansion of the trademark continued. After an extensive reorganisation and an increasing international orientation of the coatings business Herbol became part of the new founded Deco GmbH in 1997.
</p><p>BASF bought the Wyandotte Chemical Company, and its Geismar, Louisiana chemical plant in the early 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-16">[16]</a></sup> The plant produced plastics, herbicides and antifreeze. BASF soon tried to operate union-free, having already reduced or eliminated union membership in several other US plants. Challenging the Geismar OCAW union resulted in a labor dispute that saw members locked out from 1984-1989 and eventually winning their case. A worker solidarity committee at BASF's headquarters plant in Ludwigshafen, Germany, took donations from German workers to support the American strikers and organized rallies and publicity in support. The dispute was the subject of an academic study<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The union also exposed major accidental releases of phosgene, toluene and other toxic gases, these being publicized in the local media and through a video, <i>Out of Control</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>. A court threw out a $66,700 fine against BASF for five environmental violations as "too small".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-auto-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>BASF's European coatings business was taken over by <a href="/wiki/AkzoNobel" title="AkzoNobel">AkzoNobel</a> in 1999. BASF bought the <a href="/wiki/Engelhard" title="Engelhard">Engelhard</a> Corporation for $4.8 billion in 2006. Other acquisitions in 2006 were the purchase of Johnson Polymer and the construction chemicals business of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Degussa" title="Degussa">Degussa</a>. 
</p><p>The acquisition of Johnson Polymer was completed on 1 July 2006. The purchase price was $470 million on a cash and debt-free basis. It provided BASF with a range of water-based <a href="/wiki/Resin" title="Resin">resins</a> that complements its portfolio of high solids and UV resins for the coatings and paints industry and strengthened the company’s market presence, particularly in North America.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CWRYBASF.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1063" data-file-width="978" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/CWRYBASF.JPG/200px-CWRYBASF.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/CWRYBASF.JPG/300px-CWRYBASF.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/CWRYBASF.JPG/400px-CWRYBASF.JPG 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CWRYBASF.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>BASF Portsmouth Site in the West Norfolk area of <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth,_Virginia" title="Portsmouth, Virginia">Portsmouth, Virginia</a>, United States. The plant is served by the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Railway" title="Commonwealth Railway">Commonwealth Railway</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The acquisition Degussa AG's construction chemicals business was completed in 2006. The purchase price for equity was about €2.2 billion. In addition, the transaction was associated with debt of €500 million.
</p><p>The company agreed to acquire <a href="/wiki/Ciba_Specialty_Chemicals" title="Ciba Specialty Chemicals">Ciba</a> (formerly part of <a href="/wiki/Novartis" title="Novartis">Ciba-Geigy</a>) in September 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The proposed deal was reviewed by the <a href="/wiki/European_Commissioner_for_Competition" title="European Commissioner for Competition">European Commissioner for Competition</a>. On 9 April 2009, the acquisition was officially completed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 19 December 2008, BASF acquired U.S.-based Whitmire Micro-Gen together with U.K.-based Sorex Ltd, Widnes, Great Britain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Sorex is a manufacturer of branded chemical and non-chemical products for professional pest management. In March 2007 Sorex was put up for sale with a price tag of about £100 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2015, BASF agreed to sell parts of its pharmaceutical ingredients business to <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Swiss</a> drug manufacturer Siegfried Holding for a fee of €270 million, including assumed debt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2017, BASF announced it would buy seed and herbicide businesses from <a href="/wiki/Bayer" title="Bayer">Bayer</a> for €5.9 billion ($7 billion), as part of its acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Monsanto" title="Monsanto">Monsanto</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Business_segments">Business segments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BASF&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Business segments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>BASF operates in a variety of markets. Its business is organized in the segments Chemicals, Plastics, Performance Products, Functional Solutions, Agricultural Solutions and Oil &amp; Gas. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemicals">Chemicals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BASF&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Chemicals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>BASF produces a wide range of chemicals, for example, solvents, amines, resins, glues, electronic-grade chemicals, <a href="/wiki/Industrial_gas" title="Industrial gas">industrial gases</a>, basic petrochemicals, and inorganic chemicals. The most important customers for this segment are the pharmaceutical, construction, textile, and automotive industries.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plastics">Plastics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BASF&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Plastics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>BASF's plastic products include high-performance materials in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermoplastics" title="Thermoplastics">thermoplastics</a>, foams, and urethanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Engineering Plastics</b><br/>
BASF's Engineering Plastics consists of the "4 Ultras" - Ultramid <a href="/wiki/Polyamide" title="Polyamide">polyamide</a> (PA) nylon-based resins, Ultradur, <a href="/wiki/Polybutylene_terephthalate" title="Polybutylene terephthalate">polybutylene terephthalate</a> (PBT), Ultraform, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyacetal" title="Polyacetal">polyacetal</a> (POM), and Ultrason, <a href="/wiki/Polysulfone" title="Polysulfone">polysulfone</a> (PSU) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyethersulfone" title="Polyethersulfone">polyethersulfone</a> (PES).
</p><p><b>Styrenics</b><br/>
BASF Styrenics consists of the Foams and Copolymers. BASF's <a href="/wiki/Polystyrene" title="Polystyrene">styrenic copolymers</a> have applications in electronics, building and construction, and automotive components. In 2011 BASF and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/INEOS" title="INEOS">INEOS</a> blended their global business activities in the fields of <a href="/wiki/Styrene" title="Styrene">styrene</a> monomers (SM), <a href="/wiki/Polystyrene" title="Polystyrene">polystyrene</a> (PS), <a href="/wiki/Acrylonitrile_butadiene_styrene" title="Acrylonitrile butadiene styrene">acrylonitrile butadiene styrene</a> (ABS), styrene butadiene copolymers (SBC) and other styrene-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copolymers" title="Copolymers">copolymers</a> (SAN, AMSAN, ASA, MABS) into a joint venture named <a href="/wiki/Styrolution" title="Styrolution">Styrolution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Polyurethanes</b><br/>
BASF's <a href="/wiki/Polyurethane" title="Polyurethane">Polyurethanes</a> business consists of diverse technologies and finished products. Urethane chemicals are raw materials used in rigid and flexible foams commonly used for insulation in the construction and appliance industries, furniture, packaging, and transportation.
</p><p><b>Foams</b><br/>
Foams like Styropor are generally used as insulating materials. They are eco-efficient and offer advantages over other materials in terms of cost-effectiveness, preservation of resources and environmental protection. Investments made for insulating materials usually pay for themselves within a short time and contribute to retaining and even enhancing the value of buildings.
</p><p><b>Polyamides and Intermediates</b><br/>
BASF manufactures polyamide precursors and <a href="/wiki/Polyamide" title="Polyamide">polyamide</a>.
</p><p><b>Biodegradable plastics</b><br/>
BASF developed a <a href="/wiki/Biodegradable_plastic" title="Biodegradable plastic">biodegradable plastic</a> with a high content of <a href="/wiki/Polylactic_acid" title="Polylactic acid">polylactic acid</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Performance_products">Performance products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BASF&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Performance products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>BASF produces a range of performance chemicals, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coatings" title="Coatings">coatings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Functional_polymers" title="Functional polymers">functional polymers</a>. These include raw materials for detergents, textile and leather chemicals, pigments and raw materials for adhesives, <a href="/wiki/Paper_chemicals" title="Paper chemicals">paper chemicals</a>. Customers are the automotive, oil, paper, packaging, textile, sanitary products, detergents, construction materials, coatings, printing and leather industries.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Functional_Solutions">Functional Solutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BASF&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Functional Solutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Basf-ludwigshafen-ww-1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="695" data-file-width="695" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Basf-ludwigshafen-ww-1.jpg/220px-Basf-ludwigshafen-ww-1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Basf-ludwigshafen-ww-1.jpg/330px-Basf-ludwigshafen-ww-1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Basf-ludwigshafen-ww-1.jpg/440px-Basf-ludwigshafen-ww-1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Basf-ludwigshafen-ww-1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>BASF in Ludwigshafen</div></div></div>
<p>BASF's Functional Solutions segment consists of the Catalysts, Construction Chemicals and Coatings divisions. These divisions develop innovative, customer-specific products and system solutions, in particular for the automotive and construction industries.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agricultural">Agricultural</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BASF&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Agricultural">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>BASF supplies <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agricultural" title="Agricultural">agricultural</a> products and chemicals including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungicides" title="Fungicides">fungicides</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbicides" title="Herbicides">herbicides</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insecticides" title="Insecticides">insecticides</a> and seed treatment products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The company also researches <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nutrigenomics" title="Nutrigenomics">nutrigenomics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ydtyg_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-ydtyg-31">[31]</a></sup> BASF opened a new crop protection technology center in Limburgerhof, Germany in 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biotechnology">Biotechnology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BASF&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Biotechnology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>BASF is cooperating with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monsanto_Company" title="Monsanto Company">Monsanto Company</a> in research, development and marketing of biotechnology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/BASF_Plant_Science" title="BASF Plant Science">BASF Plant Science</a> subsidiary produces the <a href="/wiki/Amflora" title="Amflora">Amflora</a> and Starch Potato <a href="/wiki/Genetically_engineered_potato" title="Genetically engineered potato">genetically modified potato</a> with reduced <a href="/wiki/Amylose" title="Amylose">amylose</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AM04-1020_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-AM04-1020-35">[35]</a></sup>  In 2010 BASF conducted Department of Environment, Food and Rural Affairs approved trials of genetically modified potatoes in the United Kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Starch Potato was authorised for use in USA<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AM04-1020_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-AM04-1020-35">[35]</a></sup> in 2014.
</p><p>Other GM crops are Phytaseed Canola varieties with <a href="/wiki/Phytase" title="Phytase">phytase</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfonylurea" title="Sulfonylurea">sulfonylurea</a> <a href="/wiki/Herbicide" title="Herbicide">herbicide</a> tolerant <a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">soybean</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> and drought tolerant corn (with cold shock protein B) developed with Monsanto.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oil_and_gas">Oil and gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BASF&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Oil and gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>BASF explores for and produces oil and gas through its subsidiary <a href="/wiki/Wintershall" title="Wintershall">Wintershall</a> Holding AG. In Central and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a>, Wintershall works with its Russian partner <a href="/wiki/Gazprom" title="Gazprom">Gazprom</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Investors">Investors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BASF&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Investors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>75% of the BASF shares are held by <a href="/wiki/Institutional_investor" title="Institutional investor">institutional investors</a> (<a href="/wiki/BlackRock" title="BlackRock">BlackRock</a> more than 5%). 36% of the shares are held in Germany, 11% in the UK and 17% in the U.S.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BASF&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BASF_Ludwigshafen_Panorama.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="571" data-file-width="2656" height="71" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/BASF_Ludwigshafen_Panorama.jpg/330px-BASF_Ludwigshafen_Panorama.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/BASF_Ludwigshafen_Panorama.jpg/495px-BASF_Ludwigshafen_Panorama.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/BASF_Ludwigshafen_Panorama.jpg/660px-BASF_Ludwigshafen_Panorama.jpg 2x" width="330"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BASF_Ludwigshafen_Panorama.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ludwigshafen production site at night.</div></div></div>
<p>BASF's recent success is characterized by a focus on creating resource efficient product lines after completely abandoning consumer products. This strategy was reflected in production by a re-focus towards integrated production sites. The largest such integrated production site is located in <a href="/wiki/Ludwigshafen" title="Ludwigshafen">Ludwigshafen</a> employing 33,000 people.
</p><p>Integrated production sites are characterized by co-location of a large number of individual production lines (producing a specific chemical), which share an interconnected material flow. Piping is used ubiquitously for volume materials. All production lines use common raw material sourcing and feed back waste resources, which can be used elsewhere (e.g. steam of various temperatures, sulfuric acid, carbon monoxide). The economic incentive for this approach is high resource and energy efficiency of the overall process, reduced shipping cost and associated reduced risk of accidents. Due to the high cost of such an integrated production site, it establishes a high entry barrier for competitors trying to enter the market for volume chemicals.
</p><p>BASF built a new chemical complex in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dahej" title="Dahej">Dahej</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gujarat" title="Gujarat">Gujarat</a> at a cost of $100 million. This facility has South Asia's first <a href="/wiki/Methylene_diphenyl_diisocyanate" title="Methylene diphenyl diisocyanate">methylene diphenyl diisocyanate</a> (MDI) splitter for processing crude MDI. BASF has 8 production facilities in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>BASF SE has succeeded in developing a semi-crystalline polyamide that allows light to pass through largely unhindered. Ultramid Vision combines very high light transmission with low light scattering. This makes it the world’s first semi-crystalline polyamide for semi-transparent or transparent components in chemically challenging environments. Additionally, the unique polyamide is UV and temperature resistant, scratch-proof as well as suitable for flame-retardant requirements. Ultramid Vision can be used in various application fields: It recommends itself especially for parts for visual check, illumination or light design. Ultramid Vision presents a versatile alternative to commonly used materials such as amorphous aliphatic polyamides, polycarbonate or styrene-acrylonitrile copolymers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_record">Environmental record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=BASF&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Environmental record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2006 BASF was included in the Climate Leadership Index for their efforts in relation to <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gas</a> emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>BASF has created filters for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wastewater_treatment_plants" title="Wastewater treatment plants">wastewater treatment plants</a> that help to reduce emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Independent_sources" title="Wikipedia:Independent sources"><span title="This claim needs a reference to a independent, third-party source. (June 2017)">third-party source needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The BASF Company and <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a> formed a partnership to further research “environmentally benign and <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_energy" title="Sustainable energy">sustainable energy</a> sources”. The company has recently reported their emissions in 2006 to be “1.50 million metric tons of waste,” which is a decrease from previous years. The amount of waste BASF produces has continued to fall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>While BASF publishes its environmental information in the US and Europe, <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a> has expressed deep concerns at BASF's refusal to release environmental information on its operations in China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2009, a BASF Plant in <a href="/wiki/Hannibal,_Missouri" title="Hannibal, Missouri">Hannibal, Missouri</a>, United States, accidentally discharged chromium into the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a>. The local Department of Natural Resources performed tests in December 2009 showing the <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a> levels did not exceed regulatory safety limits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> BASF worked with the <a href="/wiki/Missouri_Department_of_Natural_Resources" title="Missouri Department of Natural Resources">Missouri Department of Natural Resources</a> (MoDNR) to resolve questions regarding the elevated level of <a href="/wiki/Hexavalent_chromium" title="Hexavalent chromium">hexavalent chromium</a> that was detected in the <a href="/wiki/Effluent" title="Effluent">effluent</a> from one of its permitted outfalls into the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a>. The state department of health reviewed the test results and determined that the amounts found were well below recommended public health screening levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2013, BASF reported a spill of several hundred kilograms of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chelating_agent" title="Chelating agent">chelating agent</a> Trilon-B (tetrasodium <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edta" title="Edta">EDTA</a>) into the river <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> from BASF's headquarters in Ludwigshafen, Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
