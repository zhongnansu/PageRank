<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Liquid rocket propellant</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Liquid-propellant_rocket" title="Liquid-propellant rocket">Liquid-propellant rocket</a></div>
<p>The highest <a href="/wiki/Specific_impulse" title="Specific impulse">specific impulse</a> chemical <b>rockets</b> use <b>liquid propellants</b> (<a href="/wiki/Liquid-propellant_rocket" title="Liquid-propellant rocket">liquid-propellant rockets</a>). They can consist of a single chemical (a <b>monopropellant</b>) or a mix of two chemicals, called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bipropellants" title="Bipropellants">bipropellants</a>. Bipropellants can further be divided into two categories; <a href="/wiki/Hypergolic_propellant" title="Hypergolic propellant">hypergolic propellants</a>, which ignite when the fuel and oxidizer make contact, and non-hypergolic propellants which require an ignition source.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquid_rocket_propellant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>About 170 different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propellants" title="Propellants">propellants</a> made of <a href="/wiki/Liquid_fuel" title="Liquid fuel">liquid fuel</a> have been tested, excluding minor changes to a specific propellant such as propellant additives, corrosion inhibitors, or stabilizers. In the U.S. alone at least 25 different propellant combinations have been flown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> No completely new propellant has been used for nearly 30 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sutton_2010_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sutton_2010-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many factors go into choosing a propellant for a liquid propellant rocket engine. The primary factors include ease of operation, cost, hazards/environment and performance.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> They can consist of a single chemical, a <a href="/wiki/Monopropellant" title="Monopropellant">monopropellant</a>, or two, called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bipropellants" title="Bipropellants">bipropellants</a> or other mixtures. Bipropellants can be either <a href="/wiki/Hypergolic_propellant" title="Hypergolic propellant">hypergolic propellant</a> or nonhypergolic. A hypergolic combination of oxidizer and fuel will start to burn upon contact. A nonhypergolic needs an ignition source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquid_rocket_propellant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Early_development.2C_1926"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_development,_1926">Early development, 1926</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquid_rocket_propellant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early development, 1926">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Goddard_and_Rocket.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2946" data-file-width="2400" height="246" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Goddard_and_Rocket.jpg/200px-Goddard_and_Rocket.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Goddard_and_Rocket.jpg/300px-Goddard_and_Rocket.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Goddard_and_Rocket.jpg/400px-Goddard_and_Rocket.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Goddard_and_Rocket.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Robert_H._Goddard" title="Robert H. Goddard">Robert H. Goddard</a> on March 16, 1926, holding the launching frame of his most notable invention — the first liquid-fueled rocket.</div></div></div>
<p>On March 16, 1926, <a href="/wiki/Robert_H._Goddard" title="Robert H. Goddard">Robert H. Goddard</a> used <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">liquid oxygen</a> (<i>LOX</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rocket_fuel" title="Rocket fuel">rocket fuels</a> for his first partially successful <a href="/wiki/Liquid-propellant_rocket" title="Liquid-propellant rocket">liquid-propellant rocket</a> launch. Both propellants are readily available, cheap and highly energetic.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Oxygen is a moderate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogen" title="Cryogen">cryogen</a> as air will not liquefy against a liquid oxygen tank, so it is possible to store LOX briefly in a rocket without excessive insulation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II_era">World War II era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquid_rocket_propellant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: World War II era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Liquid_rocket_propellant&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <p>Germany had very active rocket development before and during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, both for the strategic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V-2" title="V-2">V-2</a> rocket and other missiles. The V-2 used an alcohol/LOX <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_propellant_engine" title="Liquid propellant engine">liquid propellant engine</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a> to drive the fuel pumps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The alcohol was mixed with water for engine cooling. Both Germany and the United States developed reusable liquid propellant rocket engines that used a storeable liquid oxidizer with much greater density than LOX and a liquid fuel that ignited spontaneously on contact with the high density oxidizer. The German engine was powered by hydrogen peroxide and a fuel mixture of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrazine_hydrate" title="Hydrazine hydrate">hydrazine hydrate</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methyl_alcohol" title="Methyl alcohol">methyl alcohol</a>. The U.S. engine was powered by <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> oxidizer and <a href="/wiki/Aniline" title="Aniline">aniline</a>. Both engines were used to power aircraft, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Me-163B_Komet" title="Me-163B Komet">Me-163B Komet</a> interceptor in the case of the German engine and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RATO" title="RATO">RATO</a> units to assist take-off of aircraft in the case of the U.S. engine.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1950s_and_1960s">1950s and 1960s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquid_rocket_propellant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1950s and 1960s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>During the 1950s and 1960s there was a great burst of activity by propellant chemists to find high-energy liquid and solid propellants better suited to the military. Large strategic missiles need to sit in land-based or submarine-based silos for many years, able to launch at a moment's notice. Propellants requiring continuous refrigeration, which cause their rockets to grow ever-thicker blankets of ice, were not practical. As the military was willing to handle and use hazardous materials, a great number of dangerous chemicals were brewed up in large batches, most of which wound up being deemed unsuitable for operational systems. 
In the case of <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a>, the acid itself (<span class="chemf nowrap">HNO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>) was unstable, and corroded most metals, making it difficult to store. The addition of a modest amount of <a href="/wiki/Dinitrogen_tetroxide" title="Dinitrogen tetroxide">nitrogen tetroxide</a>, <span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>, turned the mixture red and kept it from changing composition, but left the problem that nitric acid corrodes containers it is placed in, releasing gases that can build up pressure in the process. 
The breakthrough was the addition of a little <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">hydrogen fluoride</a> (HF), which forms a self-sealing metal fluoride on the interior of tank walls that <i>Inhibited</i> Red Fuming Nitric Acid. This made "IRFNA" storeable. Propellant combinations based on IRFNA or pure <span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> as oxidizer and kerosene or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypergolic" title="Hypergolic">hypergolic</a> (self igniting) <a href="/wiki/Aniline" title="Aniline">aniline</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrazine" title="Hydrazine">hydrazine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Unsymmetrical_dimethylhydrazine" title="Unsymmetrical dimethylhydrazine">unsymmetrical dimethylhydrazine</a> (UDMH) as fuel were then adopted in the United States and the Soviet Union for use in strategic and tactical missiles. The self-igniting storeable liquid bi-propellants have somewhat lower specific impulse than LOX/kerosene but have higher density so a greater mass of propellant can be placed in the same sized tanks. Gasoline was replaced by different <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a> fuels,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> for example <a href="/wiki/RP-1" title="RP-1">RP-1</a> - a highly refined grade of <a href="/wiki/Kerosene" title="Kerosene">kerosene</a>. This combination is quite practical for rockets that need not be stored.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kerosene">Kerosene</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquid_rocket_propellant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Kerosene">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Liquid_rocket_propellant&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span> <small><i>(December 2017)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The V-1 and V-2 rockets developed by Nazi Germany used LOX and ethyl alcohol. One of the main advantages of alcohol was its water content which provided cooling in larger rocket engines. Petroleum-based fuels offered more power than alcohol, but standard gasoline and kerosene left too much silt and combustion by-products that could clog engine plumbing. In addition they lacked the cooling properties of ethyl alcohol.
</p><p>During the early 1950s, the chemical industry in the US was assigned the task of formulating an improved petroleum-based rocket propellant which would not leave residue behind and also ensure that the engines would remain cool. The result was <a href="/wiki/RP-1" title="RP-1">RP-1</a>, the specifications of which were finalized by 1954. A highly refined form of jet fuel, RP-1 burned much more cleanly than conventional petroleum fuels and also posed less of a danger to ground personnel from explosive vapors. It became the propellant for most of the early American rockets and ballistic missiles such as the Atlas, Titan I, and Thor. The Soviets quickly adopted RP-1 for their R-7 missile, but the majority of Soviet launch vehicles ultimately used storable hypergolic propellants. As of  2017<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Liquid_rocket_propellant&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, it is used in the <a href="/wiki/Multistage_rocket" title="Multistage rocket">first stages</a> of many orbital launchers.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrogen">Hydrogen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquid_rocket_propellant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Hydrogen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many early rocket theorists believed that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_gas" title="Hydrogen gas">hydrogen</a> would be a marvelous propellant, since it gives the highest <a href="/wiki/Specific_impulse" title="Specific impulse">specific impulse</a>. It is also considered the cleanest when oxidized with <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> because the only by-product is water. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_gas" title="Hydrogen gas">Hydrogen gas</a> is commercially produced by the fuel-rich burning of natural gas. Carbon forms a stronger bond with oxygen so the gaseous hydrogen is left behind. 
</p><p>Hydrogen in any state is very bulky; it is typically stored as a deeply cryogenic liquid, a technique mastered in the early 1950s as part of the <a href="/wiki/Thermonuclear_weapon#American_developments" title="Thermonuclear weapon">hydrogen bomb development program</a> at <a href="/wiki/Los_Alamos_National_Laboratory" title="Los Alamos National Laboratory">Los Alamos</a>. <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">Liquid hydrogen</a> is stored and transported without boil-off, because <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>, which has a lower boiling point than hydrogen, acts as cooling refrigerant. Only when hydrogen is loaded on a launch vehicle, where no refrigeration exists, it vents to the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the late 1950s and early 1960s it was adopted for hydrogen fueled stages such as <a href="/wiki/Centaur_(rocket_stage)" title="Centaur (rocket stage)">Centaur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saturn_I" title="Saturn I">Saturn</a> upper stages.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Even as a liquid, hydrogen has low density, requiring large tanks and pumps, and the extreme cold requires tank insulation. This extra weight reduces the mass fraction of the stage or requires extraordinary measures such as pressure stabilization of the tanks to reduce weight. Pressure stabilized tanks support most of the loads with internal pressure rather than with solid structures.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The Soviet rocket program, in part due to a lack of technical capabilities, did not use <span class="chemf nowrap">LH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> as a propellant until the 1980s when it was used for the Energiya core stage.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Upper_stage_use">Upper stage use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquid_rocket_propellant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Upper stage use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The liquid rocket engine propellant combination of <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">liquid oxygen</a> and hydrogen offers the highest specific impulse of currently used conventional rockets. This extra performance largely offsets the disadvantage of low density. Low density of a propellant leads to larger fuel tanks. However, a small increase in specific impulse in an upper stage application can have a significant increase in payload to orbit capability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sutton_2010_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sutton_2010-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_to_kerosene">Comparison to kerosene</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquid_rocket_propellant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Comparison to kerosene">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Liquid_rocket_propellant&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span> <small><i>(March 2017)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Launch pad fires due to spilled kerosene are more damaging than hydrogen fires, primarily for two reasons. First, kerosene burns about 20% hotter in absolute temperature than hydrogen. The second reason is its buoyancy. Since hydrogen is a deep cryogen it boils quickly and rises due to its very low density as a gas. Even when hydrogen burns, the <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">gaseous <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O</span></a> that is formed has a molecular weight of only 18 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_mass_unit" title="Atomic mass unit">u</a> compared to 29.9 u for air, so it rises quickly as well. Kerosene on the other hand falls to the ground and burns for hours when spilled in large quantities, unavoidably causing extensive heat damage that requires time consuming repairs and rebuilding. This is a lesson most frequently experienced by test stand crews involved with firings of large, unproven rocket engines. Hydrogen-fueled engines have special design requirements such as running propellant lines horizontally, so traps do not form in the lines and cause ruptures due to boiling in confined spaces. These considerations apply to all cryogens, such as liquid oxygen and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_natural_gas" title="Liquid natural gas">liquid natural gas</a> (LNG) as well. Use of liquid hydrogen fuel has an excellent safety record and superb performance that is well above that of all other practical chemical rocket propellants.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lithium_and_fluorine">Lithium and fluorine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquid_rocket_propellant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Lithium and fluorine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Liquid_rocket_propellant&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span> <small><i>(March 2017)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The highest specific impulse chemistry ever test-fired in a rocket engine was <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a>, with hydrogen added to improve the exhaust thermodynamics (all propellants had to be kept in their own tanks, making this a <a href="/wiki/Tripropellant_rocket" title="Tripropellant rocket">tripropellant</a>). The combination delivered 542 s specific impulse in a vacuum, equivalent to an exhaust velocity of 5320 m/s. The impracticality of this chemistry highlights why exotic propellants are not actually used: to make all three components liquids, the hydrogen must be kept below –252 °C (just 21 K) and the lithium must be kept above 180 °C (453 K). Lithium and fluorine are both extremely corrosive, lithium ignites on contact with air, fluorine ignites on contact with most fuels, including hydrogen. Fluorine and the hydrogen fluoride (HF) in the exhaust are very toxic, which makes working around the launch pad difficult, damages the environment, and makes getting a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Launch_license" title="Launch license">launch license</a> that much more difficult. Both lithium and fluorine are expensive compared to most rocket propellants. This combination has therefore never flown.
</p><p>During the 1950s, the Department of Defense initially proposed lithium/fluorine as ballistic missile propellants. A 1954 accident at a chemical works where a cloud of fluorine was released into the atmosphere convinced them to instead use LOX/RP-1.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methane">Methane</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquid_rocket_propellant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Methane">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In November 2012, <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a> CEO <a href="/wiki/Elon_Musk" title="Elon Musk">Elon Musk</a> announced plans to develop <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_methane_rocket_fuel" title="Liquid methane rocket fuel">liquid methane</a>/LOX rocket engines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fg20121120_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-fg20121120-6">[6]</a></sup> It had previously used only <a href="/wiki/RP-1" title="RP-1">RP-1</a>/LOX in <a href="/wiki/SpaceX_rocket_engines" title="SpaceX rocket engines">SpaceX rocket engines</a>. As of  March 2014<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Liquid_rocket_propellant&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, SpaceX was developing the <a href="/wiki/Raptor_(rocket_engine_family)" title="Raptor (rocket engine family)">Raptor</a> methalox bipropellant rocket engine, which by 2016 was predicted to generate 3,000 kN (670,000 lbf) of thrust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nsf20161003_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-nsf20161003-7">[7]</a></sup> The engine was slated to be used on a future super-heavy rocket, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MCT_launch_vehicle" title="MCT launch vehicle">MCT launch vehicle</a> now designated the <a href="/wiki/BFR_(rocket)" title="BFR (rocket)">BFR</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pbt20140219_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-pbt20140219-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nsf20140307_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-nsf20140307-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 2014, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Firefly_Space_Systems" title="Firefly Space Systems">Firefly Space Systems</a> announced their plans to use methane fuel for their small satellite launch vehicle, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Firefly_Space_Systems#Firefly_Alpha" title="Firefly Space Systems">Firefly Alpha</a> with an <a href="/wiki/Aerospike_engine" title="Aerospike engine">aerospike</a> engine design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>In September 2014, <a href="/wiki/Blue_Origin" title="Blue Origin">Blue Origin</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Launch_Alliance" title="United Launch Alliance">United Launch Alliance</a> announced the joint development of the <a href="/wiki/BE-4" title="BE-4">BE-4 LOX/LNG engine</a>. The BE-4 will provide 2,400 kN (550,000 lbf) of thrust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>

<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/Kerosene" title="Kerosene">Kerosene</a> (RP-1) / <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_Oxygen" title="Liquid Oxygen">Liquid Oxygen</a> (LOX)</dt>
<dd>Used for the lower stages of the <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_(rocket)" title="Soyuz (rocket)">Soyuz</a> boosters, and the first stage of the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atlas_(rocket_family)" title="Atlas (rocket family)">Atlas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Falcon_9" title="Falcon 9">Falcon 9</a> boosters.  Very similar to Robert Goddard's first rocket.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">Liquid hydrogen</a> (LH) / LOX</dt>
<dd>Used in the stages of the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Space_Launch_System" title="Space Launch System">Space Launch System</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ariane_5" title="Ariane 5">Ariane 5</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delta_IV" title="Delta IV">Delta IV</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Shepard" title="New Shepard">New Shepard</a>, <a href="/wiki/H-IIB" title="H-IIB">H-IIB</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GSLV" title="GSLV">GSLV</a> and <a href="/wiki/Centaur_(rocket_stage)" title="Centaur (rocket stage)">Centaur</a>.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Unsymmetrical_dimethylhydrazine" title="Unsymmetrical dimethylhydrazine">Unsymmetrical dimethylhydrazine</a> (UDMH or MMH) / <a href="/wiki/Dinitrogen_tetroxide" title="Dinitrogen tetroxide">Dinitrogen tetroxide</a> (NTO or <span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>)</dt>
<dd>Used in three first stages of the Russian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proton_(rocket)" title="Proton (rocket)">Proton booster</a>, Indian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vikas_engine" title="Vikas engine">Vikas engine</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PSLV" title="PSLV">PSLV</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GSLV" title="GSLV">GSLV</a> rockets, most Chinese boosters, a number of military, orbital and deep space rockets, as this fuel combination is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypergolic" title="Hypergolic">hypergolic</a> and storable for long periods at reasonable temperatures and pressures.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Hydrazine" title="Hydrazine">Hydrazine</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>)</dt>
<dd>Used in deep space missions because it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Storable_propellant" title="Storable propellant">storable</a> and hypergolic, and can be used as a monopropellant with a catalyst.</dd>
<dt><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerozine-50" title="Aerozine-50">Aerozine-50</a> (50/50 hydrazine and UDMH)</dt>
<dd>Used in deep space missions because it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Storable_propellant" title="Storable propellant">storable</a> and hypergolic, and can be used as a monopropellant with a catalyst.</dd></dl>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquid_rocket_propellant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl><dt><i>V<sub>e</sub></i></dt>
<dd>Average exhaust velocity, m/s. The same measure as specific impulse in different units, numerically equal to specific impulse in N·s/kg.</dd>
<dt><i>r</i></dt>
<dd>Mixture ratio: mass oxidizer / mass fuel</dd>
<dt><i>T<sub>c</sub></i></dt>
<dd>Chamber temperature, °C</dd>
<dt><i>d</i></dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Bulk_density" title="Bulk density">Bulk density</a> of fuel and oxidizer, g/cm³</dd>
<dt><i>C*</i></dt>
<dd>Characteristic velocity, m/s. Equal to chamber pressure multiplied by throat area, divided by <a href="/wiki/Mass_flow_rate" title="Mass flow rate">mass flow rate</a>. Used to check experimental rocket's combustion efficiency.</dd></dl>

<dd>See MIL-P-25576C, basically kerosene (approximately <span class="chemf nowrap">C<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">10</sub></span>H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">18</sub></span></span>)</dd>

<dd>Characteristic velocity, m/s. Equal to chamber pressure multiplied by throat area, divided by <a href="/wiki/Mass_flow_rate" title="Mass flow rate">mass flow rate</a>. Used to check experimental rocket's combustion efficiency.</dd>

<dd>Chamber temperature, °C</dd>
