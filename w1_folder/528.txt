<p><b><i>tert</i>-Butylthiol</b>, also known as <b>2-methylpropane-2-thiol</b>, <b>2-methyl-2-propanethiol</b>, <b><i>tert</i>-butyl mercaptan (TBM)</b>, and <b><i>t</i>-BuSH</b>, is an organosulfur compound with the formula (CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>CSH.  This <a href="/wiki/Thiol" title="Thiol">thiol</a> is used as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Odorant" title="Odorant">odorant</a> for <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, which is otherwise odorless.  It may also have been used as a flavoring agent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tert-Butylthiol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At least one publication has listed <i>tert</i>-butylthiol as a very minor component of cooked potatoes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> but because the <i>tert</i>-butyl moiety is very rare in natural products, other sources doubt the existence of natural sources of the compound<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (September 2015)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup>.  The compound was first prepared in 1890 by Leonard Dobbin<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> by the reaction of zinc sulfide and <i>t</i>-butyl chloride.
</p><p>The compound was later prepared in 1932 by the reaction of the Grignard reagent, <i>t</i>-BuMgCl, with sulfur to give the corresponding thiolate, followed by <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This preparation is shown below:
</p>
<dl><dd><i>t</i>-BuMgCl  +  S   →   <i>t</i>-BuSMgCl</dd>
<dd><i>t</i>-BuSMgCl  +  H<sub>2</sub>O   →   <i>t</i>-BuSH  +  Mg(OH)Cl</dd></dl>
<p>It is currently prepared industrially by the reaction of <a href="/wiki/Isobutylene" title="Isobutylene">isobutylene</a> with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a> over a clay (silica alumina) catalyst.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tert-Butylthiol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>tert</i>-Butylthiol can react with metal <a href="/wiki/Alkoxide" title="Alkoxide">alkoxides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Acyl_chloride" title="Acyl chloride">acyl chlorides</a> to form thiol esters, as shown in the equation:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spessard_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-spessard-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Thallium(I)_2-methylpropane-2-thiolate_synthesis.svg" title="Reaction of tert-butylthiol with an acyl chloride."><img alt="Reaction of tert-butylthiol with an acyl chloride." data-file-height="77" data-file-width="420" height="92" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Thallium%28I%29_2-methylpropane-2-thiolate_synthesis.svg/500px-Thallium%28I%29_2-methylpropane-2-thiolate_synthesis.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Thallium%28I%29_2-methylpropane-2-thiolate_synthesis.svg/750px-Thallium%28I%29_2-methylpropane-2-thiolate_synthesis.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Thallium%28I%29_2-methylpropane-2-thiolate_synthesis.svg/1000px-Thallium%28I%29_2-methylpropane-2-thiolate_synthesis.svg.png 2x" width="500"/></a></dd>
<dd><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Thallium(I)_2-methylpropane-2-thiolate_reaction.svg" title="Reaction of tert-butylthiol with an acyl chloride."><img alt="Reaction of tert-butylthiol with an acyl chloride." data-file-height="92" data-file-width="326" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Thallium%28I%29_2-methylpropane-2-thiolate_reaction.svg/500px-Thallium%28I%29_2-methylpropane-2-thiolate_reaction.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Thallium%28I%29_2-methylpropane-2-thiolate_reaction.svg/750px-Thallium%28I%29_2-methylpropane-2-thiolate_reaction.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Thallium%28I%29_2-methylpropane-2-thiolate_reaction.svg/1000px-Thallium%28I%29_2-methylpropane-2-thiolate_reaction.svg.png 2x" width="500"/></a></dd></dl>
<p>In the reaction above, thallium(I) ethoxide converts to thallium(I) <i>t</i>-butylthiolate.   In the presence of <a href="/wiki/Diethyl_ether" title="Diethyl ether">diethyl ether</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thallium" title="Thallium">thallium</a>(I) <i>t</i>-butylthiolate reacts with <a href="/wiki/Acyl_chloride" title="Acyl chloride">acyl chlorides</a> to give the corresponding tert-butyl thioesters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spessard_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-spessard-6">[6]</a></sup>  Like other thioesters, it reverts to tert-butylthiol by hydrolysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Lithium 2-methylpropane-2-thiolate can be prepared by treatment of <i>tert</i>-butylthiol with <a href="/wiki/Lithium_hydride" title="Lithium hydride">lithium hydride</a> in an aprotic solvent such as <a href="/wiki/Hexamethylphosphoramide" title="Hexamethylphosphoramide">hexamethylphosphoramide</a> (HMPA).  The resulting thiolate salt is a useful <a href="/wiki/Demethylation" title="Demethylation">demethylating</a> reagent.  For example, treatment with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/7-methylguanosine" title="7-methylguanosine">7-methylguanosine</a> gives <a href="/wiki/Guanosine" title="Guanosine">guanosine</a>.  Other <i>N</i>-methylated nucleosides in tRNA are not <a href="/wiki/Demethylation" title="Demethylation">demethylated</a> by this reagent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:T-BuSH_reaction_tRNA.png" title="Lithium 2-methylpropane-2-thiolate reaction with 7-methylguanosine."><img alt="Lithium 2-methylpropane-2-thiolate reaction with 7-methylguanosine." data-file-height="713" data-file-width="1965" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/T-BuSH_reaction_tRNA.png/450px-T-BuSH_reaction_tRNA.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/T-BuSH_reaction_tRNA.png/675px-T-BuSH_reaction_tRNA.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/T-BuSH_reaction_tRNA.png/900px-T-BuSH_reaction_tRNA.png 2x" width="450"/></a></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Metal_complexes">Metal complexes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tert-Butylthiol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Metal complexes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The anion derived from <i>tert</i>-butylthiol forms complexes with various metals.  One example is tetrakis(<i>tert</i>-butylthiolato)molybdenum(IV), Mo(<i>t</i>-BuS)<sub>4</sub>.  This complex was prepared by treating MoCl<sub>4</sub> with <i>t</i>-BuSLi:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-otsuka_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-otsuka-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>MoCl<sub>4</sub>  +  4 <i>t</i>-BuSLi   →   Mo(<i>t</i>-BuS)<sub>4</sub>  +  4 LiCl</dd></dl>
<p>Mo(<i>t</i>-BuS)<sub>4</sub> is a dark red <a href="/wiki/Diamagnetism" title="Diamagnetism">diamagnetic</a> complex that is sensitive to air and moisture. The <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum" title="Molybdenum">molybdenum</a> center has a distorted tetrahedral coordination to four sulfur atoms, with overall <i>D</i><sub>2</sub> symmetry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-otsuka_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-otsuka-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mo(t-BuS)4.png" title="Mo(t-BuS)4"><img alt="Mo(t-BuS)4" data-file-height="413" data-file-width="707" height="117" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Mo%28t-BuS%294.png/200px-Mo%28t-BuS%294.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Mo%28t-BuS%294.png/300px-Mo%28t-BuS%294.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Mo%28t-BuS%294.png/400px-Mo%28t-BuS%294.png 2x" width="200"/></a></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tert-Butylthiol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Even in well ventilated areas, extreme caution must be made when handling <i>tert</i>-butylthiol as it is a highly odorous chemical with an odor threshold of &lt;0.33 ppb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>  Extreme caution is not due to toxicity, but due to the significant odor and the concern this odor could cause to individuals that might be exposed.  The <a href="/wiki/Permissible_exposure_limit" title="Permissible exposure limit">PEL</a> for thiols of most types is 500 ppb<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, primarily due to reaction of nausea at levels of 2–3 ppm.  The LC<sub>50</sub> of tert-butylthiol is much, much higher.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_use">Commercial use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tert-Butylthiol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Commercial use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>tert</i>-Butylthiol is the main ingredient in many gas odorant blends.  It is always utilized as a blend of other compounds, typically <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfide" title="Dimethyl sulfide">dimethyl sulfide</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Methyl_ethyl_sulfide&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Methyl ethyl sulfide (page does not exist)">methyl ethyl sulfide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tetrahydrothiophene" title="Tetrahydrothiophene">tetrahydrothiophene</a> or other mercaptans such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Isopropyl_mercaptan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Isopropyl mercaptan (page does not exist)">isopropyl mercaptan</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sec-Butyl_mercaptan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sec-Butyl mercaptan (page does not exist)"><i>sec</i>-butyl mercaptan</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Butanethiol" title="Butanethiol"><i>n</i>-butyl mercaptan</a>, due to its rather high melting point of −0.5 °C (31.1 °F).  These blends are used only with natural gas and not propane, as the boiling points of these blends and propane are quite different.  Because propane is delivered as a liquid and vaporizes to gas when it is delivered to the appliance, the vapor liquid equilibrium would substantially reduce the amount of odorant blend in the vapor.
</p><p><i>tert</i>-Butylthiol has been listed on the <a href="/wiki/European_Food_Safety_Authority" title="European Food Safety Authority">European Food Safety Authority</a> (FL-no: 12.174) as a flavor additive.  There is no indication of what flavor(s) it may have been used in.  It has been removed from this list.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
