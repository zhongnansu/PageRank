<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Blast furnace</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alto_Horno,_Puerto_de_Sagunto,_Espa%C3%B1a,_2015-01-04,_DD_91.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4830" data-file-width="3095" height="343" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Alto_Horno%2C_Puerto_de_Sagunto%2C_Espa%C3%B1a%2C_2015-01-04%2C_DD_91.JPG/220px-Alto_Horno%2C_Puerto_de_Sagunto%2C_Espa%C3%B1a%2C_2015-01-04%2C_DD_91.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Alto_Horno%2C_Puerto_de_Sagunto%2C_Espa%C3%B1a%2C_2015-01-04%2C_DD_91.JPG/330px-Alto_Horno%2C_Puerto_de_Sagunto%2C_Espa%C3%B1a%2C_2015-01-04%2C_DD_91.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Alto_Horno%2C_Puerto_de_Sagunto%2C_Espa%C3%B1a%2C_2015-01-04%2C_DD_91.JPG/440px-Alto_Horno%2C_Puerto_de_Sagunto%2C_Espa%C3%B1a%2C_2015-01-04%2C_DD_91.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alto_Horno,_Puerto_de_Sagunto,_Espa%C3%B1a,_2015-01-04,_DD_91.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Former blast furnace in Port of Sagunto, Valencia, Spain.</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>blast furnace</b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Metallurgy" title="Metallurgy">metallurgical</a> <a href="/wiki/Furnace" title="Furnace">furnace</a> used for <a href="/wiki/Smelting" title="Smelting">smelting</a> to produce industrial metals, generally <a href="/wiki/Pig_iron" title="Pig iron">pig iron</a>, but also others such as <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> or <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>.  <i>Blast</i> refers to the combustion air being "forced" or supplied above atmospheric pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In a blast furnace fuel (<a href="/wiki/Coke_(fuel)" title="Coke (fuel)">coke</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ores" title="Ores">ores</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Flux_(metallurgy)" title="Flux (metallurgy)">flux</a> (limestone) are continuously supplied through the top of the furnace, while a hot blast of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a> (sometimes with <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> enrichment) is blown into the lower section of the furnace through a series of pipes called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuyeres" title="Tuyeres">tuyeres</a>, so that the <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace#Process_engineering_and_chemistry" title="Blast furnace">chemical reactions</a> take place throughout the furnace as the material falls downward. The end products are usually molten <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slag" title="Slag">slag</a> phases tapped from the bottom, and <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">flue gases</a> exiting from the top of the furnace. The downward flow of the ore and flux in contact with an upflow of hot, carbon monoxide-rich combustion gases is a <a href="/wiki/Countercurrent_exchange" title="Countercurrent exchange">countercurrent exchange</a> and chemical reaction process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In contrast, air furnaces (such as <a href="/wiki/Reverberatory_furnace" title="Reverberatory furnace">reverberatory furnaces</a>) are naturally aspirated, usually by the convection of hot gases in a chimney flue. According to this broad definition, <a href="/wiki/Bloomery" title="Bloomery">bloomeries</a> for iron, <a href="/wiki/Blowing_house" title="Blowing house">blowing houses</a> for <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smelt_mill" title="Smelt mill">smelt mills</a> for <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> would be classified as blast furnaces. However, the term has usually been limited to those used for smelting <a href="/wiki/Iron_ore" title="Iron ore">iron ore</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Pig_iron" title="Pig iron">pig iron</a>, an intermediate material used in the production of commercial iron and <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>, and the shaft furnaces used in combination with <a href="/wiki/Sinter_plant" title="Sinter plant">sinter plants</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Base_metals" title="Base metals">base metals</a> smelting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blast_furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yuan_Dynasty_-_waterwheels_and_smelting.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="592" data-file-width="800" height="185" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Yuan_Dynasty_-_waterwheels_and_smelting.png/250px-Yuan_Dynasty_-_waterwheels_and_smelting.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Yuan_Dynasty_-_waterwheels_and_smelting.png/375px-Yuan_Dynasty_-_waterwheels_and_smelting.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Yuan_Dynasty_-_waterwheels_and_smelting.png/500px-Yuan_Dynasty_-_waterwheels_and_smelting.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yuan_Dynasty_-_waterwheels_and_smelting.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An illustration of furnace bellows operated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waterwheel" title="Waterwheel">waterwheels</a>, from the <i>Nong Shu</i>, by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wang_Zhen_(official)" title="Wang Zhen (official)">Wang Zhen</a>, 1313, during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yuan_Dynasty" title="Yuan Dynasty">Yuan Dynasty</a> of China</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chinese_Fining_and_Blast_Furnace.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="625" data-file-width="794" height="197" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Chinese_Fining_and_Blast_Furnace.jpg/250px-Chinese_Fining_and_Blast_Furnace.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Chinese_Fining_and_Blast_Furnace.jpg/375px-Chinese_Fining_and_Blast_Furnace.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Chinese_Fining_and_Blast_Furnace.jpg/500px-Chinese_Fining_and_Blast_Furnace.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chinese_Fining_and_Blast_Furnace.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chinese fining and blast furnace, <i><a href="/wiki/Tiangong_Kaiwu" title="Tiangong Kaiwu">Tiangong Kaiwu</a></i>, 1637.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_ferrous_metallurgy" title="History of ferrous metallurgy">History of ferrous metallurgy</a></div>
<p>Cast iron has been found in China dating to the 5th century BC, but the earliest extant blast furnaces in China date to the 1st century AD and in the West from the <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">High Middle Ages</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Golas1999_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Golas1999-5">[5]</a></sup>  They spread from the region around <a href="/wiki/Namur_(province)" title="Namur (province)">Namur</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wallonia" title="Wallonia">Wallonia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>) in the late 15th century, being introduced to England in 1491. The fuel used in these was invariably <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a>. The successful substitution of <a href="/wiki/Coke_(fuel)" title="Coke (fuel)">coke</a> for charcoal is widely attributed to English inventor <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Darby_I" title="Abraham Darby I">Abraham Darby</a> in 1709. The efficiency of the process was further enhanced by the practice of preheating the combustion air (<a href="/wiki/Hot_blast" title="Hot blast">hot blast</a>), patented by Scottish inventor <a href="/wiki/James_Beaumont_Neilson" title="James Beaumont Neilson">James Beaumont Neilson</a> in 1828.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blast_furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_metallurgy_in_China" title="History of metallurgy in China">History of metallurgy in China</a></div>
<p>Archaeological evidence shows that bloomeries appeared in China around 800 BC. Originally it was thought that the Chinese started casting iron right from the beginning, but this theory has since been debunked by the discovery of 'more than ten' iron digging implements found in the tomb of <a href="/wiki/Duke_Jing_of_Qin" title="Duke Jing of Qin">Duke Jing of Qin</a> (d. 537 BC), whose tomb is located in <a href="/wiki/Fengxiang_County" title="Fengxiang County">Fengxiang County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shaanxi" title="Shaanxi">Shaanxi</a> (a museum exists on the site today).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> There is however no evidence of the bloomery in China after the appearance of the blast furnace and cast iron. In China blast furnaces produced cast iron, which was then either converted into finished implements in a cupola furnace, or turned into wrought iron in a fining hearth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWagner2008230_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWagner2008230-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">cast iron</a> farm tools and weapons were widespread in China by the 5th century BC, employing workforces of over 200 men in iron smelters from the 3rd century onward, the earliest extant blast furnaces were built date to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_Dynasty" title="Han Dynasty">Han Dynasty</a> in the 1st century AD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ebrey_30_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ebrey_30-9">[9]</a></sup> These early furnaces had clay walls and used <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>-containing minerals as a <a href="/wiki/Flux_(metallurgy)" title="Flux (metallurgy)">flux</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Chinese blast furnaces ranged from around two to ten meters in height, depending on the region. The largest ones were found in modern <a href="/wiki/Sichuan" title="Sichuan">Sichuan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guangdong" title="Guangdong">Guangdong</a>, while the 'dwarf" blast furnaces were found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dabieshan" title="Dabieshan">Dabieshan</a>. In construction, they are both around the same level of technological sophistication <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWagner20086_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWagner20086-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>The effectiveness of the Chinese blast furnace was enhanced during this period by the engineer <a href="/wiki/Du_Shi" title="Du Shi">Du Shi</a> (c. AD 31), who applied the power of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waterwheel" title="Waterwheel">waterwheels</a> to <a href="/wiki/Piston" title="Piston">piston</a>-<a href="/wiki/Bellows" title="Bellows">bellows</a> in forging cast iron.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Donald Wagner suggests that early blast furnace and cast iron production evolved from furnaces used to melt bronze. Certainly, though, iron was essential to military success by the time the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_of_Qin" title="State of Qin">State of Qin</a> had unified China (221 BC). Usage of the blast and cupola furnace remained widespread during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Song_Dynasty" title="Song Dynasty">Song</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tang_Dynasty" title="Tang Dynasty">Tang Dynasties</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Coming_of_the_Ages_of_Steel_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Coming_of_the_Ages_of_Steel-13">[13]</a></sup> By the 11th century, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Song_Dynasty" title="Song Dynasty">Song Dynasty</a> Chinese iron industry made a switch of resources from <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a> to <a href="/wiki/Coke_(fuel)" title="Coke (fuel)">coke</a> in casting iron and steel, sparing thousands of acres of woodland from felling. This may have happened as early as the 4th century AD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>The primary advantage of the early blast furnace was in large scale production and making iron implements more readily available to peasants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWagner2008169_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWagner2008169-16">[16]</a></sup> Cast iron is more brittle than wrought iron or steel, which required additional fining and then cementation or co-fusion to produce, but for menial activities such as farming it sufficed. By using the blast furnace, it was possible to produce larger quantities of tools such as ploughshares more efficiently than the bloomery. In areas where quality was important, such as warfare, wrought iron and steel were preferred. Nearly all Han period weapons are made of wrought iron or steel, with the exception of axe-heads, of which many are made of cast iron.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWagner20081_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWagner20081-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Blast furnaces were also later used to produce gunpowder weapons such as cast iron bomb shells and cast iron cannons during the <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">Song dynasty</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELiang2006_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELiang2006-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_Europe">Medieval Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blast_furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Medieval Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Catalan_forges">Catalan forges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blast_furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Catalan forges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The simplest forge, known as the Corsican, was used prior to the advent of Christianity. Examples of improved bloomeries are the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=St%C3%BCckofen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stückofen (page does not exist)">Stückofen</a><sup class="noprint" style="font-style: normal;">[<a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/St%C3%BCckofen" title="fr:Stückofen">fr</a>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> (sometimes called wolf-furnace<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>) or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalan_forge" title="Catalan forge">Catalan forge</a>, which remained until the beginning of the 19th century. The Catalan forge was invented in <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">Catalonia</a>, Spain, during the 8th century. Instead of using natural draught, air was pumped in by a <i><a href="/wiki/Trompe" title="Trompe">trompe</a></i>, resulting in better quality iron and an increased capacity. This pumping of airstream in with bellows is known as <i>cold blast</i>, and it increases the <a href="/wiki/Fuel_efficiency" title="Fuel efficiency">fuel efficiency</a> of the bloomery and improves yield. The Catalan forges can also be built bigger than natural draught bloomeries.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Oldest_European_blast_furnaces">Oldest European blast furnaces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blast_furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Oldest European blast furnaces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Germany_First_Coke_Blast_Furnace_Miniature_DM.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Germany_First_Coke_Blast_Furnace_Miniature_DM.jpg/300px-Germany_First_Coke_Blast_Furnace_Miniature_DM.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Germany_First_Coke_Blast_Furnace_Miniature_DM.jpg/450px-Germany_First_Coke_Blast_Furnace_Miniature_DM.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Germany_First_Coke_Blast_Furnace_Miniature_DM.jpg/600px-Germany_First_Coke_Blast_Furnace_Miniature_DM.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Germany_First_Coke_Blast_Furnace_Miniature_DM.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The first blast furnace of Germany as depicted in a miniature in the <a href="/wiki/Deutsches_Museum" title="Deutsches Museum">Deutsches Museum</a></div></div></div><p>The oldest known blast furnaces in the West were built in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=D%C3%BCrstel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dürstel (page does not exist)">Dürstel</a> in <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, the Märkische <a href="/wiki/Sauerland" title="Sauerland">Sauerland</a> in Germany, and at <a href="/wiki/Lapphyttan" title="Lapphyttan">Lapphyttan</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, where the complex was active between 1205 and 1300.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> At Noraskog in the Swedish parish of Järnboås, there have also been found traces of blast furnaces dated even earlier, possibly to around 1100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> These early blast furnaces, like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_history" title="Chinese history">Chinese</a> examples, were very inefficient compared to those used today. The iron from the Lapphyttan complex was used to produce balls of <a href="/wiki/Wrought_iron" title="Wrought iron">wrought iron</a> known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Osmond_iron" title="Osmond iron">osmonds</a>, and these were traded internationally – a possible reference occurs in a treaty with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Novgorod" title="Novgorod">Novgorod</a> from 1203 and several certain references in accounts of English customs from the 1250s and 1320s. Other furnaces of the 13th to 15th centuries have been identified in <a href="/wiki/Westphalia" title="Westphalia">Westphalia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>The technology required for blast furnaces may have either been transferred from China, or may have been an indigenous innovation. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Qazvini" title="Al-Qazvini">Al-Qazvini</a> in the 13th century and other travellers subsequently noted an iron industry in the <a href="/wiki/Alborz" title="Alborz">Alburz</a> Mountains to the south of the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a>. This is close to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silk_route" title="Silk route">silk route</a>, so that the use of technology derived from China is conceivable. Much later descriptions record blast furnaces about three metres high.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> As the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Varangian" title="Varangian">Varangian</a> <a href="/wiki/Rus%27_people" title="Rus' people">Rus' people</a> from <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a> traded with the Caspian (using their <a href="/wiki/Volga_trade_route" title="Volga trade route">Volga trade route</a>, it is possible that the technology reached Sweden by this means.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> High quality ores, water power for bellows for blast and wood for charcoal are readily obtainable in Sweden. However, since blast furnace has also been invented independently in Africa by the <a href="/wiki/Haya_people" title="Haya people">Haya people</a>, it is more likely the process has been invented in Scandinavia independently. The step from <a href="/wiki/Bloomery" title="Bloomery">bloomery</a> to true blast furnace is not big. Simply just building a bigger furnace and using bigger bellows to increase the volume of the blast and hence the amount of oxygen leads inevitably into higher temperatures, bloom melting into liquid iron and, cast iron flowing from the smelters. Already the Vikings are known to have used double bellows, which greatly increases the volumetric flow of the blast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>This Caspian region may also separately be the technological source for at furnace at <a href="/wiki/Ferriere" title="Ferriere">Ferriere</a>, described by <a href="/wiki/Filarete" title="Filarete">Filarete</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Water-powered bellows at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Semogo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Semogo (page does not exist)">Semogo</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semogo" title="it:Semogo">it</a>)</span> in northern Italy in 1226 in a two-stage process. In this, the molten iron was tapped twice a day into water thereby granulating it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cistercian_contributions">Cistercian contributions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blast_furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cistercian contributions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>One means by which certain technological advances were transmitted within Europe was a result of the General Chapter of the <a href="/wiki/Cistercians" title="Cistercians">Cistercian</a> monks. This may have included the blast furnace, as the Cistercians are known to have been skilled <a href="/wiki/Metallurgy" title="Metallurgy">metallurgists</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Woods34_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Woods34-29">[29]</a></sup> According to Jean Gimpel, their high level of industrial technology facilitated the diffusion of new techniques: "Every monastery had a model factory, often as large as the church and only several feet away, and waterpower drove the machinery of the various industries located on its floor." Iron ore deposits were often donated to the monks along with forges to extract the iron, and within time surpluses were being offered for sale. The Cistercians became the leading iron producers in <a href="/wiki/Champagne_(province)" title="Champagne (province)">Champagne</a>, France, from the mid-13th century to the 17th century,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gimpel_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gimpel-30">[30]</a></sup> also using the <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a>-rich slag from their furnaces as an agricultural fertilizer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Archaeologists are still discovering the extent of Cistercian technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Woods36_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Woods36-32">[32]</a></sup> At <a href="/wiki/Laskill" title="Laskill">Laskill</a>, an outstation of <a href="/wiki/Rievaulx_Abbey" title="Rievaulx Abbey">Rievaulx Abbey</a> and the only medieval blast furnace so far identified in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a>, the slag produced was low in iron content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Woods37_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Woods37-33">[33]</a></sup> Slag from other furnaces of the time contained a substantial concentration of iron, whereas Laskill is believed to have produced cast iron quite efficiently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Woods37_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Woods37-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Woods_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Woods-35">[35]</a></sup> Its date is not yet clear, but it probably did not survive until <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Monasteries" title="Dissolution of the Monasteries">Dissolution of the Monasteries</a> in the late 1530s, as an agreement (immediately after that) concerning the "smythes" with the <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Manners,_1st_Earl_of_Rutland" title="Thomas Manners, 1st Earl of Rutland">Earl of Rutland</a> in 1541 refers to blooms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Nevertheless, the means by which the blast furnace spread in medieval Europe has not finally been determined.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_and_spread_of_early_modern_blast_furnaces">Origin and spread of early modern blast furnaces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blast_furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Origin and spread of early modern blast furnaces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HautfourneauXVIII_1nb.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4268" data-file-width="2949" height="318" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/HautfourneauXVIII_1nb.jpg/220px-HautfourneauXVIII_1nb.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/HautfourneauXVIII_1nb.jpg/330px-HautfourneauXVIII_1nb.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/HautfourneauXVIII_1nb.jpg/440px-HautfourneauXVIII_1nb.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HautfourneauXVIII_1nb.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Period drawing of an 18th-century blast furnace</div></div></div>
<p>Due to the casting of cannon, the blast furnace came into widespread use in France in the mid 15th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tylecote_1992_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tylecote_1992-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>The direct ancestor of these used in France and England was in the Namur region in what is now Wallonia (Belgium). From there, they spread first to the <a href="/wiki/Pays_de_Bray" title="Pays de Bray">Pays de Bray</a> on the eastern boundary of <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a> and from there to the <a href="/wiki/Weald" title="Weald">Weald</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sussex" title="Sussex">Sussex</a>, where the first furnace (called Queenstock) in <a href="/wiki/Buxted" title="Buxted">Buxted</a> was built in about 1491, followed by one at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Newbridge,_East_Sussex&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Newbridge, East Sussex (page does not exist)">Newbridge</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ashdown_Forest" title="Ashdown Forest">Ashdown Forest</a> in 1496. They remained few in number until about 1530 but many were built in the following decades in the Weald, where the iron industry perhaps reached its peak about 1590. Most of the pig iron from these furnaces was taken to <a href="/wiki/Finery_forge" title="Finery forge">finery forges</a> for the production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bar_iron" title="Bar iron">bar iron</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Queenstock_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Queenstock-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first British furnaces outside the Weald appeared during the 1550s, and many were built in the remainder of that century and the following ones. The output of the industry probably peaked about 1620, and was followed by a slow decline until the early 18th century. This was apparently because it was more economic to import iron from <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> and elsewhere than to make it in some more remote British locations. Charcoal that was economically available to the industry was probably being consumed as fast as the wood to make it grew.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Backbarrow" title="Backbarrow">Backbarrow</a> blast furnace built in <a href="/wiki/Cumbria" title="Cumbria">Cumbria</a> in 1711 has been described as the first efficient example.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (November 2010)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The first blast furnace in Russia opened in 1637 near <a href="/wiki/Tula,_Russia" title="Tula, Russia">Tula</a> and was called the Gorodishche Works. The blast furnace spread from here to the central Russia and then finally to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urals" title="Urals">Urals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dwip_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-dwip-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coke_blast_furnaces">Coke blast furnaces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blast_furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Coke blast furnaces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Blast_Furnaces_at_Blists_Hill.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Blast_Furnaces_at_Blists_Hill.jpg/220px-Blast_Furnaces_at_Blists_Hill.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Blast_Furnaces_at_Blists_Hill.jpg/330px-Blast_Furnaces_at_Blists_Hill.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Blast_Furnaces_at_Blists_Hill.jpg/440px-Blast_Furnaces_at_Blists_Hill.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Blast_Furnaces_at_Blists_Hill.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The original blast furnaces at Blists Hill, <a href="/wiki/Coalbrookdale" title="Coalbrookdale">Coalbrookdale</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:THC_2003.902.116_Charging_the_Experimental_Blast_Furnace.tif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2087" data-file-width="2592" height="177" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/THC_2003.902.116_Charging_the_Experimental_Blast_Furnace.tif/lossy-page1-220px-THC_2003.902.116_Charging_the_Experimental_Blast_Furnace.tif.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/THC_2003.902.116_Charging_the_Experimental_Blast_Furnace.tif/lossy-page1-330px-THC_2003.902.116_Charging_the_Experimental_Blast_Furnace.tif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/THC_2003.902.116_Charging_the_Experimental_Blast_Furnace.tif/lossy-page1-440px-THC_2003.902.116_Charging_the_Experimental_Blast_Furnace.tif.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:THC_2003.902.116_Charging_the_Experimental_Blast_Furnace.tif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Charging the experimental blast furnace, Fixed Nitrogen Research Laboratory, 1930</div></div></div>
<p>In 1709, at <a href="/wiki/Coalbrookdale" title="Coalbrookdale">Coalbrookdale</a> in Shropshire, England, <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Darby_I" title="Abraham Darby I">Abraham Darby</a> began to fuel a blast furnace with <a href="/wiki/Coke_(fuel)" title="Coke (fuel)">coke</a> instead of <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a>.  Coke's initial advantage was its lower cost, mainly because making coke required much less labor than cutting trees and making charcoal, but using coke also overcame localized shortages of wood, especially in Britain and on the Continent.  Metallurgical grade coke will bear heavier weight than charcoal, allowing larger furnaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Landes_1969_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Landes_1969-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>  A disadvantage is that coke contains more impurities than charcoal, with sulfur being especially detrimental to the iron's quality.  Coke's impurities were more of a problem before hot blast reduced the amount of coke required and before furnace temperatures were hot enough to make slag from limestone free flowing.  (Limestone ties up sulfur.  Manganese may also be added to tie up sulfur).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>123-125</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-”McNeil1990”_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-”McNeil1990”-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tylecote_1992_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tylecote_1992-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>122-23</span></sup>
</p><p>Coke iron was initially only used for <a href="/wiki/Foundry" title="Foundry">foundry</a> work, making pots and other cast iron goods. Foundry work was a minor branch of the industry, but Darby's son built a new furnace at nearby Horsehay, and began to supply the owners of <a href="/wiki/Finery_forge" title="Finery forge">finery forges</a> with coke pig iron for the production of bar iron. Coke pig iron was by this time cheaper to produce than charcoal pig iron. The use of a coal-derived fuel in the iron industry was a key factor in the British <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Darby's original blast furnace has been archaeologically excavated and can be seen in situ at Coalbrookdale, part of the <a href="/wiki/Ironbridge_Gorge" title="Ironbridge Gorge">Ironbridge Gorge</a> Museums. Cast iron from the furnace was used to make girders for the world's first iron bridge in 1779. <a href="/wiki/The_Iron_Bridge" title="The Iron Bridge">The Iron Bridge</a> crosses the <a href="/wiki/River_Severn" title="River Severn">River Severn</a> at Coalbrookdale and remains in use for pedestrians.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Steam-powered_blast">Steam-powered blast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blast_furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Steam-powered blast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The steam engine was applied to power blast air, overcoming a shortage of water power in areas where coal and iron ore were located.  The cast iron blowing cylinder was developed in 1768 to replace the leather bellows, which wore out quickly. The steam engine and cast iron blowing cylinder led to a large increase in British iron production in the late 18th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tylecote_1992_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tylecote_1992-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hot_blast">Hot blast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blast_furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Hot blast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hot Blast was the single most important advance in fuel efficiency of the blast furnace and was one of the most important technologies developed during the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hot_blast" title="Hot blast">Hot blast</a> was patented by <a href="/wiki/James_Beaumont_Neilson" title="James Beaumont Neilson">James Beaumont Neilson</a> at <a href="/wiki/Wilsontown_Ironworks" title="Wilsontown Ironworks">Wilsontown Ironworks</a> in Scotland in 1828.  Within a few years of the introduction, hot blast was developed to the point where fuel consumption was cut by one-third using coke or two-thirds using coal, while furnace capacity was also significantly increased.  Within a few decades, the practice was to have a "stove" as large as the furnace next to it into which the waste gas (containing CO) from the furnace was directed and burnt. The resultant heat was used to preheat the air blown into the furnace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neilson_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Neilson-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hot blast enabled the use of raw <a href="/wiki/Anthracite" title="Anthracite">anthracite</a> coal, which was difficult to light, to the blast furnace.  Anthracite was first tried successfully by George Crane at <a href="/wiki/Ynyscedwyn_Ironworks" title="Ynyscedwyn Ironworks">Ynyscedwyn Ironworks</a> in south Wales in 1837.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> It was taken up in America by the <a href="/wiki/Lehigh_Crane_Iron_Company" title="Lehigh Crane Iron Company">Lehigh Crane Iron Company</a> at <a href="/wiki/Catasauqua,_Pennsylvania" title="Catasauqua, Pennsylvania">Catasauqua, Pennsylvania</a>, in 1839.  Anthracite use declined when very high capacity blast furnaces requiring coke were built in the 1870s.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_furnaces">Modern furnaces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blast_furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Modern furnaces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Iron_blast_furnaces">Iron blast furnaces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blast_furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Iron blast furnaces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The blast furnace remains an important part of modern iron production. Modern furnaces are highly efficient, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cowper_stove" title="Cowper stove">Cowper stoves</a> to <a href="/wiki/Hot_blast" title="Hot blast">pre-heat</a> the blast air and employ recovery systems to extract the heat from the hot gases exiting the furnace. Competition in industry drives higher production rates. The largest blast furnaces have a volume around 5,580 m<sup>3</sup> (197,000 cu ft)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> and can produce around 80,000 tonnes (79,000 long tons; 88,000 short tons) of iron per week.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The date of the event predicted near this tag has passed. (May 2013)">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>This is a great increase from the typical 18th-century furnaces, which averaged about 360 tonnes (350 long tons; 400 short tons) per year. Variations of the blast furnace, such as the Swedish electric blast furnace, have been developed in countries which have no native coal resources.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lead_blast_furnaces">Lead blast furnaces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blast_furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Lead blast furnaces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Blast furnaces are currently rarely used in copper smelting, but modern lead smelting blast furnaces are much shorter than iron blast furnaces and are rectangular in shape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sinclair75_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair75-55">[55]</a></sup> The overall shaft height is around 5 to 6 m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sinclair77_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair77-56">[56]</a></sup> Modern lead blast furnaces are constructed using water-cooled steel or copper jackets for the walls, and have no refractory linings in the side walls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sinclair75_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair75-55">[55]</a></sup> The base of the furnace is a hearth of <a href="/wiki/Fire_brick" title="Fire brick">refractory material</a> (bricks or castable refractory).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sinclair75_55-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair75-55">[55]</a></sup> Lead blast furnaces are often open-topped rather than having the charging bell used in iron blast furnaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>The blast furnace used at the <a href="/wiki/Nyrstar" title="Nyrstar">Nyrstar</a> <a href="/wiki/Port_Pirie" title="Port Pirie">Port Pirie</a> lead smelter differs from most other lead blast furnaces in that it has a double row of tuyeres rather than the single row normally used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sinclair77_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair77-56">[56]</a></sup> The lower shaft of the furnace has a chair shape with the lower part of the shaft being narrower than the upper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sinclair77_56-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair77-56">[56]</a></sup> The lower row of tuyeres being located in the narrow part of the shaft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sinclair77_56-3"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair77-56">[56]</a></sup> This allows the upper part of the shaft to be wider than the standard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sinclair77_56-4"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair77-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Zinc_blast_furnaces_.28Imperial_Smelting_Furnaces.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Zinc_blast_furnaces_(Imperial_Smelting_Furnaces)">Zinc blast furnaces (Imperial Smelting Furnaces)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blast_furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Zinc blast furnaces (Imperial Smelting Furnaces)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The blast furnaces used in the <a href="/wiki/Zinc_smelting#Blast_furnace_process_(Imperial_Smelting_Process)" title="Zinc smelting">Imperial Smelting Process</a> ("ISP") were developed from the standard lead blast furnace, but are fully sealed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sinclair89_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair89-58">[58]</a></sup> This is because the zinc produced by these furnaces is recovered as metal from the vapor phase, and the presence of oxygen in the off-gas would result in the formation of zinc oxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sinclair89_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair89-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>Blast furnaces used in the ISP have a more intense operation than standard lead blast furnaces, with higher air blast rates per m<sup>2</sup> of hearth area and a higher coke consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sinclair89_58-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair89-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>Zinc production with the ISP is more expensive than with <a href="/wiki/Zinc_smelting#Electrolysis_process" title="Zinc smelting">electrolytic zinc</a> plants, so several smelters operating this technology have closed in recent years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> However, ISP furnaces have the advantage of being able to treat zinc concentrates containing higher levels of lead than can electrolytic zinc plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sinclair89_58-3"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair89-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_process">Modern process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blast_furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Modern process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Blast_furnace_NT.PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="440" data-file-width="700" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Blast_furnace_NT.PNG/250px-Blast_furnace_NT.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Blast_furnace_NT.PNG/375px-Blast_furnace_NT.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Blast_furnace_NT.PNG/500px-Blast_furnace_NT.PNG 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Blast_furnace_NT.PNG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><b>Blast furnace placed in an installation</b> <div><ol><li>Iron ore + limestone sinter</li><li>Coke</li><li>Elevator</li><li>Feedstock inlet</li><li>Layer of coke</li><li>Layer of sinter pellets of ore and limestone</li><li>Hot blast (around 1200 °C)</li><li>Removal of slag</li><li>Tapping of molten pig iron</li><li>Slag pot</li><li>Torpedo car for pig iron</li><li>Dust cyclone for separation of solid particles</li><li>Cowper stoves for hot blast</li><li>Smoke outlet (can be redirected to carbon capture &amp; storage (CCS) tank)</li><li>Feed air for Cowper stoves (air pre-heaters)</li><li>Powdered coal</li><li>Coke oven</li><li>Coke</li><li>Blast furnace gas downcomer</li></ol></div></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:VysokaPec.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/VysokaPec.jpg/250px-VysokaPec.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/VysokaPec.jpg/375px-VysokaPec.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/VysokaPec.jpg/500px-VysokaPec.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:VysokaPec.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><b>Blast furnace diagram</b> <div><ol><li><a href="/wiki/Hot_blast" title="Hot blast">Hot blast</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cowper_stove" title="Cowper stove">Cowper stoves</a></li><li>Melting zone (<i>bosh</i>)</li><li>Reduction zone of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferrous_oxide" title="Ferrous oxide">ferrous oxide</a> (<i>barrel</i>)</li><li>Reduction zone of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferric_oxide" title="Ferric oxide">ferric oxide</a> (<i>stack</i>)</li><li>Pre-heating zone (<i>throat</i>)</li><li>Feed of ore, limestone, and coke</li><li>Exhaust gases</li><li>Column of ore, coke and limestone</li><li>Removal of <a href="/wiki/Slag" title="Slag">slag</a></li><li>Tapping of molten <a href="/wiki/Pig_iron" title="Pig iron">pig iron</a></li><li>Collection of waste gases</li></ol></div></div></div></div>
<p>Modern furnaces are equipped with an array of supporting facilities to increase efficiency, such as ore storage yards where barges are unloaded. The raw materials are transferred to the stockhouse complex by ore bridges, or <a href="/wiki/Hopper_car" title="Hopper car">rail hoppers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Railcar" title="Railcar">ore transfer cars</a>. Rail-mounted scale cars or computer controlled weight hoppers weigh out the various raw materials to yield the desired hot metal and slag chemistry. The raw materials are brought to the top of the blast furnace via a <a href="/wiki/Skip_(container)" title="Skip (container)">skip</a> car powered by winches or conveyor belts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AISI_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-AISI-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are different ways in which the raw materials are charged into the blast furnace. Some blast furnaces use a "double bell" system where two "bells" are used to control the entry of raw material into the blast furnace. The purpose of the two bells is to minimize the loss of hot gases in the blast furnace. First, the raw materials are emptied into the upper or small bell which then opens to empty the charge into the large bell. The small bell then closes, to seal the blast furnace, while the large bell rotates to provide specific distribution of materials before dispensing the charge into the blast furnace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-et_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-et-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> A more recent design is to use a "bell-less" system. These systems use multiple hoppers to contain each raw material, which is then discharged into the blast furnace through valves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-et_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-et-61">[61]</a></sup> These valves are more accurate at controlling how much of each constituent is added, as compared to the skip or conveyor system, thereby increasing the efficiency of the furnace. Some of these bell-less systems also implement a discharge chute in the throat of the furnace (as with the Paul Wurth top) in order to precisely control where the charge is placed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p>The iron making blast furnace itself is built in the form of a tall structure, lined with <a href="/wiki/Refractory" title="Refractory">refractory</a> brick, and profiled to allow for expansion of the charged materials as they heat during their descent, and subsequent reduction in size as melting starts to occur. Coke, <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> flux, and iron ore (iron oxide) are charged into the top of the furnace in a precise filling order which helps control gas flow and the chemical reactions inside the furnace. Four "uptakes" allow the hot, dirty gas high in carbon monoxide content to exit the furnace throat, while "bleeder valves" protect the top of the furnace from sudden gas pressure surges. The coarse particles in the exhaust gas settle in the "dust catcher" and are dumped into a railroad car or truck for disposal, while the gas itself flows through a <a href="/wiki/Venturi_scrubber" title="Venturi scrubber">venturi scrubber</a> and/or electrostatic precipitators and a gas cooler to reduce the temperature of the cleaned gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AISI_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-AISI-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>The "casthouse" at the bottom half of the furnace contains the bustle pipe, water cooled copper tuyeres and the equipment for casting the liquid iron and slag. Once a "taphole" is drilled through the refractory clay plug, liquid iron and slag flow down a trough through a "skimmer" opening, separating the iron and slag. Modern, larger blast furnaces may have as many as four tapholes and two casthouses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AISI_60-2"><a href="#cite_note-AISI-60">[60]</a></sup> Once the pig iron and slag has been tapped, the taphole is again plugged with refractory clay.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Blast_furnace_tuyeres.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1840" data-file-width="3264" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Blast_furnace_tuyeres.jpg/250px-Blast_furnace_tuyeres.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Blast_furnace_tuyeres.jpg/375px-Blast_furnace_tuyeres.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Blast_furnace_tuyeres.jpg/500px-Blast_furnace_tuyeres.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Blast_furnace_tuyeres.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tuyeres of Blast Furnace at <a href="/wiki/Gerdau" title="Gerdau">Gerdau</a>, Brazil</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tuyere" title="Tuyere">tuyeres</a> are used to implement a <a href="/wiki/Hot_blast" title="Hot blast">hot blast</a>, which is used to increase the efficiency of the blast furnace. The hot blast is directed into the furnace through water-cooled copper nozzles called tuyeres near the base. The hot blast temperature can be from 900 °C to 1300 °C (1600 °F to 2300 °F) depending on the stove design and condition. The temperatures they deal with may be 2000 °C to 2300 °C (3600 °F to 4200 °F). <a href="/wiki/Oil" title="Oil">Oil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tar" title="Tar">tar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, powdered <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> can also be injected into the furnace at tuyere level to combine with the coke to release additional energy and increase the percentage of reducing gases present which is necessary to increase productivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AISI_60-3"><a href="#cite_note-AISI-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Process_engineering_and_chemistry">Process engineering and chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blast_furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Process engineering and chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:VysokePece1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/VysokePece1.jpg/250px-VysokePece1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/VysokePece1.jpg/375px-VysokePece1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/VysokePece1.jpg/500px-VysokePece1.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:VysokePece1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Blast furnaces of <a href="/wiki/T%C5%99inec_Iron_and_Steel_Works" title="Třinec Iron and Steel Works">Třinec Iron and Steel Works</a>, Czech Republic</div></div></div>
<p>Blast furnaces operate on the principle of <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">chemical reduction</a> whereby carbon monoxide, having a stronger affinity for the oxygen in iron ore than iron does, reduces the iron to its elemental form.  Blast furnaces differ from <a href="/wiki/Bloomery" title="Bloomery">bloomeries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reverberatory_furnace" title="Reverberatory furnace">reverberatory furnaces</a> in that in a blast furnace, flue gas is in direct contact with the ore and iron, allowing carbon monoxide to diffuse into the ore and reduce the iron oxide to elemental iron mixed with carbon. The blast furnaces operates as a <a href="/wiki/Countercurrent_exchange" title="Countercurrent exchange">countercurrent exchange</a> process whereas a bloomery does not. Another difference is that bloomeries operate as a batch process while blast furnaces operate continuously for long periods because they are difficult to start up and shut down. (See: <a href="/wiki/Continuous_production" title="Continuous production">Continuous production</a>)  Also, the carbon in pig iron lowers the melting point below that of steel or pure iron; in contrast, iron does not melt in a bloomery.
</p><p>Carbon monoxide also reduces <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">silica</a> which has to be removed from the pig iron. The silica is reacted with <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">calcium oxide</a> (burned limestone) and forms a slag which floats to the surface of the molten pig iron. The direct contact of flue gas with the iron causes contamination with sulfur if it is present in the fuel. Historically, to prevent contamination from sulfur, the best quality iron was produced with charcoal.
</p><p>The downward moving column of ore, flux, <a href="/wiki/Coke_(fuel)" title="Coke (fuel)">coke</a> or charcoal and reaction products must be porous enough for the flue gas to pass through.  This requires the coke or charcoal to be in large enough particles to be permeable, meaning there cannot be an excess of fine particles. Therefore, the coke must be strong enough so it will not be crushed by the weight of the material above it. Besides physical strength of the coke, it must also be low in sulfur, phosphorus, and ash. This necessitates the use of metallurgical coal, which is a premium grade due to its relative scarcity.
</p><p>The main chemical reaction producing the molten iron is:
</p>
<dl><dd>Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO<sub>2</sub><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Formulae_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-Formulae-64">[64]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>This reaction might be divided into multiple steps, with the first being that preheated blast air blown into the furnace reacts with the carbon in the form of coke to produce <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> and heat:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 C<sub>(s)</sub> + O<sub>2</sub><sub>(g)</sub> → 2 CO<sub>(g)</sub><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InorganicChemistry_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-InorganicChemistry-65">[65]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>The hot carbon monoxide is the reducing agent for the iron ore and reacts with the <a href="/wiki/Iron_oxide" title="Iron oxide">iron oxide</a> to produce molten iron and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. Depending on the temperature in the different parts of the furnace (warmest at the bottom) the iron is reduced in several steps. At the top, where the temperature usually is in the range between 200 °C and 700 °C, the iron oxide is partially reduced to iron(II,III) oxide, Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>.
</p>
<dl><dd>3 Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub><sub>(s)</sub> + CO<sub>(g)</sub> → 2 Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub><sub>(s)</sub> + CO<sub>2</sub><sub>(g)</sub><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InorganicChemistry_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-InorganicChemistry-65">[65]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>At temperatures around 850 °C, further down in the furnace, the iron(II,III) is reduced further to iron(II) oxide:
</p>
<dl><dd>Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub><sub>(s)</sub> + CO<sub>(g)</sub> → 3 FeO<sub>(s)</sub> + CO<sub>2</sub><sub>(g)</sub><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InorganicChemistry_65-2"><a href="#cite_note-InorganicChemistry-65">[65]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>Hot carbon dioxide, unreacted carbon monoxide, and nitrogen from the air pass up through the furnace as fresh feed material travels down into the reaction zone. As the material travels downward, the counter-current gases both preheat the feed charge and decompose the limestone to <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">calcium oxide</a> and carbon dioxide:
</p>
<dl><dd>CaCO<sub>3</sub><sub>(s)</sub> → CaO<sub>(s)</sub> + CO<sub>2</sub><sub>(g)</sub><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InorganicChemistry_65-3"><a href="#cite_note-InorganicChemistry-65">[65]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>The calcium oxide formed by decomposition reacts with various acidic impurities in the iron (notably <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">silica</a>), to form a <a href="/wiki/Fayalite" title="Fayalite">fayalitic</a> slag which is essentially <a href="/wiki/Calcium_silicate" title="Calcium silicate">calcium silicate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">Ca</a><a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">Si</a><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a><sub>3</sub>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Formulae_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-Formulae-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>SiO<sub>2</sub> + CaO → CaSiO<sub>3</sub><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-form2sci_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-form2sci-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint noexcerpt Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (October 2018)">better source needed</span></a></i>]</sup></dd></dl>
<p>As the iron(II) oxide moves down to the area with higher temperatures, ranging up to 1200 °C degrees, it is reduced further to iron metal:
</p>
<dl><dd>FeO<sub>(s)</sub> + CO<sub>(g)</sub> → Fe<sub>(s)</sub> + CO<sub>2</sub><sub>(g)</sub><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InorganicChemistry_65-4"><a href="#cite_note-InorganicChemistry-65">[65]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>The carbon dioxide formed in this process is re-reduced to carbon monoxide by the <a href="/wiki/Coke_(fuel)" title="Coke (fuel)">coke</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>C<sub>(s)</sub> + CO<sub>2</sub><sub>(g)</sub> → 2 CO<sub>(g)</sub><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InorganicChemistry_65-5"><a href="#cite_note-InorganicChemistry-65">[65]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>The temperature-dependent equilibrium controlling the gas atmosphere in the furnace is called the <a href="/wiki/Boudouard_reaction" title="Boudouard reaction">Boudouard reaction</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>2CO ⇌ CO<sub>2</sub> + C</dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p>The "<a href="/wiki/Pig_iron" title="Pig iron">pig iron</a>" produced by the blast furnace has a relatively high carbon content of around 4–5%, making it very brittle, and of limited immediate commercial use. Some pig iron is used to make <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">cast iron</a>. The majority of pig iron produced by blast furnaces undergoes further processing to reduce the carbon content and produce various grades of steel used for construction materials, automobiles, ships and machinery.
</p><p>Although the efficiency of blast furnaces is constantly evolving, the chemical process inside the blast furnace remains the same. According to the <a href="/wiki/American_Iron_and_Steel_Institute" title="American Iron and Steel Institute">American Iron and Steel Institute</a>: "Blast furnaces will survive into the next millennium because the larger, efficient furnaces can produce hot metal at costs competitive with other iron making technologies."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AISI_60-4"><a href="#cite_note-AISI-60">[60]</a></sup> One of the biggest drawbacks of the blast furnaces is the inevitable carbon dioxide production as iron is reduced from iron oxides by carbon and as of 2016, there is no economical substitute – steelmaking is one of the largest industrial contributors of the CO<sub>2</sub> emissions in the world (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gases</a>).
</p><p>The challenge set by the greenhouse gas emissions of the blast furnace is being addressed in an ongoing European Program called ULCOS (Ultra Low <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_Dioxide" title="Carbon Dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> <a href="/wiki/Steelmaking" title="Steelmaking">Steelmaking</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Several new process routes have been proposed and investigated in depth to cut specific emissions (CO<sub>2</sub> per ton of steel) by at least 50%. Some rely on the capture and further storage (CCS) of CO<sub>2</sub>, while others choose decarbonizing iron and steel production, by turning to hydrogen, electricity and biomass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> In the nearer term, a technology that incorporates CCS into the blast furnace process itself and is called the Top-Gas Recycling Blast Furnace is under development, with a scale-up to a commercial size blast furnace under way. The technology should be fully demonstrated by the end of the 2010s, in line with the timeline set, for example, by the EU to cut emissions significantly. Broad deployment could take place from 2020 on.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacture_of_stone_wool">Manufacture of stone wool</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blast_furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Manufacture of stone wool">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Stone wool or <a href="/wiki/Mineral_wool" title="Mineral wool">rock wool</a> is a spun mineral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fibre" title="Fibre">fibre</a> used as an <a href="/wiki/Thermal_insulation" title="Thermal insulation">insulation</a> product and in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroponic" title="Hydroponic">hydroponics</a>. It is manufactured in a blast furnace fed with diabase rock which contains very low levels of metal oxides. The resultant slag is drawn off and spun to form the rock wool product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> Very small amounts of metals are also produced which are an unwanted <a href="/wiki/By-product" title="By-product">by-product</a> and run to waste.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decommissioned_blast_furnaces_as_museum_sites">Decommissioned blast furnaces as museum sites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blast_furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Decommissioned blast furnaces as museum sites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_preserved_historic_blast_furnaces" title="List of preserved historic blast furnaces">List of preserved historic blast furnaces</a></div>
<p>For a long time, it was normal procedure for a decommissioned blast furnace to be demolished and either be replaced with a newer, improved one, or to have the entire site demolished to make room for follow-up use of the area. In recent decades, several countries have realized the value of blast furnaces as a part of their industrial history. Rather than being demolished, abandoned steel mills were turned into museums or integrated into multi-purpose parks. The largest number of preserved historic blast furnaces exists in Germany; other such sites exist in Spain, France, the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>, Japan, <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and the United States.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_cleaning_systems">Gas cleaning systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blast_furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Gas cleaning systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The blast furnace gas can be used to generate heat. So by reducing the constituents in blast furnace gas we can increase the calorific value and can be used to generate heat and rise the temperature in any furnace.
</p><p>The gas cleaning system contains two stages, the coarse cleaning system and the fine cleaning system.
</p><p>In the coarse cleaning system a dust catcher is used. A dust catcher is a cylindrical steel structure with conical top and bottom sections. It is also lined with refractory bricks.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dust_catcher_drawing.png"><img alt="Dust catcher drawing.png" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="794" data-file-width="422" height="414" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Dust_catcher_drawing.png/220px-Dust_catcher_drawing.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Dust_catcher_drawing.png/330px-Dust_catcher_drawing.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/50/Dust_catcher_drawing.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dust_catcher_drawing.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div>
<p>The principle of the dust catcher is that the dust-laden gas is given a sudden reverse in speed and direction. Because of their mass, the coarse dust particles cannot change their velocity easily, and hence settle to the bottom.
</p>
