<p>The <i><b>Pittsburgh Post-Gazette</b></i>, also known simply as <b>the PG</b>, is the largest newspaper serving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_metropolitan_area" title="Pittsburgh metropolitan area">metropolitan</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pittsburgh,_Pennsylvania" title="Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania">Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania</a>, United States. It has won six <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzer Prizes</a> since 1938.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pittsburgh_Post-Gazette&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Post_Gazette_Building.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="537" data-file-width="799" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Post_Gazette_Building.jpg/250px-Post_Gazette_Building.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Post_Gazette_Building.jpg/375px-Post_Gazette_Building.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Post_Gazette_Building.jpg/500px-Post_Gazette_Building.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Post_Gazette_Building.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <i>Pittsburgh Post-Gazette</i> Building in <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Pittsburgh" title="Downtown Pittsburgh">Downtown Pittsburgh</a>, which housed the paper from 1962 to 2015.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gazette"><i>Gazette</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pittsburgh_Post-Gazette&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Gazette">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>Post-Gazette</i> began its history as a four-page weekly called <i>The Pittsburgh Gazette</i>, first published on July 29, 1786 with the encouragement of <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Henry_Brackenridge" title="Hugh Henry Brackenridge">Hugh Henry Brackenridge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-andrews1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-andrews1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It was the first newspaper published west of the <a href="/wiki/Allegheny_Mountains" title="Allegheny Mountains">Allegheny Mountains</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-andrews1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-andrews1-2">[2]</a></sup> Published by Joseph Hall and John Scull, the paper covered the start of the nation. As one of its first major articles, the <i>Gazette</i> published the newly adopted <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">Constitution of the United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1820, under publishers Eichbaum and Johnston and editor Morgan Neville, the name changed to <i>Pittsburgh Gazette and Manufacturing and Mercantile Advertiser</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> David MacLean bought the paper in 1822, and later reverted to the former title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Under combative editor <a href="/wiki/Neville_B._Craig" title="Neville B. Craig">Neville B. Craig</a>, whose service lasted from 1829 to 1841, the <i>Gazette</i> championed the <a href="/wiki/Anti-Masonic_Party" title="Anti-Masonic Party">Anti-Masonic</a> movement. Craig turned the <i>Gazette</i> into the city's first daily paper, issued every afternoon except Sunday starting on July 30, 1833.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1844, shortly after absorbing the <a href="/wiki/Advocate_(Pittsburgh)" title="Advocate (Pittsburgh)"><i>Advocate</i></a>, the <i>Gazette</i> switched its daily issue time to morning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Its editorial stance at the time was conservative and strongly favoring the Whig party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> By the 1850s the Gazette was credited with helping to organize a local chapter of the new <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican Party</a>, and with contributing to the election of <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a>. The paper was one of the first to suggest tensions between North and South would erupt in war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>After consolidating with the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Commercial" title="Pittsburgh Commercial"><i>Commercial</i></a> in 1877, the paper was again renamed and was then known as the <i>Commercial Gazette</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1900, <a href="/wiki/George_T._Oliver" title="George T. Oliver">George T. Oliver</a> acquired the paper, merging it six years later with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pittsburg_Times" title="The Pittsburg Times">The Pittsburg Times</a></i> to form <i>The Gazette Times</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post"><i>Post</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pittsburgh_Post-Gazette&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Post">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Pittsburgh <i>Post</i> first appeared on September 10, 1842, as the <i>Daily Morning Post</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> It had its origin in three pro-<a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic</a> weeklies, the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Mercury" title="Pittsburgh Mercury"><i>Mercury</i></a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Allegheny_Democrat" title="Allegheny Democrat">Allegheny Democrat</a></i>, and <a href="/wiki/The_American_Manufacturer" title="The American Manufacturer"><i>American Manufacturer</i></a>, which came together through a pair of mergers in the early 1840s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The three papers had for years engaged in bitter editorial battles with the <i>Gazette</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Like its predecessors, the <i>Post</i> advocated the policies of the Democratic Party. Its political opposition to the Whig and later Republican <i>Gazette</i> was so enduring that an eventual combination of the two rivals would have seemed unlikely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Block-Hearst_deal">Block-Hearst deal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pittsburgh_Post-Gazette&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Block-Hearst deal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 1920s were a time of consolidation in the long-overcrowded Pittsburgh newspaper market. In 1923, local publishers banded together to acquire and kill off the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Dispatch" title="Pittsburgh Dispatch"><i>Dispatch</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Leader" title="Pittsburgh Leader"><i>Leader</i></a>. Four years later, <a href="/wiki/William_Randolph_Hearst" title="William Randolph Hearst">William Randolph Hearst</a> negotiated with the Olivers to purchase the morning <i>Gazette Times</i> and its evening sister, the <i>Chronicle Telegraph</i>, while <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_Block_(newspaper_publisher)" title="Paul Block (newspaper publisher)">Paul Block</a> arranged to buy out the owner of the morning <i>Post</i> and evening <i>Sun</i>. After swapping the <i>Sun</i> in return for Hearst's <i>Gazette Times</i>, Block had both morning papers, which he combined to form the <i>Post-Gazette</i>. Hearst united the evening papers, creating the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Sun-Telegraph" title="Pittsburgh Sun-Telegraph"><i>Sun-Telegraph</i></a>. Both new papers debuted on August 2, 1927.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Joint_operating_agreement">Joint operating agreement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pittsburgh_Post-Gazette&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Joint operating agreement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1960, Pittsburgh had three daily papers: the <i>Post-Gazette</i> in the morning, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Press" title="Pittsburgh Press">Pittsburgh Press</a></i> and the <i>Pittsburgh Sun-Telegraph</i> in the evening and on Sunday. The <i>Post-Gazette</i> bought the <i>Sun-Telegraph</i> and moved into the <i>Sun-Telegraph'</i>s Grant Street offices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <i>Post-Gazette</i> tried to publish a Sunday paper to compete with the <i>Sunday Press</i> but it was not profitable; rising costs in general were challenging the company's bottom line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In November 1961, the <i>Post-Gazette</i> entered into an agreement with the Pittsburgh Press Company to combine their production and advertising sales operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas231_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas231-20">[20]</a></sup> The <i>Post-Gazette</i> owned and operated its own news and editorial departments, but production and distribution of the paper was handled by the larger Press office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas231_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas231-20">[20]</a></sup> This agreement stayed in place for over 30 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas,_pp._295–296_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas,_pp._295–296-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>The agreement gave the <i>Post-Gazette</i> a new home in the <i>Press</i> building, a comfortable upgrade from the hated "Sun-Telly barn."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Constructed for the <i>Press</i> in 1927 and expanded with a curtain wall in 1962, the building served as the <i>Post-Gazette</i> headquarters until 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Strike.2C_consolidation.2C_new_competition"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Strike,_consolidation,_new_competition">Strike, consolidation, new competition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pittsburgh_Post-Gazette&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Strike, consolidation, new competition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pittsburgh_Post-Gazette_Distribution_Center.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="576" data-file-width="1024" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Pittsburgh_Post-Gazette_Distribution_Center.jpg/250px-Pittsburgh_Post-Gazette_Distribution_Center.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Pittsburgh_Post-Gazette_Distribution_Center.jpg/375px-Pittsburgh_Post-Gazette_Distribution_Center.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Pittsburgh_Post-Gazette_Distribution_Center.jpg/500px-Pittsburgh_Post-Gazette_Distribution_Center.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pittsburgh_Post-Gazette_Distribution_Center.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The distribution center</div></div></div>
<p>On May 17, 1992, a strike by workers for the <i>Press</i> shut down publication of the <i>Press</i>; the joint operating agreement meant that the <i>Post-Gazette</i> also ceased to publish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> During the strike, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scripps_Howard" title="Scripps Howard">Scripps Howard</a> company sold the <i>Press</i> to the Block family, owners of the <i>Post-Gazette</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas,_pp._295–296_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas,_pp._295–296-21">[21]</a></sup> The Blocks did not resume printing the <i>Press</i>, and when the labor issue was resolved and publishing resumed, the <i>Post-Gazette</i> became the city's major paper, under the full masthead name <i>Pittsburgh Post-Gazette Sun-Telegraph/The Pittsburgh Press</i>.
</p><p>The Block ownership did not take this opportunity to address labor costs, which had led to sale of the <i>Press</i>. This would come back to haunt them and lead to financial problems (see "Financial Challenges" below).
</p><p>During the strike, publisher <a href="/wiki/Richard_Mellon_Scaife" title="Richard Mellon Scaife">Richard Mellon Scaife</a> expanded his paper, the <i><a href="/wiki/Greensburg,_Pennsylvania" title="Greensburg, Pennsylvania">Greensburg</a> Tribune-Review</i>, based in the county seat of adjoining <a href="/wiki/Westmoreland_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Westmoreland County, Pennsylvania">Westmoreland County</a>, where it had published for years. While maintaining the original paper in its facilities in Greensburg, he expanded it with a new Pittsburgh edition to serve the city and its suburbs. Scaife named this paper the <i><a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Tribune-Review" title="Pittsburgh Tribune-Review">Pittsburgh Tribune-Review</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Scaife has invested significant amounts of capital into upgraded facilities, separate offices and newsroom on Pittsburgh's North Side and a state of the art production facility in <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Township,_Allegheny_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Marshall Township, Allegheny County, Pennsylvania">Marshall Township</a> north of Pittsburgh in Allegheny County. Relations between the <i>Post-Gazette</i> and <i>Tribune-Review,</i> during its existence as a local print publication, were often competitive and frequently hostile, given Scaife's longstanding distaste for what he considered the Blocks' liberalism.
</p><p>On November 14, 2011, the <i>Post-Gazette</i> revived the <i><a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Press" title="Pittsburgh Press">Pittsburgh Press</a></i> as an afternoon <a href="/wiki/Online_newspaper" title="Online newspaper">online newspaper</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>On February 12, 2014, the paper purchased a new distribution facility in suburban <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Findlay_Township,_Pennsylvania" title="Findlay Township, Pennsylvania">Findlay Township, Pennsylvania</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2015, the paper moved into a new, state-of-the-art office building on the <a href="/wiki/North_Shore_(Pittsburgh)" title="North Shore (Pittsburgh)">North Shore</a> on a portion of the former site of <a href="/wiki/Three_Rivers_Stadium" title="Three Rivers Stadium">Three Rivers Stadium</a>, ending 53 years in the former <i>Press</i> building and more than two centuries in Downtown. Block Communications, feeling that the building is being greatly underutilized considering its proximity to <a href="/wiki/Point_State_Park" title="Point State Park">Point State Park</a>, still owns the building and plans to have the property redeveloped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Community_presence">Community presence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pittsburgh_Post-Gazette&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Community presence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Post_Gazette_building,_October_2015.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="694" data-file-width="1024" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Post_Gazette_building%2C_October_2015.JPG/220px-Post_Gazette_building%2C_October_2015.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Post_Gazette_building%2C_October_2015.JPG/330px-Post_Gazette_building%2C_October_2015.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Post_Gazette_building%2C_October_2015.JPG/440px-Post_Gazette_building%2C_October_2015.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Post_Gazette_building,_October_2015.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <i>Post-Gazette</i> building in October 2015.</div></div></div>
<p>The newspaper sponsored a major 23,000 seat outdoor amphitheater in <a href="/wiki/Burgettstown,_Pennsylvania" title="Burgettstown, Pennsylvania">Burgettstown, Pennsylvania</a>, the "Post-Gazette Pavilion", although it is still often referred to as "Star Lake", based on the original name, "Star Lake Amphitheater", and later "<a href="/wiki/Coca-Cola" title="Coca-Cola">Coca-Cola</a> Star Lake Amphitheater" under the former sponsor. They gave up <a href="/wiki/Naming_rights" title="Naming rights">naming rights</a> in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mervis_2010_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mervis_2010-29">[29]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/First_Niagara_Bank" title="First Niagara Bank">First Niagara Bank</a>, which had entered the Pittsburgh market the year before after <a href="/wiki/National_City_acquisition_by_PNC" title="National City acquisition by PNC">acquiring</a> <a href="/wiki/National_City_Corp." title="National City Corp.">National City</a> branches from Pittsburgh-based <a href="/wiki/PNC_Financial_Services" title="PNC Financial Services">PNC Financial Services</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> took over the naming rights to the facility and is now known as the <a href="/wiki/KeyBank_Pavilion" title="KeyBank Pavilion">KeyBank Pavilion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mervis_2010_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mervis_2010-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>The newspaper once had ventures in television. In 1957, the Post-Gazette partnered with the H. Kenneth Brennen family, local radio owners, to launch WIIC-TV (now <a href="/wiki/WPXI" title="WPXI">WPXI</a>) as the area's first full-time <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> affiliate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The <i>Post-Gazette</i> and the Brennens sold off the station to current owner <a href="/wiki/Cox_Enterprises" title="Cox Enterprises">Cox Enterprises</a> in 1964.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Although the <i>Post-Gazette</i> and WPXI have on occasion had some news partnerships, the Post-Gazette's primary news partner is now KDKA-TV.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Financial_challenges">Financial challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pittsburgh_Post-Gazette&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Financial challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In September 2006, the paper disclosed that it was experiencing financial challenges, largely related to its labor costs. The paper also disclosed it had not been profitable since printing had resumed in 1993. As a result of these issues, the paper is considering a number of options, including putting the paper up for sale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> While deep concern about the paper's future ensued, negotiations proved fruitful and in February, 2007 the paper's unions ratified a new agreement with management mandating job cuts, changes in funding health care benefits and so forth.
</p><p>In August 2018, the Post-Gazette ceased publishing daily. It began publishing online editions on Tuesdays and Saturdays and print editions the rest of the week.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pittsburgh_Post-Gazette&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>Post-Gazette</i> won <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzers</a> in <a href="/wiki/1938_Pulitzer_Prize" title="1938 Pulitzer Prize">1938</a>, <a href="/wiki/1986_Pulitzer_Prize" title="1986 Pulitzer Prize">1986</a>, <a href="/wiki/1987_Pulitzer_Prize" title="1987 Pulitzer Prize">1987</a> and <a href="/wiki/1998_Pulitzer_Prize" title="1998 Pulitzer Prize">1998</a>. Photographer Morris Berman maintained that the paper would have won a Pulitzer in <a href="/wiki/1964_Pulitzer_Prize" title="1964 Pulitzer Prize">1964</a> but chose not to run his iconic <a href="/wiki/Y._A._Tittle#Famous_photo" title="Y. A. Tittle">Y. A. Tittle</a> picture that he took at <a href="/wiki/Pitt_Stadium" title="Pitt Stadium">Pitt Stadium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The photo would go on to win awards, hang in the <a href="/wiki/Pro_Football_Hall_of_Fame" title="Pro Football Hall of Fame">Pro Football Hall of Fame</a>, be used for the back cover of Tittle's autobiography and used in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miller_Beer" title="Miller Beer">Miller Beer</a> High-Life commercial in 2005.
</p><p>Staff photographer <a href="/wiki/Martha_Rial" title="Martha Rial">Martha Rial</a> won the 1998 Pulitzer Prize for Spot News Photography for her photographs of Rwandan and Burundian refugees.
</p><p>In 1997 <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bill_Moushey&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bill Moushey (page does not exist)">Bill Moushey</a> won the <a href="/wiki/National_Press_Club_(United_States)" title="National Press Club (United States)">National Press Club</a>’s Freedom of Information Award on a series investigating the Federal Witness Protection Program and was a finalist for the Pulitzer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <i>Post-Gazette</i> also was instrumental in Pulitzers in <a href="/wiki/1992_Pulitzer_Prize" title="1992 Pulitzer Prize">1992</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition to the Pulitzers mentioned above, the Post-Gazette also won the prestigious and renowned <a href="/wiki/Religion_Communicators_Council#Wilbur_Awards" title="Religion Communicators Council">Wilbur Award</a> from the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Religious_Communicators_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Religious Communicators Council (page does not exist)">Religious Communicators Council</a> (RCC) in 2017 for religion editor <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Smith_(journalist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Peter Smith (journalist) (page does not exist)">Peter Smith</a>'s seminal work, <i>Silent Sanctuaries</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pittsburgh_Post-Gazette&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The paper was featured on the August 15, 2013, episode of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Colbert_Report" title="The Colbert Report">The Colbert Report</a></i> for its coverage of a <a href="/wiki/Washington_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Washington County, Pennsylvania">Washington County, Pennsylvania</a>, fracking lawsuit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prices">Prices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pittsburgh_Post-Gazette&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Prices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Post-Gazette</i> per copy prices are: daily, $2 and Sunday/Thanksgiving Day, $3 in Pennsylvania, including Allegheny/adjacent counties. May be higher outside the state; <a href="/wiki/Sales_tax" title="Sales tax">sales tax</a> is included at <a href="/wiki/Newspaper_vending_machine" title="Newspaper vending machine">newsracks</a>.
</p>
