<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Urban runoff</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Storm_Drain.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="1200" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Storm_Drain.JPG/220px-Storm_Drain.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Storm_Drain.JPG/330px-Storm_Drain.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Storm_Drain.JPG/440px-Storm_Drain.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Storm_Drain.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Urban runoff flowing into a storm drain</div></div></div>
<p><b>Urban runoff</b> is <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">surface runoff</a> of rainwater created by <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">urbanization</a>. This runoff is a major source of <a href="/wiki/Flood#Urban_flooding" title="Flood">flooding</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">water pollution</a> in urban communities worldwide.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Impervious_surfaces" title="Impervious surfaces">Impervious surfaces</a> (<a href="/wiki/Road" title="Road">roads</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parking_lot" title="Parking lot">parking lots</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sidewalk" title="Sidewalk">sidewalks</a>) are constructed during <a href="/wiki/Land_development" title="Land development">land development</a>. During <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a> storms and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> events, these surfaces (built from materials such as <a href="/wiki/Asphalt" title="Asphalt">asphalt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a>), along with <a href="/wiki/Roof" title="Roof">rooftops</a>, carry polluted <a href="/wiki/Stormwater" title="Stormwater">stormwater</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Storm_drains" title="Storm drains">storm drains</a>, instead of allowing the water to <a href="/wiki/Infiltration_(hydrology)" title="Infiltration (hydrology)">percolate</a> through <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS-runoff_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-runoff-1">[1]</a></sup> This causes lowering of the <a href="/wiki/Water_table" title="Water table">water table</a> (because <a href="/wiki/Groundwater_recharge" title="Groundwater recharge">groundwater recharge</a> is lessened) and <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flooding</a> since the amount of water that remains on the surface is greater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCE1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ASCE1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Most municipal storm sewer systems discharge stormwater, untreated, to <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">streams</a>, <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bay" title="Bay">bays</a>. This excess water can also make its way into people's properties through basement backups and seepage through building wall and floors.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_flooding">Urban flooding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_runoff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Urban flooding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Walking_in_the_Rain_New_Orleans.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2236" data-file-width="2982" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Walking_in_the_Rain_New_Orleans.jpg/220px-Walking_in_the_Rain_New_Orleans.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Walking_in_the_Rain_New_Orleans.jpg/330px-Walking_in_the_Rain_New_Orleans.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Walking_in_the_Rain_New_Orleans.jpg/440px-Walking_in_the_Rain_New_Orleans.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Walking_in_the_Rain_New_Orleans.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Flooded streets in <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Natural_%26_impervious_cover_diagrams_EPA.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="367" data-file-width="762" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Natural_%26_impervious_cover_diagrams_EPA.jpg/300px-Natural_%26_impervious_cover_diagrams_EPA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Natural_%26_impervious_cover_diagrams_EPA.jpg/450px-Natural_%26_impervious_cover_diagrams_EPA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Natural_%26_impervious_cover_diagrams_EPA.jpg/600px-Natural_%26_impervious_cover_diagrams_EPA.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Natural_%26_impervious_cover_diagrams_EPA.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Relationship between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Impervious_surfaces" title="Impervious surfaces">impervious surfaces</a> and <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">surface runoff</a></div></div></div>
<p>Urban runoff is a major cause of <a href="/wiki/Flood#Urban_flooding" title="Flood">urban flooding</a>, the inundation of land or property in a built-up environment caused by rainfall overwhelming the capacity of <a href="/wiki/Drainage" title="Drainage">drainage systems</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Storm_sewers" title="Storm sewers">storm sewers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS-water_cycle_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-water_cycle-4">[4]</a></sup> Triggered by events such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flash_flooding" title="Flash flooding">flash flooding</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Storm_surges" title="Storm surges">storm surges</a>, overbank flooding, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snow_melt" title="Snow melt">snow melt</a>, urban flooding is characterized by its repetitive, costly and systemic impacts on communities, regardless of whether or not these communities are located within formally designated floodplains or near any body of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are several ways in which stormwater enters properties: backup through sewer pipes, toilets and sinks into buildings; seepage through building walls and floors; the accumulation of water on property and in public rights-of-way; and the overflow of water from water bodies such as rivers and lakes. Where properties are built with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basements" title="Basements">basements</a>, urban flooding is the primary cause of basement flooding.
</p><p>Flood flows in urban environments have been investigated relatively recently despite many centuries of flood events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chanson_2011c_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chanson_2011c-6">[6]</a></sup> Some researchers mentioned the storage effect in urban areas. Several studies looked into the flow patterns and redistribution in streets during storm events and the implication in terms of flood modelling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Some recent research considered the criteria for safe evacuation of individuals in flooded areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chanson_2014_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chanson_2014-8">[8]</a></sup> But some recent field measurements during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%932011_Queensland_floods" title="2010–2011 Queensland floods">2010–2011 Queensland floods</a> showed that any criterion solely based upon the flow velocity, water depth or specific momentum cannot account for the hazards caused by the velocity and water depth fluctuations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chanson_2011c_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chanson_2011c-6">[6]</a></sup> These considerations ignore further the risks associated with large debris entrained by the flow motion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chanson_2014_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chanson_2014-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pollutants">Pollutants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_runoff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pollutants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pollutedflood.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Pollutedflood.JPG/220px-Pollutedflood.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Pollutedflood.JPG/330px-Pollutedflood.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Pollutedflood.JPG/440px-Pollutedflood.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pollutedflood.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A creek filled with urban runoff after a storm</div></div></div>
<p>Water running off these impervious surfaces tends to pick up <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>, <a href="/wiki/Motor_oil" title="Motor oil">motor oil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" title="Heavy metals">heavy metals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">trash</a> and other pollutants from roadways and parking lots, as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertilizers" title="Fertilizers">fertilizers</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pesticides" title="Pesticides">pesticides</a> from lawns. Roads and parking lots are major sources of <a href="/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbon" title="Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon">polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons</a> (PAHs), which are created as <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> byproducts of gasoline and other <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>, as well as of the heavy metals <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>. Roof runoff contributes high levels of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_synthesis" title="Chemical synthesis">synthetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> (from <a href="/wiki/Galvanization" title="Galvanization">galvanized</a> gutters). Fertilizer use on residential lawns, parks and golf courses is a measurable source of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrates" title="Nitrates">nitrates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a> in urban runoff when fertilizer is improperly applied or when turf is over-fertilized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCE1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ASCE1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Eroding soils or poorly maintained construction sites can often lead to increased <a href="/wiki/Nonpoint_source_pollution#Sediment" title="Nonpoint source pollution">sedimentation</a> in runoff. Sedimentation often settles to the bottom of water bodies and can directly affect water quality. Excessive levels of sediment in water bodies can increase the risk of infection and disease through high levels of nutrients present in the soil. These high levels of nutrients can reduce oxygen and boost algae growth while limiting native vegetation growth. Limited native vegetation and excessive algae has the potential to disrupt the entire aquatic ecosystem due to limited light penetration, lower oxygen levels, and reduced food reserves. Excessive levels of sediment and <a href="/wiki/Suspended_solids" title="Suspended solids">suspended solids</a> have the potential to damage existing infrastructure as well.  Sedimentation can increase runoff by plugging underground injection systems, thereby increasing the amount of runoff on the surface. Increased sedimentation levels can also reduce storage behind reservoirs.  This reduction of reservoir capacities can lead to increased expenses for public land agencies while also impacting the quality of water recreational areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Runoff can also induce heavy metal poisoning in ocean life. Small amounts of heavy metals are carried by runoff into the oceans. These metals are ingested by ocean life. These heavy metals cannot be disposed so they accumulate within the animals. Over time, these metals build up to a toxic level, and the animal dies. This heavy metal poisoning can also affect humans. If we eat a poisoned animal, we have a chance of getting heavy metal poisoning too.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>As stormwater is channeled into storm drains and surface waters, the natural <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a> load discharged to receiving waters decreases, but the water flow and velocity increases. In fact, the impervious cover in a typical city creates five times the runoff of a typical woodland of the same size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA2_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPA2-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<div style="clear:left;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_runoff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A 2008 report by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Research_Council" title="United States National Research Council">United States National Research Council</a> (textbox below) identified urban runoff as a leading source of <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">water quality</a> problems.
</p>
<blockquote class="toccolours" style="width:25%; float:right; padding:10px 15px; display:table;"><p>...further declines in water quality remain likely if the land-use changes that typify more diffuse sources of pollution are not addressed... These include land-disturbing agricultural, silvicultural, urban, industrial, and construction activities from which hard-to-monitor pollutants emerge during wet-weather events. Pollution from these landscapes has been almost universally acknowledged as the most pressing challenge to the restoration of waterbodies and aquatic ecosystems nationwide.<br/>
</p><p>  – National Research Council, <i>Urban Stormwater Management in the United States</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Weaselbrgrandavpassaicjeh.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2184" data-file-width="3072" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Weaselbrgrandavpassaicjeh.JPG/220px-Weaselbrgrandavpassaicjeh.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Weaselbrgrandavpassaicjeh.JPG/330px-Weaselbrgrandavpassaicjeh.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Weaselbrgrandavpassaicjeh.JPG/440px-Weaselbrgrandavpassaicjeh.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Weaselbrgrandavpassaicjeh.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Weasel Brook in <a href="/wiki/Passaic,_New_Jersey" title="Passaic, New Jersey">Passaic, New Jersey</a> has been <a href="/wiki/River_engineering#Channelization" title="River engineering">channelized</a> with concrete walls to control localized flooding.</div></div></div>
<p>The runoff also increases temperatures in streams, harming <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> and other organisms. (A sudden burst of runoff from a rainstorm can cause a fish-killing shock of hot water.) Also, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Road_salt" title="Road salt">road salt</a> used to melt snow on sidewalks and roadways can contaminate streams and groundwater <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>One of the most pronounced effects of urban runoff is on watercourses that historically contained little or no water during dry weather periods (often called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ephemeral_stream" title="Ephemeral stream">ephemeral streams</a></i>). When an area around such a stream is <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">urbanized</a>, the resultant runoff creates an unnatural year-round <a href="/wiki/Streamflow" title="Streamflow">streamflow</a> that hurts the vegetation, <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">wildlife</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stream_bed" title="Stream bed">stream bed</a> of the waterway. Containing little or no sediment relative to the historic ratio of sediment to water, urban runoff rushes down the stream channel, ruining natural features such as <a href="/wiki/Meander" title="Meander">meanders</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sandbar" title="Sandbar">sandbars</a>, and creates severe erosion—increasing sediment loads at the mouth while severely incising the <a href="/wiki/Stream_bed" title="Stream bed">stream bed</a> upstream. As an example, on many <a href="/wiki/Southern_California" title="Southern California">Southern California</a> beaches at the mouth of a waterway, urban runoff carries trash, pollutants, excessive silt, and other wastes, and can pose moderate to severe health hazards.
</p><p>Because of fertilizer and organic waste that urban runoff often carries, <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">eutrophication</a> often occurs in waterways affected by this type of runoff. After heavy rains, organic matter in the waterway is relatively high compared with natural levels, spurring growth of <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> blooms that soon consume most of the <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>. Once the naturally occurring oxygen in the water is depleted, the algae blooms die, and their decomposition causes further eutrophication. Algae blooms mostly occur in areas with still water, such as <a href="/wiki/Stream_pool" title="Stream pool">stream pools</a> and the pools behind <a href="/wiki/Dam" title="Dam">dams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Weir" title="Weir">weirs</a>, and some <a href="/wiki/Drop_structure" title="Drop structure">drop structures</a>. Eutrophication usually comes with deadly consequences for fish and other aquatic organisms.
</p><p>Excessive stream bank erosion may cause flooding and property damage. For many years governments have often responded to urban stream erosion problems by modifying the streams through construction of hardened <a href="/wiki/Levee" title="Levee">embankments</a> and similar control structures using concrete and masonry materials. Use of these hard materials destroys habitat for fish and other animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Such a project may stabilize the immediate area where flood damage occurred, but often it simply shifts the problem to an upstream or downstream segment of the stream.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <i>See <a href="/wiki/River_engineering" title="River engineering">River engineering</a></i>.
</p><p>Urban flooding has significant economic implications. In the US, industry experts estimate that wet basements can lower property values by 10%-25% and are cited among the top reasons for not purchasing a home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-18">[18]</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Emergency_Management_Agency" title="Federal Emergency Management Agency">U.S Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA)</a>, almost 40% of small businesses never reopen their doors following a flooding disaster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK" title="UK">UK</a>, urban flooding is estimated to cost £270 million a year in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>; 80,000 homes are at risk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>A study of <a href="/wiki/Cook_County,_Illinois" title="Cook County, Illinois">Cook County, Illinois</a>, identified 177,000 property damage insurance claims made across 96% of the county's ZIP codes over a five-year period from 2007-2011. This is the equivalent of one in six properties in the County making a claim. Average payouts per claim were $3,733 across all types of claims, with total claims amounting to $660 million over the five years examined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Despite concerted efforts, many communities lack the funds to fully address these issues, and often seek funds elsewhere. Numerous watersheds within <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_County,_California" title="Los Angeles County, California">Los Angeles County, California</a> do not meet state water quality standards, despite spending $100 million a year on clean water programs to combat issues such as urban runoff. To combat this problem, officials have introduced a measure that would assess a fee to homeowners and local businesses in attempt to raise $290 million for effective urban runoff management.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prevention_and_mitigation">Prevention and mitigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_runoff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Prevention and mitigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="mw-stack mobile-float-reset" style="box-sizing: border-box; overflow: hidden; float:right; margin:1px;">
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Percolation_trench.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="781" data-file-width="1182" height="198" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Percolation_trench.jpg/300px-Percolation_trench.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Percolation_trench.jpg/450px-Percolation_trench.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Percolation_trench.jpg/600px-Percolation_trench.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Percolation_trench.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <b><a href="/wiki/Percolation_trench" title="Percolation trench">percolation trench</a></b> <a href="/wiki/Infiltration_(hydrology)" title="Infiltration (hydrology)">infiltrates</a> stormwater through permeable soils into the <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifer</a>.</div></div></div>
<p><span id="OGS"></span>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Oil-grit_separator_USGS_2002.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1735" data-file-width="2545" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Oil-grit_separator_USGS_2002.png/220px-Oil-grit_separator_USGS_2002.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Oil-grit_separator_USGS_2002.png/330px-Oil-grit_separator_USGS_2002.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Oil-grit_separator_USGS_2002.png/440px-Oil-grit_separator_USGS_2002.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Oil-grit_separator_USGS_2002.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An <b>oil-grit separator</b> is designed to capture settleable solids, oil and grease, debris and floatables in runoff from roads and parking lots</div></div></div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff#Mitigation_and_treatment" title="Surface runoff">Surface runoff: Mitigation and treatment</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting" title="Rainwater harvesting">Rainwater harvesting</a></div>
<p>Effective control of urban runoff involves reducing the velocity and flow of stormwater, as well as reducing pollutant discharges. A variety of stormwater management practices and systems may be used to reduce the effects of urban runoff. Some of these techniques (called <a href="/wiki/Best_management_practice_for_water_pollution" title="Best management practice for water pollution">best management practices</a> (BMPs) in the US), focus on water quantity control, while others focus on improving water quality, and some perform both functions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>Pollution prevention practices include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low-impact_development_(Canada/US)" title="Low-impact development (Canada/US)">low impact development (LID)</a> or <a href="/wiki/Green_infrastructure" title="Green infrastructure">green infrastructure</a> techniques - known as <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_drainage_system" title="Sustainable drainage system">Sustainable Drainage Systems (SuDS)</a> in the UK, and <a href="/wiki/Water-sensitive_urban_design" title="Water-sensitive urban design">Water-Sensitive Urban Design (WSUD)</a> in Australia and the Middle East - such as the installation of <a href="/wiki/Green_roof" title="Green roof">green roofs</a> and improved chemical handling (e.g. management of motor fuels &amp; oil, fertilizers and pesticides).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA2_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-EPA2-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA3_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPA3-23">[23]</a></sup> Runoff mitigation systems include <a href="/wiki/Infiltration_basin" title="Infiltration basin">infiltration basins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bioretention" title="Bioretention">bioretention</a> systems, <a href="/wiki/Constructed_wetland" title="Constructed wetland">constructed wetlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Retention_basin" title="Retention basin">retention basins</a> and similar devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Providing effective urban runoff solutions often requires proper city programs that take into account the needs and differences of the community. Factors such as a city's mean temperature, precipitation levels, geographical location, and airborne pollutant levels can all effect rates of pollution in urban runoff and present unique challenges for management. Human factors such as urbanization rates, land use trends, and chosen building materials for impervious surfaces often exacerbate these issues.
</p><p>The implementation of citywide maintenance strategies such as street sweeping programs can also be an effective method in improving the quality of urban runoff. <a href="/wiki/Street_sweeper" title="Street sweeper">Street sweeping vacuums</a> collect particles of dust and suspended solids often found in public parking lots and roads that often end up in runoff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA4_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPA4-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yellow_Fish.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Yellow_Fish.jpg/220px-Yellow_Fish.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Yellow_Fish.jpg/330px-Yellow_Fish.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Yellow_Fish.jpg/440px-Yellow_Fish.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yellow_Fish.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Blue drain and yellow fish symbol used by the UK Environment Agency to raise awareness of the ecological impacts of contaminating surface drainage</div></div></div><p>Educational programs can also be an effective tool for managing urban runoff. Local businesses and individuals can have an integral role in reducing pollution in urban runoff simply through their practices, but often are unaware of regulations. Creating a productive discussion on urban runoff and the importance of effective disposal of household items can help to encourage environmentally friendly practices at a reduced cost to the city and local economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
