<p><b>Waste hierarchy</b> is a tool used in the evaluation of processes that <a href="/wiki/Environmental_protection" title="Environmental protection">protect the environment</a> alongside <a href="/wiki/Resource_consumption" title="Resource consumption">resource</a> and <a href="/wiki/Energy_consumption" title="Energy consumption">energy consumption</a> to most favourable to least favourable actions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hansen_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hansen-1">[1]</a></sup> The hierarchy establishes preferred program priorities based on <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hansen_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hansen-1">[1]</a></sup>  To be sustainable, waste management cannot be solved only with technical end-of-pipe solutions and an integrated approach is necessary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USEPA_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-USEPA-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <b>waste management hierarchy</b> indicates an order of preference for action to reduce and manage waste, and is usually presented diagrammatically in the form of a pyramid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNEP_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNEP-3">[3]</a></sup> The hierarchy captures the progression of a material or product through successive stages of <a href="/wiki/Waste_management" title="Waste management">waste management</a>, and represents the latter part of the <a href="/wiki/Product_lifecycle" title="Product lifecycle">life-cycle for each product</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNEP_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-UNEP-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The aim of the <b>waste hierarchy</b> is to extract the maximum practical benefits from products and to generate the minimum amount of waste.  The proper application of the waste hierarchy can have several benefits. It can help prevent emissions of greenhouse gases, reduces pollutants, save energy, conserves resources, create jobs and stimulate the development of green technologies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>1
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life-cycle_thinking">Life-cycle thinking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_hierarchy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life-cycle thinking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All products and services have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_impact" title="Environmental impact">environmental impacts</a>, from the extraction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raw_materials" title="Raw materials">raw materials</a> for production to manufacture, distribution, use and disposal.   Following the waste hierarchy will generally lead to the most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resource-efficient" title="Resource-efficient">resource-efficient</a> and environmentally sound choice but in some cases refining decisions within the hierarchy or departing from it can lead to better environmental outcomes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-EC-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Product_lifecycle" title="Product lifecycle">Life cycle</a> thinking and assessment can be used to support decision-making in the area of <a href="/wiki/Waste_management" title="Waste management">waste management</a> and to identify the best environmental options. It can help policy makers understand the benefits and trade-offs they have to face when making decisions on waste management strategies. <a href="/wiki/Life-cycle_assessment" title="Life-cycle assessment">Life-cycle assessment</a> provides an approach to ensure that the best outcome for the environment can be identified and put in place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-EC-5">[5]</a></sup>  It involves looking at all stages of a product’s life to find where improvements can be made to reduce environmental impacts and improve the use or <a href="/wiki/Reuse" title="Reuse">reuse</a> of resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-EC-5">[5]</a></sup>  A key goal is to avoid actions that shift negative impacts from one stage to another.  Life cycle thinking can be applied to the five stages of the waste management hierarchy.
</p><p>For example, life-cycle analysis has shown that it is often better for the environment to replace an old washing machine, despite the waste generated, than to continue to use an older machine which is less <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">energy-efficient</a>. This is because a washing machine’s greatest environmental impact is during its use phase. Buying an energy-efficient machine and using low- temperature detergent reduce environmental impacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-EC-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The European Union Waste Framework Directive has introduced the concept of life-cycle thinking into <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_policies&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Waste policies (page does not exist)">waste policies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-EC-5">[5]</a></sup>  This duality approach gives a broader view of all environmental aspects and ensures any action has an overall benefit compared to other options.  The actions to deal with waste along the hierarchy should be compatible with other <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_initiative&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Environmental initiative (page does not exist)">environmental initiatives</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Union_waste_framework_directive">European Union waste framework directive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_hierarchy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: European Union waste framework directive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1975, The European Union’s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waste_Framework_Directive" title="Waste Framework Directive">Waste Framework Directive</a> (1975/442/EEC) introduced for the first time the waste hierarchy concept into European waste policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ECWaste_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ECWaste-6">[6]</a></sup> It emphasized the importance of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waste_minimization" title="Waste minimization">waste minimization</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_protection" title="Environmental protection">protection of the environment</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_health" title="Human health">human health</a>, as a priority. Following the 1975 Directive, European Union policy and legislation adapted to the principles of the waste hierarchy.
</p><p>In 1989, it was formalized into a hierarchy of management options in the European Commission's Community Strategy for Waste Management and this waste strategy was further endorsed in the Commission's review in 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GOV.UK_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-GOV.UK-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2008, the European Union parliament introduced a new <i>five-step waste hierarchy</i> to its waste legislation, Directive 2008/98/EC, which member states must introduce into national waste management laws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ECWaste_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-ECWaste-6">[6]</a></sup> Article 4 of the directive lays down a five-step hierarchy of waste management options which must be applied by Member States in this priority order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ECWaste_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-ECWaste-6">[6]</a></sup>  Waste prevention, as the preferred option, is followed by reuse, recycling, recovery including <a href="/wiki/Energy_recovery" title="Energy recovery">energy recovery</a> and as a last option, safe disposal. Among engineers, a similar hierarchy of waste management has been known as ARRE strategy: avoid, reduce, recycle, eliminate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lienig_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-lienig-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Challenges_for_local_and_regional_authorities">Challenges for local and regional authorities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_hierarchy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Challenges for local and regional authorities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The task of implementing the waste hierarchy in waste management practices within a country may be delegated to the different levels of government (national, regional, local) and to other possible factors including industry, private companies and households. Local and regional authorities can be particularly challenged by the following issues when applying the waste hierarchy approach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hansen_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hansen-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>A coherent <a href="/wiki/Waste_management" title="Waste management">waste management</a> strategy must be set up</li>
<li>Separate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Garbage_collection" title="Garbage collection">collection</a> and <a href="/wiki/Waste_sorting" title="Waste sorting">sorting</a> systems for many different waste streams need to be established.</li>
<li>Adequate <a href="/wiki/Waste_treatment" title="Waste treatment">treatment</a> and disposal facilities must be established.</li>
<li>An effective horizontal co-operation between local authorities and municipalities and a vertical co-operation between the different levels of government, local to regional and when beneficial, also at the national level need to established</li>
<li>Finding financing for the establishing or upgrading of expensive sustainable waste management infrastructure to address the needs of managing waste</li>
<li>A lack of data available on waste management strategies must be overcome and monitoring requirements must be met to implement the waste programs</li>
<li>The enforcement and control of business plans and practices be established and applied to maximize benefits to the environment and human health</li>
<li>A lack of administrative capacity at the regional and local level. The lack of finances, information, and technical expertise must be overcome for effective implementation and success of the waste management policies.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Source_reduction">Source reduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_hierarchy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Source reduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Source_reduction" title="Source reduction">Source reduction</a> involves efforts to reduce <a href="/wiki/Hazardous_waste" title="Hazardous waste">hazardous waste</a> and other materials by modifying industrial production. Source reduction methods involve changes in manufacturing technology, <a href="/wiki/Raw_material" title="Raw material">raw material</a> inputs, and product formulation. At times, the term "pollution prevention" may refer to source reduction.
</p><p>Another method of source reduction is to increase incentives for recycling.  Many communities in the United States are implementing <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Variable-rate_pricing&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Variable-rate pricing (page does not exist)">variable-rate pricing</a> for waste disposal (also known as Pay As You Throw - <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PAYT" title="PAYT">PAYT</a>) which has been effective in reducing the size of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Municipal_waste" title="Municipal waste">municipal waste</a> stream.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ecocycle_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ecocycle-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Source reduction is typically measured by efficiencies and cutbacks in waste. <a href="/wiki/Toxics_use_reduction" title="Toxics use reduction">Toxics use reduction</a> is a more controversial approach to source reduction that targets and measures reductions in the upstream use of toxic materials. Toxics use reduction emphasizes the more preventive aspects of source reduction but, due to its emphasis on toxic chemical inputs, has been opposed more vigorously by chemical manufacturers. Toxics use reduction programs have been set up by legislation in some states, e.g., <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>. The 3 R's represent the 'Waste Hierarchy' which lists the best ways of managing waste from the most to the least desirable. Many of the things we currently throw away could be reused again with just a little thought and imagination.
</p>
