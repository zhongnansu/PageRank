<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Lipophilicity</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Lipophilicity</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> λίπος "fat" and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%86%CE%AF%CE%BB%CE%BF%CF%82" title="wikt:φίλος">φίλος</a> "friendly"), refers to the ability of a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> to dissolve in <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oil" title="Oil">oils</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipids</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-polar_solvent" title="Non-polar solvent">non-polar solvents</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Hexane" title="Hexane">hexane</a> or <a href="/wiki/Toluene" title="Toluene">toluene</a>. Such non-polar solvents are themselves lipophilic (translated as "fat-loving" or "fat-liking"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnson1999_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson1999-2">[2]</a></sup>), and the axiom that "like dissolves like" generally holds true. Thus lipophilic substances tend to dissolve in other lipophilic substances, but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophilic" title="Hydrophilic">hydrophilic</a> ("water-loving") substances tend to dissolve in water and other hydrophilic substances.
</p><p>Lipophilicity, hydrophobicity, and non-polarity may describe the same tendency towards participation in the <a href="/wiki/London_dispersion_force" title="London dispersion force">London dispersion force</a>, as the terms are often used interchangeably. However, the terms "lipophilic" and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophobicity" title="Hydrophobicity">hydrophobic</a>" are not synonymous, as can be seen with <a href="/wiki/Silicone" title="Silicone">silicones</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluorocarbons" title="Fluorocarbons">fluorocarbons</a>, which are hydrophobic but not lipophilic.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_bonding">Chemical bonding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lipophilicity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Chemical bonding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lipophilic substances interact within themselves and with other substances through the <a href="/wiki/London_dispersion_force" title="London dispersion force">London dispersion force</a>. They have little to no capacity to form <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bond" title="Hydrogen bond">hydrogen bonds</a>. When a molecule of a lipophilic substance is enveloped by water, surrounding water molecules enter into an "ice-like" structure the greater part of its molecular surface, the <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamically</a> unfavourable event that drives oily substances out of water. Being "driven out of water" is the quality of a substance referred to as hydrophobe (water-"avoiding" or "water-fearing"). Thus lipophilic substances tend to be water-insoluble. They invariably have large o/w (octanol/water) <a href="/wiki/Partition_coefficient" title="Partition coefficient">partition coefficients</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surfactants">Surfactants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lipophilicity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Surfactants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">Hydrocarbon</a>-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surfactants" title="Surfactants">surfactants</a> are compounds that are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphiphilic" title="Amphiphilic">amphiphilic</a> (or amphipathic), having a hydrophilic, water interactive "end", referred to as their "head group", and a lipophilic "end", usually a long chain hydrocarbon fragment, referred to as their "tail". They congregate at low energy surfaces, including the air-water interface (lowering <a href="/wiki/Surface_tension" title="Surface tension">surface tension</a>) and the surfaces of the water-immiscible droplets found in oil/water <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emulsions" title="Emulsions">emulsions</a> (lowering interfacial tension). At these surfaces they naturally orient themselves with their head groups in water and their tails either sticking up and largely out of water (as at the air-water interface) or dissolved in the water-immiscible phase that the water is in contact with (e.g. as the emulsified oil droplet). In both these configurations the head groups strongly interact with water while the tails avoid all contact with water. Surfactant molecules also aggregate in water as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micelles" title="Micelles">micelles</a> with their head groups sticking out and their tails bunched together. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micelles" title="Micelles">Micelles</a> draw oily substances into their hydrophobic cores, explaining the basic action of soaps and detergents used for personal cleanliness and for laundering clothes. Micelles are also biologically important for the transport of fatty substances in the small intestine surface in the first step that leads to the absorption of the components of fats (largely fatty acids and 2-monoglycerides).
</p><p>Cell membranes are bilayer structures principally formed from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phospholipids" title="Phospholipids">phospholipids</a>, molecules which have a highly water interactive, ionic <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a> head groups attached to two long alkyl tails.
</p><p>By contrast, <a href="/wiki/Fluorosurfactant" title="Fluorosurfactant">fluorosurfactants</a> are not amphiphilic or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Detergents" title="Detergents">detergents</a> because fluorocarbons are not lipophilic.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Oxybenzone" title="Oxybenzone">Oxybenzone</a>, a common cosmetic ingredient often used in sunscreens, is particularly penetrative because it is not very  lipophilic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Anywhere from 0.4% to 8.7% of oxybenzone can be absorbed after one topical sunscreen application, as measured in urine excretions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
