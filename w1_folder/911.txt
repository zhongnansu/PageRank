The <b>Bureau of Land Management</b> (<b>BLM</b>) is an agency within the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Interior" title="United States Department of the Interior">United States Department of the Interior</a> that administers more than 247.3 million acres (1,001,000 km<sup>2</sup>) of <a href="/wiki/Public_land" title="Public land">public lands</a> in the United States which constitutes one-eighth of the landmass of the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stats1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-stats1-2">[2]</a></sup> President <a href="/wiki/Harry_S._Truman" title="Harry S. Truman">Harry S. Truman</a> created the BLM in 1946 by combining two existing agencies: the <a href="/wiki/General_Land_Office" title="General Land Office">General Land Office</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grazing_Service" title="Grazing Service">Grazing Service</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-montana3_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-montana3-3">[3]</a></sup> The agency manages the federal government's nearly 700 million acres (2,800,000 km<sup>2</sup>) of subsurface <a href="/wiki/Mineral_rights" title="Mineral rights">mineral estate</a> located beneath federal, state and private lands severed from their surface rights by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homestead_Act_of_1862" title="Homestead Act of 1862">Homestead Act of 1862</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-montana3_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-montana3-3">[3]</a></sup> Most BLM public lands are located in these 12 western states: <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>, <a href="/wiki/Idaho" title="Idaho">Idaho</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wyoming" title="Wyoming">Wyoming</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cali_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-cali-4">[4]</a></sup> </p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:US_federal_land.agencies.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="765" data-file-width="990" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/US_federal_land.agencies.svg/220px-US_federal_land.agencies.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/US_federal_land.agencies.svg/330px-US_federal_land.agencies.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/US_federal_land.agencies.svg/440px-US_federal_land.agencies.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:US_federal_land.agencies.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This map shows land owned by different federal government agencies. The yellow represents the Bureau of Land Management's holdings.</div></div></div>
<p>The mission of the BLM is "to sustain the health, diversity, and productivity of the public lands for the use and enjoyment of present and future generations."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Originally BLM holdings were described as "land nobody wanted" because homesteaders had passed them by.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cali_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-cali-4">[4]</a></sup> All the same, ranchers hold nearly 18,000 permits and leases for livestock grazing on 155 million acres (630,000 km<sup>2</sup>) of BLM public lands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The agency manages 221 <a href="/wiki/Wilderness_area" title="Wilderness area">wilderness areas</a>, 27 <a href="/wiki/National_Monument_(United_States)" title="National Monument (United States)">national monuments</a> and some 636 other protected areas as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Landscape_Conservation_System" title="National Landscape Conservation System">National Landscape Conservation System</a>--now known as the National Conservation Lands, totaling about 36 million acres (150,000 km<sup>2</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLMconservation_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-BLMconservation-7">[7]</a></sup> In addition the National Conservation Lands include nearly 2,400 miles of <a href="/wiki/National_Wild_and_Scenic_Rivers_System" title="National Wild and Scenic Rivers System">Wild and Scenic Rivers</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>, and nearly 6,000 miles of National Scenic and Historic Trails.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> There are more than 63,000 oil and gas wells on BLM public lands. Total energy leases generated approximately $5.4 billion in 2013, an amount divided among the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Treasury" title="United States Department of the Treasury">Treasury</a>, the states, and <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native American</a> groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stats2_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-stats2-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bureau_of_Land_Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The BLM's roots go back to the <a href="/wiki/Land_Ordinance_of_1785" title="Land Ordinance of 1785">Land Ordinance of 1785</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Ordinance" title="Northwest Ordinance">Northwest Ordinance</a> of 1787.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gpo_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-gpo-13">[13]</a></sup> These laws provided for the survey and settlement of the lands that the original 13 colonies ceded to the federal government after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_War_of_Independence" title="American War of Independence">American Revolution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gpo_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-gpo-13">[13]</a></sup> As additional lands were acquired by the United States from <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and other countries, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> directed that they be explored, surveyed, and made available for settlement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gpo_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-gpo-13">[13]</a></sup> During the Revolutionary War, military bounty land was promised to soldiers who fought for the colonies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> After the war, the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_(1783)" title="Treaty of Paris (1783)">Treaty of Paris</a> of 1783, signed by the United States, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, ceded territory to the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In the 1780s, other states relinquished their own claims to land in modern-day <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ohio_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ohio-17">[17]</a></sup> By this time, the United States needed revenue to function.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landord_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-landord-18">[18]</a></sup> Land was sold so that the government would have money to survive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landord_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-landord-18">[18]</a></sup> In order to sell the land, surveys needed to be conducted. The <a href="/wiki/Land_Ordinance_of_1785" title="Land Ordinance of 1785">Land Ordinance of 1785</a> instructed a geographer to oversee this work as undertaken by a group of surveyors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landord_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-landord-18">[18]</a></sup> The first years of surveying were completed by trial and error; once the territory of Ohio had been surveyed, a modern public land survey system had been developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White_1991_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-White_1991-19">[19]</a></sup> In 1812, Congress established the <a href="/wiki/General_Land_Office" title="General Land Office">General Land Office</a> as part of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Treasury" title="United States Department of the Treasury">Department of the Treasury</a> to oversee the disposition of these federal lands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ohio_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-ohio-17">[17]</a></sup> By the early 1800s, promised bounty land claims were finally fulfilled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-war2_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-war2-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Over the years, other bounty land and homestead laws were enacted to dispose of federal land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gpo_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-gpo-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-war2_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-war2-20">[20]</a></sup> Several different types of patents existed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NARA_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-NARA-21">[21]</a></sup> These include cash entry, credit, homestead, Indian, military warrants, mineral certificates, private land claims, railroads, state selections, swamps, town sites, and town lots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NARA_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-NARA-21">[21]</a></sup> A system of local land offices spread throughout the territories, patenting land that was surveyed via the corresponding <a href="/wiki/Surveyor_General_of_the_Northwest_Territory" title="Surveyor General of the Northwest Territory">Office of the Surveyor General</a> of a particular territory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NARA_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-NARA-21">[21]</a></sup> This pattern gradually spread across the entire United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-White_1991_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-White_1991-19">[19]</a></sup> The laws that spurred this system with the exception of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Mining_Law_of_1872" title="General Mining Law of 1872">General Mining Law of 1872</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Desert_Land_Act" title="Desert Land Act">Desert Land Act</a> of 1877 have since been repealed or superseded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-varied_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-varied-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the early 20th century, Congress took additional steps toward recognizing the value of the assets on public lands and directed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Executive_Branch" title="Executive Branch">Executive Branch</a> to manage activities on the remaining public lands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-varied_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-varied-22">[22]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Mineral_Leasing_Act_of_1920" title="Mineral Leasing Act of 1920">Mineral Leasing Act of 1920</a> allowed leasing, exploration, and production of selected commodities, such as <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">oil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> to take place on public lands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taylor_Grazing_Act" title="Taylor Grazing Act">Taylor Grazing Act</a> of 1934 established the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Grazing_Service" title="United States Grazing Service">United States Grazing Service</a> to <a href="/wiki/Rangeland_management" title="Rangeland management">manage the public rangelands</a> by establishment of advisory boards that set grazing fees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-montana_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-montana-25">[25]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Oregon_and_California_Railroad_Revested_Lands" title="Oregon and California Railroad Revested Lands">Oregon and California Revested Lands Sustained Yield Management Act</a> of 1937, commonly referred as the O&amp;C Act, required sustained <a href="/wiki/Yield_management" title="Yield management">yield management</a> of the timberlands in western Oregon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ochistory_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-ochistory-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1946, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grazing_Service" title="Grazing Service">Grazing Service</a> was merged with the <a href="/wiki/General_Land_Office" title="General Land Office">General Land Office</a> to form the Bureau of Land Management within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_the_Interior" title="Department of the Interior">Department of the Interior</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-varied_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-varied-22">[22]</a></sup> It took several years for this new agency to integrate and reorganize.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In the end, the Bureau of Land Management became less focused on land disposal and more focused on the long term management and preservation of the land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-varied_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-varied-22">[22]</a></sup> The agency achieved its current form by combining offices in the western states and creating a corresponding office for lands both east of and alongside the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> As a matter of course,  the BLM's emphasis fell on activities in the western states as most of the mining, land sales, and federally owned areas are located west of the Mississippi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>BLM personnel on the ground have typically been oriented toward local interests, while bureau management in Washington are led by presidential guidance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-montana2_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-montana2-30">[30]</a></sup> By means of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Land_Policy_and_Management_Act" title="Federal Land Policy and Management Act">Federal Land Policy and Management Act</a> of 1976, Congress created a more unified bureau mission and recognized the value of the remaining public lands by declaring that these lands would remain in public ownership.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gpo_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-gpo-13">[13]</a></sup> The law directed that these lands be managed with a view toward "multiple use" defined as "management of the public lands and their various resource values so that they are utilized in the combination that will best meet the present and future needs of the American people."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since the <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Reagan</a> years of the 1980s, Republicans have often given priority to local control and to grazing, mining and petroleum production, while Democrats have more often emphasized environmental concerns even when granting mining and drilling leases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In September 1996, then President <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> used his authority under the <a href="/wiki/Antiquities_Act" title="Antiquities Act">Antiquities Act</a> to establish the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Staircase-Escalante_National_Monument" title="Grand Staircase-Escalante National Monument">Grand Staircase-Escalante National Monument</a> in southern Utah, the first of now 20 national monuments established on BLM lands and managed by the agency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLMconservation_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-BLMconservation-7">[7]</a></sup> The establishment of Grand Staircase-Escalante foreshadowed later creation of the BLM's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Landscape_Conservation_System" title="National Landscape Conservation System">National Landscape Conservation System</a> in 2000. Use of the Antiquities Act authority, to the extent it effectively scuttled a coal mine to have been operated by <a href="/wiki/UtahAmerican_Energy" title="UtahAmerican Energy">Andalex Resources</a>, delighted recreation and conservation enthusiasts but set up larger confrontations with state and local authorities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-headwaters_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-headwaters-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="BLM_programs">BLM programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bureau_of_Land_Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: BLM programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Public-Lands-Western-US.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="567" data-file-width="818" height="215" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Public-Lands-Western-US.png/310px-Public-Lands-Western-US.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Public-Lands-Western-US.png/465px-Public-Lands-Western-US.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Public-Lands-Western-US.png/620px-Public-Lands-Western-US.png 2x" width="310"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Public-Lands-Western-US.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Most of the public lands held by the Bureau of Land Management are located in the western states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<ul><li><b>Grazing</b>. The BLM manages livestock grazing on nearly 155 million acres (630,000 km<sup>2</sup>) million acres under the <a href="/wiki/Taylor_Grazing_Act_of_1934" title="Taylor Grazing Act of 1934">Taylor Grazing Act of 1934</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blmgrazing_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-blmgrazing-36">[36]</a></sup> The agency has granted more than 18,000 permits and leases to ranchers who graze their livestock, mostly cattle and sheep, at least part of the year on BLM public lands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blmgrazing_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-blmgrazing-36">[36]</a></sup> Permits and leases generally cover a 10-year period and are renewable if the BLM determines that the terms and conditions of the expiring permit or lease are being met.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blmgrazing_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-blmgrazing-36">[36]</a></sup> The federal grazing fee is adjusted annually and is calculated using a formula originally set by Congress in the <a href="/wiki/Public_Rangelands_Improvement_Act_of_1978" title="Public Rangelands Improvement Act of 1978">Public Rangelands Improvement Act of 1978</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blmgrazing_36-3"><a href="#cite_note-blmgrazing-36">[36]</a></sup> Under this formula, the grazing fee cannot fall below $1.35 per <a href="/wiki/Animal_unit" title="Animal unit">animal unit</a> month (AUM), nor can any fee increase or decrease exceed 25 percent of the previous year’s level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blmgrazing_36-4"><a href="#cite_note-blmgrazing-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The grazing fee for 2014 was set at $1.35 per AUM, the same level as for 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blmgrazing_36-5"><a href="#cite_note-blmgrazing-36">[36]</a></sup> Over time there has been a gradual decrease in the amount of grazing that takes place on BLM-managed land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blmgrazing_36-6"><a href="#cite_note-blmgrazing-36">[36]</a></sup> Grazing on public lands has declined from 18.2 million AUMs in 1954 to 7.9 million AUMs in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blmgrazing_36-7"><a href="#cite_note-blmgrazing-36">[36]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Mining</b>. Domestic production from over 63,000 Federal "onshore" <a href="/wiki/Oil_well" title="Oil well">oil</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_well" title="Gas well">gas wells</a> on BLM lands accounts for 11 percent of the <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> supply and five percent of the <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">oil</a> supply in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> BLM has on record a total of 290,000 mining claims under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Mining_Law_of_1872" title="General Mining Law of 1872">General Mining Law of 1872</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blmmining_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-blmmining-39">[39]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Coal leases</b>. The BLM holds the coal mineral estate to more than 570 million acres (2,300,000 km<sup>2</sup>) where the owner of the surface is the federal government, a state or local government, or a private entity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coal_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-coal-40">[40]</a></sup> As of 2013, the BLM had competitively granted 309 leases for coal mining to 474,252 acres (191,923 ha), an increase of 13,487 acres (5,458 ha) or nearly 3% increase in land subject to coal production over ten years' time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coal_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-coal-40">[40]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Recreation</b>. The BLM administers 205,498 miles (330,717 km) of fishable streams, 2.2 million acres (8,900 km<sup>2</sup>) of <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reservoir_(water)" title="Reservoir (water)">reservoirs</a>, 6,600 miles (10,600 km) of floatable rivers, over 500 boating access points, 69 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Back_Country_Byway" title="National Back Country Byway">National Back Country Byways</a>, and 300 Watchable Wildlife sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-outfit_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-outfit-41">[41]</a></sup> The agency also manages 4,500 miles (7,200 km) of <a href="/wiki/National_Scenic_Trail" title="National Scenic Trail">National Scenic</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Trail" title="National Historic Trail">National Historic</a> and <a href="/wiki/National_Recreation_Trail" title="National Recreation Trail">National Recreation Trails</a>, as well as thousands of miles of multiple use trails used by motorcyclists, hikers, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equestrians" title="Equestrians">equestrians</a>, and mountain bikers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-outfit_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-outfit-41">[41]</a></sup> In 2013, BLM lands received an estimated 61.7 million recreational visitors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stats_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-stats-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>California Desert Conservation Area</b>. The California Desert Conservation Area covers 25 million acres (100,000 km<sup>2</sup>) of land in <a href="/wiki/Southern_California" title="Southern California">southern California</a> designated by Congress in 1976 by means of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Land_Policy_and_Management_Act" title="Federal Land Policy and Management Act">Federal Land Policy and Management Act</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDCA_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDCA-43">[43]</a></sup> BLM is charged with administering about 10 million acres (40,000 km<sup>2</sup>)  of this fragile area with its potential for <a href="/wiki/Multiple_use" title="Multiple use">multiple uses</a> in mind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDCA_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-CDCA-43">[43]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Timberlands</b>. The Bureau manages 55 million acres (220,000 km<sup>2</sup>) of <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a> and <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodlands</a>, including 11 million acres (45,000 km<sup>2</sup>) of commercial forest and 44 million acres (180,000 km<sup>2</sup>) of woodlands in 11 western states and <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gaotimber_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-gaotimber-44">[44]</a></sup> 53 million acres (210,000 km<sup>2</sup>) are productive forests and woodlands on public domain lands and 2.4 million acres (9,700 km<sup>2</sup>) are on <a href="/wiki/Oregon_and_California_Railroad_Revested_Lands" title="Oregon and California Railroad Revested Lands">O&amp;C lands</a> in western Oregon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gaotimber_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-gaotimber-44">[44]</a></sup></li></ul>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Calm_Before_the_Storm_(8555289958).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2549" data-file-width="2128" height="264" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Calm_Before_the_Storm_%288555289958%29.jpg/220px-Calm_Before_the_Storm_%288555289958%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Calm_Before_the_Storm_%288555289958%29.jpg/330px-Calm_Before_the_Storm_%288555289958%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Calm_Before_the_Storm_%288555289958%29.jpg/440px-Calm_Before_the_Storm_%288555289958%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Calm_Before_the_Storm_(8555289958).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Calm Before the Storm</i>: Fatigued BLM Firefighters taking a break after a fire in Oregon in 2008</div></div></div>
<ul><li><b>Firefighting.</b> Well in excess of 3,000 <a href="/wiki/Full-time_equivalent" title="Full-time equivalent">full-time equivalent</a> firefighting personnel work for BLM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLMfire_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-BLMfire-45">[45]</a></sup> The agency fought 2,573 fires on BLM-managed lands in fiscal year 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stats_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-stats-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Mineral rights on Indian lands</b>. As part of its trust responsibilities, the BLM provides technical advice for minerals operations on 56 million acres (230,000 km<sup>2</sup>) of Indian lands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blmMinerals_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-blmMinerals-46">[46]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Leasing and Land Management of Split Estates</b>. A split estate is similar to the <a href="/wiki/Broad_form_deed" title="Broad form deed">broad form deeds</a> used, starting in the early 1900s. It is a separation of mineral rights and surface rights on a property. The BLM manages split estates, but only in cases when the "surface rights are privately owned and the rights to the minerals are held by the Federal Government."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Cadastral surveys</b>. The BLM is the official record keeper for over 200 years' worth of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cadastral_survey" title="Cadastral survey">cadastral survey</a> records and plats as part of the <a href="/wiki/Public_Land_Survey_System" title="Public Land Survey System">Public Land Survey System</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-surveys_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-surveys-48">[48]</a></sup> In addition, the Bureau still completes numerous new surveys each year, mostly in <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, and conducts resurveys to restore obliterated or lost original surveys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-surveys_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-surveys-48">[48]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Abandoned mines</b>. BLM maintains an inventory of known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abandoned_mines" title="Abandoned mines">abandoned mines</a> on the lands it manages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blmmines_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-blmmines-49">[49]</a></sup> As of April 2014, the inventory contained nearly 46,000 sites and 85,000 other features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blmmines_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-blmmines-49">[49]</a></sup> Approximately 23% of the sites had either been remediated, had reclamation actions planned or underway, or did not require further action. The remaining sites require further investigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blmmines_49-2"><a href="#cite_note-blmmines-49">[49]</a></sup>  A 2008 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inspector_General" title="Inspector General">Inspector General</a> report alleges that BLM has for decades neglected the dangers represented by these abandoned mines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IGmines_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-IGmines-50">[50]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Energy corridors</b>. Approximately 5,000 miles (8,000 km) of energy corridors for <a href="/wiki/Pipeline_transport" title="Pipeline transport">pipelines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transmission_line" title="Transmission line">transmission lines</a> are located on BLM-managed lands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apps1.eere.energy.gov_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-apps1.eere.energy.gov-51">[51]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Helium</b>. BLM operates the <a href="/wiki/National_Helium_Reserve" title="National Helium Reserve">National Helium Reserve</a> near Amarillo, Texas, a program begun in 1925 during the time of the <a href="/wiki/Zeppelin" title="Zeppelin">Zeppelin</a> Wars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slatehelium_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-slatehelium-52">[52]</a></sup> Though the reserve had been set to be moved to private hands, it remains subject to oversight of the BLM under the provisions of the unanimously-passed Responsible Helium Administration and Stewardship Act of 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slatehelium_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-slatehelium-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Revenue and fees</b>. The BLM produces significant revenue for the United States budget.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bisson_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-bisson-54">[54]</a></sup> In 2009, public lands were expected to generate an estimated $6.2 billion in revenues, mostly from energy development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bisson_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-bisson-54">[54]</a></sup> Nearly 43.5 percent of these funds are provided directly to states and counties to support roads, schools, and other community needs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bisson_54-2"><a href="#cite_note-bisson-54">[54]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Landscape_Conservation_System">National Landscape Conservation System</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bureau_of_Land_Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: National Landscape Conservation System">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Landscape_Conservation_System" title="National Landscape Conservation System">National Landscape Conservation System</a></div>
<p>Established in 2000, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Landscape_Conservation_System" title="National Landscape Conservation System">National Landscape Conservation System</a> is overseen by the BLM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilderness_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-wilderness-55">[55]</a></sup> The National Landscape Conservation System lands constitute just about 12% of the lands managed by the BLM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilderness_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-wilderness-55">[55]</a></sup> Congress passed Title II of the <a href="/wiki/Omnibus_Public_Land_Management_Act_of_2009" title="Omnibus Public Land Management Act of 2009">Omnibus Public Land Management Act of 2009</a> (Public Law 111-11) to make the system a permanent part of the public lands protection system in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilderness_55-2"><a href="#cite_note-wilderness-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> By designating these areas for conservation, the law directed the BLM to ensure these places are protected for future generations, similar to <a href="/wiki/National_park" title="National park">national parks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_refuge" title="Wildlife refuge">wildlife refuges</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilderness_55-3"><a href="#cite_note-wilderness-55">[55]</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Law_enforcement_and_security">Law enforcement and security</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bureau_of_Land_Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Law enforcement and security">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2011-08-04_20_00_00_Susie_Fire_in_the_Adobe_Range_west_of_Elko_Nevada.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/2011-08-04_20_00_00_Susie_Fire_in_the_Adobe_Range_west_of_Elko_Nevada.jpg/220px-2011-08-04_20_00_00_Susie_Fire_in_the_Adobe_Range_west_of_Elko_Nevada.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/2011-08-04_20_00_00_Susie_Fire_in_the_Adobe_Range_west_of_Elko_Nevada.jpg/330px-2011-08-04_20_00_00_Susie_Fire_in_the_Adobe_Range_west_of_Elko_Nevada.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/2011-08-04_20_00_00_Susie_Fire_in_the_Adobe_Range_west_of_Elko_Nevada.jpg/440px-2011-08-04_20_00_00_Susie_Fire_in_the_Adobe_Range_west_of_Elko_Nevada.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2011-08-04_20_00_00_Susie_Fire_in_the_Adobe_Range_west_of_Elko_Nevada.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lightning-sparked wildfires are frequent occurrences on BLM land in Nevada.</div></div></div><p>The BLM, through its Office of Law Enforcement &amp; Security, functions as a <a href="/wiki/Federal_law_enforcement_in_the_United_States" title="Federal law enforcement in the United States">federal law enforcement agency</a>  of the United States Government. BLM law enforcement <a href="/wiki/Park_ranger" title="Park ranger">rangers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Special_agent" title="Special agent">special agents</a> receive their training through <a href="/wiki/Federal_Law_Enforcement_Training_Centers" title="Federal Law Enforcement Training Centers">Federal Law Enforcement Training Centers (FLETC)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Full-time staffing for these positions approaches 300.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-green_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-green-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blmLE_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-blmLE-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>Uniformed rangers enforce laws and regulations governing BLM lands and resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blmrangers_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-blmrangers-61">[61]</a></sup> As part of that mission, these BLM rangers carry firearms, defensive equipment, make arrests, execute search warrants, complete reports and testify in court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blmrangers_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-blmrangers-61">[61]</a></sup> They seek to establish a regular and recurring presence on a vast amount of public lands, roads and recreation sites. They focus on the protection of natural and cultural resources, other BLM employees and visitors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blmrangers_61-2"><a href="#cite_note-blmrangers-61">[61]</a></sup> Given the many locations of BLM public lands, these rangers use canines, helicopters, snowmobiles, dirt bikes and boats to perform their duties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blmrangers_61-3"><a href="#cite_note-blmrangers-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p><p>By contrast BLM <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_agents" title="Special agents">special agents</a> are <a href="/wiki/Detective" title="Detective">criminal investigators</a> who plan and conduct investigations concerning possible violations of criminal and administrative provisions of the BLM and other statutes under the United States Code.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLMSA_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-BLMSA-62">[62]</a></sup> Special agents are normally plain clothes officers who carry concealed firearms, and other defensive equipment, make arrests, carry out complex criminal investigations, present cases for prosecution to local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Attorneys" title="United States Attorneys">United States Attorneys</a> and prepare investigative reports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLMSA_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-BLMSA-62">[62]</a></sup> Criminal investigators occasionally conduct internal and civil claim investigations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLMSA_62-2"><a href="#cite_note-BLMSA-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wild_horse_and_burro_program">Wild horse and burro program</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bureau_of_Land_Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Wild horse and burro program">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Mustang" title="Mustang">Mustang</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burro" title="Burro">Burro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wild_and_Free-Roaming_Horses_and_Burros_Act_of_1971" title="Wild and Free-Roaming Horses and Burros Act of 1971">Wild and Free-Roaming Horses and Burros Act of 1971</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_BLM_Herd_Management_Areas" title="List of BLM Herd Management Areas">List of BLM Herd Management Areas</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TuleHorses.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/TuleHorses.JPG/300px-TuleHorses.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/TuleHorses.JPG/450px-TuleHorses.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/TuleHorses.JPG/600px-TuleHorses.JPG 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TuleHorses.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Mustang" title="Mustang">Mustangs</a> run across <a href="/wiki/Tule_Valley" title="Tule Valley">Tule Valley</a>, Utah</div></div></div>
<p>The BLM manages <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mustang_(horse)" title="Mustang (horse)">free-roaming horses</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burro" title="Burro">burros</a> on public lands in ten western states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QF_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-QF-63">[63]</a></sup> Though they are <a href="/wiki/Feral" title="Feral">feral</a>, the agency is obligated to protect them under the <a href="/wiki/Wild_and_Free-Roaming_Horses_and_Burros_Act_of_1971" title="Wild and Free-Roaming Horses and Burros Act of 1971">Wild and Free-Roaming Horses and Burros Act of 1971</a> (WFRHBA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QF_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-QF-63">[63]</a></sup> As the horses have few natural predators, populations have grown substantially.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QF_63-2"><a href="#cite_note-QF-63">[63]</a></sup> WFRHBA as enacted provides for the removal of excess animals; the destruction of lame, old, or sick animals; the private placement or adoption of excess animals; and even the destruction of healthy animals if range management required it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iraola_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-iraola-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SterbaRevived_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-SterbaRevived-65">[65]</a></sup> In fact, the destruction of healthy or unhealthy horses has almost never occurred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Pursuant to the <a href="/wiki/Public_Rangelands_Improvement_Act_of_1978" title="Public Rangelands Improvement Act of 1978">Public Rangelands Improvement Act of 1978</a>, the BLM has established 179 "herd management areas" (HMAs) covering 31.6 million acres (128,000 km<sup>2</sup>) acres where feral horses can be found on federal lands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QF_63-3"><a href="#cite_note-QF-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1973, BLM began a pilot project on the <a href="/wiki/Pryor_Mountains_Wild_Horse_Range" title="Pryor Mountains Wild Horse Range">Pryor Mountains Wild Horse Range</a> known as the Adopt-A-Horse initiative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pitt528_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pitt528-67">[67]</a></sup> The program took advantage of provisions in the WFRHBA to allow private "qualified" individuals to "adopt" as many horses as they wanted if they could show that they could provide adequate care for the animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glover11111112_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-Glover11111112-68">[68]</a></sup> At the time, title to the horses remained permanently with the federal government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SterbaRevived_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-SterbaRevived-65">[65]</a></sup> The pilot project was so successful that BLM allowed it to go nationwide in 1976.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pitt528_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pitt528-67">[67]</a></sup> The Adopt-a-Horse program quickly became the primary method of removing excess feral horses from BLM land given the lack of other viable methods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glover11111112_68-1"><a href="#cite_note-Glover11111112-68">[68]</a></sup> The BLM also uses limited amounts of contraceptives in the herd, in the form of <a href="/wiki/Porcine_zona_pellucida" title="Porcine zona pellucida">PZP</a> vaccinations; advocates say that additional use of these vaccines would help to diminish the excess number of horses currently under BLM management.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RaiaFuture_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-RaiaFuture-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>Despite the early successes of the adoption program, the BLM has struggled to maintain acceptable herd levels, as without natural predators, herd sizes can double every four years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QF_63-4"><a href="#cite_note-QF-63">[63]</a></sup> As of 2014, there were more than 49,000 horses and burros on BLM-managed land, exceeding the BLM's estimated "appropriate management level" (AML) by almost 22,500.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QF_63-5"><a href="#cite_note-QF-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Bureau of Land Management has implemented several programs and has developed partnerships as part of their management plan for preserving wild burros and horses in the United States. There are several herds of horses and burros roaming free on 26.9 million acres of range spread out in ten western states. It is essential to maintain a balance that keeps herd management land and animal population healthy. Some programs and partnerships include the Mustang Heritage Foundation, U.S. Border Patrol, Idaho 4H, Napa Mustang Days and Little Book Cliffs Darting Team. These partnerships help with adoption and animal population as well as education and raising awareness about wild horses and burros.  <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Renewable_energy">Renewable energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bureau_of_Land_Management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Renewable energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ivanpah_Solar_Power_Facility_from_the_air_2014.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3806" data-file-width="5701" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Ivanpah_Solar_Power_Facility_from_the_air_2014.jpg/220px-Ivanpah_Solar_Power_Facility_from_the_air_2014.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Ivanpah_Solar_Power_Facility_from_the_air_2014.jpg/330px-Ivanpah_Solar_Power_Facility_from_the_air_2014.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Ivanpah_Solar_Power_Facility_from_the_air_2014.jpg/440px-Ivanpah_Solar_Power_Facility_from_the_air_2014.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ivanpah_Solar_Power_Facility_from_the_air_2014.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Aerial photograph of <a href="/wiki/Ivanpah_Solar_Power_Facility" title="Ivanpah Solar Power Facility">Ivanpah Solar Power Facility</a> located on BLM-managed land in the <a href="/wiki/Mojave_Desert" title="Mojave Desert">Mojave Desert</a></div></div></div>
<p>In 2009, BLM opened Renewable Energy Coordination Offices in order to approve and oversee wind, solar, biomass, and geothermal projects on BLM-managed lands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apps1.eere.energy.gov_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-apps1.eere.energy.gov-51">[51]</a></sup> The offices were located in the four states where energy companies had shown the greatest interest in renewable energy development: <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wyoming" title="Wyoming">Wyoming</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apps1.eere.energy.gov_51-2"><a href="#cite_note-apps1.eere.energy.gov-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Solar energy.</b> In 2010, BLM approved the first utility-scale <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar energy</a> projects on public land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sunland_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-sunland-71">[71]</a></sup> As of 2014, 70 solar energy projects covering 560,000 acres (2,300 km<sup>2</sup>) had been proposed on public lands managed by BLM primarily located in <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLMFactSolar_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-BLMFactSolar-72">[72]</a></sup> To date, it has approved 29 projects that have the potential to generate 8,786 megawatts of renewable energy or enough energy to power roughly 2.6 million homes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLMFactSolar_72-1"><a href="#cite_note-BLMFactSolar-72">[72]</a></sup> The projects range in size from a 45-megawatt photovoltaic system on 422 acres (171 ha) to a 1,000-megawatt parabolic trough system on 7,025 acres (2,843 ha).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLMFactSolar_72-2"><a href="#cite_note-BLMFactSolar-72">[72]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Wind energy.</b> BLM manages 20.6 million acres (83,000 km<sup>2</sup>) of public lands with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wind_energy" title="Wind energy">wind potential</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newenergy_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-newenergy-73">[73]</a></sup> It has authorized 39 wind energy development projects with a total approved capacity of 5,557 megawatts or enough to supply the power needs of over 1.5 million homes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BLMwind_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-BLMwind-74">[74]</a></sup>  In addition, BLM has authorized over 100 wind energy testing sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gaorenew_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-gaorenew-75">[75]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Geothermal energy.</b> BLM manages 59 geothermal leases in producing status, with a total capacity of 1,500 megawatts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geoBLM_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-geoBLM-76">[76]</a></sup> This amounts to over 40 percent of the <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_energy" title="Geothermal energy">geothermal energy</a> capacity in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geoBLM_76-1"><a href="#cite_note-geoBLM-76">[76]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Biomass and bioenergy.</b> Its large portfolio of productive timberlands leaves BLM with woody <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> among its line of forest products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biomass_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-biomass-77">[77]</a></sup> The biomass is composed of "smaller diameter materials" and other debris that result from timber production and forest management.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biomass_77-1"><a href="#cite_note-biomass-77">[77]</a></sup> Though the use of these materials as a renewable resource is nascent, the agency is engaged in pilot projects to increase the use of its biomass supplies in <a href="/wiki/Bioenergy" title="Bioenergy">bioenergy</a> programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biomass_77-2"><a href="#cite_note-biomass-77">[77]</a></sup></li></ul>
