<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Triple point</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Tripoint" title="Tripoint">Tripoint</a> and <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Tripoint_(disambiguation)" title="Tripoint (disambiguation)">Tripoint (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>, the <b>triple point</b> of a substance is the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> at which the three <a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">phases</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solid</a>) of that substance coexist in <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium" title="Thermodynamic equilibrium">thermodynamic equilibrium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gold_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-gold-1">[1]</a></sup> For example, the triple point of <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> occurs at a temperature of −38.83440 °C and a pressure of 0.2 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milli" title="Milli">m</a><a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">Pa</a>.
</p><p>In addition to the triple point for solid, liquid, and gas phases, a triple point may involve more than one solid phase, for substances with multiple <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorphs</a>. <a href="/wiki/Helium-4" title="Helium-4">Helium-4</a> is a special case that presents a triple point involving two different fluid phases (<a href="/wiki/Lambda_point" title="Lambda point">lambda point</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gold_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-gold-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The triple point of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> is used to define the <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">kelvin</a>, the <a href="/wiki/SI_base_unit" title="SI base unit">base unit</a> of thermodynamic temperature in the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a> (SI).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The value of the triple point of water is fixed by definition, rather than measured. The triple points of several substances are used to define points in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ITS-90" title="ITS-90">ITS-90</a> international temperature scale, ranging from the triple point of hydrogen (13.8033 K) to the triple point of water (273.16 K, 0.01 °C, or 32.018 °F).
</p><p>The term "triple point" was coined in 1873 by <a href="/wiki/James_Thomson_(engineer)" title="James Thomson (engineer)">James Thomson</a>, brother of <a href="/wiki/William_Thomson,_1st_Baron_Kelvin" title="William Thomson, 1st Baron Kelvin">Lord Kelvin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Triple_point_of_water">Triple point of water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triple_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Triple point of water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Gas.E2.80.93liquid.E2.80.93solid_triple_point"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas–liquid–solid_triple_point">Gas–liquid–solid triple point</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triple_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Gas–liquid–solid triple point">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Properties_of_water#Triple_point" title="Properties of water">Properties of water § Triple point</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Phase-diag2.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="443" data-file-width="530" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Phase-diag2.svg/350px-Phase-diag2.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Phase-diag2.svg/525px-Phase-diag2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Phase-diag2.svg/700px-Phase-diag2.svg.png 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Phase-diag2.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A typical phase diagram. The solid green line applies to most substances; the dotted green line gives the anomalous behavior of water</div></div></div>
<p>The single combination of pressure and temperature at which liquid <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, solid <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> can coexist in a stable equilibrium occurs at exactly 273.16 K (0.01 °C; 32.02 °F) and a partial vapor pressure of 611.657 pascals (6.11657 mbar; 0.00603659 atm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wagner_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wagner-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murphy_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Murphy-5">[5]</a></sup> At that point, it is possible to change all of the substance to ice, water, or vapor by making arbitrarily small changes in pressure and temperature. Even if the total pressure of a system is well above the triple point of water, provided that the <a href="/wiki/Partial_pressure" title="Partial pressure">partial pressure</a> of the water vapor is 611.657 pascals, then the system can still be brought to the triple point of water. Strictly speaking, the surfaces separating the different phases should also be perfectly flat, to negate the effects of surface tension.
</p><p>The gas–liquid–solid triple point of water corresponds to the minimum pressure at which liquid water can exist. At pressures below the triple point (as in <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a>), solid ice when heated at constant pressure is converted directly into water vapor in a process known as <a href="/wiki/Sublimation_(phase_transition)" title="Sublimation (phase transition)">sublimation</a>. Above the triple point, solid ice when heated at constant pressure first melts to form liquid water, and then evaporates or boils to form vapor at a higher temperature.
</p><p>For most substances the gas–liquid–solid triple point is also the minimum temperature at which the liquid can exist. For water, however, this is not true because the melting point of ordinary ice decreases as a function of pressure, as shown by the dotted green line in the phase diagram. At temperatures just below the triple point, compression at constant temperature transforms water vapor first to solid and then to liquid (water ice has lower density than liquid water, so increasing pressure leads to a <a href="/wiki/Liquefaction" title="Liquefaction">liquefaction</a>).
</p><p>The triple point pressure of water was used during the <a href="/wiki/Mariner_9" title="Mariner 9">Mariner 9</a> mission to <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> as a reference point to define "sea level". More recent missions use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laser_altimetry" title="Laser altimetry">laser altimetry</a> and gravity measurements instead of pressure to define elevation on Mars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="High_pressure_phases">High pressure phases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triple_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: High pressure phases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At high pressures, water has a complex phase diagram with 15 known <a href="/wiki/Ice#Phases" title="Ice">phases of ice</a> and several triple points including ten whose coordinates are shown in the diagram. For example, the triple point at 251 K (−22 °C) and 210 MPa (2070 atm) corresponds to the conditions for the coexistence of <a href="/wiki/Ice_Ih" title="Ice Ih">ice Ih</a> (ordinary ice), <a href="/wiki/Ice_III" title="Ice III">ice III</a> and liquid water, all at equilibrium. There are also triple points for the coexistence of three solid phases, for example <a href="/wiki/Ice_II" title="Ice II">ice II</a>, ice V and ice VI at 218 K (−55 °C) and 620 MPa (6120 atm).
</p><p>For those high-pressure forms of ice which can exist in equilibrium with liquid, the diagram shows that melting points increase with pressure. At temperatures above <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002273000000000000♠</span>273 K</span> (0 °C), increasing the pressure on water vapor results first in liquid water and then a high-pressure form of ice. In the range <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002251000000000000♠</span>251–273 K</span>, ice I is formed first, followed by liquid water and then ice III or ice V, followed by other still denser high-pressure forms.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Triple_point_cells">Triple point cells</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triple_point&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Triple point cells">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Triple point cells are used in the <a href="/wiki/Calibration" title="Calibration">calibration</a> of <a href="/wiki/Thermometer" title="Thermometer">thermometers</a>.  For exacting work, triple point cells are typically filled with a highly pure chemical substance such as hydrogen, argon, mercury, or water (depending on the desired temperature).  The purity of these substances can be such that only one part in a million is a contaminant, called "six nines" because it is 99.9999% pure.  When it is a water-based cell, a special <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopic</a> composition called <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Standard_Mean_Ocean_Water" title="Vienna Standard Mean Ocean Water">VSMOW</a> is used because it is very pure and produces temperatures that are more comparable from lab to lab.  Triple point cells are so effective at achieving highly precise, reproducible temperatures, an international calibration standard for thermometers called <a href="/wiki/International_Temperature_Scale_of_1990" title="International Temperature Scale of 1990">ITS–90</a> relies upon triple point cells of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neon" title="Neon">neon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" title="Water (molecule)">water</a> for delineating six of its defined temperature points.
</p>
