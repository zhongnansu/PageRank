<p>An <b>explosive material</b>, also called an <b>explosive</b>, is a reactive substance that contains a great amount of potential energy that can produce an <a href="/wiki/Explosion" title="Explosion">explosion</a> if released suddenly, usually accompanied by the production of <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sound</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a>. An <b>explosive charge</b> is a measured quantity of explosive material, which may be composed of a single ingredient or a combination of two or more.

</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Chemical_energy" title="Chemical energy">chemical energy</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Nitroglycerin" title="Nitroglycerin">nitroglycerin</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grain_dust" title="Grain dust">grain dust</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressurized</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_compressor" title="Gas compressor">gas</a>, such as a <a href="/wiki/Gas_cylinder" title="Gas cylinder">gas cylinder</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerosol_can" title="Aerosol can">aerosol can</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear</a> energy, such as in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fissile" title="Fissile">fissile</a> <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a> <a href="/wiki/Uranium-235" title="Uranium-235">uranium-235</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plutonium-239" title="Plutonium-239">plutonium-239</a></li></ul>
<p>Explosive materials may be categorized by the speed at which they expand. Materials that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Detonate" title="Detonate">detonate</a> (the front of the chemical reaction moves faster through the material than the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">speed of sound</a>) are said to be "high explosives" and materials that <a href="/wiki/Deflagration" title="Deflagration">deflagrate</a> are said to be "low explosives". Explosives may also be categorized by their <a href="/wiki/Sensitivity_(explosives)" title="Sensitivity (explosives)">sensitivity</a>. Sensitive materials that can be initiated by a relatively small amount of heat or pressure are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primary_explosive" title="Primary explosive">primary explosives</a> and materials that are relatively insensitive are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secondary_explosives" title="Secondary explosives">secondary</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tertiary_explosive" title="Tertiary explosive">tertiary explosives</a>.
</p><p>A wide variety of chemicals can explode; a smaller number are manufactured specifically for the purpose of being used as explosives. The remainder are too dangerous, sensitive, toxic, expensive, unstable, or prone to decomposition or degradation over short time spans.
</p><p>In contrast, some materials are merely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combustible" title="Combustible">combustible</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flammable" title="Flammable">flammable</a> if they burn without exploding.
</p><p>The distinction, however, is not razor-sharp. Certain materials—dusts, powders, gases, or volatile organic liquids—may be simply combustible or flammable under ordinary conditions, but become explosive in specific situations or forms, such as <a href="/wiki/Dust_explosion" title="Dust explosion">dispersed airborne clouds</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Gas_explosion" title="Gas explosion">confinement or sudden release</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_gunpowder" title="History of gunpowder">History of gunpowder</a></div>
<p>At its root, the history of chemical explosives lies in the history of gunpowder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> During the Tang Dynasty in the 9th century, Taoist Chinese alchemists were eagerly trying to find the elixir of immortality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In the process, they stumbled upon the explosive invention of gunpowder made from coal, saltpeter, and sulfur in 1044. Gunpowder was the first form of chemical explosives and by 1161, the Chinese were using explosives for the first time in warfare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The Chinese would incorporate explosives fired from bamboo or bronze tubes known as bamboo fire crackers. The Chinese also used inserted rats from inside the bamboo fire crackers to fire toward the enemy, creating great psychological ramifications – scaring enemy soldiers away and causing cavalry units to go wild.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Though <a href="/wiki/Early_thermal_weapons" title="Early thermal weapons">early thermal weapons</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Greek_fire" title="Greek fire">Greek fire</a>, have existed since ancient times, the first widely used explosive in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warfare" title="Warfare">warfare</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a> was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_powder" title="Black powder">black powder</a>, invented in 9th century in China by Song Chinese alchemists. This material was sensitive to water, and it produced copious amounts of dark smoke. The first useful explosive stronger than black powder was <a href="/wiki/Nitroglycerin" title="Nitroglycerin">nitroglycerin</a>, developed in 1847. Since nitroglycerin is a liquid and highly unstable, it was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Nitrocellulose" title="Nitrocellulose">nitrocellulose</a>, trinitrotoluene (<a href="/wiki/TNT" title="TNT">TNT</a>) in 1863, <a href="/wiki/Smokeless_powder" title="Smokeless powder">smokeless powder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dynamite" title="Dynamite">dynamite</a> in 1867 and <a href="/wiki/Gelignite" title="Gelignite">gelignite</a> (the latter two being sophisticated stabilized preparations of nitroglycerin rather than chemical alternatives, both invented by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Nobel" title="Alfred Nobel">Alfred Nobel</a>). World War I saw the adoption of TNT in artillery shells. World War II saw an extensive use of new explosives (see <a href="/wiki/List_of_explosives_used_during_World_War_II" title="List of explosives used during World War II">List of explosives used during World War II</a>). In turn, these have largely been replaced by more powerful explosives such as <a href="/wiki/C-4_(explosive)" title="C-4 (explosive)">C-4</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PETN" title="PETN">PETN</a>. However, C-4 and PETN react with metal and catch fire easily, yet unlike TNT, C-4 and PETN are waterproof and malleable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ankony1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ankony1-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The largest commercial application of explosives is <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a>. Whether the mine is on the surface or is buried underground, the detonation or deflagration of either a high or low explosive in a confined space can be used to liberate a fairly specific sub-volume of a brittle material in a much larger volume of the same or similar material. The mining industry tends to use nitrate-based explosives such as <a class="external text" href="http://www.idealexplosives.com/emulsion-explosives.html" rel="nofollow">emulsions of fuel oil and ammonium nitrate solutions</a>, mixtures of ammonium nitrate prills (fertilizer pellets) and fuel oil (<a href="/wiki/ANFO" title="ANFO">ANFO</a>) and <a class="external text" href="http://www.idealexplosives.com/slurry-explosives.html" rel="nofollow">gelatinous suspensions or slurries</a> of ammonium nitrate and combustible fuels.
</p><p>In Materials Science and Engineering, explosives are used in <a href="/wiki/Cladding_(metalworking)" title="Cladding (metalworking)">cladding</a> (<a href="/wiki/Explosion_welding" title="Explosion welding">explosion welding</a>). A thin plate of some material is placed atop a thick layer of a different material, both layers typically of metal. Atop the thin layer is placed an explosive. At one end of the layer of explosive, the explosion is initiated. The two metallic layers are forced together at high speed and with great force. The explosion spreads from the initiation site throughout the explosive. Ideally, this produces a metallurgical bond between the two layers.
</p><p>As the length of time the shock wave spends at any point is small, we can see mixing of the two metals and their surface chemistries, through some fraction of the depth, and they tend to be mixed in some way. It is possible that some fraction of the surface material from either layer eventually gets ejected when the end of material is reached. Hence, the mass of the now "welded" bilayer, may be less than the sum of the masses of the two initial layers.
</p><p>There are applications where a shock wave, and electrostatics, can result in high velocity projectiles.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical">Chemical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Chemical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chemical_explosive" title="Chemical explosive">Chemical explosive</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GHS-pictogram-explos.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="724" data-file-width="724" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/GHS-pictogram-explos.svg/220px-GHS-pictogram-explos.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/GHS-pictogram-explos.svg/330px-GHS-pictogram-explos.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/GHS-pictogram-explos.svg/440px-GHS-pictogram-explos.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GHS-pictogram-explos.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Globally_Harmonized_System_of_Classification_and_Labelling_of_Chemicals" title="Globally Harmonized System of Classification and Labelling of Chemicals">international</a> <a href="/wiki/Pictogram" title="Pictogram">pictogram</a> for explosive substances</div></div></div>
<p>An explosion is a type of spontaneous chemical reaction that, once initiated, is driven by both a large exothermic change (great release of heat) and a large positive <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a> change (great quantities of gases are released) in going from reactants to products, thereby constituting a thermodynamically favorable process in addition to one that propagates very rapidly. Thus, explosives are substances that contain a large amount of energy stored in <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">chemical bonds</a>. The energetic stability of the gaseous products and hence their generation comes from the formation of strongly bonded species like carbon monoxide, carbon dioxide, and (di)nitrogen, which contain strong double and triple bonds having bond strengths of nearly 1 MJ/mole. Consequently, most commercial explosives are organic compounds containing <a href="/wiki/Nitro_compound" title="Nitro compound">-NO<sub>2</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">-ONO<sub>2</sub></a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitroamine" title="Nitroamine">-NHNO<sub>2</sub></a> groups that, when detonated, release gases like the aforementioned (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Nitroglycerin" title="Nitroglycerin">nitroglycerin</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trinitrotoluene" title="Trinitrotoluene">TNT</a>, <a href="/wiki/HMX" title="HMX">HMX</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PETN" title="PETN">PETN</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrocellulose" title="Nitrocellulose">nitrocellulose</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>An explosive is classified as a low or high explosive according to its rate of <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a>: low explosives burn rapidly (or <a href="/wiki/Deflagration" title="Deflagration">deflagrate</a>), while high explosives <a href="/wiki/Detonation" title="Detonation">detonate</a>. While these definitions are distinct, the problem of precisely measuring rapid decomposition makes practical classification of explosives difficult.
</p><p>Traditional explosives mechanics is based on the shock-sensitive rapid oxidation of carbon and hydrogen to carbon dioxide, carbon monoxide and water in the form of steam. <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">Nitrates</a> typically provide the required oxygen to burn the carbon and hydrogen fuel. High explosives tend to have the oxygen, carbon and hydrogen contained in one organic molecule, and less sensitive explosives like ANFO are combinations of fuel (carbon and hydrogen fuel oil) and <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_nitrate" title="Ammonium nitrate">ammonium nitrate</a>. A sensitizer such as powdered aluminum may be added to an explosive to increase the energy of the detonation. Once detonated, the nitrogen portion of the explosive formulation emerges as nitrogen gas and toxic <a href="/wiki/NOx" title="NOx">nitric oxides</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Decomposition">Decomposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Decomposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Chemical_decomposition" title="Chemical decomposition">chemical decomposition</a> of an explosive may take years, days, hours, or a fraction of a second. The slower processes of decomposition take place in storage and are of interest only from a stability standpoint. Of more interest are the other two rapid forms besides decomposition: deflagration and detonation.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Deflagration">Deflagration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Deflagration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Deflagration" title="Deflagration">Deflagration</a></div>
<p>In deflagration, decomposition of the explosive material is propagated by a flame front which moves slowly through the explosive material at speeds less than the speed of sound within the substance (usually below 1000 m/s) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> in contrast to detonation, which occurs at speeds greater than the speed of sound. Deflagration is a characteristic of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_explosive" title="Low explosive">low explosive</a> material.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Detonation">Detonation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Detonation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Detonation" title="Detonation">Detonation</a></div>
<p>This term is used to describe an explosive phenomenon whereby the decomposition is <a href="/wiki/Wave_propagation" title="Wave propagation">propagated</a> by an explosive <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">shock wave</a> traversing the explosive material at speeds greater than the speed of sound within the substance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The shock front is capable of passing through the high explosive material at supersonic speeds, typically thousands of metres per second.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exotic">Exotic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Exotic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition to chemical explosives, there are a number of more exotic explosive materials, and exotic methods of causing explosions. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_explosive" title="Nuclear explosive">nuclear explosives</a>, and abruptly heating a substance to a <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a> state with a high-intensity <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">laser</a> or <a href="/wiki/Electric_arc" title="Electric arc">electric arc</a>.
</p><p>Laser- and arc-heating are used in laser detonators, <a href="/wiki/Exploding-bridgewire_detonator" title="Exploding-bridgewire detonator">exploding-bridgewire detonators</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Slapper_detonator" title="Slapper detonator">exploding foil initiators</a>, where a shock wave and then detonation in conventional chemical explosive material is created by laser- or electric-arc heating. Laser and electric energy are not currently used in practice to generate most of the required energy, but only to initiate reactions.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties_of_explosive_materials">Properties of explosive materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Properties of explosive materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To determine the suitability of an explosive substance for a particular use, its <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physical</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Properties" title="Properties">properties</a> must first be known. The usefulness of an explosive can only be appreciated when the properties and the factors affecting them are fully understood. Some of the more important characteristics are listed below:
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sensitivity">Sensitivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Sensitivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sensitivity_(explosives)" title="Sensitivity (explosives)">Sensitivity (explosives)</a></div>
<p>Sensitivity refers to the ease with which an explosive can be ignited or detonated, i.e., the amount and intensity of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_shock" title="Electric shock">shock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> that is required. When the term <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Sensitivity" title="wikt:Sensitivity">sensitivity</a> is used, care must be taken to clarify what kind of sensitivity is under discussion. The relative sensitivity of a given explosive to impact may vary greatly from its sensitivity to friction or heat. Some of the test methods used to determine sensitivity relate to:
</p>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Impact_(mechanics)" title="Impact (mechanics)">Impact</a></b> — Sensitivity is expressed in terms of the distance through which a standard weight must be dropped onto the material to cause it to explode.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">Friction</a></b> — Sensitivity is expressed in terms of what occurs when a weighted pendulum scrapes across the material (it may snap, crackle, ignite, and/or explode).</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">Heat</a></b> — Sensitivity is expressed in terms of the temperature at which flashing or explosion of the material occurs.</li></ul>
<p>Specific explosives (usually but not always highly sensitive on one or more of the three above axes) may be idiosyncratically sensitive to such factors as pressure drop, acceleration, the presence of sharp edges or rough surfaces, incompatible materials, or even—in rare cases—nuclear or electromagnetic radiation. These factors present special hazards that may rule out any practical utility.
</p><p>Sensitivity is an important consideration in selecting an explosive for a particular purpose. The explosive in an armor-piercing projectile must be relatively insensitive, or the shock of impact would cause it to detonate before it penetrated to the point desired. The explosive lenses around nuclear charges are also designed to be highly insensitive, to minimize the risk of accidental detonation.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sensitivity_to_initiation">Sensitivity to initiation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Sensitivity to initiation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The index of the capacity of an explosive to be initiated into detonation in a sustained manner. It is defined by the power of the detonator which is certain to prime the explosive to a sustained and continuous detonation. Reference is made to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sellier-Bellot" title="Sellier-Bellot">Sellier-Bellot</a> scale that consists of a series of 10 detonators, from n. 1 to n. 10, each of which corresponds to an increasing charge weight. In practice, most of the explosives on the market today are sensitive to an n. 8 detonator, where the charge corresponds to 2 grams of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercury_fulminate" title="Mercury fulminate">mercury fulminate</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Velocity_of_detonation">Velocity of detonation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Velocity of detonation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The velocity with which the reaction process propagates in the mass of the explosive. Most commercial mining explosives have detonation velocities ranging from 1800 m/s to 8000 m/s. Today, velocity of detonation can be measured with accuracy. Together with density it is an important element influencing the yield of the energy transmitted for both atmospheric over-pressure and ground acceleration. By definition, a "low explosive," such as black powder, or smokeless gunpowder has a burn rate of 171–631 m/s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In contrast, a "high explosive," whether a primary, such as <a href="/wiki/Detonating_cord" title="Detonating cord">detonating cord</a>, or a secondary, such as TNT or C-4 has a significantly higher burn rate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stability">Stability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Stability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chemical_stability" title="Chemical stability">Chemical stability</a></div>
<p><b>Stability </b> is the ability of an explosive to be stored without <a href="/wiki/Chemical_decomposition" title="Chemical decomposition">deterioration</a>.
</p><p>The following factors affect the stability of an explosive:
</p>
<ul><li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_constitution" title="Chemical constitution">Chemical constitution</a>.</b> In the strictest technical sense, the word "stability" is a thermodynamic term referring to the energy of a substance relative to a reference state or to some other substance. However, in the context of explosives, stability commonly refers to ease of detonation, which is concerned with <a href="/wiki/Chemical_kinetics" title="Chemical kinetics">kinetics</a> (i.e., rate of decomposition). It is perhaps best, then, to differentiate between the terms thermodynamically stable and kinetically stable by referring to the former as "inert." Contrarily, a kinetically unstable substance is said to be "labile." It is generally recognized that certain groups like nitro (–NO<sub>2</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrate</a> (–ONO<sub>2</sub>), and <a href="/wiki/Azide" title="Azide">azide</a> (–N<sub>3</sub>), are intrinsically labile. Kinetically, there exists a low activation barrier to the decomposition reaction. Consequently, these compounds exhibit high sensitivity to flame or mechanical shock. The chemical bonding in these compounds is characterized as predominantly covalent and thus they are not thermodynamically stabilized by a high ionic-lattice energy. Furthermore, they generally have positive enthalpies of formation and there is little mechanistic hindrance to internal molecular rearrangement to yield the more thermodynamically stable (more strongly bonded) decomposition products. For example, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lead_azide" title="Lead azide">lead azide</a>, Pb(N<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>, the nitrogen atoms are already bonded to one another, so decomposition into Pb and N<sub>2</sub><sup>[1]</sup> is relatively easy.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">Temperature</a> of storage.</b> The rate of decomposition of explosives increases at higher temperatures. All standard military explosives may be considered to have a high degree of stability at temperatures from –10 to +35 °C, but each has a high temperature at which its rate of <a href="/wiki/Thermal_decomposition" title="Thermal decomposition">decomposition</a> rapidly accelerates and stability is reduced. As a rule of thumb, most explosives become dangerously unstable at temperatures above 70 °C.</li>
<li><b>Exposure to <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a>.</b> When exposed to the <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> rays of sunlight, many explosive compounds containing <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> groups rapidly decompose, affecting their stability.</li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_discharge" title="Electrical discharge">Electrical discharge</a>.</b> <a href="/wiki/Electrostatic_discharge" title="Electrostatic discharge">Electrostatic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Electric_spark" title="Electric spark">spark</a> sensitivity to initiation is common in a number of explosives. Static or other electrical discharge may be sufficient to cause a reaction, even detonation, under some circumstances. As a result, safe handling of explosives and <a href="/wiki/Pyrotechnics" title="Pyrotechnics">pyrotechnics</a> usually requires proper <a href="/wiki/Ground_(electricity)" title="Ground (electricity)">electrical grounding</a> of the operator.</li></ul>
<h3><span id="Power.2C_performance.2C_and_strength"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Power,_performance,_and_strength">Power, performance, and strength</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Power, performance, and strength">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Power_(physics)" title="Power (physics)">Power (physics)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Strength_(explosive)" title="Strength (explosive)">Strength (explosive)</a></div>
<p>The term <b>power</b> or <b>performance</b> as applied to an explosive refers to its ability to do work. In practice it is defined as the explosive's ability to accomplish what is intended in the way of energy delivery (i.e., fragment projection, air blast, high-velocity jet, underwater shock and bubble energy, etc.). Explosive power or performance is evaluated by a tailored series of tests to assess the material for its intended use. Of the tests listed below, cylinder expansion and air-blast tests are common to most testing programs, and the others support specific applications.
</p>
<ul><li><b>Cylinder expansion test.</b> A standard amount of explosive is loaded into a long hollow <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)" title="Cylinder (geometry)">cylinder</a>, usually of copper, and detonated at one end. Data is collected concerning the rate of radial expansion of the cylinder and the maximum cylinder wall velocity. This also establishes the <a href="/wiki/Gurney_equations" title="Gurney equations">Gurney energy</a> or 2<i>E</i>.</li>
<li><b>Cylinder fragmentation.</b> A standard steel cylinder is loaded with explosive and detonated in a sawdust pit. The <a href="/wiki/Fragmentation_(weaponry)" title="Fragmentation (weaponry)">fragments</a> are collected and the size distribution analyzed.</li>
<li><b>Detonation pressure (<a href="/wiki/Chapman%E2%80%93Jouguet_condition" title="Chapman–Jouguet condition">Chapman–Jouguet condition</a>).</b> <a href="/wiki/Detonation" title="Detonation">Detonation</a> pressure data derived from measurements of shock waves transmitted into water by the detonation of cylindrical explosive charges of a standard size.</li>
<li><b>Determination of critical diameter.</b> This test establishes the minimum physical size a charge of a specific explosive must be to sustain its own detonation wave. The procedure involves the detonation of a series of charges of different diameters until difficulty in detonation wave propagation is observed.</li>
<li><b>Massive-diameter detonation velocity.</b> Detonation velocity is dependent on loading density (c), charge diameter, and grain size. The hydrodynamic theory of detonation used in predicting explosive phenomena does not include the diameter of the charge, and therefore a detonation velocity, for a massive diameter. This procedure requires the firing of a series of charges of the same density and physical structure, but different diameters, and the extrapolation of the resulting detonation velocities to predict the detonation velocity of a charge of a massive diameter.</li>
<li><b>Pressure versus scaled distance.</b> A charge of a specific size is detonated and its pressure effects measured at a standard distance. The values obtained are compared with those for TNT.</li>
<li><b>Impulse versus scaled distance.</b> A charge of a specific size is detonated and its impulse (the area under the pressure-time curve) measured as a function of distance. The results are tabulated and expressed as TNT equivalents.</li>
<li><b>Relative bubble energy (RBE).</b> A 5 to 50 kg charge is detonated in water and piezoelectric gauges measure peak pressure, time constant, impulse, and energy.</li></ul>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>The RBE may be defined as <i>K</i><sub><i>x</i></sub> 3</dd>
<dd>RBE = <i>K</i><sub><i>s</i></sub></dd>
<dd>where <i>K</i> = the bubble expansion period for an experimental (<i>x</i>) or a standard (<i>s</i>) charge.</dd></dl></dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brisance">Brisance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Brisance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Brisance" title="Brisance">Brisance</a></div>
<p>In addition to strength, explosives display a second characteristic, which is their shattering effect or brisance (from the French meaning to "break"), which is distinguished and separate from their total work capacity. This characteristic is of practical importance in determining the effectiveness of an explosion in fragmenting shells, bomb casings, <a href="/wiki/Grenade" title="Grenade">grenades</a>, and the like. The rapidity with which an explosive reaches its peak pressure (<a href="/wiki/Power_(physics)" title="Power (physics)">power</a>) is a measure of its brisance. Brisance values are primarily employed in France and Russia.
</p><p>The sand crush test is commonly employed to determine the relative brisance in comparison to TNT. No test is capable of directly comparing the explosive properties of two or more compounds; it is important to examine the data from several such tests (sand crush, <a href="/wiki/Trauzl_lead_block_test" title="Trauzl lead block test">trauzl</a>, and so forth) in order to gauge relative brisance. True values for comparison require field experiments.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Density">Density</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Density">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">Density</a> of loading refers to the mass of an explosive per unit volume. Several methods of loading are available, including pellet loading, cast loading, and press loading, the choice being determined by the characteristics of the explosive. Dependent upon the method employed, an average density of the loaded charge can be obtained that is within 80–99% of the theoretical maximum density of the explosive. High load density can reduce <a href="/wiki/Sensitivity_(explosives)" title="Sensitivity (explosives)">sensitivity</a> by making the <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> more resistant to internal <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a>. However, if density is increased to the extent that individual <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystals</a> are crushed, the explosive may become more sensitive. Increased load density also permits the use of more explosive, thereby increasing the power of the <a href="/wiki/Warhead" title="Warhead">warhead</a>. It is possible to compress an explosive beyond a point of sensitivity, known also as <i>dead-pressing</i>, in which the material is no longer capable of being reliably initiated, if at all.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Volatility">Volatility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Volatility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Volatility_(chemistry)" title="Volatility (chemistry)">Volatility</a> is the readiness with which a substance <a href="/wiki/Vaporization" title="Vaporization">vaporizes</a>. Excessive volatility often results in the development of pressure within rounds of ammunition and separation of mixtures into their constituents. Volatility affects the chemical composition of the explosive such that a marked reduction in stability may occur, which results in an increase in the danger of handling.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hygroscopicity_and_water_resistance">Hygroscopicity and water resistance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Hygroscopicity and water resistance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The introduction of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> into an explosive is highly undesirable since it reduces the sensitivity, strength, and velocity of detonation of the explosive. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hygroscopicity" title="Hygroscopicity">Hygroscopicity</a> is a measure of a material's moisture-absorbing tendencies. Moisture affects explosives adversely by acting as an inert material that absorbs heat when vaporized, and by acting as a solvent medium that can cause undesired chemical reactions. Sensitivity, strength, and velocity of detonation are reduced by inert materials that reduce the continuity of the explosive mass. When the moisture content evaporates during detonation, cooling occurs, which reduces the temperature of reaction. Stability is also affected by the presence of moisture since moisture promotes decomposition of the explosive and, in addition, causes corrosion of the explosive's metal container.
</p><p>Explosives considerably differ from one another as to their behavior in the presence of water. Gelatin dynamites containing nitroglycerine have a degree of water resistance. Explosives based on <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_nitrate" title="Ammonium nitrate">ammonium nitrate</a> have little or no water resistance due to the reaction between ammonium nitrate and water, which liberates ammonia, nitrogen dioxide and hydrogen peroxide. In addition, ammonium nitrate is hygroscopic, susceptible to damp, hence the above concerns.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicity">Toxicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Toxicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many explosives are <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxic</a> to some extent. Manufacturing inputs can also be organic compounds or hazardous materials that require special handing due to risks (such as <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogens</a>). The decomposition products, residual solids, or gases of some explosives can be toxic, whereas others are harmless, such as carbon dioxide and water.
</p><p>Examples of harmful by-products are:
</p>
<ul><li>Heavy metals, such as lead, mercury, and barium from primers (observed in high-volume firing ranges)</li>
<li>Nitric oxides from TNT</li>
<li>Perchlorates when used in large quantities</li></ul>
<p>"Green explosives" seek to reduce environment and health impacts. An example of such is the lead-free primary explosive copper(I) 5-nitrotetrazolate, an alternative to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lead_azide" title="Lead azide">lead azide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> One variety of a green explosive is CDP explosives, whose synthesis does not involve any toxic ingredients, consumes carbon dioxide while detonating and does not release any nitric oxides into the atmosphere when used.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Explosive_train">Explosive train</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Explosive train">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Explosive_train" title="Explosive train">Explosive train</a></div>
<p>Explosive material may be incorporated in the <a href="/wiki/Explosive_train" title="Explosive train">explosive train</a> of a device or system. An example is a pyrotechnic lead igniting a booster, which causes the main charge to detonate.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Volume_of_products_of_explosion">Volume of products of explosion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Volume of products of explosion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The most widely used explosives are condensed liquids or solids converted to gaseous products by explosive chemical reactions and the energy released by those reactions. The gaseous products of complete reaction are typically <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-z&amp;k_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-z&amp;k-15">[15]</a></sup> Gaseous volumes computed by the <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas_law" title="Ideal gas law">ideal gas law</a> tend to be too large at high pressures characteristic of explosions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Ultimate volume expansion may be estimated at three orders of magnitude, or one liter per gram of explosive. Explosives with an oxygen deficit will generate soot or gases like <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, which may react with surrounding materials such as atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-z&amp;k_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-z&amp;k-15">[15]</a></sup> Attempts to obtain more precise volume estimates must consider the possibility of such side reactions, condensation of steam, and aqueous solubility of gases like carbon dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>By comparison, CDP detonation is based on the rapid reduction of carbon dioxide to carbon with the abundant release of energy. Rather than produce typical waste gases like carbon dioxide, carbon monoxide, nitrogen and nitric oxides, CDP is different. Instead, the highly energetic reduction of carbon dioxide to carbon vaporizes and pressurizes excess dry ice at the wave front, which is the only gas released from the detonation. The velocity of detonation for CDP formulations can therefore be customized by adjusting the weight percentage of reducing agent and dry ice. CDP detonations produce a large amount of solid materials that can have great commercial value as an abrasive:
</p><p>Example – CDP Detonation Reaction with Magnesium: XCO<sub>2</sub> + 2Mg → 2MgO + C + (X-1)CO<sub>2</sub>
</p><p>The products of detonation in this example are magnesium oxide, carbon in various phases including diamond, and vaporized excess carbon dioxide that was not consumed by the amount of magnesium in the explosive formulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Oxygen_balance_.28OB.25_or_.CE.A9.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxygen_balance_(OB%_or_Ω)">Oxygen balance (OB% or <i>Ω</i>)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Oxygen balance (OB% or Ω)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_balance" title="Oxygen balance">Oxygen balance</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Oxygen_balance" title="Oxygen balance">Oxygen balance</a> is an expression that is used to indicate the degree to which an explosive can be oxidized. If an explosive molecule contains just enough oxygen to convert all of its carbon to carbon dioxide, all of its hydrogen to water, and all of its metal to metal oxide with no excess, the molecule is said to have a zero oxygen balance. The molecule is said to have a positive oxygen balance if it contains more oxygen than is needed and a negative oxygen balance if it contains less oxygen than is needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The sensitivity, <a href="/wiki/Strength_(explosive)" title="Strength (explosive)">strength</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Brisance" title="Brisance">brisance</a> of an explosive are all somewhat dependent upon oxygen balance and tend to approach their maxima as oxygen balance approaches zero.
</p><p>Oxygen balance applies to traditional explosives mechanics with the assumption that carbon is oxidized to carbon monoxide and carbon dioxide during detonation. In what seems like a paradox to an explosives expert, Cold Detonation Physics uses carbon in its most highly oxidized state as the source of oxygen in the form of carbon dioxide. Oxygen balance, therefore, either does not apply to a CDP formulation or must be calculated without including the carbon in the carbon dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_composition">Chemical composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Chemical composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A chemical explosive may consist of either a chemically pure compound, such as <a href="/wiki/Nitroglycerin" title="Nitroglycerin">nitroglycerin</a>, or a mixture of a <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a> and an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidizer" title="Oxidizer">oxidizer</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_powder" title="Black powder">black powder</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grain_dust" title="Grain dust">grain dust</a> and air.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemically_pure_compounds">Chemically pure compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Chemically pure compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Some chemical compounds are unstable in that, when shocked, they react, possibly to the point of detonation. Each molecule of the compound dissociates into two or more new molecules (generally gases) with the release of energy.
</p>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Nitroglycerin" title="Nitroglycerin">Nitroglycerin</a></b>: A highly unstable and sensitive liquid</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Acetone_peroxide" title="Acetone peroxide">Acetone peroxide</a></b>: A very unstable white <a href="/wiki/Organic_peroxide" title="Organic peroxide">organic peroxide</a></li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trinitrotoluene" title="Trinitrotoluene">TNT</a></b>: Yellow insensitive crystals that can be melted and cast without detonation</li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cellulose_nitrate" title="Cellulose nitrate">Cellulose nitrate</a></b>: A nitrated polymer which can be a high or low explosive depending on nitration level and conditions</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/RDX" title="RDX">RDX</a></b>, <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PETN" title="PETN">PETN</a></b>, <b><a href="/wiki/HMX" title="HMX">HMX</a></b>: Very powerful explosives which can be used pure or in plastic explosives
<ul><li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C4_explosive" title="C4 explosive">C-4</a></b> (or Composition C-4): An <a href="/wiki/RDX" title="RDX">RDX</a> <a href="/wiki/Plastic_explosive" title="Plastic explosive">plastic explosive</a> plasticized to be adhesive and malleable</li></ul></li></ul>
<p>The above compositions may describe most of the explosive material, but a practical explosive will often include small percentages of other substances. For example, <a href="/wiki/Dynamite" title="Dynamite">dynamite</a> is a mixture of highly sensitive nitroglycerin with <a href="/wiki/Sawdust" title="Sawdust">sawdust</a>, powdered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">silica</a>, or most commonly <a href="/wiki/Diatomaceous_earth" title="Diatomaceous earth">diatomaceous earth</a>, which act as stabilizers. Plastics and polymers may be added to bind powders of explosive compounds; waxes may be incorporated to make them safer to handle; <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> powder may be introduced to increase total energy and blast effects. Explosive compounds are also often "alloyed": HMX or RDX powders may be mixed (typically by melt-casting) with TNT to form <a href="/wiki/Octol" title="Octol">Octol</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cyclotol" title="Cyclotol">Cyclotol</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mixture_of_oxidizer_and_fuel">Mixture of oxidizer and fuel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Mixture of oxidizer and fuel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>An <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidizer" title="Oxidizer">oxidizer</a> is a pure substance (<a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecule</a>) that in a chemical reaction can contribute some atoms of one or more oxidizing elements, in which the <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a> component of the explosive burns. On the simplest level, the oxidizer may itself be an oxidizing <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">element</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_state" title="Gas state">gaseous</a> or <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a> <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>.
</p>
<ul><li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_powder" title="Black powder">Black powder</a></b>: <a href="/wiki/Potassium_nitrate" title="Potassium nitrate">Potassium nitrate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Flash_powder" title="Flash powder">Flash powder</a></b>: Fine metal powder (usually <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> or <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>) and a strong oxidizer (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Potassium_chlorate" title="Potassium chlorate">potassium chlorate</a> or <a href="/wiki/Potassium_perchlorate" title="Potassium perchlorate">perchlorate</a>)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Ammonal" title="Ammonal">Ammonal</a></b>: <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_nitrate" title="Ammonium nitrate">Ammonium nitrate</a> and aluminium powder</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Armstrong%27s_mixture" title="Armstrong's mixture">Armstrong's mixture</a></b>: <a href="/wiki/Potassium_chlorate" title="Potassium chlorate">Potassium chlorate</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_phosphorus" title="Red phosphorus">red phosphorus</a>. This is a very sensitive mixture. It is a primary high explosive in which sulfur is substituted for some or all of the phosphorus to slightly decrease sensitivity.</li>
<li>Cold Detonation Physics: Combinations of carbon dioxide in the form of dry ice (an untraditional oxygen source), and powdered reducing agents (fuel) like magnesium and aluminum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Sprengel_explosive" title="Sprengel explosive">Sprengel explosives</a></b>: A very general class incorporating any strong oxidizer and highly reactive fuel, although in practice the name was most commonly applied to mixtures of <a href="/wiki/Chlorate" title="Chlorate">chlorates</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nitroaromatic&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nitroaromatic (page does not exist)">nitroaromatics</a>.
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/ANFO" title="ANFO">ANFO</a></b>: Ammonium nitrate and <a href="/wiki/Fuel_oil" title="Fuel oil">fuel oil</a></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Cheddite" title="Cheddite">Cheddites</a></b>: <a href="/wiki/Chlorate" title="Chlorate">Chlorates</a> or <a href="/wiki/Perchlorate" title="Perchlorate">perchlorates</a> and oil</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Oxyliquit" title="Oxyliquit">Oxyliquits</a></b>: Mixtures of organic materials and <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">liquid oxygen</a></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Panclastite" title="Panclastite">Panclastites</a></b>: Mixtures of organic materials and <a href="/wiki/Dinitrogen_tetroxide" title="Dinitrogen tetroxide">dinitrogen tetroxide</a></li></ul></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Availability_and_cost">Availability and cost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Availability and cost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The availability and cost of explosives are determined by the availability of the raw materials and the cost, complexity, and safety of the manufacturing operations.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_of_explosive_materials">Classification of explosive materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Classification of explosive materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_sensitivity">By sensitivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: By sensitivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_explosive">Primary explosive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Primary explosive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A <b>primary explosive</b> is an <a class="mw-selflink selflink">explosive</a> that is extremely sensitive to stimuli such as <a href="/wiki/Impact_(mechanics)" title="Impact (mechanics)">impact</a>, <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrostatic" title="Electrostatic">static electricity</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a>. Some primary explosives are also known as <a href="/wiki/Contact_explosive" title="Contact explosive">contact explosives</a>. A relatively small amount of energy is required for <a href="/wiki/Initiation_(chemistry)" title="Initiation (chemistry)">initiation</a>. As a very general rule, primary explosives are considered to be those compounds that are more sensitive than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PETN" title="PETN">PETN</a>. As a practical measure, primary explosives are sufficiently sensitive that they can be reliably initiated with a blow from a hammer; however, PETN can also usually be initiated in this manner, so this is only a very broad guideline. Additionally, several compounds, such as <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_triiodide" title="Nitrogen triiodide">nitrogen triiodide</a>, are so sensitive that they cannot even be handled without detonating. Nitrogen triiodide is so sensitive that it can be reliably detonated by exposure to <a href="/wiki/Alpha_particle" title="Alpha particle">alpha radiation</a>; it is the only explosive for which this is true.
</p><p>Primary explosives are often used in <a href="/wiki/Detonator" title="Detonator">detonators</a> or to <a href="/wiki/Trigger_(firearms)" title="Trigger (firearms)">trigger</a> larger charges of less sensitive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secondary_explosives" title="Secondary explosives">secondary explosives</a>. Primary explosives are commonly used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blasting_cap" title="Blasting cap">blasting caps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Percussion_cap" title="Percussion cap">percussion caps</a> to translate a physical shock signal. In other situations, different signals such as electrical or physical shock, or, in the case of laser detonation systems, light, are used to initiate an action, i.e., an explosion. A small quantity, usually milligrams, is sufficient to initiate a larger charge of explosive that is usually safer to handle.

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Secondary_explosive">Secondary explosive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Secondary explosive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A <b>secondary explosive</b> is less sensitive than a primary explosive and requires substantially more energy to be initiated. Because they are less sensitive, they are usable in a wider variety of applications and are safer to handle and store. Secondary explosives are used in larger quantities in an explosive train and are usually initiated by a smaller quantity of a primary explosive.
</p><p>Examples of secondary explosives include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trinitrotoluene" title="Trinitrotoluene">TNT</a> and <a href="/wiki/RDX" title="RDX">RDX</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tertiary_explosive">Tertiary explosive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Tertiary explosive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><b>Tertiary explosives</b>, also called <b>blasting agents</b>, are so insensitive to shock that they cannot be reliably detonated by practical quantities of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primary_explosive" title="Primary explosive">primary explosive</a>, and instead require an intermediate <a href="/wiki/Explosive_booster" title="Explosive booster">explosive booster</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secondary_explosive" title="Secondary explosive">secondary explosive</a>. These are often used for safety and the typically lower costs of material and handling. The largest consumers are large-scale <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a> and <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">construction</a> operations.
</p><p>Most tertiaries include a fuel and an oxidizer. <a href="/wiki/ANFO" title="ANFO">ANFO</a> can be a tertiary explosive if its reaction rate is slow.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_velocity">By velocity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: By velocity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Low_explosives">Low explosives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Low explosives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Low explosives are compounds where the rate of decomposition proceeds through the material at less than the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">speed of sound</a>. The decomposition is propagated by a flame front (<a href="/wiki/Deflagration" title="Deflagration">deflagration</a>) which travels much more slowly through the explosive material than a <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">shock wave</a> of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_explosive" title="High explosive">high explosive</a>. <a href="/wiki/Standard_conditions_for_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard conditions for temperature and pressure">Under normal conditions</a>, low explosives undergo <a href="/wiki/Deflagration" title="Deflagration">deflagration</a> at rates that vary from a few centimetres per second to approximately 400 metres per second. It is possible for them to deflagrate very quickly, producing an effect similar to a <a href="/wiki/Detonation" title="Detonation">detonation</a>. This can happen under higher <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> or <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>, which usually occurs when ignited in a confined space.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>A low explosive is usually a mixture of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combustible" title="Combustible">combustible</a> substance and an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidant" title="Oxidant">oxidant</a> that decomposes rapidly (deflagration); however, they burn more slowly than a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_explosive" title="High explosive">high explosive</a>, which has an extremely fast burn rate.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Low explosives are normally employed as <a href="/wiki/Propellant" title="Propellant">propellants</a>. Included in this group are petroleum products such as <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gunpowder" title="Gunpowder">gunpowder</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Smokeless_powder" title="Smokeless powder">smokeless powder</a>), and light <a href="/wiki/Pyrotechnics" title="Pyrotechnics">pyrotechnics</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Flare" title="Flare">flares</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fireworks" title="Fireworks">fireworks</a>, but can replace high explosives in certain applications, see gas pressure blasting.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="High_explosives">High explosives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: High explosives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>High explosives (HE) are explosive materials that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Detonate" title="Detonate">detonate</a>, meaning that the <a href="/wiki/Explosion" title="Explosion">explosive</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shock_front" title="Shock front">shock front</a> passes through the material at a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supersonic" title="Supersonic">supersonic</a> speed. High explosives detonate with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explosive_velocity" title="Explosive velocity">explosive velocity</a> ranging from 3 to 9 km/s. For instance, TNT has a detonation (burn) rate of approximately 5.8 km/s (19,000 feet per second), Detonating cord of 6.7 km/s (22,000 feet per second), and C-4 about 8.5 km/s (29,000 feet per second). They are normally employed in mining, demolition, and military applications. They can be divided into two explosives classes differentiated by <a href="/wiki/Sensitivity_(explosives)" title="Sensitivity (explosives)">sensitivity</a>: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primary_explosive" title="Primary explosive">primary explosive</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secondary_explosive" title="Secondary explosive">secondary explosive</a>. The term <i>high explosive</i> is in contrast with the term <i>low explosive</i>, which explodes (<a href="/wiki/Deflagration" title="Deflagration">deflagrates</a>) at a lower rate.
</p><p>Countless high-explosive compounds are chemically possible, but commercially and militarily important ones have included <a href="/wiki/Nitroglycerin" title="Nitroglycerin">NG</a>, <a href="/wiki/TNT" title="TNT">TNT</a>, TNX, <a href="/wiki/RDX" title="RDX">RDX</a>, <a href="/wiki/HMX" title="HMX">HMX</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pentaerythritol_tetranitrate" title="Pentaerythritol tetranitrate">PETN</a>, <a href="/wiki/TATB" title="TATB">TATB</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hexanitrostilbene" title="Hexanitrostilbene">HNS</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_composition">By composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: By composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Priming_composition">Priming composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Priming composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Priming compositions are primary explosives mixed with other compositions to control (lessen) the sensitivity of the mixture to the desired property.
</p><p>For example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primary_explosive" title="Primary explosive">primary explosives</a> are so sensitive that they need to be stored and shipped in a wet state to prevent accidental initiation.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shipping_label_classifications">Shipping label classifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=42" title="Edit section: Shipping label classifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Shipping labels and tags may include both <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> and national markings.
</p><p>United Nations markings include numbered Hazard Class and Division (HC/D) codes and alphabetic Compatibility Group codes. Though the two are related, they are separate and distinct. Any Compatibility Group designator can be assigned to any Hazard Class and Division. An example of this hybrid marking would be a consumer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Firework" title="Firework">firework</a>, which is labeled as 1.4G or 1.4S.
</p><p>Examples of national markings would include <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Transportation" title="United States Department of Transportation">United States Department of Transportation</a> (U.S. DOT) codes.
</p>
<h4><span id="United_Nations_Organization_.28UNO.29_Hazard_Class_and_Division_.28HC.2FD.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Nations_Organization_(UNO)_Hazard_Class_and_Division_(HC/D)">United Nations Organization (UNO) Hazard Class and Division (HC/D)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=43" title="Edit section: United Nations Organization (UNO) Hazard Class and Division (HC/D)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dangclass1.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="200" data-file-width="200" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Dangclass1.svg/220px-Dangclass1.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Dangclass1.svg/330px-Dangclass1.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Dangclass1.svg/440px-Dangclass1.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dangclass1.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Explosives warning sign</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HAZMAT_Class_1_Explosives" title="HAZMAT Class 1 Explosives">HAZMAT Class 1 Explosives</a></div>
<p>The Hazard Class and Division (HC/D) is a numeric designator within a hazard class indicating the character, predominance of associated hazards, and potential for causing personnel casualties and property damage. It is an internationally accepted system that communicates using the minimum amount of markings the primary hazard associated with a substance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Listed below are the Divisions for Class 1 (Explosives):
</p>
<ul><li><b>1.1</b> Mass Detonation Hazard. With HC/D 1.1, it is expected that if one item in a container or pallet inadvertently detonates, the explosion will <a href="/wiki/Sympathetic_detonation" title="Sympathetic detonation">sympathetically detonate</a> the surrounding items. The explosion could propagate to all or the majority of the items stored together, causing a mass detonation. There will also be fragments from the item's casing and/or structures in the blast area.</li>
<li><b>1.2</b> Non-mass explosion, fragment-producing. HC/D 1.2 is further divided into three subdivisions, HC/D 1.2.1, 1.2.2 and 1.2.3, to account for the magnitude of the effects of an explosion.</li>
<li><b>1.3</b> Mass fire, minor blast or fragment hazard. Propellants and many pyrotechnic items fall into this category. If one item in a package or stack initiates, it will usually propagate to the other items, creating a mass fire.</li>
<li><b>1.4</b> Moderate fire, no blast or fragment. HC/D 1.4 items are listed in the table as explosives with no significant hazard. Most small arms ammunition (including loaded weapons) and some pyrotechnic items fall into this category. If the energetic material in these items inadvertently initiates, most of the energy and fragments will be contained within the storage structure or the item containers themselves.</li>
<li><b>1.5</b> mass detonation hazard, very insensitive.</li>
<li><b>1.6</b> <a href="/wiki/Detonation" title="Detonation">detonation</a> hazard without mass detonation hazard, extremely insensitive.</li></ul>
<p>To see an entire UNO Table, browse Paragraphs 3-8 and 3-9 of NAVSEA OP 5, Vol. 1, Chapter 3.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Class_1_Compatibility_Group">Class 1 Compatibility Group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=44" title="Edit section: Class 1 Compatibility Group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Compatibility Group codes are used to indicate storage compatibility for HC/D Class 1 (explosive) materials. Letters are used to designate 13 compatibility groups as follows.
</p>
<ul><li><b>A</b>: Primary explosive substance (1.1A).</li>
<li><b>B</b>: An article containing a primary explosive substance and not containing two or more effective protective features. Some articles, such as detonator assemblies for blasting and primers, cap-type, are included. (1.1B, 1.2B, 1.4B).</li>
<li><b>C</b>: Propellant explosive substance or other deflagrating explosive substance or article containing such explosive substance (1.1C, 1.2C, 1.3C, 1.4C). These are bulk <a href="/wiki/Propellant" title="Propellant">propellants</a>, propelling charges, and devices containing propellants with or without means of ignition. Examples include single-based propellant, double-based propellant, triple-based propellant, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Composite_propellant" title="Composite propellant">composite propellants</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid_propellant" title="Solid propellant">solid propellant</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rocket_motor" title="Rocket motor">rocket motors</a> and ammunition with inert projectiles.</li>
<li><b>D</b>: Secondary detonating explosive substance or black powder or article containing a secondary detonating explosive substance, in each case without means of initiation and without a propelling charge, or article containing a primary explosive substance and containing two or more effective protective features. (1.1D, 1.2D, 1.4D, 1.5D).</li>
<li><b>E</b>: Article containing a secondary detonating explosive substance without means of initiation, with a propelling charge (other than one containing flammable liquid, gel or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypergolic" title="Hypergolic">hypergolic</a> liquid) (1.1E, 1.2E, 1.4E).</li>
<li><b>F</b> containing a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secondary_explosive" title="Secondary explosive">secondary</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Detonating_explosive" title="Detonating explosive">detonating explosive</a> substance with its means of initiation, with a propelling charge (other than one containing flammable liquid, gel or hypergolic liquid) or without a propelling charge (1.1F, 1.2F, 1.3F, 1.4F).</li>
<li><b>G</b>: Pyrotechnic substance or article containing a pyrotechnic substance, or article containing both an explosive substance and an illuminating, incendiary, tear-producing or smoke-producing substance (other than a water-activated article or one containing white phosphorus, phosphide or flammable liquid or gel or hypergolic liquid) (1.1G, 1.2G, 1.3G, 1.4G). Examples include Flares, signals, incendiary or illuminating ammunition and other smoke and tear producing devices.</li>
<li><b>H</b>: Article containing both an explosive substance and white phosphorus (1.2H, 1.3H). These articles will spontaneously combust when exposed to the atmosphere.</li>
<li><b>J</b>: Article containing both an explosive substance and flammable liquid or gel (1.1J, 1.2J, 1.3J). This excludes liquids or gels which are spontaneously flammable when exposed to water or the atmosphere, which belong in group H. Examples include liquid or gel filled incendiary ammunition, fuel-air explosive (FAE) devices, and flammable liquid fueled missiles.</li>
<li><b>K</b>: Article containing both an explosive substance and a toxic chemical agent (1.2K, 1.3K)</li>
<li><b>L</b> Explosive substance or article containing an explosive substance and presenting a special risk (e.g., due to water-activation or presence of hypergolic liquids, phosphides, or pyrophoric substances) needing isolation of each type (1.1L, 1.2L, 1.3L). Damaged or suspect ammunition of any group belongs in this group.</li>
<li><b>N</b>: Articles containing only extremely insensitive detonating substances (1.6N).</li>
<li><b>S</b>: Substance or article so packed or designed that any hazardous effects arising from accidental functioning are limited to the extent that they do not significantly hinder or prohibit fire fighting or other emergency response efforts in the immediate vicinity of the package (1.4S).</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation">Regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=45" title="Edit section: Regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The legality of possessing or using explosives varies by jurisdiction. Various countries around the world have enacted explosives law and require licenses to manufacture, distribute, store, use, possess explosives or ingredients.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Netherlands">Netherlands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=46" title="Edit section: Netherlands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, the civil and commercial use of explosives is covered under the <i>Wet explosieven voor civiel gebruik</i> (explosives for civil use Act), in accordance with EU directive nr. 93/15/EEG<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> (Dutch). The illegal use of explosives is covered under the <i>Wet Wapens en Munitie</i> (Weapons and Munition Act)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> (Dutch).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="UK">UK</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=47" title="Edit section: UK">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><p>
The new <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Explosives_Regulation_UK&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Explosives Regulation UK (page does not exist)">Explosives Regulations 2014</a> (ER 2014) came into force on 1 October 2014<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Explosive_Substances_Act_1883" title="Explosive Substances Act 1883">Explosive Substances Act 1883</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Explosive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=48" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, numerous laws were created to regulate war related industries and increase security within the United States. In 1917, the <a href="/wiki/65th_United_States_Congress" title="65th United States Congress">65th United States Congress</a> created <a href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_federal_legislation,_1901%E2%80%932001#65th_United_States_Congress" title="List of United States federal legislation, 1901–2001">many laws</a>, including the <i><a href="/wiki/Espionage_Act_of_1917" title="Espionage Act of 1917">Espionage Act of 1917</a></i> and <i>Explosives Act of 1917</i>.
</p><p>The <i>Explosives Act of 1917</i> (session 1, chapter 83, 40 <a href="/wiki/United_States_Statutes_at_Large" title="United States Statutes at Large">Stat.</a> <a class="external text" href="http://legislink.org/us/stat-40-385" rel="nofollow">385</a>) was signed on 6 October 1917 and went into effect on 16 November 1917. The legal summary is "An Act to prohibit the manufacture, distribution, storage, use, and possession in <b>time of war</b> of explosives, providing regulations for the safe manufacture, distribution, storage, use, and possession of the same, and for other purposes". This was the first federal regulation of licensing explosives purchases. The act was deactivated after World War I ended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>After the United States entered <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the Explosives Act of 1917 was reactivated. In 1947, the act was deactivated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/President_Truman" title="President Truman">President Truman</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organized_Crime_Control_Act_of_1970" title="Organized Crime Control Act of 1970">Organized Crime Control Act of 1970</a></i> (<a class="external text" href="http://legislink.org/us/pl-91-452" rel="nofollow">Pub.L. 91–452</a>) transferred many explosives regulations to the <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Alcohol,_Tobacco,_Firearms_and_Explosives" title="Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives">Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco and Firearms</a> (ATF) of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Treasury" title="United States Department of the Treasury">Department of Treasury</a>. The bill became effective in 1971.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Currently, regulations are governed by <a href="/wiki/Title_18_of_the_United_States_Code" title="Title 18 of the United States Code">Title 18 of the United States Code</a> and <a href="/wiki/Title_27_of_the_Code_of_Federal_Regulations" title="Title 27 of the Code of Federal Regulations">Title 27 of the Code of Federal Regulations</a>:
</p>
<ul><li>"Importation, Manufacture, Distribution and Storage of Explosive Materials" (18 U.S.C. Chapter 40).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li>"Commerce in Explosives" (27 C.F.R. Chapter II, Part 555).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li></ul>
