<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Gross national income</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">Gross domestic product</a>.</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Modified_gross_national_income" title="Modified gross national income">Modified gross national income</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GNI*" title="GNI*">GNI*</a>.</div>
<p>The <b>gross national income</b> (<b>GNI</b>), previously known as <b>gross national product</b> (<b>GNP</b>), is the total domestic and foreign output claimed by residents of a country, consisting of gross domestic product (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GDP" title="GDP">GDP</a>), plus <a href="/wiki/Factor_income" title="Factor income">factor incomes</a> earned by foreign residents, minus income earned in the domestic economy by nonresidents (Todaro &amp; Smith, 2011: 44) (all figures in millions of US dollars). Comparing GNI to GDP shows the degree to which a nation's GDP represents domestic or international activity.  GNI has gradually replaced GNP in international statistics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nominal_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-nominal-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ppp_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ppp-2">[2]</a></sup> While being conceptually identical, it is calculated differently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> GNI is the basis of calculation of the largest part of contributions to the <a href="/wiki/Budget_of_the_European_Union#GNI-based_own_resources" title="Budget of the European Union">budget of the European Union</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  In February 2017, Ireland's GDP became so distorted from the <a href="/wiki/Base_erosion_and_profit_shifting" title="Base erosion and profit shifting">base erosion and profit shifting</a> ("BEPS") tax planning tools of U.S. multinationals, that the <a href="/wiki/Central_Bank_of_Ireland" title="Central Bank of Ireland">Central Bank of Ireland</a> replaced Irish GDP with a new metric, Irish <a href="/wiki/Modified_gross_national_income" title="Modified gross national income">Modified GNI*</a>.  In 2017, Irish GDP was <a href="/wiki/Modified_gross_national_income#Irish_GDP_versus_Modified_GNI_(2009-2017)" title="Modified gross national income">162% of Irish Modified GNI*</a>.  
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gross_national_product">Gross national product</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gross_national_income&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Gross national product">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Gross national product</b> (<b>GNP</b>) is the market value of all the goods and services produced in one year by labor and property supplied by the citizens of a country. Unlike <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a> (GDP), which defines production based on the geographical location of production, GNP indicates allocated production based on location of ownership. In fact it calculates income by the location of ownership and residence, and so its name is also the less ambiguous <i><a class="mw-selflink selflink">gross national income</a></i>.
</p><p>GNP is an economic statistic that is equal to GDP plus any income earned by residents from overseas investments minus income earned within the domestic economy by overseas residents.
</p><p>GNP does not distinguish between qualitative improvements in the state of the technical arts (e.g., increasing computer processing speeds), and quantitative increases in goods (e.g., number of computers produced), and considers both to be forms of "<a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>When a country's capital or labour resources are employed outside its borders, or when a foreign firm is operating in its territory, GDP and GNP can produce different measures of total output. In 2009 for instance, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> estimated its GDP at $14.119 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trillion_(short_scale)" title="Trillion (short scale)">trillion</a>, and its GNP at $14.265 trillion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The term <i><a class="mw-selflink selflink">gross national income</a></i> (GNI) has gradually replaced the <i>Gross national product</i> (GNP) in international statistics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nominal_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-nominal-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ppp_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-ppp-2">[2]</a></sup> While being conceptually identical, the precise calculation method has evolved at the same time as the name change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_GNP">Use of GNP</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gross_national_income&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Use of GNP">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> used GNP as its primary measure of total economic activity until 1991, when it began to use GDP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In making the switch, the <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Economic_Analysis" title="Bureau of Economic Analysis">Bureau of Economic Analysis</a> (BEA) noted both that GDP provided an easier comparison of other measures of economic activity in the United States and that "virtually all other countries have already adopted GDP as their primary measure of production".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-announcement_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-announcement-12">[12]</a></sup> Many economists have questioned how meaningful GNP or GDP is a measure of a nation's economic well-being, as it does not count most <a href="/wiki/Unpaid_work" title="Unpaid work">unpaid work</a> and counts much economic activity that is unproductive or actually destructive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
