<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Gas to liquids</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Gas to liquids</b> (<b>GTL</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">refinery</a> process to convert <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> or other gaseous <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a> into longer-chain hydrocarbons, such as gasoline or diesel fuel. <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">Methane</a>-rich gases are converted into liquid <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_fuel" title="Synthetic fuel">synthetic fuels</a>. Two general strategies exist: (i) direct partial combustion of methane to methanol and (ii) <a href="/wiki/Fischer%E2%80%93Tropsch_process" title="Fischer–Tropsch process">Fischer-Tropsch</a>-like processes that convert carbon monoxide and hydrogen into hydrocarbons. Strategy ii is followed by diverse methods to convert the hydrogen-carbon monoxide mixtures to liquids. Direct partial combustion has been demonstrated in nature but not replicated commercially.  Technologies reliant on partial combustion have been commercialized mainly in regions where natural gas is inexpensive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hook_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-hook-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The motivation for GTL is to produce liquid fuels, which are more readily transported than methane.  Methane must be cooled below its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Critical_temperature" title="Critical temperature">critical temperature</a> of -82.3 °C in order to be liquified under pressure.  Because of the associated cryogenic apparatus, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LNG_tanker" title="LNG tanker">LNG tankers</a> are expensive, not to mention potentially dangerous. Methanol is a conveniently handled combustable liquid, but its <a href="/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">energy density</a> is half of that of gasoline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Methanier_aspher_LNGRIVERS.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="856" data-file-width="1588" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Methanier_aspher_LNGRIVERS.jpg/300px-Methanier_aspher_LNGRIVERS.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Methanier_aspher_LNGRIVERS.jpg/450px-Methanier_aspher_LNGRIVERS.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Methanier_aspher_LNGRIVERS.jpg/600px-Methanier_aspher_LNGRIVERS.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Methanier_aspher_LNGRIVERS.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Expensive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LNG_tanker" title="LNG tanker">LNG tankers</a> are required to transport methane.</div></div></div>

<h2><span id="Fischer.E2.80.93Tropsch_process"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Fischer–Tropsch_process">Fischer–Tropsch process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas_to_liquids&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Fischer–Tropsch process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fischer%E2%80%93Tropsch_process" title="Fischer–Tropsch process">Fischer–Tropsch process</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GTL_process.GIF"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1058" data-file-width="1138" height="325" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/GTL_process.GIF/350px-GTL_process.GIF" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/GTL_process.GIF/525px-GTL_process.GIF 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/GTL_process.GIF/700px-GTL_process.GIF 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GTL_process.GIF" title="Enlarge"></a></div>GTL process using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fischer_Tropsch" title="Fischer Tropsch">Fischer Tropsch</a> method</div></div></div>
<p>The Fischer–Tropsch process starts with partial oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> (natural gas) to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_gas" title="Hydrogen gas">hydrogen gas</a> and water. The ratio of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> to hydrogen is adjusted using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_gas_shift_reaction" title="Water gas shift reaction">water gas shift reaction</a>, while the excess <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> is removed with aqueous solutions of <a href="/wiki/Alkanolamine" title="Alkanolamine">alkanolamines</a>. Removing the water yields <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthesis_gas" title="Synthesis gas">synthesis gas</a> (syngas). Syngas is allowed to react over an iron or cobalt catalyst to produce liquid hydrocarbons, including alcohols.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methane_to_methanol_process">Methane to methanol process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas_to_liquids&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Methane to methanol process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Methanol is made from <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> (natural gas) in a series of three reactions:
</p>
<dl><dt><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_reforming" title="Fossil fuel reforming">Steam reforming</a></dt>
<dd>CH<sub>4</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O → CO + 3 H<sub>2</sub> <span style="padding-left:2em;"> </span> Δ<sub>r</sub>H = +206 kJ mol<sup>−1</sup></dd>
<dt><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_gas_shift_reaction" title="Water gas shift reaction">Water shift reaction</a></dt>
<dd>CO + H<sub>2</sub>O → CO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub> <span style="padding-left:2.5em;"> </span> Δ<sub>r</sub>H = -41 kJ mol<sup>−1</sup></dd>
<dt>Synthesis</dt>
<dd>2 H<sub>2</sub> + CO → CH<sub>3</sub>OH <span style="padding-left:3.5em;"> </span> Δ<sub>r</sub>H = -92 kJ mol<sup>−1</sup></dd></dl>
<p>The methanol thus formed may be converted to gasoline by the <b>Mobil process</b> and methanol-to-olefins.
</p>
<h2><span id="Methanol_to_gasoline_.28MTG.29_and_methanol_to_olefins"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Methanol_to_gasoline_(MTG)_and_methanol_to_olefins">Methanol to gasoline (MTG) and methanol to olefins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas_to_liquids&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Methanol to gasoline (MTG) and methanol to olefins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the early 1970s, <a href="/wiki/Mobil" title="Mobil">Mobil</a> developed an alternative procedure in which natural gas is converted to syngas, and then <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>. The methanol reacts in the presence of a <a href="/wiki/Zeolite" title="Zeolite">zeolite</a> catalyst to form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkanes" title="Alkanes">alkanes</a>. In terms of mechanism, methanol is partially <a href="/wiki/Dehydration_reaction" title="Dehydration reaction">dehydrated</a> to give <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_ether" title="Dimethyl ether">dimethyl ether</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 CH<sub>3</sub>OH → CH<sub>3</sub>OCH<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>The mixture of dimethyl ether and methanol is then further dehydrated over a zeolite catalyst such as <a href="/wiki/ZSM-5" title="ZSM-5">ZSM-5</a>, which in practice is polymerized and hydrogenated to give a gasoline with hydrocarbons of five or more carbon atoms making up 80% of the fuel by weight.  The Mobile MTG process is no longer practiced. A more modern implementation of MTG is the Topsøe integrated gasoline synthesis (TIGS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Methanol can can be converted to olefins using zeolite and SAPO-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterogeneous_catalyst" title="Heterogeneous catalyst">heterogeneous catalysts</a>. Depending on the catalyst pore size, this process can afford either C2 or C3 products, which are important monomers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Syngas_to_gasoline_plus_process_.28STG.2B.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Syngas_to_gasoline_plus_process_(STG+)">Syngas to gasoline plus process (STG+)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas_to_liquids&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Syngas to gasoline plus process (STG+)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Syngas_to_gasoline_plus" title="Syngas to gasoline plus">Syngas to gasoline plus</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:STG%2B_Process.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="312" data-file-width="675" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/STG%2B_Process.jpg/350px-STG%2B_Process.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/STG%2B_Process.jpg/525px-STG%2B_Process.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d0/STG%2B_Process.jpg 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:STG%2B_Process.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The STG+ Process</div></div></div>
<p>A third gas-to-liquids process builds on the MTG technology by converting natural gas-derived syngas into drop-in gasoline and jet fuel via a thermochemical single-loop process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bridge2Biofuels_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bridge2Biofuels-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The STG+ process follows four principal steps in one continuous process loop. This process consists of four <a href="/wiki/Packed_bed" title="Packed bed">fixed bed reactors</a> in series in which a <a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">syngas</a> is converted to synthetic fuels. The steps for producing high-octane synthetic gasoline are as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STG+_White_Paper_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-STG+_White_Paper-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">Methanol Synthesis</a>: Syngas is fed to Reactor 1, the first of four reactors, which converts most of the syngas (CO and H<span style="display:inline-block; margin-bottom:-0.3em; vertical-align:-0.4em; line-height:1.2em; font-size:70%; text-align:left;"><br/>2</span>) to methanol (CH<span style="display:inline-block; margin-bottom:-0.3em; vertical-align:-0.4em; line-height:1.2em; font-size:70%; text-align:left;"><br/>3</span>OH) when passing through the catalyst bed.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_ether" title="Dimethyl ether">Dimethyl Ether</a> (DME) Synthesis: The methanol-rich gas from Reactor 1 is next fed to Reactor 2, the second STG+ reactor. The methanol is exposed to a <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalyst</a> and much of it is converted to DME, which involves a dehydration from methanol to form DME (CH<span style="display:inline-block; margin-bottom:-0.3em; vertical-align:-0.4em; line-height:1.2em; font-size:70%; text-align:left;"><br/>3</span>OCH<span style="display:inline-block; margin-bottom:-0.3em; vertical-align:-0.4em; line-height:1.2em; font-size:70%; text-align:left;"><br/>3</span>).</li>
<li>Gasoline synthesis: The Reactor 2 product gas is next fed to Reactor 3, the third reactor containing the catalyst for conversion of DME to hydrocarbons including paraffins (<a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">alkanes</a>), <a href="/wiki/Aromaticity" title="Aromaticity">aromatics</a>, naphthenes (<a href="/wiki/Cycloalkane" title="Cycloalkane">cycloalkanes</a>) and small amounts of olefins (<a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkenes</a>), mostly from C<span style="display:inline-block; margin-bottom:-0.3em; vertical-align:-0.4em; line-height:1.2em; font-size:70%; text-align:left;"><br/>6</span> (number of carbon atoms in the hydrocarbon molecule) to C<span style="display:inline-block; margin-bottom:-0.3em; vertical-align:-0.4em; line-height:1.2em; font-size:70%; text-align:left;"><br/>10</span>.</li>
<li>Gasoline Treatment: The fourth reactor provides <a href="/wiki/Transalkylation" title="Transalkylation">transalkylation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenation</a> treatment to the products coming from Reactor 3.  The treatment reduces <a href="/wiki/Durene" title="Durene">durene</a> (tetramethylbenzene)/isodurene and trimethylbenzene components that have high freezing points and must be minimized in gasoline.  As a result, the synthetic gasoline product has high octane and desirable viscometric properties.</li>
<li>Separator: Finally, the mixture from Reactor 4 is condensed to obtain gasoline. The non-condensed gas and gasoline are separated in a conventional condenser/separator. Most of the non-condensed gas from the product separator becomes recycled gas and is sent back to the feed stream to Reactor 1, leaving the synthetic gasoline product composed of paraffins, aromatics and naphthenes.</li></ol>
<h2><span id="Biological_gas-to-liquids_.28Bio-GTL.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_gas-to-liquids_(Bio-GTL)">Biological gas-to-liquids (Bio-GTL)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas_to_liquids&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Biological gas-to-liquids (Bio-GTL)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With methane as the predominant target for GTL, much attention has focused on the three enzymes that process methane. These enzymes support the existence of <a href="/wiki/Methanotroph" title="Methanotroph">methanotrophs</a>, microorganisms that metabolize methane as their only source of carbon and energy. Aerobic methanotrophs harbor enzymes that oxygenate of methane to methanol.  The relevant enzymes are <a href="/wiki/Methane_monooxygenase" title="Methane monooxygenase">methane monooxygenases</a>, which are found both in soluble and particulate (i.e. membrane-bound) varieties.  They catalyze the oxygenation according to the following stoichiometry:
</p>
<dl><dd>CH<sub>4</sub>  +  O<sub>2</sub>  +  NADPH  +  H<sup>+</sup>  →  CH<sub>3</sub>OH  +  H<sub>2</sub>O  +  NAD<sup>+</sup></dd></dl>
<p>Anaerobic methanotrophs rely on the bioconversion of methane using the enzymes called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methyl-coenzyme_M_reductase" title="Methyl-coenzyme M reductase">methyl-coenzyme M reductases</a>.  These organisms effect reverse <a href="/wiki/Methanogenesis" title="Methanogenesis">methanogenesis</a>.  Strenuous efforts have been made to elucidate the mechanisms of these methane-converting enzymes, which would enable their catalysis to be replicated in vitro.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">Biodiesel</a> can be made from CO<sub>2</sub> using the microbes <a href="/wiki/Moorella_thermoacetica" title="Moorella thermoacetica">Moorella thermoacetica</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yarrowia_lipolytica" title="Yarrowia lipolytica">Yarrowia lipolytica</a>. This process is known as biological gas-to-liquids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_uses">Commercial uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas_to_liquids&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Commercial uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Using gas-to-liquids processes, refineries can convert some of their gaseous waste products (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flare_gas" title="Flare gas">flare gas</a>) into valuable <a href="/wiki/Fuel_oil" title="Fuel oil">fuel oils</a>, which can be sold as is or blended only with <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel fuel</a>. The <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> estimates that over 150 billion cubic metres (5.3<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>12</sup> cu ft) of natural gas are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flared_gas" title="Flared gas">flared</a> or vented annually, an amount worth approximately $30.6 billion, equivalent to 25% of the United States' gas consumption or 30% of the European Union's annual gas consumption,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> a resource that could be useful using GTL.  Gas-to-liquids processes may also be used for the economic extraction of gas deposits in locations where it is not economical to build a pipeline. This process will be increasingly significant as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a> resources are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peak_Oil" title="Peak Oil">depleted</a>.
</p><p>Royal Dutch Shell produces a diesel from natural gas in a factory in <a href="/wiki/Bintulu" title="Bintulu">Bintulu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>. Another Shell GTL facility is the <a href="/wiki/Pearl_GTL" title="Pearl GTL">Pearl GTL</a> plant in <a href="/wiki/Qatar" title="Qatar">Qatar</a>, the world's largest GTL facility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> SASOL has recently built the <a href="/wiki/Oryx_GTL" title="Oryx GTL">Oryx GTL</a> facility in <a href="/wiki/Ras_Laffan_Industrial_City" title="Ras Laffan Industrial City">Ras Laffan Industrial City</a>, Qatar and together with <a href="/wiki/Uzbekneftegaz" title="Uzbekneftegaz">Uzbekneftegaz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Petronas" title="Petronas">Petronas</a> builds the <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan_GTL" title="Uzbekistan GTL">Uzbekistan GTL</a> plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-upstream080409_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-upstream080409-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters140509_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-reuters140509-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ogj080310_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-ogj080310-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Chevron_Corporation" title="Chevron Corporation">Chevron Corporation</a>, in a joint venture with the <a href="/wiki/Nigerian_National_Petroleum_Corporation" title="Nigerian National Petroleum Corporation">Nigerian National Petroleum Corporation</a> is commissioning the <a href="/wiki/Escravos_GTL" title="Escravos GTL">Escravos GTL</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>, which uses Sasol technology.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aspirational_and_emerging_ventures">Aspirational and emerging ventures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas_to_liquids&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aspirational and emerging ventures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>New generation of GTL technology is being pursued for the conversion of unconventional, remote and problem gas into valuable liquid fuels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unlocking_the_value_of_stranded_and_remote_offshore_gas_assets_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Unlocking_the_value_of_stranded_and_remote_offshore_gas_assets-18">[18]</a></sup> GTL plants based on innovative Fischer-Tropsch catalyst have been built by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=INFRA_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="INFRA Technology (page does not exist)">INFRA Technology</a>. Other mainly U.S. companies include Velocys, ENVIA Energy, Waste Management, NRG Energy, Ventech, Velocys, ThyssenKrupp Industrial Solutions, Liberty GTL, <a href="/wiki/Petrobras" title="Petrobras">Petrobras</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and Greenway Innovative Energy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Primus Green Energy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and Compact GTL.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and Petronas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>  Several of these processes have proven themselves with demonstration flights using their jet fuels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another proposed solution to stranded gas involves use of novel <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floating_Production_Storage_and_Offloading" title="Floating Production Storage and Offloading">FPSO</a> for offshore conversion of gas to liquids such as <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petrol" title="Petrol">petrol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_crude" title="Synthetic crude">synthetic crude</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Naphtha" title="Naphtha">naphtha</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
