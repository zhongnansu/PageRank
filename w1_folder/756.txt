<p><b>Social metabolism</b> or <b>socioeconomic metabolism</b> is the set of <a href="/wiki/Stock_and_flow" title="Stock and flow">flows</a> of <a href="/wiki/Material" title="Material">materials</a> and <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> that occur between <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">Nature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a>, between different societies, and within societies. These human-controlled material and energy flows are a basic feature of all societies but their magnitude and diversity largely depend on specific <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">cultures</a>, or sociometabolic regimes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Metabolism_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Metabolism-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metabolismo_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-metabolismo-2">[2]</a></sup>
Social or socioeconomic metabolism is also described as "the self-reproduction and evolution of the biophysical structures of human society. It comprises those biophysical transformation processes, distribution processes, and flows, which are controlled by humans for their purposes. The biophysical structures of society (‘in use stocks’) and socioeconomic metabolism together form the biophysical basis of society."
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pauliuk_Hertwich_2015_pp._83–93_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pauliuk_Hertwich_2015_pp._83–93-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Social metabolic processes begin with the <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> <i>appropriation</i> of materials and energy from nature. These can be <i>transformed</i> and <i>circulated</i> to be <i>consumed</i> and <i>excreted</i> finally back to Nature itself. Each of these processes has a different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_impact" title="Environmental impact">environmental impact</a> depending on how it is performed, the amount of materials and energy involved in the process, the area where it occurs, the time available or the Nature's regenerative capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Metabolism_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Metabolism-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-metabolismo_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-metabolismo-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Social metabolism represents an extension of the metabolism concept from human bodies to the biophysical basis of society. Humans build and operate mines and farms, oil refineries and power stations, factories and infrastructure to supply the energy and material flows needed for the physical reproduction of a specific culture. In-use stocks, which comprise buildings, vehicles, appliances, infrastructure, etc., are built up and maintained by the different industrial processes that are part of social metabolism. These stocks then provide service to people in form of shelter, transportation, or communication.
</p><p>Society and its metabolism together form an <a href="/wiki/Autopoiesis" title="Autopoiesis">autopoietic</a> system, a complex system that reproduces itself. Neither culture nor social metabolism can reproduce themselves in isolation. Humans need food and shelter, which is delivered by social metabolism, and the latter needs humans to operate it.
</p><p>Studies of social metabolism can be carried out at different levels of system aggregation, see <a href="/wiki/Material_flow_analysis" title="Material flow analysis">material flow analysis</a>. In material flow accounting, for example, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Factor_of_production" title="Factor of production">inputs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Output_(economics)" title="Output (economics)">outputs</a> of materials and energy of a particular <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a> or region, as well as <a href="/wiki/Import" title="Import">imports</a> and <a href="/wiki/Export" title="Export">exports</a>, are analysed. Such studies are facilitated by the ease of access to information about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commercial_transaction" title="Commercial transaction">commercial transactions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Social or socioeconomic metabolism stipulates that human society and its interaction with Nature form a complex  self-reproducing system, and it can therefore be seen as <a href="/wiki/Paradigm" title="Paradigm">paradigm</a> for studying the biophysical basis of human societies under the aspect of self-reproduction. "A common paradigm can facilitate model combination and integration, which can lead to more robust and comprehensive interdisciplinary assessments of sustainable development strategies. ... The use of social or socioeconomic metabolism as paradigm can help to justify alternative economic concepts."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pauliuk_Hertwich_2015_pp._83–93_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pauliuk_Hertwich_2015_pp._83–93-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
