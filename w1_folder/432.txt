<p><b>Mount Nyiragongo</b> is an active <a href="/wiki/Stratovolcano" title="Stratovolcano">stratovolcano</a> with an elevation of 3,470 m (11,380 ft)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GVP_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-GVP-2">[2]</a></sup> in the <a href="/wiki/Virunga_Mountains" title="Virunga Mountains">Virunga Mountains</a> associated with the <a href="/wiki/Albertine_Rift" title="Albertine Rift">Albertine Rift</a>. It is located inside <a href="/wiki/Virunga_National_Park" title="Virunga National Park">Virunga National Park</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a>, about 20 km (12 mi) north of the town of <a href="/wiki/Goma" title="Goma">Goma</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Kivu" title="Lake Kivu">Lake Kivu</a> and just west of the border with <a href="/wiki/Rwanda" title="Rwanda">Rwanda</a>. The main crater is about two kilometres wide and usually contains a <a href="/wiki/Lava_lake" title="Lava lake">lava lake</a>. The crater presently has two distinct cooled <a href="/wiki/Lava_bench" title="Lava bench">lava benches</a> within the crater walls – one at about 3,175 m (10,417 ft) and a lower one at about 2,975 m (9,760 ft).  Nyiragongo's lava lake has at times been the most voluminous known lava lake in recent history. The depth of the lava lake varies considerably.  A maximum elevation of the lava lake was recorded at about 3,250 m (10,660 ft) prior to the January 1977 eruption – a lake depth of about 600 m (2,000 ft). A recent very low elevation of the lava lake was recorded at about 2,700 m (8,900 ft). Nyiragongo and nearby <a href="/wiki/Nyamuragira" title="Nyamuragira">Nyamuragira</a> are together responsible for 40% of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>'s historical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic_eruption" title="Volcanic eruption">volcanic eruptions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNESCO_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNESCO-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Nyiragongo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nyiragongo_and_Nyamuragira_-_PIA03337.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="311" data-file-width="670" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Nyiragongo_and_Nyamuragira_-_PIA03337.jpg/300px-Nyiragongo_and_Nyamuragira_-_PIA03337.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Nyiragongo_and_Nyamuragira_-_PIA03337.jpg/450px-Nyiragongo_and_Nyamuragira_-_PIA03337.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Nyiragongo_and_Nyamuragira_-_PIA03337.jpg/600px-Nyiragongo_and_Nyamuragira_-_PIA03337.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nyiragongo_and_Nyamuragira_-_PIA03337.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nyamuragira (left) and Nyiragongo (right). Vertical scale exaggerated (1.5x).</div></div></div>
<p>The volcano partly overlaps with two older volcanoes, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Baratu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Baratu (page does not exist)">Baratu</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Shaheru&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Shaheru (page does not exist)">Shaheru</a>, and is also surrounded by hundreds of small volcanic cinder cones from flank eruptions.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava</a> emitted in eruptions at Nyiragongo is often unusually <a href="/wiki/Fluid" title="Fluid">fluid</a>. Nyiragongo's lavas are made of <a href="/wiki/Melilite" title="Melilite">melilite</a> <a href="/wiki/Nephelinite" title="Nephelinite">nephelinite</a>, an alkali-rich type of volcanic rock whose unusual chemical composition may be a factor in the unusual fluidity of the lavas there. Whereas most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lava_flow" title="Lava flow">lava flows</a> move rather slowly and rarely pose a danger to human life, Nyiragongo's lava flows may race downhill at up to 100 km/h (60 mph). This is because of the extremely low <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">silica</a> content (the lava is <a href="/wiki/Mafic" title="Mafic">mafic</a>). <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaiian</a> volcanic eruptions are also characterized by lavas with low silica content, but the Hawaiian volcanoes are broad, shallow-sloped <a href="/wiki/Shield_volcano" title="Shield volcano">shield volcanoes</a> in contrast to the steep-sided cone of Nyiragongo, and the silica content is high enough to slow most Hawaiian flows to walking pace.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Active_history">Active history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Nyiragongo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Active history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Not much is known about how long the volcano has been erupting, but since 1882, it has erupted at least 34 times, including many periods where activity was continuous for years at a time, often in the form of a churning lava lake in the crater. The existence of the lava lake had been suspected for some time, but was not scientifically confirmed until 1948.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-readersnatural_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-readersnatural-4">[4]</a></sup> At that time, it was measured at nearly 120,000 square metres (1,300,000 sq ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-readersnatural_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-readersnatural-4">[4]</a></sup> Subsequent expeditions showed that the lake fluctuated in size, depth, and temperature over time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-readersnatural_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-readersnatural-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The lava lake activity continued through 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GVP_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-GVP-2">[2]</a></sup> As of June 2010, the lake is mostly confined within a broad, steep-sided <a href="/wiki/Cinder_cone" title="Cinder cone">cinder cone</a> on the crater floor, roughly 60 feet high by 600 feet wide<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1977_Eruption">1977 Eruption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Nyiragongo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1977 Eruption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nirogongo-9-75-n-kivu.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="1687" height="130" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Nirogongo-9-75-n-kivu.jpg/220px-Nirogongo-9-75-n-kivu.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Nirogongo-9-75-n-kivu.jpg/330px-Nirogongo-9-75-n-kivu.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Nirogongo-9-75-n-kivu.jpg/440px-Nirogongo-9-75-n-kivu.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nirogongo-9-75-n-kivu.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nirogongo, 1975</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lava_Lake_Nyiragongo_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2820" data-file-width="4324" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Lava_Lake_Nyiragongo_2.jpg/220px-Lava_Lake_Nyiragongo_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Lava_Lake_Nyiragongo_2.jpg/330px-Lava_Lake_Nyiragongo_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Lava_Lake_Nyiragongo_2.jpg/440px-Lava_Lake_Nyiragongo_2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lava_Lake_Nyiragongo_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nyiragongo's lava lake</div></div></div>
<p>Between 1894 and 1977 the crater contained an active lava lake.  On 10 January 1977, the crater walls fractured, and the lava lake drained in less than an hour.  The lava flowed down the flanks of the volcano at speeds of up to 60 km/h (40 mi/h, the fastest lava flow recorded to date)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> on the upper slopes, overwhelming villages and killing at least 70 people.  The hazards posed by eruptions like this are unique to Nyiragongo.  Nowhere else in the world does such a steep-sided <a href="/wiki/Stratovolcano" title="Stratovolcano">stratovolcano</a> contain a lake of such fluid lava.  Nyiragongo's proximity to heavily populated areas increases its potential for causing a <a href="/wiki/Natural_disaster" title="Natural disaster">natural disaster</a>.  The 1977 eruption raised awareness of the unique dangers posed by Nyiragongo, and because of this it was designated a <a href="/wiki/Decade_Volcanoes" title="Decade Volcanoes">Decade Volcano</a>, worthy of particular study, in 1991.
</p><p>The 1977 eruption was preceded by the creation of a new small volcano, <a href="/wiki/Murara" title="Murara">Murara</a>, a short distance away on the slopes of <a href="/wiki/Nyamuragira" title="Nyamuragira">Nyamuragira</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="2002_Eruptions">2002 Eruptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Nyiragongo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2002 Eruptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nyiragongo_2002_eruption.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="540" data-file-width="540" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Nyiragongo_2002_eruption.jpg/220px-Nyiragongo_2002_eruption.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Nyiragongo_2002_eruption.jpg/330px-Nyiragongo_2002_eruption.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Nyiragongo_2002_eruption.jpg/440px-Nyiragongo_2002_eruption.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nyiragongo_2002_eruption.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Satellite image of the eruption plume from Nyiragongo in July 2004</div></div></div>
<p>Lava lakes reformed in the crater in eruptions in 1982–1983 and 1994.  Another major eruption of the volcano began on January 17, 2002, after several months of increased <a href="/wiki/Seismology" title="Seismology">seismic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fumarole" title="Fumarole">fumarolic</a> activity.  A 13 km <a href="/wiki/Fissure_vent" title="Fissure vent">fissure</a> opened in the south flank of the volcano, spreading in a few hours from 2800 m to 1550 m elevation and reaching the outskirts of the city of <a href="/wiki/Goma" title="Goma">Goma</a>, the provincial capital on the northern shore of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Kivu" title="Lake Kivu">Lake Kivu</a>. Lava streamed from three spatter cones at the end of the fissure and flowed in a stream 200 to 1000 m wide and up to 2 m deep through Goma. Warnings had been given and 400,000 people were evacuated from the city across the <a href="/wiki/Rwanda" title="Rwanda">Rwandan</a> border into neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Gisenyi" title="Gisenyi">Gisenyi</a> during the eruption. Lava covered the northern end of the runway at <a href="/wiki/Goma_International_Airport" title="Goma International Airport">Goma International Airport</a>, leaving the southern two-thirds usable, and reached <a href="/wiki/Lake_Kivu" title="Lake Kivu">Lake Kivu</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ERI_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ERI-7">[7]</a></sup> This raised fears that the lava might cause gas-saturated waters deep in the lake to suddenly rise to the surface, releasing lethally large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> – similar to the disaster at <a href="/wiki/Lake_Nyos" title="Lake Nyos">Lake Nyos</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a> in 1986. This did not happen, but volcanologists continue to monitor the area closely.
</p><p>About 245 people died in the eruption from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asphyxiation" title="Asphyxiation">asphyxiation</a> by <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and buildings collapsing due to the lava and earthquakes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Death_toll_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Death_toll-9">[9]</a></sup>  At least 15% of Goma comprising 4,500 buildings were destroyed, leaving about 120,000 people <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homeless" title="Homeless">homeless</a>.
</p><p>Immediately after the eruption stopped, a large number of earthquakes were felt around Goma and Gisenyi. This swarm activity continued for about three months and caused the collapse of more buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ERI_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-ERI-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Six months after the start of the 2002 eruption, Nyiragongo volcano erupted again.  Activity at Nyiragongo is ongoing, but currently confined to the crater, where another lava lake has formed about 250 metres below the level of the 1994 lava lake.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ongoing_threat">Ongoing threat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Nyiragongo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ongoing threat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Localized carbon dioxide toxicity, known locally as '<a href="/wiki/Mazuku" title="Mazuku">mazuku</a>', has killed children even more recently. In locations where the gas seeps from the ground in relatively high levels, without the dispersing effects of wind, its effects can be deadly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> On March 8, 2016, Goma Volcano Observatory discovered a new vent that opened in the northeast edge of the crater, following local reports of rumblings coming from the volcano.  Some fear that this could lead to a <a href="/wiki/Lateral_eruption" title="Lateral eruption">flank eruption</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Monitoring">Monitoring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Nyiragongo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Monitoring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The volcano is monitored by a team of scientists at the Goma Volcanic Observatory. It is monitored 24/7 365 days a year with seismic data produced every four minutes and temperature data produced every ten minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
