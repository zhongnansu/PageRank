<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Happy Planet Index</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:552px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Happy_Planet.PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="628" data-file-width="1357" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Happy_Planet.PNG/550px-Happy_Planet.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Happy_Planet.PNG/825px-Happy_Planet.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Happy_Planet.PNG/1100px-Happy_Planet.PNG 2x" width="550"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Happy_Planet.PNG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map showing countries shaded by their position in the Happy Planet Index (2006). The highest-ranked countries are bright green; the lowest are brown.<br/>Note: Score legend by colour is below. <div><ul><li>Green: 4000+</li><li>Yellow Green: 3500-4000</li><li>Yellow: 3000-3500</li><li>Dark Yellow: 2500-3000</li><li>Orange: 2000-2500</li><li>Brown: &lt;2000</li><li>Grey: Data unavailable.</li></ul></div></div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Happy Planet Index</b> (<b>HPI</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Index_(economics)" title="Index (economics)">index</a> of human <a href="/wiki/Well-being" title="Well-being">well-being</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_impact" title="Environmental impact">environmental impact</a> that was introduced by the <a href="/wiki/New_Economics_Foundation" title="New Economics Foundation">New Economics Foundation</a> (NEF) in July 2006. The index is weighted to give progressively higher scores to nations with lower <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprints</a>.
</p><p>The index is designed to challenge well-established indices of countries’ development, such as the <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a> (GDP) and the <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Index" title="Human Development Index">Human Development Index</a> (HDI), which are seen as not taking <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> into account. In particular, GDP is seen as inappropriate, as the usual ultimate aim of most people is not to be rich, but to be <a href="/wiki/Happiness" title="Happiness">happy</a> and healthy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Development_as_Freedom_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Development_as_Freedom-1">[1]</a></sup> Furthermore, it is believed that the notion of <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a> requires a measure of the environmental costs of pursuing those goals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Natural_Capitalism_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Natural_Capitalism-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Out of the 178 countries surveyed in 2006, the best scoring countries were <a href="/wiki/Vanuatu" title="Vanuatu">Vanuatu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dominica" title="Dominica">Dominica</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Index06_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Index06-3">[3]</a></sup> In 2009, <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a> was the best scoring country among the 143 analyzed, followed by the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>. <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Botswana" title="Botswana">Botswana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a> were featured at the bottom of the list.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Index09_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Index09-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>For the 2012 ranking, 151 countries were compared, and the best scoring country for the second time in a row was Costa Rica, followed by Vietnam, Colombia, <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a> and <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">El Salvador</a>. The lowest ranking countries in 2012 were Botswana, <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a> and <a href="/wiki/Qatar" title="Qatar">Qatar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Index12_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Index12-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian061412_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian061412-6">[6]</a></sup> In 2016, out of 140 countries, Costa Rica topped the index for the third time in a row. It was followed by Mexico, Colombia, Vanuatu and Vietnam. At the bottom were Chad, Luxembourg and Togo.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methodology">Methodology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Happy_Planet_Index&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Methodology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The HPI is based on general <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Utilitarian" title="Utilitarian">utilitarian</a> principles –  that most people want to live long and fulfilling lives, and the country which is doing the best is the one that allows its citizens to do so, whilst avoiding infringing on the opportunity of future people and people in other countries to do the same. In effect it operationalises the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUCN" title="IUCN">IUCN</a>'s (World Conservation Union) call for a metric capable of measuring 'the production of human well-being (not necessarily material goods) per unit of extraction of or imposition upon nature'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The IUCN is an <a href="/wiki/International_organization" title="International organization">international organization</a> working in the field of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nature_conservation" title="Nature conservation">nature conservation</a> and the sustainable use of <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a>. And everything else remains the same. It measures what matters, a sustainable well being for all. 
</p><p>Human well-being is conceptualized as <a href="/wiki/Happy_life_expectancy" title="Happy life expectancy">happy life expectancy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Extraction of or imposition upon nature is evaluated by using the <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a> per capita, which attempts to estimate the amount of natural resources required to sustain a given country's lifestyle. A country with a large per capita ecological footprint uses more than its fair share of resources, both by drawing resources from other countries, and also by causing permanent damage to the planet which will affect future generations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>As such, the HPI is <i>not</i> a measure of which are the happiest countries in the world. Countries with relatively high levels of <a href="/wiki/Life_satisfaction" title="Life satisfaction">life satisfaction</a>, as measured in surveys, are found from the very top (<a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a> in 3rd place) to the very bottom (the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">US</a> in 108th place) of the rank order. The HPI is best conceived as a measure of the <i>environmental efficiency of supporting well-being</i> in a given country. Such efficiency could emerge in a country with a medium environmental impact (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>) and very high well-being, but it could also emerge in a country with only mediocre well-being, but very minimal environmental impact (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>).
</p><p>Each country’s HPI value is a function of its average <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subjective_life_satisfaction" title="Subjective life satisfaction">subjective life satisfaction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Life_expectancy" title="Life expectancy">life expectancy</a> at birth, and <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a> per capita. The exact function is a little more complex, but conceptually it approximates multiplying life satisfaction and life expectancy, and dividing that by the ecological footprint.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Happy_Planet_Index&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Much criticism of the index has been due to commentators incorrectly understanding it to be a measure of personal <a href="/wiki/Happiness" title="Happiness">happiness</a>, when it is in fact a measure of the "happiness" of the planet. In other words it is a measure of the ecological <i>efficiency</i> of supporting well-being. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Aside from that, criticism has focused on the following:
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/World_Values_Survey" title="World Values Survey">World Values Survey</a> covers only a minority of the world's nations and is only carried out every five years. As a result, much of the data for the index must come from other sources or is estimated using regressions.</li>
<li>The subjective measures of well-being are suspect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs additional references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">additional citation(s) needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a> is a controversial and much-criticized concept.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint noexcerpt Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (November 2017)">better source needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li></ul>
<p>The index has been criticised for weighting the carbon footprint too heavily, to the point that the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">US</a> would have had to be universally happy and would have had to have a life expectancy of 439 years to equal <a href="/wiki/Vanuatu" title="Vanuatu">Vanuatu</a>'s score in the 2006 index.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Nevertheless, the HPI and its components have been considered in political circles. The ecological footprint, championed by the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature" title="World Wide Fund for Nature">WWF</a>, is widely used by both local and national governments, as well as supranational organisations such as the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a>. The HPI itself was cited in 2007, in the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">British Conservative Party</a> as a possible substitute for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gross_Domestic_Product" title="Gross Domestic Product">GDP</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> A 2007 review of progress indicators produced by the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> lists the following pros and cons to using the HPI as a measure of national <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Progress_(history)" title="Progress (history)">progress</a>:
</p>
<dl><dt>Pros</dt></dl>
<ul><li>considers the actual ‘ends’ of economic activity in the form of life satisfaction and longevity</li>
<li>combines well being and environmental aspects</li>
<li>simple and easily understandable scheme for calculating the index</li>
<li>comparability of results (‘EF’ and ‘life expectancy’ can be applied to different countries)</li>
<li>data online available, although some data gaps remain</li>
<li>mixture of ‘soft’ and ‘hard’ criteria; takes into account people’s well-being and resource use of countries</li></ul>
<dl><dt>Cons</dt></dl>
<ul><li>‘happiness’ or ‘life satisfaction’ are very subjective and personal: cultural influences and complex impact of policies on happiness.</li>
<li>confusion of name: index is not a measure of happiness but rather a measure of environmental efficiency of supporting well-being in a given country.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_rankings">International rankings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Happy_Planet_Index&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: International rankings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2012_ranking">2012 ranking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Happy_Planet_Index&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2012 ranking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For the 2012 ranking, 151 countries were compared and nine out of the ten top countries were located in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Basin" title="Caribbean Basin">Caribbean Basin</a>, despite high levels of poverty. The ranking was led by Costa Rica for the second time in a row, with its lead considered to be due to its very high life expectancy which is second highest in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Americas" title="The Americas">the Americas</a>, and higher than the US. Citizens of Costa Rica were found to experience well-being higher than many richer nations and the country had a per capita footprint one third the size of the US. The second happiest country was Vietnam. Among the top 40 countries by overall HPI score, only four countries have a GDP per capita of over <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$15,000</span>. The highest ranking <a href="/wiki/OECD" title="OECD">OECD</a> country is Israel in 15th place, and the top Western European nation is Norway in 29th place, just behind New Zealand in 28th.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian061412_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian061412-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Among the top five world's biggest economies in terms of GDP, Japan has the highest ranking in 45th place, followed by Germany in 46th, France is placed 50th, China 60, and the US is ranked 105, mainly due to its very high environmental footprint, the seventh highest of all countries rated for the 2012 index.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
