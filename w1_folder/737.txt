<p><b>Vattenfall</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_power_industry" title="Electrical power industry">power company</a>, wholly <a href="/wiki/List_of_government_enterprises_of_Sweden" title="List of government enterprises of Sweden">owned by the Swedish state</a>. Beyond Sweden, the company generates power in Denmark, Finland, Germany, the Netherlands, and the United Kingdom.
</p><p>The company's name is Swedish for "<a href="/wiki/Waterfall" title="Waterfall">waterfall</a>", and is an abbreviation of its original name, Royal Waterfall Board (<i>Kungliga Vattenfallstyrelsen</i>).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vattenfall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vattenfall (then called <i>Kungliga Vattenfallsstyrelsen</i> or Royal Waterfall Board) was founded in 1909 as a state-owned enterprise in Sweden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-history-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> From its founding until the mid-1970s, Vattenfall's business was largely restricted to <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, with a focus on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroelectric_power" title="Hydroelectric power">hydroelectric power</a> generation. Only in 1974 did the company begin to build nuclear reactors in Sweden (the <a href="/wiki/Ringhals_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Ringhals Nuclear Power Plant">Ringhals 1 and 2</a> reactors), eventually owning seven of Sweden's 12 reactors. In 1992, Vattenfall was reformed as the <a href="/wiki/Limited_liability_company" title="Limited liability company">limited liability company</a> Vattenfall AB.
</p><p>In the years 1990 through 2009, Vattenfall expanded considerably (especially into Germany, Poland and the Netherlands), acquiring stakes in Hämeen Sähkö (1996), HEW (1999, 25.1% stake from the city of <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>), the Polish heat production company EW (2000, 55% stake), Elsam A/S (2005, 35.3% stake), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuon_(company)" title="Nuon (company)">Nuon</a> (2009, 49% stake, today 100%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-history-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PM_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-PM-5">[5]</a></sup> In 2002 Vattenfall AB and its acquisitions were incorporated as Vattenfall Europe AG, making it the third-largest electricity producer in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-history-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Following the expansion period, Vattenfall started to divest parts of its business in Denmark and Poland during the years following 2009 in a strategy to focus on three core markets: Sweden, Netherlands, and Germany. Write-downs on coal-fired and nuclear power plant assets in Germany and gas power plants in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> were necessary in a difficult market environment with increasing renewable energy market share and due to the German <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_phase-out" title="Nuclear power phase-out">Nuclear power phase-out</a> decision of 2011. In summer 2013 Vattenfall announced a writedown off the value of its assets by 29.7 billion SEK (4.6 billion USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WriteDown2013_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-WriteDown2013-6">[6]</a></sup> A major part of these write-offs were attributed to Nuon Energy NV, a Netherlands-based utility that Vattenfall purchased at a 89 billion SEK (ca. 15 billion USD) price in 2009, but whose values was depreciating by 15 billion SEK (ca. 2 billion USD) since.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SVDNedskrivning2013_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-SVDNedskrivning2013-7">[7]</a></sup> The gloomy market outlook of decreasing power prices in combination with increasing risks notably on the continental market prompted the board to revise the group strategy by splitting its organizational structure into a Nordic part and a part with operations in continental Europe and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> as of 2014. Some analysts have perceived this strategic review as a precursor to a partial retreat from continental European activities with a shift of focus towards activities in the Scandinavian market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RetreatfromEurope_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-RetreatfromEurope-8">[8]</a></sup> In this context and in response to a local referendum on re-municipilization of distribution grids, Vattenfall agreed on the sale of company-owned electricity and district-heat grids in Hamburg to the City of Hamburg in early 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HamburgGrid_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-HamburgGrid-9">[9]</a></sup> In each of the second quarters of 2015 and 2016, Vattenfall filed <a href="/wiki/Impairment_(financial_reporting)" title="Impairment (financial reporting)">impairments</a> of SEK 28 billion, mostly due to lignite power stations in Germany. Operational financials were satisfactory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In 2017, Vattenfall reported a profit of SEK 9,571 billion with an operating profit (EBIT) of SEK 18,644 billion.
</p><p>Outside of Sweden, Vattenfall is known for forcing the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> government to publicly reveal the <a href="/wiki/Chernobyl_disaster" title="Chernobyl disaster">Chernobyl disaster</a>.  The Kremlin had tried to cover up the accident for a day, but elevated radiation levels at Vattenfall's <a href="/wiki/Forsmark_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Forsmark Nuclear Power Plant">Forsmark Nuclear Power Plant</a> forced the Kremlin to admit the accident had occurred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion_beyond_Sweden">Expansion beyond Sweden</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vattenfall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Expansion beyond Sweden">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pruszkow_Elektrownia_cropped.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1987" data-file-width="2843" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Pruszkow_Elektrownia_cropped.jpg/220px-Pruszkow_Elektrownia_cropped.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Pruszkow_Elektrownia_cropped.jpg/330px-Pruszkow_Elektrownia_cropped.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Pruszkow_Elektrownia_cropped.jpg/440px-Pruszkow_Elektrownia_cropped.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pruszkow_Elektrownia_cropped.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Vattenfall used to own assets in <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> until their divestment in 2011. Here exemplarily a power station in <a href="/wiki/Pruszk%C3%B3w" title="Pruszków">Pruszków</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>In 2006, Vattenfall began production of the pilot <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a> (CCS) plant at <a href="/wiki/Schwarze_Pumpe_power_station" title="Schwarze Pumpe power station">Schwarze Pumpe</a>, Germany. In 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Lillgrund_Wind_Farm" title="Lillgrund Wind Farm">Lillgrund Wind Farm</a> off the southern coast of Sweden was commissioned and began delivering electricity.
</p><p>Vattenfall has power generation branches in Sweden, Germany, <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">the Netherlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>; in Germany, Vattenfall is the <a href="/wiki/Electric_utility" title="Electric utility">electric utility</a> for the states of <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mecklenburg-Vorpommern" title="Mecklenburg-Vorpommern">Mecklenburg-Vorpommern</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brandenburg" title="Brandenburg">Brandenburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saxony-Anhalt" title="Saxony-Anhalt">Saxony-Anhalt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thuringia" title="Thuringia">Thuringia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Saxony" title="Saxony">Saxony</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Generation">Generation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vattenfall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Generation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of 2017,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The text near this tag is dated. (September 2012)">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup> Vattenfall generates <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a> (25%), <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> (41%), <a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">hydropower</a> (28%), <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind power</a> (6%) and "other sources" (biomass, waste) (0,1%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-report_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-report-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some of Vattenfall's most notable power generation plants include the 110 MW <a href="/wiki/Lillgrund_Wind_Farm" title="Lillgrund Wind Farm">Lillgrund Wind Farm</a> off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Malm%C3%B6" title="Malmö">Malmö</a>, Sweden, the world's largest<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian20100923_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian20100923-13">[13]</a></sup> offshore wind farm at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thanet_Offshore_Wind_Project" title="Thanet Offshore Wind Project">Thanet, UK</a>, the nuclear reactors <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brunsbuttel_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Brunsbuttel Nuclear Power Plant">Brunsbüttel Nuclear Power Plant</a> (67% ownership), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Krummel_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Krummel Nuclear Power Plant">Krümmel Nuclear Power Plant</a> (50% ownership), <a href="/wiki/Brokdorf_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Brokdorf Nuclear Power Plant">Brokdorf Nuclear Power Plant</a> (20% ownership) in Germany, and the <a href="/wiki/Forsmark_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Forsmark Nuclear Power Plant">Forsmark Nuclear Power Plant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ringhals_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Ringhals Nuclear Power Plant">Ringhals Nuclear Power Plant</a> in Sweden. The nuclear power stations of Brunsbüttel and Krümmel have been shut down permanently in response to a governmental order in summer 2011 after the <a href="/wiki/Fukushima_Daiichi_nuclear_disaster" title="Fukushima Daiichi nuclear disaster">Fukushima Daiichi nuclear disaster</a>.
</p><p>Vattenfall also operates <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> and other power plants in Germany and the Netherlands and Denmark.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exit_from_German_coal">Exit from German coal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vattenfall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Exit from German coal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Until 2016, Vattenfall owned <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a> and hard coal-fired power stations, including the <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A4nschwalde_Power_Station" title="Jänschwalde Power Station">Jänschwalde Power Station</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Boxberg_Power_Station" title="Boxberg Power Station">Boxberg Power Station</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lippendorf_Power_Station" title="Lippendorf Power Station">Lippendorf Power Station</a> (owned in part) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schwarze_Pumpe_Power_Station" title="Schwarze Pumpe Power Station">Schwarze Pumpe Power Station</a>. In 2014, Vattenfall had a lignite turnover of €2.3 billion and a profit of €647 million, but later lost money on lignite as power prices decreased from 40 to 20 €/MWh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> On 30<span class="nowrap"> </span>September 2016, Vattenfall completed the sale of its German lignite facilities to the Czech energy group <a href="/wiki/Energetick%C3%BD_a_pr%C5%AFmyslov%C3%BD_holding" title="Energetický a průmyslový holding">EPH</a> and its financial partner <a href="/wiki/PPF_(company)" title="PPF (company)">PPF Investments</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vattenfall-2016_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-vattenfall-2016-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_generation">Nuclear generation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vattenfall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nuclear generation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In January 2016 Vattenfall announced that its Swedish nuclear power plants, including the newer reactors, were operating at a loss due to low electricity prices and Sweden's nuclear output tax. It warned that if it was forced to shut the plants down, there would be serious consequences to Sweden's electricity supply, and argued that the nuclear output tax should be scrapped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wnn-20160108_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-wnn-20160108-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2016 Vattenfall began litigation against the German government for its 2011 decision to accelerate the phase-out of nuclear power.  Hearings are taking place at the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a>'s <a href="/wiki/International_Centre_for_Settlement_of_Investment_Disputes" title="International Centre for Settlement of Investment Disputes">International Centre for Settlement of Investment Disputes</a> (ICSID) in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_DC" title="Washington DC">Washington DC</a> and Vattenfall is claiming almost €4.7<span class="nowrap"> </span>billion in damages.  The German government regards the action as "inadmissible and unfounded".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clew-2016_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-clew-2016-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Car_seatbelt">Car seatbelt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vattenfall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Car seatbelt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The development of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Safety_belt" title="Safety belt">safety belt</a> is often incorrectly credited to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saab_automobile" title="Saab automobile">Saab</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volvo_cars" title="Volvo cars">Volvo</a>. Fatal car accidents were rapidly increasing in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> during the 1950s. When a study at Vattenfall of accidents among employees revealed that the majority of casualties came from car accidents, two Vattenfall engineers (Bengt Odelgard and Per-Olof Weman) started to develop the safety belt. Their work set the standard for safety belts in Swedish cars and was presented to Volvo in the late 1950s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vattenfall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-criticism" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="Unbalanced scales.svg" data-file-height="354" data-file-width="400" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Unbalanced_scales.svg/45px-Unbalanced_scales.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Unbalanced_scales.svg/68px-Unbalanced_scales.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Unbalanced_scales.svg/90px-Unbalanced_scales.svg.png 2x" width="45"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This article's <i>Criticism</i> or <i>Controversy</i> section <b>may compromise the article's <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Neutral_point_of_view" title="Wikipedia:Neutral point of view">neutral point of view</a> of the subject</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Neutral_point_of_view#Article_structure" title="Wikipedia:Neutral point of view">integrate the section's contents</a> into the article as a whole, or rewrite the material.</span> <small><i>(September 2012)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Baggerbesetzung_Welzow_S%C3%BCd.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1066" data-file-width="1600" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Baggerbesetzung_Welzow_S%C3%BCd.jpg/220px-Baggerbesetzung_Welzow_S%C3%BCd.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Baggerbesetzung_Welzow_S%C3%BCd.jpg/330px-Baggerbesetzung_Welzow_S%C3%BCd.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Baggerbesetzung_Welzow_S%C3%BCd.jpg/440px-Baggerbesetzung_Welzow_S%C3%BCd.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Baggerbesetzung_Welzow_S%C3%BCd.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>3,500–4,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_activist" title="Environmental activist">environmental activists</a> <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_phase-out" title="Fossil fuel phase-out">blocking a coal mine</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Schwarze_Pumpe_power_station" title="Schwarze Pumpe power station">Schwarze Pumpe power station</a> to <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation" title="Climate change mitigation">limit climate change</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ende_Gel%C3%A4nde_2016" title="Ende Gelände 2016">Ende Gelände 2016</a>)</div></div></div>
<p>Vattenfall's past expansion strategy has involved the acquisition of multiple <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">brown coal (lignite)</a>-fired power plants, which has been highly controversial in Sweden and Germany due to the fact that brown coal is among the dirtiest forms of electricity generation. In addition, brown coal is strip mined in a process that sometimes forces communities to relocate as mining fields expand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> It sold its brown coal assets in September 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to Greenpeace, Vattenfall's coal-fired power plants account for more than twice as much CO₂-emissions as the rest of Sweden combined, and, if counting their Swedish-owned but foreign-located plants as Swedish, would bring Sweden up to fourth most CO₂-emitting country, counting per capita.
</p><p>In May 2009, Vattenfall was voted the winner of the 2009 <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_Greenwash_Awards&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Climate Greenwash Awards (page does not exist)">Climate Greenwash Awards</a> for "its mastery of spin on climate change, portraying itself as a climate champion while lobbying to continue business as usual, using coal, nuclear power, and pseudo-solutions such as agrofuels and carbon capture and storage (CCS)."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-corporate-europe-observatory-2009_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-corporate-europe-observatory-2009-23">[23]</a></sup> Vattenfall owns (or has owned) four of the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dirty_thirty_(WWF)" title="Dirty thirty (WWF)">dirty thirty</a>" most polluting power stations in Europe.
</p><p>The first fire in the transformer of the nuclear power plant <a href="/wiki/Kr%C3%BCmmel_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Krümmel Nuclear Power Plant">Krümmel</a> (part owned with <a href="/wiki/E.ON" title="E.ON">E.ON</a>) in 2007 forced a closure of the power plant for over two years, while a short circuit in July 2009 in another transformer led to another closure. Due to these incidents the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minister-President#Germany" title="Minister-President">Prime Minister</a> of <a href="/wiki/Schleswig-Holstein" title="Schleswig-Holstein">Schleswig-Holstein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Harry_Carstensen" title="Peter Harry Carstensen">Peter Harry Carstensen</a> announced that this will be <i>letzter Versuch</i> (their last try) before complete closure of the facility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Vattenfall has been accused of skirting the line of illegality in its effort to maintain ownership of electrical power grids.  Most recently, Vattenfall's efforts to maintain ownership of Hamburg's power grid by lobbying the ruling SPD have drawn criticism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>In Germany, the largely successful <i>Berlin Energy Table</i> alliance united a number of NGOs and local groups initiating a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Referendum_on_the_recommunalization_of_energy_supply_in_Berlin" title="Referendum on the recommunalization of energy supply in Berlin">Referendum on the recommunalization of energy supply in Berlin</a>. The referendum took place on November 3, 2013, yet slightly missed the quorum. Just days ahead of the referendum, the <a href="/wiki/Senate_of_Berlin" title="Senate of Berlin">Senate of Berlin</a> however promised to match the citizens' initiative's key claim, regardless of the referendum's outcome: to transfer all end-user operations, which are currently driven by Vattenfall, to a <a href="/wiki/Public_utility" title="Public utility">public utility</a> company (<i>Stadtwerk</i>) soon to be founded.
</p>
