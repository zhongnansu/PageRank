<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Standard cubic foot</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_Cubic_Feet_per_Minute" title="Standard Cubic Feet per Minute">Standard Cubic Feet per Minute</a> and <a href="/wiki/Standard_conditions_for_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard conditions for temperature and pressure">Standard conditions for temperature and pressure</a></div>
<p>A <b>standard cubic foot</b> (scf) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Units_of_measurement" title="Units of measurement">unit</a> used both in the <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> industry to represent an amount of natural gas and in other industries where other gases are used. It is the unit commonly used when following the US Customary System, a collection of standards set by the US <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">National Institute of Standards and Technology</a>. Another unit used for the same purpose is the <b>standard cubic meter</b>, customary when using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_unit" title="SI unit">SI units</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NGMH_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NGMH-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Standard_cubic_foot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A standard cubic foot defines a standard unit of <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">molecular quantity</a> for gases, and not of <a href="/wiki/Volume" title="Volume">volume</a>, as the name suggests. In spite of the label "standard", there is a variety of definitions, mainly depending on the type of gas. Since, for a given volume, the quantity is proportional to the <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absolute_temperature" title="Absolute temperature">(absolute) temperature</a>, each definition fixes base values for pressure and temperature. The <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas_law" title="Ideal gas law">ideal gas law</a> allows then to compute the quantity per unit of volume for actual pressures and temperatures. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_gas">Natural gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Standard_cubic_foot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Natural gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> is an imprecise mix of various molecular species, chiefly <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> but with varying proportions of other gases, a standard cubic foot of natural gas does not represent a precise unit of mass, but only a molecular quantity, expressed in <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">moles</a>.
</p><p>In the US Customary System, one standard cubic foot (scf) of natural gas is the amount of natural gas contained in a cube whose edges are one foot long at a base temperature of 60 °F (288.706 K) and base pressure of 14.73 pounds per square inch [psi] (101.560 kPa).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NGMH_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NGMH-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The standard cubic meter of gas (scm) is used in the context of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_system" title="SI system">SI system</a>. It similarly defined as the quantity of gas contained in a cubic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meter" title="Meter">meter</a> at a temperature of 15 °C (288.150 K) and a pressure of 101.325 kPa (1 <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">atm</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NGMH_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-NGMH-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Converting between these two different standard volume units is not straightforward, because the base temperature and pressure used are different. A standard cubic foot in the US Customary System is approximately equivalent to 0.02833 standard cubic meters in the SI system.
</p><p>In the natural gas industry, where quantities are often expressed in standard cubic feet, large multiples of standard cubic feet are generally not expressed with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_Prefixes" title="SI Prefixes">metric prefixes</a>, but rather with prefixes based on roman numerals, where the <i>s</i> for "standard" is often omitted. Common units of gas volumes include <b>ccf</b> (hundred standard cubic feet), <b>Mcf</b> (thousand standard cubic feet), and <b>MMcf</b> (million standard cubic feet).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The <i>M</i> refers to the Roman numeral for thousand, while two <i>M's</i> represent one thousand thousands, or one million. <b>Bcf</b> (billion standard cubic feet), <b>Tcf</b> (trillion standard cubic feet), <b>Qcf</b> (quadrillion standard cubic feet), etc., are also used.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Compressed_or_liquefied_gases_in_refillable_cylinders">Compressed or liquefied gases in refillable cylinders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Standard_cubic_foot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Compressed or liquefied gases in refillable cylinders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Conference_on_Weights_and_Measures" title="National Conference on Weights and Measures">National Conference on Weights and Measures</a>, a US-based non-profit organization working in cooperation with the US National Institute of Standards and Technology, has defined a set of standards in a regulation entitled the "Uniform Regulation for the Method of Sale of Commodities".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-URMSC_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-URMSC-3">[3]</a></sup> This regulation defines a standard cubic foot, for compressed or liquefied gases in refillable cylinders <i>other than <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_petroleum_gas" title="Liquefied petroleum gas">LPG</a></i> by, "A standard cubic foot of gas is defined as a cubic foot at a temperature of 21 °C (70 °F) and a pressure of 101.35 kilopascals [kPa] (14.696 psia)".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-URMSC_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-URMSC-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_gases">Industrial gases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Standard_cubic_foot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Industrial gases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Yet other definitions are in use for <a href="/wiki/Industrial_gas" title="Industrial gas">industrial gas</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> where, in the US, a standard cubic foot for industrial gas use is defined at 70 °F (21.1 °C) and 14.696 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch_absolute" title="Pounds per square inch absolute">psia</a> (101.325 kPa), while in Canada, a standard cubic meter for industrial gas use is defined at 15 °C (59 °F) and 101.325 kPa (14.696 psia).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Converting_actual_volumes_to_standard_volumes">Converting actual volumes to standard volumes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Standard_cubic_foot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Converting actual volumes to standard volumes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An actual volume can be converted to a standard volume using the following equation:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>V<sub>s</sub> = V<sub>a</sub> × F<sub>p</sub> × F<sub>t</sub> × (F<sub>pv</sub>)<sup>2</sup></dd></dl>
<p>Where,
</p>
<dl><dd>V<sub>s</sub>: Standard Volume</dd>
<dd>V<sub>a</sub>: Actual Volume ( sometimes shown as V<sub>r</sub> for Registered Volume )</dd>
<dd>F<sub>p</sub>: Pressure Factor ( sometimes shown as P<sub>m</sub> for Pressure Multiplier )
<dl><dd>F<sub>p</sub> = Absolute Pressure / Standard Pressure = (Line Gauge Pressure + Atmospheric Pressure)/Base Pressure</dd></dl></dd>
<dd>F<sub>t</sub>: Temperature Factor ( sometimes shown as T<sub>m</sub> for Temperature Multiplier )
<dl><dd>F<sub>t</sub> = Absolute Standard Temperature / Absolute Line Temperature = [ 273.15 + Standard Temperature(deg C) ] / [ 273.15 + Line Temperature(deg C) ] or [ 459.67 + Standard Temperature(deg F) ] / [ 459.67 + Line Temperature(deg F) ]</dd></dl></dd>
<dd>F<sub>pv</sub>: Super Compressibility Factor (often omitted or shown as equaling 1)</dd></dl>
<p>Example:
How many standard cubic feet are in 1 cubic foot of gas at 80 °F and 50 psig? (assuming we have 13.6 psi atmospheric pressure and ignoring super compressibility)
</p>
<dl><dd>V<sub>s</sub> = 1 cu ft * [ ( 13.6 psi + 50 psi ) / 14.73 psi ] * [ ( 60°F + 459.67°F ) / ( 80°F + 459.67°F ) ]</dd>
<dd>V<sub>s</sub> = 4.16 scf</dd></dl>
