<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Chemical compound</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Substance composed of multiple elements</div>
<p>A <b>chemical compound</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_substance" title="Chemical substance">chemical substance</a> composed of many identical <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Molecular_entity" title="Molecular entity">molecular entities</a>) composed of <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> from more than one <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">element</a> held together by <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">chemical bonds</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> bonded to an identical chemical element is not a chemical compound since only one element, not two different elements, is involved.
</p><p>There are four types of compounds, depending on how the constituent atoms are held together:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> held together by <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">covalent bonds</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ionic_compound" title="Ionic compound">ionic compounds</a> held together by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionic_bond" title="Ionic bond">ionic bonds</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intermetallic_compounds" title="Intermetallic compounds">intermetallic compounds</a> held together by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metallic_bond" title="Metallic bond">metallic bonds</a></li>
<li>certain <a href="/wiki/Coordination_complex" title="Coordination complex">complexes</a> held together by <a href="/wiki/Coordinate_covalent_bond" title="Coordinate covalent bond">coordinate covalent bonds</a>.</li></ul>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formula</a> is a way of expressing information about the proportions of atoms that constitute a particular chemical compound, using the standard abbreviations for the chemical elements, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subscript" title="Subscript">subscripts</a> to indicate the number of atoms involved. For example, <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> is composed of two <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_atom" title="Hydrogen atom">hydrogen atoms</a> bonded to one <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> atom: the chemical formula is H<sub>2</sub>O. Many <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemical</a> compounds have a unique numerical identifier assigned by the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_Abstracts_Service" title="Chemical Abstracts Service">Chemical Abstracts Service</a> (CAS): its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CAS_number" title="CAS number">CAS number</a>.
</p><p>
A compound can be converted to a different chemical composition by interaction with a second chemical compound via a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reaction</a>. In this process, bonds between atoms are broken in both of the interacting compounds, and then bonds are reformed so that new associations are made between atoms.   </p><table class="infobox" style="width:22em"><tbody><tr><td colspan="2" style="text-align:center">

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemical_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Any substance consisting of two or more different types of <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> (<a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical elements</a>) in a fixed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stoichiometric" title="Stoichiometric">stoichiometric</a> proportion can be termed a <i>chemical compound</i>; the concept is most readily understood when considering pure <a href="/wiki/Chemical_substance" title="Chemical substance">chemical substances</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whitten_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whitten-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>15</span></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_p.6_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown_p.6-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hill_p.6_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hill_p.6-3">[3]</a></sup> It follows from their being composed of fixed proportions of two or more types of atoms that chemical compounds can be converted, via <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reaction</a>, into compounds or substances each having fewer atoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilbraham_p.36_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wilbraham_p.36-4">[4]</a></sup> The ratio of each element in the compound is expressed in a ratio in its chemical formula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formula</a> is a way of expressing information about the proportions of atoms that constitute a particular chemical compound, using the standard abbreviations for the chemical elements, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subscript" title="Subscript">subscripts</a> to indicate the number of atoms involved. For example, <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> is composed of two <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_atom" title="Hydrogen atom">hydrogen atoms</a> bonded to one <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> atom: the chemical formula is H<sub>2</sub>O. In the case of <a href="/wiki/Non-stoichiometric_compound" title="Non-stoichiometric compound">non-stoichiometric compounds</a>, the proportions may be reproducible with regard to their preparation, and give fixed proportions of their component elements, but proportions that are not integral [e.g., for <a href="/wiki/Palladium_hydride" title="Palladium hydride">palladium hydride</a>, PdH<sub>x</sub> (0.02 &lt; x &lt; 0.58)].<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PdH_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-PdH-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Chemical compounds have a unique and defined <a href="/wiki/Chemical_structure" title="Chemical structure">chemical structure</a> held together in a defined spatial arrangement by <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">chemical bonds</a>. Chemical compounds can be <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecular</a> compounds held together by <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">covalent bonds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a> held together by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionic_bond" title="Ionic bond">ionic bonds</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intermetallic_compounds" title="Intermetallic compounds">intermetallic compounds</a> held together by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metallic_bond" title="Metallic bond">metallic bonds</a>, or the subset of <a href="/wiki/Coordination_complex" title="Coordination complex">chemical complexes</a> that are held together by <a href="/wiki/Coordinate_covalent_bond" title="Coordinate covalent bond">coordinate covalent bonds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChemPrinciples_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ChemPrinciples-7">[7]</a></sup> Pure <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical elements</a> are generally not considered chemical compounds, failing the two or more atom requirement, though they often consist of molecules composed of multiple atoms (such as in the <a href="/wiki/Diatomic_molecule" title="Diatomic molecule">diatomic molecule</a> H<sub>2</sub>, or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyatomic_molecule" title="Polyatomic molecule">polyatomic molecule</a> S<sub>8</sub>, etc.).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChemPrinciples_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-ChemPrinciples-7">[7]</a></sup> Many <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemical</a> compounds have a unique numerical identifier assigned by the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_Abstracts_Service" title="Chemical Abstracts Service">Chemical Abstracts Service</a> (CAS): its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CAS_number" title="CAS number">CAS number</a>.
</p><p>There is varying and sometimes inconsistent nomenclature differentiating substances, which include truly non-stoichiometric examples, from chemical compounds, which require the fixed ratios. Many solid chemical substances—for example many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silicate_mineral" title="Silicate mineral">silicate minerals</a>—are chemical substances, but do not have simple formulae reflecting chemically bonding of elements to one another in fixed ratios; even so, these <a href="/wiki/Crystal_structure" title="Crystal structure">crystalline</a> substances are often called "<a href="/wiki/Non-stoichiometric_compound" title="Non-stoichiometric compound">non-stoichiometric compounds</a>".  It may be argued that they are related to, rather than being chemical compounds, insofar as the variability in their compositions is often due to either the presence of foreign elements trapped within the crystal structure of an otherwise known true <i>chemical compound</i>, or due to perturbations in structure relative to the known compound that arise because of an excess of deficit of the constituent elements at places in its structure; such non-stoichiometric substances form most of the <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> of the Earth.  Other compounds regarded as chemically identical may have varying amounts of heavy or light <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a> of the constituent elements, which changes the ratio of elements by mass slightly.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bonding_and_forces">Bonding and forces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemical_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Bonding and forces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Compounds are held together through a variety of different types of bonding and forces. The differences in the types of bonds in compounds differ based on the types of elements present in the compound.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_dispersion_forces" title="London dispersion forces">London dispersion forces</a> are the weakest force of all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intermolecular_forces" title="Intermolecular forces">intermolecular forces</a>. They are temporary attractive forces that form when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrons" title="Electrons">electrons</a> in two adjacent atoms are positioned so that they create a temporary <a href="/wiki/Dipole" title="Dipole">dipole</a>. Additionally, London dispersion forces are responsible for condensing  <a href="/wiki/Chemical_polarity" title="Chemical polarity">non polar</a> substances to liquids, and to further freeze to a solid state dependent on how low the temperature of the environment is.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">covalent bond</a>, also known as a molecular bond, involves the sharing of electrons between two atoms. Primarily, this type of bond occurs between elements that fall close to each other on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periodic_table_of_elements" title="Periodic table of elements">periodic table of elements</a>, yet it is observed between some metals and nonmetals. This is due to the mechanism of this type of bond. Elements that fall close to each other on the periodic table tend to have similar  <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">electronegativities</a>, which means they have a similar affinity for electrons. Since neither element has a stronger affinity to donate or gain electrons, it causes the elements to share electrons so both elements have a more stable <a href="/wiki/Octet_rule" title="Octet rule">octet</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ionic_bonding" title="Ionic bonding">Ionic bonding</a> occurs when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valence_electrons" title="Valence electrons">valence electrons</a> are completely transferred between elements. Opposite to covalent bonding, this chemical bond creates two oppositely charged ions. The metals in ionic bonding usually lose their valence electrons, becoming a positively charged <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">cation</a>. The nonmetal will gain the electrons from the metal, making the nonmetal a negatively charged <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">anion</a>. As outlined, ionic bonds occur between an electron donor, usually a metal, and an electron acceptor, which tends to be a nonmetal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bonding" title="Hydrogen bonding">Hydrogen bonding</a> occurs when a <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_atom" title="Hydrogen atom">hydrogen atom</a> bonded to an electronegative atom forms an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrostatic" title="Electrostatic">electrostatic</a> connection with another electronegative atom through interacting dipoles or charges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemical_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">Chemical reaction</a></div>
<p>A compound can be converted to a different chemical composition by interaction with a second chemical compound via a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reaction</a>. In this process, bonds between atoms are broken in both of the interacting compounds, and then bonds are reformed so that new associations are made between atoms. Schematically, this reaction could be described as <span class="nowrap">AB + CD → AD + CB</span>, where A, B, C, and D are each unique atoms; and AB, AD, CD, and CB are each unique compounds.
</p>
