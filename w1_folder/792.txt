<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Mass flux</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>In <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>, <b>mass flux</b> is the rate of <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> flow per unit area, perfectly overlapping with the <b>momentum density</b>, the <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a> per unit volume. The common symbols are <i>j</i>, <i>J</i>, <i>q</i>, <i>Q</i>, <i>φ</i>, or <i>Φ</i> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> lower or capital <a href="/wiki/Phi" title="Phi">Phi</a>), sometimes with subscript <i>m</i> to indicate mass is the flowing quantity. Its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_units" title="SI units">SI units</a> are kg s<sup>−1</sup> m<sup>−2</sup>. Mass flux can also refer to an alternate form of flux in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fick%27s_law" title="Fick's law">Fick's law</a> that includes the <a href="/wiki/Molecular_mass" title="Molecular mass">molecular mass</a>, or in <a href="/wiki/Darcy%27s_law" title="Darcy's law">Darcy's law</a> that includes the mass <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Unfortunately, sometimes the defining equation for mass flux in this article is used interchangeably with the defining equation in <a href="/wiki/Mass_flow_rate" title="Mass flow rate">mass flow rate</a>. For example, <i>Fluid Mechanics, Schaum's et al</i> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> uses the definition of mass flux as the equation in the mass flow rate article.
</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Area" title="Area">area</a> required to calculate the flux is real or imaginary, flat or curved, either as a cross-sectional area or a surface.
</p><p>For example, for substances passing through a <a href="/wiki/Filter_paper" title="Filter paper">filter</a> or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artificial_membrane" title="Artificial membrane">membrane</a>, the real surface is the (generally curved) surface area of the filter, <a href="/wiki/Macroscopic_scale" title="Macroscopic scale">macroscopically</a> - ignoring the area spanned by the holes in the filter/membrane.
