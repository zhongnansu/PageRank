<p>A <b>capillary</b>  is a small <a href="/wiki/Blood_vessel" title="Blood vessel">blood vessel</a> from 5 to 10 <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">micrometres</a> (µm) in diameter, and having a wall one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endothelial_cell" title="Endothelial cell">endothelial cell</a> thick. They are the smallest blood vessels in the body: they convey blood between the <a href="/wiki/Arteriole" title="Arteriole">arterioles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Venule" title="Venule">venules</a>. These <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microvessels" title="Microvessels">microvessels</a> are the site of exchange of  many substances with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstitial_fluid" title="Interstitial fluid">interstitial fluid</a> surrounding them. Substances which exit include <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> (proximal portion), <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a>; substances which enter include <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> (distal portion), <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uric_acid" title="Uric acid">uric acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urea" title="Urea">urea</a> and  <a href="/wiki/Creatinine" title="Creatinine">creatinine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lymph_capillaries" title="Lymph capillaries">Lymph capillaries</a> connect with larger lymph vessels to drain <a href="/wiki/Lymph" title="Lymph">lymphatic</a> fluid collected in the <a href="/wiki/Microcirculation" title="Microcirculation">microcirculation</a>.
</p><p>During early <a href="/wiki/Embryogenesis" title="Embryogenesis">embryonic development</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> new capillaries are formed through <a href="/wiki/Vasculogenesis" title="Vasculogenesis">vasculogenesis</a>, the process of blood vessel formation that occurs through a <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/de_novo" title="wiktionary:de novo">de novo</a></i> production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endothelial_cell" title="Endothelial cell">endothelial cells</a> which then form vascular tubes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Penn2008_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Penn2008-5">[5]</a></sup> The term <a href="/wiki/Angiogenesis" title="Angiogenesis">angiogenesis</a> denotes the formation of new capillaries from pre-existing blood vessels and already present endothelium which divides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-urlEndoderm_--_Developmental_Biology_--_NCBI_Bookshelf_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-urlEndoderm_--_Developmental_Biology_--_NCBI_Bookshelf-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capillary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Capillary.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="200" data-file-width="400" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Capillary.svg/220px-Capillary.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Capillary.svg/330px-Capillary.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Capillary.svg/440px-Capillary.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Capillary.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diagram of a capillary</div></div></div>
<p>Blood flows from the heart through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arteries" title="Arteries">arteries</a>, which branch and narrow into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arterioles" title="Arterioles">arterioles</a>, and then branch further into capillaries where nutrients and wastes are exchanged. The capillaries then join and widen to become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venules" title="Venules">venules</a>, which in turn widen and converge to become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veins" title="Veins">veins</a>, which then return blood back to the heart through the <a href="/wiki/Venae_cavae" title="Venae cavae">venae cavae</a>.
</p><p>Individual capillaries are part of the <i>capillary bed</i>, an interweaving network of capillaries supplying <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">tissues</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organ_(biology)" title="Organ (biology)">organs</a>.  The more <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolically</a> active a tissue is, the more capillaries are required to supply nutrients and carry away waste products. There are two types of capillaries: true capillaries, which branch from arterioles and provide exchange between tissue and the capillary blood, and  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metarterioles" title="Metarterioles">metarterioles</a>, found only  in the mesenteric circulation. They are short vessels that directly connect the arterioles and venules at opposite ends of the beds. <a href="/wiki/Metarteriole" title="Metarteriole">Metarterioles</a> are found primarily in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesenteric" title="Mesenteric">mesenteric</a> <a href="/wiki/Microcirculation" title="Microcirculation">microcirculation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sakai_13_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sakai_13-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sakai_13_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sakai_13-1">[1]</a></sup> The physiological mechanisms underlying <a href="/wiki/Precapillary_resistance" title="Precapillary resistance">precapillary resistance</a> is no longer considered to be a result of precapillary sphincters outside of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesentery_organ" title="Mesentery organ">mesentery organ</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sakai_13_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Sakai_13-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Lymphatic capillaries are slightly larger in diameter than blood capillaries, and have closed ends (unlike the blood capillaries open at one end to the arterioles and open at the other end to the venules). This structure permits <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstitial_fluid" title="Interstitial fluid">interstitial fluid</a> to flow into them but not out. Lymph capillaries have a greater internal <a href="/wiki/Oncotic_pressure" title="Oncotic pressure">oncotic pressure</a> than blood capillaries, due to the greater concentration of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plasma_proteins" title="Plasma proteins">plasma proteins</a> in the lymph.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Continuous">Continuous</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capillary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Continuous">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Continuous capillaries are continuous in the sense that the endothelial cells provide an uninterrupted lining, and they only allow smaller <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecules" title="Molecules">molecules</a>, such as water and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ions" title="Ions">ions</a> to pass through their <a href="/wiki/Intercellular_cleft" title="Intercellular cleft">intercellular clefts</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However lipid-soluble molecules can passively diffuse through the endothelial cell membranes along concentration gradients.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tight_junctions" title="Tight junctions">Tight junctions</a> can be further divided into two subtypes:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><ol><li>Those with numerous transport vesicles, which are found primarily in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skeletal_muscles" title="Skeletal muscles">skeletal muscles</a>, fingers, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gonads" title="Gonads">gonads</a>, and skin.</li>
<li>Those with few vesicles, which are primarily found in the <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a>. These capillaries are a constituent of the <a href="/wiki/Blood%E2%80%93brain_barrier" title="Blood–brain barrier">blood–brain barrier</a>.</li></ol></dd></dl>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fenestrated">Fenestrated</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capillary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fenestrated">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fenestra" title="Fenestra">Fenestrated</a> (derived from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fenestra_(histology)" title="Fenestra (histology)"><i>fenestra</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "window") capillaries have pores in the endothelial cells (60–80 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanometres" title="Nanometres">nm</a> in diameter) that are spanned by a diaphragm of radially oriented <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fibrils" title="Fibrils">fibrils</a> and allow small molecules and limited amounts of protein to diffuse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renal_glomerulus" title="Renal glomerulus">renal glomerulus</a> there are cells with no diaphragms, called <a href="/wiki/Podocyte" title="Podocyte">podocyte</a> foot processes or <a href="/wiki/Podocyte" title="Podocyte">pedicels</a>, which have slit pores with a function analogous to the diaphragm of the capillaries. Both of these types of blood vessels have continuous <a href="/wiki/Basal_lamina" title="Basal lamina">basal laminae</a> and are primarily located in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endocrine_glands" title="Endocrine glands">endocrine glands</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intestines" title="Intestines">intestines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pancreas" title="Pancreas">pancreas</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renal_glomerulus" title="Renal glomerulus">glomeruli</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidney</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sinusoid">Sinusoid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capillary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sinusoid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sinusoid_(blood_vessel)" title="Sinusoid (blood vessel)">Sinusoid (blood vessel)</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sinusoid_(blood_vessel)" title="Sinusoid (blood vessel)">Sinusoid capillaries</a> (also known as a <b>discontinuous</b>) are a special type of open-pore capillary, that have larger openings (30–40 µm in diameter)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> in the endothelium. These types of blood vessels allow <a href="/wiki/Red_blood_cell" title="Red blood cell">red</a> and <a href="/wiki/White_blood_cell" title="White blood cell">white blood cells</a> (7.5 µm – 25 µm diameter) and various <a href="/wiki/Blood_plasma" title="Blood plasma">serum</a> proteins to pass, aided by a discontinuous basal lamina. These capillaries lack <a href="/wiki/Pinocytosis" title="Pinocytosis">pinocytotic vesicles</a>, and therefore utilize gaps present in cell junctions to permit transfer between endothelial cells, and hence across the membrane. Sinusoid blood vessels are primarily located in the <a href="/wiki/Bone_marrow" title="Bone marrow">bone marrow</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lymph_nodes" title="Lymph nodes">lymph nodes</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and adrenal glands. Some sinusoids are distinctive in that they do not have the tight junctions between cells. They are called <i>discontinuous sinusoidal capillaries</i>, and are present in the <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spleen" title="Spleen">spleen</a>, where greater movement of cells and materials is necessary.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
A capillary wall is only 1 cell thick and is simple <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Squamous_epithelial_cell" title="Squamous epithelial cell">squamous epithelium</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capillary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2101_Blood_Flow_Through_the_Heart.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1317" data-file-width="1942" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/2101_Blood_Flow_Through_the_Heart.jpg/220px-2101_Blood_Flow_Through_the_Heart.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/2101_Blood_Flow_Through_the_Heart.jpg/330px-2101_Blood_Flow_Through_the_Heart.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/2101_Blood_Flow_Through_the_Heart.jpg/440px-2101_Blood_Flow_Through_the_Heart.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2101_Blood_Flow_Through_the_Heart.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Simplified image showing blood-flow through the body, passing through capillary networks in its path.</div></div></div>
<p>The capillary wall performs an important function by allowing nutrients and waste substances to pass across it. Molecules larger than 3 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanometres" title="Nanometres">nm</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Albumin" title="Albumin">albumin</a> and other large proteins pass through <a href="/wiki/Transcellular_transport" title="Transcellular transport">transcellular transport</a> carried inside <a href="/wiki/Vesicle_(biology_and_chemistry)" title="Vesicle (biology and chemistry)">vesicles</a>, a process which requires them to go through the cells that form the wall. Molecules smaller than 3 nm such as water, ions and gases cross the capillary wall through the space between cells in a process known as <a href="/wiki/Paracellular_transport" title="Paracellular transport">paracellular transport</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sukriti_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-sukriti-10">[10]</a></sup> These transport mechanisms allow bidirectional exchange of substances depending on <a href="/wiki/Osmosis" title="Osmosis">osmotic</a> gradients and can be further quantified by the <a href="/wiki/Starling_equation" title="Starling equation">Starling equation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nagy_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-nagy-11">[11]</a></sup> Capillaries that form part of the <a href="/wiki/Blood%E2%80%93brain_barrier" title="Blood–brain barrier">blood–brain barrier</a> however only allow for transcellular transport as <a href="/wiki/Tight_junction" title="Tight junction">tight junctions</a> between <a href="/wiki/Endothelium" title="Endothelium">endothelial</a> cells seal the paracellular space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bauer_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-bauer-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Capillary beds may control their blood flow via <a href="/wiki/Autoregulation" title="Autoregulation">autoregulation</a>. This allows an organ to maintain constant flow despite a change in central blood pressure. This is achieved by <a href="/wiki/Myogenic_response" title="Myogenic response">myogenic response</a>, and in the <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidney</a> by <a href="/wiki/Tubuloglomerular_feedback" title="Tubuloglomerular feedback">tubuloglomerular feedback</a>. When blood pressure increases, arterioles are stretched and subsequently constrict (a phenomenon known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bayliss_effect" title="Bayliss effect">Bayliss effect</a>) to counteract the increased tendency for high pressure to increase blood flow.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lungs" title="Lungs">lungs</a> special mechanisms have been adapted to meet the needs of increased necessity of blood flow during exercise. When the heart rate increases and more blood must flow through the lungs, capillaries are recruited and are also distended to make room for increased blood flow. This allows blood flow to increase while resistance decreases.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Capillary <a href="/wiki/Vascular_permeability" title="Vascular permeability">permeability</a> can be increased by the release of certain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cytokines" title="Cytokines">cytokines</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anaphylatoxins" title="Anaphylatoxins">anaphylatoxins</a>, or other mediators (such as leukotrienes, prostaglandins, histamine, bradykinin, etc.) highly influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune system</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

The solution to the equation is known as the net filtration or net fluid movement (<i>J</i><sub><i>v</i></sub>). If positive, fluid will tend to <i>leave</i> the capillary (filtration). This equation has a number of important physiologic implications, especially when pathologic processes grossly alter one or more of the variables.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <li>Filtration coefficient ( <i>K</i><sub><i>f</i></sub> )</li>
<li>Reflection coefficient ( σ )</li></ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_significance">Clinical significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capillary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Clinical significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Disorders of capillary formation as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congenital" title="Congenital">developmental defect</a> or acquired disorder are a feature in many common and serious disorders. Within a wide range of cellular factors and cytokines, issues with normal genetic expression and bioactivity of the vascular growth and permeability factor <a href="/wiki/Vascular_endothelial_growth_factor" title="Vascular endothelial growth factor">vascular endothelial growth factor</a> (VEGF) appear to play a major role in many of the disorders. Cellular factors include reduced number and function of bone-marrow derived <a href="/wiki/Endothelial_progenitor_cell" title="Endothelial progenitor cell">endothelial progenitor cells</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and reduced ability of those cells to form blood vessels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-circ.ahajournals.org_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-circ.ahajournals.org-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Formation of additional capillaries and larger blood vessels (<a href="/wiki/Angiogenesis" title="Angiogenesis">angiogenesis</a>) is a major mechanism by which a cancer may help to enhance its own growth. Disorders of retinal capillaries contribute to the pathogenesis of age-related <a href="/wiki/Macular_degeneration" title="Macular degeneration">macular degeneration</a>.</li>
<li>Reduced capillary density (capillary rarefaction)  occurs in association with cardiovascular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Risk_factors" title="Risk factors">risk factors</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and in patients with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronary_heart_disease" title="Coronary heart disease">coronary heart disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-circ.ahajournals.org_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-circ.ahajournals.org-14">[14]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Therapeutics">Therapeutics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capillary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Therapeutics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Major diseases where altering capillary formation could be helpful include conditions where there is excessive or abnormal capillary formation such as cancer and disorders harming eyesight; and medical conditions in which there is reduced capillary formation either for familial or genetic reasons, or as an acquired problem.
</p>
<ul><li>In patients with the retinal disorder, neovascular age-related <a href="/wiki/Macular_degeneration" title="Macular degeneration">macular degeneration</a>, local anti-VEGF treatment to limit the bio-activity of <a href="/wiki/Vascular_endothelial_growth_factor" title="Vascular endothelial growth factor">vascular endothelial growth factor</a> has been shown to protect vision by limiting progression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In a wide range of cancers, treatment approaches have been studied, or are in development,  aimed at decreasing tumour growth by reducing <a href="/wiki/Angiogenesis" title="Angiogenesis">angiogenesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blood_sampling">Blood sampling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capillary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Blood sampling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Capillary blood sampling can be used to test for, for example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_sugar" title="Blood sugar">blood glucose</a> (such as in <a href="/wiki/Blood_glucose_monitoring" title="Blood glucose monitoring">blood glucose monitoring</a>), <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a>, <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactate</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Capillary blood sampling is generally performed by creating a small cut using a <a href="/wiki/Blood_lancet" title="Blood lancet">blood lancet</a>, followed by <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(medicine)" title="Sampling (medicine)">sampling</a> by <a href="/wiki/Capillary_action" title="Capillary action">capillary action</a> on the cut with a <a href="/wiki/Glucose_meter" title="Glucose meter">test strip</a> or small pipe.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capillary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Contrary to a popular misconception, <a href="/wiki/William_Harvey" title="William Harvey">William Harvey</a> did not explicitly predict the existence of capillaries, but he clearly saw the need for some sort of connection between the arterial and venous systems. He wrote, "…the blood doth enter into every member through the arteries, and does return by the veins, and that the veins are the vessels and ways by which the blood is returned to the heart itself; and that the blood in the members and extremities does pass from the arteries into the veins (either mediately by an anastomosis, or immediately through the porosities of the flesh, or both ways) as before it did in the heart and thorax out of the veins, into the arteries…"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Marcello_Malpighi" title="Marcello Malpighi">Marcello Malpighi</a> was the first to observe directly and correctly describe capillaries, discovering them in a frog's lung in 1661.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
