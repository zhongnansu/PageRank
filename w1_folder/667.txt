<p><b>Methanotrophs</b> (sometimes called <b>methanophiles</b>) are <a href="/wiki/Prokaryote" title="Prokaryote">prokaryotes</a> that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metabolize" title="Metabolize">metabolize</a> <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> as their only source of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a>. They can grow <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration#Aerobic_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">aerobically</a> or <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_respiration" title="Anaerobic respiration">anaerobically</a> and require single-carbon <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">compounds</a> to survive.
These methanotrophs are currently being modified to absorb methane from the atmosphere, because methane released into the atmosphere contributes to greenhouse gasses, which heat up the earth and cause global warming.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General">General</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanotroph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: General">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Under aerobic conditions, they combine <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> and methane to form <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">formaldehyde</a>, which is then incorporated into organic compounds via the serine pathway or the ribulose monophosphate (RuMP) pathway. Type I methanotrophs are part of the <a href="/wiki/Gammaproteobacteria" title="Gammaproteobacteria">Gammaproteobacteria</a> and they use the RuMP pathway to assimilate carbon. Type II methanotrophs are part of the <a href="/wiki/Alphaproteobacteria" title="Alphaproteobacteria">Alphaproteobacteria</a> and utilize the serine pathway of carbon assimilation. They also characteristically have a system of internal membranes within which methane <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">oxidation</a> occurs. Methanotrophs occur mostly in soils, and are especially common near environments where methane is produced. Their habitats include oceans, mud, marshes, underground environments, soils, rice paddies, and landfills. They are of special interest to researchers studying <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, as they are significant in the global methane budget.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid10427012_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid10427012-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Methylococcus_capsulatus" title="Methylococcus capsulatus">Methylococcus capsulatus</a> is utilised to produce animal feed from natural gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>In contrast with aerobic methanotrophs, the anaerobic oxidation of methane (AOM) occurs in anoxic marine habitats and freshwater <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sediments" title="Sediments">sediments</a>. The anaerobic methanotrophic archaea (ANME) is considered as a reverse methanogenesis pathway coupled to the sufate reduction, denitrification. Metals, as manganese and iron, can also be used as the terminal electron acceptors of AOM. <i>Candidatus</i> Methylomirabilis oxyfera belongs to the phylum NC10 bacteria, can catalyze nitrite reduction through an “intra-aerobic” pathway, and may catalyze AOM through an aerobic methane oxidation pathway.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanotroph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Differences in the method of formaldehyde fixation and membrane structure divide the methanotrophs into several groups. These include the <i><a href="/wiki/Methylococcaceae" title="Methylococcaceae">Methylococcaceae</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Methylocystaceae" title="Methylocystaceae">Methylocystaceae</a></i>. Although both are included among the <i><a href="/wiki/Proteobacteria" title="Proteobacteria">Proteobacteria</a></i>, they are members of different subclasses. Other methanotroph species are found in the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Verrucomicrobiae&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Verrucomicrobiae (page does not exist)">Verrucomicrobiae</a></i>.
</p><p>Methanotrophy is a special case of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methylotrophy" title="Methylotrophy">methylotrophy</a>, using single-carbon compounds that are more reduced than carbon dioxide. Some methylotrophs, however, can also make use of multi-carbon compounds which differentiates them from methanotrophs that are usually <a href="/wiki/Growth_medium" title="Growth medium">fastidious</a> methane and methanol oxidizers. The only facultative methanotrophs isolated to date are members of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Methylocella" title="Methylocella">Methylocella</a></i> and Methylocystis.
</p><p>In functional terms, methanotrophs are referred to as methane-oxidizing bacteria, however, methane-oxidizing bacteria encompass other organisms that are not regarded as sole methanotrophs. For this reason methane-oxidizing bacteria have been separated into four subgroups: two methane-assimilating bacteria (MAB) groups, the methanotrophs, and two autotrophic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ammonia-oxidizing_bacteria" title="Ammonia-oxidizing bacteria">ammonia-oxidizing bacteria</a> (AAOB).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid10427012_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid10427012-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanotroph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303569">@media all and (max-width:720px){.mw-parser-output .tmulti>.thumbinner{width:100%!important;max-width:none!important}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .tsingle{float:none!important;max-width:none!important;width:100%!important;text-align:center}}</style><div class="thumb tmulti tnone center"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:792px;max-width:792px"><div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:431px;max-width:431px"><div class="thumbimage" style="height:345px;overflow:hidden"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RuMP_pathway.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="412" data-file-width="512" height="345" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/RuMP_pathway.svg/429px-RuMP_pathway.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/RuMP_pathway.svg/644px-RuMP_pathway.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/RuMP_pathway.svg/858px-RuMP_pathway.svg.png 2x" width="429"/></a></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">RuMP pathway in type I methanotrophs</div></div><div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:357px;max-width:357px"><div class="thumbimage" style="height:345px;overflow:hidden"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Serine_pathway.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="499" data-file-width="512" height="346" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Serine_pathway.svg/355px-Serine_pathway.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Serine_pathway.svg/533px-Serine_pathway.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Serine_pathway.svg/710px-Serine_pathway.svg.png 2x" width="355"/></a></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">Serine pathway in type II methanotrophs</div></div><div style="clear:left"></div></div></div>
<p>Gratuitous detoxification of some environmental contaminants such as <a href="/wiki/Organochloride" title="Organochloride">chlorinated hydrocarbons</a> by methanotrophs have made them attractive models for such <a href="/wiki/Bioremediation" title="Bioremediation">bioremediation</a> processes. Equally methane is a potential <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> far more potent than <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. Therefore, methanotrophs play a major role in the reduction of the release of methane into the atmosphere from environments such as rice paddies, landfills, bogs, and swamps where methane production is relatively high.
</p><p>Methanotrophs oxidize methane by first initiating reduction of an oxygen atom to H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2 </sub>and transformation of methane to CH<sub>3</sub>OH using methane monooxygenases (MMOs).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid8801441_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid8801441-4">[4]</a></sup> Furthermore, two types of MMO have been isolated from methanotrophs: soluble methane monooxygenase (sMMO) and particulate methane monooxygenase (pMMO). Cells containing pMMO have demonstrated higher growth capabilities and higher affinity for methane than sMMO containing cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid8801441_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid8801441-4">[4]</a></sup> It is suspected that copper ions may play a key role in both pMMO regulation and the enzyme catalysis, thus limiting pMMO cells to more copper-rich environments than sMMO producing cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Investigations in marine environments revealed that methane can be oxidized anaerobically by consortia of methane oxidizing <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulfate-reducing_bacteria" title="Sulfate-reducing bacteria">sulfate-reducing bacteria</a>. <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_oxidation_of_methane" title="Anaerobic oxidation of methane">Anaerobic oxidation of methane</a> (AOM) mainly occurs in anoxic marine sediments. The exact mechanism of methane oxidation under anaerobic conditions is still a topic of debate but the most widely accepted theory is that the archaea use the reversed <a href="/wiki/Methanogenesis" title="Methanogenesis">methanogenesis</a> pathway to produce carbon dioxide and another, unknown substance. This unknown intermediate is then used by the sulfate-reducing bacteria to gain energy from the reduction of sulfate to <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>. The anaerobic methanotrophs are not related to the known aerobic methanotrophs; the closest cultured relative to the anaerobic methanotrophs are the <a href="/wiki/Methanogen" title="Methanogen">methanogens</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">order</a> <a href="/wiki/Methanosarcinales" title="Methanosarcinales">Methanosarcinales</a>.
</p><p>Recently, a new bacterium <i>Candidatus Methylomirabilis oxyfera</i> was identified that can couple the <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_oxidation_of_methane" title="Anaerobic oxidation of methane">anaerobic oxidation of methane</a> to nitrite reduction without the need for a <a href="/wiki/Syntrophy" title="Syntrophy">syntrophic</a> partner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ettwig2010_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ettwig2010-6">[6]</a></sup> Based on the studies of Ettwig et al.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ettwig2010_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ettwig2010-6">[6]</a></sup> it is believed that <i>M. oxyfera</i> oxidizes methane anaerobically by utilizing the oxygen produced internally from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dismutation" title="Dismutation">dismutation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a> into nitrogen and oxygen gas.
</p>
