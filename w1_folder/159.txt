<p><b>Decomposition</b> is the process by which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_substance" title="Organic substance">organic substances</a> are broken down into simpler organic matter. The process is a part of the <a href="/wiki/Nutrient_cycle" title="Nutrient cycle">nutrient cycle</a> and is essential for recycling the finite matter that occupies physical space in the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>. Bodies of living organisms begin to decompose shortly after <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">death</a>. Animals, such as worms, also help decompose the organic materials. Organisms that do this are known as <a href="/wiki/Decomposer" title="Decomposer">decomposers</a>. Although no two organisms decompose in the same way, they all undergo the same sequential stages of decomposition. The science which studies decomposition is generally referred to as  <i><a href="/wiki/Taphonomy" title="Taphonomy">taphonomy</a></i> from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> word <i>taphos</i>, meaning tomb.
</p><p>One can differentiate <b>abiotic</b> from <b>biotic decomposition</b> (<a href="/wiki/Biodegradation" title="Biodegradation">biodegradation</a>). The former means "degradation of a substance by <a href="/wiki/Chemical_decomposition" title="Chemical decomposition">chemical</a> or physical processes, e.g., <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The latter means "the metabolic breakdown of materials into simpler components by living organisms",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> typically by microorganisms.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Animal_decomposition">Animal decomposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decomposition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Animal decomposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ants_cleaning_dead_snake.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Ants_cleaning_dead_snake.jpg/220px-Ants_cleaning_dead_snake.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Ants_cleaning_dead_snake.jpg/330px-Ants_cleaning_dead_snake.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Ants_cleaning_dead_snake.jpg/440px-Ants_cleaning_dead_snake.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ants_cleaning_dead_snake.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">Ants</a> eating a dead <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snake</a></div></div></div>
<p>Decomposition begins at the moment of death, caused by two factors: 1.) <a href="/wiki/Autolysis_(biology)" title="Autolysis (biology)">autolysis</a>, the breaking down of <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">tissues</a> by the body's own internal chemicals and <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a>, and 2.) <a href="/wiki/Putrefaction" title="Putrefaction">putrefaction</a>, the breakdown of tissues by <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>. These processes release compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Cadaverine" title="Cadaverine">cadaverine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Putrescine" title="Putrescine">putrescine</a>, that are the chief source of the unmistakably putrid <a href="/wiki/Odor" title="Odor">odor</a> of decaying animal tissue.
</p><p>Prime decomposers are <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a>, though larger <a href="/wiki/Scavenger" title="Scavenger">scavengers</a> also play an important role in decomposition if the body is accessible to <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acari" title="Acari">mites</a> and other animals. The most important arthropods that are involved in the process include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carrion_beetle" title="Carrion beetle">carrion beetles</a>, mites,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flesh-fly" title="Flesh-fly">flesh-flies</a> (Sarcophagidae) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blow-fly" title="Blow-fly">blow-flies</a> (<a href="/wiki/Calliphoridae" title="Calliphoridae">Calliphoridae</a>), such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green-bottle_fly" title="Green-bottle fly">green-bottle fly</a> seen in the summer. In North America, the most important non-insect animals that are typically involved in the process include mammal and bird scavengers, such as <a href="/wiki/Coyote" title="Coyote">coyotes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">dogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wolf" title="Wolf">wolves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fox" title="Fox">foxes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rat" title="Rat">rats</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crows" title="Crows">crows</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vulture" title="Vulture">vultures</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Some of these scavengers also remove and scatter bones, which they ingest at a later time. Aquatic and marine environments have break-down agents that include bacteria, fish, crustaceans, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diptera" title="Diptera">fly</a> larvae <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and other carrion scavengers.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stages_of_decomposition">Stages of decomposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decomposition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Stages of decomposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Five general stages are used to describe the process of decomposition in vertebrate animals: fresh, bloat, active decay, advanced decay, and dry/remains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Payne_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Payne-6">[6]</a></sup> The general stages of decomposition are coupled with two stages of chemical decomposition: <a href="/wiki/Autolysis_(biology)" title="Autolysis (biology)">autolysis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Putrefaction" title="Putrefaction">putrefaction</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forbes_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes-7">[7]</a></sup> These two stages contribute to the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_process_of_decomposition" title="Chemical process of decomposition">chemical process of decomposition</a>, which breaks down the main components of the body. With death the microbiome of the living organism collapses and is followed by the <a href="/wiki/Necrobiome" title="Necrobiome">necrobiome</a> that undergoes predictable changes over time.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fresh">Fresh</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decomposition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fresh">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Among those animals that have a heart, the "fresh" stage begins immediately after the heart stops beating. From the moment of death, the body begins cooling or warming to match the temperature of the ambient environment, during a stage called <a href="/wiki/Algor_mortis" title="Algor mortis">algor mortis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janaway_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Janaway-8">[8]</a></sup>  Shortly after death, within three to six hours, the muscular tissues become rigid and incapable of relaxing, during a stage called <a href="/wiki/Rigor_mortis" title="Rigor mortis">rigor mortis</a>. Since blood is no longer being pumped through the body, <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a> causes it to drain to the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dependent_portions_of_the_body&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dependent portions of the body (page does not exist)">dependent portions of the body</a>, creating an overall bluish-purple discolouration termed <a href="/wiki/Livor_mortis" title="Livor mortis">livor mortis</a> or, more commonly, lividity.
</p><p>Once the heart stops, the <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> can no longer supply <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> or remove <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> from the tissues. The resulting decrease in pH and other chemical changes causes cells to lose their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Structural_integrity" title="Structural integrity">structural integrity</a>, bringing about the release of cellular enzymes capable of initiating the breakdown of surrounding cells and tissues. This process is known as <a href="/wiki/Autolysis_(biology)" title="Autolysis (biology)">autolysis</a>.
</p><p>Visible changes caused by decomposition are limited during the fresh stage, although autolysis may cause blisters to appear at the surface of the skin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The small amount of oxygen remaining in the body is quickly depleted by cellular <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aerobic_organism" title="Aerobic organism">aerobic microbes</a> naturally present in <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_tract" title="Respiratory tract">respiratory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract" title="Gastrointestinal tract">gastrointestinal</a> tracts, creating an ideal environment for the proliferation of <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_organism" title="Anaerobic organism">anaerobic organisms</a>. These multiply, consuming the body's <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipids</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>, to produce a variety of substances including <a href="/wiki/Propionic_acid" title="Propionic acid">propionic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>. The process of microbial proliferation within a body is referred to as <a href="/wiki/Putrefaction" title="Putrefaction">putrefaction</a> and leads to the second stage of decomposition, known as bloat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carterjournal_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carterjournal-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Calliphoridae" title="Calliphoridae">Blowflies</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flesh-fly" title="Flesh-fly">flesh flies</a> are the first carrion insects to arrive, and they seek a suitable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oviposition" title="Oviposition">oviposition</a> site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Payne_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Payne-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bloat"><span id="Bloat"></span><span id="bloat"></span><span id="Bloat_stage"></span><span id="Bloated"></span><span id="bloated"></span><span id="bloating"></span><span id="Bloating"></span> Bloat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decomposition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Bloat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The bloat stage provides the first clear visual sign that microbial proliferation is underway. In this stage, anaerobic metabolism takes place, leading to the accumulation of gases, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulphide" title="Hydrogen sulphide">hydrogen sulfide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>. The accumulation of gases within the bodily cavity causes the distention of the abdomen and gives a cadaver its overall bloated appearance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carterbook_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carterbook-11">[11]</a></sup>  The gases produced also cause natural liquids and liquefying tissues to become frothy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janaway_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Janaway-8">[8]</a></sup>  As the pressure of the gases within the body increases, fluids are forced to escape from natural orifices, such as the nose, mouth, and anus, and enter the surrounding environment.  The buildup of pressure combined with the loss of integrity of the skin may also cause the body to rupture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carterbook_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Carterbook-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Intestinal anaerobic bacteria transform <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haemoglobin" title="Haemoglobin">haemoglobin</a> into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulfhemoglobin" title="Sulfhemoglobin">sulfhemoglobin</a> and other colored pigments.  The associated gases which accumulate within the body at this time aid in the transport of sulfhemoglobin throughout the body via the <a href="/wiki/Circulatory_system" title="Circulatory system">circulatory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lymphatic_system" title="Lymphatic system">lymphatic systems</a>, giving the body an overall marbled appearance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pinheiro_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pinheiro-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>If insects have access, <a href="/wiki/Maggot" title="Maggot">maggots</a> hatch and begin to feed on the body's tissues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Payne_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Payne-6">[6]</a></sup>  Maggot activity, typically confined to natural orifices, and masses under the skin, causes the skin to slip, and hair to detach from the skin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janaway_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Janaway-8">[8]</a></sup>  Maggot feeding, and the accumulation of gases within the body, eventually leads to post-mortem skin ruptures which will then further allow purging of gases and fluids into the surrounding environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carterjournal_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Carterjournal-10">[10]</a></sup>  Ruptures in the skin allow oxygen to re-enter the body and provide more surface area for the development of fly larvae and the activity of aerobic microorganisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carterbook_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Carterbook-11">[11]</a></sup>  The purging of gases and fluids results in the strong distinctive odors associated with decay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Payne_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Payne-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Active_decay">Active decay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decomposition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Active decay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Active decay is characterized by the period of greatest mass loss. This loss occurs as a result of both the voracious feeding of maggots and the purging of decomposition fluids into the surrounding environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carterbook_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Carterbook-11">[11]</a></sup>  The purged fluids accumulate around the body and create a cadaver decomposition island (CDI).  Liquefaction of tissues and disintegration become apparent during this time and strong odors persist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Payne_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Payne-6">[6]</a></sup>  The end of active decay is signaled by the migration of maggots away from the body to pupate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carterjournal_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Carterjournal-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Advanced_decay">Advanced decay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decomposition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Advanced decay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Decomposition is largely inhibited during advanced decay due to the loss of readily available cadaveric material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carterbook_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-Carterbook-11">[11]</a></sup>  Insect activity is also reduced during this stage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janaway_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Janaway-8">[8]</a></sup>  When the carcass is located on soil, the area surrounding it will show evidence of <a href="/wiki/Vegetation" title="Vegetation">vegetation</a> death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carterbook_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-Carterbook-11">[11]</a></sup>  The CDI surrounding the carcass will display an increase in soil carbon and nutrients, such as <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carterjournal_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Carterjournal-10">[10]</a></sup> changes in pH; and a significant increase in soil <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vass_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vass-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Dry.2Fremains"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Dry/remains">Dry/remains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decomposition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Dry/remains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>During the dry/remains stage, the resurgence of plant growth around the CDI may occur and is a sign that the nutrients present in the surrounding soil have not yet returned to their normal levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carterbook_11-6"><a href="#cite_note-Carterbook-11">[11]</a></sup>  All that remains of the cadaver at this stage is dry skin, <a href="/wiki/Cartilage" title="Cartilage">cartilage</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bones</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Payne_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-Payne-6">[6]</a></sup> which will become dry and bleached if exposed to the elements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janaway_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-Janaway-8">[8]</a></sup>  If all soft tissue is removed from the cadaver, it is referred to as completely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skeletonization_(forensics)" title="Skeletonization (forensics)">skeletonized</a>, but if only portions of the bones are exposed, it is referred to as partially skeletonised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dent_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dent-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Factors_affecting_decomposition_of_bodies">Factors affecting decomposition of bodies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decomposition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Factors affecting decomposition of bodies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_effects_on_forensic_entomology" title="Environmental effects on forensic entomology">Environmental effects on forensic entomology</a></div>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exposure_to_the_elements">Exposure to the elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decomposition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Exposure to the elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A dead body that has been exposed to the open elements, such as water and air, will decompose more quickly and attract much more <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insect</a> activity than a body that is buried or confined in special protective gear or artifacts. This is due, in part, to the limited number of insects that can penetrate a coffin and the lower temperatures under soil.
</p><p>The rate and manner of decomposition in an animal body is strongly affected by several factors. In roughly descending degrees of importance, they are:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">Temperature</a>;</li>
<li>The availability of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>;</li>
<li>Prior <a href="/wiki/Embalming" title="Embalming">embalming</a>;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/List_of_causes_of_death_by_rate" title="List of causes of death by rate">Cause of death</a>;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Burial" title="Burial">Burial</a>, depth of burial, and soil type;</li>
<li>Access by <a href="/wiki/Scavenger" title="Scavenger">scavengers</a>;</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_trauma" title="Physical trauma">Trauma</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Wound" title="Wound">wounds</a> and crushing blows;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">Humidity</a>, or wetness;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">Rainfall</a>;</li>
<li>Body size and weight;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Clothing" title="Clothing">Clothing</a>;</li>
<li>The surface on which the body rests;</li>
<li>Foods/objects inside the specimen's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digestive_tract" title="Digestive tract">digestive tract</a> (bacon compared to lettuce).</li></ul>
<p>The speed at which decomposition occurs varies greatly. Factors such as temperature, humidity, and the season of death all determine how fast a fresh body will skeletonize or mummify. A basic guide for the effect of environment on decomposition is given as Casper's Law (or Ratio): if all other factors are equal, then, when there is free access of air a body decomposes twice as fast than if immersed in water and eight times faster than if buried in earth. Ultimately, the rate of bacterial decomposition acting on the tissue will depend upon the temperature of the surroundings. Colder temperatures decrease the rate of decomposition while warmer temperatures increase it. A dry body will not decompose efficiently. Moisture helps the growth of microorganisms that decompose the organic matter, but too much moisture could lead to anaerobic conditions slowing down the decomposition process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>The most important variable is a body's accessibility to insects, particularly flies. On the surface in tropical areas, invertebrates alone can easily reduce a fully fleshed corpse to clean bones in under two weeks. The skeleton itself is not permanent; <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a> in <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soils</a> can reduce it to unrecognizable components. This is one reason given for the lack of human remains found in the wreckage of the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Titanic" title="RMS Titanic"><i>Titanic</i></a>, even in parts of the ship considered inaccessible to scavengers. Freshly skeletonized bone is often called "green" bone and has a characteristic greasy feel. Under certain conditions (normally cool, damp soil), bodies may undergo <a href="/wiki/Saponification" title="Saponification">saponification</a> and develop a waxy substance called <a href="/wiki/Adipocere" title="Adipocere">adipocere</a>, caused by the action of soil chemicals on the body's <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fats</a>. The formation of adipocere slows decomposition by inhibiting the bacteria that cause putrefaction.
</p><p>In extremely dry or cold conditions, the normal process of decomposition is halted – by either lack of moisture or temperature controls on bacterial and enzymatic action – causing the body to be preserved as a <a href="/wiki/Mummy" title="Mummy">mummy</a>. Frozen mummies commonly restart the decomposition process when thawed (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%96tzi_the_Iceman" title="Ötzi the Iceman">Ötzi the Iceman</a>), whilst heat-desiccated mummies remain so unless exposed to moisture.
</p><p>The bodies of newborns who never ingested food are an important exception to the normal process of decomposition. They lack the internal microbial flora that produce much of decomposition and quite commonly mummify if kept in even moderately dry conditions.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anaerobic_vs_Aerobic">Anaerobic vs Aerobic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decomposition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Anaerobic vs Aerobic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Aerobic decomposition takes place in the presence of oxygen. This is most common to occur in nature. Living organisms that use oxygen to survive feed on the body. Anaerobic decomposition takes place in the absence of oxygen. This could be place where the body is buried in organic material and oxygen can not reach it. This process of putrefaction has a bad odor accompanied by it due to the hydrogen sulfide and organic matter containing sulfur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Artificial_preservation">Artificial preservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decomposition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Artificial preservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Embalming" title="Embalming">Embalming</a> is the practice of delaying decomposition of human and animal remains. Embalming slows decomposition somewhat, but does not forestall it indefinitely. Embalmers typically pay great attention to parts of the body seen by mourners, such as the face and hands. The chemicals used in embalming repel most insects, and slow down bacterial putrefaction by either killing existing bacteria in or on the body themselves or by "fixing" cellular proteins, which means that they cannot act as a nutrient source for subsequent bacterial infections. In sufficiently dry environments, an embalmed body may end up <a href="/wiki/Mummy" title="Mummy">mummified</a> and it is not uncommon for bodies to remain preserved to a viewable extent after decades. Notable viewable embalmed bodies include those of:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Eva_Per%C3%B3n" title="Eva Perón">Eva Perón</a> of <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, whose body was injected with <a href="/wiki/Paraffin_wax" title="Paraffin wax">paraffin</a> was kept perfectly preserved for many years, and still is as far as is known (her body is no longer on public display).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">Vladimir Lenin</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, whose body was kept submerged in a special tank of fluid for decades and is on public display in <a href="/wiki/Lenin%27s_Mausoleum" title="Lenin's Mausoleum">Lenin's Mausoleum</a>.
<ul><li>Other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist" title="Communist">Communist</a> leaders with pronounced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cults_of_personality" title="Cults of personality">cults of personality</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">Mao Zedong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kim_Il-sung" title="Kim Il-sung">Kim Il-sung</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh" title="Ho Chi Minh">Ho Chi Minh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kim_Jong-il" title="Kim Jong-il">Kim Jong-il</a> and most recently <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Ch%C3%A1vez" title="Hugo Chávez">Hugo Chávez</a> have also had their cadavers preserved in the fashion of Lenin's preservation and are now displayed in their respective mausoleums.</li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pope_John_XXIII" title="Pope John XXIII">Pope John XXIII</a>, whose preserved body can be viewed in <a href="/wiki/St._Peter%27s_Basilica" title="St. Peter's Basilica">St. Peter's Basilica</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Padre_Pio" title="Padre Pio">Padre Pio</a>, whose body was injected with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formalin" title="Formalin">formalin</a> prior to burial in a dry vault<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> from which he was later removed and placed on public display at the <a href="/wiki/San_Giovanni_Rotondo" title="San Giovanni Rotondo">San Giovanni Rotondo</a>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_preservation">Environmental preservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decomposition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Environmental preservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A body buried in a sufficiently dry environment may be well preserved for decades. This was observed in the case for murdered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_rights" title="Civil rights">civil rights</a> activist <a href="/wiki/Medgar_Evers" title="Medgar Evers">Medgar Evers</a>, who was found to be almost perfectly preserved over 30 years after his death, permitting an accurate <a href="/wiki/Autopsy" title="Autopsy">autopsy</a> when the case of his murder was re-opened in the 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Bodies submerged in a <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a> <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bog</a> may become naturally "embalmed", arresting decomposition and resulting in a preserved specimen known as a <a href="/wiki/Bog_body" title="Bog body">bog body</a>. The time for an embalmed body to be reduced to a <a href="/wiki/Skeleton" title="Skeleton">skeleton</a> varies greatly. Even when a body is decomposed, embalming treatment can still be achieved (the arterial system decays more slowly) but would not restore a natural appearance without extensive reconstruction and cosmetic work, and is largely used to control the foul odors due to decomposition.
</p><p>An animal can be preserved almost perfectly, for millions of years in a resin such as amber.
</p><p>There are some examples where bodies have been inexplicably preserved (with no human intervention) for decades or centuries and appear almost the same as when they died. In some religious groups, this is known as <a href="/wiki/Incorruptibility" title="Incorruptibility">incorruptibility</a>. It is not known whether or for how long a body can stay free of decay without artificial preservation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Importance_to_forensic_sciences">Importance to forensic sciences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decomposition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Importance to forensic sciences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Forensic_entomological_decomposition" title="Forensic entomological decomposition">Forensic entomological decomposition</a></div>
<p>Various sciences study the decomposition of bodies under the general rubric of <a href="/wiki/Forensic_science" title="Forensic science">forensic science</a> because the usual motive for such studies is to determine the time and cause of death for <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">legal</a> purposes:
</p>
<ul><li>Forensic taphonomy specifically studies the processes of decomposition in order to apply the biological and chemical principles to forensic cases in order to determine <a href="/wiki/Post-mortem_interval" title="Post-mortem interval">post-mortem interval</a> (PMI), post-burial interval as well as to locate clandestine graves.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Forensic_pathology" title="Forensic pathology">Forensic pathology</a> studies the clues to the cause of death found in the corpse as a <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medical</a> phenomenon.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Forensic_entomology" title="Forensic entomology">Forensic entomology</a> studies the insects and other <a href="/wiki/Vermin" title="Vermin">vermin</a> found in corpses; the sequence in which they appear, the kinds of insects, and where they are found in their life cycle are clues that can shed light on the time of death, the length of a corpse's exposure, and whether the corpse was moved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Forensic_anthropology" title="Forensic anthropology">Forensic anthropology</a> is the medico-legal branch of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_anthropology" title="Physical anthropology">physical anthropology</a> that studies skeletons and human remains, usually to seek clues as to the identity, age, sex, height and ethnicity of their former owner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Tennessee_Anthropological_Research_Facility" title="University of Tennessee Anthropological Research Facility">University of Tennessee Anthropological Research Facility</a> (better known as the Body Farm) in <a href="/wiki/Knoxville,_Tennessee" title="Knoxville, Tennessee">Knoxville, Tennessee</a> has a number of bodies laid out in various situations in a fenced-in plot near the medical center. Scientists at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body_Farm" title="Body Farm">Body Farm</a> study how the human body decays in various circumstances to gain a better understanding of decomposition.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plant_decomposition">Plant decomposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decomposition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Plant decomposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Composting" title="Composting">Composting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_digestion" title="Anaerobic digestion">Anaerobic digestion</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fungal_extracellular_enzyme_activity" title="Fungal extracellular enzyme activity">Fungal extracellular enzyme activity</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DecayingPeachSmall.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="190" data-file-width="200" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/27/DecayingPeachSmall.gif" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption">A decaying peach over a period of six days. Each frame is approximately 12 hours apart, as the fruit shrivels and becomes covered with <a href="/wiki/Mold" title="Mold">mold</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Decomposition of plant matter occurs in many stages. It begins with leaching by water; the most easily lost and soluble carbon compounds are liberated in this process. Another early process is physical breakup or fragmentation of the plant material into smaller bits which have greater surface area for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microbial" title="Microbial">microbial</a> colonization and attack. In smaller dead plants, this process is largely carried out by the soil invertebrate fauna, whereas in the larger plants, primarily parasitic life-forms such as insects and fungi play a major breakdown role and are not assisted by numerous <a href="/wiki/Detritivore" title="Detritivore">detritivore</a> species.
</p><p>
Following this, the plant <a href="/wiki/Detritus" title="Detritus">detritus</a> (consisting of <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hemicellulose" title="Hemicellulose">hemicellulose</a>, microbial products, and <a href="/wiki/Lignin" title="Lignin">lignin</a>) undergoes</p><div class="floatleft"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Decomposition_of_leafs_.jpg" title="leafs decomposing after time goes on"><img alt="leafs decomposing after time goes on" data-file-height="1334" data-file-width="750" height="391" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Decomposition_of_leafs_.jpg/220px-Decomposition_of_leafs_.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Decomposition_of_leafs_.jpg/330px-Decomposition_of_leafs_.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Decomposition_of_leafs_.jpg/440px-Decomposition_of_leafs_.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a></div><p> chemical alteration by microbes. Different types of compounds decompose at different rates. This is dependent on their <a href="/wiki/Chemical_structure" title="Chemical structure">chemical structure</a>.
</p><p>For instance, <a href="/wiki/Lignin" title="Lignin">lignin</a> is a component of wood, which is relatively resistant to decomposition and can in fact only be decomposed by certain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a>, such as the black-rot fungi. Wood decomposition is a complex process involving fungi which transport nutrients to the nutritionally scarce wood from outside environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Because of this nutritional enrichment the fauna of <a href="/wiki/Xylophagy" title="Xylophagy">saproxylic</a> insects may develop<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">[23]</a></sup> and in turn affect dead wood, contributing to wood decomposition and nutrient cycling in the forest floor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">[23]</a></sup> Lignin is one such remaining product of decomposing plants with a very complex chemical structure causing the rate of microbial breakdown to slow. Warmth increases the speed of plant decay, by the same amount regardless of the composition of the plant<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mit_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-mit-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>In most <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grassland</a> ecosystems, natural damage from <a href="/wiki/Fire" title="Fire">fire</a>, insects that feed on decaying matter, <a href="/wiki/Termite" title="Termite">termites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grazing" title="Grazing">grazing</a> mammals, and the physical movement of animals through the grass are the primary agents of breakdown and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nutrient_cycling" title="Nutrient cycling">nutrient cycling</a>, while bacteria and fungi play the main roles in further decomposition.
</p><p>The chemical aspects of plant decomposition always involve the release of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. In fact, decomposition contributes over 90 percent of carbon dioxide released each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mit_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-mit-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_decomposition">Food decomposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decomposition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Food decomposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Meat_spoilage" title="Meat spoilage">Meat spoilage</a></div>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg"><img alt="[icon]" data-file-height="31" data-file-width="44" height="14" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" width="20"/></a></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs expansion</b> with: information from the main article. <small>You can help by <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Decomposition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=">adding to it</a>.</small> <small><i>(July 2010)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The decomposition of food, either plant or animal, called <i>spoilage</i> in this context, is an important field of study within <a href="/wiki/Food_science" title="Food science">food science</a>. Food decomposition can be slowed down by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_conservation" title="Food conservation">conservation</a>. The spoilage of meat occurs, if the meat is untreated, in a matter of hours or days and results in the meat becoming unappetizing, poisonous or infectious. Spoilage is caused by the practically unavoidable infection and subsequent decomposition of meat by bacteria and fungi, which are borne by the animal itself, by the people handling the meat, and by their implements. Meat can be kept edible for a much longer time – though not indefinitely – if proper hygiene is observed during production and processing, and if appropriate food safety, food preservation and food storage procedures are applied.
</p><p>Spoilage of food is attributed to contamination from microorganisms such as bacteria, molds, and yeasts, along with natural decay of the food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-25">[25]</a></sup> These decomposition bacteria reproduce at rapid rates under conditions of moisture and preferred temperatures. When the proper conditions are lacking the bacteria may form spores which lurk until suitable conditions arise to continue reproduction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rate_of_decomposition">Rate of decomposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decomposition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Rate of decomposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The rate of decomposition is governed by three sets of factors—the physical environment (temperature, moisture and soil properties), the quantity and quality of the dead material available to decomposers, and the nature of the microbial community itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin159_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin159-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Decomposition rates are low under very wet or very dry conditions. Decomposition rates are highest in wet, moist conditions with adequate levels of oxygen. Wet soils tend to become deficient in oxygen (this is especially true in <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a>), which slows microbial growth. In dry soils, decomposition slows as well, but bacteria continue to grow (albeit at a slower rate) even after soils become too dry to support plant growth.  When the rains return and soils become wet, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Osmotic_gradient" title="Osmotic gradient">osmotic gradient</a> between the bacterial cells and the soil water causes the cells to gain water quickly. Under these conditions, many bacterial cells burst, releasing a pulse of nutrients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin159_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin159-26">[26]</a></sup> Decomposition rates also tend to be slower in acidic soils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin159_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin159-26">[26]</a></sup> Soils which are rich in <a href="/wiki/Clay_minerals" title="Clay minerals">clay minerals</a> tend to have lower decomposition rates, and thus, higher levels of organic matter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin159_26-3"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin159-26">[26]</a></sup> The smaller particles of clay result in a larger surface area that can hold water. The higher the water content of a soil, the lower the oxygen content<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin61_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin61-27">[27]</a></sup> and consequently, the lower the rate of decomposition. Clay minerals also bind particles of organic material to their surface, making them less accessible to microbes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin159_26-4"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin159-26">[26]</a></sup> Soil disturbance like <a href="/wiki/Tillage" title="Tillage">tilling</a> increases decomposition by increasing the amount of oxygen in the soil and by exposing new organic matter to soil microbes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin159_26-5"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin159-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>
The quality and quantity of the material available to decomposers is another major factor that influences the rate of decomposition. Substances like sugars and amino acids decompose readily and are considered labile. <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">Cellulose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hemicellulose" title="Hemicellulose">hemicellulose</a>, which are broken down more slowly, are "moderately labile". Compounds which are more resistant to decay, like lignin or <a href="/wiki/Cutin" title="Cutin">cutin</a>, are considered recalcitrant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin159_26-6"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin159-26">[26]</a></sup> Litter with a higher proportion of labile compounds decomposes much more rapidly than does litter with a higher proportion of recalcitrant material. Consequently, dead animals decompose more rapidly than dead leaves, which themselves decompose more rapidly than fallen branches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin159_26-7"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin159-26">[26]</a></sup> As organic material in the soil ages, its quality decreases. The more labile compounds decompose quickly, leaving an increasing proportion of recalcitrant material. Microbial cell walls also contain recalcitrant materials like <a href="/wiki/Chitin" title="Chitin">chitin</a>, and these also accumulate as the microbes die, further reducing the quality of older <a href="/wiki/Soil_organic_matter" title="Soil organic matter">soil organic matter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin159_26-8"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin159-26">[26]</a></sup></p><div></div>
