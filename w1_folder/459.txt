<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Hyperventilation</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Hyperventilation</b> (a.k.a. <b>overbreathing</b>) occurs when the rate or tidal volume of <a href="/wiki/Breathing" title="Breathing">breathing</a> eliminates more carbon dioxide than the body can produce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guyton_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guyton-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrisons18th_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harrisons18th-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brandis_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brandis-3">[3]</a></sup> This leads to <a href="/wiki/Hypocapnia" title="Hypocapnia">hypocapnia</a>, a reduced concentration of carbon dioxide dissolved in the blood. The body normally attempts to compensate for this <a href="/wiki/Homeostasis" title="Homeostasis">homeostatically</a> but if this fails or is overridden, the <a href="/wiki/PH#Living_systems" title="PH">blood pH</a> will rise, leading to <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_alkalosis" title="Respiratory alkalosis">respiratory alkalosis</a>.  The symptoms of respiratory alkalosis include: dizziness, tingling in the lips, hands or feet, headache, weakness, fainting and seizures.  In extreme cases it may cause carpopedal <a href="/wiki/Tetany" title="Tetany">spasms</a>, a flapping and contraction of the hands and feet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brandis_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brandis-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Factors that may induce or sustain<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrisons18th_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Harrisons18th-2">[2]</a></sup> hyperventilation include: <a href="/wiki/Stress_(biology)" title="Stress (biology)">physiological stress</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anxiety" title="Anxiety">anxiety</a> or <a href="/wiki/Panic_disorder" title="Panic disorder">panic disorder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Altitude_sickness" title="Altitude sickness">high altitude</a>, head injury, <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a>, respiratory disorders such as <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hyperventilation_syndrome" title="Hyperventilation syndrome">hyperventilation syndrome</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edward_Newton_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Edward_Newton-5">[5]</a></sup> cardiovascular problems such as <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_embolism" title="Pulmonary embolism">pulmonary embolisms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anemia" title="Anemia">anemia</a>, an incorrectly calibrated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_respirator" title="Medical respirator">medical respirator</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guyton_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guyton-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brandis_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Brandis-3">[3]</a></sup> and adverse reactions to certain drugs. 
Hyperventilation can also be  induced intentionally to achieve an altered state of consciousness such as in the <a href="/wiki/Choking_game" title="Choking game">choking game</a>, or in an attempt to extend a <a href="/wiki/Freediving_blackout#Shallow_water_blackout" title="Freediving blackout">breath-hold dive</a>.
</p>
