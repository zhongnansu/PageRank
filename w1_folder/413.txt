<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Biosynthesis</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Biosynthesis</b> (also called <b>anabolism</b>) is a multi-step, <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzyme</a>-<a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalyzed</a> process where <a href="/wiki/Substrate_(chemistry)" title="Substrate (chemistry)">substrates</a> are converted into more complex <a href="/wiki/Product_(chemistry)" title="Product (chemistry)">products</a> in living organisms. In biosynthesis, simple <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">compounds</a> are modified, converted into other compounds, or joined together to form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macromolecules" title="Macromolecules">macromolecules</a>. This process often consists of <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_pathway" title="Metabolic pathway">metabolic pathways</a>. Some of these biosynthetic pathways are located within a single cellular <a href="/wiki/Organelle" title="Organelle">organelle</a>, while others involve enzymes that are located within multiple cellular organelles. Examples of these biosynthetic pathways include the production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lipid_membrane" title="Lipid membrane">lipid membrane</a> components and <a href="/wiki/Nucleotide" title="Nucleotide">nucleotides</a>.
</p><p>The prerequisite elements for biosynthesis include: <a href="/wiki/Precursor_(chemistry)" title="Precursor (chemistry)">precursor</a> compounds, <a href="/wiki/Chemical_energy" title="Chemical energy">chemical energy</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a>), and catalytic enzymes which may require <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coenzymes" title="Coenzymes">coenzymes</a> (e.g.<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NADH" title="NADH">NADH</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NADPH" title="NADPH">NADPH</a>). These elements create <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monomers" title="Monomers">monomers</a>, the building blocks for macromolecules. Some important biological macromolecules include: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proteins" title="Proteins">proteins</a>, which are composed of <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acid</a> monomers joined via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peptide_bonds" title="Peptide bonds">peptide bonds</a>, and <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> molecules, which are composed of nucleotides joined via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphodiester_bonds" title="Phosphodiester bonds">phosphodiester bonds</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties_of_chemical_reactions">Properties of chemical reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosynthesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties of chemical reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Biosynthesis occurs due to a series of chemical reactions. For these reactions to take place, the following elements are necessary:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-8153-3218-1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-8153-3218-1-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Precursor_(chemistry)" title="Precursor (chemistry)">Precursor compounds</a>: these compounds are the starting molecules or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Substrate_(biochemistry)" title="Substrate (biochemistry)">substrates</a> in a reaction. These may also be viewed as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reactants" title="Reactants">reactants</a> in a given chemical process.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chemical_energy" title="Chemical energy">Chemical energy</a>: chemical energy can be found in the form of high energy molecules. These molecules are required for energetically unfavorable reactions.  Furthermore, the <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a> of these compounds drives a reaction forward. High energy molecules, such as <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a>, have three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphates" title="Phosphates">phosphates</a>. Often, the terminal phosphate is split off during hydrolysis and transferred to another molecule.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">Catalytic enzymes</a>: these molecules are special <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proteins" title="Proteins">proteins</a> that catalyze a reaction by increasing the <a href="/wiki/Reaction_rate" title="Reaction rate">rate of the reaction</a> and lowering the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_of_activation" title="Energy of activation">activation energy</a>.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coenzymes" title="Coenzymes">Coenzymes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cofactor_(biochemistry)" title="Cofactor (biochemistry)">cofactors</a>: cofactors are molecules that assist in chemical reactions. These may be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal_ions" title="Metal ions">metal ions</a>, vitamin derivatives such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NADH" title="NADH">NADH</a> and <a href="/wiki/Acetyl-CoA" title="Acetyl-CoA">acetyl CoA</a>, or non-vitamin derivatives such as ATP. In the case of NADH, the molecule transfers a hydrogen, whereas acetyl CoA transfers an <a href="/wiki/Acetyl_group" title="Acetyl group">acetyl group</a>, and ATP transfers a phosphate.</li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lipid">Lipid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosynthesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lipid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_lipid_and_lipid_bilayer.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="354" data-file-width="300" height="260" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/The_lipid_and_lipid_bilayer.png/220px-The_lipid_and_lipid_bilayer.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/79/The_lipid_and_lipid_bilayer.png 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_lipid_and_lipid_bilayer.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lipid membrane bilayer</div></div></div>
<p>Many intricate macromolecules are synthesized in a pattern of simple, repeated structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Molecular_Cell_Biology_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Molecular_Cell_Biology-4">[4]</a></sup> For example, the simplest structures of lipids are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatty_acids" title="Fatty acids">fatty acids</a>. Fatty acids are <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a> derivatives; they contain a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carboxyl_group" title="Carboxyl group">carboxyl group</a> "head" and a hydrocarbon chain "tail".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Molecular_Cell_Biology_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Molecular_Cell_Biology-4">[4]</a></sup> These fatty acids create larger components, which in turn incorporate noncovalent interactions to form the lipid bilayer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Molecular_Cell_Biology_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Molecular_Cell_Biology-4">[4]</a></sup>
Fatty acid chains are found in two major components of membrane lipids: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phospholipids" title="Phospholipids">phospholipids</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sphingolipids" title="Sphingolipids">sphingolipids</a>. A third major membrane component, <a href="/wiki/Cholesterol" title="Cholesterol">cholesterol</a>, does not contain these fatty acid units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lehninger_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lehninger-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phospholipids">Phospholipids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosynthesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Phospholipids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The foundation of all biomembranes consists of a <a href="/wiki/Bilayer" title="Bilayer">bilayer</a> structure of phospholipids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hanin_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hanin-6">[6]</a></sup>  The phospholipid molecule is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphipathic" title="Amphipathic">amphipathic</a>; it contains a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophilic" title="Hydrophilic">hydrophilic</a> polar head and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophobic" title="Hydrophobic">hydrophobic</a> nonpolar tail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Molecular_Cell_Biology_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Molecular_Cell_Biology-4">[4]</a></sup>  The phospholipid heads interact with each other and aqueous media, while the hydrocarbon tails orient themselves in the center, away from water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vance_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vance-7">[7]</a></sup> These latter interactions drive the bilayer structure that acts as a barrier for ions and molecules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Katsaras_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Katsaras-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are various types of phospholipids; consequently, their synthesis pathways differ. However, the first step in phospholipid synthesis involves the formation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphatidate" title="Phosphatidate">phosphatidate</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diacylglycerol_3-phosphate" title="Diacylglycerol 3-phosphate">diacylglycerol 3-phosphate</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Endoplasmic_reticulum" title="Endoplasmic reticulum">endoplasmic reticulum</a>  and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Outer_mitochondrial_membrane" title="Outer mitochondrial membrane">outer mitochondrial membrane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vance_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vance-7">[7]</a></sup>  The synthesis pathway is found below:
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Phosphatidic_acid_synthesis.svg" title="Phosphatidic acid synthesis"><img alt="Phosphatidic acid synthesis" data-file-height="222" data-file-width="813" height="181" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Phosphatidic_acid_synthesis.svg/663px-Phosphatidic_acid_synthesis.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Phosphatidic_acid_synthesis.svg/995px-Phosphatidic_acid_synthesis.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Phosphatidic_acid_synthesis.svg/1326px-Phosphatidic_acid_synthesis.svg.png 2x" width="663"/></a></div></div>
<p>The pathway starts with glycerol 3-phosphate, which gets converted to lysophosphatidate via the addition of a fatty acid chain provided by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acyl_coenzyme_a" title="Acyl coenzyme a">acyl coenzyme A</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stryer_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stryer-9">[9]</a></sup> Then, lysophosphatidate is converted to phosphatidate via the addition of another fatty acid chain contributed by a second acyl CoA; all of these steps are catalyzed by the glycerol phosphate <a href="/wiki/Acyltransferase" title="Acyltransferase">acyltransferase</a> enzyme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stryer_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stryer-9">[9]</a></sup> Phospholipid synthesis continues in the endoplasmic reticulum, and the biosynthesis pathway diverges depending on the components of the particular phospholipid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stryer_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Stryer-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sphingolipids">Sphingolipids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosynthesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sphingolipids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like phospholipids, these fatty acid derivatives have a polar head and nonpolar tails.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lehninger_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lehninger-5">[5]</a></sup>  Unlike phospholipids, sphingolipids have a <a href="/wiki/Sphingosine" title="Sphingosine">sphingosine</a> backbone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Sphingolipids exist in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eukaryotic" title="Eukaryotic">eukaryotic</a> cells and are particularly abundant in the <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vance_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Vance-7">[7]</a></sup>  For example, sphingomyelin is part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myelin_sheath" title="Myelin sheath">myelin sheath</a> of nerve fibers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Siegel_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Siegel-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sphingolipids are formed from <a href="/wiki/Ceramide" title="Ceramide">ceramides</a> that consist of a fatty acid chain attached to the amino group of a sphingosine backbone. These ceramides are synthesized from the <a href="/wiki/Acylation" title="Acylation">acylation</a> of sphingosine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Siegel_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Siegel-11">[11]</a></sup>  The biosynthetic pathway for sphingosine is found below:
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cholesterol">Cholesterol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosynthesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cholesterol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipid</a> belongs to a class of molecules called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sterols" title="Sterols">sterols</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lehninger_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lehninger-5">[5]</a></sup> Sterols have four fused rings and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl_group" title="Hydroxyl group">hydroxyl group</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lehninger_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lehninger-5">[5]</a></sup> Cholesterol is a particularly important molecule. Not only does it serve as a component of lipid membranes, it is also a precursor to several <a href="/wiki/Steroid" title="Steroid">steroid</a> hormones, including <a href="/wiki/Cortisol" title="Cortisol">cortisol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Testosterone" title="Testosterone">testosterone</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Estrogen" title="Estrogen">estrogen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harris_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harris-12">[12]</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nucleotides">Nucleotides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosynthesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nucleotides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The biosynthesis of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nucleotides" title="Nucleotides">nucleotides</a> involves enzyme-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyzed</a> reactions that convert substrates into more complex products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-8153-3218-1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-8153-3218-1-1">[1]</a></sup> Nucleotides are the building blocks of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> and <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a>.  Nucleotides are composed of a five-membered ring formed from <a href="/wiki/Ribose" title="Ribose">ribose</a> sugar in RNA, and <a href="/wiki/Deoxyribose" title="Deoxyribose">deoxyribose</a> sugar in DNA; these sugars are linked to a <a href="/wiki/Purine" title="Purine">purine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pyrimidine" title="Pyrimidine">pyrimidine</a> base with a <a href="/wiki/Glycosidic_bond" title="Glycosidic bond">glycosidic bond</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a> group at the <a href="/wiki/Ribonucleotide" title="Ribonucleotide">5' location</a> of the sugar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watson's_Molecular_Biology_of_the_Gene_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Watson's_Molecular_Biology_of_the_Gene-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Purine_nucleotides">Purine nucleotides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosynthesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Purine nucleotides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:382px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nucleotides_syn1.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="958" data-file-width="1062" height="343" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Nucleotides_syn1.svg/380px-Nucleotides_syn1.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Nucleotides_syn1.svg/570px-Nucleotides_syn1.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Nucleotides_syn1.svg/760px-Nucleotides_syn1.svg.png 2x" width="380"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nucleotides_syn1.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><div style="border-width: 0px; border-bottom: 1px solid black; text-align: left;"><b>The synthesis of IMP</b>.</div></div></div></div>
<p>The DNA nucleotides <a href="/wiki/Adenosine" title="Adenosine">adenosine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guanosine" title="Guanosine">guanosine</a> consist of a purine base attached to a ribose sugar with a glycosidic bond.  In the case of RNA nucleotides <a href="/wiki/Deoxyadenosine" title="Deoxyadenosine">deoxyadenosine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deoxyguanosine" title="Deoxyguanosine">deoxyguanosine</a>, the purine bases are attached to a deoxyribose sugar with a glycosidic bond.  The purine bases on DNA and RNA nucleotides are synthesized in a twelve-step reaction mechanism present in most single-celled organisms.  Higher <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eukaryotes" title="Eukaryotes">eukaryotes</a> employ a similar <a href="/wiki/Reaction_mechanism" title="Reaction mechanism">reaction mechanism</a> in ten reaction steps.  Purine bases are synthesized by converting <a href="/wiki/Phosphoribosyl_pyrophosphate" title="Phosphoribosyl pyrophosphate">phosphoribosyl pyrophosphate</a> (PRPP) to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inosine_monophosphate" title="Inosine monophosphate">inosine monophosphate</a> (IMP), which is the first key intermediate in purine base biosynthesis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kappock/Purine_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kappock/Purine-14">[14]</a></sup>  Further enzymatic modification of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inosine_monophosphate" title="Inosine monophosphate">IMP</a> produces the adenosine and guanosine bases of nucleotides.
</p>
<ol><li>The first step in purine biosynthesis is a <a href="/wiki/Condensation_reaction" title="Condensation reaction">condensation reaction</a>, performed by <a href="/wiki/Amidophosphoribosyltransferase" title="Amidophosphoribosyltransferase">glutamine-PRPP amidotransferase</a>.  This enzyme transfers the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amino_group" title="Amino group">amino group</a> from <a href="/wiki/Glutamine" title="Glutamine">glutamine</a> to PRPP, forming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/5-phosphoribosylamine" title="5-phosphoribosylamine">5-phosphoribosylamine</a>.  The following step requires the activation of <a href="/wiki/Glycine" title="Glycine">glycine</a> by the addition of a <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a> group from <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a>.</li>
<li>GAR synthetase<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>  performs the condensation of activated glycine onto PRPP, forming <a href="/wiki/Glycineamide_ribonucleotide" title="Glycineamide ribonucleotide">glycineamide ribonucleotide</a> (GAR).</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GAR_transformylase" title="GAR transformylase">GAR transformylase</a> adds a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formyl_group" title="Formyl group">formyl group</a> onto the amino group of GAR, forming formylglycinamide ribonucleotide  (FGAR).</li>
<li>FGAR amidotransferase<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>  catalyzes the addition of a nitrogen group to FGAR, forming formylglycinamidine ribonucleotide (FGAM).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/AIR_synthetase_(FGAM_cyclase)" title="AIR synthetase (FGAM cyclase)">FGAM cyclase</a> catalyzes ring closure, which involves removal of a water molecule, forming the 5-membered <a href="/wiki/Imidazole" title="Imidazole">imidazole</a> ring <a href="/wiki/5-Aminoimidazole_ribotide" title="5-Aminoimidazole ribotide">5-aminoimidazole ribonucleotide</a> (AIR).</li>
<li>N5-CAIR synthetase transfers a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carboxyl" title="Carboxyl">carboxyl</a> group, forming the intermediate N5-carboxyaminoimidazole ribonucleotide (N5-CAIR).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/5-(carboxyamino)imidazole_ribonucleotide_mutase" title="5-(carboxyamino)imidazole ribonucleotide mutase">N5-CAIR mutase</a> rearranges the carboxyl functional group and transfers it onto the imidazole ring, forming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/5%27-phosphoribosyl-4-carboxy-5-aminoimidazole" title="5'-phosphoribosyl-4-carboxy-5-aminoimidazole">carboxyamino- imidazole ribonucleotide</a> (CAIR).  The two step mechanism of CAIR formation from AIR is mostly found in single celled organisms.  Higher eukaryotes contain the enzyme AIR carboxylase,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>  which transfers a carboxyl group directly to AIR imidazole ring, forming CAIR.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Phosphoribosylaminoimidazolesuccinocarboxamide_synthase" title="Phosphoribosylaminoimidazolesuccinocarboxamide synthase">SAICAR synthetase</a> forms a <a href="/wiki/Peptide_bond" title="Peptide bond">peptide bond</a> between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aspartate" title="Aspartate">aspartate</a> and the added carboxyl group of the imidazole ring, forming <a href="/wiki/Phosphoribosylaminoimidazolesuccinocarboxamide" title="Phosphoribosylaminoimidazolesuccinocarboxamide">N-succinyl-5-aminoimidazole-4-carboxamide ribonucleotide</a> (SAICAR).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Adenylosuccinate_lyase" title="Adenylosuccinate lyase">SAICAR lyase</a> removes the carbon skeleton of the added aspartate, leaving the amino group and forming <a href="/wiki/AICA_ribonucleotide" title="AICA ribonucleotide">5-aminoimidazole-4-carboxamide ribonucleotide</a> (AICAR).</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AICAR_transformylase" title="AICAR transformylase">AICAR transformylase</a> transfers a carbonyl group to AICAR, forming <a href="/wiki/5-Formamidoimidazole-4-carboxamide_ribotide" title="5-Formamidoimidazole-4-carboxamide ribotide">N-formylaminoimidazole- 4-carboxamide ribonucleotide</a> (FAICAR).</li>
<li>The final step involves the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Inosine_monophosphate_synthase" title="Inosine monophosphate synthase">IMP synthase</a>, which performs the purine ring closure and forms the inosine monophosphate intermediate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lehninger_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Lehninger-5">[5]</a></sup></li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pyrimidine_nucleotides">Pyrimidine nucleotides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosynthesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pyrimidine nucleotides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:528px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nucleotides_syn2.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="519" data-file-width="526" height="519" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6b/Nucleotides_syn2.png" width="526"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption">Uridine monophosphate (UMP) biosynthesis</div></div></div>
<p>Other DNA and RNA nucleotide bases that are linked to the ribose sugar via a glycosidic bond are <a href="/wiki/Thymine" title="Thymine">thymine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cytosine" title="Cytosine">cytosine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uracil" title="Uracil">uracil</a> (which is only found in RNA).
<a href="/wiki/Uridine_monophosphate" title="Uridine monophosphate">Uridine monophosphate</a> biosynthesis involves an enzyme that is located in the <a href="/wiki/Inner_mitochondrial_membrane" title="Inner mitochondrial membrane">mitochondrial inner membrane</a> and multifunctional enzymes that are located in the <a href="/wiki/Cytosol" title="Cytosol">cytosol</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Srere/Metabolic_enzymes_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Srere/Metabolic_enzymes-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>The first step involves the enzyme <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbamoyl_phosphate_synthase" title="Carbamoyl phosphate synthase">carbamoyl phosphate synthase</a> combining <a href="/wiki/Glutamine" title="Glutamine">glutamine</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CO2" title="CO2">CO<sub>2</sub></a> in an ATP dependent reaction to form <a href="/wiki/Carbamoyl_phosphate" title="Carbamoyl phosphate">carbamoyl phosphate</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aspartate_carbamoyltransferase" title="Aspartate carbamoyltransferase">Aspartate carbamoyltransferase</a> <a href="/wiki/Condensation_reaction" title="Condensation reaction">condenses</a> carbamoyl phosphate with aspartate to form uridosuccinate.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dihydroorotase" title="Dihydroorotase">Dihydroorotase</a> performs <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ring-closure_reaction" title="Ring-closure reaction">ring closure</a>, a reaction that loses water, to form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dihydroorotate" title="Dihydroorotate">dihydroorotate</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dihydroorotate_dehydrogenase" title="Dihydroorotate dehydrogenase">Dihydroorotate dehydrogenase</a>, located within the mitochondrial inner membrane,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Srere/Metabolic_enzymes_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Srere/Metabolic_enzymes-19">[19]</a></sup> oxidizes dihydroorotate to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orotate" title="Orotate">orotate</a>.</li>
<li>Orotate phosphoribosyl hydrolase (OMP pyrophosphorylase) condenses orotate with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PRPP" title="PRPP">PRPP</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Orotidine_5%27-monophosphate" title="Orotidine 5'-monophosphate">orotidine-5'-phosphate</a>.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OMP_decarboxylase" title="OMP decarboxylase">OMP decarboxylase</a> catalyzes the conversion of orotidine-5'-phosphate to <a href="/wiki/Uridine_monophosphate" title="Uridine monophosphate">UMP</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Broach/Yeast_nucleotide_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Broach/Yeast_nucleotide-20">[20]</a></sup></li></ol>
<p>After the uridine nucleotide base is synthesized, the other bases, cytosine and thymine are synthesized. Cytosine biosynthesis is a two-step reaction which involves the conversion of UMP to <a href="/wiki/Uridine_triphosphate" title="Uridine triphosphate">UTP</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">Phosphate</a> addition to UMP is catalyzed by a <a href="/wiki/Kinase" title="Kinase">kinase</a> enzyme.  The enzyme <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CTP_synthase" title="CTP synthase">CTP synthase</a> catalyzes the next reaction step: the conversion of UTP to <a href="/wiki/Cytidine_triphosphate" title="Cytidine triphosphate">CTP</a> by transferring an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amino_group" title="Amino group">amino group</a> from glutamine to uridine; this forms the cytosine base of CTP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O'Donovan/_pyrimidine_microorg_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-O'Donovan/_pyrimidine_microorg-21">[21]</a></sup> The mechanism, which depicts the reaction UTP + ATP + glutamine ⇔ CTP + ADP + glutamate, is below:
</p>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Thymidylate_synthase_reaction.svg" title="'Thymidylate synthase reaction: dUMP + 5,10-methylenetetrahydrofolate ⇔ dTMP + dihydrofolate"><img alt="'Thymidylate synthase reaction: dUMP + 5,10-methylenetetrahydrofolate ⇔ dTMP + dihydrofolate" data-file-height="518" data-file-width="512" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Thymidylate_synthase_reaction.svg/247px-Thymidylate_synthase_reaction.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Thymidylate_synthase_reaction.svg/371px-Thymidylate_synthase_reaction.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Thymidylate_synthase_reaction.svg/494px-Thymidylate_synthase_reaction.svg.png 2x" width="247"/></a></div>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ctp_synthase_mechanism.jpg" title="Ctp synthase mechanism: UTP + ATP + glutamine ⇔ CTP + ADP + glutamate"><img alt="Ctp synthase mechanism: UTP + ATP + glutamine ⇔ CTP + ADP + glutamate" data-file-height="255" data-file-width="1092" height="112" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Ctp_synthase_mechanism.jpg/480px-Ctp_synthase_mechanism.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Ctp_synthase_mechanism.jpg/720px-Ctp_synthase_mechanism.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Ctp_synthase_mechanism.jpg/960px-Ctp_synthase_mechanism.jpg 2x" width="480"/></a></div></div>
<p>Cytosine is a nucleotide that is present in both DNA and RNA.  However, uracil is only found in RNA. Therefore, after UTP is synthesized, it is must be converted into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deoxy" title="Deoxy">deoxy</a> form to be incorporated into DNA.  This conversion involves the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Ribonucleoside-triphosphate_reductase" title="Ribonucleoside-triphosphate reductase">ribonucleoside triphosphate reductase</a>.  This reaction that removes the 2'-OH of the ribose sugar to generate deoxyribose is not affected by the bases attached to the sugar.  This non-specificity allows ribonucleoside triphosphate reductase to convert all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nucleotide_triphosphates" title="Nucleotide triphosphates">nucleotide triphosphates</a> to <a href="/wiki/Deoxyribonucleotide" title="Deoxyribonucleotide">deoxyribonucleotide</a> by a similar mechanism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O'Donovan/_pyrimidine_microorg_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-O'Donovan/_pyrimidine_microorg-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>In contrast to uracil, thymine bases are found mostly in DNA, not RNA. Cells do not normally contain thymine bases that are linked to ribose sugars in RNA, thus indicating that cells only synthesize deoxyribose-linked thymine. The enzyme <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thymidylate_synthetase" title="Thymidylate synthetase">thymidylate synthetase</a> is responsible for synthesizing thymine residues from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DUMP" title="DUMP">dUMP</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DTMP" title="DTMP">dTMP</a>. This reaction transfers a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methyl" title="Methyl">methyl</a> group onto the uracil base of dUMP to generate dTMP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O'Donovan/_pyrimidine_microorg_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-O'Donovan/_pyrimidine_microorg-21">[21]</a></sup> The thymidylate synthase reaction, dUMP + 5,10-methylenetetrahydrofolate ⇔ dTMP + dihydrofolate, is shown to the right.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="DNA">DNA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosynthesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: DNA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:693px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DNA_replication_en.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="336" data-file-width="691" height="336" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/DNA_replication_en.svg/691px-DNA_replication_en.svg.png" width="691"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption">As DNA polymerase moves in a 3' to 5' direction along the template strand, it synthesizes a new strand in the 5' to 3' direction</div></div></div>
<p>Although there are differences between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eukaryotic" title="Eukaryotic">eukaryotic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prokaryotic" title="Prokaryotic">prokaryotic</a> DNA synthesis, the following section denotes key characteristics of DNA replication shared by both organisms.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> is composed of <a href="/wiki/Nucleotide" title="Nucleotide">nucleotides</a> that are joined by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphodiester_bonds" title="Phosphodiester bonds">phosphodiester bonds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Molecular_Cell_Biology_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Molecular_Cell_Biology-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/DNA_replication" title="DNA replication">DNA synthesis</a>, which takes place in the <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">nucleus</a>, is a <a href="/wiki/Semiconservative_replication" title="Semiconservative replication">semiconservative</a> process, which means that the resulting DNA molecule contains an original strand from the parent structure and a new strand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karp's_Cell_and_Molecular_Biology_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Karp's_Cell_and_Molecular_Biology-22">[22]</a></sup>  DNA synthesis is catalyzed by a family of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DNA_polymerases" title="DNA polymerases">DNA polymerases</a> that require four deoxynucleoside triphosphates, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Template_strand#Elongation" title="Template strand">template strand</a>, and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rna_primer" title="Rna primer">primer</a> with a free 3'OH in which to incorporate nucleotides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Griffiths_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Griffiths-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>In order for DNA replication to occur, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Replication_fork#Replication_fork" title="Replication fork">replication fork</a> is created by enzymes called <a href="/wiki/Helicase" title="Helicase">helicases</a> which unwind the DNA helix.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Griffiths_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Griffiths-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Topoisomerase" title="Topoisomerase">Topoisomerases</a> at the replication fork remove <a href="/wiki/DNA_supercoil" title="DNA supercoil">supercoils</a> caused by DNA unwinding, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single-strand_binding_protein" title="Single-strand binding protein">single-stranded DNA binding proteins</a> maintain the two single-stranded DNA templates stabilized prior to replication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watson's_Molecular_Biology_of_the_Gene_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Watson's_Molecular_Biology_of_the_Gene-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>DNA synthesis is initiated by the <a href="/wiki/RNA_polymerase" title="RNA polymerase">RNA polymerase</a> <a href="/wiki/Primase" title="Primase">primase</a>, which makes an RNA primer with a free 3'OH.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Griffiths_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Griffiths-23">[23]</a></sup>  This primer is attached to the single-stranded DNA template, and DNA polymerase elongates the chain by incorporating nucleotides; DNA polymerase also proofreads the newly synthesized DNA strand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Griffiths_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-Griffiths-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the polymerization reaction catalyzed by DNA polymerase, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nucleophilic_attack" title="Nucleophilic attack">nucleophilic attack</a> occurs by the 3'OH of the growing chain on the innermost phosphorus atom of a deoxynucleoside triphosphate; this yields the formation of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphodiester_bridges" title="Phosphodiester bridges">phosphodiester bridge</a> that attaches a new nucleotide and releases <a href="/wiki/Pyrophosphate" title="Pyrophosphate">pyrophosphate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stryer_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-Stryer-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Two types of strands are created simultaneously during replication: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leading_strand#Replication_fork" title="Leading strand">leading strand</a>, which is synthesized continuously and grows towards the replication fork, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lagging_strand#Replication_fork" title="Lagging strand">lagging strand</a>, which is made discontinuously in <a href="/wiki/Okazaki_fragments" title="Okazaki fragments">Okazaki fragments</a> and grows away from the replication fork.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karp's_Cell_and_Molecular_Biology_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Karp's_Cell_and_Molecular_Biology-22">[22]</a></sup>  Okazaki fragments are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Covalently" title="Covalently">covalently</a> joined by <a href="/wiki/DNA_ligase" title="DNA ligase">DNA ligase</a> to form a continuous strand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karp's_Cell_and_Molecular_Biology_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Karp's_Cell_and_Molecular_Biology-22">[22]</a></sup>
Then, to complete DNA replication, RNA primers are removed, and the resulting gaps are replaced with DNA and joined via DNA ligase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karp's_Cell_and_Molecular_Biology_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-Karp's_Cell_and_Molecular_Biology-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Amino_acids">Amino acids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosynthesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Amino acids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A protein is a polymer that is composed from <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a> that are linked by <a href="/wiki/Peptide_bond" title="Peptide bond">peptide bonds</a>.  There are more than <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid#Non-standard_amino_acids" title="Amino acid">300 amino acids</a> found in nature of which only twenty, known as the <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid#Essential_amino_acids" title="Amino acid">standard amino acids</a>, are the building blocks for protein.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AA_structure_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-AA_structure-24">[24]</a></sup>   Only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_plants" title="Green plants">green plants</a> and most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microbes" title="Microbes">microbes</a> are able to <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid_synthesis" title="Amino acid synthesis">synthesize</a> all of the 20 standard amino acids that are needed by all living species.  <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">Mammals</a> can only synthesize ten of the twenty standard amino acids.  The other amino acids, <a href="/wiki/Valine" title="Valine">valine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methionine" title="Methionine">methionine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leucine" title="Leucine">leucine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isoleucine" title="Isoleucine">isoleucine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phenylalanine" title="Phenylalanine">phenylalanine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lysine" title="Lysine">lysine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Threonine" title="Threonine">threonine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tryptophan" title="Tryptophan">tryptophan</a> for adults and <a href="/wiki/Histidine" title="Histidine">histidine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Arginine" title="Arginine">arginine</a> for babies are obtained through diet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Amino_acid_basic_structure">Amino acid basic structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosynthesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Amino acid basic structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:197px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:L-amino_acid_general.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="156" data-file-width="195" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/L-amino_acid_general.svg/195px-L-amino_acid_general.svg.png" width="195"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption">L-amino acid</div></div></div><p>The general structure of the standard amino acids includes a <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">primary amino group</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carboxyl_group" title="Carboxyl group">carboxyl group</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Functional_group" title="Functional group">functional group</a> attached to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha-carbon" title="Alpha-carbon">α-carbon</a>.  The different amino acids are identified by the functional group.  As a result of the three different groups attached to the α-carbon, amino acids are <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)#Symmetry" title="Chirality (chemistry)">asymmetrical molecules</a>.  For all standard amino acids, except <a href="/wiki/Glycine" title="Glycine">glycine</a>, the α-carbon is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chiral_center" title="Chiral center">chiral center</a>.  In the case of glycine, the α-carbon has two hydrogen atoms, thus adding symmetry to this molecule.  With the exception of <a href="/wiki/Proline" title="Proline">proline</a>, all of the amino acids found in life have the <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)#By_configuration:_D-_and_L-" title="Chirality (chemistry)">L-isoform</a> conformation.  Proline has a functional group on the α-carbon that forms a ring with the amino group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AA_structure_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-AA_structure-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Glutamine_oxoglutarate_aminotransferase_and_Glutamine_synthetase.svg" title="Glutamine oxoglutarate aminotransferase and glutamine synthetase"><img alt="Glutamine oxoglutarate aminotransferase and glutamine synthetase" data-file-height="469" data-file-width="620" height="182" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Glutamine_oxoglutarate_aminotransferase_and_Glutamine_synthetase.svg/240px-Glutamine_oxoglutarate_aminotransferase_and_Glutamine_synthetase.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Glutamine_oxoglutarate_aminotransferase_and_Glutamine_synthetase.svg/360px-Glutamine_oxoglutarate_aminotransferase_and_Glutamine_synthetase.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Glutamine_oxoglutarate_aminotransferase_and_Glutamine_synthetase.svg/480px-Glutamine_oxoglutarate_aminotransferase_and_Glutamine_synthetase.svg.png 2x" width="240"/></a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nitrogen_source">Nitrogen source</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosynthesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Nitrogen source">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One major step in amino acid biosynthesis involves incorporating a nitrogen group onto the α-carbon.  In cells, there are two major pathways of incorporating nitrogen groups.  One pathway involves the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Glutamine_oxoglutarate_aminotransferase" title="Glutamine oxoglutarate aminotransferase">glutamine oxoglutarate aminotransferase</a> (GOGAT) which removes the <a href="/wiki/Amide" title="Amide">amide</a> amino group of <a href="/wiki/Glutamine" title="Glutamine">glutamine</a> and transfers it onto <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2-oxoglutarate" title="2-oxoglutarate">2-oxoglutarate</a>, producing two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glutamate" title="Glutamate">glutamate</a> molecules.  In this catalysis reaction, glutamine serves as the nitrogen source.  An image illustrating this reaction is found to the right.
</p><p>The other pathway for incorporating nitrogen onto the α-carbon of amino acids involves the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Glutamate_dehydrogenase" title="Glutamate dehydrogenase">glutamate dehydrogenase</a> (GDH).  GDH is able to transfer <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> onto 2-oxoglutarate and form glutamate.  Furthermore, the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Glutamine_synthetase" title="Glutamine synthetase">glutamine synthetase</a> (GS) is able to transfer ammonia onto glutamate and synthesize glutamine, replenishing glutamine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AA_Metabolism_Miflin_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-AA_Metabolism_Miflin-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_glutamate_family_of_amino_acids">The glutamate family of amino acids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosynthesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: The glutamate family of amino acids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glutamate" title="Glutamate">glutamate</a> family of amino acids includes the amino acids that derive from the amino acid glutamate.  This family includes: glutamate, <a href="/wiki/Glutamine" title="Glutamine">glutamine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Proline" title="Proline">proline</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Arginine" title="Arginine">arginine</a>.  This family also includes the amino acid <a href="/wiki/Lysine" title="Lysine">lysine</a>, which is derived from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%91-ketoglutarate" title="Α-ketoglutarate">α-ketoglutarate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amino_Families_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Amino_Families-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>The biosynthesis of glutamate and glutamine is a key step in the nitrogen assimilation discussed above.  The enzymes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GOGAT" title="GOGAT">GOGAT</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glutamate_dehydrogenase" title="Glutamate dehydrogenase">GDH</a> catalyze the <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_assimilation" title="Nitrogen assimilation">nitrogen assimilation</a> reactions.
</p><p>In bacteria, the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Glutamate_5-kinase" title="Glutamate 5-kinase">glutamate 5-kinase</a> initiates the biosynthesis of proline by transferring a phosphate group from ATP onto glutamate.  The next reaction is catalyzed by the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Glutamate-5-semialdehyde_dehydrogenase" title="Glutamate-5-semialdehyde dehydrogenase">pyrroline-5-carboxylate synthase</a> (P5CS), which catalyzes the reduction of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma-carboxylation" title="Gamma-carboxylation">ϒ-carboxyl</a> group of L-glutamate 5-phosphate. This results in the formation of glutamate semialdehyde, which spontaneously cyclizes to pyrroline-5-carboxylate.  Pyrroline-5-carboxylate is further reduced by the enzyme pyrroline-5-carboxylate reductase (P5CR) to yield a proline amino acid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the first step of arginine biosynthesis in bacteria, glutamate is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acetylated" title="Acetylated">acetylated</a> by transferring the acetyl group from acetyl-CoA at the N-α position; this prevents spontaneous cyclization.  The enzyme <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N-acetylglutamate_synthase" title="N-acetylglutamate synthase">N-acetylglutamate synthase</a> (glutamate N-acetyltransferase) is responsible for catalyzing the acetylation step.  Subsequent steps are catalyzed by the enzymes <a href="/wiki/Acetylglutamate_kinase" title="Acetylglutamate kinase">N-acetylglutamate kinase</a>, <a href="/wiki/N-acetyl-gamma-glutamyl-phosphate_reductase" title="N-acetyl-gamma-glutamyl-phosphate reductase">N-acetyl-gamma-glutamyl-phosphate reductase</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Acetylornithine_transaminase" title="Acetylornithine transaminase">acetylornithine/succinyldiamino pimelate aminotransferase</a> and yield the N-acetyl-L-ornithine.  The acetyl group of acetylornithine is removed by the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Acetylornithine_deacetylase" title="Acetylornithine deacetylase">acetylornithinase</a> (AO) or <a href="/wiki/Glutamate_N-acetyltransferase" title="Glutamate N-acetyltransferase">ornithine acetyltransferase</a> (OAT), and this yields <a href="/wiki/Ornithine" title="Ornithine">ornithine</a>.  Then, the enzymes <a href="/wiki/Citrulline" title="Citrulline">citrulline</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Argininosuccinate" title="Argininosuccinate">argininosuccinate</a> convert ornithine to arginine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lysine_Biosynthesis.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2130" data-file-width="2227" height="478" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Lysine_Biosynthesis.png/500px-Lysine_Biosynthesis.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Lysine_Biosynthesis.png/750px-Lysine_Biosynthesis.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Lysine_Biosynthesis.png/1000px-Lysine_Biosynthesis.png 2x" width="500"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lysine_Biosynthesis.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The diaminopimelic acid pathway</div></div></div>
<p>There are two distinct lysine biosynthetic pathways: the diaminopimelic acid pathway and the <a href="/wiki/Alpha-aminoadipate_pathway" title="Alpha-aminoadipate pathway">α-aminoadipate pathway</a>.  The most common of the two synthetic pathways is the diaminopimelic acid pathway; it consists of several enzymatic reactions that add carbon groups to aspartate to yield lysine:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Aspartate_kinase" title="Aspartate kinase">Aspartate kinase</a> initiates the diaminopimelic acid pathway by phosphorylating aspartate and producing aspartyl phosphate.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aspartate-semialdehyde_dehydrogenase" title="Aspartate-semialdehyde dehydrogenase">Aspartate semialdehyde dehydrogenase</a> catalyzes the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NADPH" title="NADPH">NADPH</a>-dependent reduction of aspartyl phosphate to yield aspartate semialdehyde.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/4-hydroxy-tetrahydrodipicolinate_synthase" title="4-hydroxy-tetrahydrodipicolinate synthase">4-hydroxy-tetrahydrodipicolinate synthase</a> adds a <a href="/wiki/Pyruvic_acid" title="Pyruvic acid">pyruvate</a> group to the β-aspartyl-4-semialdehyde, and a water molecule is removed. This causes <a href="/wiki/Cyclic_compound" title="Cyclic compound">cyclization</a> and gives rise to (2S,4S)-4-hydroxy-2,3,4,5-tetrahydrodipicolinate.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dihydrodipicolinate_reductase" title="Dihydrodipicolinate reductase">4-hydroxy-tetrahydrodipicolinate reductase</a> catalyzes the reduction of (2S,4S)-4-hydroxy-2,3,4,5-tetrahydrodipicolinate by NADPH to yield Δ'-piperideine-2,6-dicarboxylate (2,3,4,5-tetrahydrodipicolinate) and H<sub>2</sub>O.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tetrahydrodipicolinate_N-acetyltransferase" title="Tetrahydrodipicolinate N-acetyltransferase">Tetrahydrodipicolinate acyltransferase</a> catalyzes the acetylation reaction that results in ring opening and yields N-acetyl α-amino-ε-ketopimelate.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Succinyldiaminopimelate_transaminase" title="Succinyldiaminopimelate transaminase">N-succinyl-α-amino-ε-ketopimelate-glutamate aminotransaminase</a> catalyzes the transamination reaction that removes the keto group of N-acetyl α-amino-ε-ketopimelate and replaces it with an amino group to yield N-succinyl-L-diaminopimelate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Succinyl-diaminopimelate_desuccinylase" title="Succinyl-diaminopimelate desuccinylase">N-acyldiaminopimelate deacylase</a> catalyzes the deacylation of N-succinyl-L-diaminopimelate to yield L,L-diaminopimelate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Diaminopimelate_epimerase" title="Diaminopimelate epimerase">DAP epimerase</a> catalyzes the conversion of L,L-diaminopimelate to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meso_form" title="Meso form">meso</a> form of L,L-diaminopimelate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Diaminopimelate_decarboxylase" title="Diaminopimelate decarboxylase">DAP decarboxylase</a> catalyzes the removal of the carboxyl group, yielding L-lysine.</li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_serine_family_of_amino_acids">The serine family of amino acids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosynthesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: The serine family of amino acids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Serine" title="Serine">serine</a> family of amino acid includes: serine, <a href="/wiki/Cysteine" title="Cysteine">cysteine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Glycine" title="Glycine">glycine</a>.  Most microorganisms and plants obtain the sulfur for synthesizing <a href="/wiki/Methionine" title="Methionine">methionine</a> from the amino acid cysteine.  Furthermore, the conversion of serine to glycine provides the carbons needed for the biosynthesis of the methionine and <a href="/wiki/Histidine" title="Histidine">histidine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amino_Families_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Amino_Families-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>During serine biosynthesis,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Phosphoglycerate_dehydrogenase" title="Phosphoglycerate dehydrogenase">phosphoglycerate dehydrogenase</a> catalyzes the initial reaction that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidizes" title="Oxidizes">oxidizes</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3-phospho-D-glycerate" title="3-phospho-D-glycerate">3-phospho-D-glycerate</a> to yield <a href="/wiki/Phosphohydroxypyruvic_acid" title="Phosphohydroxypyruvic acid">3-phosphonooxypyruvate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>  The following reaction is catalyzed by the enzyme <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphoserine_aminotransferase" title="Phosphoserine aminotransferase">phosphoserine aminotransferase</a>, which transfers an amino group from glutamate onto 3-phosphonooxypyruvate to yield <a href="/wiki/Phosphoserine" title="Phosphoserine">L-phosphoserine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>  The final step is catalyzed by the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Phosphoserine_phosphatase" title="Phosphoserine phosphatase">phosphoserine phosphatase</a>, which <a href="/wiki/Dephosphorylation" title="Dephosphorylation">dephosphorylates</a> L-phosphoserine to yield <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L-serine" title="L-serine">L-serine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are two known pathways for the biosynthesis of glycine.  Organisms that use <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Acetate" title="Acetate">acetate</a> as the major carbon source utilize the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glyconeogenesis" title="Glyconeogenesis">glyconeogenic</a> pathway to synthesize <a href="/wiki/Glycine" title="Glycine">glycine</a>.  The other pathway of glycine biosynthesis is known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glycolytic" title="Glycolytic">glycolytic</a> pathway.  This pathway converts serine synthesized from the intermediates of <a href="/wiki/Glycolysis" title="Glycolysis">glycolysis</a> to glycine.  In the glycolytic pathway, the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Serine_hydroxymethyltransferase" title="Serine hydroxymethyltransferase">serine hydroxymethyltransferase</a> catalyzes the cleavage of serine to yield glycine and transfers the cleaved carbon group of serine onto <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetrahydrofolate" title="Tetrahydrofolate">tetrahydrofolate</a>, forming <a href="/wiki/5,10-Methylenetetrahydrofolate" title="5,10-Methylenetetrahydrofolate">5,10-methylene-tetrahydrofolate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cysteine biosynthesis is a two-step reaction that involves the incorporation of inorganic <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>.  In microorganisms and plants, the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Serine_O-acetyltransferase" title="Serine O-acetyltransferase">serine acetyltransferase</a> catalyzes the transfer of acetyl group from <a href="/wiki/Acetyl-CoA" title="Acetyl-CoA">acetyl-CoA</a> onto L-serine to yield <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/O-acetyl-L-serine" title="O-acetyl-L-serine">O-acetyl-L-serine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>  The following reaction step, catalyzed by the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Cysteine_synthase" title="Cysteine synthase">O-acetyl serine (thiol) lyase</a>, replaces the acetyl group of O-acetyl-L-serine with sulfide to yield cysteine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_aspartate_family_of_amino_acids">The aspartate family of amino acids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosynthesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: The aspartate family of amino acids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aspartate" title="Aspartate">aspartate</a> family of amino acids includes: <a href="/wiki/Threonine" title="Threonine">threonine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lysine" title="Lysine">lysine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methionine" title="Methionine">methionine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isoleucine" title="Isoleucine">isoleucine</a>, and aspartate.  Lysine and isoleucine are considered part of the aspartate family even though part of their carbon skeleton is derived from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyruvate" title="Pyruvate">pyruvate</a>. In the case of methionine, the methyl carbon is derived from serine and the sulfur group, but in most organisms, it is derived from cysteine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amino_Families_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-Amino_Families-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>
The biosynthesis of aspartate is a one step reaction that is catalyzed by a single enzyme.  The enzyme <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aspartate_aminotransferase" title="Aspartate aminotransferase">aspartate aminotransferase</a> catalyzes the transfer of an amino group from aspartate onto <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%91-ketoglutarate" title="Α-ketoglutarate">α-ketoglutarate</a> to yield glutamate and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxaloacetate" title="Oxaloacetate">oxaloacetate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>  Asparagine is synthesized by an ATP-dependent addition of an amino group onto aspartate; <a href="/wiki/Asparagine_synthetase" title="Asparagine synthetase">asparagine synthetase</a> catalyzes the addition of nitrogen from glutamine or soluble ammonia to aspartate to yield asparagine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> </p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lysine_Biosynthesis.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2130" data-file-width="2227" height="383" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Lysine_Biosynthesis.png/400px-Lysine_Biosynthesis.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Lysine_Biosynthesis.png/600px-Lysine_Biosynthesis.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Lysine_Biosynthesis.png/800px-Lysine_Biosynthesis.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lysine_Biosynthesis.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The diaminopimelic acid lysine biosynthetic pathway</div></div></div>
<p>The diaminopimelic acid biosynthetic pathway of lysine belongs to the aspartate family of amino acids.  This pathway involves nine enzyme-catalyzed reactions that convert aspartate to lysine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lysine_pathways_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lysine_pathways-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Aspartate_kinase" title="Aspartate kinase">Aspartate kinase</a> catalyzes the initial step in the diaminopimelic acid pathway by transferring a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphoryl" title="Phosphoryl">phosphoryl</a> from ATP onto the carboxylate group of aspartate, which yields aspartyl-β-phosphate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aspartate-semialdehyde_dehydrogenase" title="Aspartate-semialdehyde dehydrogenase">Aspartate-semialdehyde dehydrogenase</a> catalyzes the reduction reaction by <a href="/wiki/Dephosphorylation" title="Dephosphorylation">dephosphorylation</a> of aspartyl-β-phosphate to yield aspartate-β-semialdehyde.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/4-hydroxy-tetrahydrodipicolinate_synthase" title="4-hydroxy-tetrahydrodipicolinate synthase">Dihydrodipicolinate synthase</a> catalyzes the <a href="/wiki/Condensation_reaction" title="Condensation reaction">condensation</a> reaction of aspartate-β-semialdehyde with pyruvate to yield dihydrodipicolinic acid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dihydrodipicolinate_reductase" title="Dihydrodipicolinate reductase">4-hydroxy-tetrahydrodipicolinate reductase</a> catalyzes the reduction of dihydrodipicolinic acid to yield tetrahydrodipicolinic acid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/2,3,4,5-tetrahydropyridine-2,6-dicarboxylate_N-succinyltransferase" title="2,3,4,5-tetrahydropyridine-2,6-dicarboxylate N-succinyltransferase">Tetrahydrodipicolinate N-succinyltransferase</a> catalyzes the transfer of a succinyl group from succinyl-CoA on to tetrahydrodipicolinic acid to yield N-succinyl-L-2,6-diaminoheptanedioate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></li>
<li>N-succinyldiaminopimelate aminotransferase catalyzes the transfer of an amino group from glutamate onto N-succinyl-L-2,6-diaminoheptanedioate to yield N-succinyl-L,L-diaminopimelic acid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Succinyldiaminopimelate_transaminase" title="Succinyldiaminopimelate transaminase">Succinyl-diaminopimelate desuccinylase</a> catalyzes the removal of acyl group from N-succinyl-L,L-diaminopimelic acid to yield L,L-diaminopimelic acid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Diaminopimelate_epimerase" title="Diaminopimelate epimerase">Diaminopimelate epimerase</a> catalyzes the inversion of the α-carbon of L,L-diaminopimelic acid to yield <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meso-diaminopimelic_acid" title="Meso-diaminopimelic acid">meso-diaminopimelic acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></li>
<li>Siaminopimelate decarboxylase catalyzes the final step in lysine biosynthesis that removes the carbon dioxide group from meso-diaminopimelic acid to yield L-lysine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></li></ol>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proteins">Proteins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosynthesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Proteins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:578px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Peptide_syn.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="368" data-file-width="576" height="368" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0f/Peptide_syn.png" width="576"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption">The tRNA anticodon interacts with the mRNA codon in order to bind an amino acid to growing polypeptide chain.</div></div></div><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:722px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Charge_tRNA.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="459" data-file-width="720" height="459" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3f/Charge_tRNA.png" width="720"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption">The process of tRNA charging</div></div></div>
<p>Protein synthesis occurs via a process called <a href="/wiki/Translation_(biology)" title="Translation (biology)">translation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weaver_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weaver-53">[53]</a></sup>  During translation, genetic material called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRNA" title="MRNA">mRNA</a>  is read by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ribosomes" title="Ribosomes">ribosomes</a> to generate a protein <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polypeptide" title="Polypeptide">polypeptide</a> chain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weaver_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-Weaver-53">[53]</a></sup>  This process requires <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TRNA" title="TRNA">transfer RNA</a> (tRNA) which serves as an adaptor by binding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amino_acids" title="Amino acids">amino acids</a> on one end and interacting with mRNA at the other end; the latter pairing between the tRNA and mRNA ensures that the correct amino acid is added to the chain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weaver_53-2"><a href="#cite_note-Weaver-53">[53]</a></sup>  Protein synthesis occurs in three phases: initiation, elongation, and termination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watson's_Molecular_Biology_of_the_Gene_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Watson's_Molecular_Biology_of_the_Gene-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Prokaryotic_translation" title="Prokaryotic translation">Prokaryotic translation</a> differs from <a href="/wiki/Eukaryotic_translation" title="Eukaryotic translation">eukaryotic translation</a>; however, this section will mostly focus on the commonalities between the two organisms.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Additional_background">Additional background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosynthesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Additional background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Before translation can begin, the process of binding a specific amino acid to its corresponding tRNA must occur. This reaction, called tRNA charging, is catalyzed by <a href="/wiki/Aminoacyl_tRNA_synthetase" title="Aminoacyl tRNA synthetase">aminoacyl tRNA synthetase</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Copper_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Copper-54">[54]</a></sup>  A specific tRNA synthetase is responsible for recognizing and charging a particular amino acid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Copper_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-Copper-54">[54]</a></sup>  Furthermore, this enzyme has special discriminator regions to ensure the correct binding between tRNA and its cognate amino acid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Copper_54-2"><a href="#cite_note-Copper-54">[54]</a></sup>  The first step for joining an amino acid to its corresponding tRNA is the formation of aminoacyl-AMP:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Copper_54-3"><a href="#cite_note-Copper-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The combination of these two steps, both of which are catalyzed by aminoacyl tRNA synthetase, produces a charged tRNA that is ready to add amino acids to the growing polypeptide chain.
</p><p>In addition to binding an amino acid, tRNA has a three nucleotide unit called an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anticodon#Anticodon" title="Anticodon">anticodon</a> that <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Basepairing&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Basepairing (page does not exist)">base pairs</a> with specific nucleotide triplets on the mRNA called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Codons" title="Codons">codons</a>; codons  encode a specific amino acid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackson_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jackson-55">[55]</a></sup>  This interaction is possible thanks to the ribosome, which serves as the site for protein synthesis. The ribosome possesses three tRNA binding sites: the aminoacyl site (A site), the peptidyl site (P site), and the exit site (E site).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Green_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Green-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are numerous codons within an mRNA transcript, and it is very common for an amino acid to be specified by more than one codon; this phenomenon is called <a href="/wiki/Codon_degeneracy" title="Codon degeneracy">degeneracy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weissbach_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weissbach-57">[57]</a></sup>  In all, there are 64 codons, 61 of each code for one of the 20 amino acids, while the remaining codons specify chain termination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weissbach_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-Weissbach-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Translation_in_steps">Translation in steps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosynthesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Translation in steps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As previously mentioned, translation occurs in three phases: initiation, elongation, and termination. 
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:313px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TRNA_ribosomes_diagram_en.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="311" height="500" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/TRNA_ribosomes_diagram_en.svg/311px-TRNA_ribosomes_diagram_en.svg.png" width="311"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption">Translation</div></div></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Step_1:_Initiation">Step 1: Initiation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosynthesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Step 1: Initiation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The completion of the initiation phase is dependent on the following three events:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Watson's_Molecular_Biology_of_the_Gene_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Watson's_Molecular_Biology_of_the_Gene-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>1.	The recruitment of the ribosome to mRNA
</p><p>2.	The binding of a charged initiator tRNA into the P site of the ribosome
</p><p>3.	The proper alignment of the ribosome with mRNA's start codon
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Step_2:_Elongation">Step 2: Elongation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosynthesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Step 2: Elongation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Following initiation, the polypeptide chain is extended via anticodon:codon interactions, with the ribosome adding amino acids to the polypeptide chain one at a time. The following steps must occur to ensure the correct addition of amino acids:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frank_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frank-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>1.	The binding of the correct tRNA into the A site of the ribosome
</p><p>2.	The formation of a <a href="/wiki/Peptide_bond" title="Peptide bond">peptide bond</a> between the tRNA in the A site and the polypeptide chain attached to the tRNA in the P site
</p><p>3.	<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protein_translocation#Protein_translocation" title="Protein translocation">Translocation</a> or advancement of the tRNA-mRNA complex by three nucleotides
</p><p>Translocation "kicks off" the tRNA at the E site and shifts the tRNA from the A site into the P site, leaving the A site free for an incoming tRNA to add another amino acid.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Step_3:_Termination">Step 3: Termination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosynthesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Step 3: Termination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The last stage of translation occurs when a <a href="/wiki/Stop_codon" title="Stop codon">stop codon</a> enters the A site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-8153-3218-1_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-8153-3218-1-1">[1]</a></sup> Then, the following steps occur:
</p><p>1.  The recognition of codons by <a href="/wiki/Release_factor" title="Release factor">release factors</a>, which causes the <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a> of the polypeptide chain from the tRNA located in the P site<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-8153-3218-1_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-8153-3218-1-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>2.	The release of the polypeptide chain<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weissbach_57-2"><a href="#cite_note-Weissbach-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>3.	The dissociation and "recycling" of the ribosome for future translation processes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weissbach_57-3"><a href="#cite_note-Weissbach-57">[57]</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diseases_associated_with_macromolecule_deficiency">Diseases associated with macromolecule deficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosynthesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Diseases associated with macromolecule deficiency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Xanthelasma_palpebrarum.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="283" data-file-width="425" height="120" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Xanthelasma_palpebrarum.jpg/180px-Xanthelasma_palpebrarum.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Xanthelasma_palpebrarum.jpg/270px-Xanthelasma_palpebrarum.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Xanthelasma_palpebrarum.jpg/360px-Xanthelasma_palpebrarum.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Xanthelasma_palpebrarum.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Familial hypercholesterolemia causes cholesterol deposits</div></div></div>
<p>Errors in biosynthetic pathways can have deleterious consequences including the malformation of macromolecules or the underproduction of functional molecules. Below are examples that illustrate the disruptions that occur due to these inefficiencies.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Familial_hypercholesterolemia" title="Familial hypercholesterolemia">Familial hypercholesterolemia</a>: this disorder is characterized by the absence of functional <a href="/wiki/Receptor_(biochemistry)" title="Receptor (biochemistry)">receptors</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LDL" title="LDL">LDL</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bandeali-_Familial_hypercholesterolemia_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bandeali-_Familial_hypercholesterolemia-59">[59]</a></sup> Deficiencies in the formation of LDL receptors may cause faulty receptors which disrupt the <a href="/wiki/Endocytosis" title="Endocytosis">endocytic</a> pathway, inhibiting the entry of LDL into the liver and other cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bandeali-_Familial_hypercholesterolemia_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bandeali-_Familial_hypercholesterolemia-59">[59]</a></sup>  This causes a buildup of LDL in the blood plasma, which results in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atherosclerotic_plaques" title="Atherosclerotic plaques">atherosclerotic plaques</a> that narrow arteries and increase the risk of heart attacks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bandeali-_Familial_hypercholesterolemia_59-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bandeali-_Familial_hypercholesterolemia-59">[59]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lesch%E2%80%93Nyhan_syndrome" title="Lesch–Nyhan syndrome">Lesch–Nyhan syndrome</a>: this genetic disease is characterized by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self_mutilation" title="Self mutilation">self- mutilation</a>, mental deficiency, and <a href="/wiki/Gout" title="Gout">gout</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kang-_Lesch-Nyhan_syndrome_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kang-_Lesch-Nyhan_syndrome-60">[60]</a></sup>  It is caused by the absence of <a href="/wiki/Hypoxanthine-guanine_phosphoribosyltransferase" title="Hypoxanthine-guanine phosphoribosyltransferase">hypoxanthine-guanine phosphoribosyltransferase</a>, which is a necessary enzyme for purine nucleotide formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kang-_Lesch-Nyhan_syndrome_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kang-_Lesch-Nyhan_syndrome-60">[60]</a></sup>  The lack of enzyme reduces the level of necessary nucleotides and causes the accumulation of biosynthesis <a href="/wiki/Reaction_intermediate" title="Reaction intermediate">intermediates</a>, which results in the aforementioned unusual behavior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kang-_Lesch-Nyhan_syndrome_60-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kang-_Lesch-Nyhan_syndrome-60">[60]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Severe_combined_immunodeficiency" title="Severe combined immunodeficiency">Severe combined immunodeficiency (SCID)</a>: SCID is characterized by a loss of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T_cells" title="T cells">T cells</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janeway's_Immunobiology_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Janeway's_Immunobiology-61">[61]</a></sup> Shortage of these immune system components increases the susceptibility to infectious agents because the affected individuals cannot develop <a href="/wiki/Immunological_memory" title="Immunological memory">immunological</a> memory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janeway's_Immunobiology_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-Janeway's_Immunobiology-61">[61]</a></sup> This immunological disorder results from a deficiency in <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_deaminase" title="Adenosine deaminase">adenosine deanimase</a> activity, which causes a buildup of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DATP" title="DATP">dATP</a>. These dATP molecules then inhibit ribonucleotide reductase, which prevents of DNA synthesis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janeway's_Immunobiology_61-2"><a href="#cite_note-Janeway's_Immunobiology-61">[61]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Huntington%27s_disease" title="Huntington's disease">Huntington's disease</a>: this <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neurological" title="Neurological">neurological</a> disease is caused from errors that occur during DNA synthesis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hughes_Huntington's_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hughes_Huntington's-62">[62]</a></sup> These errors or mutations lead to the expression of a mutant <a href="/wiki/Huntingtin" title="Huntingtin">huntingtin</a> protein, which contains repetitive <a href="/wiki/Glutamine" title="Glutamine">glutamine</a> residues that are encoded by expanding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trinucleotide_repeat#CAG_Repeats" title="Trinucleotide repeat">CAG trinucleotide repeats</a> in the gene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hughes_Huntington's_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hughes_Huntington's-62">[62]</a></sup>  Huntington's disease is characterized by neuronal loss and <a href="/wiki/Gliosis" title="Gliosis">gliosis</a>. Symptoms of the disease include: movement disorder, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cognitive" title="Cognitive">cognitive</a> decline, and behavioral disorder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biglan-_Huntington's_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Biglan-_Huntington's-63">[63]</a></sup></li></ul>
