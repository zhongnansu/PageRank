<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Arterial blood gas test</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><table class="infobox" style="width:22em"><tbody><tr><th colspan="2" style="text-align:center;font-size:125%;font-weight:bold;background-color: lightblue">Arterial-blood gas test</th></tr><tr><td colspan="2" style="text-align:center">

<p>An <b>arterial-blood gas</b> (<b>ABG</b>) test measures the amounts of arterial gases, such as <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. An ABG test requires that a small volume of blood be drawn from the <a href="/wiki/Radial_artery" title="Radial artery">radial artery</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Syringe" title="Syringe">syringe</a> and a thin <a href="/wiki/Hypodermic_needle" title="Hypodermic needle">needle</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> but sometimes the <a href="/wiki/Femoral_artery" title="Femoral artery">femoral artery</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Groin" title="Groin">groin</a> or another site is used. The blood can also be drawn from an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arterial_catheter" title="Arterial catheter">arterial catheter</a>. An ABG test measures the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Blood-gas_tension&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Blood-gas tension (page does not exist)">blood-gas tension</a> values of the <a href="/wiki/Oxygen#Partial_pressure" title="Oxygen">arterial partial pressure of oxygen</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PaCO2" title="PaCO2">arterial partial pressure of carbon dioxide</a>, and the blood's <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a>. In addition, the arterial <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_saturation_(medicine)" title="Oxygen saturation (medicine)">oxygen saturation</a> can be determined. Such information is vital when caring for patients with critical illnesses or respiratory disease. Therefore, the ABG test is one of the most common tests performed on patients in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intensive-care_unit" title="Intensive-care unit">intensive-care units</a>. In other <a href="/wiki/Therapy#Levels_of_care" title="Therapy">levels of care</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pulse_oximetry" title="Pulse oximetry">pulse oximetry</a> plus transcutaneous carbon-dioxide measurement is a less invasive, alternative method of obtaining similar information.
</p><p>An ABG test can also measure the level of <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> in the blood.  Many blood-gas analyzers will also report concentrations of <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a>, several <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolytes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxyhemoglobin" title="Oxyhemoglobin">oxyhemoglobin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carboxyhemoglobin" title="Carboxyhemoglobin">carboxyhemoglobin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Methemoglobin" title="Methemoglobin">methemoglobin</a>. ABG testing is mainly used in <a href="/wiki/Pulmonology" title="Pulmonology">pulmonology</a> and critical-care medicine to determine <a href="/wiki/Gas_exchange" title="Gas exchange">gas exchange</a> across the alveolar-capillary membrane.  ABG testing also has a variety of applications in other areas of medicine. Combinations of disorders can be complex and difficult to interpret, so calculators,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABG_interpreter_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ABG_interpreter-2">[2]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nomograms" title="Nomograms">nomograms</a>, and rules of thumb<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> are commonly used.
</p><p>ABG <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(medicine)" title="Sampling (medicine)">samples</a> originally were sent from the clinic to the <a href="/wiki/Medical_laboratory" title="Medical laboratory">medical laboratory</a> for analysis. Today, the analysis can be done either in the laboratory or as <a href="/wiki/Point-of-care_testing" title="Point-of-care testing">point-of-care testing</a>, depending on the equipment available in each clinic.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sampling_and_analysis">Sampling and analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arterial_blood_gas_test&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Sampling and analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arterial_blood_gas_device.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2236" data-file-width="2708" height="182" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Arterial_blood_gas_device.jpg/220px-Arterial_blood_gas_device.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Arterial_blood_gas_device.jpg/330px-Arterial_blood_gas_device.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Arterial_blood_gas_device.jpg/440px-Arterial_blood_gas_device.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arterial_blood_gas_device.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bench top analyzer ABL800 FLEX - <a href="/wiki/Radiometer_(company)" title="Radiometer (company)">Radiometer Medical</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cobas221A.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1456" data-file-width="1743" height="184" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Cobas221A.png/220px-Cobas221A.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Cobas221A.png/330px-Cobas221A.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Cobas221A.png/440px-Cobas221A.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cobas221A.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Modern, blood-gas analyzer. This device is capable of reporting pH, <a href="/wiki/PCO2" title="PCO2">pCO<sub>2</sub></a>, pO<sub>2</sub>, SatO<sub>2</sub>, Na<sup>+</sup>, K<sup>+</sup>, Cl<sup>−</sup>, Ca<sup>2+</sup>, Hemoglobin (total and derivatives: O<sub>2</sub>Hb, MetHb, COHb, HHb, CNHb, SHb ), Hematocrit, Total bilirubin, Glucose, Lactate and Urea. (Cobas b 221 - Roche Diagnostics).</div></div></div>
<p>Arterial blood for blood-gas analysis is usually drawn by a <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_therapist" title="Respiratory therapist">respiratory therapist</a> and sometimes a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phlebotomist" title="Phlebotomist">phlebotomist</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nurse" title="Nurse">nurse</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Paramedic" title="Paramedic">paramedic</a> or a doctor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Blood is most commonly drawn from the <a href="/wiki/Radial_artery" title="Radial artery">radial artery</a> because it is easily accessible, can be compressed to control bleeding, and has less risk for occlusion. The selection of which radial artery to draw from is based on the outcome of an <a href="/wiki/Allen%27s_test" title="Allen's test">Allen's test</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Brachial_artery" title="Brachial artery">brachial artery</a> (or less often, the <a href="/wiki/Femoral_artery" title="Femoral artery">femoral artery</a>) is also used, especially during emergency situations or with children. Blood can also be taken from an arterial catheter already placed in one of these arteries.
</p><p>There are plastic and glass syringes used for blood gas samples.  Most syringes come pre-packaged and contain a small amount of <a href="/wiki/Heparin" title="Heparin">heparin</a>, to prevent <a href="/wiki/Coagulation" title="Coagulation">coagulation</a>. Other syringes may need to be heparinised, by drawing up a small amount of liquid heparin and squirting it out again to remove air bubbles. Once the sample is obtained, care is taken to eliminate visible gas bubbles, as these bubbles can dissolve into the sample and cause inaccurate results.  The sealed syringe is taken to a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_gas_analyzer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Blood gas analyzer (page does not exist)">blood gas analyzer</a>.  If a plastic blood gas syringe is used, the sample should be transported and kept at room temperature and analyzed within 30 min.  If prolonged time delays are expected (i.e., greater than 30 min) prior to analysis, the sample should be drawn in a glass syringe and immediately placed on ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>  Standard blood tests can also be performed on arterial blood, such as measuring <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactate</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hemoglobins" title="Hemoglobins">hemoglobins</a>, dys-haemoglobins, <a href="/wiki/Bilirubin" title="Bilirubin">bilirubin</a> and  <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolytes</a>.
</p><p>Derived parameters include bicarbonate concentration, SaO2, and base excess.  Bicarbonate concentration is calculated from the measured pH and PCO2 using the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation.  SaO2 is derived from the measured PO2 and calculated based on the assumption that all measured hemoglobin is normal (oxy- or deoxy-) hemoglobin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Calculations">Calculations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arterial_blood_gas_test&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Calculations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cobas_b_121_Measurement_Chamber_(detail).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1456" data-file-width="2592" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Cobas_b_121_Measurement_Chamber_%28detail%29.jpg/220px-Cobas_b_121_Measurement_Chamber_%28detail%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Cobas_b_121_Measurement_Chamber_%28detail%29.jpg/330px-Cobas_b_121_Measurement_Chamber_%28detail%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Cobas_b_121_Measurement_Chamber_%28detail%29.jpg/440px-Cobas_b_121_Measurement_Chamber_%28detail%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cobas_b_121_Measurement_Chamber_(detail).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Detail of measurement chamber of a modern blood gas analyzer showing the measurement electrodes. (Cobas b 121 - Roche Diagnostics)</div></div></div>
<p>The machine used for analysis aspirates this blood from the syringe and measures the <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Partial_pressure" title="Partial pressure">partial pressures</a> of oxygen and carbon dioxide. The bicarbonate concentration is also calculated. These results are usually available for interpretation within five minutes.
</p><p>Two methods have been used in medicine in the management of blood gases  of patients in hypothermia: pH-stat method and alpha-stat method. Recent studies suggest that the α-stat method is superior.
</p>
<ul><li>pH-stat: The pH and other ABG results are measured at the patient's actual temperature. The goal is to maintain a pH of 7.40 and the arterial carbon dioxide tension (paCO<sub>2</sub>) at 5.3 kPa (40 mmHg) at the actual patient temperature. It is necessary to add CO<sub>2</sub> to the oxygenator to accomplish this goal.</li>
<li>α-stat (alpha-stat): The pH and other ABG results are measured at 37 °C, despite the patient's actual temperature. The goal is to maintain the arterial carbon dioxide tension at 5.3 kPa (40mmHg) and the pH at 7.40 when measured at +37 °C.</li></ul>
<p>Both the pH-stat and alpha-stat strategies have theoretical disadvantages. α-stat method is the method of choice for optimal myocardial function. The pH-stat method may result in loss of autoregulation in the brain (coupling of the cerebral blood flow with the metabolic rate in the brain). By increasing the cerebral blood flow beyond the metabolic requirements, the pH-stat method may lead to cerebral microembolisation and intracranial hypertension.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Guidelines">Guidelines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arterial_blood_gas_test&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Guidelines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ol><li>A 1 mmHg change in PaCO<sub>2</sub> above or below 40 mmHg results in 0.008 unit change in pH in the opposite direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>The PaCO<sub>2</sub> will decrease by about 1 mmHg for every 1 mEq/L reduction in [<span class="chemf nowrap">HCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>] below 24 mEq/L</li>
<li>A change in [<span class="chemf nowrap">HCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>] of 10 mEq/L will result in a change in pH of approximately 0.15 pH units in the same direction.</li>
<li>Assess relation of pCO<sub>2</sub> with pH: If pCO<sub>2</sub> &amp; pH are moving in opposite directions i.e., pCO<sub>2</sub> ↑ when pH is &lt;7.4 or pCO<sub>2</sub> ↓ when pH &gt; 7.4, it is a primary respiratory disorder. If pCO<sub>2</sub> &amp; pH are moving in same direction i.e., pCO<sub>2</sub> ↑when pH is &gt;7.4 or pCO<sub>2</sub> ↓ when pH &lt; 7.4, it is a primary metabolic disorder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EduLanche_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-EduLanche-8">[8]</a></sup></li></ol>

<p>The normal range for pH is 7.35–7.45. As the pH decreases (&lt; 7.35), it implies <a href="/wiki/Acidosis" title="Acidosis">acidosis</a>, while if the pH increases (&gt; 7.45) it implies <a href="/wiki/Alkalosis" title="Alkalosis">alkalosis</a>. In the context of arterial blood gases, the most common occurrence will be that of <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_acidosis" title="Respiratory acidosis">respiratory acidosis</a>.  Carbon dioxide is dissolved in the blood as carbonic acid, a weak acid; however, in large concentrations, it can affect the pH drastically. Whenever there is poor pulmonary ventilation, the carbon dioxide levels in the blood are expected to rise. This leads to a rise of carbonic acid, leading to a decrease in pH. The first buffer of pH will be the plasma proteins, since these can accept some H<sup>+</sup> ions to try to maintain homeostasis. As carbon dioxide concentrations continue to increase (<i>Pa</i>CO<sub>2</sub> &gt; 45 mmHg), a condition known as respiratory acidosis occurs. The body tries to maintain homeostasis by increasing the respiratory rate, a condition known as tachypnea. This allows much more carbon dioxide to escape the body through the lungs, thus increasing the pH by having less carbonic acid. If a person is in a critical setting and intubated, one must increase the number of breaths mechanically.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Respiratory_alkalosis" title="Respiratory alkalosis">Respiratory alkalosis</a> (<i>Pa</i> CO<sub>2</sub> &lt; 35 mmHg) occurs when there is too little carbon dioxide in the blood. This may be due to hyperventilation or else excessive breaths given via a mechanical ventilator in a critical care setting. The action to be taken is to calm the person and try to reduce the number of breaths being taken to normalize the pH. The respiratory pathway tries to compensate for the change in pH in a matter of 2–4 hours. If this is not enough, the metabolic pathway takes place.
</p><p>Under normal conditions, the <a href="/wiki/Henderson%E2%80%93Hasselbalch_equation" title="Henderson–Hasselbalch equation">Henderson–Hasselbalch equation</a> will give the blood pH

</math></span><img alt="{\displaystyle {\ce {pH}}=6.1+\log _{10}\left({\frac {[{\ce {HCO3^-}}]}{0.03\times Pa{\ce {CO2}}}}\right)}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3884b205575677308f0e22448b2ee3f465dee15b" style="vertical-align: -3.171ex; width:35.802ex; height:7.509ex;"/></span></dd></dl>
<p>where:
</p>
<ul><li>6.1 is the <a href="/wiki/Acid_dissociation_constant" title="Acid dissociation constant">acid dissociation constant</a> (p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub>) of <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>) at normal body temperature</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">HCO<sub>3</sub></a><sup>–</sup> is the concentration of bicarbonate in the blood in mEq/L</li>
<li><i>Pa</i>CO<sub>2</sub> is the partial pressure of carbon dioxide in the arterial blood in torr</li></ul>
<p>The kidney and the liver are two main organs responsible for the metabolic homeostasis of pH. Bicarbonate is a base that helps to accept excess hydrogen ions whenever there is acidaemia. However, this mechanism is slower than the respiratory pathway and may take from a few hours to 3 days to take effect. In acidaemia, the bicarbonate levels rise, so that they can neutralize the excess acid, while the contrary happens when there is alkalaemia. Thus when an arterial blood gas test reveals, for example, an elevated bicarbonate, the problem has been present for a couple of days, and metabolic compensation took place over a blood acedemia problem.
</p><p>In general, it is much easier to correct acute pH derangement by adjusting respiration. Metabolic compensations take place at a much later stage. However, in a critical setting, a person with a normal pH, a high CO<sub>2</sub>, and a high bicarbonate means that, although there is a high carbon dioxide level, there is metabolic compensation. As a result, one must be careful as to not artificially adjust breaths to lower the carbon dioxide. In such case, lowering the carbon dioxide abruptly means that the bicarbonate will be in excess and will cause a metabolic alkalosis. In such a case, carbon dioxide levels should be slowly diminished.
</p>
