<p><b>Perfluorotributylamine</b> (<b>PFTBA</b>), also referred to as FC43, is a colorless liquid with the formula N(C<sub>4</sub>F<sub>9</sub>)<sub>3</sub>.  The compound consists of three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Butyl" title="Butyl">butyl</a> groups connected to an <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amine</a> center, in which all of the hydrogen atoms have been replaced with <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a>. The compound is produced for the electronics industry, along with other perfluoroalkylamines.  Unlike ordinary amines, perfluoroamines are nonbasic.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Perfluorotributylamine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is prepared by electrofluorination of <a href="/wiki/Tributylamine" title="Tributylamine">tributylamine</a> using <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">hydrogen fluoride</a> as solvent and source of fluorine:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>N(C<sub>4</sub>H<sub>9</sub>)<sub>3</sub>  +  27 HF   →   N(C<sub>4</sub>F<sub>9</sub>)<sub>3</sub>  +  27 H<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Perfluorotributylamine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The compound has two commercial uses.
It is used as an ingredient in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluosol" title="Fluosol">Fluosol</a>, artificial blood.  This application exploits the high solubility of oxygen and carbon dioxide in the solvent, as well as the low viscosity and toxicity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  It is also a component of <a href="/wiki/Fluorinert" title="Fluorinert">Fluorinert</a> coolant liquids.  CPUs of some computers are immersed in this liquid to facilitate cooling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Niche">Niche</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Perfluorotributylamine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Niche">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The compound is used as a calibrant<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Gas_chromatography" title="Gas chromatography">gas chromatography</a> when the <a href="/wiki/Analytical_technique" title="Analytical technique">analytical technique</a> uses <a href="/wiki/Mass_spectrometry" title="Mass spectrometry">mass spectrometry</a> as a detector to identify and quantify chemical compounds in gases or liquids. When undergoing ionization in the mass spectrometer, the compound decomposes in a repeatable pattern to form fragments of specific masses, which can be used to tune the mass response and accuracy of the mass spectrometer. Most commonly used ions are those with approximate mass of 69, 131, 219, 414 and 502 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_mass_units" title="Atomic mass units">atomic mass units</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Perfluorotributylamine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fluorofluids are generally of very low toxicity, so much that they have been evaluated as synthetic blood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impact">Environmental impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Perfluorotributylamine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Environmental impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> with warming properties more than 7,000 times that of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> over a 100 year period,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and, as such, is the most potent greenhouse gas ever discovered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  Its concentration in the atmosphere is approximately 0.18 parts per trillion.  The compound can persist in the atmosphere for up to 500 years. <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_hexafluoride" title="Sulfur hexafluoride">Sulfur hexafluoride</a>, however, has a GWP of 23,900,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> which would make it much more powerful.
</p>
