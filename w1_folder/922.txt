<p><b>Magnesite</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> with the chemical formula <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">Mg</a><a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">C</a><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a><sub>3</sub> (<a href="/wiki/Magnesium_carbonate" title="Magnesium carbonate">magnesium carbonate</a>). Mixed crystals of <a href="/wiki/Iron(II)_carbonate" title="Iron(II) carbonate">iron(II) carbonate</a> and magnesite (mixed crystals known as <a href="/wiki/Ankerite" title="Ankerite">ankerite</a>) possess a layered structure: <a href="/wiki/Monolayer" title="Monolayer">monolayers</a> of carbonate groups alternate with magnesium monolayers as well as iron(II) carbonate monolayers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">Manganese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> may also occur in small amounts.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnesite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Magnesite occurs as veins in and an alteration product of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultramafic_rocks" title="Ultramafic rocks">ultramafic rocks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serpentinite" title="Serpentinite">serpentinite</a> and other magnesium rich rock types in both contact and regional <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphic</a> terrains. These magnesites are often <a href="/wiki/Cryptocrystalline" title="Cryptocrystalline">cryptocrystalline</a> and contain silica in the form of <a href="/wiki/Opal" title="Opal">opal</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chert" title="Chert">chert</a>.
</p><p>Magnesite is also present within the <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">regolith</a> above ultramafic rocks as a secondary carbonate within soil and <a href="/wiki/Subsoil" title="Subsoil">subsoil</a>, where it is deposited as a consequence of dissolution of magnesium-bearing minerals by carbon dioxide in groundwaters.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnesite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Magnesite can be formed via <a href="/wiki/Talc_carbonate" title="Talc carbonate">talc carbonate</a> <a href="/wiki/Metasomatism" title="Metasomatism">metasomatism</a> of <a href="/wiki/Peridotite" title="Peridotite">peridotite</a> and other ultramafic rocks. Magnesite is formed via carbonation of <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">olivine</a> in the presence of water and carbon dioxide at elevated temperatures and high pressures typical of the <a href="/wiki/Greenschist#Greenschist_facies" title="Greenschist">greenschist facies</a>.
</p><p>Magnesite can also be formed via the carbonation of magnesium <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serpentine_group" title="Serpentine group">serpentine</a> (lizardite) via the following <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_reaction" title="Metamorphic reaction">reaction</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Mg<sub>3</sub>Si<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>(OH)<sub>4</sub> + 3 CO<sub>2</sub> → Mg<sub>3</sub>Si<sub>4</sub>O<sub>10</sub>(OH)<sub>2</sub> + 3 MgCO<sub>3</sub> + 3 H<sub>2</sub>O.</dd></dl>
<p>However, when performing this reaction in the laboratory, the trihydrated form of <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_carbonate" title="Magnesium carbonate">magnesium carbonate</a> (nesquehonite) will form at room temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This very observation led to the postulation of a "dehydration barrier" being involved in the low-temperature formation of anhydrous magnesium carbonate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Laboratory experiments with <a href="/wiki/Formamide" title="Formamide">formamide</a>, a liquid resembling water, have shown how no such dehydration barrier can be involved. The fundamental difficulty to nucleate anhydrous magnesium carbonate remains when using this non-aqueous solution. Not cation dehydration, but rather the spatial configuration of carbonate anions creates the barrier in the low-temperature nucleation of magnesite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Magnesite has been found in modern sediments, caves and soils. Its low-temperature (around 40 °C [104 °F]) formation is known to require alternations between precipitation and dissolution intervals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mineraly.sk_-_magnezit.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="200" data-file-width="340" height="129" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Mineraly.sk_-_magnezit.jpg/220px-Mineraly.sk_-_magnezit.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Mineraly.sk_-_magnezit.jpg/330px-Mineraly.sk_-_magnezit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/30/Mineraly.sk_-_magnezit.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mineraly.sk_-_magnezit.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Magnesite in a natural form (from <a href="/wiki/Luben%C3%ADk" title="Lubeník">Lubeník</a> in Slovakia)</div></div></div>
<p>Magnesite was detected in <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">meteorite</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ALH84001" title="ALH84001">ALH84001</a> and on planet <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> itself. Magnesite was identified on Mars using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infra-red_spectroscopy" title="Infra-red spectroscopy">infra-red spectroscopy</a> from satellite orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Controversy still exists over the temperature of formation of this magnesite. Low-temperature formation has been suggested for the magnesite from the Mars derived ALH84001 meteorite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The low-temperature formation of magnesite might well be of significance toward large-scale <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">carbon sequestration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Magnesium-rich <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">olivine</a> (<a href="/wiki/Forsterite" title="Forsterite">forsterite</a>) favors production of magnesite from peridotite. Iron-rich olivine (<a href="/wiki/Fayalite" title="Fayalite">fayalite</a>) favors production of magnetite-magnesite-silica compositions.
</p><p>Magnesite can also be formed by way of metasomatism in <a href="/wiki/Skarn" title="Skarn">skarn</a> deposits, in <a href="/wiki/Dolomite" title="Dolomite">dolomitic</a> <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestones</a>, associated with <a href="/wiki/Wollastonite" title="Wollastonite">wollastonite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Periclase" title="Periclase">periclase</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Talc" title="Talc">talc</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnesite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dyed_magnesite_beads.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2536" data-file-width="1892" height="228" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Dyed_magnesite_beads.jpg/170px-Dyed_magnesite_beads.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Dyed_magnesite_beads.jpg/255px-Dyed_magnesite_beads.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Dyed_magnesite_beads.jpg/340px-Dyed_magnesite_beads.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dyed_magnesite_beads.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dyed and polished magnesite beads</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Magnesite_of_Salem.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1836" data-file-width="3264" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Magnesite_of_Salem.jpg/220px-Magnesite_of_Salem.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Magnesite_of_Salem.jpg/330px-Magnesite_of_Salem.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Magnesite_of_Salem.jpg/440px-Magnesite_of_Salem.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Magnesite_of_Salem.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Magnesite of <a href="/wiki/Salem,_Tamil_Nadu" title="Salem, Tamil Nadu">Salem</a></div></div></div>
<p>Similar to the production of lime, magnesite can be burned in the presence of charcoal to produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MgO" title="MgO">MgO</a>, which, in the form of a mineral, is known as <a href="/wiki/Periclase" title="Periclase">periclase</a>. Large quantities of magnesite are burnt to make <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_oxide" title="Magnesium oxide">magnesium oxide</a>: an important <a href="/wiki/Refractory" title="Refractory">refractory</a> material used as a lining in <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnaces</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kiln" title="Kiln">kilns</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incinerators" title="Incinerators">incinerators</a>.  Calcination temperatures determine the reactivity of resulting oxide products and the classifications of light burnt and dead burnt refer to the surface area and resulting reactivity of the product, typically as determined by an industry metric of the iodine number.  'Light burnt' product generally refers to calcination commencing at 450 °C and proceeding to an upper limit of 900 °C - which results in good surface area and reactivity.  Above 900 °C, the material loses its reactive crystalline structure and reverts to the chemically inert 'dead-burnt' product- which is preferred for use in refractory materials such as furnace linings.
</p><p>Magnesite can also be used as a binder in flooring material (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnesite_screed" title="Magnesite screed">magnesite screed</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Furthermore, it is being used as a catalyst and filler in the production of <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_rubber" title="Synthetic rubber">synthetic rubber</a> and in the preparation of magnesium chemicals and fertilizers.
</p><p>In fire assay, magnesite cupels can be used for <a href="/wiki/Cupellation" title="Cupellation">cupellation</a> as the magnesite cupel will resist the high temperatures involved.
</p><p>Magnesite can be cut, drilled, and polished to form beads that are used in jewelry-making. Magnesite beads can be dyed into a broad spectrum of bold colors, including a light blue color that mimics the appearance of <a href="/wiki/Turquoise" title="Turquoise">turquoise</a>.
</p><p>Research is proceeding to evaluate the practicality of sequestering the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> in magnesite on a large scale<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Occupational_safety_and_health">Occupational safety and health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnesite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Occupational safety and health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>People can be exposed to magnesite in the workplace by inhaling it, skin contact, and eye contact.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="USA">USA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnesite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: USA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">Occupational Safety and Health Administration</a> (OSHA) has set the legal limit (<a href="/wiki/Permissible_exposure_limit" title="Permissible exposure limit">permissible exposure limit</a>) for magnesite exposure in the workplace as 15 mg/m<sup>3</sup> total exposure and 5 mg/m<sup>3</sup> respiratory exposure over an 8-hour workday. The <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health" title="National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health">National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health</a> (NIOSH) has set a <a href="/wiki/Recommended_exposure_limit" title="Recommended exposure limit">recommended exposure limit</a> (REL) of 10 mg/m<sup>3</sup> total exposure and 5 mg/m<sup>3</sup> respiratory exposure over an 8-hour workday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p><br/>
</p>
