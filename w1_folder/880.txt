<p>A <b>formation</b> or <b>geological formation</b> is the fundamental unit of <a href="/wiki/Lithostratigraphy" title="Lithostratigraphy">lithostratigraphy</a>. A formation consists of a certain amount of rock <a href="/wiki/Stratum" title="Stratum">strata</a> that have a comparable <a href="/wiki/Lithology" title="Lithology">lithology</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sedimentary_facies" title="Sedimentary facies">facies</a> or other similar properties. Formations are not defined by the <a href="/wiki/Thickness_(geology)" title="Thickness (geology)">thickness</a> of their rock strata; therefore the thickness of different formations can vary widely.
</p><p>The concept of formally defined layers or strata is central to the geologic discipline of <a href="/wiki/Stratigraphy" title="Stratigraphy">stratigraphy</a>. <a href="/wiki/Group_(stratigraphy)" title="Group (stratigraphy)">Groups</a> of strata are divided into formations, which are divided into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geological_unit#Member" title="Geological unit">members</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usefulness_of_formations">Usefulness of formations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geological_formation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usefulness of formations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The definition and recognition of formations allow <a href="/wiki/Geologist" title="Geologist">geologists</a> to correlate geologic strata across wide distances between <a href="/wiki/Outcrop" title="Outcrop">outcrops</a> and exposures of rock <a href="/wiki/Stratum" title="Stratum">strata</a>. Formations were at first described as the essential <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geologic_time" title="Geologic time">geologic time</a> markers, based on their relative ages and the <a href="/wiki/Law_of_superposition" title="Law of superposition">law of superposition</a>. The divisions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geological_time_scale" title="Geological time scale">geological time scale</a> were described and put in chronological order by the geologists and stratigraphers of the 18th and 19th centuries.
</p><p>The lithology of a rock is a description of its readily visible physical characteristics. Modern geology prefers to use lithology, that it an examination of the visible features of the component rocks, to identify discrete formations.
</p><p>Geologic formations are divided into the broad categories of: <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rock</a> layers; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rocks" title="Metamorphic rocks">metamorphic rocks</a>; and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic" title="Volcanic">volcanic</a> rocks. Intrusive igneous rocks are generally not considered to be formations.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Defining_lithostratigraphic_formations">Defining lithostratigraphic formations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geological_formation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Defining lithostratigraphic formations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SEUtahStrat.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1794" data-file-width="3161" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/SEUtahStrat.JPG/300px-SEUtahStrat.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/SEUtahStrat.JPG/450px-SEUtahStrat.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/SEUtahStrat.JPG/600px-SEUtahStrat.JPG 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SEUtahStrat.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">Permian</a> through <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> strata of the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Plateau" title="Colorado Plateau">Colorado Plateau</a> area of southeastern <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a>  demonstrate the principles of <a href="/wiki/Stratigraphy" title="Stratigraphy">stratigraphy</a>.  These strata make up much of the famous prominent rock formations in widely spaced protected areas such as <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Reef_National_Park" title="Capitol Reef National Park">Capitol Reef National Park</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canyonlands_National_Park" title="Canyonlands National Park">Canyonlands National Park</a>.  From top to bottom: Rounded tan domes of the <a href="/wiki/Navajo_Sandstone" title="Navajo Sandstone">Navajo Sandstone</a>, layered red <a href="/wiki/Kayenta_Formation" title="Kayenta Formation">Kayenta Formation</a>, cliff-forming, vertically jointed, red <a href="/wiki/Wingate_Sandstone" title="Wingate Sandstone">Wingate Sandstone</a>, slope-forming, purplish <a href="/wiki/Chinle_Formation" title="Chinle Formation">Chinle Formation</a>, layered, lighter-red <a href="/wiki/Moenkopi_Formation" title="Moenkopi Formation">Moenkopi Formation</a>, and white, layered <a href="/wiki/Cutler_Formation" title="Cutler Formation">Cutler Formation</a> sandstone.  Picture from <a href="/wiki/Glen_Canyon_National_Recreation_Area" title="Glen Canyon National Recreation Area">Glen Canyon National Recreation Area</a>, Utah.</div></div></div>
<p>The contrast in lithology between formations required to justify their establishment varies with the complexity of the geology of a region. Formations must be able to be delineated at the scale of geologic mapping normally practiced in the region; the thickness of formations may range from less than a meter to several thousand meters. Geologic formations are typically named after the geographic area in which they were first described.
</p><p>Strictly, formations cannot be defined by any criteria other than primary lithology.  Nevertheless, it is often useful to define <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geologic_unit" title="Geologic unit">biostratigraphic units</a> on <a href="/wiki/Paleontology" title="Paleontology">paleontological</a> criteria, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geologic_unit" title="Geologic unit">chronostratigraphic units</a> on the age of the rocks, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geologic_unit" title="Geologic unit">chemostratigraphic units</a> on geochemical criteria.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses_of_the_term">Other uses of the term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geological_formation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other uses of the term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term "formation" is often used informally to refer to a specific grouping of rocks, such as those encountered within a certain depth range in an oil well
</p><p>"Formation" is also used informally to describe the odd shapes (forms) that rocks acquire through erosional or depositional processes. Such a formation is abandoned when it is no longer affected by the geologic agent that produced it. Some well-known <a href="/wiki/Cave" title="Cave">cave</a> formations include <a href="/wiki/Stalactite" title="Stalactite">stalactites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stalagmite" title="Stalagmite">stalagmites</a>.
</p>
