<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Carbon dioxide cleaning</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Engineers_Clean_JWST_Secondary_Reflector_with_Carbon_Dioxide_Snow.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2989" data-file-width="4546" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Engineers_Clean_JWST_Secondary_Reflector_with_Carbon_Dioxide_Snow.jpg/330px-Engineers_Clean_JWST_Secondary_Reflector_with_Carbon_Dioxide_Snow.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Engineers_Clean_JWST_Secondary_Reflector_with_Carbon_Dioxide_Snow.jpg/495px-Engineers_Clean_JWST_Secondary_Reflector_with_Carbon_Dioxide_Snow.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Engineers_Clean_JWST_Secondary_Reflector_with_Carbon_Dioxide_Snow.jpg/660px-Engineers_Clean_JWST_Secondary_Reflector_with_Carbon_Dioxide_Snow.jpg 2x" width="330"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Engineers_Clean_JWST_Secondary_Reflector_with_Carbon_Dioxide_Snow.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Engineers in a <a href="/wiki/Cleanroom" title="Cleanroom">cleanroom</a>, using CO<sub>2</sub> snow to clean a gold-coated test mirror for the <a href="/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope" title="James Webb Space Telescope">James Webb Space Telescope</a></div></div></div>
<p><b>Carbon dioxide cleaning</b> (<b>CO<sub>2</sub> cleaning</b>) comprises a family of methods for <a href="/wiki/Parts_cleaning" title="Parts cleaning">parts cleaning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sterilization_(microbiology)" title="Sterilization (microbiology)">sterilization</a>, using <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> in its various <a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">phases</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-co2clean-methods_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-co2clean-methods-1">[1]</a></sup> It is often preferred for use on delicate surfaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-co2clean-main_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-co2clean-main-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coldjet-main_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-coldjet-main-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherman_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sherman-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>275</span></sup> CO<sub>2</sub> cleaning has found application in the <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_manufacturer" title="Aerospace manufacturer">aerospace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Automotive_industry" title="Automotive industry">automotive</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electronics_manufacturing_services" title="Electronics manufacturing services">electronics</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Health_care_industry" title="Health care industry">medical</a>, and other industries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-co2clean-applications_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-co2clean-applications-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coldjet-industries_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-coldjet-industries-6">[6]</a></sup> Carbon dioxide snow cleaning has been used to remove particles and organic residues from metals, <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glasses</a>, and other materials, and from surfaces including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hard_drive" title="Hard drive">hard drives</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)" title="Lens (optics)">optical surfaces</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherman_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sherman-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>270</span></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_cleaning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>CO<sub>2</sub> cleaning has found application in many industries and technical areas, including aerospace, automotive, electronics, medical, manufacturing, basic and applied research, and <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">optics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-co2clean-applications_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-co2clean-applications-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coldjet-industries_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-coldjet-industries-6">[6]</a></sup> The different carbon dioxide cleaning methods can remove gross contamination, <a href="/wiki/Paint" title="Paint">paint</a>, <a href="/wiki/Overlayer" title="Overlayer">overlayers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grease_(lubricant)" title="Grease (lubricant)">grease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fingerprint" title="Fingerprint">fingerprints</a>, particles down to nanometers in size, <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic</a> <a href="/wiki/Residue_(chemistry)" title="Residue (chemistry)">residues</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> residues.  Materials cleaned include metals, polymers, ceramics, and glasses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherman_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sherman-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>270</span></sup> The key limitation is that the contamination must be on the surface, not buried within the material.  Porous materials are not good candidates for pellets or snow, but can be cleaned using liquid or supercritical CO<sub>2</sub>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods">Methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_cleaning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carbon dioxide cleaning refers to several different methods for parts cleaning, making use of all phases of CO<sub>2</sub>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> basic methods include solid <a href="/wiki/Dry_ice" title="Dry ice">dry ice</a> pellets, liquid CO<sub>2</sub>, CO<sub>2</sub> snow (a hybrid method), and <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_fluid" title="Supercritical fluid">supercritical</a> CO<sub>2</sub>. The different forms of CO<sub>2</sub> cleaning can clean many types of objects, from large generators to small and delicate parts, including hard drives and optics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherman_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Sherman-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>270</span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pellets">Pellets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_cleaning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pellets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bakerycleaning.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="220" data-file-width="220" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/a/ad/Bakerycleaning.jpg" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bakerycleaning.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dry-ice blasting used to clean bakery equipment</div></div></div>
<p>In pellet cleaning ("<a href="/wiki/Dry-ice_blasting" title="Dry-ice blasting">dry-ice blasting</a>"), relatively large pellets of solid CO<sub>2</sub> are fired at the surface to be cleaned. These pellets impinge on the surface, mechanically dislodging contaminant particles. Pellet cleaning is only appropriate for surfaces which can withstand significant impacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-co2clean-methods_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-co2clean-methods-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherman_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Sherman-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>276</span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Snow_cleaning">Snow cleaning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_cleaning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Snow cleaning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In CO<sub>2</sub> snow cleaning, compressed liquid or gaseous <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> is expelled from a nozzle, condensing into a mixture of solid particles and gas, which impact the surface to be cleaned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-co2clean-methods_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-co2clean-methods-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherman_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-Sherman-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>276</span></sup> Jet velocities are frequently supersonic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coldjet-how_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-coldjet-how-8">[8]</a></sup> Snow cleaning works by a combination of momentum transfer (mechanically dislodging contaminant particles) and <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvent</a> action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-co2clean-methods_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-co2clean-methods-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherman_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-Sherman-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>273</span></sup> The CO<sub>2</sub> <a href="/wiki/Sublimation_(phase_transition)" title="Sublimation (phase transition)">sublimates</a> on contact, increasing in volume up to 800 times, thereby generating pressure to sweep particles away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coldjet-how_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-coldjet-how-8">[8]</a></sup> The CO<sub>2</sub> also dissolves hydrocarbon contaminants, and its low temperature <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/embrittlement" title="wikt:embrittlement">embrittles</a> residues such as fingerprints, making them easier to blow away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-co2clean-main_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-co2clean-main-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fraunhofer-gesellschaft_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-fraunhofer-gesellschaft-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Snow cleaning has found application in the <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_manufacturer" title="Aerospace manufacturer">aeronautical</a>, <a href="/wiki/Automotive_industry" title="Automotive industry">automotive</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medical_device" title="Medical device">medical</a>, <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">optical</a>, <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor_device_fabrication" title="Semiconductor device fabrication">semiconductor</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Space_industry" title="Space industry">space</a> industries. It can provide a gentle cleaning, appropriate for delicate surfaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-co2clean-main_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-co2clean-main-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherman_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-Sherman-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>270</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fraunhofer-gesellschaft_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-fraunhofer-gesellschaft-9">[9]</a></sup> The effectiveness of carbon dioxide snow cleaning has been demonstrated via light <a href="/wiki/Microscopy" title="Microscopy">microscopy</a>, particle counting, <a href="/wiki/Scanning_electron_microscope" title="Scanning electron microscope">scanning electron microscopy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microprobe" title="Microprobe">microprobing</a>, <a href="/wiki/X-ray_photoelectron_spectroscopy" title="X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy">X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic-force_microscopy" title="Atomic-force microscopy">atomic-force microscopy</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-co2clean-afm_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-co2clean-afm-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chernoff_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chernoff-11">[11]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_spectroscopy" title="Mass spectroscopy">mass spectroscopy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherman_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-Sherman-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>279</span></sup>
</p><p>Equipment costs for a carbon dioxide snow cleaning system can range from US$1500 for a basic system to $50,000 for a high-end automated unit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherman_4-9"><a href="#cite_note-Sherman-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>292</span></sup> Material costs are comparatively low, although ultra-pure CO<sub>2</sub> must often be used to avoid the introduction of new contaminants.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Supercritical_fluid">Supercritical fluid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_cleaning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Supercritical fluid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At temperatures and pressures above its <a href="/wiki/Critical_point_(thermodynamics)" title="Critical point (thermodynamics)">critical point</a>, CO<sub>2</sub> can be maintained as a <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_fluid" title="Supercritical fluid">supercritical fluid</a>, exhibiting extremely low <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> and high <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvency</a>. To apply this method, parts to be cleaned are enclosed in a pressure vessel that is then filled with supercritical CO<sub>2</sub>. This method is appropriate for small and delicate parts such as microelectronics, and is not ideal for particulate removal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ober_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ober-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-co2clean-methods_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-co2clean-methods-1">[1]</a></sup> Aside from cleaning, applications of <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_carbon_dioxide" title="Supercritical carbon dioxide">supercritical carbon dioxide</a> include targeted chemical <a href="/wiki/Extraction_(chemistry)" title="Extraction (chemistry)">supercritical fluid extraction</a> and materials processing.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Liquid_CO2_washing">Liquid CO<sub>2</sub> washing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_cleaning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Liquid CO2 washing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Liquid CO<sub>2</sub> washing, like supercritical fluid CO<sub>2</sub> washing, relies on the high solvent power of CO<sub>2</sub>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherman_4-10"><a href="#cite_note-Sherman-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>275</span></sup> but at lower temperatures and pressures, the latter making it simpler to implement. Because liquid CO<sub>2</sub> does not have the solvent power of supercritical fluid, agitation and <a href="/wiki/Surfactant" title="Surfactant">surfactants</a> may be added to improve the effectiveness of the method.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-co2clean-methods_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-co2clean-methods-1">[1]</a></sup>  Liquid CO<sub>2</sub> has been used in <a href="/wiki/Dry_cleaning" title="Dry cleaning">dry cleaning</a> and machined parts <a href="/wiki/Solvent_degreasing" title="Solvent degreasing">degreasing</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_cleaning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carbon dioxide cleaning was contemplated in the 1930s, and the "pellet" approach was developed in the 1970s by E.E. Rice, C.H. Franklin, and C.C. Wong.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherman_4-11"><a href="#cite_note-Sherman-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>276</span></sup>
</p><p>The introduction of CO<sub>2</sub> snow cleaning, with its ability to remove sub-micron-scale particles, is credited to Stuart Hoenig of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Arizona" title="University of Arizona">University of Arizona</a>, who first published on the topic in 1985-1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherman_4-12"><a href="#cite_note-Sherman-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>277</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-US5125979_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-US5125979-13">[13]</a></sup> Hoenig traveled the US to demonstrate the technology, eventually attracting the interest of <a href="/wiki/The_BOC_Group" title="The BOC Group">The BOC Group</a>, which developed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venturi_tube" title="Venturi tube">Venturi nozzles</a> for the process, and <a href="/wiki/Hughes_Aircraft_Company" title="Hughes Aircraft Company">Hughes Aircraft</a>, which developed straight nozzles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-co2clean-faqs_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-co2clean-faqs-14">[14]</a></sup> CO<sub>2</sub> snow cleaning was further developed by the <a href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_Society" title="Fraunhofer Society">Fraunhofer Institute for Manufacturing Engineering and Automation IPA</a>, for the purpose of removing <a href="/wiki/Paint" title="Paint">paint</a> from aircraft <a href="/wiki/Fuselage" title="Fuselage">fuselages</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fraunhofer-gesellschaft_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-fraunhofer-gesellschaft-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Nozzle design is the most significant factor in carbon dioxide snow cleaning performance, affecting the size and velocity of the dry ice particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherman_4-13"><a href="#cite_note-Sherman-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>277–278</span></sup> Variations in nozzle design have been developed by W.H. Whitlock, L.L. Layden, Applied Surface Technologies, and Sierra Systems Group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherman_4-14"><a href="#cite_note-Sherman-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>277</span></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Issues">Issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_cleaning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_cleaning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CO<sub>2</sub> cleaning may present certain safety risks. If the process is used to remove hazardous materials, precautions must be taken to avoid exposure to these materials in the vent stream. Because the CO<sub>2</sub> stream is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogenic" title="Cryogenic">cryogenic</a>, it may cause injury with direct skin contact. In addition, care must be taken to prevent the concentration of carbon dioxide in the work area from <a href="/wiki/Hypercapnia" title="Hypercapnia">exceeding safe levels</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherman_4-15"><a href="#cite_note-Sherman-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>272</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-co2clean-safety_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-co2clean-safety-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contamination">Contamination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_cleaning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Contamination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some commercial grades of carbon dioxide may contain traces of heavy <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a>, which can be left behind on the surface being cleaned. Abrasive particles originating in the cleaning equipment itself may need to be filtered out as well. The low temperature of the carbon dioxide stream can also induce moisture <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensation</a> on the part, which may be mitigated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_plates" title="Hot plates">hot plates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heat_gun" title="Heat gun">heat guns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Infrared_lamp" title="Infrared lamp">heat lamps</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Dry_box" title="Dry box">dry boxes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherman_4-16"><a href="#cite_note-Sherman-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>292–294</span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Static_charge">Static charge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_cleaning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Static charge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ionization" title="Ionization">Ionization</a> caused by the flowing gas can result in potentially damaging <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Static_charge" title="Static charge">static charge</a> buildup on non-<a href="/wiki/Electrical_resistivity_and_conductivity" title="Electrical resistivity and conductivity">conductive</a> parts. This can be mitigated by <a href="/wiki/Ground_(electricity)" title="Ground (electricity)">grounding</a> or positive ionization sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherman_4-17"><a href="#cite_note-Sherman-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>294</span></sup>
</p>
