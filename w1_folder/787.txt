<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Evapotranspiration</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Surface_water_cycle.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="380" data-file-width="250" height="395" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Surface_water_cycle.svg/260px-Surface_water_cycle.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Surface_water_cycle.svg/390px-Surface_water_cycle.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Surface_water_cycle.svg/520px-Surface_water_cycle.svg.png 2x" width="260"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Surface_water_cycle.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Water cycle of the Earth's surface, showing the individual components of transpiration and evaporation that make up evapotranspiration. Other closely related processes shown are <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">runoff</a> and <a href="/wiki/Groundwater_recharge" title="Groundwater recharge">groundwater recharge</a>.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Evapotranspiration</b> (<b>ET</b>) is the <a href="/wiki/Summation" title="Summation">sum</a> of <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plant</a> <a href="/wiki/Transpiration" title="Transpiration">transpiration</a> from the Earth's land and ocean surface to the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmosphere</a>. Evaporation accounts for the movement of water to the air from sources such as the <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canopy_interception" title="Canopy interception">canopy interception</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waterbody" title="Waterbody">waterbodies</a>. Transpiration accounts for the movement of water within a <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plant</a> and the subsequent loss of water as vapor through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stomata" title="Stomata">stomata</a> in its <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a>. Evapotranspiration is an important part of the <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">water cycle</a>.  An element (such as a tree) that contributes to evapotranspiration can be called an <b>evapotranspirator</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Potential_evaporation" title="Potential evaporation"><b>Potential evapotranspiration</b> (<b>PET</b>)</a> is a representation of the environmental demand for evapotranspiration and represents the evapotranspiration rate of a short green crop (grass), completely shading the ground, of uniform height and with adequate water status in the soil profile. It is a reflection of the <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> available to evaporate water, and of the <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> available to transport the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_vapour" title="Water vapour">water vapour</a> from the ground up into the lower atmosphere. Often a value for the potential evapotranspiration is calculated at a nearby climatic station on a reference surface, conventionally short grass. This value is called the reference evapotranspiration (ET<sub>0</sub>). Actual evapotranspiration is said to equal potential evapotranspiration when there is ample water.  Some US states utilize a full cover alfalfa reference crop that is 0.5 m in height, rather than the short green grass reference, due to the higher value of ET from the alfalfa reference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extension.uidaho.edu_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-extension.uidaho.edu-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_cycle">Water cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evapotranspiration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Water cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Evapotranspiration is a significant water loss from <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">drainage basins</a>. Types of vegetation and land use significantly affect evapotranspiration, and therefore the amount of water leaving a drainage basin. Because water transpired through leaves comes from the roots, plants with deep reaching roots can more constantly transpire water. <a href="/wiki/Herbaceous_plant" title="Herbaceous plant">Herbaceous plants</a> generally transpire less than <a href="/wiki/Woody_plant" title="Woody plant">woody plants</a> because they usually have less extensive foliage. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conifer" title="Conifer">Conifer</a> forests tend to have higher rates of evapotranspiration than <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a> forests, particularly in the dormant and early spring seasons. This is primarily due to the enhanced amount of precipitation intercepted and evaporated by conifer foliage during these periods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Factors that affect evapotranspiration include the plant's growth stage or level of maturity, percentage of soil cover, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a>.  Isotope measurements indicate transpiration is the larger component of evapotranspiration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Through evapotranspiration, forests reduce water yield, except in unique ecosystems called <a href="/wiki/Cloud_forest" title="Cloud forest">cloud forests</a>. Trees in cloud forests collect the liquid water in fog or low clouds onto their surface, which drips down to the ground. These trees still contribute to evapotranspiration, but often collect more water than they evaporate or transpire.
</p><p>In areas that are not irrigated, actual evapotranspiration is usually no greater than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>, with some buffer in time depending on the soil's ability to hold water. It will usually be less because some water will be lost due to <a href="/wiki/Percolation" title="Percolation">percolation</a> or surface runoff. An exception is areas with high <a href="/wiki/Water_table" title="Water table">water tables</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Capillary_action" title="Capillary action">capillary action</a> can cause water from the groundwater to rise through the soil matrix to the surface. If potential evapotranspiration is greater than the actual precipitation, then soil will dry out, unless <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> is used.
</p><p>Evapotranspiration can never be greater than <a href="#Potential_evapotranspiration">PET</a>, but can be lower if there is not enough water to be evaporated or plants are unable to transpire readily.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Estimating_evapotranspiration">Estimating evapotranspiration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evapotranspiration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Estimating evapotranspiration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Evapotranspiration can be measured or estimated using several methods.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indirect_methods">Indirect methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evapotranspiration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Indirect methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pan_evaporation" title="Pan evaporation">Pan evaporation</a> data can be used to estimate lake evaporation, but transpiration and evaporation of intercepted rain on vegetation are unknown. There are three general approaches to estimate evapotranspiration indirectly.
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_balance">Energy balance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evapotranspiration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Energy balance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A third methodology to estimate the actual evapotranspiration is the use of the energy balance.

</p><p>where λE is the energy needed to change the phase of water from liquid to gas, R<sub>n</sub> is the net radiation, G is the soil heat flux and H is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sensible_heat_flux" title="Sensible heat flux">sensible heat flux</a>. Using instruments like a <a href="/wiki/Scintillometer" title="Scintillometer">scintillometer</a>, soil heat flux plates or radiation meters, the components of the energy balance can be calculated and the energy available for actual evapotranspiration can be solved.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/SEBAL" title="SEBAL">SEBAL</a> and <a href="/wiki/METRIC" title="METRIC">METRIC</a> algorithms solve the energy balance at the earth's surface using satellite imagery. This allows for both actual and potential evapotranspiration to be calculated on a pixel-by-pixel basis. Evapotranspiration is a key indicator for water management and irrigation performance. SEBAL and METRIC can map these key indicators in time and space, for days, weeks or years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Experimental_methods_for_measuring_evapotranspiration">Experimental methods for measuring evapotranspiration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evapotranspiration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Experimental methods for measuring evapotranspiration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One method for measuring evapotranspiration is with a weighing <a href="/wiki/Lysimeter" title="Lysimeter">lysimeter</a>.  The weight of a soil column is measured continuously and the change in storage of water in the soil is modeled by the change in weight.  The change in weight is converted to units of length using the surface area of the weighing lysimeter and the unit weight of water.  evapotranspiration is computed as the change in weight plus rainfall minus percolation.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Remote_sensing">Remote sensing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evapotranspiration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Remote sensing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In recent decades, estimating evapotranspiration has been improved by advances in remote sensing, particularly in agricultural studies. However, quantifying evapotranspiration from mixed vegetation environs, particularly urban parklands, is still challenging because of the heterogeneity of plant species, canopy covers and microclimates and because the methodology is costly.  Different remote sensing-based approaches for estimating evapotranspiration have various advantages and disadvantages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nouri2015_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nouri2015-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Eddy_covariance">Eddy covariance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evapotranspiration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Eddy covariance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Eddy_covariance" title="Eddy covariance">Eddy covariance</a></div>
<p>The most direct method of measuring evapotranspiration is with the <a href="/wiki/Eddy_covariance" title="Eddy covariance">eddy covariance</a> technique in which fast fluctuations of vertical wind speed are correlated with fast fluctuations in atmospheric water <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vapor_density" title="Vapor density">vapor density</a>. This directly estimates the transfer of water vapor (evapotranspiration) from the land (or canopy) surface to the atmosphere.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrometeorological_equations">Hydrometeorological equations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evapotranspiration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Hydrometeorological equations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The most general and widely used equation for calculating reference ET is the <a href="/wiki/Penman_equation" title="Penman equation">Penman equation</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penman-Monteith" title="Penman-Monteith">Penman-Monteith</a> variation is recommended by the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">Food and Agriculture Organization</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/American_Society_of_Civil_Engineers" title="American Society of Civil Engineers">American Society of Civil Engineers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RojasSheffield2013_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-RojasSheffield2013-8">[8]</a></sup> The simpler <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blaney-Criddle_equation" title="Blaney-Criddle equation">Blaney-Criddle equation</a> was popular in the Western <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> for many years but it is not as accurate in regions with higher humidities.  Other solutions  used includes Makkink, which is simple but must be calibrated to a specific location, and <a class="external text" href="http://bioma.jrc.ec.europa.eu/components/componentstools/evapotranspiration/help/Hargreaves.html" rel="nofollow">Hargreaves</a>. To convert the reference evapotranspiration to actual crop evapotranspiration, a <a href="/wiki/Crop_coefficient" title="Crop coefficient">crop coefficient</a> and a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stress_coefficient&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stress coefficient (page does not exist)">stress coefficient</a> must be used.  Crop <a href="/wiki/Coefficient" title="Coefficient">coefficients</a> as used in many hydrological models usually change along the year to accommodate to the fact that crops are seasonal and, in general, plants behave differently along the seasons: perennial plants mature over multiple seasons, and stress responses can significantly depend upon many aspects of plant condition.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_landscape_plants">Urban landscape plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evapotranspiration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Urban landscape plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Methods for measuring evapotranspiration can be adapted to an urban setting to estimate the water requirements of urban landscape vegetation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nouri_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nouri-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Evapotranspiration_of_Urban_Vegetation">Evapotranspiration of Urban Vegetation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evapotranspiration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Evapotranspiration of Urban Vegetation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The water <big>requirement</big> of urban landscapes, particularly urban parklands, is of growing concern. The estimation of evapotranspiration (ET) and subsequently plant water requirements in urban vegetation needs to consider the heterogeneity of plants, soils, water, and climate characteristics. In a research in South Australia, two practical ET estimation approaches are compared to a detailed Soil Water Balance (SWB) analysis over a one-year period. One approach is the Water Use Classification of Landscape Plants (WUCOLS) method, which is based on expert opinion on the water needs of different classes of landscape plants. The other is a remote sensing approach based on the Enhanced Vegetation Index (EVI) from Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer (MODIS) sensors on the Terra satellite. Both methods require knowledge of reference ET calculated from meteorological data. More information is available in:
</p><p><i>Comparing Three Approaches of Evapotranspiration Estimation in Mixed Urban Vegetation: Field-Based, Remote Sensing-Based and Observational-Based Methods</i> *<a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.mdpi.com/2072-4292/8/6/492" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>
</p><p><i>Water requirements of urban landscape plants: a comparison of three factor-based approaches</i>*<a class="external autonumber" href="http://profdoc.um.ac.ir/pubs_files/p11038342.pdf" rel="nofollow">[2]</a>
</p><p><i>High Spatial Resolution WorldView-2 Imagery for Mapping NDVI and Its Relationship to Temporal Urban Landscape Evapotranspiration Factors</i>*<a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.mdpi.com/2072-4292/6/1/580" rel="nofollow">[3]</a>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Potential_evapotranspiration">Potential evapotranspiration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evapotranspiration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Potential evapotranspiration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Potential_evapotranspiration_Hawaii.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="163" data-file-width="351" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Potential_evapotranspiration_Hawaii.gif/350px-Potential_evapotranspiration_Hawaii.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d3/Potential_evapotranspiration_Hawaii.gif 1.5x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Potential_evapotranspiration_Hawaii.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Monthly estimated potential evapotranspiration and measured pan evaporation for two locations in <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>, Hilo and Pahala.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Potential_evaporation" title="Potential evaporation">Potential evaporation</a></div>
<p>Potential evapotranspiration (PET) is the amount of water that would be evaporated and transpired by a specific crop or ecosystem if there were sufficient <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> available. This demand incorporates the energy available for evaporation and the ability of the lower atmosphere to transport evaporated moisture away from the land surface. Potential evapotranspiration is higher in the summer, on less cloudy days, and closer to the equator, because of the higher levels of solar radiation that provides the energy for evaporation. Potential evapotranspiration is also higher on windy days because the evaporated moisture can be quickly moved from the ground or plant surface, allowing more evaporation to fill its place.
</p><p>Potential evapotranspiration is expressed in terms of a depth of water, and can be graphed during the year (see figure).
</p><p>Potential evapotranspiration is usually measured indirectly, from other climatic factors, but also depends on the surface type, such as free water (for <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceans</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> type for bare soil, and the <a href="/wiki/Vegetation" title="Vegetation">vegetation</a>.  Often a value for the potential evapotranspiration is calculated at a nearby climate station on a reference surface, conventionally short grass.  This value is called the reference evapotranspiration, and can be converted to a potential evapotranspiration by multiplying with a surface coefficient.  In agriculture, this is called a crop coefficient.  The difference between potential evapotranspiration and precipitation is used in <a href="/wiki/Irrigation_scheduling" title="Irrigation scheduling">irrigation scheduling</a>.
</p><p>Average annual potential evapotranspiration is often compared to average annual precipitation, P. The ratio of the two, P/PET, is the <a href="/wiki/Aridity_index" title="Aridity index">aridity index</a>.
</p>
