<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Tetrahedral molecular geometry</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>

<p>In a <b>tetrahedral molecular geometry</b>, a central <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a> is located at the center with four <a href="/wiki/Substituent" title="Substituent">substituents</a> that are located at the corners of a <a href="/wiki/Tetrahedron" title="Tetrahedron">tetrahedron</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bond_angle" title="Bond angle">bond angles</a> are cos<sup>−1</sup>(−⅓) = 109.4712206...° ≈ 109.5° when all four substituents are the same, as in <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> (CH<sub>4</sub>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> as well as <a href="/wiki/Group_14_hydride" title="Group 14 hydride">its heavier analogues</a>. Methane and other perfectly symmetrical tetrahedral molecules belong to <a href="/wiki/Point_group" title="Point group">point group</a> T<sub>d</sub>, but most tetrahedral molecules have <a href="/wiki/Tetrahedron#Isometries_of_irregular_tetrahedra" title="Tetrahedron">lower symmetry</a>. Tetrahedral molecules can be <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)" title="Chirality (chemistry)">chiral</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tetrahedral_molecular_geometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_group_chemistry">Main group chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tetrahedral_molecular_geometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Main group chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:174px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ch4-structure.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="178" data-file-width="172" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/55/Ch4-structure.png" width="172"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ch4-structure.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The tetrahedral molecule methane (CH<sub>4</sub>)</div></div></div>
<p>Aside from virtually all saturated organic compounds, most compounds of Si, Ge, and Sn are tetrahedral. Often tetrahedral molecules feature multiple bonding to the outer ligands, as in <a href="/wiki/Xenon_tetroxide" title="Xenon tetroxide">xenon tetroxide</a> (XeO<sub>4</sub>), the <a href="/wiki/Perchlorate" title="Perchlorate">perchlorate</a> ion (<span class="chemf nowrap">ClO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>), the <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfate</a> ion (<span class="chemf nowrap">SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>), the <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a> ion (<span class="chemf nowrap">PO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>). <a href="/wiki/Thiazyl_trifluoride" title="Thiazyl trifluoride">Thiazyl trifluoride</a> (SNF<sub>3</sub>) is tetrahedral, featuring a sulfur-to-nitrogen triple bond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other molecules have a tetrahedral arrangement of electron pairs around a central atom; for example <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> (NH<sub>3</sub>) with the nitrogen atom surrounded by three hydrogens and one <a href="/wiki/Lone_pair" title="Lone pair">lone pair</a>. However the usual classification considers only the bonded atoms and not the lone pair, so that ammonia is actually considered as <a href="/wiki/Trigonal_pyramidal_molecular_geometry" title="Trigonal pyramidal molecular geometry">pyramidal</a>. The H–N–H angles are 107°, contracted from 109.5. This difference is attributed to the influence of the lone pair which exerts a greater repulsive influence than a bonded atom.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transition_metal_chemistry">Transition metal chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tetrahedral_molecular_geometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Transition metal chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Again the geometry is widespread, particularly so for complexes where the metal has d<sup>0</sup> or d<sup>10</sup> configuration. Illustrative examples include <a href="/wiki/Tetrakis(triphenylphosphine)palladium(0)" title="Tetrakis(triphenylphosphine)palladium(0)">tetrakis(triphenylphosphine)palladium(0)</a> (Pd[P(C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>)<sub>3</sub>]<sub>4</sub>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nickel_carbonyl" title="Nickel carbonyl">nickel carbonyl</a> (Ni(CO)<sub>4</sub>), and <a href="/wiki/Titanium_tetrachloride" title="Titanium tetrachloride">titanium tetrachloride</a> (TiCl<sub>4</sub>).  Many complexes with incompletely filled d-shells are often tetrahedral, e.g. the tetrahalides of iron(II), cobalt(II), and nickel(II).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_structure">Water structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tetrahedral_molecular_geometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Water structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the gas phase, a single water molecule has an oxygen atom surrounded by two hydrogens and two lone pairs, and the H<sub>2</sub>O geometry is simply described as <a href="/wiki/Bent_molecular_geometry" title="Bent molecular geometry">bent</a> without considering the nonbonded lone pairs. 
</p><p>However in liquid water or in ice, the lone pairs form <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bond" title="Hydrogen bond">hydrogen bonds</a> with neighboring water molecules. The most common arrangement of hydrogen atoms around an oxygen is tetrahedral with two hydrogen atoms covalently bonded to oxygen and two attached by hydrogen bonds.  Since the hydrogen bonds vary in length many of these water molecules are not symmetrical and form transient irregular tetrahedra between their four associated hydrogen atoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bitetrahedral_structures">Bitetrahedral structures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tetrahedral_molecular_geometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bitetrahedral structures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many compounds and complexes adopt bitetrahedral structures.  In this motif, the two tetrahedra share a common edge.  The inorganic polymer <a href="/wiki/Silicon_disulfide" title="Silicon disulfide">silicon disulfide</a> features an infinite chain of edge-shared tetrahedra.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gallium-trichloride-from-xtal-2004-3D-balls.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="670" data-file-width="1100" height="134" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Gallium-trichloride-from-xtal-2004-3D-balls.png/220px-Gallium-trichloride-from-xtal-2004-3D-balls.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Gallium-trichloride-from-xtal-2004-3D-balls.png/330px-Gallium-trichloride-from-xtal-2004-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Gallium-trichloride-from-xtal-2004-3D-balls.png/440px-Gallium-trichloride-from-xtal-2004-3D-balls.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gallium-trichloride-from-xtal-2004-3D-balls.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bitetrahedral structure adopted by Al<sub>2</sub>Br<sub>6</sub> ("<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminium_tribromide" title="Aluminium tribromide">aluminium tribromide</a>") and Ga<sub>2</sub>Cl<sub>6</sub> ("<a href="/wiki/Gallium_trichloride" title="Gallium trichloride">gallium trichloride</a>").</div></div></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exceptions_and_distortions">Exceptions and distortions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tetrahedral_molecular_geometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Exceptions and distortions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Inversion of tetrahedral occurs widely in organic and main group chemistry.  The so-called <a href="/wiki/Walden_inversion" title="Walden inversion">Walden inversion</a> illustrates the stereochemical consequences of inversion at carbon.  <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_inversion" title="Nitrogen inversion">Nitrogen inversion</a> in ammonia also entails transient formation of planar NH<sub>3</sub>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inverted_tetrahedral_geometry">Inverted tetrahedral geometry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tetrahedral_molecular_geometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Inverted tetrahedral geometry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Geometrical constraints in a molecule can cause a severe distortion of idealized tetrahedral geometry. In compounds featuring "inverted" tetrahedral geometry at a carbon atom, all four groups attached to this carbon are on one side of a plane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The carbon atom lies  at or near the apex of a square <a href="/wiki/Pyramid_(geometry)" title="Pyramid (geometry)">pyramid</a> with the other four groups at the corners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kenny_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kenny-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewars_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lewars-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Invertedcarbon.png" title="Inverted carbon"><img alt="Inverted carbon" data-file-height="293" data-file-width="720" height="81" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Invertedcarbon.png/200px-Invertedcarbon.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Invertedcarbon.png/300px-Invertedcarbon.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Invertedcarbon.png/400px-Invertedcarbon.png 2x" width="200"/></a></dd></dl>
<p>The simplest examples of organic molecules displaying inverted tetrahedral geometry are the smallest <a href="/wiki/Propellane" title="Propellane">propellanes</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/1.1.1-Propellane" title="1.1.1-Propellane">[1.1.1]propellane</a>; or more generally the <a href="/wiki/Paddlane" title="Paddlane">paddlanes</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyramidane" title="Pyramidane">pyramidane</a> ([3.3.3.3]fenestrane).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kenny_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kenny-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewars_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lewars-7">[7]</a></sup> Such molecules are typically <a href="/wiki/Strain_energy" title="Strain energy">strained</a>, resulting in increased reactivity.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Planarization">Planarization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tetrahedral_molecular_geometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Planarization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A tetrahedron can also be distorted by increasing the angle between two of the bonds.  In the extreme case, flattening results. For carbon this phenomenon can be observed in a class of compounds called the <a href="/wiki/Fenestrane" title="Fenestrane">fenestranes</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tetrahedral_molecules_with_no_central_atom">Tetrahedral molecules with no central atom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tetrahedral_molecular_geometry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Tetrahedral molecules with no central atom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A few molecules have a tetrahedral geometry with no central atom. An inorganic example is <a href="/wiki/Allotropes_of_phosphorus#White_phosphorus" title="Allotropes of phosphorus">tetraphosphorus</a> (P<sub>4</sub>) which has four phosphorus atoms at the vertices of a tetrahedron and each bonded to the other three. An organic example is <a href="/wiki/Tetrahedrane" title="Tetrahedrane">tetrahedrane</a> (C<sub>4</sub>H<sub>4</sub>) with four carbon atoms each bonded to one hydrogen and the other three carbons.
</p>
