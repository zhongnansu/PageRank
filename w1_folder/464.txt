<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Bosch reaction</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>Bosch reaction</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reaction</a> between <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> that produces elemental <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> (<a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a>), <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, and  a 10% return of invested <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a>. It is named after the German chemist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Bosch" title="Carl Bosch">Carl Bosch</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This reaction requires the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a> and requires a temperature level of 530-730 degrees Celsius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The overall reaction is as follows:
</p><p>CO<sub>2</sub>(<a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">g</a>) + 2 H<sub>2</sub>(g) → C(<a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">s</a>) + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O(g)
</p><p>The above reaction is actually the result of two reactions. The first reaction, the reverse <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_gas_shift_reaction" title="Water gas shift reaction">water gas shift reaction</a>, is a fast one:
</p><p>CO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub> → CO + H<sub>2</sub>O
</p><p>The second reaction controls the <a href="/wiki/Reaction_rate" title="Reaction rate">reaction rate</a>:
</p><p>CO + H<sub>2</sub> → C + H<sub>2</sub>O
</p><p>The overall reaction produces 2.3×10<sup>3</sup> <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">joules</a> for every gram of carbon dioxide reacted at 650 °C. Reaction temperatures are in the range of 450 to 600 °C.
</p><p>The reaction can be accelerated in the presence of an <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium" title="Ruthenium">Ruthenium</a> also serves to speed up the reaction.  
</p><p>Together with the <a href="/wiki/Sabatier_reaction" title="Sabatier reaction">Sabatier reaction</a>, the Bosch reaction is studied as a way to remove carbon dioxide and to generate clean water aboard a <a href="/wiki/Space_station" title="Space station">space station</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The reaction is also used to produce graphite for <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">radiocarbon dating</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accelerator_Mass_Spectrometry" title="Accelerator Mass Spectrometry">Accelerator Mass Spectrometry</a>.
</p><p>The Bosch reaction is being investigated for use in maintaining space station life support. Though the Bosch reaction would present a completely closed hydrogen and oxygen cycle which only produces atomic carbon as waste, difficulties in maintaining its higher required temperature and properly handling carbon deposits mean that significantly more research will be required before a Bosch reactor can become a reality. One problem is that the production of elemental carbon tends to foul the catalyst's surface, which is detrimental to the reaction's efficiency.
</p>
