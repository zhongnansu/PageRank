<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Alkyl</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Isopropyl_group.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="489" data-file-width="512" height="96" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Isopropyl_group.svg/100px-Isopropyl_group.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Isopropyl_group.svg/150px-Isopropyl_group.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Isopropyl_group.svg/200px-Isopropyl_group.svg.png 2x" width="100"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Isopropyl_group.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Isopropyl group</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:62px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alkyl.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="80" data-file-width="60" height="80" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8c/Alkyl.png" width="60"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alkyl.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Methyl group</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a>, an <b>alkyl</b> <a href="/wiki/Substituent" title="Substituent">substituent</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">alkane</a> missing one <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
The term alkyl is intentionally unspecific to include many possible substitutions. 
An acyclic alkyl has the general formula C<sub><i>n</i></sub>H<sub>2<i>n</i>+1</sub>. A <b>cycloalkyl</b> is derived from a <a href="/wiki/Cycloalkane" title="Cycloalkane">cycloalkane</a> by removal of a hydrogen atom from a ring and has the general formula C<sub><i>n</i></sub>H<sub>2<i>n</i>-1</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
Typically an alkyl is a part of a larger molecule. In <a href="/wiki/Structural_formula" title="Structural formula">structural formula</a>, the symbol R is used to designate a generic (unspecified) alkyl group. The smallest alkyl group is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methyl" title="Methyl">methyl</a>, with the formula CH<sub>3</sub>−.
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_everyday_life">In everyday life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alkyl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In everyday life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word root <i>alkyl</i> is encountered in several contexts.  <a href="/wiki/Alkylation" title="Alkylation">Alkylation</a> is an important operation in refineries, for example in the production of high-octane gasoline. <a href="/wiki/Alkylating_antineoplastic_agent" title="Alkylating antineoplastic agent">Alkylating antineoplastic agents</a> refer to a class of compounds that are used to treat cancer.  In such case, the term alkyl is used loosely.  For example, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_mustard" title="Nitrogen mustard">nitrogen mustards</a> are well-known alkylating agents, but they are more complex than a mere hydrocarbon.  In chemistry, alkyl refers to a group, a substituent, that is attached to other molecular fragments.  For example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkyl_lithium_reagent" title="Alkyl lithium reagent">alkyl lithium reagents</a> have the empirical formula Li(alkyl), where alkyl = methyl, ethyl, etc. A <i>dialkyl</i> ether is an <a href="/wiki/Ether" title="Ether">ether</a> with two alkyl groups, e.g., diethyl ether (O(C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>5</sub>)<sub>2</sub>). Alkyl also refers to an active ingredient in <a href="/wiki/Green_algae" title="Green algae">green algae</a> preventatives used in swimming pools.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_medicinal_chemistry">In medicinal chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alkyl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In medicinal chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Medicinal_chemistry" title="Medicinal chemistry">medicinal chemistry</a>, the incorporation of alkyl chains into some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_compounds" title="Chemical compounds">chemical compounds</a> increases their <a href="/wiki/Lipophilicity" title="Lipophilicity">lipophilicity</a>.  This strategy has been used to increase the antimicrobial activity of <a href="/wiki/Flavanone" title="Flavanone">flavanones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chalcone" title="Chalcone">chalcones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-medchem1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-medchem1-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Alkyl_cations.2C_anions.2C_and_radicals"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Alkyl_cations,_anions,_and_radicals">Alkyl cations, anions, and radicals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alkyl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Alkyl cations, anions, and radicals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Usually, alkyl groups are attached to other atoms or groups of atoms.  Free alkyls occur as neutral compounds, as anions, or as cations. The cations are called <a href="/wiki/Carbocation" title="Carbocation">carbocations</a>. The anions are called <a href="/wiki/Carbanion" title="Carbanion">carbanions</a>. The neutral alkyl <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_radical" title="Free radical">free radicals</a> have no special name. Such species are usually encountered only as transient intermediates, but some are quite stable and can be "put into a bottle". Typically alkyl cations are generated using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Super_acid" title="Super acid">super acids</a>, alkyl anions are observed in the presence of strong bases, and alkyl radicals are generated by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photochemical_reaction" title="Photochemical reaction">photochemical reaction</a>. Alkyls are commonly observed in <a href="/wiki/Mass_spectrometry" title="Mass spectrometry">mass spectrometry</a> of <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature">Nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alkyl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Alkyls form <a href="/wiki/Homologous_series" title="Homologous series">homologous series</a>. The simplest series have the general formula C<sub><i>n</i></sub>H<sub>2<i>n</i>+1</sub>. Alkyls include <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methyl" title="Methyl">methyl</a></i>, CH<sub>3</sub><b>·</b> (named after <i><a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a></i>), <i><a href="/wiki/Ethyl_group" title="Ethyl group">ethyl</a></i> (C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>5</sub><b>·</b>), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propyl" title="Propyl">propyl</a></i> (C<sub>3</sub>H<sub>7</sub><b>·</b>), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Butyl" title="Butyl">butyl</a></i> (C<sub>4</sub>H<sub>9</sub><b>·</b>), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pentyl" title="Pentyl">pentyl</a></i> (C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>11</sub><b>·</b>), and so on. Alkyl groups that contain one ring have the formula C<sub><i>n</i></sub>H<sub>2<i>n</i>−1</sub>, e.g. cyclopropyl and cyclohexyl.
The naming convention is taken from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAC_nomenclature" title="IUPAC nomenclature">IUPAC nomenclature</a>:
</p>

<p><br/>
These names  are used to name branched chained structures, for example <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3-methylpentane" title="3-methylpentane">3-methylpentane</a>:
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:3-MethylPentaneHighlighted.png"><img alt="3-MethylPentaneHighlighted.png" data-file-height="149" data-file-width="235" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1a/3-MethylPentaneHighlighted.png" width="235"/></a></div></div>
<p>The structure of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3-methylpentane" title="3-methylpentane">3-methylpentane</a> is viewed as consisting of two parts. First, five atoms comprise the longest straight chain of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> centers.  The parent five-carbon compound is named <a href="/wiki/Pentane" title="Pentane">pentane</a> (highlighted blue). The methyl "substituent" or "group" is highlighted red. According to the usual rules of nomenclature, alkyl groups are included in the name of the molecule before the root, as in <a href="/wiki/Methylpentane" title="Methylpentane">methylpentane</a>. This name is, however, ambiguous, as the methyl branch could be on various carbon atoms. Thus, the name is 3-methylpentane to avoid ambiguity: The 3- is because the methyl is attached to the third of the five carbon atoms.
</p><p>If there is more than one of the same alkyl group attached to a chain, then the prefixes are used on the alkyl groups to indicate multiples (i.e., di, tri, tetra, etc.)
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2,3,3triMethylPentane.png"><img alt="2,3,3triMethylPentane.png" data-file-height="222" data-file-width="235" height="222" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/db/2%2C3%2C3triMethylPentane.png" width="235"/></a></div></div>
<p>This compound is known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2,3,3-trimethylpentane" title="2,3,3-trimethylpentane">2,3,3-trimethylpentane</a>. Here three identical alkyl groups attached to carbon atoms 2, 3, and 3. These numbers are included in the name to avoid ambiguity on the position of the groups, and "tri" indicates that there are three identical methyl groups. If one of the methyl groups attached to the third carbon atom were an ethyl group, then the name would become 3-ethyl-2,3-dimethylpentane. Note that when there are different alkyl groups, they are listed in alphabetical order.
</p><p>In addition, each position on an alkyl chain can be described according to how many other carbon atoms are attached to it. The terms <a href="/wiki/Primary_carbon" title="Primary carbon">primary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Secondary_carbon" title="Secondary carbon">secondary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tertiary_carbon" title="Tertiary carbon">tertiary</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_carbon" title="Quaternary carbon">quaternary</a> refer to a carbon attached to one, two, three, or four other carbons respectively.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alkyl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first named alkyl radical was ethyl, named so by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liebig" title="Liebig">Liebig</a> in 1833 from the German word "aether" (which in turn had been derived from the Greek word "aither") (<a class="external free" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ether">https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ether</a> , i.e., the substance now known as diethyl ether) and the Greek word ύλη (<a href="/wiki/Hyle" title="Hyle">hyle</a>), meaning "matter".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This was followed by methyl (<a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Dumas" title="Jean-Baptiste Dumas">Dumas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne-Melchior_P%C3%A9ligot" title="Eugène-Melchior Péligot">Peligot</a> in 1834, meaning "spirit of wood"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>) and amyl (<a href="/wiki/Auguste_Andr%C3%A9_Thomas_Cahours" title="Auguste André Thomas Cahours">Auguste Cahours</a> in 1840<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>). The word alkyl was introduced by <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Wislicenus" title="Johannes Wislicenus">Johannes Wislicenus</a> in or before 1882, based on the German word "Alkoholradicale" and then-common suffix -yl.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
