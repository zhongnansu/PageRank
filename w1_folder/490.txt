<p>An <b>ideal gas</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Scientific_theory" title="Scientific theory">theoretical</a> <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> composed of many randomly moving <a href="/wiki/Point_particle" title="Point particle">point particles</a> whose only interactions are perfectly <a href="/wiki/Elastic_collision" title="Elastic collision">elastic collisions</a>. The ideal gas concept is useful because it obeys the <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas_law" title="Ideal gas law">ideal gas law</a>, a simplified <a href="/wiki/Equation_of_state" title="Equation of state">equation of state</a>, and is amenable to analysis under <a href="/wiki/Statistical_mechanics" title="Statistical mechanics">statistical mechanics</a>.
</p><p>In most usual conditions (for instance at standard temperature and pressure), most <a href="/wiki/Real_gas" title="Real gas">real gases</a> behave qualitatively like an ideal gas. Many gases such as <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Noble_gas" title="Noble gas">noble gases</a>, and some heavier gases like <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> can be treated like ideal gases within reasonable tolerances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boles_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-boles-1">[1]</a></sup> Generally, a gas behaves more like an ideal gas at higher <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> and lower <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boles_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-boles-1">[1]</a></sup> as the <a href="/wiki/Potential_energy" title="Potential energy">potential energy</a> due to intermolecular forces becomes less significant compared with the particles' <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a>, and the size of the molecules becomes less significant compared to the empty space between them. One <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mole</a> of an ideal gas has a volume of 22.710947(13) litres<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mvol_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-mvol-2">[2]</a></sup> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard temperature and pressure">standard temperature and pressure</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> of 273.15 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a> and an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absolute_pressure" title="Absolute pressure">absolute pressure</a> of exactly 10<sup>5</sup> <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">Pa</a>) as defined by <a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Pure_and_Applied_Chemistry" title="International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry">IUPAC</a> since 1982.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[note 1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The ideal gas model tends to fail at lower temperatures or higher pressures, when intermolecular forces and molecular size becomes important.  It also fails for most heavy gases, such as many <a href="/wiki/Refrigerant" title="Refrigerant">refrigerants</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boles_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-boles-1">[1]</a></sup> and for gases with strong intermolecular forces, notably <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a>. At high pressures, the volume of a real gas is often considerably larger than that of an ideal gas. At low temperatures, the pressure of a real gas is often considerably less than that of an ideal gas. At some point of low temperature and high pressure, real gases undergo a <a href="/wiki/Phase_transition" title="Phase transition">phase transition</a>, such as to a <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solid</a>. The model of an ideal gas, however, does not describe or allow phase transitions.  These must be modeled by more complex equations of state. The deviation from the ideal gas behaviour can be described by a <a href="/wiki/Dimensionless_quantity" title="Dimensionless quantity">dimensionless quantity</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Compressibility_factor" title="Compressibility factor">compressibility factor</a>, <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">Z</span>.
</p><p>The ideal gas model has been explored in both the <a href="/wiki/Newtonian_dynamics" title="Newtonian dynamics">Newtonian dynamics</a> (as in "<a href="/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases" title="Kinetic theory of gases">kinetic theory</a>") and in <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a> (as a "<a href="/wiki/Gas_in_a_box" title="Gas in a box">gas in a box</a>"). The ideal gas model has also been used to model the behavior of electrons in a metal (in the <a href="/wiki/Drude_model" title="Drude model">Drude model</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Free_electron_model" title="Free electron model">free electron model</a>), and it is one of the most important models in statistical mechanics.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_ideal_gas">Types of ideal gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ideal_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types of ideal gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are three basic classes of ideal gas:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="to verify this whole section (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<ul><li>the classical or <a href="/wiki/Maxwell%E2%80%93Boltzmann_statistics" title="Maxwell–Boltzmann statistics">Maxwell–Boltzmann</a> ideal gas,</li>
<li>the ideal quantum <a href="/wiki/Bose_gas" title="Bose gas">Bose gas</a>, composed of <a href="/wiki/Boson" title="Boson">bosons</a>, and</li>
<li>the ideal quantum <a href="/wiki/Fermi_gas" title="Fermi gas">Fermi gas</a>, composed of <a href="/wiki/Fermion" title="Fermion">fermions</a>.</li></ul>
<p>The classical ideal gas can be separated into two types: The classical thermodynamic ideal gas and the ideal quantum Boltzmann gas. Both are essentially the same, except that the classical thermodynamic ideal gas is based on classical <a href="/wiki/Statistical_mechanics" title="Statistical mechanics">statistical mechanics</a>, and certain thermodynamic parameters such as the <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a> are only specified to within an undetermined additive constant. The ideal quantum Boltzmann gas overcomes this limitation by taking the limit of the quantum Bose gas and quantum Fermi gas in the limit of high temperature to specify these additive constants. The behavior of a quantum Boltzmann gas is the same as that of a classical ideal gas except for the specification of these constants. The results of the quantum Boltzmann gas are used in a number of cases including the <a href="/wiki/Sackur%E2%80%93Tetrode_equation" title="Sackur–Tetrode equation">Sackur–Tetrode equation</a> for the entropy of an ideal gas and the <a href="/wiki/Saha_ionization_equation" title="Saha ionization equation">Saha ionization equation</a> for a weakly ionized <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_thermodynamic_ideal_gas">Classical thermodynamic ideal gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ideal_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classical thermodynamic ideal gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

</p>

<p>where
</p>
<ul><li><span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">N</span> is the number of gas particles</li>
<li><span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">k<sub>B</sub></span> is the <a href="/wiki/Boltzmann_constant" title="Boltzmann constant">Boltzmann constant</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6977138099999999999♠</span>1.381<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>−23</sup> J·K<sup>−1</sup></span>).</li></ul>
<p>The probability distribution of particles by velocity or energy is given by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maxwell_speed_distribution" title="Maxwell speed distribution">Maxwell speed distribution</a>.
</p><p>The ideal gas model depends on the following assumptions:
</p>
<ul><li>The molecules of the gas are indistinguishable, small, hard spheres</li>
<li>All collisions are elastic and all motion is frictionless (no energy loss in motion or collision)</li>
<li>Newton's laws apply</li>
<li>The average distance between molecules is much larger than the size of the molecules</li>
<li>The molecules are constantly moving in random directions with a distribution of speeds</li>
<li>There are no attractive or repulsive forces between the molecules apart from those that determine their point-like collisions</li>
<li>The only forces between the gas molecules and the surroundings are those that determine the point-like collisions of the molecules with the walls</li>
<li>In the simplest case, there are no long-range forces between the molecules of the gas and the surroundings.</li></ul>
<p>The assumption of spherical particles is necessary so that there are no rotational modes allowed, unlike in a diatomic gas. The following three assumptions are very related: molecules are hard, collisions are elastic, and there are no inter-molecular forces. The assumption that the space between particles is much larger than the particles themselves is of paramount importance, and explains why the ideal gas approximation fails at high pressures.
</p>

<p>Using the results of <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a> only, we can go a long way in determining the expression for the <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a> of an ideal gas. </p><p>This is about as far as we can go using thermodynamics alone. Note that the above equation is flawed — as the temperature approaches zero, the entropy approaches negative infinity, in contradiction to the <a href="/wiki/Third_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Third law of thermodynamics">third law of thermodynamics</a>. In the above "ideal" development, there is a critical point, not at absolute zero, at which the argument of the logarithm becomes unity, and the entropy becomes zero. This is unphysical.  The above equation is a good approximation only when the argument of the logarithm is much larger than unity — the concept of an ideal gas breaks down at low values of <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;"><span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">V</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">N</span></span></span>. Nevertheless, there will be a "best" value of the constant in the sense that the predicted entropy is as close as possible to the actual entropy, given the flawed assumption of ideality.  A quantum-mechanical derivation of this constant is developed in the derivation of the <a href="/wiki/Sackur%E2%80%93Tetrode_equation" title="Sackur–Tetrode equation">Sackur–Tetrode equation</a> which expresses the entropy of a monatomic (<span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;"><i>ĉ<sub>V</sub></i></span> = <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">3</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">2</span></span>) ideal gas. In the Sackur–Tetrode theory the constant depends only upon the mass of the gas particle. The Sackur–Tetrode equation also suffers from a divergent entropy at absolute zero, but is a good approximation for the entropy of a monatomic ideal gas for high enough temperatures.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ideal_quantum_gases">Ideal quantum gases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ideal_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ideal quantum gases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the above-mentioned <a href="/wiki/Sackur%E2%80%93Tetrode_equation" title="Sackur–Tetrode equation">Sackur–Tetrode equation</a>, the best choice of the entropy constant was found to be proportional to the quantum <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_wavelength" title="Thermal wavelength">thermal wavelength</a> of a particle, and the point at which the argument of the logarithm becomes zero is roughly equal to the  point at which the average distance between particles becomes equal to the thermal wavelength. In fact, <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum</a> theory itself predicts the same thing. Any gas behaves as an ideal gas at high enough temperature and low enough density, but at the point where the Sackur–Tetrode equation begins to break down, the gas will begin to behave as a quantum gas, composed of either <a href="/wiki/Boson" title="Boson">bosons</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fermion" title="Fermion">fermions</a>. (See the <a href="/wiki/Gas_in_a_box" title="Gas in a box">gas in a box</a> article for a derivation of the ideal quantum gases, including the ideal Boltzmann gas.)
</p><p>Gases tend to behave as an ideal gas over a wider range of pressures when the temperature reaches the <a href="/wiki/Boyle_temperature" title="Boyle temperature">Boyle temperature</a>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ideal_Bose_and_Fermi_gases">Ideal Bose and Fermi gases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ideal_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Ideal Bose and Fermi gases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An ideal <a href="/wiki/Bose_gas" title="Bose gas">gas of bosons</a> (e.g. a <a href="/wiki/Photon_gas" title="Photon gas">photon gas</a>) will be governed by <a href="/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_statistics" title="Bose–Einstein statistics">Bose–Einstein statistics</a> and the distribution of energy will be in the form of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_distribution" title="Bose–Einstein distribution">Bose–Einstein distribution</a>. An ideal <a href="/wiki/Fermi_gas" title="Fermi gas">gas of fermions</a> will be governed by <a href="/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_statistics" title="Fermi–Dirac statistics">Fermi–Dirac statistics</a> and the distribution of energy will be in the form of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_distribution" title="Fermi–Dirac distribution">Fermi–Dirac distribution</a>.
</p>
