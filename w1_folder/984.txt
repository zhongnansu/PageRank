<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Crown Estate</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about the United Kingdom's sovereign estate holdings. <p>The <b>Crown Estate</b> is a collection of lands and holdings in the United Kingdom belonging to the British monarch as a <a href="/wiki/Corporation_sole#The_Crown" title="Corporation sole">corporation sole</a>, making it the "Sovereign's public estate", which is neither government property nor part of the monarch's private estate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-report_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-report-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-backgrounder_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-backgrounder-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guidance_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-guidance-4">[4]</a></sup> As a result of this arrangement, the sovereign is not involved with the management or administration of the estate, and exercises only very limited control of its affairs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Act_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Act-5">[5]</a></sup> Instead, the estate's extensive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Financial_portfolio" title="Financial portfolio">portfolio</a> is overseen by a semi-independent, incorporated public body headed by the <b>Crown Estate Commissioners</b>, who exercise "the powers of ownership" of the estate, although they are not "owners in their own right".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-report_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-report-1">[1]</a></sup> The revenues from these hereditary possessions have been placed by the monarch at the disposition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Government" title="Her Majesty's Government">Her Majesty's Government</a> in exchange for relief from the responsibility to fund the Civil Government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> These revenues thus proceed directly to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Treasury" title="Her Majesty's Treasury">Her Majesty's Treasury</a>, for the benefit of the British nation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-report_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-report-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The Crown Estate is formally accountable to the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament of the United Kingdom</a>, where it is legally mandated to make an annual report to the sovereign, a copy of which is forwarded to the House of Commons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Act_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Act-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Crown Estate is one of the largest property managers in the United Kingdom, overseeing property worth £12 billion,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> with urban properties valued at £9.1 billion<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cereport_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-cereport-11">[11]</a></sup> representing the majority of the estate by value. These include a large number of properties in central London, but the estate also controls 792,000 ha (1,960,000 acres) of agricultural land and forest and more than half of the UK's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foreshore" title="Foreshore">foreshore</a>, and retains various other traditional holdings and rights, including <a href="/wiki/Ascot_Racecourse" title="Ascot Racecourse">Ascot Racecourse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Windsor_Great_Park" title="Windsor Great Park">Windsor Great Park</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Naturally occurring gold and silver in the UK, collectively known as "Mines Royal", are managed by the Crown Estate and leased to mining operators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Historically, Crown Estate properties were administered by the reigning monarch to help fund the business of governing the country. However, in <a href="/wiki/Civil_List_Act_1760" title="Civil List Act 1760">1760</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_III_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George III of the United Kingdom">George III</a> surrendered control over the Estate's revenues to the Treasury,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guidance_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-guidance-4">[4]</a></sup> thus relieving him of the responsibility of paying for the costs of the civil service, defence costs, the national debt, and his own personal debts. In return, he received an annual grant known as the <a href="/wiki/Civil_list" title="Civil list">Civil List</a>. By tradition, each subsequent monarch agreed to this arrangement upon his or her accession. However, from 1 April 2012, under the terms of the <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_Grant_Act_2011" title="Sovereign Grant Act 2011">Sovereign Grant Act 2011</a> (SSG), the Civil List was abolished and the monarch was thenceforth provided with a stable source of revenue indexed to a percentage of the Crown Estate's annual net revenue (currently set at 25%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> This was intended to provide a long-term solution and remove the politically sensitive issue of Parliament having to debate the Civil List allowance every ten years. Subsequently, the Sovereign Grant Act allows for all future monarchs to simply extend these provisions for their reigns by <a href="/wiki/Order_in_Council" title="Order in Council">Order in Council</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-backgrounder_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-backgrounder-2">[2]</a></sup> The act does not imply any legal change in the nature of the estate's ownership, but is simply a benchmark by which the sovereign grant is set as a grant by Parliament.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crown_Estate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crown_land_in_England_and_Wales">Crown land in England and Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crown_Estate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Crown land in England and Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The history of the Crown lands in England and Wales begins with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norman_conquest" title="Norman conquest">Norman conquest</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ-9">[9]</a></sup> When <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_I_of_England" title="William I of England">William I</a> died, the land he had acquired by <a href="/wiki/Right_of_conquest" title="Right of conquest">right of conquest</a> was still largely intact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> His successors, however, granted large estates to the nobles and barons who supplied them with men and arms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <span id="Royal_manor"></span> The monarch's remaining land was divided into <b>royal manors</b>, each managed separately by a <a href="/wiki/Seneschal" title="Seneschal">seneschal</a>. The period between the reigns of William I and <a href="/wiki/Anne,_Queen_of_Great_Britain" title="Anne, Queen of Great Britain">Queen Anne</a> was one of continuous <a href="/wiki/Alienation_(property_law)" title="Alienation (property law)">alienation</a> of lands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The Crown lands were augmented as well as depleted over the centuries: <a href="/wiki/Edward_I_of_England" title="Edward I of England">Edward I</a> extended his possessions into <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" title="James I of England">James VI &amp; I</a> had his own Crown lands in Scotland which were ultimately combined with the Crown lands of England and Wales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> However, the disposals outweighed the acquisitions: at the time of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_Restoration" title="English Restoration">Restoration</a> in 1660, the total revenue arising from Crown lands was estimated to be £263,598 (equal to £36,318,804 today).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> By the end of the reign of <a href="/wiki/William_III_of_England" title="William III of England">William III</a> (1689–1702), however, it was reduced to some £6,000 (equal to £901,648 today).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blue_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-blue-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Before the reign of William III all the revenues of the kingdom were bestowed on the monarch for the general expenses of government. These revenues were of two kinds:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-best1_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-best1-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>the hereditary revenues, derived principally from the Crown lands, feudal rights (commuted for the hereditary excise duties in 1660), profits of the post office, with licences, etc.</li>
<li>the temporary revenues derived from taxes granted to the king for a term of years or for life.</li></ul>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Glorious_Revolution" title="Glorious Revolution">Glorious Revolution</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_Parliament" title="English Parliament">Parliament</a> retained under its own control the greater part of the temporary revenues, and relieved the sovereign of the cost of the naval and military services and the burden of the <a href="/wiki/Government_debt" title="Government debt">national debt</a>. During the reigns of William III, Anne, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_I_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George I of the United Kingdom">George I</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_II_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George II of the United Kingdom">George II</a> the sovereign remained responsible for the maintenance of the civil government and for the support of the royal household and dignity, being allowed for these purposes the hereditary revenues and certain taxes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-best1_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-best1-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>As the state machinery expanded, the cost of the civil government exceeded the income from the Crown lands and feudal rights; this created a personal debt for the monarch.
</p><p>On George III's accession he surrendered the income from the Crown lands to Parliament, and abrogated responsibility for the cost of the civil government and the clearance of associated debts. As a result, and to avoid pecuniary embarrassment, he was granted a fixed <a href="/wiki/Civil_list" title="Civil list">civil list</a> payment and the income retained from the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Lancaster" title="Duchy of Lancaster">Duchy of Lancaster</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-best2_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-best2-23">[23]</a></sup> The King surrendered to parliamentary control the hereditary excise duties, post office revenues, and "the small branches" of hereditary revenue including rents of the Crown lands in England (which amounted to about £11,000, or £1,526,315 today), and was granted a civil list annuity of £800,000 (equal to £111,004,717 today) for the support of his household, subject to the payment of certain annuities to members of the royal family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-best2_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-best2-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the King had retained large hereditary revenues, his income proved insufficient for his charged expenses because he used the privilege to reward supporters with bribes and gifts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Debts amounting to over £3 million (equal to £220,071,696 today) over the course of George's reign were paid by Parliament, and the civil list annuity was then increased from time to time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Every succeeding sovereign down to and including <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Elizabeth II</a> renewed the arrangement made between George III and Parliament; and the practice was, by the 19th century, recognised as "an integral part of the Constitution [which] would be difficult to abandon".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-best1_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-best1-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Nevertheless, a review of funding arrangements for the monarchy led to the passage of the <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_Grant_Act_2011" title="Sovereign Grant Act 2011">Sovereign Grant Act 2011</a>, which according to HM Treasury, is:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote><p>A new consolidated grant rounding together the Civil List, Royal Palaces and Royal Travel grants-in-aid. It is intended that future funding will be set as a fraction of The Crown Estate revenue and paid through the annual Treasury Estimates process, and subject to full National Audit Office audit....</p></blockquote>
<blockquote><p>The Grant is to enable The Queen to discharge her duties as Head of State. i.e. it meets the central staff costs and running expenses of Her Majesty's official Household – such things as official receptions, investitures, garden parties and so on. It will also cover the maintenance of the Royal Palaces in England and the cost of travel to carry out royal engagements such as opening buildings and other royal visits....</p></blockquote>
<blockquote><p>While the amount of the Grant will be linked to the profits of the Crown Estate, those profits will continue to be paid in to the Exchequer; they are not to be hypothecated. Setting the Grant at a percentage of profits of the Crown Estate will help to put in place a durable and transparent framework.</p></blockquote>
<p>In April 2014 it was reported that the Crown Estate was proposing to sell about 200 of its 750 rural homes in the UK, and was evicting tenants in preparation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crown_land_in_Ireland">Crown land in Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crown_Estate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Crown land in Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1793 George III surrendered the hereditary revenues of Ireland, and was granted a civil list annuity for certain expenses of Irish civil government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-best2_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-best2-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nai_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-nai-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Most of the crown land by then was from forfeitures after the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Rebellion_of_1641" title="Irish Rebellion of 1641">1641 rebellion</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Williamite_War_in_Ireland" title="Williamite War in Ireland">1688–91 revolution</a>, with some smaller older parcels remaining from earlier rebellions, the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Monasteries" title="Dissolution of the Monasteries">Dissolution of the Monasteries</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norman_Ireland" title="Norman Ireland">Norman period</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Commissioners1806_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Commissioners1806-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quitrent1827_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-quitrent1827-33">[33]</a></sup> Most confiscated land had been granted away again, as under the <a href="/wiki/Adventurers%27_Act" title="Adventurers' Act">Adventurers' Act</a> 1642, <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Settlement_1662" title="Act of Settlement 1662">Act of Settlement 1662</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Resumption&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Act of Resumption (page does not exist)">Act of Resumption</a> 1700.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Commissioners1806_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Commissioners1806-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quitrent1827_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-quitrent1827-33">[33]</a></sup> The balance which remained in Crown hands included the "undisposed lands" of the 1662 settlement (worth less than the small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quit_rent" title="Quit rent">quit rent</a> that a grantee would have had to pay) and the balance unsold by the trustees under the 1700 act at its 1703 time limit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Commissioners1806_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-Commissioners1806-32">[32]</a></sup> The scattered crown lands were <a href="/wiki/Farm_(revenue_leasing)" title="Farm (revenue leasing)">farmed</a> out on long leases with little regard to the collection of rent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Commissioners1806_32-3"><a href="#cite_note-Commissioners1806-32">[32]</a></sup> Responsibility lay with the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Quit_Rent_Office&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Quit Rent Office (page does not exist)">Quit Rent Office</a>, which was absorbed in 1827 by the <a href="/wiki/Commissioners_of_Woods,_Forests_and_Land_Revenues" title="Commissioners of Woods, Forests and Land Revenues">Commissioners of Woods, Forests and Land Revenues</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nai_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-nai-30">[30]</a></sup> The largest Crown estate in the 1820s was <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pobble_O%27Keefe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pobble O'Keefe (page does not exist)">Pobble O'Keefe</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sliabh_Luachra" title="Sliabh Luachra">Sliabh Luachra</a> at 5,000 acres (2,000 ha).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Commissioners1806_32-4"><a href="#cite_note-Commissioners1806-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quitrent1827_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-quitrent1827-33">[33]</a></sup> In 1828 the lease expired, and  <a href="/wiki/Sir_Richard_Griffith,_1st_Baronet" title="Sir Richard Griffith, 1st Baronet">Richard Griffith</a> was appointed to supervise its improvement, including the foundation of the <a href="/wiki/Model_village" title="Model village">model village</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingwilliamstown" title="Kingwilliamstown">Kingwilliamstown</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> In the early 1830s the Crown Estate resumed possession of land in Ballykilcline following the insanity of the head lessee. The occupational sub-lessees were seven years in arrears with their rent and the result was the Ballykilcline "removals" – <a href="/wiki/Assisted_colonization" title="Assisted colonization">free emigration</a> to the new world in 1846. There was further state-assisted emigration from overpopulated Crown estates during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Famine_(Ireland)" title="Great Famine (Ireland)">Great Famine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> There is evidence of Crown Estate public work schemes to employ the more distressed in improving drainage etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In 1854 a select committee of the House of Lords concluded that the small estates in Ireland should be sold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> 7,000 acres (2,800 ha) were subsequently sold for circa. £25,000 (equal to £2,153,459 today) at auction and £10,000 (equal to £861,384 today) by private treaty: a major <a href="/wiki/Disinvestment" title="Disinvestment">disinvestment</a>, with reinvestment in Great Britain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blue_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-blue-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Article 11 of the 1922 <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_Irish_Free_State" title="Constitution of the Irish Free State">Constitution of the Irish Free State</a> provided that Crown Estate land within the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a> would belong to the state,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> which took over administrative responsibilities on 1 April 1923. At the time of handover, <a href="/wiki/Quit-rent" title="Quit-rent">quit rents</a> totalled £23,418 (equal to £1,225,747 today) and rent from property £1,191 (equal to £62,339 today).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blue_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-blue-21">[21]</a></sup> The estates handed over mostly comprised foreshore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pugh17_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-pugh17-39">[39]</a></sup> The Crown estate in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> in 1960 comprised "a few quit rents ... yielding yearly only £38."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pugh17_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-pugh17-39">[39]</a></sup> By 2016 it had an income of £1.4m, from cables, pipelines and windfarms on the foreshore, and goldmining in Tyrone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Development of the seabed below low tide is hampered by <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_border#Waters_around_Northern_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland–United Kingdom border">a sovereignty dispute</a> with the Republic of Ireland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crown_land_in_Scotland">Crown land in Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crown_Estate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Crown land in Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It was not until 1830 that <a href="/wiki/William_IV_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="William IV of the United Kingdom">King William IV</a> revoked the income from the Crown estates in Scotland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The hereditary land revenues of the Crown in Scotland, formerly under the management of the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Exchequer_(Scotland)" title="Court of Exchequer (Scotland)">Barons of the Exchequer</a>, were transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Commissioners_of_Woods,_Forests_and_Land_Revenues" title="Commissioners of Woods, Forests and Land Revenues">Commissioners of Woods, Forests, Land Revenues, Works and Buildings</a> and their successors under the Crown Lands (Scotland) Acts of 1832, 1833 and 1835.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> These holdings mainly comprised former ecclesiastical land (following the abolition of the episcopacy in 1689) in <a href="/wiki/Caithness" title="Caithness">Caithness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a>, and ancient royal possession in <a href="/wiki/Stirling" title="Stirling">Stirling</a> and Edinburgh, and feudal dues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pugh17_39-2"><a href="#cite_note-pugh17-39">[39]</a></sup> There was virtually no urban property. Most of the present Scottish estate excepting foreshore and salmon fishing is due to inward investment, including <a href="/wiki/Glenlivet_Estate" title="Glenlivet Estate">Glenlivet Estate</a>, the largest area of land managed by the Crown Estate in Scotland, purchased in 1937,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Applegirth, Fochabers and Whitehill estates, purchased in 1963, 1937 and 1969 respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>After winning the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament_general_election,_2011" title="Scottish Parliament general election, 2011">2011 Scottish election</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_National_Party" title="Scottish National Party">Scottish National Party</a> (SNP) called for the <a href="/wiki/Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Devolution in the United Kingdom">devolution</a> of the Crown Estate income to Scotland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snp_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-snp-47">[47]</a></sup> In response to this demand, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scotland_Office" title="Scotland Office">Scotland Office</a> decided against dividing up the Crown Estates. However, plans have been developed to allocate some of the Crown Estate income to the <a href="/wiki/Big_Lottery_Fund" title="Big Lottery Fund">Big Lottery Fund</a>, which would then distribute funds to coastal communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snp_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-snp-47">[47]</a></sup> These plans have also been criticised by the SNP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snp_47-2"><a href="#cite_note-snp-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crown_Estate_Scotland">Crown Estate Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crown_Estate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Crown Estate Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Scottish government has taken control of a portfolio of assets totalling £272 million ($339.6 million) after a devolved Scottish Crown Estate was established, including the rights to develop marine energy projects in the country.
</p><p>A new public body, called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crown_Estate_Scotland_(Interim_Management)" title="Crown Estate Scotland (Interim Management)">Crown Estate Scotland</a> (CES), will oversee seabed areas hosting offshore wind, wave and tidal projects, and some continental shelf activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>Prior to the handover, the Crown Estate owned a multi-million stake in Fort Kinnaird retail park Edinburgh representing about 60% of the value of all crown assets in Scotland. This was not passed to Crown Estates Scotland with other Scottish properties in 2016. Two years later, the Crown Estate sold its stake and used the funds to assume full ownership of the Gallagher Retail Park in Cheltenham.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Present_day">Present day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crown_Estate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Present day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crown_Estate_Act_1961">Crown Estate Act 1961</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crown_Estate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Crown Estate Act 1961">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Crown Estate is now a <a href="/wiki/Statutory_corporation" title="Statutory corporation">statutory corporation</a> run on commercial lines by the Crown Estate Commissioners under the provisions of the <b>Crown Estate Act 1961</b>. Under that Act, the Crown Estate Commissioners have a duty "while maintaining the Crown Estate as an estate in land [...] to maintain and enhance its value and the return obtained from it, but with due regard to the requirements of good management".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The Act provides among other things that (Section 1(5)) "The validity of transactions entered into by the Commissioners shall not be called in question on any suggestion of their not having acted in accordance with the provisions of this Act regulating the exercise of their powers, or of their having otherwise acted in excess of their authority, nor shall any person dealing with the Commissioners be concerned to inquire as to the extent of their authority or the observance of any restrictions on the exercise of their powers".
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Summary_of_the_Act">Summary of the Act</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crown_Estate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Summary of the Act">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Act includes the following:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>The Crown Estate is an estate in land only, apart from cash and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gilts" title="Gilts">gilts</a> holdings necessary for the conduct of business.</li>
<li>The Crown Estate Commissioners, who comprise the main board, are approved by the monarch on the advice of the Prime Minister. They are limited to eight persons.</li>
<li>The board of Commissioners have a duty to:
<ul><li>maintain and enhance the capital value of the estate and its revenue income; but at the same time</li>
<li>take into account the need to observe a high standard of estate management practice.</li></ul></li>
<li>When selling or letting its property the Crown Estate should always seek to achieve the best consideration (i.e. price) which can reasonably be obtained in all the circumstances, but discounting any monopoly value (mainly from ownership of the foreshore and seabed).</li>
<li>The Crown Estate cannot grant leases for a term of longer than 150 years.</li>
<li>The Crown Estate cannot grant land options for more than ten years unless the property is re-valued when the option is exercised.</li>
<li>The Crown Estate cannot borrow money.</li>
<li>Donations can be made for religious or educational purposes connected with the estate or for tenants' welfare. Otherwise, charitable donations are forbidden.</li>
<li>The character of the Windsor Estate (Park and Forest) must be preserved; no part of the estate may be sold.</li>
<li>A report should be submitted to the Queen and to Parliament annually, showing the performance of the estate over the previous year.</li>
<li>The Crown Estate should observe professional accounting practices and distinguish in its accounts between capital and revenue.</li>
<li>Money received as a premium from a tenant on the granting of a new lease should be allocated between capital and revenue as follows:
<ul><li>where the lease is for a term of thirty years or less it must be treated as revenue;</li>
<li>for leases of more than thirty years it must be treated as capital.</li></ul></li></ul>
<p>In 2010 a UK Parliament Treasury Committee report on the Crown Estate, the first for twenty years, reported that
</p>
<ul><li>it is "alarmed" that the Crown Estate in 2007 started investing in joint ventures such as the Gibraltar Limited Partnership, which it says is in "grave" financial difficulties. The Crown Estate owns 50% of the partnership, which owns the <a href="/wiki/Fort_Kinnaird" title="Fort Kinnaird">Fort Kinnaird</a> retail park near Edinburgh;</li>
<li>the Crown Estate has a monopoly over the marine environment, and has focused too strongly on collecting revenues rather than acting in the long-term public interest around ports and harbours;</li>
<li>the quality of residential property management in the urban estate falls short. Consultation processes have lacked transparency, and the Committee was "particularly concerned" that the Crown Estate had failed to consult local bodies which had rights to nominate key workers;</li>
<li>some non-commercial historic properties should be reviewed with a view to transferring management to conservation bodies such as English Heritage;</li>
<li>Ministers should take a greater interest in the Crown Estate, because its overall management struggles to balance revenue generation with acting in the wider public interest.</li></ul>
<p>Crown Estate chief executive Roger Bright said: "We welcome the Committee’s recognition that we run a successful business operation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Holdings">Holdings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crown_Estate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Holdings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_portfolio">Urban portfolio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crown_Estate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Urban portfolio">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>This includes the entirety of <a href="/wiki/Regent_Street" title="Regent Street">Regent Street</a> and around half of <a href="/wiki/St_James%27s" title="St James's">St James's</a> in <a href="/wiki/West_End_of_London" title="West End of London">London's West End</a> as well as retail property across the UK in locations including <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Exeter" title="Exeter">Exeter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nottingham" title="Nottingham">Nottingham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harlow" title="Harlow">Harlow</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Swansea" title="Swansea">Swansea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2002 the Crown Estate began implementing a £1 billion investment programme to improve Regent Street's commercial, retail, and visitor facilities and public realm. In addition, they are investing £500 million in St James's, including a number of major redevelopments.
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Marine_holdings">Marine holdings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crown_Estate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Marine holdings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

</p>
<table class="wikitable">
<tbody><tr>
<td><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foreshore" title="Foreshore">Foreshore</a>
</td>
<td>Approximately 55% of the UK's foreshore is owned by the Crown Estate; other owners of UK foreshore include the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Cornwall" title="Duchy of Cornwall">Duchy of Cornwall</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Lancaster" title="Duchy of Lancaster">Duchy of Lancaster</a>. In Orkney and Shetland, the Crown does not claim ownership of foreshore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schedule_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-schedule-58">[58]</a></sup>
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td>Territorial seabed
</td>
<td>The Crown Estate owns virtually all of the UK's seabed from mean low water to the 12-nautical-mile (22 km) limit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schedule_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-schedule-58">[58]</a></sup>
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td>Continental shelf and extraterritorial rights
</td>
<td>Sovereign rights of the UK in the seabed and its resources vested by the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Shelf_Act_1964_(United_Kingdom)" title="Continental Shelf Act 1964 (United Kingdom)">Continental Shelf Act 1964</a> (sub-soil and substrata below the surface of the seabed, but excluding oil, gas and coal), the Energy Acts 2004 (renewable energy) and 2008 (gas and carbon storage).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schedule_58-2"><a href="#cite_note-schedule-58">[58]</a></sup>
</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The Crown Estate plays a major role in the development of the offshore wind energy industry in the UK. Other commercial activity managed by the Crown Estate on the seabed includes wave and tidal energy, carbon capture and storage, aggregates, submarine cables and pipelines and the mining of <a href="/wiki/Potash" title="Potash">potash</a>. In terms of the foreshore, the Crown Estate issue licences or leases for around 850 aquaculture sites and owns marina space for approximately 18,000 moorings.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_rights_and_interests">Other rights and interests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crown_Estate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Other rights and interests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Other rights and interests include:
</p>
<table class="wikitable">
<tbody><tr>
<td>Shopping centres
</td>
<td><a href="/wiki/CrownGate_Shopping_Centre" title="CrownGate Shopping Centre">CrownGate Shopping Centre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Worcester" title="Worcester">Worcester</a>.
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Westgate_Shopping_Centre,_Oxford" title="Westgate Shopping Centre, Oxford">Westgate Shopping Centre</a> in Oxford and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Princesshay_Shopping_Centre" title="Princesshay Shopping Centre">Princesshay Shopping Centre</a> in Exeter are a 50:50 joint venture partnership with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_Securities" title="Land Securities">Land Securities</a>. The Crown Estate also has a 4.97% share of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lend_Lease_Group" title="Lend Lease Group">Lend Lease Retail Partnership</a> which provides an equity interest in the <a href="/wiki/Bluewater_(shopping_centre)" title="Bluewater (shopping centre)">Bluewater</a> Shopping Centre in Kent and the <a href="/wiki/Touchwood,_Solihull" title="Touchwood, Solihull">Touchwood</a> Shopping Centre in Solihull.
</p>
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td>Retail parks
</td>
<td>Crown Point Shopping Park in <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>, MK1 Shopping Park in <a href="/wiki/Milton_Keynes" title="Milton Keynes">Milton Keynes</a>, Aintree Shopping Park in Merseyside, Altrincham Retail Park in <a href="/wiki/Trafford" title="Trafford">Trafford</a>, Bath Road Shopping Park in <a href="/wiki/Slough" title="Slough">Slough</a>, Morfa Shopping Park in <a href="/wiki/Swansea" title="Swansea">Swansea</a>, Ocean Retail Park in <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth" title="Portsmouth">Portsmouth</a>, Queensgate Centre in <a href="/wiki/Harlow" title="Harlow">Harlow</a>, South Aylesford Retail Park in <a href="/wiki/Maidstone" title="Maidstone">Maidstone</a>, Apsley Mills Retail Park in <a href="/wiki/Hemel_Hempstead" title="Hemel Hempstead">Hemel Hempstead</a>, Victoria Retail Park in Nottingham, <a href="/wiki/Morfa,_Swansea" title="Morfa, Swansea">Morfa Shopping Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Swansea" title="Swansea">Swansea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Coliseum Retail Park in Cheshire Oaks, <a href="/wiki/Ellesmere_Port" title="Ellesmere Port">Ellesmere Port</a> has been bought for £81m.
<p><a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Fort_Kinnaird" title="Fort Kinnaird">Fort Kinnaird</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham" title="Cheltenham">Cheltenham</a>'s Gallagher Retail Park and <a href="/wiki/Warwick" title="Warwick">Warwick</a>'s Leamington Shopping Park are owned 50/50 through "The Gibraltar Limited Partnership" with The Hercules Unit Trust, a <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a>-based property unit trust. The estate recently purchased the new Rushden Lakes site in Northamptonshire from its developers.
</p>
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td>Retail/office buildings
</td>
<td>Princes Street, London W1B (near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxford_Circus" title="Oxford Circus">Oxford Circus</a>) with a 66.67% interest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schedule_58-3"><a href="#cite_note-schedule-58">[58]</a></sup>
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td>Savoy Estate apportionment
</td>
<td>Right to receive 23% of the income from the Duchy of Lancaster's Savoy Estate in London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schedule_58-4"><a href="#cite_note-schedule-58">[58]</a></sup>
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td>Native mussels and oysters in Scotland
</td>
<td>Wild crustaceans (does not include cultivated crustaceans)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="Mussels and oysters are not crustaceans; is this a separate item? (September 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td>Reversionary and contingent interests
</td>
<td>Some properties are sold by the Crown Estate for public benefit (such as educational or religious use) with a reverter clause, which means ownership may revert to the Crown Estate in the event of a change of use.
<p>Hereditary properties of the monarch currently in government use will revert to the Crown Estate in the event of the government use ceasing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schedule_58-5"><a href="#cite_note-schedule-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p>
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td>Escheated land
</td>
<td>Land that has no owner other than the Crown as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_paramount" title="Lord paramount">lord paramount</a> of the whole soil of the country. Escheat can result from bankruptcy or the dissolution of companies. Freehold land owned by dissolved companies which were registered in England or Wales are dealt with by the Treasury Solicitor as <i><a href="/wiki/Bona_vacantia" title="Bona vacantia">bona vacantia</a></i>.
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td>Licences and right granted at nil rent
</td>
<td>Includes: water mains, cables, substations and war memorials.
</td></tr></tbody></table>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Finances">Finances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crown_Estate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Finances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 2015/2016 fiscal year, the Crown Estate's property evaluation was £12 billion with a £304.1 million net revenue profit (up 6.7%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical">Historical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crown_Estate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Historical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chairmen_and_chief_executives_of_the_Crown_Estate_Commissioners">Chairmen and chief executives of the Crown Estate Commissioners</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crown_Estate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Chairmen and chief executives of the Crown Estate Commissioners">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Chairmen (First Commissioner)</b>
</p>
<ul><li>1955–62 – <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Trustram_Eve,_1st_Baron_Silsoe" title="Malcolm Trustram Eve, 1st Baron Silsoe">Sir Malcolm Trustram Eve</a> (later Lord Silsoe) (1894–1976)</li>
<li>1962–77 – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Drummond,_8th_Earl_of_Perth" title="John Drummond, 8th Earl of Perth">The Earl of Perth</a> (1907–2002)</li>
<li>1977–80 – <a href="/wiki/George_Thomson,_Baron_Thomson_of_Monifieth" title="George Thomson, Baron Thomson of Monifieth">Lord Thomson of Monifieth</a> (1921–2008)</li>

<li>1985–95 – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Murray,_8th_Earl_of_Mansfield_and_Mansfield" title="William Murray, 8th Earl of Mansfield and Mansfield">The Earl of Mansfield and Mansfield</a> (1930–2015)</li>
<li>1995–2002 – <a href="/wiki/Denys_Henderson" title="Denys Henderson">Sir Denys Hartley Henderson</a> (1932–2016)</li>
<li>2002–2009 – <a href="/wiki/Ian_David_Grant" title="Ian David Grant">Sir Ian David Grant</a> (born 1943)</li>
<li>2010–2016 – <a href="/wiki/Stuart_Hampson" title="Stuart Hampson">Sir Stuart Hampson</a> (born 1947)</li>
<li>2016– <a href="/wiki/Robin_Budenberg" title="Robin Budenberg">Robin Budenberg</a> (born 1959)</li></ul>
<p><b>Chief executives (Second Commissioner)</b>
</p>
<ul><li>1955–60 – Sir Ronald Montague Joseph Harris (1913–1995)</li>
<li>1960–68 – Sir Jack Alexander Sutherland-Harris (1908–1986)</li>
<li>1968–78 – Sir William Alan Wood (1916–2010)</li>
<li>1978–83 – Sir John Michael Moore (born 1921)</li>
<li>1983–89 – Dr Keith Dexter (1928–1989)</li>
<li>1989-2001– <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Howes" title="Christopher Howes">Sir Christopher Howes</a> (born 1942)</li>
<li>2001–2011 Roger Martin Francis Bright (born 1951)</li>
<li>2012– <a href="/wiki/Alison_Nimmo" title="Alison Nimmo">Alison Nimmo</a> (born 1964)</li></ul>
<p>The chairman (formally titled "first commissioner") is part-time. The chief executive (the "second commissioner") is the only full-time executive member of the Crown Estate's board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p>
