<p><b>Ruminants</b> are <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> that are able to acquire nutrients from plant-based food by <a href="/wiki/Enteric_fermentation" title="Enteric fermentation">fermenting</a> it in a specialized <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomach</a> prior to digestion, principally through microbial actions. The process, which takes place in the front part of the digestion system and therefore is called <a href="/wiki/Foregut_fermentation" title="Foregut fermentation">foregut fermentation</a>, typically requires the fermented ingesta (known as <a href="/wiki/Cud" title="Cud">cud</a>) to be regurgitated and chewed again. The process of rechewing the cud to further break down plant matter and stimulate digestion is called <b>rumination</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The word "ruminant" comes from the Latin <i>ruminare</i>, which means "to chew over again".
</p><p>The roughly 200 species of living ruminants include both domestic and wild species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup> Ruminating mammals include <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goat" title="Goat">goats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">sheep</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giraffe" title="Giraffe">giraffes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yak" title="Yak">yaks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">deer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antelope" title="Antelope">antelope</a>, and some <a href="/wiki/Macropodidae" title="Macropodidae">macropods</a> (<a href="/wiki/Kangaroo" title="Kangaroo">kangaroos</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fowler,_M.E._2010_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fowler,_M.E._2010-4">[4]</a></sup> It has also been suggested that <a href="/wiki/Notoungulata" title="Notoungulata">notoungulates</a> also relied on rumination, as opposed to other <a href="/wiki/Atlantogenata" title="Atlantogenata">atlantogenates</a> that rely on the more typical <a href="/wiki/Hindgut_fermentation" title="Hindgut fermentation">hindgut fermentation</a>, though this is not entirely certain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Taxonomically, the suborder <a href="/wiki/Ruminantia" title="Ruminantia">Ruminantia</a> (also known as ruminants) is a lineage of herbivorous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artiodactyla" title="Artiodactyla">artiodactyls</a> that includes the most advanced and widespread of the world's <a href="/wiki/Ungulate" title="Ungulate">ungulates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The term 'ruminant' is not synonymous with Ruminantia.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The suborder Ruminantia includes many ruminant species, but does not include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tylopod" title="Tylopod">tylopods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marsupial" title="Marsupial">marsupials</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fowler,_M.E._2010_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fowler,_M.E._2010-4">[4]</a></sup> The suborder Ruminantia includes six different families: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tragulidae" title="Tragulidae">Tragulidae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giraffidae" title="Giraffidae">Giraffidae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antilocapridae" title="Antilocapridae">Antilocapridae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Musk_deer" title="Musk deer">Moschidae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">Cervidae</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bovidae" title="Bovidae">Bovidae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The primary difference between ruminants and nonruminants is that ruminants' stomachs have four compartments:
</p>
<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Rumen" title="Rumen">rumen</a>—primary site of microbial fermentation</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Reticulum_(anatomy)" title="Reticulum (anatomy)">reticulum</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Omasum" title="Omasum">omasum</a>—receives chewed cud, and absorbs volatile fatty acids</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Abomasum" title="Abomasum">abomasum</a>—true stomach</li></ol>
<p>The first two chambers are the rumen and the reticulum. These two compartments make up the fermentation vat, they are the major site of microbial activity. Fermentation is crucial to digestion because it breaks down complex carbohydrates, such as cellulose, and enables the animal to utilize them. Microbes function best in a warm, moist, anaerobic environment with a temperature range of 100 to 108 degrees F and a pH between 6.0 and 6.4. Without the help of microbes, ruminants would not be able to utilize nutrients from forages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">[8]</a></sup> The food is mixed with <a href="/wiki/Saliva" title="Saliva">saliva</a> and separates into layers of solid and liquid material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Solids clump together to form the cud or <a href="/wiki/Bolus_(digestion)" title="Bolus (digestion)">bolus</a>.
</p><p>The cud is then regurgitated and chewed to completely mix it with saliva and to break down the particle size. Smaller particle size allows for increased nutrient absorption. Fiber, especially <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hemicellulose" title="Hemicellulose">hemicellulose</a>, is primarily broken down in these chambers by microbes (mostly <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, as well as some <a href="/wiki/Protozoa" title="Protozoa">protozoa</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">yeast</a>) into the three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volatile_fatty_acids" title="Volatile fatty acids">volatile fatty acids</a> (VFAs): <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Propionic_acid" title="Propionic acid">propionic acid</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Butyric_acid" title="Butyric acid">butyric acid</a>. Protein and nonstructural carbohydrate (<a href="/wiki/Pectin" title="Pectin">pectin</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sugars" title="Sugars">sugars</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Starches" title="Starches">starches</a>) are also fermented. Saliva is very important because it provides liquid for the microbial population, recirculates nitrogen and minerals, and acts as a buffer for the rumen pH.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">[8]</a></sup> The type of feed the animal consumes affects the amount of saliva that is produced. 
</p><p>Though the rumen and reticulum have different names, they have very similar tissue layers and textures, making it difficult to visually separate them. They also perform similar tasks. Together, these chambers are called the reticulorumen. The degraded digesta, which is now in the lower liquid part of the reticulorumen, then passes into the next chamber, the omasum. This chamber controls what is able to pass into the abomasum. It keeps the particle size as small as possible in order to pass into the abomasum. The omasum also absorbs volatile fatty acids and ammonia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>After this, the digesta is moved to the true stomach, the abomasum. This is the gastric compartment of the ruminant stomach. The abomasum is the direct equivalent of the <a href="/wiki/Monogastric" title="Monogastric">monogastric</a> stomach, and digesta is digested here in much the same way. This compartment releases acids and enzymes that further digest the material passing through. This is also where the ruminant digests the microbes produced in the rumen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">[8]</a></sup> Digesta is finally moved into the <a href="/wiki/Small_intestine" title="Small intestine">small intestine</a>, where the digestion and absorption of nutrients occurs. The small intestine is the main site of nutrient absorption. the surface area of the digesta is greatly increased here because of the villi that are in the small intestine. This increased surface area allows for greater nutrient absorption. Microbes produced in the reticulorumen are also digested in the small intestine. After the small intestine is the large intestine. The major roles here are breaking down mainly fiber by fermentation with microbes, absorption of water (ions and minerals) and other fermented products, and also expelling waste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Fermentation continues in the <a href="/wiki/Large_intestine" title="Large intestine">large intestine</a> in the same way as in the reticulorumen.
</p><p>Only small amounts of <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> are absorbed from dietary carbohydrates. Most dietary carbohydrates are fermented into VFAs in the rumen. The glucose needed as energy for the brain and for <a href="/wiki/Lactose" title="Lactose">lactose</a> and milk fat in milk production, as well as other uses, comes from nonsugar sources, such as the VFA propionate, glycerol, lactate, and protein. The VFA propionate is used for around 70% of the glucose and <a href="/wiki/Glycogen" title="Glycogen">glycogen</a> produced and protein for another 20% (50% under starvation conditions).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_and_taxonomy">Classification and taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruminant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification and taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hofmann and Stewart divided ruminants into three major categories based on their feed type and feeding habits: concentrate selectors, intermediate types, and grass/roughage eaters, with the assumption that feeding habits in ruminants cause morphological differences in their digestive systems, including salivary glands, rumen size, and rumen papillae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> However, Woodall found that there is little correlation between the fiber content of a ruminant's diet and morphological characteristics, meaning that the categorical divisions of ruminants by Hofmann and Stewart warrant further research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Also, some mammals are <a href="/wiki/Pseudoruminant" title="Pseudoruminant">pseudoruminants</a>, which have a three-compartment stomach instead of four like ruminants. The <a href="/wiki/Hippopotamidae" title="Hippopotamidae">Hippopotamidae</a> (comprising <a href="/wiki/Hippopotamus" title="Hippopotamus">hippopotami</a>) are well-known examples. Pseudoruminants, like traditional ruminants, are foregut fermentors and most ruminate or chew <a href="/wiki/Cud" title="Cud">cud</a>. However, their anatomy and method of digestion differs significantly from that of a four-chambered ruminant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fowler,_M.E._2010_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Fowler,_M.E._2010-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Monogastric <a href="/wiki/Herbivore" title="Herbivore">herbivores</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Rhinoceros" title="Rhinoceros">rhinoceroses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horses</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rabbits" title="Rabbits">rabbits</a>, are not ruminants, as they have a simple single-chambered stomach. These <a href="/wiki/Hindgut_fermentation" title="Hindgut fermentation">hindgut fermenters</a> digest cellulose in an enlarged <a href="/wiki/Cecum" title="Cecum">cecum</a> through the reingestion of the <a href="/wiki/Cecotrope" title="Cecotrope">cecotrope</a>.
</p>
<h2><span id="Abundance.2C_distribution.2C_and_domestication"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Abundance,_distribution,_and_domestication">Abundance, distribution, and domestication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruminant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Abundance, distribution, and domestication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wild ruminants number at least 75 million and are native to all continents except Antarctica.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup> Nearly 90% of all species are found in Eurasia and Africa. Species inhabit a wide range of climates (from tropic to arctic) and habitats (from open plains to forests).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hackmann_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-hackmann-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>The population of domestic ruminants is greater than 3.5 billion, with cattle, sheep, and goats accounting for about 95% of the total population. Goats were domesticated in the Near East <i>circa</i> 8000 BC. Most other species were domesticated by 2500 BC., either in the Near East or southern Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hackmann_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-hackmann-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ruminant_physiology">Ruminant physiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruminant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ruminant physiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ruminating animals have various physiological features that enable them to survive in nature. One feature of ruminants is their continuously growing teeth. During grazing, the silica content in <a href="/wiki/Forage" title="Forage">forage</a> causes abrasion of the teeth. This abrasion is compensated for by continuous tooth growth throughout the ruminant's life, as opposed to humans or other nonruminants, whose teeth stop growing after a particular age. Most ruminants do not have upper incisors; instead, they have a thick <a href="/wiki/Dental_pad" title="Dental pad">dental pad</a> to thoroughly chew plant-based food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Another feature of ruminants is the large ruminal storage capacity that gives them the ability to consume feed rapidly and complete the chewing process later. This is known as rumination, which consists of the regurgitation of feed, rechewing, resalivation, and reswallowing. Rumination reduces particle size, which enhances microbial function and allows the digesta to pass more easily through the digestive tract.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rumen_microbiology">Rumen microbiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruminant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Rumen microbiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">Vertebrates</a> lack the ability to hydrolyse the beta [1–4] glycosidic bond of plant cellulose due to the lack of the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Cellulase" title="Cellulase">cellulase</a>. Thus, ruminants must completely depend on the microbial flora, present in the rumen or hindgut, to digest cellulose. Digestion of food in the rumen is primarily carried out by the rumen microflora, which contains dense populations of several species of <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Protozoa" title="Protozoa">protozoa</a>, sometimes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yeasts" title="Yeasts">yeasts</a> and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a> – 1 ml of rumen is estimated to contain 10–50 billion bacteria and 1 million protozoa, as well as several yeasts and fungi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since the environment inside a rumen is <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/anaerobic" title="wikt:anaerobic">anaerobic</a>, most of these microbial species are <a href="/wiki/Obligate_anaerobe" title="Obligate anaerobe">obligate</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Facultative_anaerobic_organism" title="Facultative anaerobic organism">facultative</a> anaerobes that can decompose complex plant material, such as <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hemicellulose" title="Hemicellulose">hemicellulose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Protein_(nutrient)" title="Protein (nutrient)">proteins</a>. The hydrolysis of cellulose results in sugars, which are further fermented to acetate, lactate, propionate, butyrate, carbon dioxide, and methane.
</p><p>As bacteria conduct fermentation in the rumen, they consume about 10% of the carbon, 60% of the phosphorus, and 80% of the nitrogen that the ruminant ingests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Callewaert_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Callewaert-19">[19]</a></sup> To reclaim these nutrients, the ruminant then digests the bacteria in the <a href="/wiki/Abomasum" title="Abomasum">abomasum</a>. The enzyme <a href="/wiki/Lysozyme" title="Lysozyme">lysozyme</a> has adapted to facilitate digestion of bacteria in the ruminant abomasum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irwin_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Irwin-20">[20]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pancreatic_ribonuclease" title="Pancreatic ribonuclease">Pancreatic ribonuclease</a> also degrades bacterial RNA in the ruminant small intestine as a source of nitrogen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>During grazing, ruminants produce large amounts of saliva – estimates range from 100 to 150 litres of saliva per day for a cow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The role of saliva is to provide ample fluid for rumen fermentation and to act as a buffering agent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Rumen fermentation produces large amounts of organic acids, thus maintaining the appropriate pH of rumen fluids is a critical factor in rumen fermentation. After digesta pass through the rumen, the omasum absorbs excess fluid so that digestive enzymes and acid in the abomasum are not diluted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tannin_toxicity_in_ruminant_animals">Tannin toxicity in ruminant animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruminant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tannin toxicity in ruminant animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tannin" title="Tannin">Tannins</a> are <a href="/wiki/Polyphenol" title="Polyphenol">phenolic compounds</a> that are commonly found in plants. Found in the leaf, bud, seed, root, and stem tissues, tannins are widely distributed in many different species of plants. Tannins are separated into two classes: hydrolysable tannins and <a href="/wiki/Condensed_tannin" title="Condensed tannin">condensed tannins</a>. Depending on their concentration and nature, either class can have adverse or beneficial effects. Tannins can be beneficial, having been shown to increase milk production, wool growth, ovulation rate, and lambing percentage, as well as reducing bloat risk and reducing internal parasite burdens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barry_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barry-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Tannins can be toxic to ruminants, in that they precipitate proteins, making them unavailable for digestion, and they inhibit the absorption of nutrients by reducing the populations of proteolytic rumen bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barry_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barry-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bate-Smith_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bate-Smith-26">[26]</a></sup> Very high levels of tannin intake can produce <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxicity</a> that can even cause death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Animals that normally consume tannin-rich plants can develop defensive mechanisms against tannins, such as the strategic deployment of <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Extracellular" title="Extracellular">extracellular</a> <a href="/wiki/Polysaccharide" title="Polysaccharide">polysaccharides</a> that have a high affinity to binding to tannins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barry_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-Barry-25">[25]</a></sup> Some ruminants (goats, deer, elk, moose) are able to consume feed high in tannins (leaves, twigs, bark) due to the presence in their saliva of tannin-binding proteins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_importance">Religious importance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruminant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Religious importance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/613_Mitzvot" title="613 Mitzvot">Law of Moses</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> only allowed the eating of mammals that had <a href="/wiki/Cloven_hoof" title="Cloven hoof">cloven hooves</a> (i.e. members of the order <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artiodactyla" title="Artiodactyla">Artiodactyla</a>) and "that chew the cud",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> a stipulation preserved to this day in Jewish <a href="/wiki/Kashrut" title="Kashrut">dietary laws</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruminant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The verb 'to ruminate' has been extended <a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">metaphorically</a> to mean to ponder thoughtfully or to <a href="/wiki/Meditation" title="Meditation">meditate</a> on some topic. Similarly, ideas may be 'chewed on' or 'digested'. 'Chew the (one's) cud' is to reflect or meditate. In psychology, <a href="/wiki/Rumination_(psychology)" title="Rumination (psychology)">"rumination"</a> refers to a pattern of thinking, and is unrelated to digestive physiology.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ruminants_and_climate_change">Ruminants and climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruminant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Ruminants and climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_agriculture" title="Climate change and agriculture">Climate change and agriculture</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">Methane</a> is produced by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archea" title="Archea">archea</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methanogens" title="Methanogens">methanogens</a>, described above within the rumen, and this methane is released to the atmosphere. The rumen is the major site of methane production in ruminants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Methane is a strong <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_potential" title="Global warming potential">global warming potential</a> of 86 compared to CO<sub>2</sub> over a 20-year period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AR5WG1_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-AR5WG1-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2010, <a href="/wiki/Enteric_fermentation" title="Enteric fermentation">enteric fermentation</a> accounted for 43% of the total greenhouse gas emissions from all agricultural activity in the world,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> 26% of the total <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions from agricultural activity in the U.S., and 22% of the total U.S. <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inventory_of_U.S._Greenhouse_Gas_Emissions_and_Sinks:_1990-2014_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Inventory_of_U.S._Greenhouse_Gas_Emissions_and_Sinks:_1990-2014-35">[35]</a></sup> The meat from domestically-raised ruminants has a higher carbon equivalent footprint than other meats or vegetarian sources of protein based on a global meta-analysis of lifecycle assessment studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Methane production by meat animals, principally ruminants, is estimated 15–20% global production of methane, unless the animals were hunted in the wild.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
