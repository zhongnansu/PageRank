<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Standard conditions for temperature and pressure</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Standard_state" title="Standard state">Standard state</a>.</div>
<p><b>Standard conditions for temperature and pressure</b> are <a href="/wiki/Technical_standard" title="Technical standard">standard</a> sets of conditions for experimental measurements to be established to allow comparisons to be made between different sets of data. The most used standards are those of the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Pure_and_Applied_Chemistry" title="International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry">International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry</a> (IUPAC) and the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">National Institute of Standards and Technology</a> (NIST), although these are not universally accepted standards. Other organizations have established a variety of alternative definitions for their standard reference conditions.
</p><p>In chemistry, IUPAC changed the definition of <b>standard temperature and pressure</b> (<b>STP</b>) in 1982:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUPAC_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-IUPAC-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Until 1982, STP was defined as a <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> of 273.15 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a> (0 °C, 32 °F) and an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absolute_pressure" title="Absolute pressure">absolute pressure</a> of exactly 1 <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">atm</a> (1.01325 × 10<sup>5</sup> <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">Pa</a>).</li>
<li>Since 1982, STP is defined as a <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> of 273.15 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a> (0 °C, 32 °F) and an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absolute_pressure" title="Absolute pressure">absolute pressure</a> of exactly 10<sup>5</sup> <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">Pa</a> (100 kPa, 1 bar).</li></ul>
<p>STP should not be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Standard_state" title="Standard state">standard state</a> commonly used in thermodynamic evaluations of the <a href="/wiki/Gibbs_free_energy" title="Gibbs free energy">Gibbs energy</a> of a reaction.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">NIST</a> uses a temperature of 20 °C (293.15 K, 68
 °F) and an absolute pressure of 1 atm (14.696 psi, 101.325 kPa). This standard is also called <b>normal temperature and pressure</b> (abbreviated as <b>NTP</b>).
</p><p>The International Standard Metric Conditions for natural gas and similar fluids are 288.15 K (15.00 °C; 59.00 °F) and 101.325 kPa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISO13443_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ISO13443-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Commerce" title="Commerce">commerce</a>, standard conditions for temperature and pressure are often necessary to define the standard reference conditions to express the volumes of gases and liquids and related quantities such as the rate of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volumetric_flow" title="Volumetric flow">volumetric flow</a> (the volumes of gases vary significantly with temperature and pressure) – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_cubic_meters_per_second" title="Standard cubic meters per second">standard cubic meters per second</a> (<b>sm3/s</b>), and <b>normal cubic meters per second</b> (<b>nm3/s</b>). However, many technical publications (books, journals, advertisements for equipment and machinery) simply state "standard conditions" without specifying them, often leading to confusion and errors. Good practice always incorporates the reference conditions of temperature and pressure.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Standard_conditions_for_temperature_and_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Past_uses">Past uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Standard_conditions_for_temperature_and_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Past uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Before 1918, many professionals and scientists using the metric system of units defined the standard reference conditions of temperature and pressure for expressing gas volumes as being 15 °C (288.15 K; 59.00 °F) and 101.325 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">kPa</a> (1.00 <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">atm</a>; 760 <a href="/wiki/Torr" title="Torr">Torr</a>). During those same years, the most commonly used standard reference conditions for people using the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_units" title="Imperial units">imperial</a> or <a href="/wiki/United_States_customary_units" title="United States customary units">U.S. customary</a> systems was 60 °F (15.56 °C; 288.71 K) and 14.696 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pound-force_per_square_inch" title="Pound-force per square inch">psi</a> (1 atm) because it was almost universally used by the oil and gas industries worldwide. The above definitions are no longer the most commonly used in either system of units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_use">Current use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Standard_conditions_for_temperature_and_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Current use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many different definitions of standard reference conditions are currently being used by organizations all over the world. The table below lists a few of them, but there are more. Some of these organizations used other standards in the past. For example, IUPAC has, since 1982, defined standard reference conditions as being 0 °C and 100 kPa (1 bar), in contrast to its old standard of 0 °C and 101.325 kPa (1 atm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUPAC_2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-IUPAC_2-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Natural gas companies in Europe, Australia, and South America have adopted 15 °C (59 °F) and 101.325 kPa (14.696 psi) as their standard gas volume reference conditions, used as the base values for defining the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_cubic_meter" title="Standard cubic meter">standard cubic meter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Also, the <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">International Organization for Standardization</a> (ISO), the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA) and <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">National Institute of Standards and Technology</a> (NIST) each have more than one definition of standard reference conditions in their various standards and regulations.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_Standard_Atmosphere">International Standard Atmosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Standard_conditions_for_temperature_and_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: International Standard Atmosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Aeronautics" title="Aeronautics">aeronautics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">fluid dynamics</a> the "<a href="/wiki/International_Standard_Atmosphere" title="International Standard Atmosphere">International Standard Atmosphere</a>" (ISA) is a specification of pressure, temperature, density, and speed of sound at each altitude. The International Standard Atmosphere is representative of atmospheric conditions at mid latitudes. In the USA this information is specified the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Standard_Atmosphere" title="U.S. Standard Atmosphere">U.S. Standard Atmosphere</a> which is identical to the "International Standard Atmosphere" at all altitudes up to 65,000 feet above sea level.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Standard_laboratory_conditions">Standard laboratory conditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Standard_conditions_for_temperature_and_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Standard laboratory conditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to the fact that many definitions of standard temperature and pressure differ in temperature significantly from standard laboratory temperatures (e.g., 0 °C vs. ~25 °C), reference is often made to "standard laboratory conditions" (a term deliberately chosen to be different from the term "standard conditions for temperature and pressure", despite its semantic near identity when interpreted literally). However, what is a "standard" <a href="/wiki/Laboratory" title="Laboratory">laboratory</a> temperature and pressure is inevitably geography-bound, given that different parts of the world differ in climate, altitude and the degree of use of heat/cooling in the workplace. For example, schools in <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> use 25 °C at 100 kPa for standard laboratory conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[30]</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/ASTM_International" title="ASTM International">ASTM International</a> has published <a href="/wiki/Technical_standard" title="Technical standard">Standard</a> ASTM E41- Terminology Relating to Conditioning and hundreds of special conditions for particular materials and <a href="/wiki/Test_method" title="Test method">test methods</a>. Other <a href="/wiki/Standards_organization" title="Standards organization">standards organizations</a> also have specialized standard test conditions.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Molar_volume_of_a_gas">Molar volume of a gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Standard_conditions_for_temperature_and_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Molar volume of a gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is equally as important to indicate the applicable reference conditions of temperature and pressure when stating the <a href="/wiki/Molar_volume" title="Molar volume">molar volume</a> of a gas<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[31]</a></sup> as it is when expressing a gas volume or volumetric flow rate. Stating the molar volume of a gas without indicating the reference conditions of temperature and pressure has very little meaning and can cause confusion.
</p><p>The molar volume of gases around STP and at atmospheric pressure can be calculated with an accuracy that is usually sufficient by using the <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas_law" title="Ideal gas law">ideal gas law</a>. The molar volume of any ideal gas may be calculated at various standard reference conditions as shown below:
</p>
<ul><li><i>V</i><sub>m</sub> = 8.3145 × 273.15 / 101.325 = 22.414 <a href="/wiki/Decimetre" title="Decimetre">dm</a><sup>3</sup>/mol at 0 °C and 101.325 kPa</li>
<li><i>V</i><sub>m</sub> = 8.3145 × 273.15 / 100.000 = 22.711 dm<sup>3</sup>/mol at 0 °C and 100 kPa</li>
<li><i>V</i><sub>m</sub> = 8.3145 × 298.15 / 101.325 = 24.466 dm<sup>3</sup>/mol at 25 °C and 101.325 kPa</li>
<li><i>V</i><sub>m</sub> = 8.3145 × 298.15 / 100.000 = 24.790 dm<sup>3</sup>/mol at 25 °C and 100 kPa</li>
<li><i>V</i><sub>m</sub> = 10.7316 × 519.67 / 14.696 = 379.48 ft<sup>3</sup>/lbmol at 60 °F and 14.696 psi (or about 0.8366 ft<sup>3</sup>/gram mole)</li>
<li><i>V</i><sub>m</sub> = 10.7316 × 519.67 / 14.730 = 378.61 ft<sup>3</sup>/lbmol at 60 °F and 14.73 psi</li></ul>
<p>Technical literature can be confusing because many authors fail to explain whether they are using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ideal_gas_constant" title="Ideal gas constant">ideal gas constant</a> <i>R</i>, or the specific gas constant <i>R</i><sub>s</sub>. The relationship between the two constants is <i>R</i><sub>s</sub> = <i>R</i> / <i>m</i>, where <i>m</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Molecular_mass" title="Molecular mass">molecular mass</a> of the gas.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Standard_Atmosphere" title="US Standard Atmosphere">US Standard Atmosphere</a> (USSA) uses 8.31432 m<sup>3</sup>·Pa/(mol·K) as the value of <i>R</i>. However, the USSA,1976 does recognize that this value is not consistent with the values of the Avogadro constant and the Boltzmann constant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USSA1976_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-USSA1976-34">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
