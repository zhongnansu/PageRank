<p>In <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>, in particular <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">fluid dynamics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrometry" title="Hydrometry">hydrometry</a>, the <b>volumetric flow rate</b> (also known as <b>volume flow rate</b>, <b>rate of fluid flow</b> or <b>volume velocity</b>) is the volume of fluid which passes per unit time; usually represented by the symbol <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">Q</span> (sometimes <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">V̇</span>). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_unit" title="SI unit">SI unit</a> is m<sup>3</sup>/s (<a href="/wiki/Cubic_metre_per_second" title="Cubic metre per second">cubic metres per second</a>). Another unit used is sccm (standard cubic centimeters per minute).
</p><p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_customary_units" title="US customary units">US customary units</a> and <a href="/wiki/Imperial_units" title="Imperial units">imperial units</a>, volumetric flow rate is often expressed as ft<sup>3</sup>/s (<a href="/wiki/Cubic_foot" title="Cubic foot">cubic feet</a> per second) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallons_per_minute" title="Gallons per minute">gallons per minute</a> (either US or imperial definitions).
</p><p>Volumetric flow rate should not be confused with <a href="/wiki/Volumetric_flux" title="Volumetric flux">volumetric flux</a>, as defined by <a href="/wiki/Darcy%27s_law" title="Darcy's law">Darcy's law</a> and represented by the symbol <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">q</span>, with units of m<sup>3</sup>/(m<sup>2</sup>·s), that is, m·s<sup>−1</sup>. The integration of a <a href="/wiki/Flux" title="Flux">flux</a> over an area gives the volumetric flow rate.
</p>

<p>That is, the flow of <a href="/wiki/Volume" title="Volume">volume</a> of fluid <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">V</span> through a surface per unit time <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">t</span>.
</p><p>Since this is only the time derivative of volume, a scalar quantity, the volumetric flow rate is also a scalar quantity. The change in volume is the amount that flows <i>after</i> crossing the boundary for some time duration, not simply the initial amount of volume at the boundary minus the final amount at the boundary, since the change in volume flowing through the area would be zero for steady flow.
</p>
