<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Natural-gas processing</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:301px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NaturalGasProcessingPlant.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="197" data-file-width="299" height="197" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f3/NaturalGasProcessingPlant.jpg" width="299"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NaturalGasProcessingPlant.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A natural-gas processing plant</div></div></div>
<p><b>Natural-gas processing</b> is a complex industrial process designed to clean raw natural gas by separating impurities and various <a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound" title="Volatile organic compound">non-methane hydrocarbons</a> and fluids to produce what is known as <i>pipeline quality</i> dry natural gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Natural-gas processing begins at the well head. The composition of the raw natural gas extracted from producing wells depends on the type, depth, and location of the underground deposit and the geology of the area. Oil and natural gas are often found together in the same reservoir. The natural gas produced from oil wells is generally classified as <i>associated-dissolved</i>, meaning that the natural gas is associated with or dissolved in crude oil. Natural gas production absent any association with crude oil is classified as “non-associated.” In 2009, 89 percent of U.S. wellhead production of natural gas was non-associated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Natural-gas processing plants purify raw <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> by removing common contaminants such as water, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a> (H<sub>2</sub>S). Some of the substances which contaminate natural gas have economic value and are further processed or sold. A fully operational plant delivers pipeline-quality dry natural gas that can be used as <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a> by residential, commercial and industrial consumers.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_raw-natural-gas_wells">Types of raw-natural-gas wells</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural-gas_processing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types of raw-natural-gas wells">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Raw natural gas comes primarily from any one of three types of wells: crude oil wells, gas wells, and condensate wells.
</p><p>Natural gas that comes from crude oil wells is typically called <i>associated gas</i>. This gas can have existed as a gas cap above the crude oil in the underground formation, or could have been dissolved in the crude oil.
</p><p>Natural gas from gas wells and from condensate wells, in which there is little or no crude oil, is called <i>non-associated gas</i>. Gas wells typically produce only raw natural gas, while condensate wells produce raw natural gas along with other low molecular weight hydrocarbons. Those that are liquid at ambient conditions (i.e., <a href="/wiki/Pentane" title="Pentane">pentane</a> and heavier) are called <i><a href="/wiki/Natural-gas_condensate" title="Natural-gas condensate">natural-gas condensate</a></i> (sometimes also called <i><a href="/wiki/Natural_gasoline" title="Natural gasoline">natural gasoline</a></i> or simply <i>condensate</i>).
</p><p>Natural gas is called <i>sweet gas</i> when relatively free of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>; gas that does contain hydrogen sulfide is called <i><a href="/wiki/Sour_gas" title="Sour gas">sour gas</a></i>. Natural gas, or any other gas mixture, containing significant quantities of hydrogen sulfide, carbon dioxide or similar acidic gases, is called <i><a href="/wiki/Acid_gas" title="Acid gas">acid gas</a>.</i>
</p><p>Raw natural gas can also come from methane deposits in the pores of coal seams, and especially in a more concentrated state of <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a> onto the surface of the coal itself. Such gas is referred to as <i>coalbed gas</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Coalbed_methane" title="Coalbed methane">coalbed methane</a></i> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_seam_gas" title="Coal seam gas">coal seam gas</a></i> in Australia). Coalbed gas has become an important source of energy in recent decades.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contaminants_in_raw_natural_gas">Contaminants in raw natural gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural-gas_processing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Contaminants in raw natural gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Raw natural gas typically consists primarily of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> (CH<sub>4</sub>), the shortest and lightest <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a> molecule. It also contains varying amounts of:
</p>
<ul><li>Heavier gaseous hydrocarbons: <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">ethane</a> (C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>6</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a> (C<sub>3</sub>H<sub>8</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Butane" title="Butane">normal butane</a> (n-C<sub>4</sub>H<sub>10</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Isobutane" title="Isobutane">isobutane</a> (i-C<sub>4</sub>H<sub>10</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Pentane" title="Pentane">pentanes</a> and even higher <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_weight" title="Molecular weight">molecular weight</a> hydrocarbons. When processed and purified into finished by-products, all of these are collectively referred to as Natural Gas Liquids or <b>NGL</b>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Acid_gas" title="Acid gas">Acid gases</a>: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a> (H<sub>2</sub>S) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercaptan" title="Mercaptan">mercaptans</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Methanethiol" title="Methanethiol">methanethiol</a> (CH<sub>3</sub>SH) and <a href="/wiki/Ethanethiol" title="Ethanethiol">ethanethiol</a> (C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>5</sub>SH).</li>
<li>Other gases: <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> (N<sub>2</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> (He).</li>
<li>Water: <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> and liquid water. Also dissolved salts and dissolved gases (acids).</li>
<li>Liquid hydrocarbons: perhaps some <a href="/wiki/Natural-gas_condensate" title="Natural-gas condensate">natural-gas condensate</a> (also referred to as <i>casinghead gasoline</i> or <i>natural gasoline</i>) and/or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">Mercury</a>: very small amounts of mercury primarily in elemental form, but chlorides and other species are possibly present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UOPHg_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-UOPHg-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Naturally_occurring_radioactive_material" title="Naturally occurring radioactive material">Naturally occurring radioactive material</a> (NORM): natural gas may contain <a href="/wiki/Radon" title="Radon">radon</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Produced_water" title="Produced water">produced water</a> may contain dissolved traces of <a href="/wiki/Radium" title="Radium">radium</a>, which can accumulate within piping and processing equipment.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  This can render piping and equipment radioactive over time.</li></ul>
<p>The raw natural gas must be purified to meet the quality standards specified by the major <a href="/wiki/Pipeline_transport" title="Pipeline transport">pipeline</a> transmission and distribution companies. Those quality standards vary from pipeline to pipeline and are usually a function of a pipeline system's design and the markets that it serves. In general, the standards specify that the natural gas:
</p>
<ul><li>Be within a specific range of heating value (caloric value). For example, in the United States, it should be about 1035 ± 5% <a href="/wiki/British_thermal_unit" title="British thermal unit">BTU</a> per cubic foot of gas at 1 atmosphere and 60°F (41 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megajoule" title="Megajoule">MJ</a> ± 5% per cubic metre of gas at 1 atmosphere and 15.6°C).</li>
<li>Be delivered at or above a specified <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon_dew_point" title="Hydrocarbon dew point">hydrocarbon dew point</a> temperature (below which some of the hydrocarbons in the gas might condense at pipeline pressure forming liquid slugs that could damage the pipeline).</li>
<li>Dew-point adjustment serves the reduction of the concentration of water and heavy hydrocarbons in natural gas to such an extent that no condensation occurs during the ensuing transport in the pipelines</li>
<li>Be free of particulate solids and liquid water to prevent erosion, corrosion or other damage to the pipeline.</li>
<li>Be dehydrated of water vapor sufficiently to prevent the formation of methane hydrates within the gas processing plant or subsequently within the sales gas transmission pipeline. A typical water content specification in the U.S. is that gas must contain no more than seven pounds of water per million <a href="/wiki/Standard_cubic_foot" title="Standard cubic foot">standard cubic feet</a> (MMSCF) of gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ProcessGroup_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ProcessGroup-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>Contain no more than trace amounts of components such as hydrogen sulfide, carbon dioxide, mercaptans, and nitrogen. The most common specification for hydrogen sulfide content is 0.25 <a href="/wiki/Grain_(unit)" title="Grain (unit)">grain</a> H<sub>2</sub>S per 100 cubic feet of gas, or approximately 4 ppm. Specifications for CO<sub>2</sub> typically limit the content to no more than two or three percent.</li>
<li>Maintain mercury at less than detectable limits (approximately 0.001 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_billion" title="Parts per billion">ppb</a> by volume) primarily to avoid damaging equipment in the gas processing plant or the pipeline transmission system from mercury amalgamation and embrittlement of aluminum and other metals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UOPHg_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-UOPHg-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description_of_a_natural-gas_processing_plant">Description of a natural-gas processing plant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural-gas_processing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description of a natural-gas processing plant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are a great many ways in which to configure the various <a href="/wiki/Unit_process" title="Unit process">unit processes</a> used in the processing of raw natural gas. The <a href="/wiki/Process_flow_diagram" title="Process flow diagram">block flow diagram</a> below is a generalized, typical configuration for the processing of raw natural gas from non-associated gas wells. It shows how raw natural gas is processed into sales gas pipelined to the end user markets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>  It also shows how processing of the raw natural gas yields these byproducts:
</p>
<ul><li>Natural-gas condensate</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">Sulfur</a></li>
<li>Ethane</li>
<li>Natural-gas liquids (NGL): propane, butanes and C<sub>5</sub>+  (which is the commonly used term for pentanes plus higher molecular weight hydrocarbons)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Raw natural gas is commonly collected from a group of adjacent wells and is first processed at that collection point for removal of free liquid water and natural gas condensate. The condensate is usually then transported to an oil refinery and the water is disposed of as wastewater.
</p><p>The raw gas is then pipelined to a gas processing plant where the initial purification is usually the removal of acid gases (hydrogen sulfide and carbon dioxide). There are many processes that are available for that purpose as shown in the flow diagram, but <a href="/wiki/Amine_gas_treating" title="Amine gas treating">amine treating</a> is the process that was historically used. However, due to a range of performance and environmental constraints of the amine process, a newer technology based on the use of polymeric membranes to separate the carbon dioxide and hydrogen sulfide from the natural gas stream has gained increasing acceptance.  Membranes are attractive since no reagents are consumed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>The acid gases, if present, are removed by membrane or amine treating can then be routed into a sulfur recovery unit which converts the hydrogen sulfide in the acid gas into either elemental sulfur or sulfuric acid. Of the processes available for these conversions, the <a href="/wiki/Claus_process" title="Claus process">Claus process</a> is by far the most well known for recovering elemental sulfur, whereas the conventional <a href="/wiki/Contact_process" title="Contact process">Contact process</a> and the WSA (<a href="/wiki/Wet_sulfuric_acid_process" title="Wet sulfuric acid process">Wet sulfuric acid process</a>) are the most used technologies for recovering <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>. 
</p><p>The residual gas from the Claus process is commonly called <i>tail gas</i> and that gas is then processed in a tail gas treating unit (TGTU) to recover and recycle residual sulfur-containing compounds back into the Claus unit. Again, as shown in the flow diagram, there are a number of processes available for treating the Claus unit tail gas and for that purpose a WSA process is also very suitable since it can work autothermally on tail gases.
</p><p>The next step in the gas processing plant is to remove water vapor from the gas using either the regenerable <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(chemistry)" title="Absorption (chemistry)">absorption</a> in liquid <a href="/wiki/Triethylene_glycol" title="Triethylene glycol">triethylene glycol</a> (TEG),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ProcessGroup_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-ProcessGroup-5">[5]</a></sup> commonly referred to as <a href="/wiki/Glycol_dehydration" title="Glycol dehydration">glycol dehydration</a>, deliquescent chloride desiccants, and or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pressure_Swing_Adsorption" title="Pressure Swing Adsorption">Pressure Swing Adsorption</a> (PSA) unit which is regenerable <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a> using a solid adsorbent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Other newer processes like <a href="/wiki/Membrane_technology" title="Membrane technology">membranes</a> may also be considered.
</p><p>Mercury is then removed by using adsorption processes (as shown in the flow diagram) such as <a href="/wiki/Activated_carbon" title="Activated carbon">activated carbon</a> or regenerable <a href="/wiki/Molecular_sieve" title="Molecular sieve">molecular sieves</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UOPHg_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-UOPHg-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although not common, nitrogen is sometimes removed and rejected using one of the three processes indicated on the flow diagram:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogenic" title="Cryogenic">Cryogenic</a> process (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_Rejection_Unit" title="Nitrogen Rejection Unit">Nitrogen Rejection Unit</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> using low temperature <a href="/wiki/Continuous_distillation" title="Continuous distillation">distillation</a>. This process can be modified to also recover helium, if desired (see also <a href="/wiki/Industrial_gas" title="Industrial gas">industrial gas</a>).</li>
<li>Absorption process,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GPTechnologies_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-GPTechnologies-19">[19]</a></sup> using lean oil or a special solvent<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> as the absorbent.</li>
<li>Adsorption process, using activated carbon or molecular sieves as the adsorbent. This process may have limited applicability because it is said to incur the loss of butanes and heavier hydrocarbons.</li></ul>
<p>The next step is to recover the natural gas liquids (NGL) for which most large, modern gas processing plants use another cryogenic low temperature distillation process involving expansion of the gas through a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turbo-expander" title="Turbo-expander">turbo-expander</a> followed by distillation in a demethanizing <a href="/wiki/Fractionating_column" title="Fractionating column">fractionating column</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Some gas processing plants use lean oil absorption process<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GPTechnologies_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-GPTechnologies-19">[19]</a></sup> rather than the cryogenic turbo-expander process.
</p><p>The recovered NGL stream is sometimes processed through a fractionation train consisting of three distillation towers in series: a deethanizer, a depropanizer and a debutanizer. The overhead product from the deethanizer is ethane and the bottoms are fed to the depropanizer. The overhead product from the depropanizer is propane and the bottoms are fed to the debutanizer. The overhead product from the debutanizer is a mixture of normal and iso-butane, and the bottoms product is a C<sub>5</sub>+ mixture. The recovered streams of propane, butanes and C<sub>5</sub>+ may be "sweetened" in a <a href="/wiki/Merox" title="Merox">Merox</a> process unit to convert undesirable mercaptans into <a href="/wiki/Disulfide" title="Disulfide">disulfides</a> and, along with the recovered ethane, are the final NGL by-products from the gas processing plant. Currently, most cryogenic plants do not include fractionation for economic reasons, and the NGL stream is instead transported as a mixed product to standalone fractionation complexes located near refineries or chemical plants that use the components for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feedstock" title="Feedstock">feedstock</a>. In case laying pipeline is not possible for geographical reason,or the distance between source and consumer exceed 3000 km, natural gas is then transported by ship as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LNG" title="LNG">LNG</a> (liquefied natural gas) and again converted into its gaseous state in the vicinity of the consumer.
</p><p>The residue gas from the NGL recovery section is the final, purified sales gas which is pipelined to the end-user markets. Rules and agreements are made between buyer and seller regarding the quality of the gas. These usually specify the maximum allowable concentration of CO<sub>2</sub>, H<sub>2</sub>S and H<sub>2</sub>O as well as requiring the gas to be commercially free from objectionable odours and materials, and dust or other solid or liquid matter, waxes, gums and gum forming constituents, which might damage or adversely affect operation of the buyers equipment. When an upset occurs on the treatment plant buyers can usually refuse to accept the gas, lower the flow rate or re-negotiate the price. 
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Helium_recovery">Helium recovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural-gas_processing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Helium recovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>If the gas contains significant helium content, the helium may be recovered by fractional distillation. Natural gas may contain as much as 7% helium, and is the commercial source of the noble gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> For instance, the <a href="/wiki/Hugoton_Gas_Field" title="Hugoton Gas Field">Hugoton Gas Field</a> in Kansas and Oklahoma in the United States contains concentrations of helium from 0.3% to 1.9%, which is separated out as a valuable byproduct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consumption">Consumption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural-gas_processing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Consumption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Natural gas consumption patterns, across nations, vary based on access. Countries with large reserves tend to handle the raw-material natural gas more generously, while countries with scarce or lacking resources tend to be more economical. Despite the considerable findings, the predicted availability of the natural-gas reserves has hardly changed.
</p>
