<p><b>Sustainable automotive air conditioning</b> is the subject of a debate – nicknamed the <i>Cool War</i> – about the next-generation <a href="/wiki/Refrigerant" title="Refrigerant">refrigerant</a> in car <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">air conditioning</a>. The <b>Alliance for CO<sub>2</sub> Solutions</b> supports the uptake of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) as a refrigerant in passenger cars, and the chemical industry is developing new chemical blends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Alliance and its supporters – scientists, <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organization" title="Non-governmental organization">NGOs</a> and business leaders – urge the car industry to replace high global warming chemical substances with the <a href="/wiki/Natural_refrigerant" title="Natural refrigerant">natural refrigerant</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R744" title="R744">R744</a>/ R-744) in car cooling and heating. This, they argue, would lead to 10% less car emissions, and knock out 1% of total <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> If CO<sub>2</sub> Technology is applied in other sectors, such as commercial and industrial refrigeration, heat pumps for water heating etc., it may even save up to 3% of the world’s greenhouse gases.
</p><p>Opponents of the Alliance claim that CO<sub>2</sub> Technology is not cost-efficient and safe, hence seeking to postpone the global industry decision to be taken to develop new chemical blends instead.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_automotive_air_conditioning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Cool War has emanated from the decision of the European Union to phase out the current high global warming refrigerant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HFC-134a" title="HFC-134a">HFC-134a</a> in car air conditioning from January 2011 onwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> To comply with the legislation, carmakers have to decide today on a new refrigerant, as they typically need 3–4 years to develop and introduce a new car platform including the air conditioning system. (It would take much less time to design merely a new air conditioning system to install in a new version of an existing model.) The current total value of the car air conditioning market is estimated to be $14.5 billion in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arguments">Arguments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_automotive_air_conditioning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Arguments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arguments_for_CO2">Arguments for CO<sub>2</sub></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_automotive_air_conditioning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Arguments for CO2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Alliance for CO<sub>2</sub> Solutions and its supporters agree that the refrigerant CO<sub>2</sub> is:
</p>
<ul><li>More environmentally friendly with the lowest <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_potential" title="Global warming potential">Global warming potential</a> (GWP) of all currently used and proposed refrigerants. CO<sub>2</sub> does not deplete the ozone layer. Since the carbon dioxide used in car air conditioning is a recycled industrial waste product it becomes environmentally neutral.  Overall, using a CO<sub>2</sub>-based air conditioning system will reduce total car emissions by 10%, thereby sparing the planet 1% of total greenhouse gases.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>More technically ready because CO<sub>2</sub> models have been developed and tested in all climates, being now ready for mass production. They are faster to heat and cool a car, and show a superior performance in over 90% of all driving conditions.</li>
<li>More cost-efficient because as a refrigerant itself, CO<sub>2</sub> is cheap and worldwide available. The servicing of CO<sub>2</sub> systems will be less costly and less complicated than that for present systems. For the consumer the total cost of ownership is lowest with CO<sub>2</sub> as it will significantly cut fuel consumption by the air conditioning device. Carmakers have to make an initial investment estimated at €20 per unit, with no additional costs once CO<sub>2</sub> Technology enters into mass production.</li>
<li>Usable in Heat Pumps because at least one CO<sub>2</sub> system under development can act as a <a href="/wiki/Heat_pump" title="Heat pump">heat pump</a>, supplying cabin heat and windshield defrosting even before the engine has warmed up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>Although the Alliance may not mention it, since CO<sub>2</sub> is so cheap and relatively harmless to the environment, the reservoirs in such systems could store additional liquid R744 to keep a vehicle cool even when the engine (or compressor) was not running.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arguments_against_CO2">Arguments against CO<sub>2</sub></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_automotive_air_conditioning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Arguments against CO2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CO<sub>2</sub> Technology requires the design of completely new high-pressure systems whereas so-called "drop-in solutions" (the adaptation of current systems to new substances) are potentially more cost-efficient. 
</p><p>The Alliance for CO<sub>2</sub> Solutions claims, however that the initial costs of CO<sub>2</sub> systems will be around €5 higher than drop-in solutions and that over a car’s life cycle, CO<sub>2</sub> air conditioning systems will be more cost-efficient than any currently used or proposed new chemical blends. (see <a href="#Arguments_for_CO2">Arguments for CO<sub>2</sub></a>).
CO<sub>2</sub> has been classified as Safety Class A1 (low-toxic, non-flammable refrigerant) by the American Society of Heating, Refrigerating and Air-Conditioning Engineers (ASHRAE)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> – the highest safety class possible.  As the charge of CO<sub>2</sub> to the air conditioning systems is very small (200-400 g) there is no realistic danger for the passengers, even in case of accidental release.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arguments_for_non-CO2_refrigerants">Arguments for non-CO<sub>2</sub> refrigerants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_automotive_air_conditioning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Arguments for non-CO2 refrigerants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Refrigerants such as the greenpeace-developed '<a href="/wiki/Isobutane#Uses" title="Isobutane">Greenfreeze</a>', based on purified <a href="/wiki/Butane" title="Butane">butane</a>/<a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a> mixtures, are entirely 'natural', and due to increased efficiency over refrigerants such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R134a" title="R134a">R134a</a>, allow the use of very small amounts of refrigerant to be used.</li>
<li>Use of pure hydrocarbon refrigerants, which are 'backward compatible' with even early <a href="/wiki/Freon" title="Freon">Freon</a> (R-12) car air conditioning systems, would allow these systems to be easily converted (without modification), increasing their efficiency, and preventing further release of harmful R-134a and R-12 to the atmosphere.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arguments_against_non-CO2_refrigerants">Arguments against non-CO<sub>2</sub> refrigerants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_automotive_air_conditioning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Arguments against non-CO2 refrigerants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Butane and propane are very flammable petroleum products; they are used as fuels for gas <a href="/wiki/Barbecue" title="Barbecue">barbecue</a> grills, disposable lighters, etc. Like gasoline, to which it chemically is closely related, propane has a tendency to explode if mixed with oxygen and ignited in an enclosed container.
</p><p>The use of highly flammable hydrocarbon gases such as butane and propane as automotive refrigerants raises serious safety concerns.  The EPA, in evaluating motor vehicle air conditioning substitutes for CFC-12 (Freon, or R-12) under its SNAP program, has classified as "Unacceptable Substitutes" other "Flammable blend[s] of hydrocarbons" by reason of "insufficient data to demonstrate safety."  The EPA defines "Unacceptable" in this context as "illegal for use as a CFC-12 substitute in motor vehicle air conditioners".  All of the refrigerants which EPA approved for motor vehicle use in place of CFC-12 (as of 28 September 2006) contain no more than 4% total flammable hydrocarbons (butane, isobutane, and/or isopentane).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  Therefore, it appears unlikely, for safety reasons, that EPA will approve 'Greenfreeze' or similar hydrocarbon-based refrigerants for automotive use.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Latest_and_next_steps">Latest and next steps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_automotive_air_conditioning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Latest and next steps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In September 2007, the German Association of the Automotive Industry (VDA) officially announced its decision to use CO<sub>2</sub> as the refrigerant in next-generation air conditioning. Other carmakers from Europe and the rest of the world may follow the German lead.
</p><p>A working group at ACEA, the European carmakers’ association, was to be drafting a common position on the issue to be adopted across the whole industry by end-2007.
</p><p>However, on 9 April 2009, German public television channel ARD aired a report claiming that VDA members would be using loopholes in the law to avoid complying with the EU directive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
