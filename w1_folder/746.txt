<p><b>Environmental assessment</b> (<b>EA</b>) is the assessment of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_impact" title="Environmental impact">environmental consequences</a> (positive and negative) of a plan, policy, program, or actual projects prior to the decision to move forward with the proposed action. In this context, the term "<b>environmental impact assessment</b>" (<b>EIA</b>) is usually used when applied to actual projects by individuals or companies and the term "<a href="/wiki/Strategic_environmental_assessment" title="Strategic environmental assessment">strategic environmental assessment</a>" (SEA) applies to policies, plans and programmes most often proposed by organs of state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Environmental assessments may be governed by rules of <a href="/wiki/Administrative_law" title="Administrative law">administrative procedure</a> regarding public participation and documentation of decision making, and may be subject to judicial review. 
</p><p>The purpose of the assessment is to ensure that decision makers consider the environmental impacts when deciding whether or not to proceed with a project. The International Association for Impact Assessment (IAIA) defines an environmental impact assessment as "the process of identifying, predicting, evaluating and mitigating the <a href="/wiki/Biophysics" title="Biophysics">biophysical</a>, social, and other relevant effects of development proposals prior to major decisions being taken and commitments made".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> EIAs are unique in that they do not require adherence to a predetermined environmental outcome, but rather they require decision makers to <a href="/wiki/Environmental_full-cost_accounting" title="Environmental full-cost accounting">account for environmental values</a> in their decisions and to justify those decisions in light of detailed environmental studies and public comments on the potential environmental impacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Environmental impact assessments commenced in the 1960s, as part of increasing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_awareness" title="Environmental awareness">environmental awareness</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[notes 1]</a></sup> EIAs involved a technical evaluation intended to contribute to more objective decision making. In the United States, environmental impact assessments obtained formal status in 1969, with enactment of the <a href="/wiki/National_Environmental_Policy_Act" title="National Environmental Policy Act">National Environmental Policy Act</a>. EIAs have been used increasingly around the world. The number of "Environmental Assessments" filed every year "has vastly overtaken the number of more rigorous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_Impact_Statement" title="Environmental Impact Statement">Environmental Impact Statements</a> (EIS)."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEClarkCanter1997199_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEClarkCanter1997199-6">[5]</a></sup> An Environmental Assessment is a "mini-EIS designed to provide sufficient information to allow the agency to decide whether the preparation of a full-blown Environmental Impact Statement (EIS) is necessary."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERychlakCase2010111-120_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERychlakCase2010111-120-7">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKershner2011_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKershner2011-8">[7]</a></sup>
EIA is an activity that is done to find out the impact that would be done before development will occur.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods">Methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>General and industry specific assessment methods are available including:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Industrial products</i> – Product environmental <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_cycle_analysis" title="Life cycle analysis">life cycle analysis</a> (LCA) is used for identifying and measuring the impact of industrial products on the environment. These EIAs consider activities related to extraction of raw materials, ancillary materials, equipment; production, use, disposal and ancillary equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Genetically modified plants</i> – Specific methods available to perform EIAs of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_organisms" title="Genetically modified organisms">genetically modified organisms</a> include GMP-RAM and INOVA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Fuzzy_logic" title="Fuzzy logic">Fuzzy logic</a></i> – EIA methods need measurement data to estimate values of impact indicators. However, many of the environment impacts cannot be quantified, e.g. landscape quality, lifestyle quality and social acceptance. Instead information from similar EIAs, expert judgment and community sentiment are employed. Approximate reasoning methods known as fuzzy logic can be used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> A fuzzy arithmetic approach has also been proposed<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> and implemented using a software tool (TDEIA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Follow-up">Follow-up</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Follow-up">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the end of the project, an audit evaluates the accuracy of the EIA by comparing actual to predicted impacts. The objective is to make future EIAs more valid and effective. Two primary considerations are:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Scientific</i> – to examine the accuracy of predictions and explain errors</li>
<li><i>Management</i> – to assess the success of mitigation in reducing impacts</li></ul>
<p>Audits can be performed either as a rigorous assessment of the <a href="/wiki/Null_hypothesis" title="Null hypothesis">null hypothesis</a> or with a simpler approach comparing what actually occurred against the predictions in the EIA document.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>After an EIA, the <a href="/wiki/Precautionary_principle" title="Precautionary principle">precautionary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polluter_pays_principle" title="Polluter pays principle">polluter pays principles</a> may be applied to decide whether to reject, modify or require <a href="/wiki/Strict_liability" title="Strict liability">strict liability</a> or <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a> coverage to a project, based on predicted harms.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Hydropower_Sustainability_Assessment_Protocol" title="Hydropower Sustainability Assessment Protocol">Hydropower Sustainability Assessment Protocol</a> is a sector specific method for checking the quality of Environmental and Social assessments and management plans.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Around_the_world">Around the world</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Around the world">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The history of EIA in Australia could be linked to the enactment of the <a href="/wiki/National_Environmental_Policy_Act" title="National Environmental Policy Act">U.S. National Environment Policy Act</a> (NEPA) in 1970, which made the preparation of environmental impact statements a requirement. In Australia, one might say that the EIA procedures were introduced at a State Level prior to that of the Commonwealth (Federal), with a majority of the states having divergent views to the Commonwealth. One of the pioneering states was New South Wales, whose State Pollution Control Commission issued EIA guidelines in 1974. At a Commonwealth (i.e. Federal) level, this was followed by passing of the <i>Environment Protection (Impact of Proposals) Act 1974</i> (Cth) in 1974. The <i>Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999</i> (Cth) (EPBC Act) superseded the <i>Environment Protection (Impact of Proposals) Act 1974</i> (Cth) and is the current central piece for EIA in Australia on a Commonwealth (i.e. Federal) level. An important point to note is that this federal legislation does not override the validity of the States or Territories environmental and development assessments and approvals; rather the EPBC Act runs as a parallel to the State/Territory Systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elliott_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Elliott-15">[14]</a></sup> Overlap between federal and state requirements is addressed via bilateral agreements or one-off accreditation of state processes, as provided for in the EPBC Act.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Commonwealth_Level">The Commonwealth Level</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Commonwealth Level">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The EPBC Act provides a legal framework to protect and manage nationally and internationally important flora, fauna, ecological communities and heritage places-defined in the EPBC Act as matters of ‘National Environmental Significance’. Following are the nine matters of ‘National Environmental Significance’ to which the EPBC Act applies:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-department_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-department-16">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>World Heritage properties;</li>
<li>National Heritage places;</li>
<li>Wetlands of international importance (listed under the Ramsar Convention);</li>
<li>Listed threatened species and ecological communities;</li>
<li>Migratory species protected under international agreements;</li>
<li>Commonwealth marine areas;</li>
<li>the Great Barrier Reef Marine Park;</li>
<li>Nuclear actions (including uranium mining); and</li>
<li>Water resources, in relation with coal seam gas development and large coal mining development.</li></ul>
<p>In addition to this, the EPBC Act aims at providing a streamlined national assessment and approval process for activities. These activities could be by the Commonwealth, or its agents, anywhere in the world or activities on Commonwealth land; and activities that are listed as having a ‘significant impact’ on matters of ‘national environment significance'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-department_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-department-16">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>The EPBC Act comes into play when a person (a ‘proponent') wants an action (often called a ‘proposal’ or ‘project’) assessed for environmental impacts under the EPBC Act, he or she must refer the project to the Department of the Environment and Energy (Commonwealth). This ‘referral’ is then released to the public, as well as relevant state, territory and Commonwealth ministers, for comment on whether the project is likely to have a significant impact on matters of national environmental significance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-department_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-department-16">[15]</a></sup> The Department of the Environment and Energy assess the process and makes recommendation to the minister or the delegate for the feasibility. The final discretion on the decision remains of the minister, which is not solely based on matters of ‘national environmental significance’ but also the consideration of social and economic impact of the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-department_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-department-16">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Australian Government Minister for the Environment and Energy cannot intervene in a proposal if it has no significant impact on one of the eight matters of ‘national environmental significance’ despite the fact that there may be other undesirable environmental impacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-department_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-department-16">[15]</a></sup> This is primarily due to the division of powers between the States and the Federal government and due to which the Australian Government environment minister cannot overturn a state decision.
</p><p>There are strict civil and criminal penalties for the breach of EPBC Act. Depending on the kind of breach, civil penalty (maximum) may go up to $550,000 for an individual and $5.5 million for a body corporate, or for criminal penalty (maximum) of seven years imprisonment and/or penalty of $46,200.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-department_16-5"><a href="#cite_note-department-16">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_State_and_Territory_Level">The State and Territory Level</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The State and Territory Level">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<h5><span id="Australian_Capital_Territory_.28ACT.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Australian_Capital_Territory_(ACT)">Australian Capital Territory (ACT)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Australian Capital Territory (ACT)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>EIA provisions within Ministerial Authorities in the ACT are found in the Chapters 7 and 8 of the <i>Planning and Development Act 2007</i> (ACT). EIA in ACT was previously administered with the help of Part 4 of the Land (Planning and Environment) Act 1991 (Land Act) and Territory Plan (plan for land-use).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elliott_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Elliott-15">[14]</a></sup>
Note that some EIA may occur in the ACT on Commonwealth land under the EPBC Act (Cth). Further provisions of the <i>Australian Capital Territory (Planning and Land Management) Act 1988</i> (Cth) may also be applicable particularly to national land and "designated areas".
</p>
<h5><span id="New_South_Wales_.28NSW.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="New_South_Wales_(NSW)">New South Wales (NSW)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: New South Wales (NSW)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>In New South Wales, the <i>Environment Planning and Assessment Act 1979</i> (EP&amp;A Act) establishes two pathways for EIA. The first is under Division 5.2 of the EP&amp;A Act, which provides for EIA of 'State Significant Infrastructure' projects (from June 2011, this Part replaced the previous Part 3A, which previously covered EIA of major projects). The second is under Part 4 of the EP&amp;A Act dealing with development assessments for local, regional, and State Significant Developments (other than State Significant Infrastructure).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elliott_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Elliott-15">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span id="Northern_Territory_.28NT.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_Territory_(NT)">Northern Territory (NT)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Northern Territory (NT)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>The EIA process in Northern Territory is chiefly administered under the Environmental Assessment Act (EAA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> Although EAA is the primary tool for EIA in Northern Territory, there are further provisions for proposals in the Inquiries Act 1985 (NT).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elliott_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-Elliott-15">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span id="Queensland_.28QLD.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Queensland_(QLD)"><b>Queensland (QLD)</b></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Queensland (QLD)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>There are four main EIA processes in Queensland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Defenders_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Defenders-18">[17]</a></sup> Firstly, under the Integrated Planning Act 1997 (IPA) for development projects other than mining. Secondly, under the Chapter 3 of the <i>Environmental Protection Act 1994</i> (Qld) (EP Act) for some mining and petroleum activities. Thirdly, under the <i>State Development and Public Works Organisation Act 1971</i> (Qld) (State Development Act) for ‘significant projects’. Finally, under the <i>Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999</i> (Cth) for ‘controlled actions’.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Defenders_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Defenders-18">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p><b>South Australia (SA)</b>
</p><p>The local governing tool for EIA in South Australia is the <i>Development Act 1993</i> (SA). There are three levels of assessment possible under the Act in the form of an environment impact statement (EIS), a public environmental report (PER) or a Development Report (DR).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elliott_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-Elliott-15">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span id="Tasmania_.28TAS.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Tasmania_(TAS)">Tasmania (TAS)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Tasmania (TAS)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>In Tasmania, an integrated system of legislation is used to govern development and approval process, this system is a mixture of the E<i>nvironmental Management and Pollution Control Act 1994</i> (Tas) (EMPC Act), <i>Land Use Planning and Approvals Act 1993</i> (Tas) (LUPA Act), <i>State Policies and Projects Act 1993</i> (Tas) (SPPA), and <i>Resource Management and Planning Appeals Tribunal Act 1993</i> (Tas).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elliott_15-5"><a href="#cite_note-Elliott-15">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span id="Victoria_.28VIC.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Victoria_(VIC)">Victoria (VIC)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Victoria (VIC)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>The EIA process in Victoria is intertwined with the <i><a href="/wiki/Environment_Effects_Act_1978" title="Environment Effects Act 1978">Environment Effects Act 1978</a></i> (Vic) and the Ministerial Guidelines for Assessment of Environmental Effects (made under the s 10 of the EE Act).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span id="Western_Australia_.28WA.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_Australia_(WA)">Western Australia (WA)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Western Australia (WA)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Part 4 of the <i>Environmental Protection Act 1986</i> (WA) provides the legislative framework for the EIA process in Western Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> The EPA Act oversees the planning and development proposals and assesses their likely impacts on the environment.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><p>
In <i>Friends of the Oldman River Society v. Canada (Minister of Transportation)</i>,(<a href="#CITEREFSCC1992">SCC 1992</a>) <a href="/wiki/G%C3%A9rard_La_Forest" title="Gérard La Forest">La Forest J</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Canada" title="Supreme Court of Canada">Supreme Court of Canada</a> described environmental impact assessment in terms of the proper scope of federal jurisdiction with respect to environments matters,</p><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>"Environmental impact assessment is, in its simplest form, a planning tool that is now generally regarded as an integral component of sound decision-making."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESCC1992_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESCC1992-21">[20]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/G%C3%A9rard_La_Forest" title="Gérard La Forest">Supreme Court Justice La Forest</a> cited (<a href="#CITEREFCottonEmond1981245">Cotton, Emond &amp; 1981 245</a>), "The basic concepts behind environmental assessment are simply stated: (1) early identification and evaluation of all potential environmental consequences of a proposed undertaking; (2) decision making that both guarantees the adequacy of this process and reconciles, to the greatest extent possible, the proponent’s development desires with environmental protection and preservation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cotton1981_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cotton1981-22">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>La Forest referred to (<a href="#CITEREFJeffrey1989">Jeffrey 1989</a>, 1.2,1.4) and (<a href="#CITEREFEmond1978">Emond 1978</a>, p. 5) who described "...environmental assessments as a planning tool with both an information-gathering and a decision-making component" that provide "...an objective basis for granting or denying approval for a proposed development."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jeffrey1989_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jeffrey1989-23">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emond1978_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emond1978-24">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Justice La Forest addressed his concerns about the implications of Bill C-45 regarding public navigation rights on lakes and rivers that would contradict previous cases.(<a href="#CITEREFLa_Forest1973178-80">La Forest, 1973 &amp; 178-80</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LaForest1973_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-LaForest1973-25">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Environmental_Assessment_Act" title="Canadian Environmental Assessment Act">Canadian Environmental Assessment Act 2012</a> (CEAA 2012)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CanLII18july2012_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-CanLII18july2012-26">[25]</a></sup> "and its regulations establish the legislative basis for the federal practice of environmental assessment in most regions of Canada."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-canliiCEAAlatest_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-canliiCEAAlatest-27">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-canliiCEAA2012_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-canliiCEAA2012-28">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEAA2012_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-CEAA2012-29">[28]</a></sup> CEAA 2012 came into force July 6, 2012 and replaces the former <i>Canadian Environmental Assessment Act</i> (1995). EA is defined as a planning tool to identify, understand, assess and mitigate, where possible, the environmental effects of a project.
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition">Opposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Opposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Environmental Lawyer Dianne Saxe argued that the CEAA 2012 "allows the federal government to create mandatory timelines for assessments of even the largest and most important projects, regardless of public opposition." (<a href="#CITEREFSaxe2012">Saxe 2012</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saxe9aug2012_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Saxe9aug2012-31">[30]</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Environmental Impact Assessment Law (EIA Law) requires that an environmental impact assessment be completed prior to project construction. However, if a developer completely ignores this requirement and builds a project without submitting an environmental impact statement, the only penalty is that the environmental protection bureau (EPB) may require the developer to do a make-up environmental assessment. If the developer does not complete this make-up assessment within the designated time, only then is the EPB authorized to fine the developer. Even so, the possible fine is capped at a maximum of about US$25,000, a fraction of the overall cost of most major projects. The lack of more stringent enforcement mechanisms has resulted in a significant percentage of projects not completing legally required environmental impact assessments prior to construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>China's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_Environmental_Protection_Administration" title="State Environmental Protection Administration">State Environmental Protection Administration</a> (SEPA) used the legislation to halt 30 projects in 2004, including three hydro-power plants under the <a href="/wiki/Three_Gorges" title="Three Gorges">Three Gorges</a> Project Company. Although one month later (Note as a point of reference, that the typical EIA for a major project in the USA takes one to two years.), most of the 30 halted projects resumed their construction, reportedly having passed the environmental assessment, the fact that these key projects' construction was ever suspended was notable.
</p><p>A joint investigation by SEPA and the Ministry of Land and Resources in 2004 showed that 30–40% of the mining construction projects went through the procedure of environment impact assessment as required, while in some areas only 6–7% did so. This partly explains why China has witnessed so many mining accidents in recent years.
</p><p>SEPA alone cannot guarantee the full enforcement of environmental laws and regulations, observed Professor <a href="/wiki/Wang_Canfa" title="Wang Canfa">Wang Canfa</a>, director of the centre to help environmental victims at <a href="/wiki/China_University_of_Political_Science_and_Law" title="China University of Political Science and Law">China University of Political Science and Law</a>. In fact, according to Wang, the rate of China's environmental laws and regulations that are actually enforced is estimated at barely 10%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Egypt">Egypt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Egypt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA)
EIA is implemented in Egypt under the umbrella of the Ministry of state for environmental affairs. The Egyptian Environmental Affairs Agency (EEAA) is responsible for the EIA services.
</p><p>In June 1997, the responsibility of Egypt's first full-time Minister of State for Environmental Affairs was assigned as stated in the Presidential Decree no.275/1997. From thereon, the new ministry has focused, in close collaboration with the national and international development partners, on defining environmental policies, setting priorities and implementing initiatives within a context of sustainable development.
</p><p>According to the Law 4/1994 for the Protection of the Environment, the Egyptian Environmental Affairs Agency (EEAA) was restructured with the new mandate to substitute the institution initially established in 1982. At the central level, EEAA represents the executive arm of the Ministry.
</p><p>The purpose of EIA is to ensure the protection and conservation of the environment and natural resources including human health aspects against uncontrolled development. The long-term objective is to ensure a sustainable economic development that meets present needs without compromising future generations ability to meet their own needs. EIA is an important tool in the integrated environmental management approach.
</p><p>EIA must be performed for new establishments or projects and for expansions or renovations of existing establishments according to the Law for the Environment.
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="EU">EU</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: EU">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A wide range of instruments exist in the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_policy_of_the_European_Union" title="Environmental policy of the European Union">Environmental policy of the European Union</a>. Among them the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> has established a mix of mandatory and discretionary procedures to assess environmental impacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-watson_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-watson-43">[42]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Union_Directive" title="European Union Directive">European Union Directive</a> (85/337/EEC) on Environmental Impact Assessments (known as the <i>EIA Directive</i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup> was first introduced in 1985 and was amended in 1997. The directive was amended again in 2003, following EU signature of the 1998 <a href="/wiki/Aarhus_Convention" title="Aarhus Convention">Aarhus Convention</a>, and once more in 2009. The initial Directive of 1985 and its three amendments have been codified in Directive 2011/92/EU of 13 December 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup>
In 2001, the issue was enlarged to the assessment of plans and programmes by the so-called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strategic_Environmental_Assessment" title="Strategic Environmental Assessment">Strategic Environmental Assessment (SEA)</a> Directive</i> (2001/42/EC), which is now in force.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-watson_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-watson-43">[42]</a></sup>
Under the EU directive, an EIA must provide certain information to comply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup> There are seven key areas that are required:
</p>
<ol><li>Description of the project
<ul><li>Description of actual project and site description</li>
<li>Break the project down into its key components, i.e. construction, operations, decommissioning</li>
<li>For each component list all of the sources of environmental disturbance</li>
<li>For each component all the inputs and outputs must be listed, e.g., <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a>, noise, <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrology</a></li></ul></li>
<li>Alternatives that have been considered
<ul><li>Examine alternatives that have been considered</li>
<li>Example: in a <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> power station, will the fuel be sourced locally or nationally?</li></ul></li>
<li>Description of the environment
<ul><li>List of all aspects of the environment that may be affected by the development</li>
<li>Example: populations, <a href="/wiki/Fauna" title="Fauna">fauna</a>, flora, air, soil, water, humans, landscape, cultural heritage</li>
<li>This section is best carried out with the help of local experts, e.g. the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RSPB" title="RSPB">RSPB</a> in the UK</li></ul></li>
<li>Description of the significant effects on the environment
<ul><li>The word significant is crucial here as the definition can vary</li>
<li>'Significant' must be defined</li>
<li>The most frequent method used here is use of the <a href="/wiki/Leopold_matrix" title="Leopold matrix">Leopold matrix</a></li>
<li>The matrix is a tool used in the systematic examination of potential interactions</li>
<li>Example: in a windfarm development a significant impact may be collisions with birds</li></ul></li>
<li>Mitigation
<ul><li>This is where EIA is most useful</li>
<li>Once section 4 is complete, it is obvious where impacts are greatest</li>
<li>Using this information ways to avoid negative impacts should be developed</li>
<li>Best working with the developer with this section as they know the project best</li>
<li>Using the windfarm example again, construction could be out of bird nesting seasons.  Or removal of hardstanding on a potentially contaminated land site, out of the rainy season.</li></ul></li>
<li>Non-technical summary (EIS)
<ul><li>The EIA is in the public domain and be used in the decision making process</li>
<li>It is important that the information is available to the public</li>
<li>This section is a summary that does not include jargon or complicated diagrams</li>
<li>It should be understood by the informed lay-person</li></ul></li>
<li>Lack of know-how/technical difficulties
<ul><li>This section is to advise any areas of weakness in knowledge</li>
<li>It can be used to focus areas of future research</li>
<li>Some developers see the EIA as a starting block for poor environmental management</li></ul></li></ol>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Annexed_projects">Annexed projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Annexed projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>All projects are either classified as Annex 1 or Annex 2 projects. Those lying in Annex 1 are large scale developments such as motorways, chemical works, bridges, powerstations etc. These always require an EIA under the Environmental Impact Assessment Directive (85,337,EEC as amended). Annex 2 projects are smaller in scale than those referred to in Annex 1. Member States must determine whether these project shall be made subject to an assessment subject to a set of criteria set out in Annex 3 of codified Directive 2011/92/EU.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Netherlands">The Netherlands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: The Netherlands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>EIA was implemented in Dutch legislation on September 1, 1987. The categories of projects that require an EIA are summarised in Dutch legislation, the Wet milieubeheer. The use of thresholds for activities makes sure that EIA is obligatory for those activities that may have considerable impacts on the environment.
</p><p>For projects and plans that fit these criteria, an EIA report is required. The EIA report defines a.o. the proposed initiative, it makes clear the impact of that initiative on the environment and compares this with the impact of possible alternatives with less a negative impact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hong_Kong">Hong Kong</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Hong Kong">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>EIA in Hong Kong, since 1998, is regulated by the <i>Environmental Impact Assessment Ordinance 1997</i>.
</p><p>The original proposal to construct the <a href="/wiki/Lok_Ma_Chau_Spur_Line" title="Lok Ma Chau Spur Line">Lok Ma Chau Spur Line</a> overground across the <a href="/wiki/Long_Valley,_Hong_Kong" title="Long Valley, Hong Kong">Long Valley</a> failed to get through EIA, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kowloon%E2%80%93Canton_Railway_Corporation" title="Kowloon–Canton Railway Corporation">Kowloon–Canton Railway Corporation</a> had to change its plan and build the railway underground. In April 2011, the EIA of the Hong Kong section of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hong_Kong-Zhuhai-Macau_Bridge" title="Hong Kong-Zhuhai-Macau Bridge">Hong Kong-Zhuhai-Macau Bridge</a> was found to have breached the ordinance, and was declared unlawful. The appeal by the government was allowed in September 2011. However, it was estimated that this EIA court case had increased the construction cost of the Hong Kong section of the bridge by HK$6.5 billion in money-of-the-day prices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iraq">Iraq</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Iraq">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ministry of Environment of the federal government of Iraq is in charge of issuing Environmental compliance certificate based on an EIA report prepared by professional consultant and thoroughly reviewed by MOE. Any project or activity prior to its establishment or even already existing project has to be approved and obtain such certificate from the MOE. Projects are classified into 3 categories; “A”, “B” and “C”. EIA report is usually obligatory for those projects and activities falling under categories “A” (large-scale) and “B” (small-scale) that may have considerable impacts on environment<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup>. An example of “A” category activities such as: dams and reservoirs, forestry production projects, industrial plants, irrigation, drainage and flood control, land clearance and leveling, port and harbor development, river basin development, thermal power and hydro-power development, manufacture, transportation and use of pesticides or other hazardous materials, hazardous waste management and disposal….etc. An example of “B” category activities such as: agro-industries, electrical transmission, renewable energy, rural electrification, tourism, rehabilitation or maintenance of highway or rural roads, rehabilitation or modification of existing industrial facilities…etc. preparation of EIA report is usually exempt for projects falling under category “C” that may have low to no impact on environment, and example of “C” category activities: small fish breeding pond, institutional development, most human resources projects…etc.
</p><p>The main environmental legislations in Iraq are: Law No.64 for cities and land use (1965), Law No.21 for noise prevention (1966), Law No.25 for system of rivers and other water resources protection (1967), Law No.99 for ionized radiation (1980), Law No.89 for public health (drinking water provision, sanitation and environmental monitoring (1981), Law No.79 for protection and improvement of environment (1986), Environmental criteria for agricultural, industrial and public service projects (1990), Law No.3 for protection and improvement of environment (1997), Law No.2 for water systems protection (2001), Law No.44 for creation of Ministry of Environment instead of the council of protection and improvement of environment (2003), Law No.27 for environmental protection and improvement (2009)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[49]</a></sup> , Law No.4 for protection of ambient air system (2012). 
</p><p>Meanwhile, Environmental Protection and Improvement Board in the regional government of Kurdistan in the northern Iraq (Erbil, Duhok, Sulaimany and Garmyan) is responsible of issuing Environmental compliance certificate, the board was established according to law No.3 Environmental protection and improvement board in Iraqi Kurdistan Region (2010)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup>. The board is responsible of issuing such certificate for all projects and activities except of petroleum operation which EIA process is organized and implemented by the Ministry of Natural Resources of Kurdistan Regional government<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[51]</a></sup>. The same Environmental Legislations mentioned are adopted but the procedure of EIA in Iraqi-Kurdistan region government may differ from the one in the Federal government of Iraq.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Environment_and_Forests_(India)" title="Ministry of Environment and Forests (India)">Ministry of Environment, Forests and Climate Change</a> (MoEFCC) of India has been in a great effort in Environmental Impact Assessment in India. The main laws in action are the Water Act(1974), the Indian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wildlife_Protection_Act_of_1972" title="Wildlife Protection Act of 1972">Wildlife (Protection) Act (1972)</a>, the Air (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act (1981) and the Environment (Protection) Act (1986),Biological Diversity Act(2002).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[52]</a></sup> The responsible body for this is the Central Pollution Control Board.
Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) studies need a significant amount of primary and secondary environmental data. Primary data are those collected in the field to define the status of the environment (like air quality data, water quality data etc.). Secondary data are those collected over the years that can be used to understand the existing environmental scenario of the study area. The environmental impact assessment (EIA) studies are conducted over a short period of time and therefore the understanding of the environmental trends, based on a few months of primary data, has limitations. Ideally, the primary data must be considered along with the secondary data for complete understanding of the existing environmental status of the area. In many EIA studies, the secondary data needs could be as high as 80% of the total data requirement. EIC is the repository of one stop secondary data source for environmental impact assessment in India.
</p><p>The Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) experience in India indicates that the lack of timely availability of reliable and authentic environmental data has been a major bottle neck in achieving the full benefits of EIA. The environment being a multi-disciplinary subject, a multitude of agencies are involved in collection of environmental data. However, no single organization in India tracks available data from these agencies and makes it available in one place in a form required by environmental impact assessment practitioners. Further, environmental data is not available in enhanced forms that improve the quality of the EIA. This makes it harder and more time-consuming to generate environmental impact assessments and receive timely environmental clearances from regulators. With this background, the Environmental Information Centre (EIC) has been set up to serve as a professionally managed clearing house of environmental information that can be used by MoEF, project proponents, consultants, NGOs and other stakeholders involved in the process of environmental impact assessment in India. EIC caters to the need of creating and disseminating of organized environmental data for various developmental initiatives all over the country.
</p><p>EIC stores data in GIS format and makes it available to all environmental impact assessment studies and to EIA stakeholders in a cost effective and timely manner. So that we can manage that in different proportions such as remedy measures etc.,
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Malaysia">Malaysia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Malaysia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nepal">Nepal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Nepal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Nepal, EIA has been integrated in major development projects since the early 1980s. In the planning history of Nepal, the sixth plan (1980–85), for the first time, recognized the need for EIA with the establishment of Environmental Impact Study Project (EISP) under the Department of Soil Conservation in 1982 to develop necessary instruments for integration of EIA in infrastructure
development projects. However, the government of Nepal enunciated environment conservation related
policies in the seventh plan (NPC, 1985–1990). To enforce this policy and make necessary arrangements, a series of guidelines were developed, thereby incorporating the elements of environmental factors right from the project formulation stage of the development plans
and projects and to avoid or minimize adverse effects on the ecological system. In addition, it has also emphasized that EIAs of industry, tourism, water resources, transportation, urbanization, agriculture, forest and other developmental projects be conducted.
</p><p>In Nepal, the government’s Environmental Impact Assessment Guideline of 1993 inspired the enactment of the Environment Protection Act (EPA) of 1997 and the Environment Protection Rules (EPR) of 1997 (EPA and EPR have been enforced since 24 and 26 June 1997 respectively in Nepal) to internalizing the environmental assessment system. The process institutionalized the EIA process in development proposals and enactment, which makes the integration of IEE and EIA legally binding to the prescribed projects. The projects, requiring EIA or IEE, are included in Schedules 1 and 2 of the EPR, 1997
(GoN/MoLJPA 1997).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Zealand">New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In New Zealand, EIA is usually referred to as <i>Assessment of Environmental Effects</i> (AEE).  The first use of EIA's dates back to a Cabinet minute passed in 1974 called Environmental Protection and Enhancement Procedures. This had no legal force and only related to the activities of government departments. When the <a href="/wiki/Resource_Management_Act_1991" title="Resource Management Act 1991">Resource Management Act</a> was passed in 1991, an EIA was required as part of a resource consent application. Section 88 of the Act specifies that the AEE must include "such detail as corresponds with the scale and significance of the effects that the activity may have on the environment".  While there is no duty to consult any person when making a resource consent application (Sections 36A and Schedule 4), proof of consultation is almost certain required by local councils when they decide whether or not to publicly notify the consent application under Section 93.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Russian_Federation">Russian Federation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Russian Federation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of 2004, the state authority responsible for conducting the State EIA in Russia has been split between two Federal bodies: 1) Federal service for monitoring the use of natural resources – a part of the Russian Ministry for Natural Resources and Environment and 2) Federal Service for Ecological, Technological and Nuclear Control. The two main pieces of environmental legislation in Russia are: The Federal Law ‘On Ecological Expertise, 1995 and the ‘Regulations on Assessment of Impact from Intended Business and Other Activity on Environment in the Russian Federation, 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Federal_Service_for_monitoring_the_use_of_natural_resources">Federal Service for monitoring the use of natural resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Federal Service for monitoring the use of natural resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 2006, the parliament committee on ecology in conjunction with the Ministry for Natural Resources and Environment, created a working group to prepare a number of amendments to existing legislation to cover such topics as stringent project documentation for building of potentially environmentally damaging objects as well as building of projects on the territory of protected areas.
There has been some success in this area, as evidenced from abandonment of plans to construct a gas pipe-line through the only remaining habitat of the critically endangered Amur leopard in the Russian Far East.
</p>
<h4><span id="Federal_Service_for_Ecological.2C_Technological_and_Nuclear_Control"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Federal_Service_for_Ecological,_Technological_and_Nuclear_Control">Federal Service for Ecological, Technological and Nuclear Control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Federal Service for Ecological, Technological and Nuclear Control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The government’s decision to hand over control over several important procedures, including state EIA in the field of all types of energy projects, to the Federal Service for Ecological, Technological and Nuclear Control had caused a major controversy and criticism from environmental groups that blamed the government for giving nuclear power industry control over the state EIA.
</p><p>Not surprisingly the main problem concerning State EIA in Russia is the clear differentiation of jurisdiction between the two above-mentioned Federal bodies.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sri_Lanka">Sri Lanka</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Sri Lanka">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The National Environmental Act, 1998 requires environmental impact assessment for large scale projects in sensitive areas. It is enforced by the Central Environmental Authority.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[55]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ukraine">Ukraine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Ukraine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The new law of Ukraine on evaluation of impact on surroundings prescribes the requirements of environmental safety, rational use of national resources, minimizing of harmful impact on surroundings in the process of making managerial decisions about planned activity. The designing of the conclusion of evaluation of impact is a result of its conducting. The key moment of the law on evaluation of impact on surroundings is a substitution of conclusion of state environmental experise on the conclusion of evaluation of impact on surroundings. Business entity is forbidden to conduct or to start its planned activity without the conclusion of impact on surroundings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[56]</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/National_Environmental_Policy_Act" title="National Environmental Policy Act">National Environmental Policy Act</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Environmental_Policy_Act" title="National Environmental Policy Act">National Environmental Policy Act</a> of 1969 (NEPA), enacted in 1970, established a policy of environmental impact assessment for federal agency actions, federally funded activities or federally permitted/licensed activities that in the U. S. is termed "environmental review" or simply "the NEPA process."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[57]</a></sup> The law also created the <a href="/wiki/Council_on_Environmental_Quality" title="Council on Environmental Quality">Council on Environmental Quality</a>, which promulgated regulations to codify the law's requirements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[58]</a></sup> Under <a href="/wiki/United_States_environmental_law" title="United States environmental law">United States environmental law</a> an Environmental Assessment (EA) is compiled to determine the need for an <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_Impact_Statement" title="Environmental Impact Statement">Environmental Impact Statement</a></i> (EIS).  Federal or federalized actions expected to subject or be subject to significant environmental impacts will publish a Notice of Intent to Prepare an EIS as soon as significance is known.  Certain actions of <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">federal</a> <a href="/wiki/Government_agency" title="Government agency">agencies</a> must be preceded by the NEPA process.  Contrary to a widespread misconception, NEPA does not prohibit the federal government or its licensees/permittees from harming the environment, nor does it specify any penalty if an environmental impact assessment turns out to be inaccurate, intentionally or otherwise.  NEPA requires that plausible statements as to the prospective impacts be disclosed in advance. The purpose of NEPA process is to ensure that the decision maker is fully informed of the environmental aspects and consequences prior to making the final decision.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_assessment">Environmental assessment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Environmental assessment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>An <b>environmental assessment</b> (EA) is an environmental analysis prepared pursuant to the National Environmental Policy Act to determine whether a federal action would significantly affect the environment and thus require a more detailed <i>Environmental Impact Statement (EIS)</i>. The certified release of an Environmental Assessment results in either a <i>Finding of No Significant Impact (FONSI)</i> or an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_Impact_Statement" title="Environmental Impact Statement">EIS</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Council_on_Environmental_Quality" title="Council on Environmental Quality">Council on Environmental Quality</a> (CEQ), which oversees the administration of NEPA, issued regulations for implementing the NEPA in 1979. Eccleston reports that the NEPA regulations barely mention preparation of EAs. This is because the EA was originally intended to be a simple document used in relatively rare instances where an agency was not sure if the potential significance of an action would be sufficient to trigger preparation of an EIS. But today, because EISs are so much longer and complicated to prepare, federal agencies are going to great effort to avoid preparing EISs by using EAs, even in cases where the use of EAs may be inappropriate. The ratio of EAs that are being issued compared to EISs is about 100 to 1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>Likewise, even the preparation of an accurate EA is viewed today as an onerous burden by many entities responsible for the environmental review of a proposal. Federal agencies have responded by streamlining their regulations that implement NEPA environmental review, by defining categories of projects that by their well understood nature may be safely excluded from review under NEPA, and by drawing up lists of project types that have negligible material impact upon the environment and can thus be exempted.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Content">Content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>The Environmental Assessment is a concise public document prepared by the federal action agency that serves to:
</p>
<ol><li>briefly provide sufficient evidence and analysis for determining whether to prepare an EIS or a Finding of No Significant Impact (FONSI)</li>
<li>Demonstrate compliance with the act when no EIS is required</li>
<li>facilitate the preparation of an EIS when a FONSI cannot be demonstrated</li></ol>
<p>The Environmental Assessment includes a brief discussion of the purpose and need of the proposal and of its alternatives as required by NEPA 102(2)(E), and of the human environmental impacts resulting from and occurring to the proposed actions and alternatives considered practicable, plus a listing of studies conducted and agencies and stakeholders consulted to reach these conclusions.  The action agency must approve an EA before it is made available to the public. The EA is made public through notices of availability by local, state, or regional clearing houses, often triggered by the purchase of a public notice advertisement in a newspaper of general circulation in the proposed activity area.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>The structure of a generic Environmental Assessment is as follows:
</p>
<ol><li>Summary</li>
<li>Introduction
<ul><li>Background</li>
<li>Purpose and Need for Action</li>
<li>Proposed Action</li>
<li>Decision Framework</li>
<li>Public Involvement</li>
<li>Issues</li></ul></li>
<li>Alternatives, including the Proposed Action
<ul><li>Alternatives</li>
<li>Mitigation Common to All Alternatives</li>
<li>Comparison of Alternatives</li></ul></li>
<li>Environmental Consequences</li>
<li>Consultation and Coordination</li></ol>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Procedure">Procedure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Procedure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>The EA becomes a draft public document when notice of it is published, usually in a newspaper of general circulation in the area affected by the proposal. There is a 15-day review period required for an Environmental Assessment (30 days if exceptional circumstances) while the document is made available for public commentary, and a similar time for any objection to improper process. Commenting on the Draft EA is typically done in writing or email, submitted to the lead action agency as published in the notice of availability. An EA does not require a public hearing for verbal comments.  Following the mandated public comment period, the lead action agency responds to any comments, and certifies either a FONSI or a Notice of Intent (NOI) to prepare an EIS in its public environmental review record.  The preparation of an EIS then generates a similar but more lengthy, involved and expensive process.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impact_statement">Environmental impact statement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: Environmental impact statement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_statement" title="Environmental impact statement">Environmental impact statement</a></div>
<p>The adequacy of an environmental impact statement (EIS) can be challenged in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_federal_courts" title="United States federal courts">federal court</a>.  Major proposed projects have been blocked because of an agency's failure to prepare an acceptable EIS.  One prominent example was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Westway_(New_York)" title="Westway (New York)">Westway</a> landfill and highway development in and along the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River" title="Hudson River">Hudson River</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York,_New_York" title="New York, New York">New York City</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[61]</a></sup> Another prominent case involved the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club" title="Sierra Club">Sierra Club</a> suing the <a href="/wiki/Nevada_Department_of_Transportation" title="Nevada Department of Transportation">Nevada Department of Transportation</a> over its denial of the club's request to issue a supplemental EIS addressing air emissions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">particulate matter</a> and hazardous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_pollutants" title="Air pollutants">air pollutants</a> in the case of widening <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_95_in_Nevada" title="U.S. Route 95 in Nevada">U.S. Route 95</a> through <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas" title="Las Vegas">Las Vegas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[62]</a></sup> The case reached the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Ninth_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit">United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit</a>, which led to construction on the highway being halted until the court's final decision. The case was settled prior to the court's final decision.
</p><p>Several <a href="/wiki/State_governments_of_the_United_States" title="State governments of the United States">state governments</a> that have adopted "little NEPAs," <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_law" title="State law">state laws</a> imposing EIS requirements for particular state actions. Some of those state laws such as the <a href="/wiki/California_Environmental_Quality_Act" title="California Environmental Quality Act">California Environmental Quality Act</a> refer to the required environmental impact study as an <b>environmental impact report</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p>This variety of state requirements produces voluminous data not just upon impacts of individual projects, but also in insufficiently researched scientific domains. For example, in a seemingly routine <i>Environmental Impact Report</i> for the city of <a href="/wiki/Monterey,_California" title="Monterey, California">Monterey, California</a>, information came to light that led to the official federal endangered species listing of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hickman%27s_potentilla" title="Hickman's potentilla">Hickman's potentilla</a>, a rare coastal <a href="/wiki/Wildflower" title="Wildflower">wildflower</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[66]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transboundary_application">Transboundary application</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Transboundary application">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Environmental threats do not respect national borders. International pollution can have detrimental effects on the atmosphere, oceans, rivers, <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifers</a>, farmland, the weather and biodiversity. Global climate change is transnational. Specific pollution threats include <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_contamination" title="Radioactive contamination">radioactive contamination</a>, <a href="/wiki/Space_debris" title="Space debris">debris in outer space</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratospheric</a> <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">ozone depletion</a> and toxic <a href="/wiki/Oil_spill" title="Oil spill">oil spills</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Chernobyl_disaster" title="Chernobyl disaster">Chernobyl disaster</a>, precipitated by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_and_radiation_accidents" title="Nuclear and radiation accidents">nuclear accident</a> on April 26, 1986, is a stark reminder of the devastating effects of transboundary nuclear pollution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p>Environmental protection is inherently a cross-border issue and has led to the creation of transnational regulation via multilateral and bilateral treaties. The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Conference_on_the_Human_Environment" title="United Nations Conference on the Human Environment">United Nations Conference on the Human Environment</a> (UNCHE or Stockholm Conference) held in <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a> in 1972 and the <a href="/wiki/Earth_Summit" title="Earth Summit">United Nations Conference on the Environment and Development</a> (UNCED or Rio Summit, Rio Conference, or Earth Summit) held in <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a> in 1992 were key in the creation of about 1,000 international instruments that include at least some provisions related to the environment and its protection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[68]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Economic_Commission_for_Europe" title="United Nations Economic Commission for Europe">United Nations Economic Commission for Europe's</a> <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_Environmental_Impact_Assessment_in_a_Transboundary_Context" title="Convention on Environmental Impact Assessment in a Transboundary Context">Convention on Environmental Impact Assessment in a Transboundary Context</a> was negotiated to provide an international legal framework for transboundary EIA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, as there is no universal legislature or administration with a comprehensive mandate, most international treaties exist parallel to one another and are further developed without the benefit of consideration being given to potential conflicts with other agreements. There is also the issue of international enforcement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[70]</a></sup>
This has led to duplications and failures, in part due to an inability to enforce agreements. An example is the failure of many international 
fisheries regimes to restrict harvesting practises.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[71]</a></sup>
Application shall be achieved by the willing of counties authorities. / Aphro10
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As per Jay <i>et al.</i>, EIA is used as a decision aiding tool rather than decision making tool. There is growing dissent about them as their influence on decisions is limited. Improved training for practitioners, guidance on bestpractice and continuing research have all been proposed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[72]</a></sup>
</p><p>EIAs have been criticized for excessively limiting their scope in space and time. No accepted procedure exists for determining such boundaries. The boundary refers to ‘the spatial and temporal boundary of the proposal’s effects’. This boundary is determined by the applicant and the lead assessor, but in practice, almost all EIAs address only direct and immediate on-site effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[73]</a></sup>
</p><p>Development causes both direct and indirect effects. Consumption of goods and services, production, use and disposal of building materials and machinery, additional land use for activities of manufacturing and services, mining and refining, etc., all have environmental impacts. The indirect effects of development can be much higher than the direct effects examined by an EIA. Proposals such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airports" title="Airports">airports</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shipyards" title="Shipyards">shipyards</a> cause wide-ranging national and international effects, which should be covered in EIAs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[74]</a></sup>
</p><p>Broadening the scope of EIA can benefit the conservation of threatened species. Instead of concentrating on the project site, some EIAs employed a <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a>-based approach that focused on much broader relationships among humans and the environment. As a result, alternatives that reduce the negative effects to the population of whole species, rather than local subpopulations, can be assessed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[75]</a></sup>
</p><p>Thissen and Agusdinata<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[76]</a></sup> have argued that little attention is given to the systematic identification and assessment of uncertainties in environmental studies which is critical in situations where uncertainty cannot be easily reduced by doing more research. In line with this, Maier et al.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[77]</a></sup> have concluded on the need to consider uncertainty at all stages of the decision-making process. In such a way decisions can be made with confidence or known uncertainty. These proposals are justified on data that shows that environmental assessments fail to predict accurately the impacts observed. Tenney et al.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-79">[78]</a></sup> and Wood et al.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[79]</a></sup> have reported evidence of the intrinsic uncertainty attached to EIAs predictions from a number of case studies worldwide. The gathered evidence consisted of comparisons between predictions in EIAs and the impacts measured during, or following project implementation. In explaining this trend, Tenney et al.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_79-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-79">[78]</a></sup> have highlighted major causes such as project changes, modelling errors, errors in data and assumptions taken and bias introduced by people in the projects analyzed.
</p>
