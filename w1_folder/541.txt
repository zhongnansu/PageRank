<p>A <b>conjugate acid</b>, within the <a href="/wiki/Br%C3%B8nsted%E2%80%93Lowry_acid%E2%80%93base_theory" title="Brønsted–Lowry acid–base theory">Brønsted–Lowry acid–base theory</a>, is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_species" title="Chemical species">species</a> formed by the <a href="/wiki/Protonation" title="Protonation">reception of a proton</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hydron_(chemistry)" title="Hydron (chemistry)">H<sup>+</sup></a>) by a <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">base</a>—in other words, it is a base with a <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> ion added to it. On the other hand, a <b>conjugate base</b> is what is left over after an acid has donated a proton during a chemical reaction. Hence, a conjugate base is a species formed by the <a href="/wiki/Deprotonation" title="Deprotonation">removal of a proton</a> from an acid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Because <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyprotic_acid" title="Polyprotic acid">some acids</a> are capable of releasing multiple protons, the conjugate base of an acid may itself be acidic.
</p><p>In summary, this can be represented as the following chemical reaction:
</p>
<dl><dd>Acid + Base ⇌ Conjugate Base + Conjugate Acid</dd></dl>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303569">@media all and (max-width:720px){.mw-parser-output .tmulti>.thumbinner{width:100%!important;max-width:none!important}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .tsingle{float:none!important;max-width:none!important;width:100%!important;text-align:center}}</style><div class="thumb tmulti tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:208px;max-width:208px"><div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:102px;max-width:102px"><div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Johannes_Br%C3%B8nsted.jpg"><img alt="" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="343" height="131" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Johannes_Br%C3%B8nsted.jpg/100px-Johannes_Br%C3%B8nsted.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Johannes_Br%C3%B8nsted.jpg/150px-Johannes_Br%C3%B8nsted.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Johannes_Br%C3%B8nsted.jpg/200px-Johannes_Br%C3%B8nsted.jpg 2x" width="100"/></a></div></div><div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:102px;max-width:102px"><div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Thomas_Martin_Lowry2.jpg"><img alt="" data-file-height="1593" data-file-width="1248" height="128" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Thomas_Martin_Lowry2.jpg/100px-Thomas_Martin_Lowry2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Thomas_Martin_Lowry2.jpg/150px-Thomas_Martin_Lowry2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Thomas_Martin_Lowry2.jpg/200px-Thomas_Martin_Lowry2.jpg 2x" width="100"/></a></div></div><div style="clear:left"></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left;text-align:left;background-color:transparent"><a href="/wiki/Johannes_Nicolaus_Br%C3%B8nsted" title="Johannes Nicolaus Brønsted">Johannes Nicolaus Brønsted</a> (left) and <a href="/wiki/Martin_Lowry" title="Martin Lowry">Martin Lowry</a> (right).</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Johannes_Nicolaus_Br%C3%B8nsted" title="Johannes Nicolaus Brønsted">Johannes Nicolaus Brønsted</a> and <a href="/wiki/Martin_Lowry" title="Martin Lowry">Martin Lowry</a> introduced the Brønsted–Lowry theory,
which proposed that any compound that can transfer a proton to any other compound is an acid, and the compound that accepts the proton is a base. A proton is a nuclear particle with a unit positive electrical charge; it is represented by the symbol H<sup>+</sup> because it constitutes the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">nucleus</a> of a hydrogen <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> that is, a <a href="/wiki/Hydron_(chemistry)" title="Hydron (chemistry)">hydrogen cation</a>.
</p><p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">cation</a> can be a conjugate acid, and an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">anion</a> can be a conjugate base, depending on which <a href="/wiki/Chemical_substance" title="Chemical substance">substance</a> is involved and which <a href="/wiki/Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction" title="Acid–base reaction">acid–base theory</a> is the viewpoint. The simplest anion which can be a conjugate base is the <a href="/wiki/Solvated_electron" title="Solvated electron">solvated electron</a> whose conjugate acid is the atomic hydrogen.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acid-base_reactions">Acid-base reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conjugate_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Acid-base reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acid-base_reaction" title="Acid-base reaction">acid-base reaction</a>, an acid plus a base reacts to form a conjugate base plus a conjugate acid:
</p><p>Conjugates are formed when an acid loses a hydrogen proton or a base gains a hydrogen proton. Refer to the following figure:
</p><p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Conjugate_base_reaction.jpg"><img alt="Conjugate base reaction.jpg" data-file-height="304" data-file-width="513" height="304" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/18/Conjugate_base_reaction.jpg" width="513"/></a>
</p><p>We say that the water molecule is the conjugate acid of the hydroxide ion after the latter received the hydrogen proton donated by <a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">ammonium</a>. On the other hand, <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> is the conjugate base for the acid ammonium after ammonium has donated a hydrogen ion towards the production of the water molecule.  We can also refer to OH- as a conjugate base of <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O</span>, since the water molecule donates a proton towards the production of <span class="chemf nowrap">NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> in the reverse reaction, which is the predominating process in nature due to the strength of the base <span class="chemf nowrap">NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> over the hydroxide ion. Based on this information, it is clear that the terms "Acid", "Base", "conjugate acid", and "conjugate base" are not fixed for a certain chemical species; but are interchangeable according to the reaction taking place.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Strength_of_conjugates">Strength of conjugates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conjugate_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Strength of conjugates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The strength of a conjugate acid is directly proportional to its <a href="/wiki/Dissociation_constant" title="Dissociation constant">dissociation constant</a>. If a conjugate acid is strong, its dissociation will have a higher <a href="/wiki/Equilibrium_constant" title="Equilibrium constant">equilibrium constant</a> and the products of the reaction will be favored. The strength of a conjugate base can be seen as the tendency of the species to "pull" hydrogen protons towards itself. If a conjugate base is classified as strong, it will "hold on" to the hydrogen proton when in solution and its acid will not dissociate. 
</p><p>On the other hand, if a species is classified as a strong acid, its conjugate base will be weak in nature. An example of this case would be the dissociation of <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">Hydrochloric acid</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">HCl</span> in water. Since HCl is a strong acid (it dissociates to a great extent), its conjugate base (<span class="chemf nowrap">Cl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>) will be a weak conjugate base. Therefore, in this system, most <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> will be in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Hydronium" title="Hydronium">Hydronium</a> ion <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> instead of attached to a Cl anion and the conjugate base will be weaker than a water molecule.
</p><p>Similarly, if an acid is weak, its conjugate base will be strong. When considering the fact that the Kw is equal to the product of the concentrations of H+ and OH. A weak acid will have a low concentration of H+. The Kw (1.0 * 10^-14) divided by a low H+ concentration will result in a low OH- concentration as well. Therefore, weak acids will have weak conjugate bases, unlike the misconception that they have strong conjugate bases.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Identifying_conjugate_acid-base_pairs">Identifying conjugate acid-base pairs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conjugate_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Identifying conjugate acid-base pairs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The acid and conjugate base as well as the base and conjugate acid are known as conjugate pairs. When finding a conjugate acid or base, it is important to look at the <a href="/wiki/Reagent" title="Reagent">reactants</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_equation" title="Chemical equation">chemical equation</a>. In this case, the reactants are the acids and bases, and the acid corresponds to the conjugate base on the product side of the chemical equation; as does the base to the conjugate acid on the product side of the equation.
</p><p>To identify the conjugate acid, look for the pair of compounds that are related. The <a href="/wiki/Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction" title="Acid–base reaction">acid–base reaction</a> can be viewed in a before and after sense. The before is the reactant side of the equation, the after is the product side of the equation. The conjugate acid in the after side of an equation gains a hydrogen ion, so in the before side of the equation the compound that has one less hydrogen ion of the conjugate acid is the base. The conjugate base in the after side of the equation lost a hydrogen ion, so in the before side of the equation, the compound that has one more hydrogen ion of the conjugate base is the acid.
</p><p>Consider the following acid–base reaction:

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conjugate_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One use of conjugate acids and bases lies in buffering systems, which include a <a href="/wiki/Buffer_solution" title="Buffer solution">buffer solution</a>. In a buffer, a weak acid and its conjugate base (in the form of a salt), or a weak base and its conjugate acid, are used in order to limit the pH change during a titration process. Buffers have both organic and non-organic chemical applications. For example, besides buffers being used in lab processes, our blood acts as a buffer to maintain pH. <p>A second common application with an organic compound would be the production of a buffer with acetic acid. If acetic acid, a weak acid with the formula <span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>COOH</span>, was made into a buffer solution, it would need to be combined with its conjugate base <span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>COO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> in the form of a salt. The resulting mixture is called an acetate buffer, consisting of aqueous <span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>COOH</span> and aqueous <span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>COONa</span>. Acetic acid, along with many other weak acids, serve as useful components of buffers in different lab settings, each useful within their own pH range.
</p><p>An example with an inorganic compound would be the medicinal use of lactic acid’s conjugate base known as lactate in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lactated_Ringer%27s_solution" title="Lactated Ringer's solution">Lactated Ringer's solution</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hartmann%27s_solution" title="Hartmann's solution">Hartmann's solution</a>. Lactic acid has the formula <span class="chemf nowrap">C<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> and its conjugate base is used in intravenous fluids that consist of sodium and potassium cations along with lactate and chloride anions in solution with distilled water. These fluids are commonly isotonic in relation to human blood and are commonly used for spiking up the fluid level in a system after severe blood loss due to trauma, surgery, or burn injury.
</p>
