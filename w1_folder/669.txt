<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Methyl group</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Methylation" title="Methylation">Methylation</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:112px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Methyl_Group_General_Formulae_V.1.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3723" data-file-width="1017" height="403" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Methyl_Group_General_Formulae_V.1.png/110px-Methyl_Group_General_Formulae_V.1.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Methyl_Group_General_Formulae_V.1.png/165px-Methyl_Group_General_Formulae_V.1.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Methyl_Group_General_Formulae_V.1.png/220px-Methyl_Group_General_Formulae_V.1.png 2x" width="110"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Methyl_Group_General_Formulae_V.1.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Different ways of representing a methyl group (highlighted in <span style="color:blue;"><b>blue</b></span>)</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>methyl group</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Alkyl" title="Alkyl">alkyl</a> derived from <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, containing one <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> atom <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">bonded</a> to three <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> atoms — CH<sub>3</sub>. In <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formulas</a>, the group is often <a href="/wiki/Skeletal_formula#Pseudoelement_symbols" title="Skeletal formula">abbreviated</a> <b>Me</b>. Such <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a> groups occur in many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_compounds" title="Organic compounds">organic compounds</a>. It is a very stable group in most molecules.  While the methyl group is usually part of a larger <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecule</a>, it can be found on its own in any of three forms: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">anion</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">cation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Radical_(chemistry)" title="Radical (chemistry)">radical</a>. The anion has eight valence electrons, the radical seven and the cation six. All three forms are highly reactive and rarely observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span id="Methyl_cation.2C_anion.2C_and_radical"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Methyl_cation,_anion,_and_radical">Methyl cation, anion, and radical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methyl_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Methyl cation, anion, and radical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Methyl_cation">Methyl cation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methyl_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Methyl cation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The methylium cation (CH<sub>3</sub><sup>+</sup>) exists in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_phase" title="Gas phase">gas phase</a>, but is otherwise not encountered.  Some compounds are considered to be sources of the CH<sub>3</sub><sup>+</sup> cation, and this simplification is used pervasively in organic chemistry.  For example, <a href="/wiki/Protonation" title="Protonation">protonation</a> of methanol gives a strongly electrophilic methylating reagent:
</p>
<dl><dd>CH<sub>3</sub>OH + H<sup>+</sup>  →  CH<sub>3</sub><sup>+</sup> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Methyl_iodide" title="Methyl iodide">methyl iodide</a> and methyl <a href="/wiki/Triflate" title="Triflate">triflate</a> are viewed as the equivalent of the methyl cation because they readily undergo S<sub>N</sub>2 reactions by weak <a href="/wiki/Nucleophile" title="Nucleophile">nucleophiles</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Methyl_anion">Methyl anion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methyl_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Methyl anion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The methanide anion (CH<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>) exists only in rarefied gas phase or under exotic conditions. It can be produced by electrical discharge in <a href="/wiki/Ketene" title="Ketene">ketene</a> at low pressure (less than one <a href="/wiki/Torr" title="Torr">torr</a>) and its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_reaction" title="Enthalpy of reaction">enthalpy of reaction</a> is determined to be about 252.2±3.3 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilojoule" title="Kilojoule">kJ</a>/<a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mol</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ellison78_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ellison78-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In discussions mechanisms of organic reactions, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methyl_lithium" title="Methyl lithium">methyl lithium</a> and related <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grignard_reagents" title="Grignard reagents">Grignard reagents</a> are often considered to be salts of "CH<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>"; and though the model may be useful for description and analysis, it is only a useful fiction. Such reagents are generally prepared from the methyl halides:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 M + CH<sub>3</sub>X → MCH<sub>3</sub> + MX</dd></dl>
<p>where M is an alkali metal.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Methyl_radical">Methyl radical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methyl_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Methyl radical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Methyl_radical" title="Methyl radical">Methyl radical</a></div>
<p>The methyl <a href="/wiki/Radical_(chemistry)" title="Radical (chemistry)">radical</a> has the formula CH<sub>3</sub>.  It exists in dilute gases, but in more concentrated form it readily <a href="/wiki/Dimer_(chemistry)" title="Dimer (chemistry)">dimerizes</a> to <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">ethane</a>. It can be produced by <a href="/wiki/Thermal_decomposition" title="Thermal decomposition">thermal decomposition</a> of only certain compounds, especially those with an -N=N- linkage.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactivity">Reactivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methyl_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reactivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The reactivity of a methyl group depends on the adjacent <a href="/wiki/Substituent" title="Substituent">substituents</a>.  Methyl groups can be quite unreactive.  For example, in organic compounds, the methyl group resists attack by even the strongest <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxidation">Oxidation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methyl_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Oxidation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidation</a> of a methyl group occurs widely in nature and industry. The oxidation products derived from methyl are CH<sub>2</sub>OH, CHO, and CO<sub>2</sub>H.   For example, <a href="/wiki/Permanganate" title="Permanganate">permanganate</a> often converts a methyl group to a carboxyl (-COOH) group, e.g. the conversion of <a href="/wiki/Toluene" title="Toluene">toluene</a> to <a href="/wiki/Benzoic_acid" title="Benzoic acid">benzoic acid</a>. Ultimately oxidation of methyl groups gives <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, as seen in combustion.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Methylation">Methylation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methyl_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Methylation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Methylation" title="Methylation">Methylation</a></div>
<p>Demethylation (the transfer of the methyl group to another compound) is a common process, and <a href="/wiki/Reagent" title="Reagent">reagents</a> that undergo this reaction are called methylating agents.  Common methylating agents are <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfate" title="Dimethyl sulfate">dimethyl sulfate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methyl_iodide" title="Methyl iodide">methyl iodide</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methyl_triflate" title="Methyl triflate">methyl triflate</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Methanogenesis" title="Methanogenesis">Methanogenesis</a>, the source of natural gas, arises via a demethylation reaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deprotonation">Deprotonation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methyl_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Deprotonation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Certain methyl groups can be deprotonated. For example, the acidity of the methyl groups in <a href="/wiki/Acetone" title="Acetone">acetone</a> ((CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>CO) is about 10<sup>20</sup> more acidic than methane. The resulting <a href="/wiki/Carbanion" title="Carbanion">carbanions</a> are key intermediates in many reactions in <a href="/wiki/Organic_synthesis" title="Organic synthesis">organic synthesis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biosynthesis" title="Biosynthesis">biosynthesis</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Fatty_acid" title="Fatty acid">Fatty acids</a> are produced in this way.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Free_radical_reactions">Free radical reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methyl_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Free radical reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When placed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benzylic" title="Benzylic">benzylic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allylic" title="Allylic">allylic</a> positions, the strength of the C-H bond is decreased, and the reactivity of the methyl group increases.  One manifestation of this enhanced reactivity is the <a href="/wiki/Photochemistry" title="Photochemistry">photochemical</a> <a href="/wiki/Halogenation" title="Halogenation">chlorination</a> of the methyl group in <a href="/wiki/Toluene" title="Toluene">toluene</a> to give <a href="/wiki/Benzyl_chloride" title="Benzyl chloride">benzyl chloride</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chiral_methyl">Chiral methyl</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methyl_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Chiral methyl">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the special case where one hydrogen is replaced by <a href="/wiki/Deuterium" title="Deuterium">deuterium</a> (D) and another hydrogen by <a href="/wiki/Tritium" title="Tritium">tritium</a> (T), the methyl substituent becomes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chiral" title="Chiral">chiral</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>  Methods exist to produce optically pure methyl compounds, e.g., chiral <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a> (CHDTCO<sub>2</sub>H). Through the use of chiral methyl groups, the <a href="/wiki/Stereochemistry" title="Stereochemistry">stereochemical</a> course of several <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemical</a> transformations have been analyzed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methyl_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> chemists <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Dumas" title="Jean-Baptiste Dumas">Jean-Baptiste Dumas</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eugene_Peligot" title="Eugene Peligot">Eugene Peligot</a>, after determining methanol's chemical structure, introduced "methylene" from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> <i>methy</i> "wine" and <i>hȳlē</i> "wood, patch of trees" with the intention of highlighting its origins, "alcohol made from wood (substance)".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The term "methyl" was derived in about 1840 by <a href="/wiki/Back-formation" title="Back-formation">back-formation</a> from "methylene", and was then applied to describe "methyl alcohol".
</p><p><i>Methyl</i> is the <a href="/wiki/IUPAC_nomenclature_of_organic_chemistry" title="IUPAC nomenclature of organic chemistry">IUPAC nomenclature of organic chemistry</a> term for an <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">alkane</a> (or alkyl) molecule, using the prefix "meth-" to indicate the presence of a single carbon.
</p>
