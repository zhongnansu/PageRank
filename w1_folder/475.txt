<p>A <b>meromictic lake</b> has layers of water that do not intermix.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wetzel_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wetzel-1">[1]</a></sup> In ordinary, "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holomictic" title="Holomictic">holomictic</a>" <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a>, at least once each year, there is a physical mixing of the surface and the deep waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewis1983_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lewis1983-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The term "meromictic" was coined by the Austrian <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ingo_Findenegg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ingo Findenegg (page does not exist)">Ingo Findenegg</a> in 1935, apparently based on the older word "holomictic". The concepts and terminology used in describing meromictic lakes were essentially complete following some additions by <a href="/wiki/G._Evelyn_Hutchinson" title="G. Evelyn Hutchinson">G. Evelyn Hutchinson</a> in 1937.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hakala2004_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hakala2004-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Findenegg_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Findenegg-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huthcinson_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Huthcinson-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Most lakes are <i>holomictic</i>; that is, at least once per year, physical mixing occurs between the surface and the deep waters. In so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monomictic" title="Monomictic">monomictic</a> lakes, the mixing occurs once per year; in <a href="/wiki/Dimictic_lake" title="Dimictic lake">dimictic</a> lakes, the mixing occurs twice a year (typically spring and autumn), and in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polymictic" title="Polymictic">polymictic</a> lakes, the mixing occurs several times a year. In meromictic lakes, however, the layers of the lake water can remain unmixed for years, decades, or centuries.
</p><p>Meromictic lakes can usually be divided into three sections or layers. The bottom layer is known as the <i>monimolimnion</i>; the waters in this portion of the lake circulate little, and are generally <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(environmental)" title="Hypoxia (environmental)">hypoxic</a> and saltier than the rest of the lake. The top layer is called the <i>mixolimnion</i>, and essentially behaves like a holomictic lake. The area in between is referred to as the <i>chemocline</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walker1974_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Walker1974-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The lack of mixing between layers creates radically different environments for organisms to live in: among the consequences of this stratification, or stable layering, of lake waters is that the bottom layer receives little oxygen from the atmosphere, hence becomes depleted of oxygen. While the surface layer may have 10 mg/L or more dissolved oxygen in summer, the depths of a meromictic lake can have less than 1 mg/L.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LampertSommer_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-LampertSommer-7">[7]</a></sup> Very few organisms can live in such an oxygen-poor environment. One exception is  <a href="/wiki/Purple_sulfur_bacteria" title="Purple sulfur bacteria">purple sulfur bacteria</a>. These bacteria, commonly found at the top of the monimolimnion in such lakes, use <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> compounds such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulfides" title="Sulfides">sulfides</a> in <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>. These compounds are produced by decomposition of organic sediments in oxygen-poor environments.  The monimolimnion is often rich in phosphorus and nitrogen. These factors combine to create an ideal environment for bacterial growth. The mixolimnion can have similar qualities. However, the types of bacteria that can grow at the surface are determined by the amount of light received at the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fry1986_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fry1986-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>A meromictic lake may form for a number of reasons:
</p>
<ul><li>The basin is unusually deep and steep-sided compared to the lake's surface area</li>
<li>The lower layer of the lake is highly <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">saline</a> and denser than the upper layers of water</li></ul>
<p>The layers of sediment at the bottom of a meromictic lake remain relatively undisturbed because there is little physical mixing and few living organisms to agitate them.  There is also little chemical decomposition. For this reason, <a href="/wiki/Core_sample" title="Core sample">cores</a> of the sediment at the bottom of meromictic lakes are important in tracing past changes in climate at the lake, by examining trapped pollen grains and the types of sediments [see <i><a href="/wiki/Proxy_(climate)" title="Proxy (climate)">Proxy (climate)</a></i>].
</p><p>When the layers do mix for whatever reason, the consequences can be devastating for organisms that normally live in the mixolimnion. This layer is usually much smaller in volume than the monimolimnion. When the layers mix, the oxygen concentration at the surface will decrease dramatically. This can result in the death of many organisms, such as fish, that require oxygen.
</p><p>Occasionally, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) or other dissolved <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gases</a> can build up relatively undisturbed in the lower layers of a meromictic lake. When the stratification is disturbed, as could happen from an <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquake</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Limnic_eruption" title="Limnic eruption">limnic eruption</a> may result. In 1986, a notable event of this type took place at <a href="/wiki/Lake_Nyos" title="Lake Nyos">Lake Nyos</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a>, causing nearly 1,800 deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krajick_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Krajick-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>While it is mainly lakes that are meromictic, the world's largest meromictic basin is the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>. The deep waters below 50 metres (150 feet) do not mix with the upper layers that receive oxygen from the atmosphere. As a result, over 90% of the deeper Black Sea volume is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anoxic_sea_water" title="Anoxic sea water">anoxic water</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a> is anoxic below 100 metres (300 feet). The <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic Sea</a> is persistently stratified, with dense, highly saline water comprising the bottom layer, and large areas of hypoxic sediments (see <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea_hypoxia" title="Baltic Sea hypoxia">Baltic Sea hypoxia</a>).
</p>

<p>There are meromictic lakes all over the world. The distribution appears to be clustered, but this may be due to incomplete investigations. Depending on the exact definition of "meromictic", the ratio between meromictic and holomictic lakes worldwide is around 1:1000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hakala2005_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hakala2005-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
