<p><i><b>Popular Science</b></i> (also known as <i><b>PopSci</b></i>) is an American quarterly magazine carrying <a href="/wiki/Popular_science" title="Popular science">popular science</a> content, which refers to articles for the general reader on science and technology subjects. <i>Popular Science</i> has won over 58 awards, including the <a href="/wiki/American_Society_of_Magazine_Editors" title="American Society of Magazine Editors">American Society of Magazine Editors</a> awards for its journalistic excellence in both 2003 (for General Excellence) and 2004 (for Best Magazine Section). With roots beginning in 1872,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <i>Popular Science</i> has been translated into over 30 languages and is distributed to at least 45 countries.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Popular_Science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/The_Popular_Science_Monthly" title="wikisource:The Popular Science Monthly">The Popular Science Monthly</a></i>, as the publication was originally called, was founded in May 1872<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Edward_L._Youmans" title="Edward L. Youmans">Edward L. Youmans</a> to disseminate scientific knowledge to the educated layman. Youmans had previously worked as an editor for the weekly <i>Appleton's Journal</i> and persuaded them to publish his new journal. Early issues were mostly reprints of English periodicals. The journal became an outlet for writings and ideas of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Henry_Huxley" title="Thomas Henry Huxley">Thomas Henry Huxley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louis_Pasteur" title="Louis Pasteur">Louis Pasteur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Ward_Beecher" title="Henry Ward Beecher">Henry Ward Beecher</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Sanders_Peirce" title="Charles Sanders Peirce">Charles Sanders Peirce</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_James" title="William James">William James</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">Thomas Edison</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Dewey" title="John Dewey">John Dewey</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_McKeen_Cattell" title="James McKeen Cattell">James McKeen Cattell</a>. <a href="/wiki/William_Jay_Youmans" title="William Jay Youmans">William Jay Youmans</a>, Edward's brother, helped found <i>Popular Science Monthly</i> in 1872 and was an editor as well. He became editor-in-chief on Edward's death in 1887.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  The publisher, <a href="/wiki/D._Appleton_%26_Company" title="D. Appleton &amp; Company">D. Appleton &amp; Company</a>, was forced for economic reasons to sell the journal in 1900.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mass_Market_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mass_Market-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/James_McKeen_Cattell" title="James McKeen Cattell">James McKeen Cattell</a> became the editor in 1900 and the publisher in 1901. Cattell had a background in academics and continued publishing articles for educated readers. By 1915 the readership was declining and publishing a science journal was a financial challenge. In a September 1915 editorial, Cattell related these difficulties to his readers and announced that the <i>Popular Science Monthly</i> name had been "transferred" to a group that wanted the name for a general audience magazine, a publication which fit the name better. The existing journal would continue the academic tradition as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_Monthly" title="Scientific Monthly">Scientific Monthly</a></i>. Existing subscribers would remain subscribed under the new name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PSM_Sep_1915_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-PSM_Sep_1915-6">[6]</a></sup> <i>Scientific Monthly</i> was published until 1958 when it was absorbed into <i><a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Modern Publishing Company acquired the <i>Popular Science Monthly</i> name. This company had purchased <i><a href="/wiki/Electrician_and_Mechanic" title="Electrician and Mechanic">Electrician and Mechanic</a></i> magazine in 1914 and over the next two years merged several magazines together into a science magazine for a general audience. The magazine had a series of name changes: <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_Electrics_and_Mechanics" title="Modern Electrics and Mechanics">Modern Electrics and Mechanics</a></i>, <i>Popular Electricity and Modern Mechanics</i>, <i>Modern Mechanics</i> and finally <i>World's Advance</i>, before the publishers purchased the name <i>Popular Science Monthly</i>. The October 1915 issue was titled <i>Popular Science Monthly and World's Advance</i>. The volume number (Vol. 87, No. 4) was that of <i>Popular Science</i> but the content was that of <i>World's Advance</i>. The new editor was <a href="/wiki/Waldemar_Kaempffert" title="Waldemar Kaempffert">Waldemar Kaempffert</a>, a former editor of <i><a href="/wiki/Scientific_American" title="Scientific American">Scientific American</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTimes_Aug_29_1915_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes_Aug_29_1915-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ALA_1918_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ALA_1918-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The change in <i>Popular Science Monthly</i> was dramatic. The old version was a scholarly journal that had eight to ten articles in a 100-page issue. There would be ten to twenty photographs or illustrations. The new version had hundreds of short, easy to read articles with hundreds of illustrations. Editor Kaempffert was writing for "the home craftsman and hobbyist who wanted to know something about the world of science." The circulation doubled in the first year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mass_Market_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mass_Market-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>From the mid-1930s to the 1960s, the magazine featured fictional stories of Gus Wilson's Model Garage, centered on car problems.
</p><p>An annual review of changes to the new model year cars ran in 1940 and '41, but did not return after the war until 1954. It continued until the mid-1970s when the magazine reverted to publishing the new models over multiple issues as information became available.
</p><p>From 1935 to 1949, the magazine sponsored a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Popular_Science_(film)" title="Popular Science (film)">series of short films</a>, produced by <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Fairbanks" title="Jerry Fairbanks">Jerry Fairbanks</a> and released by <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">Paramount Pictures</a>.
</p><p>From July 1952 to December 1989, <i>Popular Science</i> carried <a href="/wiki/Roy_Doty" title="Roy Doty">Roy Doty</a>'s Wordless Workshop as a regular feature.
</p><p>From July 1969 to May 1989, the cover and table of contents carried the subtitle, "The What's New Magazine." The cover removed the subtitle the following month and the contents page removed it in February 1990. In 1983, the magazine introduced a new logo using the <a href="/wiki/ITC_Avant_Garde" title="ITC Avant Garde">ITC Avant Garde</a> font, which it used until late 1995. Within the next 11 years, its font changed 4 times (in 1995, 1997, 2001, and 2002, respectively). In 2009, the magazine used a new font for its logo, which was used until the January 2014 issue.
</p><p>In 2014, <i>Popular Science</i> sported a new look and introduced a new logo for the first time in 8 years, complete with a major overhaul of its articles.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_history">Recent history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Popular_Science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Recent history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Popular Science Publishing Company, which the magazine bears its name, was acquired in 1967 by the Los Angeles-based <a href="/wiki/Times_Mirror_Company" title="Times Mirror Company">Times Mirror Company</a>. In 2000, Times Mirror merged with the Chicago-based <a href="/wiki/Tribune_Media" title="Tribune Media">Tribune Company</a>, which then sold the Times Mirror magazines to <a href="/wiki/Time_Inc." title="Time Inc.">Time Inc.</a> (then a subsidiary of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_Warner" title="Time Warner">Time Warner</a>) the following year. On January 25, 2007, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_Warner" title="Time Warner">Time Warner</a> sold this magazine, along with 17 other special interest magazines, to <a href="/wiki/Bonnier_Group" title="Bonnier Group">Bonnier</a> Magazine Group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> On September 24, 2008, Australian publishing company Australian Media Properties (part of the WW Media Group) launched a local version of <i>Popular Science</i>. It is a monthly magazine, like its American counterpart, and uses content from the American version of the magazine as well as local material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Australian Media Properties also launched www.popsci.com.au at the same time, a localised version  of the <i>Popular Science</i> website.
</p><p>In January 2016, <i>Popular Science</i> switched to bi-monthly publication after 144 years of monthly publication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>In April 2016 it was announced that editor-in-chief Cliff Ransom would step down. It was also announced that he would remain on staff as an editor-at-large.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Popular Science</i> is headquartered in New York, New York.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio">Radio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Popular_Science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Radio">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Popular Science Radio</i> is a partnership between <i>Popular Science</i> and Entertainment Radio Network.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tablet">Tablet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Popular_Science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tablet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On March 27, 2011, <i>Popular Science</i> magazine sold the 10,000th subscription to its iPad edition, nearly six weeks after accepting Apple's terms for selling subs on its tablet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mobile">Mobile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Popular_Science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mobile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In August 2009, <i>Popular Science</i> launched a free iPhone app called PopSci.com,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> which delivers content from their Web site. The app got a redesign and major update in November 2010. Since January 2011, <i>Popular Science</i> is also available for Android phones and tablets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Popular_Science.2B"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_Science+">Popular Science+</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Popular_Science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Popular Science+">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In early 2010, Bonnier partnered with London-based design firm BERG to create Mag+, a magazine publishing platform for tablets. In April 2010, Popular Science+,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> the first title on the Mag+ platform, launched in the <a href="/wiki/ITunes_Store" title="ITunes Store">iTunes Store</a> the same day the iPad launched.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The app contains all the content in the print version as well as added content and digital-only extras. Bonnier has since launched several more titles on the Mag+ platform, including <i>Popular Photography</i>+ and <i>Transworld Snowboarding</i>+.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_Science_Predictions_Exchange">Popular Science Predictions Exchange</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Popular_Science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Popular Science Predictions Exchange">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In July 2007, Popular Science launched the <a href="/wiki/Popular_Science_Predictions_Exchange" title="Popular Science Predictions Exchange"><b>P</b>opular Science <b>P</b>redictions E<b>X</b>change</a> (PPX). People were able to place virtual bets on what the next <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Innovations" title="Innovations">innovations</a> in <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a>, the environment, and science would be. Bets have included whether Facebook would have an <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a> by 2008, when a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPod_touch" title="IPod touch">touchscreen iPod</a> would be launched, and whether <a href="/wiki/Dongtan,_Shanghai" title="Dongtan, Shanghai">Dongtan</a>, China's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eco-city" title="Eco-city">eco-city</a>, would be inhabited by 2010. The PPX shut down in 2009.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television-Future_Of...">Television-Future Of...</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Popular_Science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Television-Future Of...">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Popular Science's Future Of...</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> show premiered on Monday, August 10, 2009 on the Science Channel. The show is concerned with the future of technology and science in a particular topic area that varies from week to week. As of December 2009, a new episode is premiered every Monday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_Science_on_Google_Books">Popular Science on Google Books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Popular_Science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Popular Science on Google Books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since March 5, 2010, all <i>Popular Science</i> issues since the first issue of May 1872 through March 2009 (except October–December 1915 and January-June 1917) are available for free on <a href="/wiki/Google_Books" title="Google Books">Google Books</a> although several 1910s/20s issues are apparently scanned from bound library volumes that had been stripped of full-page ads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_Languages">Other Languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Popular_Science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other Languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In June 2014, Popular Science was launched in Italy by Kekoa Publishing. Directed by <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Maria_Avitto" title="Francesco Maria Avitto">Francesco Maria Avitto</a> is available in print and digital version.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>In April 2017, Popular Science was launched in Arabic by UAE based publisher Haykal Media. The magazine is available in print bimonthly, and through a daily updated portal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
