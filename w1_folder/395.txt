<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Mariana Trench</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">The deepest part of Earth's oceans, where the Pacific Plate is subducted under the Mariana Plate</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about the deep sea trench. For the Canadian band named after the geological formation, see <a href="/wiki/Marianas_Trench_(band)" title="Marianas Trench (band)">Marianas Trench (band)</a>.</div>
<p><span style="font-size: small;"><span id="coordinates"><a href="/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system" title="Geographic coordinate system">Coordinates</a>: <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Mariana_Trench&amp;params=11_21_N_142_12_E_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">11°21′N</span> <span class="longitude">142°12′E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">11.350°N 142.200°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">11.350; 142.200</span></span></span></a></span></span></span>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Marianatrenchmap.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="985" data-file-width="756" height="378" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Marianatrenchmap.png/290px-Marianatrenchmap.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Marianatrenchmap.png/435px-Marianatrenchmap.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Marianatrenchmap.png/580px-Marianatrenchmap.png 2x" width="290"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Marianatrenchmap.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Location of the Mariana Trench</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Mariana Trench</b> or <b>Marianas Trench</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USNGA_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-USNGA-1">[1]</a></sup> is located in the western <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> approximately 200 kilometres (124 mi) east of the <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Islands" title="Mariana Islands">Mariana Islands</a>, and has the <a href="/wiki/Deep_sea" title="Deep sea">deepest</a> natural point in the world. It is a crescent-shaped trough in the Earth's crust averaging about 2,550 km (1,580 mi) long and 69 km (43 mi) wide. The maximum known depth is 10,994 metres (36,070 ft) (± 40 metres [130 ft]) at the southern end of a small slot-shaped valley in its floor known as the <a href="/wiki/Challenger_Deep" title="Challenger Deep">Challenger Deep</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smmt_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-smmt-2">[2]</a></sup> However, some, unrepeated, measurements place the deepest portion at 11,034 metres (36,201 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mariana_britannica_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-mariana_britannica-3">[3]</a></sup> For comparison: if <a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">Mount Everest</a> were dropped into the trench at this point, its peak would still be over 2 kilometres (1.2 mi) under water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[a]</a></sup>
</p><p>At the bottom of the trench the <a href="/wiki/Water_column" title="Water column">water column</a> above exerts a pressure of 1,086 bars (15,750 psi), more than 1,000 times the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_atmospheric_pressure" title="Standard atmospheric pressure">standard atmospheric pressure</a> at sea level. At this pressure, the density of water is increased by 4.96%, so that 95.27 litres of water under the pressure of the Challenger Deep would contain the same mass as 100 litres at the surface. The temperature at the bottom is 1 to 4 °C (34 to 39 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The trench is not the part of the seafloor closest to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_center" title="Earth's center">centre of the Earth</a>. This is because the Earth is not a perfect <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">sphere</a>; its radius is about 25 kilometres (16 mi) smaller at the poles than at the equator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> As a result, parts of the Arctic Ocean seabed are at least 13 kilometres (8.1 mi) closer to the Earth's centre than the Challenger Deep seafloor.
</p><p>In 2009, the Marianas Trench was established as a United States National Monument.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Xenophyophore" title="Xenophyophore">Xenophyophores</a> have been found in the trench by <a href="/wiki/Scripps_Institution_of_Oceanography" title="Scripps Institution of Oceanography">Scripps Institution of Oceanography</a> researchers at a record depth of 10.6 kilometres (6.6 mi) below the sea surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gaf_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-gaf-9">[8]</a></sup> On 17 March 2013, researchers from the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Association_for_Marine_Science" title="Scottish Association for Marine Science">Scottish Association for Marine Science</a> reported data that suggested <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microbes" title="Microbes">microbial life forms</a> thrive within the trench.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LS-20130317_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-LS-20130317-10">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NG-20130317_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-NG-20130317-11">[10]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names">Names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariana_Trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Mariana Trench is named for the nearby <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Islands" title="Mariana Islands">Mariana Islands</a> (in turn named Las Marianas in honor of Spanish Queen <a href="/wiki/Mariana_of_Austria" title="Mariana of Austria">Mariana of Austria</a>, widow of <a href="/wiki/Philip_IV_of_Spain" title="Philip IV of Spain">Philip IV of Spain</a>). The islands are part of the <a href="/wiki/Island_arc" title="Island arc">island arc</a> that is formed on an over-riding plate, called the Mariana Plate (also named for the islands), on the western side of the trench.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariana_Trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cross_section_of_mariana_trench.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="319" data-file-width="600" height="186" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Cross_section_of_mariana_trench.svg/350px-Cross_section_of_mariana_trench.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Cross_section_of_mariana_trench.svg/525px-Cross_section_of_mariana_trench.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Cross_section_of_mariana_trench.svg/700px-Cross_section_of_mariana_trench.svg.png 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cross_section_of_mariana_trench.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Pacific plate is subducted beneath the Mariana Plate, creating the Mariana trench, and (further on) the arc of the Mariana islands, as water trapped in the plate is released and explodes upward to form island volcanoes and  earthquakes .</div></div></div>
<p>The Mariana Trench is part of the <a href="/wiki/Izu-Bonin-Mariana_Arc" title="Izu-Bonin-Mariana Arc">Izu-Bonin-Mariana</a> <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subduction</a> system that forms the <a href="/wiki/Convergent_boundary" title="Convergent boundary">boundary</a> between two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic_plate" title="Tectonic plate">tectonic plates</a>. In this system, the western edge of one plate, the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Plate" title="Pacific Plate">Pacific Plate</a>, is <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subducted</a> (i.e., thrust) beneath the smaller <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Plate" title="Mariana Plate">Mariana Plate</a> that lies to the west. Crustal material at the western edge of the Pacific Plate is some of the oldest oceanic crust on earth (up to 170 million years old), and is therefore cooler and more dense; hence its great height difference relative to the higher-riding (and younger) Mariana Plate. The deepest area at the plate boundary is the Mariana Trench proper.
</p><p>The movement of the Pacific and Mariana plates is also indirectly responsible for the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Islands" title="Mariana Islands">Mariana Islands</a>. These volcanic islands are caused by <a href="/wiki/Flux_melting" title="Flux melting">Flux melting</a> of the upper mantle due to release of water that is trapped in minerals of the subducted portion of the Pacific Plate.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurements">Measurements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariana_Trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Measurements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Map00148_(28286522445).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2959" data-file-width="1777" height="366" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Map00148_%2828286522445%29.jpg/220px-Map00148_%2828286522445%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Map00148_%2828286522445%29.jpg/330px-Map00148_%2828286522445%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Map00148_%2828286522445%29.jpg/440px-Map00148_%2828286522445%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Map00148_(28286522445).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ocean trenches in the western Pacific</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Challenger_Deep" title="Challenger Deep">Challenger Deep</a></div>
<p>The trench was first <a href="/wiki/Depth_sounding" title="Depth sounding">sounded</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Challenger_expedition" title="Challenger expedition"><i>Challenger</i> expedition</a> in 1875, using a weighted rope, which recorded a depth of 4,475 <a href="/wiki/Fathom" title="Fathom">fathoms</a> (8,184 metres; 26,850 feet).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> In 1877, a map was published called <i>Tiefenkarte des Grossen Ozeans</i> ("Deep map of the Great Ocean") by Petermann, which showed a <i>Challenger Tief</i> ("Challenger deep") at the location of that sounding. In 1899, <a href="/wiki/USS_Nero_(AC-17)" title="USS Nero (AC-17)">USS Nero</a>, a converted collier, recorded a depth of 5,269 fathoms (9,636 metres; 31,614 feet).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Theberge_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Theberge-13">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1951, <i><a href="/wiki/HMS_Challenger_(1931)" title="HMS Challenger (1931)">Challenger II</a></i> surveyed the trench using <a href="/wiki/Echo_sounding" title="Echo sounding">echo sounding</a>, a much more precise and vastly easier way to measure depth than the sounding equipment and drag lines used in the original expedition. During this survey, the deepest part of the trench was recorded when the <i>Challenger II</i> measured a depth of 5,960 fathoms (10,900 metres; 35,760 feet) at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Mariana_Trench&amp;params=11_19_N_142_15_E_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">11°19′N</span> <span class="longitude">142°15′E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">11.317°N 142.250°E</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">11.317; 142.250</span></span></span></a></span>, known as the <a href="/wiki/Challenger_Deep" title="Challenger Deep">Challenger Deep</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marianatrench.com_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-marianatrench.com-14">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1957, the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> vessel <a href="/wiki/RV_Vityaz_(1939)" title="RV Vityaz (1939)"><i>Vityaz</i></a> reported a depth of 11,034 metres (36,201 ft) at a location dubbed the <i>Mariana Hollow</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mariana_britannica_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-mariana_britannica-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1962, the surface ship M.V. <i>Spencer F. Baird</i> recorded a maximum depth of 10,915 metres (35,810 ft) using precision depth gauges.
</p><p>In 1984, the Japanese survey vessel <i>Takuyō</i> (拓洋) collected data from the Mariana Trench using a narrow, multi-beam echo sounder; it reported a maximum depth of 10,924 metres (35,840 ft), also reported as 10,920 metres (35,830 ft) ±10 m (33 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tani_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tani-15">[14]</a></sup> Remotely Operated Vehicle <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kaik%C5%8D" title="Kaikō">KAIKO</a></i> reached the deepest area of the Mariana Trench and made the deepest diving record of 10,911 metres (35,797 ft) on March 24, 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>During surveys carried out between 1997 and 2001, a spot was found along the Mariana Trench that had depth similar to that of the Challenger Deep, possibly even deeper. It was discovered while scientists from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Hawaii" title="University of Hawaii">Hawaii Institute of Geophysics and Planetology</a> were completing a survey around <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a>; they used a sonar mapping system towed behind the research ship to conduct the survey. This new spot was named the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HMRG_Deep" title="HMRG Deep">HMRG (Hawaii Mapping Research Group) Deep</a>, after the group of scientists who discovered it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 1 June 2009, sonar mapping of the Challenger Deep by the Simrad EM120 sonar multibeam bathymetry system for deep water,  mapping aboard the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RV_Kilo_Moana" title="RV Kilo Moana">RV Kilo Moana</a></i> (mothership of the Nereus vehicle), indicated a spot with a depth of 10,971 metres (35,994 ft). The sonar system uses phase and amplitude bottom detection, with an accuracy of better than 0.2% of water depth across the entire swath (implying that the depth figure is accurate to ± 22 metres (72 ft)).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daily_Reports_for_R/V_KILO_MOANA_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Daily_Reports_for_R/V_KILO_MOANA-18">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scientic_Equipment_aboard_the_R/V_KILO_MOANA_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scientic_Equipment_aboard_the_R/V_KILO_MOANA-19">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2011, it was announced at the <a href="/wiki/American_Geophysical_Union" title="American Geophysical Union">American Geophysical Union</a> Fall Meeting that a US Navy hydrographic ship equipped with a <a href="/wiki/Multibeam_echosounder" title="Multibeam echosounder">multibeam echosounder</a> conducted a survey which mapped the entire trench to 100 metres (330 ft) resolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smmt_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-smmt-2">[2]</a></sup> The mapping revealed the existence of four rocky outcrops thought to be former <a href="/wiki/Seamount" title="Seamount">seamounts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fourbrid_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-fourbrid-20">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Mariana Trench is a site chosen by researchers at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_University" title="Washington University">Washington University</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Woods_Hole_Oceanographic_Institution" title="Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution">Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution</a> in 2012 for a seismic survey to investigate the subsurface <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">water cycle</a>. Using both <a href="/wiki/Ocean-bottom_seismometer" title="Ocean-bottom seismometer">ocean-bottom seismometers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrophone" title="Hydrophone">hydrophones</a> the scientists are able to map structures as deep as 97 kilometres (60 mi) beneath the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ssatmt_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-ssatmt-21">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Descents">Descents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariana_Trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Descents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bathyscaphe_Trieste.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="583" data-file-width="716" height="179" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Bathyscaphe_Trieste.jpg/220px-Bathyscaphe_Trieste.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Bathyscaphe_Trieste.jpg/330px-Bathyscaphe_Trieste.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Bathyscaphe_Trieste.jpg/440px-Bathyscaphe_Trieste.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bathyscaphe_Trieste.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Bathyscaphe" title="Bathyscaphe">bathyscaphe</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Bathyscaphe_Trieste" title="Bathyscaphe Trieste">Trieste</a></i> (designed by <a href="/wiki/Auguste_Piccard" title="Auguste Piccard">Auguste Piccard</a>), the first manned vehicle to reach the bottom of the Mariana Trench.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Four descents have been achieved. The first was the manned descent by Swiss-designed, Italian-built, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a>-owned <a href="/wiki/Bathyscaphe" title="Bathyscaphe">bathyscaphe</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Bathyscaphe_Trieste" title="Bathyscaphe Trieste">Trieste</a></i> which reached the bottom at 1:06 pm on 23 January 1960, with <a href="/wiki/Don_Walsh" title="Don Walsh">Don Walsh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Piccard" title="Jacques Piccard">Jacques Piccard</a> on board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marianatrench.com_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-marianatrench.com-14">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eahapr_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-eahapr-23">[22]</a></sup> Iron shot was used for <a href="/wiki/Sailing_ballast" title="Sailing ballast">ballast</a>, with gasoline for <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marianatrench.com_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-marianatrench.com-14">[13]</a></sup> The onboard systems indicated a depth of 11,521 m (37,799 ft), but this was later revised to 10,916 m (35,814 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_Man_at_the_Deepest_Depth_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_Man_at_the_Deepest_Depth-24">[23]</a></sup> The depth was estimated from a conversion of <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> measured and calculations based on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Density_of_saltwater" title="Density of saltwater">water density</a> from sea surface to seabed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eahapr_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-eahapr-23">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>This was followed by the unmanned <a href="/wiki/Remotely_operated_underwater_vehicle" title="Remotely operated underwater vehicle">ROVs</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kaik%C5%8D" title="Kaikō">Kaikō</a></i> in 1996 and <a href="/wiki/Nereus_(underwater_vehicle)" title="Nereus (underwater vehicle)"><i>Nereus</i></a> in 2009. The first three expeditions directly measured very similar depths of 10,902 to 10,916 m (35,768 to 35,814 ft). The fourth was made by Canadian film director <a href="/wiki/James_Cameron" title="James Cameron">James Cameron</a> in 2012. On 26 March, he reached the bottom of the Mariana Trench in the submersible vessel <i><a href="/wiki/Deepsea_Challenger" title="Deepsea Challenger">Deepsea Challenger</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSNBC-20120325_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-MSNBC-20120325-25">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20120325_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20120325-26">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NGS-20120325_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-NGS-20120325-27">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 2015, members of the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration, Oregon State University, and the Coast Guard submerged a hydrophone into the deepest part of the Mariana Trench, the Challenger Deep, never having deployed one past a mile. The titanium-shelled hydrophone was designed to withstand the immense pressure 7 miles under.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup> Although researchers were unable to retrieve the hydrophone until November, the data capacity was full within the first 23 days. After months of analyzing the sounds, the experts were surprised to pick up natural <i>and</i> man-made sounds such as boats, earthquakes, a typhoon, and baleen whales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPR4Mar2016_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-NPR4Mar2016-29">[28]</a></sup> Due to the mission's success, the researchers announced plans to deploy a second hydrophone in 2017 for an extended period of time.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Planned_descents">Planned descents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariana_Trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Planned descents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Update" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="Ambox current red.svg" data-file-height="290" data-file-width="360" height="34" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/42px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/63px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/84px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png 2x" width="42"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section needs to be <b>updated</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please update this article to reflect recent events or newly available information.</span> <small><i>(May 2018)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>As of  February 2012<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mariana_Trench&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, at least two other teams are planning piloted submarines to reach the bottom of the Mariana Trench. Triton Submarines, a Florida-based company that designs and manufactures private submarines, plans for a crew of three to take 120 minutes to reach the seabed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/DOER_Marine" title="DOER Marine">DOER Marine</a>, a marine technology company based near <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> and set up in 1992, plans for a crew of two or three to take 90 minutes to reach the seabed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariana_Trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The expedition conducted in 1960 claimed to have observed, with great surprise because of the high pressure, large creatures living at the bottom, such as a <a href="/wiki/Flatfish" title="Flatfish">flatfish</a> about 30 cm (12 in) long,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_Man_at_the_Deepest_Depth_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_Man_at_the_Deepest_Depth-24">[23]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Shrimp" title="Shrimp">shrimp</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup> According to Piccard, "The bottom appeared light and clear, a waste of firm <a href="/wiki/Diatom" title="Diatom">diatomaceous</a> ooze".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_Man_at_the_Deepest_Depth_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_Man_at_the_Deepest_Depth-24">[23]</a></sup> Many marine biologists are now skeptical of the supposed sighting of the flatfish, and it is suggested that the creature may instead have been a <a href="/wiki/Sea_cucumber" title="Sea cucumber">sea cucumber</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup> During the second expedition, the unmanned vehicle Kaikō collected mud samples from the <a href="/wiki/Seabed" title="Seabed">seabed</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snw_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-snw-35">[34]</a></sup> Tiny organisms were found to be living in those samples.
</p><p>In July 2011, a research expedition deployed untethered landers, called dropcams, equipped with digital video cameras and lights to explore this region of the deep sea.
Amongst many other living organisms, some gigantic single-celled amoebas with a size of more than 10 cm (4 in), belonging to the class of <a href="/wiki/Xenophyophore" title="Xenophyophore">xenophyophores</a> were observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup> Xenophyophores are noteworthy for their size, their extreme abundance on the seafloor and their role as hosts for a variety of organisms.
</p><p>In December 2014, a new species of <a href="/wiki/Snailfish" title="Snailfish">snailfish</a> was discovered at a depth of 8,145 m (26,722 ft), breaking the previous record for the deepest living fish seen on video.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCDeept_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBCDeept-37">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the 2014 expedition, several new species were filmed including huge <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crustaceans" title="Crustaceans">crustaceans</a> known as supergiants. <a href="/wiki/Deep-sea_gigantism" title="Deep-sea gigantism">Deep-sea gigantism</a> is the process where species grow larger than their shallow water relatives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCDeept_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBCDeept-37">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2017, an unidentified type of snailfish was filmed at a depth of 8,178 metres (26,800 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pollution">Pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariana_Trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2016, a research expedition looked at the chemical makeup of crustacean scavengers collected from a range of 7,841–10,250 metres within the trench. Within these organisms, the researchers found extremely elevated concentrations of <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_biphenyl" title="Polychlorinated biphenyl">PCBs</a>, a chemical toxin banned for its environmental harm in the 1970s, concentrated at all depths within the sediment of the trench.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_nuclear_waste_disposal_site">Possible nuclear waste disposal site</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mariana_Trench&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Possible nuclear waste disposal site">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like other oceanic trenches, the Mariana Trench has been proposed as a site for <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_waste" title="Radioactive waste">nuclear waste</a> disposal,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-387-95560-7_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-387-95560-7-40">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-7546-7133-X_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-7546-7133-X-41">[40]</a></sup> in the hope that tectonic plate <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subduction</a> occurring at the site might eventually push the nuclear waste deep into the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a>, the second layer of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. However, ocean dumping of nuclear waste is prohibited by international law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-387-95560-7_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-387-95560-7-40">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-7546-7133-X_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-7546-7133-X-41">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup> Furthermore, plate subduction zones are associated with very large <a href="/wiki/Megathrust_earthquake" title="Megathrust earthquake">megathrust earthquakes</a>, the effects of which are unpredictable for the safety of long-term disposal of nuclear wastes within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hadopelagic" title="Hadopelagic">hadopelagic</a> <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-7546-7133-X_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-7546-7133-X-41">[40]</a></sup>
</p>

<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Marianas_Trench_Marine_National_Monument" title="Marianas Trench Marine National Monument">Marianas Trench Marine National Monument</a>, United States national monument at the trench. This National Monument protects 95,216 square miles (246,610 km<sup>2</sup>) of submerged lands and waters of the Mariana Archipelago. It includes some of the Mariana Trench, but not the deepest part, the <a href="/wiki/Challenger_Deep" title="Challenger Deep">Challenger Deep</a>, which lies just outside the monument area.</li></ul>
