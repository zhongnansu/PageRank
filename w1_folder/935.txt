<p><b>Potassium carbonate</b> (<a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">K<sub>2</sub></a><a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">CO<sub>3</sub></a>) is a white salt, which is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soluble" title="Soluble">soluble</a> in <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> (insoluble in <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and forms a strongly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkaline" title="Alkaline">alkaline</a> solution. It can be made as the product of <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">potassium hydroxide</a>'s absorbent reaction with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. It is <a href="/wiki/Hygroscopy#Deliquescence" title="Hygroscopy">deliquescent</a>, often appearing a damp or wet <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solid</a>. Potassium carbonate is used in the production of <a href="/wiki/Soap" title="Soap">soap</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potassium_carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Potassium carbonate is the primary component of <a href="/wiki/Potash" title="Potash">potash</a> and the more refined <b>pearl ash</b> or salts of tartar.  Historically, pearl ash was created by baking potash in a <a href="/wiki/Kiln" title="Kiln">kiln</a> to remove impurities.  The fine, white powder remaining was the pearl ash.  The first <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a> issued by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Patent_and_Trademark_Office" title="United States Patent and Trademark Office">US Patent Office</a> was awarded to <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Hopkins_(inventor)" title="Samuel Hopkins (inventor)">Samuel Hopkins</a>  in 1790 for an improved method of making potash and pearl ash.
</p><p>In late 18th century <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, before the development of <a href="/wiki/Baking_powder" title="Baking powder">baking powder</a>, pearl ash was used as a <a href="/wiki/Leavening_agent" title="Leavening agent">leavening agent</a> in <a href="/wiki/Quick_bread" title="Quick bread">quick breads</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potassium_carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Today, potassium carbonate is prepared commercially by the <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Potassium_chloride" title="Potassium chloride">potassium chloride</a>.  The resulting <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">potassium hydroxide</a> is then carbonated using <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> to form potassium carbonate, which is often used to produce other <a href="/wiki/Category:Potassium_compounds" title="Category:Potassium compounds">Potassium compounds</a>.
</p>
<dl><dd>2KOH + CO<sub>2</sub> → K<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Potassium_carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>(historically) for <a href="/wiki/Soap" title="Soap">soap</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dishware" title="Dishware">china</a> production</li>
<li>as a mild <a href="/wiki/Desiccant" title="Desiccant">drying agent</a> where other drying agents, such as <a href="/wiki/Calcium_chloride" title="Calcium chloride">calcium chloride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_sulfate" title="Magnesium sulfate">magnesium sulfate</a>, may be incompatible. It is not suitable for acidic compounds, but can be useful for drying an organic phase if one has a small amount of acidic impurity. It may also be used to dry some ketones, alcohols, and amines prior to distillation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>in cuisine, where it has many traditional uses. It is an ingredient in the production of <a href="/wiki/Grass_jelly" title="Grass jelly">grass jelly</a>, a food consumed in Chinese and Southeast Asian cuisines, as well as Chinese noodles and moon cake. It is used to tenderize <a href="/wiki/Tripe" title="Tripe">tripe</a>. German <a href="/wiki/Gingerbread" title="Gingerbread">gingerbread</a> recipes often use potassium carbonate as a baking agent, although in combination with <a href="/wiki/Hartshorn" title="Hartshorn">hartshorn</a>. It is however important that the right quantities are used to prevent harm, and cooks should not use it without guidance.</li>
<li>in the production of cocoa powder to balance the pH (i.e., reduce the acidity) of natural cocoa beans; it also enhances aroma. The process of adding potassium carbonate to cocoa powder is usually called "Dutching" (and the products referred to as Dutch-processed cocoa powder), as the process was first developed in 1828 by <a href="/wiki/Coenraad_Johannes_van_Houten" title="Coenraad Johannes van Houten">Coenrad Johannes van Houten</a>, a Dutchman.</li>
<li>as a <a href="/wiki/Buffering_agent" title="Buffering agent">buffering agent</a> in the production of <a href="/wiki/Mead" title="Mead">mead</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a>.</li>
<li>in antique documents, it is reported to have been used to soften <a href="/wiki/Hard_water" title="Hard water">hard water</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>as a fire suppressant in extinguishing deep-fat fryers and various other B class-related fires.</li>
<li>in <a href="/wiki/Condensed_aerosol_fire_suppression" title="Condensed aerosol fire suppression">condensed aerosol fire suppression</a>, although as the byproduct of potassium nitrate.</li>
<li>as an ingredient in welding fluxes, and in the flux coating on arc-welding rods.</li>
<li>as an animal feed ingredient to satisfy the potassium requirements of farmed animals such as broiler breeders.</li>
<li>as an acidity regulator in Swedish snus</li></ul>
