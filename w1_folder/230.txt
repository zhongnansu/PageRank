<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Metal carbon dioxide complex</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Metal carbon dioxide complexes</b> are coordination complexes that contain <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> <a href="/wiki/Ligand" title="Ligand">ligands</a>.  Aside from the fundamental interest in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coordination_chemistry" title="Coordination chemistry">coordination chemistry</a> of simple molecules, studies in this field are motivated by the possibility that transition metals might catalyze useful transformations of CO<sub>2</sub>.  This research is relevant both to <a href="/wiki/Organic_synthesis" title="Organic synthesis">organic synthesis</a> and to the production of "solar fuels" that would avoid the use of petroleum-based fuels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structural_trends">Structural trends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metal_carbon_dioxide_complex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structural trends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl><dd><div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:602px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MCO2structures.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="520" data-file-width="2977" height="105" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/MCO2structures.png/600px-MCO2structures.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/MCO2structures.png/900px-MCO2structures.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/MCO2structures.png/1200px-MCO2structures.png 2x" width="600"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MCO2structures.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illustrative structures of transition metal carbon dioxide complexes, from the left: Ni(η<sup>2</sup>-CO<sub>2</sub>)(PCy<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>, Rh(η<sup>1</sup>-CO<sub>2</sub>)ClL<sub>4</sub> (L<sub>4</sub> = (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diars" title="Diars">diars</a>)<sub>2</sub>), the <a href="/wiki/Metallacarboxylic_acid" title="Metallacarboxylic acid">metallacarboxylic ester</a> CpFe(CO)<sub>2</sub>(η<sup>1</sup>:η<sup>1</sup>-CO<sub>2</sub>)Re(CO)<sub>5</sub>, and another dimetalla-ester.</div></div></div></div></dd></dl>
<p>Carbon dioxide binds to metals in only a few ways. The bonding mode depends on the electrophilicity and basicity of the metal centre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  Most common is the η<sup>2</sup>-CO<sub>2</sub> coordination mode as illustrated by Aresta's complex, Ni(CO<sub>2</sub>)(<a href="/wiki/Tricyclohexylphosphine" title="Tricyclohexylphosphine">PCy<sub>3</sub></a>)<sub>2</sub>, which was the first reported complex  of CO<sub>2</sub><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aresta_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aresta-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>.  This <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Square-planar_compound" title="Square-planar compound">square-planar compound</a> is a derivative of Ni(II) with a reduced CO<sub>2</sub> ligand. In rare cases, CO<sub>2</sub> binds to metals as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_base" title="Lewis base">Lewis base</a> through its oxygen centres, but such adducts are weak and mainly of theoretical interest. A variety of multinuclear complexes are also known often involving Lewis basic and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_acid" title="Lewis acid">Lewis acidic</a> metals, e.g. metallacarboxylate salts (C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>2</sub>)Fe(CO)<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup>K<sup>+</sup>.  In multinuclear cases (compounds containing more than one metal), more complicated and more varied coordination geometries are observed.  One example is the unsymmetrical compound containing four <a href="/wiki/Rhenium" title="Rhenium">rhenium</a> centres, [(CO)<sub>5</sub>ReCO<sub>2</sub>Re(CO)<sub>4</sub>]<sub>2</sub>. Carbon dioxide can also bind to ligands on a metal complex (vs just the metal), e.g. by converting hydroxy ligands to carbonato ligands.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metal_carbon_dioxide_complex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">Transition metal</a> carbon dioxide complexes undergo a variety of reactions.  Metallacarboxylic acids protonate at oxygen and eventually convert to carbonyl complexes:
</p>
<dl><dd>[L<sub>n</sub>MCO<sub>2</sub>]<sup>−</sup>  +  2 H<sup>+</sup>   →   [L<sub>n</sub>MCO]<sup>+</sup>  +  H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>This reaction is relevant to the potential catalytic conversion of CO<sub>2</sub> to fuels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbonation_of_metal-carbon_bonds">Carbonation of metal-carbon bonds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metal_carbon_dioxide_complex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Carbonation of metal-carbon bonds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Insertion_into_Cu-C_bonds">Insertion into Cu-C bonds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metal_carbon_dioxide_complex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Insertion into Cu-C bonds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N-heterocyclic_carbene" title="N-heterocyclic carbene">N-heterocyclic carbene</a> (NHC) supported Cu<sup>I</sup> complexes catalyze carboxylation of organoboronic esters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>. The catalyst forms <i>in situ</i> from CuCl, an NHC ligand, and KO<sup>t</sup>Bu. Copper <i>tert</i>-butoxide can <a href="/wiki/Transmetalation" title="Transmetalation">transmetallate</a> with the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Organoboronic_ester&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Organoboronic ester (page does not exist)">organoboronic ester</a> to generate the Cu<sup>I</sup>-C bond, which intermediate can insert into CO<sub>2</sub> smoothly to get the respective carboxylate. Salt metathesis with KO<sup>t</sup>Bu releases product and regenerates catalyst (Scheme 2). 
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Scheme_2_Copper_catalyzed_carboxylation_of_organoboronic_ester.jpg" title="Scheme 2 Copper catalyzed carboxylation of organoboronic ester"><img alt="Scheme 2 Copper catalyzed carboxylation of organoboronic ester" data-file-height="494" data-file-width="615" height="494" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d4/Scheme_2_Copper_catalyzed_carboxylation_of_organoboronic_ester.jpg" width="615"/></a></div></div><p>Apart from transmetallation, there are other approaches forming Cu-C bond. C-H functionalization is a straightforward and atom economic method. Base can help deprotonate acidic C-H protons and form Cu-C bond.  [(<a href="/wiki/Phenanthroline" title="Phenanthroline">Phenanthroline</a>)Cu(PR<sub>3</sub>)] catalyst effect C-H carboxylation on terminal <a href="/wiki/Alkyne" title="Alkyne">alkynes</a> together with Cs<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> NHC-Cu-H species to deprotonate acidic proton to effect carboxylation of terminal alkynes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Cu-H species were generated from Cu-F and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Organosilanes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Organosilanes (page does not exist)">organosilanes</a>. The carboxylate product was trapped by silyl fluoride to get silyl ether.  For non-acidic C-H bonds, directed metalation with <sup>i</sup>Bu<sub>3</sub>Al(TMP)Li is adopted followed by transmetallation with copper to get Cu-C bond. <a href="/wiki/Allyl_group" title="Allyl group">Allylic</a> C-H bonds and <a href="/wiki/Phenyl_group" title="Phenyl group">phenyl</a> C-H bonds got carboxylated with this approach by Hou and co-workers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Scheme_3_Copper_catalysed_boracarboxylation_of_internal_alkynes.jpg" title="Scheme 3 Copper catalysed boracarboxylation of internal alkynes"><img alt="Scheme 3 Copper catalysed boracarboxylation of internal alkynes" data-file-height="511" data-file-width="655" height="511" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2d/Scheme_3_Copper_catalysed_boracarboxylation_of_internal_alkynes.jpg" width="655"/></a></div></div><p>Cabometallation to alkynes and <a href="/wiki/Allene" title="Allene">allenes</a> using <a href="/wiki/Organozinc_compound" title="Organozinc compound">organozinc</a> and <a href="/wiki/Organoaluminium_chemistry" title="Organoaluminium chemistry">organoaluminum</a> reagents followed by transmetallation to copper is also a strategy to initiate carboxylation. <a href="/wiki/Trimethylaluminium" title="Trimethylaluminium">Trimethylaluminu</a>m is able to insert into unbiased aliphatic internal alkynes with syn fashion directed by ether directing group. Vinyl copper complexes are formed by transmetallation and carboxylation is realized with a similar pathway giving tetrasubstituted aliphatic vinyl carboxylic acids<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>. In this case, regioslectivity is controlled by the favor of six-membered aluminum ring formation. Furthermore, carboxylation can be achieved on ynamides and allenamides using less reactive dimethyl zinc via similar approach<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>.
</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Insertion_in_Pd-C_bonds">Insertion in Pd-C bonds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metal_carbon_dioxide_complex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Insertion in Pd-C bonds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the presence of <a href="/wiki/Palladium(II)_acetate" title="Palladium(II) acetate">palladium acetate</a> under 1-30 bar of CO<sub>2</sub>, simple aromatic compounds convert to aromatic carboxylic acids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>. A PSiP-pincer ligand (<b>5</b>) promotes carboxylation of allene without using pre-functionalized substrates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>. Catalyst regeneration, Et<sub>3</sub>Al was added to do transmetallation with palladium. Catalyst is regenerated by the following <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=%CE%92-H_elimination&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Β-H elimination (page does not exist)">β-H elimination</a>. Apart from terminal allenes, some of internal allenes are also tolerated in this reaction, generating allyl carboxylic acid with the yield between 54% and 95%. This system was also applied to 1,3-diene, generating carboxylic acid in 1,2 addition fashion<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>. In 2015, Iwasawa <i>et al.</i> reported the <a href="/wiki/Germanium" title="Germanium">germanium</a> analogue (<b>6</b>) and combined CO<sub>2</sub> source together with hydride source to formate salts<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>.
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Scheme_4_Pincer_Pd_complexes_for_catalytic_carboxylation.jpg" title="Scheme 4 Pincer Pd complexes for catalytic carboxylation"><img alt="Scheme 4 Pincer Pd complexes for catalytic carboxylation" data-file-height="208" data-file-width="625" height="208" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5c/Scheme_4_Pincer_Pd_complexes_for_catalytic_carboxylation.jpg" width="625"/></a></div></div><p>Palladium has shown huge power to catalyze <a href="/wiki/Carbon%E2%80%93hydrogen_bond_activation" title="Carbon–hydrogen bond activation">C-H functionalization</a>. If the Pd-C intermediate in carboxylation reaction comes from C-H activation, such methodology must promote metal catalyzed carboxylation to a much higher level in utility. Iwasawa and co-workers reported direct carboxylation by styrenyl C-H activation generating coumarin derivatives<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>. Benzene rings with different electronic properties and some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heteroaromatic" title="Heteroaromatic">heteroaromatic</a> rings are tolerated in this reaction with yield from 50% to 90%. C-H activation was demonstrated by <a href="/wiki/Crystallography" title="Crystallography">crystallography</a> study.
</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Insertion_by_Rh-C_bonds">Insertion by Rh-C bonds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metal_carbon_dioxide_complex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Insertion by Rh-C bonds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Similar to Cu(I) chemistry mentioned above, Rh(I) complexes can also transmetallate with arylboronic esters to get aryl rhodium intermediates, to which CO<sub>2</sub> is inserted giving carboxylic acids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>. Later, Iwasawa <i>et al</i>. described C-H carboxylation strategy. Rh(I) undergoes oxidative addition to aryl C-H bond followed by transmetallation with alkyl aluminum species. Ar-Rh(I) regenerates by reductive elimination releasing methane. Ar-Rh(I) attacks CO<sub>2</sub> then transmetallates with aryl boronic acid to release the boronic acid of product, giving final carboxylic acid by hydrolysis. Directed and non-directed versions are both achieved<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>.
</p><p>Iwasawa and co-workers developed Rh(I) catalyzed carbonation reaction initiated by Rh-H insertion to vinylarenes. In order to regenerate reactive Rh-H after nucleophilic addition to CO<sub>2</sub>, <a href="/wiki/Photocatalysis" title="Photocatalysis">photocatalytic</a> <a href="/wiki/Proton-coupled_electron_transfer" title="Proton-coupled electron transfer">proton coupled electron transfer</a> approach was adopted<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>. In this system, excess amount of diethylpropylethylamine works as sacrificial electron donor (Scheme 5).
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Insertion_by_Ni-C_bond">Insertion by Ni-C bond</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metal_carbon_dioxide_complex&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Insertion by Ni-C bond">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Carboxylation of benzyl halides has been reported<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>.  The reaction mechanism is proposed to involve oxidative addition of benzyl chloride to Ni(0). The Ni(II) benzyl complex is reduced to Ni(I), e.g., by zinc, which inserts CO<sub>2</sub> delivering the nickel carboxylate. Reduction of the Ni(I) carboxylate to Ni(0) releases the zinc carboxylate (Scheme 6). Similarly, such carboxylation has been achieved on aryl and benzyl pivalate<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>, alkyl halides<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>, and allyl esters<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>.
</p>
