<p>A <b>fossil fuel power station</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">power station</a> which burns a <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>. Central station fossil fuel power plants are designed on a large scale for continuous operation. In many countries, such plants provide most of the <a href="/wiki/Electrical_energy" title="Electrical energy">electrical energy</a> used. Fossil fuel power stations have machinery to convert the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_energy" title="Heat energy">heat energy</a> of <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> into <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_energy" title="Mechanical energy">mechanical energy</a>, which then operates an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_generator" title="Electrical generator">electrical generator</a>. The <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/prime_mover" title="wikt:prime mover">prime mover</a> may be a <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine" title="Steam turbine">steam turbine</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbine</a> or, in small plants, a reciprocating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internal_combustion" title="Internal combustion">internal combustion</a> engine. All plants use the energy extracted from expanding gas, either steam or combustion gases. 
Although different energy conversion methods exist, all thermal power station conversion methods have efficiency limited by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carnot_efficiency" title="Carnot efficiency">Carnot efficiency</a> and therefore produce <a href="/wiki/Waste_heat" title="Waste heat">waste heat</a>.
</p><p>By-products of thermal power plant operation must be considered in their design and operation. Waste heat energy, which remains due to the finite efficiency, is released directly to the atmosphere or to a body of water, or indirectly to the atmosphere using a <a href="/wiki/Cooling_tower" title="Cooling tower">cooling tower</a> with river or lake water used as a cooling medium. 
The <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">flue gas</a> from combustion of the fossil fuels is discharged to the air. 
This gas contains <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and water vapor, as well as other substances such as <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxides</a> (NO<sub>x</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_oxide" title="Sulfur oxide">sulfur oxides</a> (SO<sub>x</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>, traces of other metals, and, for coal-fired plants, <a href="/wiki/Fly_ash" title="Fly ash">fly ash</a>. 
Solid waste ash from coal-fired boilers must also be removed. 
Some coal ash can be recycled for building materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCA_Manual_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-PCA_Manual-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fossil fueled power stations are major emitters of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>), a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> which is a major contributor to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. 
The results of a recent study<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> show that the <a href="/wiki/Net_income" title="Net income">net income</a> available to shareholders of large companies could see a significant reduction from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emissions" title="Greenhouse gas emissions">greenhouse gas emissions</a> liability related to only natural disasters in the United States from a single coal-fired power plant. 
However, as of 2015, no such cases have awarded damages in the United States. 
Per unit of electric energy, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brown_coal" title="Brown coal">brown coal</a> emits nearly two times as much CO<sub>2</sub> as natural gas, and black coal emits somewhat less than brown. 
<a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">Carbon capture and storage</a> of emissions has been proposed to limit the environmental impact of fossil fuel power stations, but it is still at a demonstration stage.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_concepts">Basic concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Basic concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a fossil fuel power plant the chemical energy stored in fossil fuels such as <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fuel_oil" title="Fuel oil">fuel oil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> or <a href="/wiki/Oil_shale" title="Oil shale">oil shale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a> is converted successively into <a href="/wiki/Thermal_energy" title="Thermal energy">thermal energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_energy" title="Mechanical energy">mechanical energy</a> and, finally, <a href="/wiki/Electrical_energy" title="Electrical energy">electrical energy</a>. Each fossil fuel power plant is a complex, custom-designed system. Construction costs, as of  2004<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, run to <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US$</a>1,300 per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilowatt" title="Kilowatt">kilowatt</a>, or $650 million for a 500 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MWe" title="MWe">MWe</a> unit.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Multiple generating units may be built at a single site for more efficient use of <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_(economics)" title="Labor (economics)">labor</a>. Most <a href="/wiki/Thermal_power_station" title="Thermal power station">thermal power stations</a> in the world use fossil fuel, outnumbering <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power" title="Geothermal power">geothermal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar thermal</a> plants.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heat_into_mechanical_energy">Heat into mechanical energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Heat into mechanical energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">second law of thermodynamics</a> states that any <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_cycle" title="Thermodynamic cycle">closed-loop cycle</a> can only convert a fraction of the heat produced during combustion into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanical_work" title="Mechanical work">mechanical work</a>. The rest of the heat, called <a href="/wiki/Waste_heat" title="Waste heat">waste heat</a>, must be released into a cooler environment during the return portion of the cycle. The fraction of heat released into a cooler medium must be equal or larger than the ratio of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absolute_temperature" title="Absolute temperature">absolute temperatures</a> of the cooling system (environment) and the heat source (combustion furnace). Raising the furnace temperature improves the efficiency but complicates the design, primarily by the selection of alloys used for construction, making the furnace more expensive. The waste heat cannot be converted into mechanical energy without an even cooler cooling system. However, it may be used in <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">cogeneration</a> plants to heat buildings, produce hot water, or to heat materials on an industrial scale, such as in some <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">oil refineries</a>, plants, and <a href="/wiki/Chemical_synthesis" title="Chemical synthesis">chemical synthesis</a> plants.
</p><p>Typical thermal efficiency for utility-scale electrical generators is around 37% for coal and oil-fired plants<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Who_Has_the_World's_Most_Efficient_Coal_Power_Plant_Fleet?_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Who_Has_the_World's_Most_Efficient_Coal_Power_Plant_Fleet?-4">[4]</a></sup>, and 56 – 60% (LEV) for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combined-cycle" title="Combined-cycle">combined-cycle</a> gas-fired plants. Plants designed to achieve peak efficiency while operating at capacity will be less efficient when operating off-design (i.e. temperatures too low.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ELECTRIC_GENERATION_EFFICIENCY_Page_5_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ELECTRIC_GENERATION_EFFICIENCY_Page_5-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Practical fossil fuels stations operating as heat engines cannot exceed the <a href="/wiki/Carnot_cycle" title="Carnot cycle">Carnot cycle</a> limit for conversion of heat energy into useful work. <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">Fuel cells</a> do not have the same thermodynamic limits as they are not heat engines.
</p><p><b>Temperature of Hot Steam</b>.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coal">Coal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Coal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coal_fired_power_plant_diagram.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="466" data-file-width="751" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Coal_fired_power_plant_diagram.svg/290px-Coal_fired_power_plant_diagram.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Coal_fired_power_plant_diagram.svg/435px-Coal_fired_power_plant_diagram.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Coal_fired_power_plant_diagram.svg/580px-Coal_fired_power_plant_diagram.svg.png 2x" width="290"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coal_fired_power_plant_diagram.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diagram of a typical steam-cycle coal power plant (proceeding from left to right)</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Thermal_power_station" title="Thermal power station">Thermal power station</a></div>
<p>Coal is the most abundant <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> on the planet, and widely used as the source of energy in <a href="/wiki/Thermal_power_station" title="Thermal power station">thermal power stations</a>. It is a relatively cheap fuel, with some of the largest deposits in regions that are stable politically, such as <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. This contrasts with <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, the largest deposits of which are located in Russia, Iran, Qatar, Turkmenistan and the US. Solid coal cannot directly replace natural gas or petroleum in most applications, petroleum is mostly used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transportation" title="Transportation">transportation</a> and the natural gas not used for <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">electricity generation</a> is used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_heating" title="Space heating">space</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_heating" title="Water heating">water</a> and industrial heating. Coal can be converted to gas or liquid fuel, but the efficiencies and economics of such processes can make them unfeasible.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Vehicles or heaters may require modification to use coal-derived fuels. Coal is an impure fuel and produces more <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> than an equivalent amount of petroleum or natural gas. For instance, the operation of a 1000-MWe coal-fired power plant results in a nuclear radiation dose of 490 person-rem/year, compared to 136 person-rem/year, for an equivalent nuclear power plant including uranium mining, reactor operation and waste disposal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of  2009<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> the largest coal-fired power station is <a href="/wiki/Taichung_Power_Plant" title="Taichung Power Plant">Taichung Power Plant</a> in <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>. The world's most energy-efficient coal-fired power plant is the <a href="/wiki/Aved%C3%B8re_Power_Station" title="Avedøre Power Station">Avedøre Power Station</a> in <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fuel_transport_and_delivery">Fuel transport and delivery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fuel transport and delivery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Castle_Gate_Power_Plant,_Utah_2007.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1439" data-file-width="2293" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Castle_Gate_Power_Plant%2C_Utah_2007.jpg/220px-Castle_Gate_Power_Plant%2C_Utah_2007.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Castle_Gate_Power_Plant%2C_Utah_2007.jpg/330px-Castle_Gate_Power_Plant%2C_Utah_2007.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Castle_Gate_Power_Plant%2C_Utah_2007.jpg/440px-Castle_Gate_Power_Plant%2C_Utah_2007.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Castle_Gate_Power_Plant,_Utah_2007.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Coal-fired power plants provide about 32 percent of consumed electricity in the United States, As of September 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This is the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_Power_Plant" title="Carbon Power Plant">Castle Gate Plant</a> near <a href="/wiki/Helper,_Utah" title="Helper, Utah">Helper, Utah</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Coal is delivered by highway <a href="/wiki/Truck" title="Truck">truck</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railroad" title="Railroad">rail</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barge" title="Barge">barge</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collier_(ship_type)" title="Collier (ship type)">collier</a> ship or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_slurry_pipeline" title="Coal slurry pipeline">coal slurry pipeline</a>. Some plants are even built near coal mines and coal is delivered by conveyors. A large coal <a href="/wiki/Train" title="Train">train</a> called a "unit train" may be 2 kilometres (1.2 mi) long, containing 130-140 cars with around 100 tonnes (98 long tons; 110 short tons) of coal in each one, for a total load of over 10,000 tonnes (9,800 long tons; 11,000 short tons). A large plant under full load requires at least one coal delivery this size every day. Plants may get as many as three to five trains a day, especially in "peak season" during the hottest summer or coldest winter months (depending on local climate) when power consumption is high. A large thermal power plant such as the now decommissioned <a href="/wiki/Nanticoke_Generating_Station" title="Nanticoke Generating Station">Nanticoke</a>, Ontario stores several million metric tons of coal for winter use when the lakes are frozen.
</p><p>Modern unloaders use rotary dump devices, which eliminate problems with coal freezing in bottom dump cars. The unloader includes a train positioner arm that pulls the entire train to position each car over a coal hopper. The dumper clamps an individual car against a platform that swivels the car upside down to dump the coal. Swiveling couplers enable the entire operation to occur while the cars are still coupled together. Unloading a unit train takes about three hours.
</p><p>Shorter trains may use railcars with an "air-dump", which relies on air pressure from the engine plus a "hot shoe" on each car. This "hot shoe" when it comes into contact with a "hot rail" at the unloading trestle, shoots an electric charge through the air dump apparatus and causes the doors on the bottom of the car to open, dumping the coal through the opening in the trestle. Unloading one of these trains takes anywhere from an hour to an hour and a half. Older unloaders may still use manually operated bottom-dump rail cars and a "shaker" attached to dump the coal. Generating stations adjacent to a mine may receive coal by <a href="/wiki/Conveyor_belt" title="Conveyor belt">conveyor belt</a> or massive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diesel-electric" title="Diesel-electric">diesel-electric</a>-drive <a href="/wiki/Haul_truck" title="Haul truck">trucks</a>.
</p><p>A collier (cargo ship carrying coal) may hold 40,000 long tons of coal and takes several days to unload. Some colliers carry their own conveying equipment to unload their own bunkers; others depend on equipment at the plant. For transporting coal in calmer waters, such as rivers and lakes, flat-bottomed <a href="/wiki/Barge" title="Barge">barges</a> are often used. Barges are usually unpowered and must be moved by <a href="/wiki/Tugboat" title="Tugboat">tugboats</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Towboat" title="Towboat">towboats</a>.
</p><p>For start up or auxiliary purposes, the plant may use fuel oil as well. Fuel oil can be delivered to plants by <a href="/wiki/Pipeline_transport" title="Pipeline transport">pipeline</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tanker_(ship)" title="Tanker (ship)">tanker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tank_car" title="Tank car">tank car</a> or truck. Oil is stored in vertical cylindrical steel tanks with capacities as high as 90,000 barrels (14,000 m<sup>3</sup>)' worth. The <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">heavier</a> no. 5 "bunker" and no. 6 fuels are typically steam-heated before pumping in cold climates.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fuel_processing">Fuel processing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fuel processing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Coal is prepared for use by crushing the rough coal to pieces less than 2 inches (5 cm) in size. The coal is then transported from the storage yard to in-plant storage silos by <a href="/wiki/Conveyor_belt" title="Conveyor belt">conveyor belts</a> at rates up to 4,000 short tons per hour.
</p><p>In plants that burn pulverized coal, silos feed coal to <a href="/wiki/Pulverizer" title="Pulverizer">pulverizers</a> (coal mills) that take the larger 2-inch (51 mm) pieces, grind them to the consistency of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Talcum_powder" title="Talcum powder">talcum powder</a>, sort them, and mix them with primary combustion air which transports the coal to the boiler furnace and preheats the coal in order to drive off excess moisture content. A 500 MWe plant may have six such pulverizers, five of which can supply coal to the furnace at 250 tons per hour under full load.
</p><p>In plants that do not burn pulverized coal, the larger 2-inch (51 mm) pieces may be directly fed into the silos which then feed either mechanical distributors that drop the coal on a traveling grate or the <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_furnace" title="Cyclone furnace">cyclone</a> burners, a specific kind of combustor that can efficiently burn larger pieces of fuel.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Combined_heat_and_power">Combined heat and power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Combined heat and power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combined_heat_and_power" title="Combined heat and power">Combined heat and power</a> (CHP), also known as <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">cogeneration</a>, is the use of a <a href="/wiki/Thermal_power_station" title="Thermal power station">thermal power station</a> to provide both electric power and heat (the latter being used, for example, for <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">district heating</a> purposes). This technology is practiced not only for domestic heating (low temperature) but also for industrial process heat, which is often high temperature heat. Calculations show that Combined Heat and Power District Heating (CHPDH) is the cheapest method in reducing (but not eliminating) carbon emissions, if conventional fossil fuels remain to be burned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_turbine_plants">Gas turbine plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Gas turbine plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GE_H_series_Gas_Turbine.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="367" data-file-width="500" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/GE_H_series_Gas_Turbine.jpg/220px-GE_H_series_Gas_Turbine.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/GE_H_series_Gas_Turbine.jpg/330px-GE_H_series_Gas_Turbine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/GE_H_series_Gas_Turbine.jpg/440px-GE_H_series_Gas_Turbine.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GE_H_series_Gas_Turbine.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>480 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">megawatt</a> GE H series power generation gas turbine</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Currant_Creek_Power_Plant.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Currant_Creek_Power_Plant.jpg/220px-Currant_Creek_Power_Plant.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Currant_Creek_Power_Plant.jpg/330px-Currant_Creek_Power_Plant.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Currant_Creek_Power_Plant.jpg/440px-Currant_Creek_Power_Plant.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Currant_Creek_Power_Plant.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Currant Creek Power Plant near <a href="/wiki/Mona,_Utah" title="Mona, Utah">Mona, Utah</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> fired electrical plant.</div></div></div>
<p>One type of fossil fuel power plant uses a <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbine</a> in conjunction with a <a href="/wiki/Heat_recovery_steam_generator" title="Heat recovery steam generator">heat recovery steam generator</a> (HRSG). It is referred to as a <a href="/wiki/Combined_cycle" title="Combined cycle">combined cycle</a> power plant because it combines the <a href="/wiki/Brayton_cycle" title="Brayton cycle">Brayton cycle</a> of the gas turbine with the <a href="/wiki/Rankine_cycle" title="Rankine cycle">Rankine cycle</a> of the HRSG. The thermal efficiency of these plants has reached a record <a href="/wiki/Thermal_efficiency" title="Thermal efficiency">heat rate</a> of 5690 Btu/(kW·h), or just under 60%, at a facility in Baglan Bay, Wales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The turbines are fueled either with natural gas, <a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">syngas</a> or fuel oil. While more efficient and faster to construct (a 1,000 MW plant may be completed in as little as 18 months from start of construction), the economics of such plants is heavily influenced by the volatile cost of fuel, normally natural gas. The combined cycle plants are designed in a variety of configurations composed of the number of gas turbines followed by the steam turbine. For example, a 3-1 combined cycle facility has three gas turbines tied to one steam turbine. The configurations range from (1-1), (2-1), (3-1), (4-1), (5-1), to (6-1)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Simple-cycle or open cycle gas turbine plants, without a steam cycle, are sometimes installed as emergency or <a href="/wiki/Peaking_power_plant" title="Peaking power plant">peaking</a> capacity; their thermal efficiency is much lower. The high running cost per hour is offset by the low capital cost and the intention to run such units only a few hundred hours per year. Other gas turbine plants are installed in stages, with an open cycle gas turbine the first stage and additional turbines or conversion to a closed cycle part of future project plans.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dash_for_gas">Dash for gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Dash for gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 1990s was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dash_for_gas" title="Dash for gas">dash for gas</a> where 30 gas-fired power stations were built in Britain due to plentiful gas supplies from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Sea_Gas" title="North Sea Gas">North Sea oil wells</a>. According to the 2012 forecast by the U.S. Energy Information Administration, 27 gigawatts of capacity from coal-fired generators is to be retired from 175 US coal-fired power plants before 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Natural gas showed a corresponding jump, increasing by a third over 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epm_312_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-epm_312-12">[12]</a></sup> Some <a href="/wiki/Thermal_power_station" title="Thermal power station">coal power plants</a> such as the 1200 MW <a href="/wiki/Hearn_Generating_Station" title="Hearn Generating Station">Hearn Generating Station</a> have stopped burning coal by switching the plant to natural gas. Coal's share of electricity generation dropped to just over 36%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epm_312_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-epm_312-12">[12]</a></sup> Natural gas accounted for 81% of new power generation in the US between 2000 and 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>  Coal-fired generation puts out about twice the amount of carbon dioxide - around 2,000 pounds for every megawatt hour generated - than electricity generated by burning natural gas at 1,100 pounds of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> per megawatt hour. As the fuel mix in the United States has changed to reduce coal and increase natural gas generation, carbon dioxide emissions have unexpectedly fallen. Carbon dioxide measured in the first quarter of 2012 was the lowest recorded of any year since 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/List_of_natural_gas_power_stations" title="List of natural gas power stations">list of natural gas power stations</a> has over 100 power stations that generate between 100 MW and 5,600 MW of electricity. Natural gas plants are increasing in popularity and in 2014 generated 22% of the worlds total electricity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reciprocating_engines">Reciprocating engines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Reciprocating engines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">Diesel engine</a> generator sets are often used for prime power in communities not connected to a widespread power grid. Emergency (standby) power systems may use reciprocating internal combustion engines operated by fuel oil or natural gas. Standby generators may serve as emergency power for a factory or data center, or may also be operated in parallel with the local utility system to reduce peak power demand charge from the utility. Diesel engines can produce strong torque at relatively low rotational speeds, which is generally desirable when driving an <a href="/wiki/Alternator" title="Alternator">alternator</a>, but diesel fuel in long-term storage can be subject to problems resulting from water accumulation and <a href="/wiki/Chemical_decomposition" title="Chemical decomposition">chemical decomposition</a>. Rarely used generator sets may correspondingly be installed as natural gas or LPG to minimize the fuel system maintenance requirements.
</p><p>Spark-ignition internal combustion engines operating on gasoline (petrol), <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_petroleum_gas" title="Liquefied petroleum gas">LPG</a> are commonly used as portable temporary power sources for construction work, emergency power, or recreational uses.
</p><p>Reciprocating external combustion engines such as the <a href="/wiki/Stirling_engine" title="Stirling engine">Stirling engine</a> can be run on a variety of fossil fuels, as well as renewable fuels or industrial waste heat. Installations of Stirling engines for power production are relatively uncommon.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impacts">Environmental impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Environmental impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mohave_Generating_Station_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1474" data-file-width="2003" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Mohave_Generating_Station_1.jpg/220px-Mohave_Generating_Station_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Mohave_Generating_Station_1.jpg/330px-Mohave_Generating_Station_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Mohave_Generating_Station_1.jpg/440px-Mohave_Generating_Station_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mohave_Generating_Station_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Mohave_Power_Station" title="Mohave Power Station">Mohave Power Station</a>, a 1,580 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">MW</a> coal power station near <a href="/wiki/Laughlin,_Nevada" title="Laughlin, Nevada">Laughlin, Nevada</a>, out of service since 2005 due to environmental restrictions<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Thermal power plants are one of the main artificial sources of producing toxic gases and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particulate_matter" title="Particulate matter">particulate matter</a>. Fossil fuel power plants cause the emission of pollutants such as NOx, SOx, CO2, CO, PM, organic gases and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FouladiFard05_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FouladiFard05-17">[17]</a></sup> World organizations and international agencies, like the IEA, are concerned about the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_fossil_fuels" title="Environmental impact of fossil fuels">environmental impact of burning fossil fuels</a>, and coal in particular. The combustion of coal contributes the most to <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a>, and has been connected with <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. Due to the chemical composition of coal there are difficulties in removing impurities from the solid fuel prior to its combustion. Modern day coal power plants pollute less than older designs due to new "<a href="/wiki/Scrubber" title="Scrubber">scrubber</a>" technologies that filter the exhaust air in smoke stacks; however emission levels of various pollutants are still on average several times greater than natural gas power plants. In these modern designs, pollution from coal-fired power plants comes from the emission of gases such as carbon dioxide, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxides" title="Nitrogen oxides">nitrogen oxides</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> into the air.
</p><p>Acid rain is caused by the emission of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxides" title="Nitrogen oxides">nitrogen oxides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a>. These gases may be only mildly acidic themselves, yet when they react with the atmosphere, they create acidic compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Sulfurous_acid" title="Sulfurous acid">sulfurous acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> which fall as rain, hence the term acid rain. In Europe and the U.S.A., stricter emission laws and decline in heavy industries have reduced the environmental hazards associated with this problem, leading to lower emissions after their peak in 1960s.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_dioxide">Carbon dioxide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Carbon dioxide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">Carbon dioxide</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Taichung_Thermal_Power_Plant.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1173" data-file-width="1564" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Taichung_Thermal_Power_Plant.JPG/220px-Taichung_Thermal_Power_Plant.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Taichung_Thermal_Power_Plant.JPG/330px-Taichung_Thermal_Power_Plant.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Taichung_Thermal_Power_Plant.JPG/440px-Taichung_Thermal_Power_Plant.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Taichung_Thermal_Power_Plant.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Taichung_Power_Plant" title="Taichung Power Plant">Taichung coal-fired power plant</a> in <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, the world's largest carbon dioxide emitter<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Electricity generation using carbon-based fuels is responsible for a large fraction of carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>) emissions worldwide and for 34% of U.S. man-made carbon dioxide emissions in 2010. In the U.S. 70% of electricity is generated by combustion of fossil fuels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Coal contains more carbon than oil or natural gas fossil fuels, resulting in greater volumes of carbon dioxide emissions per unit of electricity generated. In 2010, coal contributed about 81% of CO<sub>2</sub> emissions from generation and contributed about 45% of the electricity generated in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>  In 2000, the carbon intensity (CO<sub>2</sub> emissions) of U.S. coal thermal combustion was 2249 lbs/MWh (1,029 kg/MWh)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> while the carbon intensity of U.S. oil thermal generation was 1672 lb/MWh (758 kg/MWh or 211 kg/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigajoule" title="Gigajoule">GJ</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and the carbon intensity of U.S. natural gas thermal production was 1135 lb/MWh (515 kg/MWh or 143 kg/GJ).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPCC" title="IPCC">IPCC</a>) reports that increased quantities of the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> carbon dioxide within the atmosphere will "very likely" lead to higher average temperatures on a global scale (<a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>). Concerns regarding the potential for such warming to change the global climate prompted IPCC recommendations calling for large cuts to CO<sub>2</sub> emissions worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc_summary_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc_summary-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Emissions can be reduced with higher combustion temperatures, yielding more efficient production of electricity within the cycle. <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">Carbon capture and storage</a> (CCS) of emissions from coal-fired power stations is another alternative but the technology is still being developed and will increase the cost of fossil fuel-based production of electricity. CCS may not be economically viable, unless the price of emitting CO<sub>2</sub> to the atmosphere rises.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Particulate_matter">Particulate matter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Particulate matter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another problem related to coal combustion is the emission of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">particulates</a> that have a serious impact on public health. Power plants remove particulate from the flue gas with the use of a <a href="/wiki/Dust_collector#Fabric_filters" title="Dust collector">bag house</a> or <a href="/wiki/Electrostatic_precipitator" title="Electrostatic precipitator">electrostatic precipitator</a>. Several newer plants that burn coal use a different process, <a href="/wiki/Combined_cycle#Integrated_Gasification_Combined_Cycle_(IGCC)" title="Combined cycle">Integrated Gasification Combined Cycle</a> in which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthesis_gas" title="Synthesis gas">synthesis gas</a> is made out of a reaction between coal and water. The synthesis gas is processed to remove most pollutants and then used initially to power gas turbines. Then the hot exhaust gases from the gas turbines are used to generate steam to power a steam turbine. The pollution levels of such plants are drastically lower than those of "classic" coal power plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Particulate matter from coal-fired plants can be harmful and have negative health impacts. Studies have shown that exposure to particulate matter is related to an increase of respiratory and cardiac mortality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nel,_A._2005_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nel,_A._2005-29">[29]</a></sup> Particulate matter can irritate small airways in the lungs, which can lead to increased problems with asthma, chronic bronchitis, airway obstruction, and gas exchange.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nel,_A._2005_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nel,_A._2005-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are different types of particulate matter, depending on the chemical composition and size. The dominant form of particulate matter from coal-fired plants is <a href="/wiki/Fly_ash" title="Fly ash">coal fly ash</a>, but secondary sulfate and nitrate also comprise a major portion of the particulate matter from coal-fired plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grahame,_T._2007_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grahame,_T._2007-30">[30]</a></sup> Coal fly ash is what remains after the coal has been combusted, so it consists of the incombustible materials that are found in the coal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schobert,_H._H._2002_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schobert,_H._H._2002-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>The size and chemical composition of these particles affects the impacts on human health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nel,_A._2005_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nel,_A._2005-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grahame,_T._2007_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Grahame,_T._2007-30">[30]</a></sup> Currently coarse (diameter greater than 2.5 μm) and fine (diameter between 0.1 μm and 2.5 μm) particles are regulated, but ultrafine particles (diameter less than 0.1 μm) are currently unregulated, yet they pose many dangers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nel,_A._2005_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-Nel,_A._2005-29">[29]</a></sup> Unfortunately much is still unknown as to which kinds of particulate matter pose the most harm, which makes it difficult to come up with adequate legislation for regulating particulate matter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grahame,_T._2007_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-Grahame,_T._2007-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are several methods of helping to reduce the particulate matter emissions from coal-fired plants. Roughly 80% of the ash falls into an ash hopper, but the rest of the ash then gets carried into the atmosphere to become coal-fly ash.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schobert,_H._H._2002_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schobert,_H._H._2002-31">[31]</a></sup> Methods of reducing these emissions of particulate matter include:
</p>
<ol><li>a <a href="/wiki/Baghouse#Fabric_filters" title="Baghouse">baghouse</a></li>
<li>an <a href="/wiki/Electrostatic_precipitator" title="Electrostatic precipitator">electrostatic precipitator</a> (ESP)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Combined_cycle#Integrated_Gasification_Combined_Cycle_(IGCC)" title="Combined cycle">cyclone collector</a></li></ol>
<p>The baghouse has a fine filter that collects the ash particles, electrostatic precipitators use an electric field to trap ash particles on high-voltage plates, and cyclone collectors use centrifugal force to trap particles to the walls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schobert,_H._H._2002_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-Schobert,_H._H._2002-31">[31]</a></sup> A recent study indicates that sulfur emissions from fossil fueled power stations in China may have caused a 10-year lull in global warming (1998-2008)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radioactive_trace_elements">Radioactive trace elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Radioactive trace elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Coal is a sedimentary rock formed primarily from accumulated plant matter, and it includes many inorganic minerals and elements which were deposited along with organic material during its formation. As the rest of the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a>, coal also contains low levels of <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a>, and other naturally occurring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive_isotopes" title="Radioactive isotopes">radioactive isotopes</a> whose release into the environment leads to <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_contamination" title="Radioactive contamination">radioactive contamination</a>. While these substances are present as very small trace impurities, enough coal is burned that significant amounts of these substances are released. A 1,000 MW coal-burning power plant could have an uncontrolled release of as much as 5.2 metric tons per year of uranium (containing 74 pounds (34 kg) of <a href="/wiki/Uranium-235" title="Uranium-235">uranium-235</a>) and 12.8 metric tons per year of thorium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ORNL_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-ORNL-33">[33]</a></sup> In comparison, a 1,000 MW nuclear plant will generate about 30 metric tons of high-level radioactive solid packed waste per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> It is estimated that during 1982, US coal burning released 155 times as much uncontrolled radioactivity into the atmosphere as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three_Mile_Island_incident" title="Three Mile Island incident">Three Mile Island incident</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The collective radioactivity resulting from all coal burning worldwide between 1937 and 2040 is estimated to be 2,700,000 curies or 0.101 EBq.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ORNL_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-ORNL-33">[33]</a></sup> During normal operation, the effective dose equivalent from coal plants is 100 times that from nuclear plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ORNL_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-ORNL-33">[33]</a></sup> Normal operation however, is a deceiving baseline for comparison: just the <a href="/wiki/Chernobyl_disaster" title="Chernobyl disaster">Chernobyl nuclear disaster</a> released, in iodine-131 alone, an estimated 1.76 EBq.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newscientist1_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-newscientist1-36">[36]</a></sup> of radioactivity, a value one order of magnitude above this value for total emissions from all coal burned within a century, while the iodine-131, the major radioactive substance which comes out in accident situations, has a half life of just 8 days.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_and_air_contamination_by_coal_ash">Water and air contamination by coal ash</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Water and air contamination by coal ash">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A study released in August 2010 that examined state pollution data in the United States by the organizations <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Integrity_Project" title="Environmental Integrity Project">Environmental Integrity Project</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club" title="Sierra Club">Sierra Club</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earthjustice" title="Earthjustice">Earthjustice</a> found that coal ash produced by coal-fired power plants dumped at sites across 21 U.S. states has contaminated ground water with toxic elements. The contaminants including the poisons <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcclatchydc.com_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcclatchydc.com-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Arsenic has been shown to cause <a href="/wiki/Skin_cancer" title="Skin cancer">skin cancer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bladder_cancer" title="Bladder cancer">bladder cancer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lung_cancer" title="Lung cancer">lung cancer</a>, and lead damages the <a href="/wiki/Nervous_system" title="Nervous system">nervous system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EJ2010_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-EJ2010-38">[38]</a></sup> Coal ash contaminants are also linked to respiratory diseases and other health and developmental problems, and have disrupted local aquatic life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcclatchydc.com_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-mcclatchydc.com-37">[37]</a></sup>  Coal ash also releases a variety of toxic contaminants into nearby air, posing a health threat to those who breath in fugitive coal dust.
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EJ2010_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-EJ2010-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>Currently, the EPA does not regulate the disposal of coal ash; regulation is up to the states and the electric power industry has been lobbying to maintain this status quo. Most states require no monitoring of drinking water near coal ash dump sites. The study found an additional 39 contaminated U.S. sites and concluded that the problem of coal ash-caused water contamination is even more extensive in the United States than has been estimated. The study brought to 137 the number of ground water sites across the United States that are contaminated by power plant-produced coal ash.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcclatchydc.com_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-mcclatchydc.com-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mercury_contamination">Mercury contamination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Mercury contamination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">Mercury (element)</a></div>
<p>U.S. government scientists tested fish in 291 streams around the country for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercury_contamination" title="Mercury contamination">mercury contamination</a>. They found mercury in every fish tested, according to the study by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_the_Interior" title="U.S. Department of the Interior">U.S. Department of the Interior</a>. They found mercury even in fish of isolated rural waterways. Twenty five percent of the fish tested had mercury levels above the safety levels determined by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="U.S. Environmental Protection Agency">U.S. Environmental Protection Agency</a> for people who eat the fish regularly. The largest source of mercury contamination in the United States is coal-fueled power plant emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Greening_of_fossil_fuel_power_plants">Greening of fossil fuel power plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Greening of fossil fuel power plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Coal_pollution_mitigation" title="Coal pollution mitigation">Coal pollution mitigation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Landfill#Reclaiming_materials" title="Landfill">Landfill § Reclaiming materials</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero_Emission_Fossil_Fuel_Power_Plants" title="Zero Emission Fossil Fuel Power Plants">Zero Emission Fossil Fuel Power Plants</a></div>
<p>Several methods exist to improve the efficiency of fossil fuel power plants. A frequently used and cost-efficient method is to convert a plant to run on a different fuel. This includes conversions of coal power plants to biomass or waste<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> and conversions of natural gas power plants to biogas. Conversions of coal powered power plants to waste-fired power plants have an extra benefit in that they can reduce <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfilling</a>. In addition, waste-fired power plants can be equipped with material recovery, which is also beneficial to the environment. In some instances, <a href="/wiki/Torrefaction" title="Torrefaction">torrefaction</a> of biomass may be needed if biomass is the material the converted fossil fuel power plant will be using.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>Improving energy efficiency of a coal-fired power plant also reduces emissions. For example, emissions can be reduced by upgrading existing plants or building new high-efficiency, low-emissions plants. Such plants emit almost 20% less CO<sub>2</sub> than a subcritical unit operating at a similar load. Over the longer term, HELE plants can further facilitate emission reductions because coal-fired plants operating at the highest efficiencies are also the most appropriate option for <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a> retrofit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>Regardless of the conversion, a truly low-carbon fossil fuel power plant implements carbon capture and storage, which means that the exhaust CO<sub>2</sub> is not released into the environment and the fossil fuel power plant becomes an <a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20090705094616/http://www.zero-emissionplatform.eu/website/library/" rel="nofollow">emissionless power plant</a>. A 2006 example of a carbon capture and storage fossil fuel power plant is the pilot Elsam power station near Esbjerg, Denmark.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coal_Pollution_Mitigation">Coal Pollution Mitigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Coal Pollution Mitigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Coal_pollution_mitigation" title="Coal pollution mitigation">Coal Pollution Mitigation</a> is a process whereby coal is chemically washed of <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a> and impurities, sometimes <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">gasified</a>, burned and the resulting flue gases treated with steam, with the purpose of removing sulfur dioxide, and reburned so as to make the carbon dioxide in the flue gas economically recoverable, and storable underground (the latter of which is called "carbon capture and storage"). The coal industry uses the term "clean coal" to describe technologies designed to enhance both the efficiency and the environmental acceptability of coal extraction, preparation and use,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> but has provided no specific quantitative limits on any emissions, particularly carbon dioxide. Whereas contaminants like sulfur or mercury can be removed from coal, carbon cannot be effectively removed while still leaving a usable fuel, and clean coal plants without carbon sequestration and storage do not significantly reduce carbon dioxide emissions. <a href="/wiki/James_Hansen" title="James Hansen">James Hansen</a> in an open letter to U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> has advocated a "moratorium and phase-out of coal plants that do not capture and store CO<sub>2</sub>". In his book <i><a href="/wiki/Storms_of_My_Grandchildren" title="Storms of My Grandchildren">Storms of My Grandchildren</a></i>, similarly, Hansen discusses his <i>Declaration of Stewardship</i> the first principle of which requires "a moratorium on coal-fired power plants that do not capture and sequester carbon dioxide".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Clean_gas">Clean gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Clean gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gas-fired power plants can also be modified to run on <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, the latter of which can be created on-site from natural gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Since 2013, the conversion process has been improved by scientists at Karlsruhe Liquid-metal Laboratory (KALLA) as they succeeded in allowing the soot to be easily removed (soot is a byproduct of the process and damaged the working parts in the past -most notably the nickel-iron-cobaltcatalyst-).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The soot (which contains the carbon) can then be stored underground and is not released into the atmosphere.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternatives_to_fossil_fuel_power_plants">Alternatives to fossil fuel power plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Alternatives to fossil fuel power plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:U.S._2014_Electricity_Generation_By_Type.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="580" data-file-width="823" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/U.S._2014_Electricity_Generation_By_Type.png/220px-U.S._2014_Electricity_Generation_By_Type.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/U.S._2014_Electricity_Generation_By_Type.png/330px-U.S._2014_Electricity_Generation_By_Type.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/U.S._2014_Electricity_Generation_By_Type.png/440px-U.S._2014_Electricity_Generation_By_Type.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:U.S._2014_Electricity_Generation_By_Type.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>U.S. 2014 Electricity Generation By Type.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Alternatives to fossil fuel power plants include <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power" title="Geothermal power">geothermal power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tidal_power" title="Tidal power">tidal power</a>, hydroelectric power (<a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectricity</a>), <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass power plants</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energies</a> (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-carbon_economy" title="Non-carbon economy">non-carbon economy</a>). Some of these are proven technologies on an industrial scale (i.e. nuclear, wind, tidal, hydroelectric and biomass fired power) others are still in prototype form.
</p><p>Nuclear power, and geothermal power may be classed as heat pollutants as they add heat energy to the biosphere that would not otherwise be released.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The net quantity of energy conversion within the biosphere due to the utilisation of wind power, solar power, tidal power, hydroelectric power (hydroelectricity) is static and is derived from the effects of sunlight and the movement of the moon and planets.
</p><p>Generally, the cost of electrical energy produced by non fossil fuel burning power plants is greater than that produced by burning fossil fuels. This statement however only includes the cost to produce the electrical energy and does not take into account indirect costs associated with the many pollutants created by burning fossil fuels (e.g. increased hospital admissions due to respiratory diseases caused by fine smoke particles).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relative_cost_by_generation_source">Relative cost by generation source</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fossil_fuel_power_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Relative cost by generation source">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relative_cost_of_electricity_generated_by_different_sources" title="Relative cost of electricity generated by different sources">Relative cost of electricity generated by different sources</a></div>
<p>When comparing power plant costs, it is customary to start by calculating the cost of power at the generator terminals by considering several main factors. External costs such as connections costs, the effect of each plant on the distribution grid are considered separately as an additional cost to the calculated power cost at the terminals.
</p><p>Initial factors considered are:
</p>
<ul><li>Capital costs, including waste disposal and decommissioning costs for nuclear energy.</li>
<li>Operating and maintenance costs.</li>
<li>Fuel costs for fossil fuel and biomass sources, and which may be negative for wastes.</li>
<li>Likely annual hours per year run or load factor, which may be as low as 30% for wind energy, or as high as 90% for nuclear energy.</li>
<li>Offset sales of heat, for example in combined heat and power district heating (CHP/DH).</li></ul>
<p>These costs occur over the 30–50 year life of the fossil fuel power plants, using <a href="/wiki/Discounted_cash_flow" title="Discounted cash flow">discounted cash flows</a>. In general large fossil plants are attractive due to their low initial capital costs—typically around £750–£1000 per kilowatt electrical compared to perhaps £1500 per kilowatt for onshore wind.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
