<p><b>Photovoltaic solar panels</b> absorb <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> as a source of energy to generate <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" title="Photovoltaic">photovoltaic</a> (PV) module is a packaged, connected assembly of typically 6x10 photovoltaic <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">solar cells</a>. Photovoltaic modules constitute the photovoltaic array of a <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_system" title="Photovoltaic system">photovoltaic system</a> that generates and supplies <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_electricity" title="Solar electricity">solar electricity</a> in commercial and residential applications.  
</p><p>Each module is rated by its <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">DC</a> output power under standard test conditions (STC), and typically ranges from 100 to 365 <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">Watts (W)</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell_efficiency" title="Solar cell efficiency">efficiency</a> of a module determines the area of a module given the same rated output –  an 8% efficient 230 W module will have twice the area of a 16% efficient 230 W module. There are a few commercially available solar modules that exceed efficiency of 24%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>A single solar module can produce only a limited amount of power; most installations contain multiple modules. A photovoltaic system typically includes an array of photovoltaic modules, an <a href="/wiki/Power_inverter" title="Power inverter">inverter</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">battery pack</a> for storage, interconnection wiring, and optionally a <a href="/wiki/Solar_tracker" title="Solar tracker">solar tracking</a> mechanism. 
</p><p>The most common application of solar energy collection outside agriculture is <a href="/wiki/Solar_water_heating" title="Solar water heating">solar water heating</a> systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The price of solar electrical power has continued to fall so that in many countries it has become cheaper than ordinary <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> electricity from <a href="/wiki/Mains_electricity" title="Mains electricity">the electricity grid</a> since 2012, a phenomenon known as <a href="/wiki/Grid_parity" title="Grid parity">grid parity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UN-Energy-2012_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-UN-Energy-2012-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theory_and_construction">Theory and construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_panel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Theory and construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">Solar cell</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:From_a_solar_cell_to_a_PV_system.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2406" data-file-width="2927" height="181" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/From_a_solar_cell_to_a_PV_system.svg/220px-From_a_solar_cell_to_a_PV_system.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/From_a_solar_cell_to_a_PV_system.svg/330px-From_a_solar_cell_to_a_PV_system.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/From_a_solar_cell_to_a_PV_system.svg/440px-From_a_solar_cell_to_a_PV_system.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:From_a_solar_cell_to_a_PV_system.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>From a <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">solar cell</a> to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PV_system" title="PV system">PV system</a></div></div></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" title="Photovoltaic">Photovoltaic</a> modules use light energy (<a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a>) from the Sun to generate electricity through the <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_effect" title="Photovoltaic effect">photovoltaic effect</a>. The majority of modules use <a href="/wiki/Wafer_(electronics)" title="Wafer (electronics)">wafer</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Crystalline_silicon" title="Crystalline silicon">crystalline silicon</a> cells or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thin_film_solar_cell" title="Thin film solar cell">thin-film cells</a>. The structural (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dead_and_live_loads" title="Dead and live loads">load carrying</a>) member of a module can either be the top layer or the back layer. Cells must also be protected from mechanical damage and moisture. Most modules are rigid, but semi-flexible ones based on thin-film cells are also available. The cells must be connected electrically in series, one to another. 
</p><p>A PV <a href="/wiki/Junction_box" title="Junction box">junction box</a> is attached to the back of the solar panel and it is its output interface.Externally, most of photovoltaic modules use <a href="/wiki/MC4_connector" title="MC4 connector">MC4 connectors</a> type to facilitate easy weatherproof connections to the rest of the system. Also, USB power interface can be used.
</p><p>Module electrical connections are made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Series_circuits" title="Series circuits">in series</a> to achieve a desired output voltage or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parallel_circuits" title="Parallel circuits">in parallel</a> to provide a desired current capability (amperes). The conducting wires that take the current off the modules may contain silver, copper or other non-magnetic conductive transition metals. Bypass <a href="/wiki/Diode" title="Diode">diodes</a> may be incorporated or used externally, in case of partial module shading, to maximize the output of module sections still illuminated.
</p><p>Some special solar PV modules include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_concentrator" title="Solar concentrator">concentrators</a> in which light is focused by <a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)" title="Lens (optics)">lenses</a> or mirrors onto smaller cells. This enables the use of cells with a high cost per unit area (such as <a href="/wiki/Gallium_arsenide" title="Gallium arsenide">gallium arsenide</a>) in a cost-effective way. 
</p><p>Solar panels also use metal frames consisting of racking components, brackets, reflector shapes, and troughs to better support the panel structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_panel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_solar_cells" title="Timeline of solar cells">Timeline of solar cells</a></div>
<p>In 1839, the ability of some materials to create an electrical charge from light exposure was first observed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexandre-Edmond_Becquerel" title="Alexandre-Edmond Becquerel">Alexandre-Edmond Becquerel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup> This observation was not replicated again until 1873, when <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Willoughey_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Willoughey Smith (page does not exist)">Willoughey Smith</a> discovered that the charge could be caused by light hitting selenium. After this discovery, <a href="/wiki/William_Grylls_Adams" title="William Grylls Adams">William Grylls Adams</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Evans_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Richard Evans Day (page does not exist)">Richard Evans Day</a> published "The action of light on selenium" in 1876, describing the experiment they used to replicate Smith's results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In 1881, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Fritts" title="Charles Fritts">Charles Fritts</a> created the first commercial solar panel, which was reported by Fritts as "continuous, constant and of considerable force not only by exposure to sunlight but also to dim, diffused daylight."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> However, these solar panels were very inefficient, especially compared to coal-fired power plants. In 1939, <a href="/wiki/Russell_Ohl" title="Russell Ohl">Russell Ohl</a> created the solar cell design that is used in many modern solar panels. He patented his design in 1941.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1954, this design was first used by <a href="/wiki/Bell_Labs" title="Bell Labs">Bell Labs</a> to create the first commercially viable silicon solar cell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Efficiencies">Efficiencies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_panel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Efficiencies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell_efficiency" title="Solar cell efficiency">Solar cell efficiency</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Best_Research-Cell_Efficiencies.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1555" data-file-width="2800" height="194" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Best_Research-Cell_Efficiencies.png/350px-Best_Research-Cell_Efficiencies.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Best_Research-Cell_Efficiencies.png/525px-Best_Research-Cell_Efficiencies.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Best_Research-Cell_Efficiencies.png/700px-Best_Research-Cell_Efficiencies.png 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Best_Research-Cell_Efficiencies.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Reported timeline of <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">solar cell</a> energy conversion efficiencies since 1976 (<a href="/wiki/National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory" title="National Renewable Energy Laboratory">National Renewable Energy Laboratory</a>)</div></div></div>
<p>Depending on construction, photovoltaic modules can produce electricity from a range of <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequencies of light</a>, but usually cannot cover the entire solar range (specifically, <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> and low or diffused light). Hence, much of the incident <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> energy is wasted by solar modules, and they can give far higher efficiencies if illuminated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monochromatic" title="Monochromatic">monochromatic</a> light. Therefore, another design concept is to split the light into six to eight different wavelength ranges that will produce a different color of light, and direct the beams onto different cells tuned to those ranges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> This has been projected to be capable of raising efficiency by 50%. 
</p><p>Scientists from Spectrolab, a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a>, have reported development of <a href="/wiki/Multi-junction_solar_cell" title="Multi-junction solar cell">multi-junction solar cells</a> with an efficiency of more than 40%, a new world record for solar photovoltaic cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The Spectrolab scientists also predict that concentrator solar cells could achieve efficiencies of more than 45% or even 50% in the future, with theoretical efficiencies being about 58% in cells with more than three junctions.
</p><p>Currently, the best achieved sunlight conversion rate (solar module efficiency) is around 21.5% in new commercial products<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> typically lower than the efficiencies of their cells in isolation. The most efficient mass-produced solar modules<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Disputed statement"><span title="This claim has reliable sources with contradicting facts (January 2013)">disputed</span></a> <span class="metadata"> – <a href="/wiki/Talk:Solar_panel" title="Talk:Solar panel">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> have power density values of up to 175 W/m<sup>2</sup> (16.22 W/ft<sup>2</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Research by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_College,_London" title="Imperial College, London">Imperial College, London</a> has shown that the efficiency of a solar panel can be improved by studding the light-receiving semiconductor surface with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum" title="Aluminum">aluminum</a> nanocylinders similar to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ridges" title="Ridges">ridges</a> on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lego_blocks" title="Lego blocks">Lego blocks</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_scattering" title="Light scattering">scattered</a> <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> then travels along a longer path in the semiconductor which means that more photons can be absorbed and converted into current. Although these nanocylinders have been used previously (aluminum was preceded by <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> and <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>), the light scattering occurred in the near infrared region and visible light was absorbed strongly. Aluminum was found to have absorbed the ultraviolet part of the spectrum, while the visible and near infrared parts of the spectrum were found to be scattered by the aluminum surface. This, the research argued, could bring down the cost significantly and improve the efficiency as aluminum is more abundant and less costly than gold and silver. The research also noted that the increase in current makes thinner film solar panels technically feasible without "compromising power conversion efficiencies, thus reducing material consumption".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Efficiencies of solar panel can be calculated by MPP (maximum power point) value of solar panels</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Solar_inverter" title="Solar inverter">Solar inverters</a> convert the DC power to <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">AC</a> power by performing MPPT process: solar inverter samples the output Power (I-V curve) from the solar cell and applies the proper resistance (load) to solar cells to obtain maximum power.</li>
<li>MPP (Maximum power point) of the solar panel consists of MPP voltage (V mpp) and MPP current (I mpp): it is a capacity of the solar panel and the higher value can make higher MPP.</li></ul>
<p>Micro-inverted solar panels are wired in <a href="/wiki/Series_and_parallel_circuits#Parallel_circuits" title="Series and parallel circuits">parallel</a>, which produces more output than normal panels which are wired in <a href="/wiki/Series_and_parallel_circuits#Series_circuits" title="Series and parallel circuits">series</a> with the output of the series determined by the lowest performing panel (this is known as the "Christmas light effect"). Micro-inverters work independently so each panel contributes its maximum possible output given the available sunlight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology">Technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_panel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Crystalline_silicon" title="Crystalline silicon">Crystalline silicon</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thin_film_solar_cell" title="Thin film solar cell">Thin film solar cell</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Global_Market_Share_by_PV_Technology_from_1990_to_2013.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="810" data-file-width="1252" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Global_Market_Share_by_PV_Technology_from_1990_to_2013.svg/220px-Global_Market_Share_by_PV_Technology_from_1990_to_2013.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Global_Market_Share_by_PV_Technology_from_1990_to_2013.svg/330px-Global_Market_Share_by_PV_Technology_from_1990_to_2013.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Global_Market_Share_by_PV_Technology_from_1990_to_2013.svg/440px-Global_Market_Share_by_PV_Technology_from_1990_to_2013.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Global_Market_Share_by_PV_Technology_from_1990_to_2013.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Market-share of PV technologies since 1990</div></div></div>
<p>Most solar modules are currently produced from crystalline silicon (c-Si) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_cells" title="Solar cells">solar cells</a> made of <a href="/wiki/Polycrystalline_silicon" title="Polycrystalline silicon">multicrystalline</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monocrystalline_silicon" title="Monocrystalline silicon">monocrystalline silicon</a>. In 2013, crystalline silicon accounted for more than 90 percent of worldwide PV production, while the rest of the overall market is made up of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thin_film_solar_cells" title="Thin film solar cells">thin-film technologies</a> using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CdTe_PV" title="CdTe PV">cadmium telluride</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CIGS_panel" title="CIGS panel">CIGS</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amorphous_silicon" title="Amorphous silicon">amorphous silicon</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Emerging, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thin_film_solar_cell#Emerging_photovoltaics" title="Thin film solar cell">third generation</a> solar technologies use advanced thin-film cells. They produce a relatively high-efficiency conversion for the low cost compared to other solar technologies. Also, high-cost, high-efficiency, and close-packed rectangular <a href="/wiki/Multi-junction_solar_cell" title="Multi-junction solar cell">multi-junction (MJ) cells</a> are preferably used in <a href="/wiki/Solar_panels_on_spacecraft" title="Solar panels on spacecraft">solar panels on spacecraft</a>, as they offer the highest ratio of generated power per kilogram lifted into space. MJ-cells are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compound_semiconductor" title="Compound semiconductor">compound semiconductors</a> and made of <a href="/wiki/Gallium_arsenide" title="Gallium arsenide">gallium arsenide</a> (GaAs) and other semiconductor materials. Another emerging PV technology using MJ-cells is <a href="/wiki/Concentrator_photovoltaics" title="Concentrator photovoltaics">concentrator photovoltaics</a> ( CPV ).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thin_film">Thin film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_panel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Thin film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In rigid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thin-film_module" title="Thin-film module">thin-film modules</a>, the cell and the module are manufactured in the same production line. The cell is created on a glass <a href="/wiki/Substrate_(materials_science)" title="Substrate (materials science)">substrate</a> or superstrate, and the electrical connections are created <i>in situ</i>, a so-called "monolithic integration". The substrate or superstrate is laminated with an encapsulant to a front or back <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sheet" title="wikt:sheet">sheet</a>, usually another sheet of glass. The main cell technologies in this category are <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_telluride" title="Cadmium telluride">CdTe</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Amorphous_silicon" title="Amorphous silicon">a-Si</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micromorphous_silicon" title="Micromorphous silicon">a-Si+uc-Si tandem</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Copper_indium_gallium_selenide" title="Copper indium gallium selenide">CIGS</a> (or variant). Amorphous silicon has a sunlight conversion rate of 6–12%
</p><p>Flexible thin film cells and modules are created on the same production line by depositing the <a href="/wiki/Photoactive_layer" title="Photoactive layer">photoactive layer</a> and other necessary layers on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flexible_substrate" title="Flexible substrate">flexible substrate</a>. If the substrate is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insulator_(electrical)" title="Insulator (electrical)">insulator</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Polyester" title="Polyester">polyester</a> or <a href="/wiki/Polyimide" title="Polyimide">polyimide</a> film) then <a href="/wiki/Die_(integrated_circuit)" title="Die (integrated circuit)">monolithic</a> integration can be used. If it is a conductor then another technique for electrical connection must be used. The cells are assembled into modules by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laminating" title="Laminating">laminating</a> them to a transparent colourless <a href="/wiki/Fluoropolymer" title="Fluoropolymer">fluoropolymer</a> on the front side (typically <a href="/wiki/ETFE" title="ETFE">ETFE</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fluorinated_ethylene_propylene" title="Fluorinated ethylene propylene">FEP</a>) and a polymer suitable for bonding to the final substrate on the other side.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Smart_solar_modules">Smart solar modules</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_panel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Smart solar modules">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Smart_module" title="Smart module">Smart module</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_micro-inverter" title="Solar micro-inverter">Solar micro-inverter</a></div>
<p>Several companies have begun embedding electronics into PV modules. This enables performing <a href="/wiki/Maximum_power_point_tracking" title="Maximum power point tracking">maximum power point tracking</a> (MPPT) for each module individually, and the measurement of performance data for monitoring and fault detection at module level. Some of these solutions make use of <a href="/wiki/Power_optimizer" title="Power optimizer">power optimizers</a>, a DC-to-DC converter technology developed to maximize the power harvest from solar photovoltaic systems. As of about 2010, such electronics can also compensate for shading effects, wherein a shadow falling across a section of a module causes the electrical output of one or more strings of cells in the module to fall to zero, but not having the output of the entire module fall to zero.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Performance_and_degradation">Performance and degradation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_panel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Performance and degradation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Original_research" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" data-file-height="40" data-file-width="40" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/40px-Ambox_important.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/60px-Ambox_important.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/80px-Ambox_important.svg.png 2x" width="40"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section  <b>possibly contains <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research">original research</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Solar_panel&amp;action=edit">improve it</a> by <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verifying</a> the claims made and adding <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#Inline_citations" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">inline citations</a>. Statements consisting only of original research should be removed.</span> <small><i>(August 2013)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Module performance is generally rated under standard test conditions (STC): <a href="/wiki/Irradiance" title="Irradiance">irradiance</a> of 1,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/W/m2" title="W/m2">W/m<sup>2</sup></a>, solar <a href="/wiki/Spectrum" title="Spectrum">spectrum</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airmass" title="Airmass">AM</a> 1.5 and module temperature at 25°C.
</p><p>Electrical characteristics include nominal power (P<sub>MAX</sub>, measured in <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">W</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_circuit_voltage" title="Open circuit voltage">open circuit voltage</a> (V<sub>OC</sub>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Short_circuit_current" title="Short circuit current">short circuit current</a> (I<sub>SC</sub>, measured in <a href="/wiki/Ampere" title="Ampere">amperes</a>), maximum power voltage (V<sub>MPP</sub>), maximum power current (I<sub>MPP</sub>), peak power, (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Watt-peak" title="Watt-peak">watt-peak</a>, W<sub>p</sub>), and module efficiency (%).
</p><p>Nominal voltage <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>refers to the voltage of the battery that the module is best suited to charge; this is a leftover term from the days when solar modules were only used to charge batteries. The actual voltage output of the module changes as lighting, temperature and load conditions change, so there is never one specific voltage at which the module operates. Nominal voltage allows users, at a glance, to make sure the module is compatible with a given system.
</p><p>Open circuit voltage or V<sub>OC</sub> is the maximum voltage that the module can produce when not connected to an electrical circuit or system. V<sub>OC</sub> can be measured with a <a href="/wiki/Voltmeter" title="Voltmeter">voltmeter</a> directly on an illuminated module's terminals or on its disconnected cable.
</p><p>The peak power rating, W<sub>p</sub>, is the maximum output under standard test conditions (not the maximum possible output). Typical modules, which could measure approximately 1 m × 2 m or 3 ft 3 in × 6 ft 7 in, will be rated from as low as 75 W to as high as 350 W, depending on their efficiency. At the time of testing, the test modules are binned according to their test results, and a typical manufacturer might rate their modules in 5 W increments, and either rate them at +/- 3%, +/-5%, +3/-0% or +5/-0%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solar_heater_dsc00632.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Solar_heater_dsc00632.jpg/220px-Solar_heater_dsc00632.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Solar_heater_dsc00632.jpg/330px-Solar_heater_dsc00632.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Solar_heater_dsc00632.jpg/440px-Solar_heater_dsc00632.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solar_heater_dsc00632.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Solar_water_heating" title="Solar water heating">Solar water heater</a></div></div></div>
<p>The ability of solar modules to withstand damage by rain, <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a>, heavy snow load, and cycles of heat and cold varies by manufacturer, although most solar panels on the U.S. market are UL listed, meaning they have gone through testing to withstand hail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Many <a href="/wiki/Crystalline_silicon" title="Crystalline silicon">crystalline silicon</a> module manufacturers offer a <a href="/wiki/Warranty#Defects_In_Materials_and_Workmanship" title="Warranty">limited warranty</a> that guarantees electrical production for 10 years at 90% of rated power output and 25 years at 80%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Potential_induced_degradation" title="Potential induced degradation">Potential induced degradation</a> (also called PID) is a potential induced performance degradation in crystalline photovoltaic modules, caused by so-called stray currents. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>This effect may cause power loss of up to 30%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>The largest challenge for <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaics" title="Photovoltaics">photovoltaic</a> technology is said to be the purchase price per watt of electricity produced, new materials and manufacturing techniques continue to improve the price to power performance. The problem resides in the enormous activation energy that must be overcome for a photon to excite an electron for harvesting purposes. Advancements in photovoltaic technologies have brought about the process of "doping" the silicon substrate to lower the activation energy thereby making the panel more efficient in converting photons to retrievable electrons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Chemicals such as Boron (p-type) are applied into the semiconductor crystal in order to create donor and acceptor energy levels substantially closer to the valence and conductor <a class="external text" href="http://2012books.lardbucket.org/books/principles-of-general-chemistry-v1.0/section_16/6520efadc9fafd08d3043572e61e9765.jpg" rel="nofollow">bands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In doing so, the addition of Boron impurity allows the activation energy to decrease 20 fold from 1.12 eV to 0.05 eV. Since the potential difference (E<sub>B</sub>) is so low, the Boron is able to thermally ionize at room temperatures. This allows for free energy carriers in the conduction and valence bands thereby allowing greater conversion of photons to electrons.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Maintenance">Maintenance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_panel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Maintenance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Solar panel conversion efficiency, typically in the 20% range, is reduced by dust, grime, pollen, and other particulates that accumulate on the solar panel. "A dirty solar panel can reduce its power capabilities by up to 30% in high dust/pollen or desert areas", says Seamus Curran, associate professor of physics at the University of Houston and director of the Institute for NanoEnergy, which specializes in the design, engineering, and assembly of nanostructures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Paying to have solar panels cleaned is often not a good investment; researchers found panels that had not been cleaned, or rained on, for 145 days during a summer drought in California, lost only 7.4% of their efficiency. Overall, for a typical residential solar system of 5 kW, washing panels halfway through the summer would translate into a mere $20 gain in electricity production until the summer drought ends—in about 2 ½ months. For larger commercial rooftop systems, the financial losses are bigger but still rarely enough to warrant the cost of washing the panels. On average, panels lost a little less than 0.05% of their overall efficiency per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recycling">Recycling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_panel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Recycling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most parts of a solar module can be recycled including up to 95% of certain semiconductor materials or the glass as well as large amounts of ferrous and non-ferrous metals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Some private companies and non-profit organizations are currently engaged in take-back and recycling operations for end-of-life modules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wambach_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wambach-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Recycling possibilities depend on the kind of technology used in the modules:
</p>
<ul><li>Silicon based modules: aluminum frames and junction boxes are dismantled manually at the beginning of the process. The module is then crushed in a mill and the different fractions are separated - glass, plastics and metals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> It is possible to recover more than 80% of the incoming weight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> This process can be performed by flat glass recyclers since morphology and composition of a PV module is similar to those flat glasses used in the building and automotive industry. The recovered glass for example is readily accepted by the glass foam and glass insulation industry.</li>
<li>Non-silicon based modules: they require specific recycling technologies such as the use of chemical baths in order to separate the different semiconductor materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> For <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_telluride" title="Cadmium telluride">cadmium telluride</a> modules, the recycling process begins by crushing the module and subsequently separating the different fractions. This recycling process is designed to recover up to 90% of the glass and 95% of the semiconductor materials contained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Some commercial-scale recycling facilities have been created in recent years by private companies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> For aluminium flat plate reflector: the trendiness of the reflectors has been brought up by fabricating them using a thin layer (around 0.016 mm to 0.024 mm) of Aluminum coating present inside the non-recycled plastic food packages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Since 2010, there is an annual European conference bringing together manufacturers, recyclers and researchers to look at the future of PV module recycling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_panel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In 2010, 15.9 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GigaWatt" title="GigaWatt">GW</a> of solar PV system installations were completed, with solar PV pricing survey and market research company PVinsights reporting growth of 117.8% in solar PV installation on a year-on-year basis.
</p><p>With over 100% year-on-year growth in PV system installation, PV module makers dramatically increased their shipments of solar modules in 2010. They actively expanded their capacity and turned themselves into gigawatt <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GigaWatt" title="GigaWatt">GW</a> players.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> According to PVinsights, five of the top ten PV module companies in 2010 are GW players. Suntech, First Solar, Sharp, Yingli and Trina Solar are GW producers now, and most of them doubled their shipments in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pvmarketresearch1_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-pvmarketresearch1-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>The basis of producing solar panels revolves around the use of silicon cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> These silicon cells are typically 10-20% efficient<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>  at converting sunlight into electricity, with newer production models now exceeding 22%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
In order for solar panels to become more efficient, researchers across the world have been trying to develop new technologies to make solar panels more effective at turning sunlight into energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2014, the world's top four solar module producers in terms of shipped capacity during the calendar year of 2014 were  <a href="/wiki/Yingli" title="Yingli">Yingli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trina_Solar" title="Trina Solar">Trina Solar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sharp_Solar" title="Sharp Solar">Sharp Solar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Solar" title="Canadian Solar">Canadian Solar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Price">Price</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_panel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Price">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Grid_parity" title="Grid parity">Grid parity</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Swansons-law.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Swansons-law.svg/220px-Swansons-law.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Swansons-law.svg/330px-Swansons-law.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Swansons-law.svg/440px-Swansons-law.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Swansons-law.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Swanson%27s_law" title="Swanson's law">Swanson's law</a> states that with every doubling of production of panels, there has been a 20 percent reduction in the cost of panels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Average pricing information divides in three pricing categories: those buying small quantities (modules of all sizes in the kilowatt range annually), mid-range buyers (typically up to 10 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MWp" title="MWp">MWp</a> annually), and large quantity buyers (self-explanatory—and with access to the lowest prices). Over the long term there is clearly a systematic reduction in the price of cells and modules. For example, in 2012 it was estimated that the quantity cost per watt was about US$0.60, which was 250 times lower than the cost in 1970 of US$150.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> A 2015 study shows price/kWh dropping by 10% per year since 1980, and predicts that solar could contribute 20% of total electricity consumption by 2030, whereas the <a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">International Energy Agency</a> predicts 16% by 2050.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>Real world energy production costs depend a great deal on local weather conditions. In a cloudy country such as the United Kingdom, the cost per produced kWh is higher than in sunnier countries like Spain.
</p><p>Following to <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountain_Institute" title="Rocky Mountain Institute">RMI</a>, <a href="/wiki/Balance_of_system" title="Balance of system">Balance-of-System</a> (BoS) elements, this is, non-module cost of non-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microinverter" title="Microinverter">microinverter</a> solar modules (as wiring, converters, racking systems and various components) make up about half of the total costs of installations.
</p><p>For merchant solar power stations, where the electricity is being sold into the electricity transmission network, the cost of <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar energy</a> will need to match the wholesale electricity price. This point is sometimes called 'wholesale grid parity' or 'busbar parity'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UN-Energy-2012_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-UN-Energy-2012-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some photovoltaic systems, such as rooftop installations, can supply power directly to an electricity user. In these cases, the installation can be competitive when the output cost matches the price at which the user pays for his electricity consumption. This situation is sometimes called 'retail grid parity', 'socket parity' or 'dynamic grid parity'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Research carried out by <a href="/wiki/UN-Energy" title="UN-Energy">UN-Energy</a> in 2012 suggests areas of sunny countries with high electricity prices, such as Italy, Spain and Australia, and areas using diesel generators, have reached retail grid parity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UN-Energy-2012_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-UN-Energy-2012-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mounting_and_tracking">Mounting and tracking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_panel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Mounting and tracking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_mounting_system" title="Photovoltaic mounting system">Photovoltaic mounting system</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_tracker" title="Solar tracker">Solar tracker</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EarthRangersCentre-ImageEnhancement.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3264" data-file-width="4928" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/EarthRangersCentre-ImageEnhancement.jpg/220px-EarthRangersCentre-ImageEnhancement.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/EarthRangersCentre-ImageEnhancement.jpg/330px-EarthRangersCentre-ImageEnhancement.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/EarthRangersCentre-ImageEnhancement.jpg/440px-EarthRangersCentre-ImageEnhancement.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EarthRangersCentre-ImageEnhancement.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Solar modules mounted on solar trackers</div></div></div>
<p>Ground-mounted photovoltaic system are usually large, utility-scale <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_power_plants" title="Solar power plants">solar power plants</a>. Their solar modules are held in place by racks or frames that are attached to ground-based mounting supports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Ground based mounting supports include:
</p>
<ul><li>Pole mounts, which are driven directly into the ground or embedded in concrete.</li>
<li>Foundation mounts, such as concrete slabs or poured footings</li>
<li>Ballasted footing mounts, such as concrete or steel bases that use weight to secure the solar module system in position and do not require ground penetration. This type of mounting system is well suited for sites where excavation is not possible such as capped landfills and simplifies decommissioning or relocation of solar module systems.</li></ul>
<p>Roof-mounted solar power systems consist of solar modules held in place by racks or frames attached to roof-based mounting supports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eco_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-eco-53">[53]</a></sup> Roof-based mounting supports include:
</p>
<ul><li>Pole mounts, which are attached directly to the roof structure and may use additional rails for attaching the module racking or frames.</li>
<li>Ballasted footing mounts, such as concrete or steel bases that use weight to secure the panel system in position and do not require through penetration. This mounting method allows for decommissioning or relocation of solar panel systems with no adverse effect on the roof structure.</li>
<li>All wiring connecting adjacent solar modules to the energy harvesting equipment must be installed according to local electrical codes and should be run in a conduit appropriate for the climate conditions</li></ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Solar_tracker" title="Solar tracker">Solar trackers</a> increase the amount of energy produced per module at a cost of mechanical complexity and need for maintenance. They sense the direction of the Sun and tilt or rotate the modules as needed for maximum exposure to the light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NREL_tracker_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-NREL_tracker-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Alternatively, fixed racks hold modules stationary as the sun moves across the sky. The fixed rack sets the angle at which the module is held. Tilt angles equivalent to an installation's latitude are common. Most of these fixed racks are set on poles above ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mounting_tilt_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-mounting_tilt-56">[56]</a></sup> Panels that face West or East may provide slightly lower energy, but evens out the supply, and may provide more power during peak demand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations">Limitations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_panel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Limitations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><dl><dt>Pollution and Energy in Production</dt></dl></li></ul>
<p>Solar panel has been a well-known method of generating clean, emission free electricity. However, it produces only direct current electricity (DC), which is not what normal appliances use. Solar photovoltaic systems (solar PV systems) are often made of solar PV panels (modules) and inverter (changing DC to AC). Solar PV panels are mainly made of solar photovoltaic cells, which has no fundamental difference to the material for making computer chips. The process of producing solar PV cells (computer chips) is energy intensive and involves highly poisonous and environmental toxic chemicals. There are few solar PV manufacturing plants around the world producing PV modules with energy produced from PV. This measure greatly reduces the carbon footprint during the manufacturing process. Managing the chemicals used in the manufacturing process is subject to the factories' local laws and regulations. 
</p>
<ul><li><dl><dt>Impact on Electricity Network</dt></dl></li></ul>
<p>With the increasing levels of rooftop photovoltaic systems, the energy flow becomes 2-way. When there is more local generation than consumption, electricity is exported to the grid. However, electricity network traditionally is not designed to deal with the 2- way energy transfer. Therefore, some technical issues may occur. For example in Queensland Australia, there have been more than 30% of households with rooftop PV by the end of 2017. The famous Californian 2020 duck curve appears very often for a lot of communities from 2015 onwards. An over-voltage issue may come out as the electricity flows from these PV households back to the network.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> There are solutions to manage the over voltage issue, such as regulating PV inverter power factor, new voltage and energy control equipment at electricity distributor level, re-conducting the electricity wires, demand side management, etc. There are often limitations and costs related to these solutions.
</p>
<ul><li><dl><dt>Implication onto Electricity Bill Management and Energy Investment</dt></dl></li></ul>
<p>There is no silver bullet in electricity or energy demand and bill management, because customers (sites) have different specific situations, e.g. different comfort/convenience needs, different electricity tariffs, or different usage patterns. Electricity tariff may have a few elements, such as daily access and metering charge, energy charge (based on kWh, MWh) or peak demand charge (e.g. a price for the highest 30min energy consumption in a month). PV is a promising option for reducing energy charge when electricity price is reasonably high and continuously increasing, such as in Australia and Germany. However for sites with peak demand charge in place, PV may be less attractive if peak demands mostly occur in the late afternoon to early evening, for example residential communities. Overall, energy investment is largely an economical decision and it is better to make investment decisions based on systematical evaluation of options in operational improvement, energy efficiency, onsite generation and energy storage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p>
