<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Energy density</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Energy density</b> is the amount of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> stored in a given system or region of space per unit <a href="/wiki/Volume" title="Volume">volume</a>. Colloquially it may also be used for energy per unit <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a>, though the accurate term for this is <a href="/wiki/Specific_energy" title="Specific energy">specific energy</a>.  Often only the <i>useful</i> or extractable energy is measured, which is to say that inaccessible energy (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rest_mass" title="Rest mass">rest mass</a> energy) is ignored.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Physical_cosmology" title="Physical cosmology">cosmological</a> and other <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativistic</a> contexts, however, the energy densities considered are those that correspond to the elements of the <a href="/wiki/Stress%E2%80%93energy_tensor" title="Stress–energy tensor">stress–energy tensor</a> and therefore do include mass energy as well as energy densities associated with the pressures described in the next paragraph.
</p><p>Energy per unit volume has the same physical units as <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a>, and in many circumstances is a <a href="/wiki/Synonym" title="Synonym">synonym</a>: for example, the energy density of a magnetic field may be expressed as (and behaves as) a physical pressure, and the energy required to compress a compressed gas a little more may be determined by multiplying the difference between the gas pressure and the external pressure by the change in volume. In short, pressure is a measure of the <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy" title="Enthalpy">enthalpy</a> per unit volume of a system. A pressure gradient has the potential to perform work on the surroundings by converting enthalpy to work until equilibrium is reached.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction_to_energy_density">Introduction to energy density</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_density&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Introduction to energy density">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are many different types of energy stored in materials, and it takes a particular type of reaction to release each type of energy. In order of the typical magnitude of the energy released, these types of reactions are: nuclear, chemical, electrochemical, and electrical.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reaction" title="Nuclear reaction">Nuclear reactions</a> are used by stars and nuclear power plants, both of which derive energy from the binding energy of nuclei. <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">Chemical reactions</a> are used by animals to derive energy from food, and by automobiles to derive energy from gasoline. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrochemical_reaction" title="Electrochemical reaction">Electrochemical reactions</a> are used by most mobile devices such as laptop computers and mobile phones to release the energy from batteries.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_density_in_energy_storage_and_in_fuel">Energy density in energy storage and in fuel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_density&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Energy density in energy storage and in fuel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Energy_density.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="675" data-file-width="1080" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Energy_density.svg/400px-Energy_density.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Energy_density.svg/600px-Energy_density.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Energy_density.svg/800px-Energy_density.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Energy_density.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Selected energy densities plot<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tzar1_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-tzar1-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tzar2_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-tzar2-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tzar3_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-tzar3-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tzar4_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-tzar4-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tzar5_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-tzar5-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tzar6_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-tzar6-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tzar7_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-tzar7-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tzar8_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-tzar8-37">[37]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Energy_storage" title="Energy storage">energy storage</a> applications the energy density relates the <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> of an energy store to the volume of the storage facility, e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a> tank. The higher the energy density of the fuel, the more energy may be stored or transported for the same amount of volume. The energy density of a fuel per unit mass is called the <a href="/wiki/Specific_energy" title="Specific energy">specific energy</a> of that fuel. In general an <a href="/wiki/Engine" title="Engine">engine</a> using that fuel will generate less <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> due to <a href="/wiki/Inefficiency" title="Inefficiency">inefficiencies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamic</a> considerations—hence the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thrust_specific_fuel_consumption" title="Thrust specific fuel consumption">specific fuel consumption</a> of an engine will always be greater than its rate of production of the kinetic energy of motion.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_energy_sources">Nuclear energy sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_density&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Nuclear energy sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The greatest energy source by far is mass itself.  This energy, <i>E = mc<sup>2</sup></i>, where <i>m = ρV</i>, <i>ρ</i> is the mass per unit volume, <i>V</i> is the volume of the mass itself and <i>c</i> is the speed of light.  This energy, however, can be released only by the processes of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">nuclear fission</a> (0.1%), <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a> (1%),<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> or the annihilation of some or all of the matter in the volume <i>V</i> by matter-<a href="/wiki/Antimatter" title="Antimatter">antimatter</a> collisions (100%).  Nuclear reactions cannot be realized by chemical reactions such as combustion.  Although greater matter densities can be achieved, the density of a <a href="/wiki/Neutron_star" title="Neutron star">neutron star</a> would approximate the most dense system capable of matter-antimatter annihilation possible.  A <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">black hole</a>, although denser than a neutron star, does not have an equivalent anti-particle form, but would offer the same 100% conversion rate of mass to energy in the form of Hawking radiation. In the case of relatively small black holes (smaller than astronomical objects) the power output would be tremendous.
</p><p>The highest density sources of energy aside from antimatter are <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">fusion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">fission</a>. Fusion includes energy from the sun which will be available for billions of years (in the form of sunlight) but so far (2016), sustained <a href="/wiki/Fusion_power" title="Fusion power">fusion power</a> production continues to be elusive. Power from fission of uranium and thorium in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> plants will be available for many decades or even centuries because of the plentiful supply of the elements on earth,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> though the full potential of this source can only be realised through <a href="/wiki/Breeder_reactor" title="Breeder reactor">breeder reactors</a>, which are, apart from the <a href="/wiki/BN-600_reactor" title="BN-600 reactor">BN-600 reactor</a>, not yet used commercially.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cohen_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-cohen-39">[39]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">Coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> are the current primary energy sources in the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> but have a much lower energy density. Burning local <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> fuels supplies household energy needs (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biomass_Cook_Stoves" title="Biomass Cook Stoves">cooking fires</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oil_lamp" title="Oil lamp">oil lamps</a>, etc.) worldwide.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Broad_implications">Broad implications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_density&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Broad implications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Energy density differs from <a href="/wiki/Energy_conversion_efficiency" title="Energy conversion efficiency">energy conversion efficiency</a> (net output per input) or <a href="/wiki/Embodied_energy" title="Embodied energy">embodied energy</a> (the energy output costs to provide, as <a href="/wiki/Energy_industry" title="Energy industry">harvesting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">refining</a>, distributing, and dealing with <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> all use energy). Large scale, intensive energy use impacts and is impacted by <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_waste" title="Nuclear waste">waste storage</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">environmental consequences</a>.
</p><p>No single energy storage method boasts the best in <a href="/wiki/Power-to-weight_ratio" title="Power-to-weight ratio">specific power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Specific_energy" title="Specific energy">specific energy</a>, and energy density. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peukert%27s_Law" title="Peukert's Law">Peukert's Law</a> describes how the amount of useful energy that can be obtained (for a lead-acid cell) depends on how quickly we pull it out.  To maximize both specific energy and energy density, one can compute the <a href="/wiki/Specific_energy" title="Specific energy">specific energy density</a> of a substance by multiplying the two values together, where the higher the number, the better the substance is at storing energy efficiently.
</p><p>Alternative options are discussed for energy storage to increase energy density and decrease charging time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravimetric" title="Gravimetric">Gravimetric</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volumetric" title="Volumetric">volumetric</a> energy density of some fuels and storage technologies (modified from the <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">Gasoline</a> article):
</p>
<dl><dd>Note: Some values may not be precise because of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isomers" title="Isomers">isomers</a> or other irregularities.  See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heating_value" title="Heating value">Heating value</a> for a comprehensive table of specific energies of important fuels.</dd>
<dd>Note: Also it is important to realise that generally the density values for chemical fuels do not include the weight of oxygen required for combustion. This is typically two oxygen atoms per carbon atom, and one per two hydrogen atoms. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_weight" title="Atomic weight">atomic weight</a> of carbon and oxygen are similar, while hydrogen is much lighter than oxygen. Figures are presented this way for those fuels where in practice air would only be drawn in locally to the burner. This explains the apparently lower energy density of materials that already include their own oxidiser (such as gunpowder and TNT), where the mass of the oxidiser in effect adds dead weight, and absorbs some of the energy of combustion to dissociate and liberate oxygen to continue the reaction. This also explains some apparent anomalies, such as the energy density of a sandwich appearing to be higher than that of a stick of dynamite.</dd></dl>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_density_of_electric_and_magnetic_fields">Energy density of electric and magnetic fields</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_density&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Energy density of electric and magnetic fields">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
