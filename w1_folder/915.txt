<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Caprock</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For the region near Texas, see <a href="/wiki/Caprock,_Texas" title="Caprock, Texas">Caprock, Texas</a>.</div><p><b>Caprock</b> or <b>cap rock</b> is a harder or more resistant <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rock</a> type overlying a weaker or less resistant rock type.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup>  Common types of caprock are <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mafic_rock" title="Mafic rock">mafic rock</a> types. An analogy of caprock could be the outer crust on a cake that is a bit harder than the underlying layer. Common caprock locations are <a href="/wiki/Escarpment" title="Escarpment">escarpments</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesa" title="Mesa">mesa</a> formations, and <a href="/wiki/Salt_dome" title="Salt dome">salt domes</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_industry" title="Petroleum industry">petroleum industry</a>, caprock is generalized to any <a href="/wiki/Permeability_(earth_sciences)" title="Permeability (earth sciences)">nonpermeable</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formation_(stratigraphy)" title="Formation (stratigraphy)">formation</a> that may prevent oil, gas, or water from migrating to the surface.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caprock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DSCN4413_horseshoefalls_e.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="581" data-file-width="935" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/DSCN4413_horseshoefalls_e.jpg/220px-DSCN4413_horseshoefalls_e.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/DSCN4413_horseshoefalls_e.jpg/330px-DSCN4413_horseshoefalls_e.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/DSCN4413_horseshoefalls_e.jpg/440px-DSCN4413_horseshoefalls_e.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DSCN4413_horseshoefalls_e.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Horseshoe_Falls" title="Horseshoe Falls">Horseshoe Falls</a>, part of the <a href="/wiki/Niagara_Escarpment" title="Niagara Escarpment">Niagara Escarpment</a></div></div></div><p>Caprock is a harder or more resistant <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rock</a> type overlying a weaker or less resistant rock type.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup>  Common types of caprock are <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mafic_rock" title="Mafic rock">mafic rock</a> types. An analogy of caprock could be the outer crust on a cake that is a bit harder than the underlying layer. In processes such as <a href="/wiki/Scarp_retreat" title="Scarp retreat">scarp retreat</a>, the caprock controls the rate of erosion of the scarp.  As the softer rock is cut away, periodically the caprock shears off.  Caprock is also found on the top of <a href="/wiki/Mesa" title="Mesa">mesa</a> formations.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Niagara_Escarpment" title="Niagara Escarpment">Niagara Escarpment</a>, over which <a href="/wiki/Niagara_Falls" title="Niagara Falls">Niagara Falls</a> flows, is an example of a scarp or <a href="/wiki/Escarpment" title="Escarpment">escarpment</a>. At Niagara Falls, the caprock is the <a href="/wiki/Stream_bed" title="Stream bed">riverbed</a> above the falls, and is what prevents the river from eroding the face of the falls very quickly. In the photo, the dark thin layer in the foreground where water is not yet running, is the caprock. The Niagara caprock is made of dolomitic limestone (<i><a href="/wiki/Dolostone" title="Dolostone">dolostone</a></i>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Petroleum">Petroleum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caprock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Petroleum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_industry" title="Petroleum industry">petroleum industry</a>, <i>caprock</i> is any <a href="/wiki/Permeability_(earth_sciences)" title="Permeability (earth sciences)">nonpermeable</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formation_(stratigraphy)" title="Formation (stratigraphy)">formation</a> that may trap oil, gas or water, preventing it from migrating to the surface. This caprock or <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_reservoir" title="Oil reservoir">trap</a></i> can create a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_reservoir" title="Oil reservoir">reservoir</a></i> of oil, gas or water beneath it and is a primary target for the petroleum industry.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Salt_dome_caprock">Salt dome caprock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caprock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Salt dome caprock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The tops of <a href="/wiki/Salt_dome" title="Salt dome">salt domes</a> such as in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> dissolve in a characteristic manner, and can range between 0–1500 ft thick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/Halite" title="Halite">halite</a> (salt) is removed first, leaving behind <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anhydrite" title="Anhydrite">anhydrite</a>.  The anhydrite and gypsum react with organic material to form <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a>.  The classic Murray 1966 paper<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> describes the generalized sequence as sediments-calcite-gypsum-anhydrite-salt.
</p>
