<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Redox_(disambiguation)" title="Redox (disambiguation)">Redox (disambiguation)</a>.</div><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NaF.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="245" data-file-width="560" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/NaF.gif/350px-NaF.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/NaF.gif/525px-NaF.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a8/NaF.gif 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NaF.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">Sodium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a> bonding ionically to form <a href="/wiki/Sodium_fluoride" title="Sodium fluoride">sodium fluoride</a>. The oppositely charged ions are then attracted to each other. The sodium is oxidized, and the fluorine is reduced.</div></div></div>

<p><b>Redox</b> (short for <i>reduction–oxidation</i> reaction) (pronunciation: <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɛ/: 'e' in 'dress'">ɛ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling">redoks</i></a> or <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling">reedoks</i></a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reaction</a> in which the <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation states</a> of atoms are changed. Any such reaction involves both a reduction process and a complementary oxidation process, two key concepts involved with <a href="/wiki/Electron_transfer" title="Electron transfer">electron transfer</a> processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Redox reactions include all chemical reactions in which atoms have their oxidation state changed; in general, redox reactions involve the transfer of <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> between <a href="/wiki/Chemical_species" title="Chemical species">chemical species</a>. The chemical species from which the electron is stripped is said to have been oxidized, while the chemical species to which the electron is added is said to have been reduced. It can be explained in simple terms:
</p>
<ul><li><b>Oxidation</b> is the <i>loss</i> of <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> or an <i>increase</i> in oxidation state by a <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecule</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ion</a>.</li>
<li><b>Reduction</b> is the <i>gain</i> of electrons or a <i>decrease</i> in oxidation state by a molecule, atom, or ion.</li></ul>
<p>As an example, during the <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> of wood, oxygen from the air is reduced, gaining electrons from carbon which is oxidized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gale_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-gale-3">[3]</a></sup> Although oxidation reactions are commonly associated with the formation of oxides from oxygen molecules, oxygen is not necessarily included in such reactions, as other chemical species can serve the same function.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gale_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-gale-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The reaction can occur relatively slowly, as with the formation of <a href="/wiki/Rust" title="Rust">rust</a>, or more quickly, in the case of fire. There are simple redox processes, such as the oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> to yield <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) or the reduction of carbon by <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> to yield <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> (CH<sub>4</sub>), and more complex processes such as the oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> (C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>12</sub>O<sub>6</sub>) in the human body.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Redox" is a <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> of the words "reduction" and "oxidation". The word <i>oxidation</i> originally implied reaction with oxygen to form an oxide, since <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dioxygen" title="Dioxygen">dioxygen</a> (O<sub>2</sub> (g)) was historically the first recognized <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_agent" title="Oxidizing agent">oxidizing agent</a>. Later, the term was expanded to encompass oxygen-like substances that accomplished parallel chemical reactions. Ultimately, the meaning was generalized to include all processes involving loss of electrons.
</p><p>The word <i>reduction</i> originally referred to the loss in weight upon heating a metallic <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ore</a> such as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal_oxide" title="Metal oxide">metal oxide</a> to extract the metal. In other words, ore was "reduced" to metal. <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Lavoisier" title="Antoine Lavoisier">Antoine Lavoisier</a> (1743–1794) showed that this loss of weight was due to the loss of oxygen as a gas. Later, scientists realized that the metal atom gains electrons in this process. The meaning of <i>reduction</i> then became generalized to include all processes involving a gain of electrons. Even though "reduction" seems counter-intuitive when speaking of the <b>gain</b> of electrons, it might help to think of reduction as the loss of oxygen, which was its historical meaning. Since electrons are negatively charged, it is also helpful to think of this as reduction in electrical charge.
</p><p>The electrochemist <a href="/wiki/John_Bockris" title="John Bockris">John Bockris</a> has used the words <i>electronation</i> and <i>deelectronation</i> to describe reduction and oxidation processes respectively when they occur at <a href="/wiki/Electrode" title="Electrode">electrodes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> These words are analogous to <a href="/wiki/Protonation" title="Protonation">protonation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deprotonation" title="Deprotonation">deprotonation</a>, but they have not been widely adopted by chemists worldwide.
</p><p>The term "hydrogenation" could be used instead of reduction, since hydrogen is the reducing agent in a large number of reactions, especially in organic chemistry and biochemistry. But, unlike oxidation, which has been generalized beyond its root element, hydrogenation has maintained its specific connection to reactions that <i>add</i> hydrogen to another substance (e.g., the hydrogenation of unsaturated fats into saturated fats, R−CH=CH−R + H<sub>2</sub> → R−CH<sub>2</sub>−CH<sub>2</sub>−R). The word "redox" was first used in 1928.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The processes of oxidation and reduction occur simultaneously and cannot happen independently of one another, similar to the <a href="/wiki/Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction" title="Acid–base reaction">acid–base reaction</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gale_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-gale-3">[3]</a></sup> The oxidation alone and the reduction alone are each called a <i><a href="/wiki/Half-reaction" title="Half-reaction">half-reaction</a></i>, because two half-reactions always occur together to form a whole reaction. When writing half-reactions, the gained or lost electrons are typically included explicitly in order that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balanced_reaction" title="Balanced reaction">half-reaction be balanced</a> with respect to electric charge.
</p><p>Though sufficient for many purposes, these general descriptions are not precisely correct. Although oxidation and reduction properly refer to <i>a change in <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a></i> — the actual transfer of electrons may never occur. The oxidation state of an atom is the fictitious charge that an atom would have if all bonds between atoms of different elements were 100% ionic. Thus, oxidation is best defined as an <i>increase in oxidation state</i>, and reduction as a <i>decrease in oxidation state</i>. In practice, the transfer of electrons will always cause a change in oxidation state, but there are many reactions that are classed as "redox" even though no electron transfer occurs (such as those involving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Covalent" title="Covalent">covalent</a> bonds).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxidizing_and_reducing_agents">Oxidizing and reducing agents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Oxidizing and reducing agents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In redox processes, the reductant transfers electrons to the oxidant. Thus, in the reaction, the reductant or <i>reducing agent</i> loses electrons and is oxidized, and the oxidant or <i>oxidizing agent</i> gains electrons and is reduced. The pair of an oxidizing and reducing agent that are involved in a particular reaction is called a <b>redox pair</b>. A <b>redox couple</b> is a reducing species and its corresponding oxidizing form, e.g., Fe<sup>2+</sup>/Fe<sup>3+</sup>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxidizers">Oxidizers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Oxidizers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GHS-pictogram-rondflam.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="724" data-file-width="724" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/GHS-pictogram-rondflam.svg/220px-GHS-pictogram-rondflam.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/GHS-pictogram-rondflam.svg/330px-GHS-pictogram-rondflam.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/GHS-pictogram-rondflam.svg/440px-GHS-pictogram-rondflam.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GHS-pictogram-rondflam.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Globally_Harmonized_System_of_Classification_and_Labeling_of_Chemicals" title="Globally Harmonized System of Classification and Labeling of Chemicals">international</a> <a href="/wiki/GHS_hazard_pictograms" title="GHS hazard pictograms">pictogram</a> for oxidizing chemicals.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_agent" title="Oxidizing agent">Oxidizing agent</a></div>
<p>Substances that have the ability to <b>oxidize</b> other substances (cause them to lose electrons) are said to be <b>oxidative</b> or <b>oxidizing</b> and are known as <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_agent" title="Oxidizing agent">oxidizing agents</a>, oxidants, or oxidizers. That is, the oxidant (oxidizing agent) removes electrons from another substance, and is thus itself reduced. And, because it "accepts" electrons, the oxidizing agent is also called an <a href="/wiki/Electron_acceptor" title="Electron acceptor">electron acceptor</a>. <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">Oxygen</a> is the quintessential oxidizer.

style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></a></span>), or else highly <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">electronegative</a> elements (<a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O<sub>2</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">F<sub>2</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">Cl<sub>2</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">Br<sub>2</sub></a>) that can gain extra electrons by oxidizing another substance.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reducers">Reducers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reducers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Reducing_agent" title="Reducing agent">Reducing agent</a></div>
<p>Substances that have the ability to <b>reduce</b> other substances (cause them to gain electrons) are said to be <b>reductive</b> or <b>reducing</b> and are known as <a href="/wiki/Reducing_agent" title="Reducing agent">reducing agents</a>, reductants, or reducers. The reductant (reducing agent) transfers electrons to another substance, and is thus itself oxidized. And, because it "donates" electrons, the reducing agent is also called an <a href="/wiki/Electron_donor" title="Electron donor">electron donor</a>. Electron donors can also form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charge_transfer_complex" title="Charge transfer complex">charge transfer complexes</a> with electron acceptors.
</p><p>Reductants in chemistry are very diverse. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electropositive" title="Electropositive">Electropositive</a> elemental <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metals</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, are good reducing agents. These metals donate or <i>give away</i> electrons readily. <i>Hydride transfer reagents</i>, such as <a href="/wiki/Sodium_borohydride" title="Sodium borohydride">NaBH<sub>4</sub></a> and <a href="/wiki/Lithium_aluminium_hydride" title="Lithium aluminium hydride">LiAlH<sub>4</sub></a>, are widely used in <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> primarily in the reduction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonyl" title="Carbonyl">carbonyl</a> compounds to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcohols" title="Alcohols">alcohols</a>. Another method of reduction involves the use of hydrogen gas (H<sub>2</sub>) with a <a href="/wiki/Palladium" title="Palladium">palladium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a>. These <i>catalytic reductions</i> are used primarily in the reduction of carbon-carbon double or triple bonds.
</p>
<h2><span id="Standard_electrode_potentials_.28reduction_potentials.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Standard_electrode_potentials_(reduction_potentials)">Standard electrode potentials (reduction potentials)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Standard electrode potentials (reduction potentials)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Each half-reaction has a <i>standard <a href="/wiki/Electrode_potential" title="Electrode potential">electrode potential</a></i> (<i>E</i><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">0</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">cell</sub></span>), which is equal to the potential difference or <a href="/wiki/Voltage" title="Voltage">voltage</a> at equilibrium under <a href="/wiki/Standard_state" title="Standard state">standard conditions</a> of an <a href="/wiki/Electrochemical_cell" title="Electrochemical cell">electrochemical cell</a> in which the <a href="/wiki/Cathode" title="Cathode">cathode</a> reaction is the <a href="/wiki/Half-reaction" title="Half-reaction">half-reaction</a> considered, and the <a href="/wiki/Anode" title="Anode">anode</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Standard_hydrogen_electrode" title="Standard hydrogen electrode">standard hydrogen electrode</a> where hydrogen is oxidized:
</p>
<dl><dd>​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> H<sub>2</sub> → H<sup>+</sup> + e<sup>−</sup>.</dd></dl>
<p>The electrode potential of each half-reaction is also known as its <i>reduction potential</i> <i>E</i><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">0</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">red</sub></span>, or potential when the half-reaction takes place at a cathode. The reduction potential is a measure of the tendency of the oxidizing agent to be reduced. Its value is zero for H<sup>+</sup> + e<sup>−</sup> → ​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> H<sub>2</sub> by definition, positive for oxidizing agents stronger than H<sup>+</sup> (e.g., +2.866 V for F<sub>2</sub>) and negative for oxidizing agents that are weaker than H<sup>+</sup> (e.g., −0.763 V for Zn<sup>2+</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>For a redox reaction that takes place in a cell, the potential difference is:
</p>
<dl><dd><i>E</i><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">0</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">cell</sub></span> = <i>E</i><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">0</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">cathode</sub></span> – <i>E</i><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">0</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">anode</sub></span></dd></dl>
<p>However, the potential of the reaction at the anode was sometimes expressed as an <i>oxidation potential</i>:
</p>
<dl><dd><i>E</i><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">0</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">ox</sub></span> = –<i>E</i><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">0</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">red</sub></span>.</dd></dl>
<p>The oxidation potential is a measure of the tendency of the reducing agent to be oxidized, but does not represent the physical potential at an electrode. With this notation, the cell voltage equation is written with a plus sign
</p>
<dl><dd><i>E</i><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">0</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">cell</sub></span> = <i>E</i><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">0</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">red(cathode)</sub></span> + <i>E</i><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">0</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">ox(anode)</sub></span></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_of_redox_reactions">Examples of redox reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Examples of redox reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Redox_reaction.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="168" data-file-width="391" height="129" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Redox_reaction.png/300px-Redox_reaction.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ee/Redox_reaction.png 1.5x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Redox_reaction.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illustration of a redox reaction</div></div></div>
<p>A good example is the reaction between <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a> in which hydrogen is being oxidized and fluorine is being reduced:
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">F<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> → 2 HF</dd></dl>
<p>We can write this overall reaction as two <a href="/wiki/Half-reaction" title="Half-reaction">half-reactions</a>:
</p><p>the oxidation reaction:
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> → 2 <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_ion" title="Hydrogen ion">H<sup>+</sup></a> + 2 <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">e<sup>−</sup></a></dd></dl>
<p>and the reduction reaction:
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="chemf nowrap">F<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> + 2 e<sup>−</sup> → 2 <a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">F<sup>−</sup></a></dd></dl>
<p>Analyzing each half-reaction in isolation can often make the overall chemical process clearer. Because there is no net change in charge during a redox reaction, the number of electrons in excess in the oxidation reaction must equal the number consumed by the reduction reaction (as shown above).
</p><p>Elements, even in molecular form, always have an oxidation state of zero. In the first half-reaction, hydrogen is oxidized from an oxidation state of zero to an oxidation state of +1. In the second half-reaction, fluorine is reduced from an oxidation state of zero to an oxidation state of −1.
</p><p>When adding the reactions together the electrons are canceled:
</p>

</td>
<td>→
</td>
<td align="left">2 H<sup>+</sup> + 2 e<sup>−</sup>
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td align="right"><span class="chemf nowrap">F<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> + 2 e<sup>−</sup>
</td>
<td>→
</td>
<td align="left">2 F<sup>−</sup>
</td></tr>
<tr>

</td></tr>

<td align="right">H<sub>2</sub> + F<sub>2</sub>
</td>

</td></tr></tbody></table></dd></dl>
<p>And the ions combine to form <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">hydrogen fluoride</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 H<sup>+</sup> + 2 F<sup>−</sup> → 2 HF</dd></dl>
<p>The overall reaction is:
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">F<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> → 2 HF</dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metal_displacement">Metal displacement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Metal displacement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Galvanic_cell_with_no_cation_flow.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="422" data-file-width="727" height="203" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Galvanic_cell_with_no_cation_flow.png/350px-Galvanic_cell_with_no_cation_flow.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Galvanic_cell_with_no_cation_flow.png/525px-Galvanic_cell_with_no_cation_flow.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Galvanic_cell_with_no_cation_flow.png/700px-Galvanic_cell_with_no_cation_flow.png 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Galvanic_cell_with_no_cation_flow.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A redox reaction is the force behind an <a href="/wiki/Electrochemical_cell" title="Electrochemical cell">electrochemical cell</a> like the <a href="/wiki/Galvanic_cell" title="Galvanic cell">Galvanic cell</a> pictured. The battery is made out of a zinc electrode in a ZnSO<sub>4</sub> solution connected with a wire and a porous disk to a copper electrode in a CuSO<sub>4</sub> solution.</div></div></div>
<p>In this type of reaction, a metal atom in a compound (or in a solution) is replaced by an atom of another metal. For example, <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> is deposited when <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> metal is placed in a <a href="/wiki/Copper(II)_sulfate" title="Copper(II) sulfate">copper(II) sulfate</a> solution:
</p><p>Zn(s)+ CuSO<sub>4</sub>(aq) → ZnSO<sub>4</sub>(aq) + Cu(s)
</p><p>In the above reaction, zinc metal displaces the copper(II) ion from copper sulfate solution and thus liberates free copper metal.
</p><p>The ionic equation for this reaction is:
</p>
<dl><dd>Zn + Cu<sup>2+</sup> → Zn<sup>2+</sup> + Cu</dd></dl>
<p>As two <a href="/wiki/Half-reaction" title="Half-reaction">half-reactions</a>, it is seen that the zinc is oxidized:
</p>
<dl><dd>Zn → Zn<sup>2+</sup> + 2 e<sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<p>And the copper is reduced:
</p>
<dl><dd>Cu<sup>2+</sup> + 2 e<sup>−</sup> → Cu</dd></dl>

<dl><dd>2 <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> + 10 e<sup>−</sup> + 12 H<sup>+</sup> → N<sub>2</sub> + 6 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl></li>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Corrosion_and_rusting">Corrosion and rusting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Corrosion and rusting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rust_screw.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Rust_screw.jpg/220px-Rust_screw.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Rust_screw.jpg/330px-Rust_screw.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Rust_screw.jpg/440px-Rust_screw.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rust_screw.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Oxides, such as <a href="/wiki/Iron(III)_oxide" title="Iron(III) oxide">iron(III) oxide</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rust" title="Rust">rust</a>, which consists of hydrated <a href="/wiki/Iron(III)_oxide" title="Iron(III) oxide">iron(III) oxides</a> Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>·<i>n</i>H<sub>2</sub>O and <a href="/wiki/Iron(III)_oxide-hydroxide" title="Iron(III) oxide-hydroxide">iron(III) oxide-hydroxide</a> (FeO(OH), Fe(OH)<sub>3</sub>), form when oxygen combines with other elements</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PyOx.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/PyOx.JPG/220px-PyOx.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/PyOx.JPG/330px-PyOx.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/PyOx.JPG/440px-PyOx.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PyOx.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Iron rusting in <a href="/wiki/Pyrite" title="Pyrite">pyrite</a> cubes</div></div></div>
<ul><li>The term <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> refers to the electrochemical oxidation of metals in reaction with an oxidant such as oxygen. <a href="/wiki/Rust" title="Rust">Rusting</a>, the formation of <a href="/wiki/Iron_oxide" title="Iron oxide">iron oxides</a>, is a well-known example of electrochemical corrosion; it forms as a result of the oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> metal. Common rust often refers to <a href="/wiki/Iron(III)_oxide" title="Iron(III) oxide">iron(III) oxide</a>, formed in the following chemical reaction:
<dl><dd>4 Fe + 3 O<sub>2</sub> → 2 Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></dd></dl></li>
<li>The oxidation of iron(II) to iron(III) by <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a> in the presence of an acid:
<dl><dd>Fe<sup>2+</sup> → Fe<sup>3+</sup> + e<sup>−</sup></dd>
<dd>H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> + 2 e<sup>−</sup> → 2 OH<sup>−</sup></dd></dl></li></ul>
<dl><dd>Overall equation:
<dl><dd>2 Fe<sup>2+</sup> + H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> + 2 H<sup>+</sup> → 2 Fe<sup>3+</sup> + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Redox_reactions_in_industry">Redox reactions in industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Redox reactions in industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cathodic_protection" title="Cathodic protection">Cathodic protection</a> is a technique used to control the corrosion of a metal surface by making it the cathode of an electrochemical cell. A simple method of protection connects protected metal to a more easily corroded "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacrificial_anode" title="Sacrificial anode">sacrificial anode</a>" to act as the anode. The sacrificial metal instead of the protected metal, then, corrodes. A common application of cathodic protection is in <a href="/wiki/Galvanization" title="Galvanization">galvanized</a> steel, in which a sacrificial coating of zinc on steel parts protects them from rust.
</p><p>Oxidation is used in a wide variety of industries such as in the production of <a href="/wiki/Category:Cleaning_products" title="Category:Cleaning products">cleaning products</a> and oxidizing <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a>, which is used in most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertilizers" title="Fertilizers">fertilizers</a>.
</p><p>Redox reactions are the foundation of <a href="/wiki/Electrochemical_cell" title="Electrochemical cell">electrochemical cells</a>, which can generate electrical energy or support <a href="/wiki/Electrosynthesis" title="Electrosynthesis">electrosynthesis</a>. Metal <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ores</a> often contain metals in oxidized states such as oxides or sulfides, from which the pure metals are extracted by <a href="/wiki/Smelting" title="Smelting">smelting</a> at high temperature in the presence of a reducing agent. The process of <a href="/wiki/Electroplating" title="Electroplating">electroplating</a> uses redox reactions to coat objects with a thin layer of a material, as in <a href="/wiki/Chrome_plating" title="Chrome plating">chrome-plated</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automotive" title="Automotive">automotive</a> parts, <a href="/wiki/Plating#Silver_plating" title="Plating">silver plating</a> <a href="/wiki/Cutlery" title="Cutlery">cutlery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galvanization" title="Galvanization">galvanization</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gold-plated" title="Gold-plated">gold-plated</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewelry" title="Jewelry">jewelry</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Redox_reactions_in_biology">Redox reactions in biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Redox reactions in biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright" style="background-color: #f9f9f9; border: 1px solid #CCCCCC; margin:0.5em;">
<table border="0" cellpadding="2" cellspacing="0" style="font-size: 85%; border: 1px solid #CCCCCC; margin: 0.3em;" width="150px">

<td><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ascorbic_acid_structure.png" title="ascorbic acid"><img alt="ascorbic acid" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="621" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Ascorbic_acid_structure.png/150px-Ascorbic_acid_structure.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Ascorbic_acid_structure.png/225px-Ascorbic_acid_structure.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Ascorbic_acid_structure.png/300px-Ascorbic_acid_structure.png 2x" width="150"/></a>

<td><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dehydroascorbic_acid.png" title="dehydroascorbic acid"><img alt="dehydroascorbic acid" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="621" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Dehydroascorbic_acid.png/150px-Dehydroascorbic_acid.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Dehydroascorbic_acid.png/225px-Dehydroascorbic_acid.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Dehydroascorbic_acid.png/300px-Dehydroascorbic_acid.png 2x" width="150"/></a>

<p>Many important <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biological</a> processes involve redox reactions.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">Cellular respiration</a>, for instance, is the oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> (C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>12</sub>O<sub>6</sub>) to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> and the reduction of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> to <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>. The summary equation for cell respiration is:
</p>
<dl><dd>C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>12</sub>O<sub>6</sub> + 6 O<sub>2</sub> → 6 CO<sub>2</sub> + 6 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>The process of cell respiration also depends heavily on the reduction of <a href="/wiki/Nicotinamide_adenine_dinucleotide" title="Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide">NAD<sup>+</sup></a> to NADH and the reverse reaction (the oxidation of NADH to NAD<sup>+</sup>). <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">Photosynthesis</a> and cellular respiration are complementary, but photosynthesis is not the reverse of the redox reaction in cell respiration:
</p>
<dl><dd>6 CO<sub>2</sub> + 6 H<sub>2</sub>O + <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">light energy</a> → C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>12</sub>O<sub>6</sub> + 6 O<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Biological energy is frequently stored and released by means of redox reactions. Photosynthesis involves the reduction of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> into <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugars</a> and the oxidation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" title="Water (molecule)">water</a> into molecular oxygen. The reverse reaction, respiration, oxidizes sugars to produce carbon dioxide and water. As intermediate steps, the reduced carbon compounds are used to reduce <a href="/wiki/Nicotinamide_adenine_dinucleotide" title="Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide">nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide</a> (NAD<sup>+</sup>) to NADH, which then contributes to the creation of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proton_gradient" title="Proton gradient">proton gradient</a>, which drives the synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">adenosine triphosphate</a> (ATP) and is maintained by the reduction of oxygen.
In animal cells, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitochondria" title="Mitochondria">mitochondria</a> perform similar functions. See the <i><a href="/wiki/Membrane_potential" title="Membrane potential">Membrane potential</a></i> article.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_radical" title="Free radical">Free radical</a> reactions are redox reactions that occur as a part of <a href="/wiki/Homeostasis" title="Homeostasis">homeostasis</a> and killing microorganisms, where an electron detaches from a molecule and then reattaches almost instantaneously. Free radicals are a part of redox molecules and can become harmful to the human body if they do not reattach to the redox molecule or an <a href="/wiki/Antioxidant" title="Antioxidant">antioxidant</a>. Unsatisfied free radicals can spur the mutation of cells they encounter and are, thus, causes of cancer.
</p><p>The term <b>redox state</b> is often used to describe the balance of <a href="/wiki/Glutathione" title="Glutathione">GSH/GSSG</a>, NAD<sup>+</sup>/NADH and <a href="/wiki/Nicotinamide_adenine_dinucleotide_phosphate" title="Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate">NADP<sup>+</sup>/NADPH</a> in a biological system such as a cell or organ. The redox state is reflected in the balance of several sets of metabolites (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactate</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyruvate" title="Pyruvate">pyruvate</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta-hydroxybutyrate" title="Beta-hydroxybutyrate">beta-hydroxybutyrate</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acetoacetate" title="Acetoacetate">acetoacetate</a>), whose interconversion is dependent on these ratios. An abnormal redox state can develop in a variety of deleterious situations, such as <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(medical)" title="Hypoxia (medical)">hypoxia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shock_(circulatory)" title="Shock (circulatory)">shock</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sepsis" title="Sepsis">sepsis</a>. Redox mechanism also control some cellular processes. Redox proteins and their genes must be co-located for redox regulation according to the <a href="/wiki/CoRR_hypothesis" title="CoRR hypothesis">CoRR hypothesis</a> for the function of DNA in mitochondria and chloroplasts.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Redox_cycling">Redox cycling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Redox cycling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A wide variety of <a href="/wiki/Aromaticity" title="Aromaticity">aromatic compounds</a> are <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymatically</a> reduced to form <a href="/wiki/Radical_(chemistry)" title="Radical (chemistry)">free radicals</a> that contain one more electron than their parent compounds. In general, the electron donor is any of a wide variety of flavoenzymes and their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coenzyme" title="Coenzyme">coenzymes</a>. Once formed, these anion free radicals reduce molecular oxygen to <a href="/wiki/Superoxide" title="Superoxide">superoxide</a>, and regenerate the unchanged parent compound. The net reaction is the oxidation of the flavoenzyme's coenzymes and the reduction of molecular oxygen to form superoxide. This catalytic behavior has been described as a <a href="/wiki/Futile_cycle" title="Futile cycle">futile cycle</a> or redox cycling.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Redox_reactions_in_geology">Redox reactions in geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Redox reactions in geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:UraniumMineUtah.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/UraniumMineUtah.JPG/220px-UraniumMineUtah.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/UraniumMineUtah.JPG/330px-UraniumMineUtah.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/UraniumMineUtah.JPG/440px-UraniumMineUtah.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:UraniumMineUtah.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Charles_Steen#Mi_Vida" title="Charles Steen">Mi Vida uranium mine</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Moab,_Utah" title="Moab, Utah">Moab</a>, Utah. The alternating red and white/green bands of <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> correspond to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidized" title="Oxidized">oxidized</a> and reduced conditions in groundwater redox chemistry.</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, redox is important to both the formation of minerals and the mobilization of minerals, and is also important in some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Depositional_environments" title="Depositional environments">depositional environments</a>. In general, the redox state of most rocks can be seen in the color of the rock. The rock forms in oxidizing conditions, giving it a red color. It is then "bleached" to a green—or sometimes white—form when a reducing fluid passes through the rock. The reduced fluid can also carry uranium-bearing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uranium_ore_deposits#Uranium_minerals" title="Uranium ore deposits">minerals</a>. Famous examples of redox conditions affecting geological processes include <a href="/wiki/Uranium_mining" title="Uranium mining">uranium deposits</a> and <a href="/wiki/Navajo_Sandstone#Iron_oxide_concretions_(Moqui_marbles)" title="Navajo Sandstone">Moqui marbles</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Balancing_redox_reactions">Balancing redox reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Balancing redox reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Describing the overall electrochemical reaction for a redox process requires a <i>balancing</i> of the component <a href="/wiki/Half-reaction" title="Half-reaction">half-reactions</a> for oxidation and reduction. In general, for reactions in aqueous solution, this involves adding <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">H<sup>+</sup></a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">OH<sup>−</sup></a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" title="Water (molecule)">H<sub>2</sub>O</a>, and electrons to compensate for the oxidation changes.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acidic_media">Acidic media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Acidic media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In acidic media, H<sup>+</sup> ions and water are added to half-reactions to balance the overall reaction.
</p><p>For instance, when <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a>(II) reacts with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_bismuthate" title="Sodium bismuthate">sodium bismuthate</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd><table>
<tbody><tr>
<td>Unbalanced reaction:
</td>
<td>Mn<sup>2+</sup>(aq) + NaBiO<sub>3</sub>(s) → Bi<sup>3+</sup>(aq) + <span class="chemf nowrap">MnO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> (aq)
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td>Oxidation:
</td>
<td>4 H<sub>2</sub>O(l) + Mn<sup>2+</sup>(aq) → <span class="chemf nowrap">MnO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>(aq) + 8 H<sup>+</sup>(aq) + 5 e<sup>−</sup>
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td>Reduction:
</td>
<td>2 e<sup>−</sup> + 6 H<sup>+</sup> + <span class="chemf nowrap">BiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>(s) → Bi<sup>3+</sup>(aq) + 3 H<sub>2</sub>O(l)
</td></tr></tbody></table></dd></dl>
<p>The reaction is balanced by scaling the two half-cell reactions to involve the same number of electrons (multiplying the oxidation reaction by the number of electrons in the reduction step and vice versa):
</p>
<dl><dd>8 H<sub>2</sub>O(l) + 2 Mn<sup>2+</sup>(aq) → 2 <span class="chemf nowrap">MnO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>(aq) + 16 H<sup>+</sup>(aq) + 10 e<sup>−</sup></dd>
<dd>10 e<sup>−</sup> + 30 H<sup>+</sup> + 5 <span class="chemf nowrap">BiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>(s) → 5 Bi<sup>3+</sup>(aq) + 15 H<sub>2</sub>O(l)</dd></dl>
<p>Adding these two reactions eliminates the electrons terms and yields the balanced reaction:
</p>
<dl><dd>14 H<sup>+</sup>(aq) + 2 Mn<sup>2+</sup>(aq) + 5 NaBiO<sub>3</sub>(s) → 7 H<sub>2</sub>O(l) + 2 <span class="chemf nowrap">MnO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>(aq) + 5 Bi<sup>3+</sup>(aq) + 5 <span class="chemf nowrap">Na<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>(aq)</dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_media">Basic media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Basic media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In basic media, <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">OH<sup>−</sup></a> ions and water are added to half reactions to balance the overall reaction.
</p><p>For example, in the reaction between <a href="/wiki/Potassium_permanganate" title="Potassium permanganate">potassium permanganate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_sulfite" title="Sodium sulfite">sodium sulfite</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd><table>
<tbody><tr>
<td>Unbalanced reaction:
</td>
<td>KMnO<sub>4</sub> + Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O → MnO<sub>2</sub> + Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> + KOH
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td>Reduction:
</td>
<td>3 e<sup>−</sup> + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O + <span class="chemf nowrap">MnO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> → MnO<sub>2</sub> + 4 OH<sup>−</sup>
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td>Oxidation:
</td>
<td>2 OH<sup>−</sup> + <span class="chemf nowrap">SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> → <span class="chemf nowrap">SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> + H<sub>2</sub>O + 2 e<sup>−</sup>
</td></tr></tbody></table></dd></dl>
<p>Balancing the number of electrons in the two half-cell reactions gives:
</p>
<dl><dd>6 e<sup>−</sup> + 4 H<sub>2</sub>O + 2 <span class="chemf nowrap">MnO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> → 2 MnO<sub>2</sub> + 8 OH<sup>−</sup></dd>
<dd>6 OH<sup>−</sup> + 3 <span class="chemf nowrap">SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> → 3 <span class="chemf nowrap">SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> + 3 H<sub>2</sub>O + 6 e<sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<p>Adding these two half-cell reactions together gives the balanced equation:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 KMnO<sub>4</sub> + 3 Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O → 2 MnO<sub>2</sub> + 3 Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> + 2 KOH</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mnemonics">Mnemonics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Mnemonics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The key terms involved in redox are often confusing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robertson_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Robertson-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chemistry_ConceptsAndApplications_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chemistry_ConceptsAndApplications-10">[10]</a></sup> For example, a reagent that is oxidized loses electrons; however, that reagent is referred to as the reducing agent. Likewise, a reagent that is reduced gains electrons and is referred to as the oxidizing agent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rodgers_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rodgers-11">[11]</a></sup> These <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mnemonics" title="Mnemonics">mnemonics</a> are commonly used by students to help memorise the terminology:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zumhahl_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zumhahl-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>"OIL RIG" — <b>o</b>xidation <b>i</b>s <b>l</b>oss of electrons, <b>r</b>eduction <b>i</b>s <b>g</b>ain of electrons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robertson_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Robertson-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chemistry_ConceptsAndApplications_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chemistry_ConceptsAndApplications-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rodgers_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rodgers-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zumhahl_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zumhahl-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>"LEO the lion says GER" — <b>l</b>oss of <b>e</b>lectrons is <b>o</b>xidation, <b>g</b>ain of <b>e</b>lectrons is <b>r</b>eduction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robertson_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Robertson-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chemistry_ConceptsAndApplications_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chemistry_ConceptsAndApplications-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rodgers_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Rodgers-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zumhahl_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Zumhahl-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>"LEORA says GEROA" — <b>l</b>oss of <b>e</b>lectrons is <b>o</b>xidation (<b>r</b>educing <b>a</b>gent), <b>g</b>ain of <b>e</b>lectrons is <b>r</b>eduction (<b>o</b>xidizing <b>a</b>gent).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rodgers_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Rodgers-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>"RED CAT" and "AN OX", or "AnOx RedCat" ("an ox-red cat") — <b>red</b>uction occurs at the <b>cat</b>hode and the <b>an</b>ode is for <b>ox</b>idation.</li>
<li>"RED CAT gains what AN OX loses" – <b>red</b>uction at the <b>cat</b>hode gains (electrons) what <b>an</b>ode <b>ox</b>idation loses (electrons).</li></ul>
