<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Parliamentary Office of Science and Technology</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Houses.of.parliament.overall.arp.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1351" data-file-width="1800" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Houses.of.parliament.overall.arp.jpg/200px-Houses.of.parliament.overall.arp.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Houses.of.parliament.overall.arp.jpg/300px-Houses.of.parliament.overall.arp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Houses.of.parliament.overall.arp.jpg/400px-Houses.of.parliament.overall.arp.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Houses.of.parliament.overall.arp.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Westminster" title="Palace of Westminster">Palace of Westminster</a></div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Parliamentary Office of Science and Technology</b>, (<b>POST</b>) is the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament of the United Kingdom</a>'s in-house source of independent, balanced and accessible analysis of public policy issues related to science and technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-POST_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-POST-1">[1]</a></sup> POST (rather distinctively) serves BOTH Houses of Parliament (the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a> and equally the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>) as a joint establishment, through output that is apolitical and of widely acclaimed value to Parliamentarians of all parties. Thorough quality-control ensures that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" title="Member of Parliament">MPs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peerage" title="Peerage">Peers</a> can have confidence in its analyses should they wish to cite them in debate. These principles are reflected in the structure of <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_Office_of_Science_and_Technology#The_POST_Board" title="Parliamentary Office of Science and Technology">POST’s Board</a> with members from the Commons and Lords together with distinguished scientists and engineers from the wider world.
</p><p><br/>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliamentary_Office_of_Science_and_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="wikitable">
<tbody><tr>
<th>Year</th>
<th>Event
</th></tr>
<tr>
<td>1980s</td>
<td>Office first suggested
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td>1989</td>
<td>POST launched (with charitable funding). Among other support, the UK Centre for Economic and Environmental Development raised funds to provide POST with its first in-house environmental specialist adviser.
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td>1992</td>
<td>POST was adopted as Parliamentary body by both Houses of the UK Parliament and subjected to five year reviews.
<p>The UK Parliament made POST a permanent institution in 2001, after Dr Ian Gibson MP, POST's Chairman, Lord Flowers - vice-Chairman and Professor David Cope, Director, presented evidence to the then House of Commons Administration Committee to support the decision, which was endorsed later by the House of Lords.
</p>
</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Since 1939, a group of MPs and peers interested in science and technology, through the first parliamentary "All Party Group",   the UK Parliamentary and Scientific Committee<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PS_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-PS-2">[2]</a></sup> (P&amp;S), had encouraged UK Parliamentarians to explore the implications of scientific developments for society and public policy. As the UK economy became more dependent on technological progress, and the varied effects of technology (especially on the environment) became more apparent, it was felt that UK Parliament needed its own resources on such issues. Parliamentarians not only required access to knowledge and insights into the implications of technology for their constituents and society, but also needed to exercise their scrutiny functions over UK government legislation and administration. This thinking was also influenced by the fact that specialised parliamentary science and technology organisations already existed overseas.
</p><p>P&amp;S members (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ian_Lloyd_(UK_politician)" title="Ian Lloyd (UK politician)">Sir Ian Lloyd MP</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trevor_Skeet" title="Trevor Skeet">Sir Trevor Skeet MP</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Vaughan_(British_politician)" title="Gerard Vaughan (British politician)">Sir Gerry Vaughan MP</a>, Lords <a href="/wiki/Wayland_Young,_2nd_Baron_Kennet" title="Wayland Young, 2nd Baron Kennet">Kennet</a>, Gregson and <a href="/wiki/Brian_Flowers,_Baron_Flowers" title="Brian Flowers, Baron Flowers">Flowers</a> among others) visited already established organisations in the US, Germany and France, and this reinforced their view that modern Parliaments needed their own ‘intelligence’ on science and technology-related issues. Initially they asked the then <a href="/wiki/Premiership_of_Margaret_Thatcher" title="Premiership of Margaret Thatcher">Thatcher government</a> to fund such services at <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Westminster</a> but were asked first to demonstrate a real need. This led to the P&amp;S creating a charitable foundation to raise funds from P&amp;S members.
</p><p>The parliamentary reaction was positive and led to the appointment of a first Director, Dr Michael Norton.  In 1989, POST was formally established as a charitable foundation, though not an internal part of Parliament.
</p><p>POST had attracted more resources by 1992 and then recruited 3 <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_Office_of_Science_and_Technology#Permanent_staff" title="Parliamentary Office of Science and Technology">specialist science advisers</a> and began its <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_Office_of_Science_and_Technology#Fellows" title="Parliamentary Office of Science and Technology">fellowship</a> programme with the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=UK_research_councils&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="UK research councils (page does not exist)">UK research councils</a>.
</p><p>In 1992 the House of Commons Information Committee, supported by the House of Lords, recommended that Parliament should itself fund POST for 3 years, and a subsequent review in 1995 extended this for a further 5 years. This was the result of POST demonstrating real interest and demand from MPs and peers.
</p><p>POST's financial reliance on donations from bodies external to Parliament, even those as prestigious as the Royal Society, had always slightly compromised the perceived independence of the office.
</p><p>From 1997 the chair of the POST board was appointed by government Whips. Since then, chairs have been <a href="/wiki/Ian_Gibson_(politician)" title="Ian Gibson (politician)">Dr Ian Gibson MP</a> 1997-2001, <a href="/wiki/Phyllis_Starkey" title="Phyllis Starkey">Dr Phyllis Starkey MP</a> 2001-2005, and <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_Office_of_Science_and_Technology#Officers" title="Parliamentary Office of Science and Technology">Dr Ashok Kumar MP</a> 2005-2010. Dr Kumar died shortly before the UK 2010 General Election.  Since then, subject to parliamentary approval, the Chair of POST has been Adam Afiyie, MP.
</p><p>In 1998 <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_Office_of_Science_and_Technology#Officers" title="Parliamentary Office of Science and Technology">Professor David Cope</a> took over as Director of POST. He guided the Office towards the goal of establishing POST as a permanent office of the UK Parliament - and dramatically expanded its staffing, wider links and more general recognition of its role as a special, distinctive, institution that the UK Parliament had agreed, enthusiastically, to endorse.
</p><p>In 2001, both Houses decided that POST should indeed be established as a permanent <a href="/wiki/Bicameralism" title="Bicameralism">bicameral</a> institution, funded exclusively by Parliament itself, through the two Houses, in a ratio of 70%/30%, Commons/Lords.
</p><p>In 2009, POST celebrated its 20th anniversary with, among other events, a special conference, arranged by the Director, Prof Cope, on "Images of the Future".  The keynote participants were the Hon. Bart Gordon, Chair of the US House of Representatives' Committee on Science and Technology and Dr Jim Dator of the University of Hawaii Futures Research Centre, along with many other Members and staff of Parliaments across the world.
</p><p>Because of the enthusiasm of Members of both Houses, POST had enjoyed a unique status within Parliament. It was from its inception attached administratively to the House of Commons COMMISSION (though always with its link to the House of Lords.)
</p><p>This arrangement was intended specifically to distinguish the office and its functions from the Libraries of either House.  These conduct non peer-reviewed "studies", of varying quality.  POST - on the other hand - was expected to provide proactive analysis and options, freed from the immediate pressure of political or administrative expectations, trying to embrace futures thinking, and above all, to subject all its work to the most critical peer review analysis.
</p><p>POST'S Director, Prof David Cope, returned to Cambridge University in 2012 and Dr. Chris Tyler was appointed by parliamentary authorities to a reduced role, which saw the fundamental rationale of POST as a "science and technology assessment" institution increasingly changed to one which more engaged with the ongoing debate on "science policy".
</p><p>POST, from its foundation, had always been an institution "of", rather than "for" - science and technology -in other words - it did not in any way lobby for more public funds for scientific or technological research.  Dr Tyler, however, was closely involved with the "Campaign for Science and Engineering" - a UK body which does so lobby.  On appointment, he did not immediately resign his role in any lobbying body, and this continued seriously to undermine his perceived independence while head of POST.
</p><p>Dr. Tyler left POST in 2017 for a secondary position at University College London.
</p><p>The Acting Directorship of POST was taken over by a longstanding staff member, Dr Chandrika Nath, who was totally immune from any such lobbying role. In 2018, in a reconfirmed acknowledgement of POST's immutable science and technology assessment role, she was appointed Executive Director of the international Scientific Committee on Antarctic Research, the highly prestigious intergovernmental organisation.
</p><p>Dr. Grant Hill-Cawthorne (University of Sydney) became Head of POST from May 2018.  
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Activities">Activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliamentary_Office_of_Science_and_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Science_and_Technology_in_Parliament">Science and Technology in Parliament</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliamentary_Office_of_Science_and_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Science and Technology in Parliament">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most parliamentarians do not have a scientific or technological background but science and technology issues are increasingly integral to public policy. Parliamentarians are bombarded daily with lobbying, public enquiries and media stories about science and technology. These cover diverse areas such as medical advances, environmental issues and global communications. POST helps parliamentarians examine such issues effectively by providing information resources, in depth analysis and impartial advice. POST works closely with a wide range of organisations involved in science and technology, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Select_committee_(Westminster_system)" title="Select committee (Westminster system)">select committees</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/All-Party_Parliamentary_Group" title="All-Party Parliamentary Group">all-party parliamentary groups</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Departments_of_the_United_Kingdom_Government" title="Departments of the United Kingdom Government">government departments</a>, scientific societies, <a href="/wiki/Think_tank" title="Think tank">policy think tanks</a>, business, academia and research funders.
</p>
<h3><span id="POST.27s_aim"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="POST's_aim">POST's aim</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliamentary_Office_of_Science_and_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: POST's aim">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>POST's aim is to inform parliamentary debate through:
</p>
<ul><li>Publishing POSTnotes (short briefing notes) and longer reports. POSTnotes can be downloaded from the publications section of the POST website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Both focus on current science and technology issues and aim to anticipate policy implications for parliamentarians.</li>
<li>Supporting select committees, with informal advice, oral briefings, data analyses, background papers or follow-up research. Committees may approach POST for such advice at any stage in an inquiry.</li>
<li>Informing both Houses on public dialogue activities in science and technology.</li>
<li>Organising discussions to stimulate debate on a wide range of topical issues, from small working groups to large lectures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seminars_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Seminars-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>Horizon-scanning to anticipate issues of science and technology that are likely to impact on policy</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="How_POST_works">How POST works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliamentary_Office_of_Science_and_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: How POST works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A parliamentary board guides POST's choice of subjects. A team of advisers conduct analyses, drawing on a wide range of external expertise. All reports and POSTnotes are externally peer reviewed, and scrutinised by the board before publication.
</p><p>POST's work falls into four topical areas:
</p>
<ul><li>Biological Sciences and Health</li>
<li>Physical Sciences and ICT</li>
<li>Environment and Energy</li>
<li>Social Sciences</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_activities">International activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliamentary_Office_of_Science_and_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: International activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>POST is a member of the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliamentary_Technology_Assessment" title="European Parliamentary Technology Assessment">European Parliamentary Technology Assessment</a> network, which brings together parliamentary organisations throughout Europe sharing information and working on joint projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPTA_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPTA-5">[5]</a></sup> POST also liaises with science and technology organisations across the world.
</p><p>Between November 2005 to 2009, POST, collaborating with four of its sister organisations - at the <a href="/wiki/Folketing" title="Folketing">Danish</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/States-General_of_the_Netherlands" title="States-General of the Netherlands">Dutch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Parliament" title="Flemish Parliament">Flemish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bundestag" title="Bundestag">German</a> Parliaments - provided <a href="/wiki/Technology_assessment" title="Technology assessment">technology assessment</a> services to the <a href="/wiki/Science_and_Technology_Options_Assessment" title="Science and Technology Options Assessment">Science and Technology Options Assessment</a> unit of the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="POST_Africa_Programme">POST Africa Programme</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliamentary_Office_of_Science_and_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: POST Africa Programme">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>From 2001 POST received a growing number of requests for advice from parliamentarians in developing countries. It became clear that a real need existed to strengthen capacity in developing country parliaments. In 2005, POST held discussions on this issue with the Gatsby Foundation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gatsby_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gatsby-6">[6]</a></sup> which led to a special initiative to assist African Parliaments, and other organisations in their countries, in building parliamentary capacity to handle policy issues related to science and technology. At a time when there is growing awareness of the importance of science and technology in decision making, as demonstrated by, for example, the focus on science, technology and innovation at the <a href="/wiki/African_Union" title="African Union">African Union</a> summit meeting in January 2007, this programme continues to contribute towards the overall objective of ensuring that parliaments have the capacity to scrutinise decision making processes and act as the national fora for discussion and debate on the broad implications of issues with a basis in science and technology. By sharing information and best practice with overseas parliaments and assemblies, the programme supports one of the primary objectives of the House: to promote public knowledge and understanding of the work and role of Parliament through the provision of information and access.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_POST_Board">The POST Board</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliamentary_Office_of_Science_and_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The POST Board">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>(Appointed 2010)
</p><p>The POST Board oversees POST's objectives, outputs and future work programme. It meets quarterly. The Board comprises:
</p>
<ul><li>14 parliamentarians drawn from the House of Commons (10) and the House of Lords (4), roughly reflecting the balance of parties in Parliament.</li>
<li>Leading non-parliamentarians from the science and technology community.</li>
<li>Representatives of the House of Lords and the Department of Information Services of the House of Commons.</li></ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Permanent_staff">Permanent staff</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliamentary_Office_of_Science_and_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Permanent staff">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fellows">Fellows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parliamentary_Office_of_Science_and_Technology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Fellows">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>POST runs formal fellowship schemes with scientific societies and research councils, whereby PhD students can spend three months working at POST through an extension of their maintenance grants. These include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Arts_and_Humanities_Research_Council" title="Arts and Humanities Research Council">Arts and Humanities Research Council</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Biotechnology_and_Biological_Sciences_Research_Council" title="Biotechnology and Biological Sciences Research Council">Biotechnology and Biological Sciences Research Council</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/British_Ecological_Society" title="British Ecological Society">British Ecological Society</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/British_Psychological_Society" title="British Psychological Society">British Psychological Society</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Economic_and_Social_Research_Council" title="Economic and Social Research Council">Economic and Social Research Council</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Engineering_and_Physical_Sciences_Research_Council" title="Engineering and Physical Sciences Research Council">Engineering and Physical Sciences Research Council</a></li>
<li>The Institute of Food Science and Technology<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IFST_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-IFST-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Physics" title="Institute of Physics">Institute of Physics</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_Research_Council_(UK)" title="Medical Research Council (UK)">Medical Research Council</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Natural_Environment_Research_Council" title="Natural Environment Research Council">Natural Environment Research Council</a></li>
<li>Nuffield Foundation Flowers Fellowship</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_Chemistry" title="Royal Society of Chemistry">Royal Society of Chemistry</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Science_%26_Technology_Facilities_Council" title="Science &amp; Technology Facilities Council">Science &amp; Technology Facilities Council</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wellcome_Trust" title="Wellcome Trust">Wellcome Trust Ethics and Society</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wellcome_Trust" title="Wellcome Trust">Wellcome Trust Medical History and Humanities</a></li></ul>
<p>The Following two organisations collaborate to offer an annual Fellowship in memory of the late Chemical Engineer and Parliamentarian <a href="/wiki/Ashok_Kumar_(British_politician)" title="Ashok Kumar (British politician)">Ashok Kumar MP</a>.  This Fellowship enables an Engineering or Science PhD student to spend three months working at POST and get a better understanding of how parliament works. By 2017 the 6th Ashok Kumar Fellow had been appointed to work with POST she was a postgraduate engineering student, Erin Johnson, from Imperial College, London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Institution_of_Chemical_Engineers" title="Institution of Chemical Engineers">Institution of Chemical Engineers</a> (IChemE)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northeast_of_England_Process_Industry_Cluster" title="Northeast of England Process Industry Cluster">Northeast of England Process Industry Cluster</a> (NEPIC)</li></ul>
