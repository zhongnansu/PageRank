<p><b>Population density</b> (in <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>: <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Stock_(disambiguation)" title="Stock (disambiguation)">standing stock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Standing_crop" title="Standing crop">standing crop</a>) is a measurement of <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">population</a> per unit area or unit volume; it is a quantity of type <a href="/wiki/Number_density" title="Number density">number density</a>. It is frequently applied to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Living_organisms" title="Living organisms">living organisms</a>, and most of the time to <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a>. It is a key geographical term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-about-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_population_densities">Biological population densities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_density&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biological population densities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Population density is population divided by total land area or water volume, as appropriate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-about-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Low densities may cause an <a href="/wiki/Extinction_vortex" title="Extinction vortex">extinction vortex</a> and lead to further reduced fertility. This is called the <a href="/wiki/Allee_effect" title="Allee effect">Allee effect</a> after the scientist who identified it. Examples of the causes in low population densities include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Increased problems with locating sexual mates</li>
<li>Increased <a href="/wiki/Inbreeding" title="Inbreeding">inbreeding</a></li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Monaco_by_night.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Monaco_by_night.JPG/220px-Monaco_by_night.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Monaco_by_night.JPG/330px-Monaco_by_night.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Monaco_by_night.JPG/440px-Monaco_by_night.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Monaco_by_night.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Monaco" title="Monaco">Monaco</a> in <a href="/wiki/Southern_Europe" title="Southern Europe">Southern Europe</a>, currently holds the record for being the most densely populated nation in the world.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TariatLandscape.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1135" data-file-width="1685" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/TariatLandscape.jpg/220px-TariatLandscape.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/TariatLandscape.jpg/330px-TariatLandscape.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/TariatLandscape.jpg/440px-TariatLandscape.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TariatLandscape.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a> is the least densely populated country in the world.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EU_Pop2008_1024.PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1534" data-file-width="1536" height="350" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/EU_Pop2008_1024.PNG/350px-EU_Pop2008_1024.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/EU_Pop2008_1024.PNG/525px-EU_Pop2008_1024.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/EU_Pop2008_1024.PNG/700px-EU_Pop2008_1024.PNG 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EU_Pop2008_1024.PNG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This population <a href="/wiki/Cartogram" title="Cartogram">cartogram</a> of the European Union (2007–2012) uses areas and colors to represent population.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_countries_and_territories_by_population_density" title="List of countries and territories by population density">List of countries and territories by population density</a></div>
<p>For <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a>, population density is the number of people per unit of area, usually quoted per square kilometer or square mile (which may include or exclude, for example, areas of water or glaciers). Commonly this may be calculated for a <a href="/wiki/County" title="County">county</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_cities_by_population_density" title="List of cities by population density">city</a>, <a href="/wiki/Country" title="Country">country</a>, another <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Territory_(country_subdivision)" title="Territory (country subdivision)">territory</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">entire world</a>.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World%27s_population" title="World's population">world's population</a> is around 7,500,000,000<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s total area (including land and water) is 510,000,000 square kilometers (197,000,000 sq. mi.).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Therefore, the worldwide human population density is around 7,500,000,000 ÷ 510,000,000 = 14.7 per km<sup>2</sup> (38 per sq. mi). If only the Earth's land area of 150,000,000  km<sup>2</sup> (58,000,000 sq. mi.) is taken into account, then human population density increases to 50 per km<sup>2</sup> (129 per sq. mile). This includes all continental and island land area, including <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>. If Antarctica is also excluded, then population density rises to over 55 people per km<sup>2</sup> (over 142 per sq. mile).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-about-1">[1]</a></sup> However, over half<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> of the Earth's land mass consists of areas inhospitable to human habitation, such as deserts and high mountains, and population tends to cluster around seaports and fresh-water sources. Thus, this number by itself does not give any helpful measurement of human population density.
</p><p>Several of the most densely populated territories in the world are <a href="/wiki/City-state" title="City-state">city-states</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microstate" title="Microstate">microstates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dependent_territory" title="Dependent territory">dependencies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unpop_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-unpop-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> These territories have a relatively small area and a high <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">urbanization</a> level, with an economically specialized <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">city</a> population drawing also on rural resources outside the area, illustrating the difference between high population density and <a href="/wiki/Human_overpopulation" title="Human overpopulation">overpopulation</a>
</p><p>The potential to maintain the agricultural aspects of deserts is extremely limited as there is not enough precipitation to support a sustainable land. The population in these areas are extremely low. Therefore cities in the Middle East, such as Dubai, have been increasing in population and infrastructure growth at a fast pace. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cities with high population densities are, by some, considered to be overpopulated, though this will depend on factors like quality of housing and infrastructure and access to resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Most of the most densely populated cities are in <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, though <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lagos" title="Lagos">Lagos</a> in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> also fall into this category.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>City population and especially area are, however, heavily dependent on the definition of "urban area" used: densities are almost invariably higher for the central city area than when suburban settlements and the intervening rural areas are included, as in the areas of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urban_agglomeration" title="Urban agglomeration">agglomeration</a> or <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_area" title="Metropolitan area">metropolitan area</a>, the latter sometimes including neighboring cities. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee" title="Milwaukee">Milwaukee</a> has a greater population density when just the inner city is measured, and the surrounding suburbs excluded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>In comparison, based on a world population of seven billion, the world's inhabitants, as a loose crowd taking up ten square feet (one square metre) per person (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jacobs_Method" title="Jacobs Method">Jacobs Method</a>), would occupy a space a little larger than <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a>'s land area.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_methods_of_measurement">Other methods of measurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_density&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other methods of measurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although arithmetic density is the most common way of measuring population density, several other methods have been developed to provide a more accurate measure of population density over a specific area.
</p>
<ul><li><b>Arithmetic density</b>: The total number of people / area of land (measured in square miles or square kilometers)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Physiological_density" title="Physiological density">Physiological density</a></b>: The total population / area of <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable land</a></li>
<li><b>Agricultural density</b>: The total <i>rural</i> population / area of <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable land</a></li>
<li><b>Residential density</b>: The number of people living in an urban area / area of residential land</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Urban_density" title="Urban density">Urban density</a></b>: The number of people inhabiting an urban area / total area of urban land</li>
<li><b>Ecological optimum</b>: The density of population that can be supported by the natural resources</li></ul>
