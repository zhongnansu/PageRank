<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Food additive</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:230px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lec_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1077" data-file-width="794" height="309" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Lec_2.jpg/228px-Lec_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Lec_2.jpg/342px-Lec_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Lec_2.jpg/456px-Lec_2.jpg 2x" width="228"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lec_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The different forms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emulsifier" title="Emulsifier">emulsifier</a> <a href="/wiki/Lecithin" title="Lecithin">lecithin</a> – powder, two different concentration liquids, granular and powder lecithin</div></div></div>
<p><b>Food additives</b> are substances added to <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> to preserve <a href="/wiki/Flavor" title="Flavor">flavor</a> or enhance its taste, appearance, or other qualities. Some additives have been used for centuries; for example, preserving food by <a href="/wiki/Pickling" title="Pickling">pickling</a> (with <a href="/wiki/Vinegar" title="Vinegar">vinegar</a>), <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salting</a>, as with <a href="/wiki/Bacon" title="Bacon">bacon</a>, preserving <a href="/wiki/Candy" title="Candy">sweets</a> or using <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> as with <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wines</a>. With the advent of processed foods in the second half of the twentieth century, many more additives have been introduced, of both natural and artificial origin.  Food additives also include substances that may be introduced to food indirectly (called "indirect additives") in the manufacturing process, through <a href="/wiki/Food_packaging" title="Food packaging">packaging</a>, or during storage or transport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FDAoverview_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FDAoverview-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Numbering">Numbering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_additive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Numbering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To regulate these additives, and inform consumers, each additive is assigned a unique number, termed as "<a href="/wiki/E_number" title="E number">E numbers</a>", which is used in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> for all approved additives.  This numbering scheme has now been adopted and extended by the <i><a href="/wiki/Codex_Alimentarius" title="Codex Alimentarius">Codex Alimentarius</a></i> Commission to internationally identify all additives,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> regardless of whether they are approved for use.
</p><p>E numbers are all prefixed by "<a href="/wiki/E" title="E">E</a>", but countries outside Europe use only the number, whether the additive is approved in Europe or not.
For example, <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a> is written as E260 on products sold in Europe, but is simply known as additive 260 in some countries.  Additive 103, <a href="/wiki/Alkannin" title="Alkannin">alkannin</a>, is not approved for use in Europe so does not have an E number, although it is approved for use in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>  and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>.  Since 1987, Australia has had an approved system of labelling for additives in packaged foods. Each food additive has to be named or numbered. The numbers are the same as in Europe, but without the prefix "E".
</p><p>The United States <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> (FDA) lists these items as "<a href="/wiki/Generally_recognized_as_safe" title="Generally recognized as safe">generally recognized as safe</a>" (GRAS);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> they are listed under both their <a href="/wiki/Chemical_Abstracts_Service" title="Chemical Abstracts Service">Chemical Abstracts Service</a> number and FDA regulation under the United States <a href="/wiki/Code_of_Federal_Regulations" title="Code of Federal Regulations">Code of Federal Regulations</a>.
</p>
<ul><li>See <a href="/wiki/List_of_food_additives" title="List of food additives">list of food additives</a> for a complete list of all the names.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Categories">Categories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_additive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Categories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Food additives can be divided into several groups, although there is some overlap because some additives exert more than one effect.  For example, salt is both a preservative as well as a flavor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FDAoverview_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-FDAoverview-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Acidulents </dt>
<dd><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Acidulent&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Acidulent (page does not exist)">Acidulents</a> confer sour or acid taste. Common acidulents include <a href="/wiki/Vinegar" title="Vinegar">vinegar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid" title="Citric acid">citric acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tartaric_acid" title="Tartaric acid">tartaric acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malic_acid" title="Malic acid">malic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fumaric_acid" title="Fumaric acid">fumaric acid</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactic acid</a>.</dd>
<dt>Acidity regulators </dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Acidity_regulator" title="Acidity regulator">Acidity regulators</a> are used for controlling the <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> of foods for stability or to affect activity of enzymes.</dd>
<dt>Anticaking agents </dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Anticaking_agent" title="Anticaking agent">Anticaking agents</a> keep powders such as milk powder from caking or sticking.</dd>
<dt>Antifoaming and foaming agents </dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Defoamer" title="Defoamer">Antifoaming agents</a> reduce or prevent foaming in foods.  Foaming agents do the reverse.</dd>
<dt>Antioxidants </dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Antioxidant" title="Antioxidant">Antioxidants</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_C" title="Vitamin C">vitamin C</a> are preservatives by inhibiting the degradation of food by <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>.</dd>
<dt>Bulking agents </dt>
<dd>Bulking agents such as <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a> are additives that increase the bulk of a food without affecting its taste.</dd>
<dt>Food coloring </dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Food_coloring" title="Food coloring">Colorings</a> are added to food to replace colors lost during preparation or to make food look more attractive.</dd>
<dt>Fortifying agents</dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Vitamin" title="Vitamin">Vitamins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mineral_(nutrient)" title="Mineral (nutrient)">minerals</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dietary_supplement" title="Dietary supplement">dietary supplements</a> to increase the nutritional value</dd>
<dt>Color retention agents </dt>
<dd>In contrast to colorings, <a href="/wiki/Colour_retention_agent" title="Colour retention agent">color retention agents</a> are used to preserve a food's existing color.</dd>
<dt>Emulsifiers </dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Emulsion#Emulsifiers" title="Emulsion">Emulsifiers</a> allow water and oils to remain mixed together in an <a href="/wiki/Emulsion" title="Emulsion">emulsion</a>, as in <a href="/wiki/Mayonnaise" title="Mayonnaise">mayonnaise</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice_cream" title="Ice cream">ice cream</a>, and homogenized <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">milk</a>.</dd>
<dt>Flavors </dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Flavor" title="Flavor">Flavors</a> are additives that give food a particular taste or smell, and may be derived from natural ingredients or created artificially.</dd>
<dt>Flavor enhancers </dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Flavor" title="Flavor">Flavor enhancers</a> enhance a food's existing flavors.  A popular example is <a href="/wiki/Monosodium_glutamate" title="Monosodium glutamate">monosodium glutamate</a>.  Some flavor enhancers have their own flavors that are independent of the food.</dd>
<dt>Flour treatment agents </dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Flour_treatment_agent" title="Flour treatment agent">Flour treatment agents</a> are added to <a href="/wiki/Flour" title="Flour">flour</a> to improve its color or its use in <a href="/wiki/Baking" title="Baking">baking</a>.</dd>
<dt>Glazing agents</dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Glazing_agent" title="Glazing agent">Glazing agents</a> provide a shiny appearance or protective coating to foods.</dd>
<dt>Humectants </dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Humectant" title="Humectant">Humectants</a> prevent foods from drying out.</dd>
<dt>Tracer gas</dt>
<dd><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tracer_gas" title="Tracer gas">Tracer gas</a> allow for package integrity testing to prevent foods from being exposed to atmosphere, thus guaranteeing shelf life.</dd>
<dt>Preservatives </dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Preservative" title="Preservative">Preservatives</a> prevent or inhibit spoilage of food due to <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a>.</dd>
<dt>Stabilizers </dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Stabilizer_(chemistry)" title="Stabilizer (chemistry)">Stabilizers</a>, thickeners and gelling agents, like <a href="/wiki/Agar" title="Agar">agar</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pectin" title="Pectin">pectin</a> (used in <a href="/wiki/Fruit_preserves" title="Fruit preserves">jam</a> for example) give foods a firmer texture.  While they are not true emulsifiers, they help to stabilize <a href="/wiki/Emulsion" title="Emulsion">emulsions</a>.</dd>
<dt>Sweeteners </dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Sugar_substitute" title="Sugar substitute">Sweeteners</a> are added to foods for flavoring. Sweeteners other than <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a> are added to keep the <a href="/wiki/Food_energy" title="Food energy">food energy</a> (<a href="/wiki/Calorie" title="Calorie">calories</a>) low, or because they have beneficial effects regarding <a href="/wiki/Diabetes_mellitus" title="Diabetes mellitus">diabetes mellitus</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dental_caries" title="Dental caries">tooth decay</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Diarrhea" title="Diarrhea">diarrhea</a>.</dd>
<dt>Thickeners </dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Thickening_agent" title="Thickening agent">Thickening agents</a> are substances which, when added to the mixture, increase its <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> without substantially modifying its other properties.</dd>
<dt>Packaging </dt>
<dd><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bisphenols" title="Bisphenols">Bisphenols</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phthalates" title="Phthalates">phthalates</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Perfluorinated_compound" title="Perfluorinated compound">perfluoroalkyl chemicals</a> (PFCs) are indirect additives used in manufacturing or packaging.  In July 2018 the American Academy of Pediatrics called for more careful study of those three substances, along with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrates" title="Nitrates">nitrates</a> and food coloring, as they might harm children during development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_and_regulation">Safety and regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_additive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Safety and regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With the increasing use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Processed_food" title="Processed food">processed foods</a> since the 19th century, food additives are more widely used. Many countries regulate their use. For example, <a href="/wiki/Boric_acid" title="Boric acid">boric acid</a> was widely used as a food preservative from the 1870s to the 1920s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NutInj_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-NutInj-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> but was banned after <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> due to its toxicity, as demonstrated in animal and human studies. During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the urgent need for cheap, available food preservatives led to it being used again, but it was finally banned in the 1950s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NutInj_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-NutInj-7">[7]</a></sup>  Such cases led to a general mistrust of food additives, and an application of the <a href="/wiki/Precautionary_principle" title="Precautionary principle">precautionary principle</a> led to the conclusion that only additives that are known to be safe should be used in foods.  In the United States, this led to the adoption of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delaney_clause" title="Delaney clause">Delaney clause</a>, an amendment to the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Food,_Drug,_and_Cosmetic_Act" title="Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act">Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act</a> of 1938, stating that no <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogenic</a> substances may be used as food additives.  However, after the banning of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_cyclamate" title="Sodium cyclamate">cyclamates</a> in the United States and Britain in 1969, <a href="/wiki/Saccharin" title="Saccharin">saccharin</a>, the only remaining legal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artificial_sweetener" title="Artificial sweetener">artificial sweetener</a> at the time, was found to cause cancer in rats.  Widespread public outcry in the United States, partly communicated to Congress by postage-paid postcards supplied in the packaging of <a href="/wiki/Sweetened_beverage" title="Sweetened beverage">sweetened soft drinks</a>, led to the retention of saccharin, despite its violation of the Delaney clause.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> However, in 2000, saccharin was found to be carcinogenic in rats due only to their unique urine chemistry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whysner-1996_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whysner-1996-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dybing-2002_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dybing-2002-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hyperactivity">Hyperactivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_additive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hyperactivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Periodically, concerns have been expressed about a linkage between additives and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperactivity" title="Hyperactivity">hyperactivity</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> however "no clear evidence of ADHD was
provided".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2007, <a href="/wiki/Food_Standards_Australia_New_Zealand" title="Food Standards Australia New Zealand">Food Standards Australia New Zealand</a> published an official shoppers' guidance with which the concerns of food additives and their labeling are mediated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crso_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-crso-14">[14]</a></sup>  In the EU it can take 10 years or more to obtain approval for a new food additive. This includes five years of safety testing, followed by two years for evaluation by the <a href="/wiki/European_Food_Safety_Authority" title="European Food Safety Authority">European Food Safety Authority</a> and another three years before the additive receives an EU-wide approval for use in every country in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Apart from testing and analyzing food products during the whole production process to ensure safety and compliance with regulatory standards, <a href="/wiki/Trading_Standards" title="Trading Standards">Trading Standards</a> officers (in the UK) protect the public from any illegal use or potentially dangerous mis-use of food additives by performing random testing of food products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>There has been significant controversy associated with the risks and benefits of food additives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Natural additives may be similarly harmful or be the cause of allergic reactions in certain individuals. For example, <a href="/wiki/Safrole" title="Safrole">safrole</a> was used to flavor <a href="/wiki/Root_beer" title="Root beer">root beer</a> until it was shown to be carcinogenic.  Due to the application of the Delaney clause, it may not be added to foods, even though it occurs naturally in <a href="/wiki/Sassafras" title="Sassafras">sassafras</a> and sweet <a href="/wiki/Basil" title="Basil">basil</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Micronutrients">Micronutrients</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_additive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Micronutrients">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A subset of food additives, <a href="/wiki/Micronutrient" title="Micronutrient">micronutrients</a> added in <a href="/wiki/Food_fortification" title="Food fortification">food fortification</a> processes preserve <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nutrient_value&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nutrient value (page does not exist)">nutrient value</a> by providing <a href="/wiki/Vitamin" title="Vitamin">vitamins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mineral_(nutrient)" title="Mineral (nutrient)">minerals</a> to  foods such as flour, cereal, margarine and milk which normally would not retain such high levels. <a href="/wiki/Preservative" title="Preservative">Preservatives</a> also reduce spoilage from sources such as air, bacteria, fungi, and yeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Standardization_of_its_derived_products">Standardization of its derived products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_additive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Standardization of its derived products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">ISO</a> has published a series of standards regarding the topic and these standards are covered by ICS 67.220.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scfa_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-scfa-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Science">Science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Food_additive&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many food additives absorb radiation in the <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> or <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible</a> regions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_spectrum" title="Light spectrum">spectrum</a>. This <a href="/wiki/Absorbance" title="Absorbance">absorbance</a> can be used to determine the concentration of an additive in a sample using external calibration. However, additives may occur together and the absorbance by one could interfere with the absorbance of another. A prior separation stage is necessary and the additives are first separated by <a href="/wiki/High-performance_liquid_chromatography" title="High-performance liquid chromatography">high-performance liquid chromatography</a> (HPLC) and then determined on-line using a UV and/or visible light detector.
</p>
