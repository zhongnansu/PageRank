<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Methanation</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>Methanation is the conversion of CO<sub>x</sub> to methane CH<sub>4</sub> through <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenation</a>. The <b>methanation</b> reactions of COx were first discovered by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Sabatier_(chemist)" title="Paul Sabatier (chemist)">Sabatier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Senderens" title="Jean-Baptiste Senderens">Senderens</a> in 1902.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>CO<sub><i>x</i></sub> methanation has many practical applications. It is a means of carbon oxide removal from process gases and is also being discussed as an alternative to <a href="/wiki/PROX" title="PROX">PROX</a> in fuel processors for mobile fuel cell applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Methanation as a means of producing synthetic natural gas has been considered since the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup> More recently it has been considered as a way to store energy produced from solar or wind power. This would also allow it to serve as a potential <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture</a> mechanism.
</p>

<p>The methanation reactions are classified as <a href="/wiki/Exothermic_reaction" title="Exothermic reaction">exothermic</a> and their energy of formations are listed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>There is disagreement on whether the CO<sub>2</sub> methanation occurs by first associatively adsorbing an <a href="/wiki/Adatom" title="Adatom">adatom</a> hydrogen  and forming oxygen intermediates before hydrogenation or dissociating and forming a carbonyl before being hydrogenated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup> CO methanation is believed to be methanated through a dissociative mechanism where the carbon oxygen bond is broken before hydrogenation with an associative mechanism only being observed at high H<sub>2</sub> concentrations.
</p><p>Methanation reaction over different carried metal <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalysts</a> including Ni,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Ru <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and Rh<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> has been widely investigated for the production of CH<sub>4</sub> from syngas and other power to gas initiatives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup> Nickel is the most widely used catalyst due to its high selectivity and low cost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_applications">Industrial applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Industrial applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Creation_of_synthetic_natural_gas">Creation of synthetic natural gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Creation of synthetic natural gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Methanation is an important step in the creation of synthetic or <a href="/wiki/Substitute_natural_gas" title="Substitute natural gas">substitute natural gas</a> (SNG).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Coal or wood undergo gasification which creates a producer gas that must undergo methanation in order to produce a usable gas that just needs to undergo a final purification step.
</p><p>The first commercial synthetic gas plant opened in 1984 and is the <a href="/wiki/Dakota_Gasification_Company" title="Dakota Gasification Company">Great Plains Synfuel</a> plant in Beulah, North Dakota.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup> It is still operational and produces 1500 MW worth of SNG using coal as the carbon source. In the years since its opening, other commercial facilities have been opened using other carbons sources such as wood chips.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In France, the AFUL Chantrerie, located in Nantes, started in November 2017 the demonstrator MINERVE. This methanation unit of 14 Nm3 / day was carried out by Top Industrie, with the support of Leaf. This installation is used to feed a CNG station and to inject methane into the natural gas boiler. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ammonia_synthesis">Ammonia synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ammonia synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In ammonia production CO and CO<sub>2</sub> are considered <a href="/wiki/Catalyst_poisoning" title="Catalyst poisoning">poisons</a> to most commonly used catalysts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Methanation catalysts are added after several hydrogen producing steps to prevent carbon oxide buildup in the ammonia synthesis loop as methane does not have similar adverse effects on ammonia synthesis rates.
</p>
