<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Horticulture</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:20150319-OC-LSC-0826_(16868799861).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3280" data-file-width="4928" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/20150319-OC-LSC-0826_%2816868799861%29.jpg/220px-20150319-OC-LSC-0826_%2816868799861%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/20150319-OC-LSC-0826_%2816868799861%29.jpg/330px-20150319-OC-LSC-0826_%2816868799861%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/20150319-OC-LSC-0826_%2816868799861%29.jpg/440px-20150319-OC-LSC-0826_%2816868799861%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:20150319-OC-LSC-0826_(16868799861).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A horticulture student tending to plants in a <a href="/wiki/Market_garden" title="Market garden">garden</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lawrenceville,_Georgia" title="Lawrenceville, Georgia">Lawrenceville, Georgia</a>, 2015</div></div></div>
<p><b>Horticulture</b> has been defined as the culture of plants for food, comfort and beauty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> A more precise definition can be given as "The cultivation, processing, and sale of fruits, nuts, vegetables, ornamental plants, and flowers as well as many additional services".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It also includes <a href="/wiki/Conservation_biology" title="Conservation biology">plant conservation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Restoration_ecology" title="Restoration ecology">landscape restoration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soil_management" title="Soil management">soil management</a>, landscape and <a href="/wiki/Garden_design" title="Garden design">garden design</a>, construction, and maintenance, and <a href="/wiki/Arboriculture" title="Arboriculture">arboriculture</a>. In contrast to <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, horticulture does not include <a href="/wiki/Intensive_crop_farming" title="Intensive crop farming">large-scale crop production</a> or <a href="/wiki/Animal_husbandry" title="Animal husbandry">animal husbandry</a>.
</p><p>Horticulturists apply their knowledge, skills, and technologies used to grow intensively produced plants for human food and non-food uses and for personal or social needs. Their work involves <a href="/wiki/Plant_propagation" title="Plant propagation">plant propagation</a> and cultivation with the aim of improving plant growth, yields, quality, nutritional value, and resistance to insects, diseases, and environmental stresses. They work as gardeners, growers, therapists, designers, and technical advisors in the food and non-food sectors of horticulture. Horticulture even refers to the growing of plants in a field or garden.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horticulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <i>horticulture</i> is modeled after <i><a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a></i>, and comes from the Greek <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%87%CF%8C%CF%81%CF%84%CE%BF%CF%82" title="wikt:χόρτος">χόρτος</a></i>, which in Latin became <i>hortus</i> "garden"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and <i>cultūra</i> "cultivation", from <i>cultus</i>, the perfect passive <a href="/wiki/Participle" title="Participle">participle</a> of the verb <i>colō</i> "I cultivate".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <i>Hortus</i> is <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">cognate</a> with the native English word <i>yard</i> (in the meaning of land associated with a building) and also the borrowed word <i>garden</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scope">Scope</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horticulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Scope">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The major areas of Horticulture include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Arboriculture" title="Arboriculture">Arboriculture</a> is the study of, and the selection, plant, care, and removal of, individual trees, shrubs, vines, and other perennial woody plants.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Turf_management" title="Turf management">Turf management</a> includes all aspects of the production and maintenance of turf grass for sports, leisure use or amenity use.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Floriculture" title="Floriculture">Floriculture</a> includes the production and marketing of floral crops.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Landscape_architecture" title="Landscape architecture">Landscape horticulture</a> includes the production, marketing and maintenance of landscape plants.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Olericulture" title="Olericulture">Olericulture</a> includes the production and marketing of <a href="/wiki/Vegetable" title="Vegetable">vegetables</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pomology" title="Pomology">Pomology</a> includes the production and marketing of <a href="/wiki/Pome" title="Pome">pome</a> fruits.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Viticulture" title="Viticulture">Viticulture</a> includes the production and marketing of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grapes" title="Grapes">grapes</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Oenology" title="Oenology">Oenology</a> includes all aspects of <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Winemaking" title="Winemaking">winemaking</a>.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postharvest_physiology" title="Postharvest physiology">Postharvest physiology</a> involves maintaining the quality of and preventing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leaf_decomposition" title="Leaf decomposition">spoilage</a> of plants and animals.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horticulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Horticulture has a very long history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  The study and science of horticulture dates all the way back to the times of <a href="/wiki/Cyrus_the_Great" title="Cyrus the Great">Cyrus the Great</a> of ancient Persia, and has been going on ever since, with present-day horticulturists such as Freeman S. Howlett and <a href="/wiki/Luther_Burbank" title="Luther Burbank">Luther Burbank</a>. The practice of horticulture can be retraced for many thousands of years. The cultivation of taro and yam in Papua New Guinea dates back to at least 6950–6440 cal BP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The origins of horticulture lie in the transition of human communities from nomadic <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunter-gatherers</a> to sedentary or semi-sedentary horticultural communities, cultivating a variety of crops on a small scale around their dwellings or in specialized plots visited occasionally during migrations from one area to the next (such as the "<a href="/wiki/Milpa" title="Milpa">milpa</a>" or maize field of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesoamerican" title="Mesoamerican">Mesoamerican</a> cultures).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In the Pre-Columbian Amazon Rainforest, natives are believed to have used <a href="/wiki/Biochar#History" title="Biochar">biochar</a> to enhance soil productivity by smoldering plant waste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> European settlers called it <a href="/wiki/Terra_preta" title="Terra preta"><i>Terra Preta de Indio</i></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In forest areas such horticulture is often carried out in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swidden" title="Swidden">swiddens</a> ("<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slash_and_burn" title="Slash and burn">slash and burn</a>" areas).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> A characteristic of horticultural communities is that useful trees are often to be found planted around communities or specially retained from the natural ecosystem.
</p><p>Horticulture primarily differs from agriculture in two ways. First, it generally encompasses a smaller scale of cultivation, using small plots of mixed crops rather than large fields of single crops. Secondly, horticultural cultivations generally include a wide variety of crops, even including fruit trees with ground crops. Agricultural cultivations however as a rule focus on one primary crop. In pre-contact North America the semi-sedentary horticultural communities of the Eastern Woodlands (growing maize, squash and sunflower) contrasted markedly with the mobile <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherer" title="Hunter-gatherer">hunter-gatherer</a> communities of the <a href="/wiki/Plains_Indians" title="Plains Indians">Plains people</a>. In Central America, <a href="/wiki/Maya_civilization" title="Maya civilization">Maya</a> horticulture involved augmentation of the forest with useful trees such as <a href="/wiki/Papaya" title="Papaya">papaya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avocado" title="Avocado">avocado</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theobroma_cacao" title="Theobroma cacao">cacao</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ceiba" title="Ceiba">ceiba</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sapodilla" title="Sapodilla">sapodilla</a>. In the cornfields, multiple crops were grown such as beans (using cornstalks as supports), squash, pumpkins and chilli peppers, in some cultures tended mainly or exclusively by women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organizations">Organizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horticulture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Organizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since 1804 <a href="/wiki/Royal_Horticultural_Society" title="Royal Horticultural Society">The Royal Horticultural Society</a>, UK charity leads on the encouragement and improvement of the science, art and practice of horticulture in all its branches<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and shares this knowledge through its community and learning programmes, world class gardens and shows. The oldest Horticultural society in the world, founded in 1768, is the Ancient Society of York Florists. They still have four shows a year in York, UK.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>The professional body representing horticulturists in Great Britain and Ireland is the Institute of Horticulture (IOH).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>  Also, the IOH has an international branch for members outside of these islands.
</p><p>The International Society for Horticultural Science<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> promotes and encourages research and education in all branches of horticultural science.
</p><p>The American Society of Horticultural Science<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>  promotes and encourages research and education in all branches of horticultural science in the Americas.
</p><p>The Australian Society of Horticultural Science was established in 1990 as a professional society for the promotion and enhancement of Australian horticultural science and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horticultural_industry" title="Horticultural industry">industry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>The National Junior Horticultural Association (NJHA) was established in 1934 and was the first organisation in the world dedicated solely to youth and horticulture. NJHA programs are designed to help young people obtain a basic understanding of, and develop skills in, the ever-expanding art and science of horticulture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>The New Zealand Horticulture Institute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Global Horticulture Initiative (GlobalHort) fosters more efficient and effective partnerships and collective action among different stakeholders in horticulture. The organisation has a special focus on horticulture for development (H4D), i.e. using horticulture to reduce poverty and improve nutrition worldwide. To be efficient, GlobalHort is organised in a consortium of national and international organisations to collaborate in research, training, and technology-generating activities designed to meet mutually-agreed-upon objectives. GlobalHort is a not-for-profit organisation registered in Belgium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
