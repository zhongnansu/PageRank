<p>The <b>IPCC Third Assessment Report</b> (<b>TAR</b>), <i>Climate Change 2001</i>, is an assessment of available scientific and socio-economic information on climate change by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPCC" title="IPCC">IPCC</a>.  The IPCC was  established in 1988 by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Environment_Programme" title="United Nations Environment Programme">United Nations Environment Programme</a> (UNEP) and the UN's <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> (WMO) "... to assess scientific, technical and socio-economic information relevant for the understanding of climate change, its potential impacts and options for adaptation and mitigation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The Third Assessment Report is the third of a series of assessments; it has been superseded by the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">IPCC Fourth Assessment Report</a> (AR4), released in 2007.
</p><p>Statements of the IPCC or information from the TAR are often used as a reference showing a <a href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_climate_change" title="Scientific opinion on climate change">scientific consensus</a> on the subject of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, although <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientists_opposing_the_mainstream_scientific_assessment_of_global_warming" title="Scientists opposing the mainstream scientific assessment of global warming">a small minority of scientists</a> take issue with the UN assessments (see also <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_controversy" title="Global warming controversy">Global warming controversy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_global_warming" title="Politics of global warming">Politics of global warming</a>).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Working_groups">Working groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IPCC_Third_Assessment_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Working groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The IPCC is organized as three <a href="/wiki/Working_group" title="Working group">working groups</a> (WG) and a <a href="/wiki/Task_force" title="Task force">task force</a> <a class="external autonumber" href="https://web.archive.org/web/19960101/http://www.ipcc.ch/about/about.htm" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>:
</p>
<ul><li>WGI: Scientific aspects of climate (see <a href="#CITEREFIPCC_TAR_WG12001">IPCC TAR WG1 2001</a>).</li>
<li>WGII: Vulnerability, consequences, and options (see <a href="#CITEREFIPCC_TAR_WG22001">IPCC TAR WG2 2001</a>).</li>
<li>WGIII: Limitation and mitigation options (see <a href="#CITEREFIPCC_TAR_WG32001">IPCC TAR WG3 2001</a>).</li>
<li>Task Force: National Greenhouse Gas Inventories Programme <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.ipcc-nggip.iges.or.jp" rel="nofollow">[2]</a></li></ul>
<p>WG I covers the same areas as the <i>Second Assessment Report</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SAR_(IPCC)" title="SAR (IPCC)">SAR</a>) of 1995, but WG II &amp; III cover slightly different areas in the TAR.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conclusions">Conclusions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IPCC_Third_Assessment_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Conclusions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Working_Group_I">Working Group I</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IPCC_Third_Assessment_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Working Group I">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The key conclusions of <b>Working Group I</b> (<a class="external text" href="http://www.grida.no/climate/ipcc_tar/wg1/005.htm" rel="nofollow">The Scientific Basis, Summary for Policymakers</a>, in <a href="#CITEREFIPCC_AR3_WG12001">IPCC AR3 WG1 2001</a>) were:
</p>
<ol><li>An increasing body of observations gives a collective picture of a warming world and other changes in the climate system (The global <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_temperature_record" title="Instrumental temperature record">average surface temperature</a> has increased over the 20th century by about 0.6 °C; Temperatures have risen during the past four decades in the lowest 8 kilometres of the atmosphere; Snow cover and ice extent have decreased)</li>
<li>Emissions of greenhouse gases and aerosols due to human activities continue to alter the atmosphere in ways that are expected to affect the climate (Anthropogenic <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosols</a> (i.e., human emitted aerosols) are short-lived and mostly produce negative radiative forcing; Natural factors have made small contributions to <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a> over the past century)</li>
<li>Confidence in the ability of models to project future climate has increased (Complex physically based climate models are required to provide detailed estimates of feedbacks and of regional features. Such models cannot yet simulate all aspects of climate (e.g., they still cannot account fully for the observed trend in the surface-troposphere temperature difference since 1979) and there are particular uncertainties associated with clouds and their interaction with radiation and aerosols. Nevertheless, confidence in the ability of these models to provide useful projections of future climate has improved due to their demonstrated performance on a range of space and time-scales <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.grida.no/climate/ipcc_tar/wg1/007.htm" rel="nofollow">[3]</a>.)</li>
<li>There is new and stronger evidence that most of the warming observed over the last 50 years is attributable to human activities</li>
<li>Human influences will continue to change atmospheric composition throughout the 21st century</li>
<li>Global average temperature and sea level are projected to rise under all IPCC <a href="/wiki/Special_Report_on_Emissions_Scenarios" title="Special Report on Emissions Scenarios">SRES scenarios</a>.</li></ol>
<p>The TAR estimate for the <a href="/wiki/Climate_sensitivity" title="Climate sensitivity">climate sensitivity</a> is 1.5 to 4.5 °C; and the average surface temperature is projected to increase by 1.4 to 5.8 Celsius degrees over the period 1990 to 2100, and the sea level is projected to rise by 0.1 to 0.9 metres over the same period.  The wide range in projections is based upon several different scenarios that assume different levels of future CO<sub>2</sub> emissions (see the section below on <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Third_Assessment_Report#Projections_in_the_TAR" title="IPCC Third Assessment Report">Projections in the TAR</a>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis_Report">Synthesis Report</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IPCC_Third_Assessment_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Synthesis Report">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The TAR Synthesis Report includes a summary of the TAR's main findings and uncertainties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tar_robust_findings_and_key_uncertainties_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-tar_robust_findings_and_key_uncertainties-2">[2]</a></sup>  "Robust findings" of the TAR include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tar_robust_findings_and_key_uncertainties_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-tar_robust_findings_and_key_uncertainties-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Observed warming of the Earth's surface, attribution of observed warming to human activities, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_climate_model#Projections_of_future_climate_change" title="Global climate model">projected increases</a> in future global mean temperature, rising <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_sea_level_rise" title="Current sea level rise">sea levels</a>, and increased frequency of <a href="/wiki/Heat_wave" title="Heat wave">heat waves</a>.</li>
<li>Future warming will have both beneficial and adverse effects, but for higher levels of warming, adverse effects will predominate.</li>
<li>Developing countries and poor persons are most vulnerable to climate change.</li></ul>
<p>"Key uncertainties" in the TAR include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tar_robust_findings_and_key_uncertainties_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-tar_robust_findings_and_key_uncertainties-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Estimated climate <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">forcings</a> of natural climatic factors and anthropogenic <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosols</a> (e.g., sulfate, which is produced when sulfur-rich coal is burnt), future changes in <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> (GHG) emissions, and the role of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_feedback" title="Climate change feedback">climate feedbacks</a>, which may amplify or reduce the magnitude of future climate change;</li>
<li>Assigning probabilities to projections of changes in sea level and temperature, as well as uncertainties related to regional projections of climate change.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Projections">Projections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IPCC_Third_Assessment_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Projections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Projections are used in the TAR as a guide to the possible future effects of climate change, e.g., changes in global mean temperature and sea level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tar_projected_climate_change_impacts_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-tar_projected_climate_change_impacts-3">[3]</a></sup> In the TAR, the word "projection" is favoured over "prediction".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  This is because many future changes related to climate are highly uncertain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>  For example, climate change projections are affected by highly uncertain changes in future GHG emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The TAR projects impacts according to possible future changes in global mean temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  Other projections are based on <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_scenario" title="Climate change scenario">scenarios</a> that the IPCC has developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tar_projected_climate_change_impacts_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-tar_projected_climate_change_impacts-3">[3]</a></sup>  In 2000,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>  the IPCC published 40 different scenarios<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SRES_Approach_to_Scenario_Development_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-SRES_Approach_to_Scenario_Development-9">[9]</a></sup> (the "<a href="/wiki/Special_Report_on_Emissions_Scenarios" title="Special Report on Emissions Scenarios">SRES" scenarios</a>) which contain estimates of future changes in anthropogenic emissions of greenhouse gases and aerosols. The SRES scenarios project a wide range of possible changes in future social and economic development,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SRES_Approach_to_Scenario_Development_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-SRES_Approach_to_Scenario_Development-9">[9]</a></sup> and projected climate change impacts vary according to the scenario considered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SRES_impacts_figure_SPM-3_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-SRES_impacts_figure_SPM-3-10">[10]</a></sup>  The IPCC has not assigned probabilities to the 40 SRES scenarios.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SRES_impacts_figure_SPM-3_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-SRES_impacts_figure_SPM-3-10">[10]</a></sup>  Some authors<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>  have argued that some SRES scenarios are more likely to occur than others.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_opinion">Scientific opinion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IPCC_Third_Assessment_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Scientific opinion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_climate_change" title="Scientific opinion on climate change">scientific opinion on climate change</a></div>
<p>The IPCC is backed by the scientific community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-royal_society_misleading_arguments_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-royal_society_misleading_arguments-13">[13]</a></sup>  For example, a joint statement of support was issued in May 2001 by the science academies of Australia, Belgium, Brazil, Canada, the Caribbean, China, France, Germany, India, Indonesia, Ireland, Italy, Malaysia, New Zealand, Sweden and the UK. It states: "We recognise the IPCC as the world's most reliable source of information on climate change and its causes, and we endorse its method of achieving consensus."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-royal_society_misleading_arguments_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-royal_society_misleading_arguments-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2001, the executive branch of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_federal_government" title="US federal government">US federal government</a> asked the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_National_Research_Council" title="US National Research Council">US National Research Council</a> (US NRC, 2001)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>  to produce an assessment of climate change science. Part of the assessment by US NRC (2001)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-us_nrc_2001_summary_p1_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-us_nrc_2001_summary_p1-15">[15]</a></sup>  looks at the report produced by Working Group I (WG I) in the TAR. Working Group I's contribution to the TAR assesses the physical scientific aspects of the climate system and climate change. US NRC (2001)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-us_nrc_2001_summary_p1_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-us_nrc_2001_summary_p1-15">[15]</a></sup> generally agrees with findings of the WG I report, for example, US NRC (2001)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> state that "[the] IPCC’s conclusion that most of the observed warming of the last 50 years is likely to have been due to the increase in greenhouse gas concentrations accurately reflects the current thinking of the scientific community on this issue."
</p><p>US NRC (2001)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-us_nrc_2001_ipcc_assessment_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-us_nrc_2001_ipcc_assessment-17">[17]</a></sup> emphasise the need for governments to have a good understanding of uncertainties in climate change science. The example cited by US NRC (2001)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-us_nrc_2001_ipcc_assessment_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-us_nrc_2001_ipcc_assessment-17">[17]</a></sup>  is the uncertainty over future changes in GHG emissions, which may be less or more than that projected by the TAR. US NRC (2001)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-us_nrc_2001_ipcc_assessment_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-us_nrc_2001_ipcc_assessment-17">[17]</a></sup>  also state:
</p>
<blockquote><p>The most valuable contribution U.S. scientists can make is to continually question basic assumptions and conclusions, promote clear and careful appraisal and presentation of the uncertainties about climate change as well as those areas in which science is leading to robust conclusions, and work toward a significant improvement in the ability to project the future.</p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IPCC_Third_Assessment_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_scientists_opposing_the_mainstream_scientific_assessment_of_global_warming" title="List of scientists opposing the mainstream scientific assessment of global warming">List of scientists opposing the mainstream scientific assessment of global warming</a></div>
<p>The IPCC's work has been the subject of criticism,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>  and there are small number of climate scientists<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_climate_change_sceptics_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-king_climate_change_sceptics-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>  who disagree with aspects of the IPCC's work. Perhaps the best known is <a href="/wiki/Richard_Lindzen" title="Richard Lindzen">Richard Lindzen</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-king_climate_change_sceptics_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-king_climate_change_sceptics-19">[19]</a></sup> professor of <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a>. 
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="IPCC_process">IPCC process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=IPCC_Third_Assessment_Report&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: IPCC process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A report by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK_Parliament" title="UK Parliament">UK Parliament</a>'s <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> Economic Affairs <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Select_committees_of_the_Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Select committees of the Parliament of the United Kingdom">Select Committee</a> (EAC, 2005)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eac_ipcc_criticism_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-eac_ipcc_criticism-21">[21]</a></sup> contains criticisms of the IPCC's work, including the "<a href="/wiki/Special_Report_on_Emissions_Scenarios" title="Special Report on Emissions Scenarios">SRES</a>" greenhouse gas emissions scenarios,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> which are used in the TAR. The Economic Affairs Select Committee is made up of members of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>, which scrutinizes and votes on government legislation. One of the criticisms made by the EAC (2005)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> is an apparent inconsistency between the Working Group II Summary for Policymakers and a statement made in the full WGII report: "The IPCC Summary for policy makers says that economic studies underestimate damage, whereas the chapter says the direction of the bias is not known."
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK_Government" title="UK Government">UK Government</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> issued a response to the report by EAC (2005).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eac_ipcc_criticism_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-eac_ipcc_criticism-21">[21]</a></sup> The UK Government acknowledged the discrepancy between the WGII SPM and full WGII report which was referred to by the EAC (2005),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> but remained generally supportive of the IPCC's procedures. The UK Government rebutted a number of other criticisms of the TAR which were made by the EAC (2005).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
