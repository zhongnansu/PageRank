<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Ice cap</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about the glacial feature. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Icecap_(disambiguation)" title="Icecap (disambiguation)">Icecap (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vatnaj%C3%B6kull.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="477" data-file-width="619" height="231" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Vatnaj%C3%B6kull.jpeg/300px-Vatnaj%C3%B6kull.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Vatnaj%C3%B6kull.jpeg/450px-Vatnaj%C3%B6kull.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Vatnaj%C3%B6kull.jpeg/600px-Vatnaj%C3%B6kull.jpeg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vatnaj%C3%B6kull.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Vatnaj%C3%B6kull" title="Vatnajökull">Vatnajökull</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a></div></div></div>
<p>An <b> ice cap</b> is a mass of <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a> that covers less than 50,000 km<sup>2</sup> of land area (usually covering a highland area). Larger ice masses covering more than 50,000 km<sup>2</sup> are termed <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DougBenn_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-DougBenn-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bennett_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GreveBlatter2009_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-GreveBlatter2009-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ice caps are not constrained by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topographical" title="Topographical">topographical</a> features (i.e., they will lie over the top of mountains). By contrast, ice masses of similar size that <i>are</i> constrained by topographical features are known as <a href="/wiki/Ice_field" title="Ice field">ice fields</a>. The <i>dome</i> of an ice cap is usually centred on the highest point of a <a href="/wiki/Massif" title="Massif">massif</a>. Ice flows away from this high point (the <a href="/wiki/Ice_divide" title="Ice divide">ice divide</a>) towards the ice cap's periphery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DougBenn_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-DougBenn-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GreveBlatter2009_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-GreveBlatter2009-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ice caps have significant effects on the <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">geomorphology</a> of the area that they occupy. Plastic moulding, gouging and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glacial" title="Glacial">glacial</a> <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosional</a> features become present upon the glacier's retreat. Many lakes, such as the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> in North America, as well as numerous valleys have been formed by glacial action over hundreds of thousands of years.
</p><p>On Earth, there are about 30 million km<sup>3</sup> of total ice mass.  The average temperature of an ice mass ranges between −20 °C and −30 °C. The core of an ice cap exhibits a constant temperature that ranges between −15 °C and −20 °C.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>A high-latitude region covered in ice, though strictly not an ice cap (since they exceed the maximum area specified in the definition above), are called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_ice_caps" title="Polar ice caps">polar ice caps</a>; the usage of this designation is widespread in the mass media<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and arguably recognized by experts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Vatnaj%C3%B6kull" title="Vatnajökull">Vatnajökull</a> is an example of an ice cap in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
