<p><b>Alessandro Giuseppe Antonio Anastasio Volta</b> (<small>Italian: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Italian" title="Help:IPA/Italian">[alesˈsandro ˈvɔlta]</a></span>; 18 February 1745 – 5 March 1827) was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_people" title="Italian people">Italian</a> <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">chemist</a>, and a pioneer of <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Power_(physics)" title="Power (physics)">power</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wmnqsy_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-wmnqsy-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nxukip_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-nxukip-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> who is credited as the inventor of the <a href="/wiki/Electric_battery" title="Electric battery">electric battery</a> and the discoverer of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>. He invented the <a href="/wiki/Voltaic_pile" title="Voltaic pile">Voltaic pile</a> in 1799, and reported the results of his experiments in 1800 in a two-part letter to the President of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RoyalSocRef_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-RoyalSocRef-6">[6]</a></sup> With this invention Volta proved that electricity could be generated chemically and debunked the prevalent theory that electricity was generated solely by living beings. Volta's invention sparked a great amount of scientific excitement and led others to conduct similar experiments which eventually led to the development of the field of <a href="/wiki/Electrochemistry" title="Electrochemistry">electrochemistry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RoyalSocRef_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-RoyalSocRef-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Alessandro Volta also drew admiration from <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon Bonaparte</a> for his invention, and was invited to the <a href="/wiki/Institut_de_France" title="Institut de France">Institute of France</a> to demonstrate his invention to the members of the Institute. Volta enjoyed a certain amount of closeness with the Emperor throughout his life and he was conferred numerous honours by him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArchiveBook_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-ArchiveBook-1">[1]</a></sup> Alessandro Volta held the chair of experimental physics at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pavia" title="University of Pavia">University of Pavia</a> for nearly 40 years and was widely idolised by his students.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArchiveBook_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-ArchiveBook-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Despite his professional success, Volta tended to be a person inclined towards domestic life and this was more apparent in his later years. At this time he tended to live secluded from public life and more for the sake of his family until his eventual death in 1827 from a series of illnesses which began in 1823.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArchiveBook_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-ArchiveBook-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">SI</a> unit of <a href="/wiki/Electric_potential" title="Electric potential">electric potential</a> is named in his honour as the <a href="/wiki/Volt" title="Volt">volt</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_works">Early life and works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alessandro_Volta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Volta was born in <a href="/wiki/Como" title="Como">Como</a>, a town in present-day northern <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, on 18 February 1745. In 1794, Volta married an aristocratic lady also from Como, Teresa Peregrini, with whom he raised three sons: Zanino, Flaminio, and Luigi. His father, Filippo Volta, was of noble lineage. His mother, Donna Maddalena, came from the family of the Inzaghis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alessandrovolta.info_February_18_2015c_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alessandrovolta.info_February_18_2015c-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1774, he became a professor of physics at the Royal School in Como. A year later, he improved and popularised the <a href="/wiki/Electrophorus" title="Electrophorus">electrophorus</a>, a device that produced <a href="/wiki/Static_electricity" title="Static electricity">static electricity</a>. His promotion of it was so extensive that he is often credited with its invention, even though a machine operating on the same principle was described in 1762 by the Swedish experimenter <a href="/wiki/Johan_Wilcke" title="Johan Wilcke">Johan Wilcke</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1777, he travelled through Switzerland. There he befriended <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H._B._de_Saussure" title="H. B. de Saussure">H. B. de Saussure</a>.
</p><p>In the years between 1776 and 1778, Volta studied the <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> of gases. He researched and discovered <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> after reading a paper by <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a> of the United States on "flammable air". In November 1776, he found methane at <a href="/wiki/Lake_Maggiore" title="Lake Maggiore">Lake Maggiore</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and by 1778 he managed to isolate methane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> He devised experiments such as the <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">ignition</a> of methane by an electric <a href="/wiki/Electrostatic_discharge" title="Electrostatic discharge">spark</a> in a closed vessel.
</p><p>Volta also studied what we now call electrical <a href="/wiki/Capacitance" title="Capacitance">capacitance</a>, developing separate means to study both electrical potential (<i>V</i> ) and charge (<i>Q</i> ), and discovering that for a given object, they are proportional.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williams2014_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Williams2014-12">[12]</a></sup> This is called Volta's Law of Capacitance, and it was for this work the unit of electrical potential has been named the <a href="/wiki/Volt" title="Volt">volt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williams2014_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Williams2014-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1779 he became a professor of experimental physics at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pavia" title="University of Pavia">University of Pavia</a>, a chair that he occupied for almost 40 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArchiveBook_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-ArchiveBook-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Volta_and_Galvani">Volta and Galvani</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alessandro_Volta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Volta and Galvani">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Luigi_Galvani,_oil-painting.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="875" height="302" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Luigi_Galvani%2C_oil-painting.jpg/220px-Luigi_Galvani%2C_oil-painting.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Luigi_Galvani%2C_oil-painting.jpg/330px-Luigi_Galvani%2C_oil-painting.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Luigi_Galvani%2C_oil-painting.jpg/440px-Luigi_Galvani%2C_oil-painting.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Luigi_Galvani,_oil-painting.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Luigi_Galvani" title="Luigi Galvani">Luigi Galvani</a>, Volta's rival</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Luigi_Galvani" title="Luigi Galvani">Luigi Galvani</a>, an Italian physicist, discovered something he named, "animal electricity" when two different metals were connected in series with a frog's leg and to one another. Volta realised that the frog's leg served as both a conductor of electricity (what we would now call an <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolyte</a>) and as a detector of electricity. He replaced the frog's leg with brine-soaked paper, and detected the flow of electricity by other means familiar to him from his previous studies.
</p><p>In this way he discovered the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrochemical_series" title="Electrochemical series">electrochemical series</a>, and the law that the <a href="/wiki/Electromotive_force" title="Electromotive force">electromotive force</a> (emf) of a <a href="/wiki/Galvanic_cell" title="Galvanic cell">galvanic cell</a>, consisting of a pair of metal <a href="/wiki/Electrode" title="Electrode">electrodes</a> separated by electrolyte, is the difference between their two electrode potentials (thus, two identical electrodes and a common electrolyte give zero net emf). This may be called Volta's Law of the electrochemical series.
</p><p>In 1800, as the result of a professional disagreement over the galvanic response advocated by Galvani, Volta invented the <a href="/wiki/Voltaic_pile" title="Voltaic pile">voltaic pile</a>, an early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">electric battery</a>, which produced a steady electric current.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Routledge_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Routledge-13">[13]</a></sup> Volta had determined that the most effective pair of dissimilar metals to produce electricity was <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> and <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>. Initially he experimented with individual cells in series, each cell being a wine goblet filled with <a href="/wiki/Brine" title="Brine">brine</a> into which the two dissimilar electrodes were dipped. The voltaic pile replaced the goblets with cardboard soaked in brine.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_battery">Early battery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alessandro_Volta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early battery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Voltaic_pile.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="550" height="245" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Voltaic_pile.svg/300px-Voltaic_pile.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Voltaic_pile.svg/450px-Voltaic_pile.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Voltaic_pile.svg/600px-Voltaic_pile.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Voltaic_pile.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Voltaic_pile" title="Voltaic pile">voltaic pile</a></div></div></div>
<p>In announcing his discovery of the voltaic pile, Volta paid tribute to the influences of <a href="/wiki/William_Nicholson_(chemist)" title="William Nicholson (chemist)">William Nicholson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tiberius_Cavallo" title="Tiberius Cavallo">Tiberius Cavallo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Bennet" title="Abraham Bennet">Abraham Bennet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>The battery made by Volta is credited as one of the first electrochemical cells. It consists of two electrodes: one made of <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>, the other of <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolyte</a> is either <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> mixed with water or a form of saltwater <a href="/wiki/Brine" title="Brine">brine</a>. The electrolyte exists in the form 2H<sup>+</sup> and SO<sub>4</sub><sup>2−</sup>. The zinc, which is higher in the <a href="/wiki/Standard_electrode_potential_(data_page)" title="Standard electrode potential (data page)">electrochemical series</a> than both copper and hydrogen, reacts with the negatively charged sulfate (SO<sub>4</sub><sup>2−</sup>). The positively charged hydrogen ions (<a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a>) capture <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> from the copper, forming bubbles of hydrogen gas, H<sub>2</sub>. This makes the zinc rod the negative electrode and the copper rod the positive electrode.
</p><p>Thus, there are two terminals, and an <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric current</a> will flow if they are connected. The <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reactions</a> in this voltaic cell are as follows:
</p>
<dl><dd>Zinc:
<dl><dd>Zn <big><big>→</big></big> Zn<sup>2+</sup> + 2e<sup>−</sup></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<dl><dd>Sulfuric acid:
<dl><dd>2H<sup>+</sup> + 2e<sup>−</sup> <big><big>→</big></big> H<sub>2</sub></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p>The copper does not react, but rather it functions as an electrode for the electric current.
</p><p>However, this cell also has some disadvantages. It is unsafe to handle, since sulfuric acid, even if diluted, can be hazardous. Also, the power of the cell diminishes over time because the hydrogen gas is not released. Instead, it accumulates on the surface of the copper electrode and forms a barrier between the metal and the electrolyte solution.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Last_years_and_retirement">Last years and retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alessandro_Volta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Last years and retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Painting_of_Volta_by_Bertini_(photo).jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="1200" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Painting_of_Volta_by_Bertini_%28photo%29.jpeg/300px-Painting_of_Volta_by_Bertini_%28photo%29.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Painting_of_Volta_by_Bertini_%28photo%29.jpeg/450px-Painting_of_Volta_by_Bertini_%28photo%29.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Painting_of_Volta_by_Bertini_%28photo%29.jpeg/600px-Painting_of_Volta_by_Bertini_%28photo%29.jpeg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Painting_of_Volta_by_Bertini_(photo).jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Volta explains the principle of the <i>"electric column"</i> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_Bonaparte" title="Napoleon Bonaparte">Napoleon</a> in 1801</div></div></div>
<p>In 1809 Volta became associated member of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Netherlands_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences" title="Royal Netherlands Academy of Arts and Sciences">Royal Institute of the Netherlands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In honour of his work, Volta was made a count by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_Bonaparte" title="Napoleon Bonaparte">Napoleon Bonaparte</a> in 1810.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wmnqsy_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-wmnqsy-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Volta retired in 1819 to his estate in Camnago, a <a href="/wiki/Frazione" title="Frazione">frazione</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Como,_Italy" title="Como, Italy">Como, Italy</a>, now named "Camnago Volta" in his honour. He died there on 5 March 1827, just after his 82nd birthday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Volta's remains were buried in Camnago Volta.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alessandro_Volta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Volta's legacy is celebrated by the <a href="/wiki/Tempio_Voltiano" title="Tempio Voltiano">Tempio Voltiano</a> memorial located in the public gardens by the lake. There is also a museum which has been built in his honour, which exhibits some of the equipment that Volta used to conduct experiments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Nearby stands the <a href="/wiki/Villa_Olmo" title="Villa Olmo">Villa Olmo</a>, which houses the Voltian Foundation, an organization promoting scientific activities. Volta carried out his experimental studies and produced his first inventions near Como.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>His image was depicted on the <a href="/wiki/Italian_lira" title="Italian lira">Italian 10,000 lira note</a> (1990-1997) along with a sketch of his voltaic pile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Desmond2016_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Desmond2016-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_beliefs">Religious beliefs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alessandro_Volta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Religious beliefs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Volta was raised as a Catholic and for all of his life continued to maintain his belief.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Because he was not ordained a clergyman as his family expected, he was sometimes accused of being irreligious and some people have speculated about his possible unbelief, stressing that "he did not join the Church",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> or that he virtually "ignored the church's call".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>  Nevertheless, he cast out doubts in a declaration of faith in which he said:
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>I do not understand how anyone can doubt the sincerity and constancy of my attachment to the religion which I profess, the Roman, Catholic and Apostolic religion in which I was born and brought up, and of which I have always made confession, externally and internally. I have, indeed, and only too often, failed in the performance of those good works which are the mark of a Catholic Christian, and I have been guilty of many sins: but through the special mercy of God I have never, as far as I know, wavered in my faith... In this faith I recognise a pure gift of God, a supernatural grace; but I have not neglected those human means which confirm belief, and overthrow the doubts which at times arise. I studied attentively the grounds and basis of religion, the works of apologists and assailants, the reasons for and against, and I can say that the result of such study is to clothe religion with such a degree of probability, even for the merely natural reason, that every spirit unperverted by sin and passion, every naturally noble spirit must love and accept it. May this confession which has been asked from me and which I willingly give, written and subscribed by my own hand, with authority to show it to whomsoever you will, for I am not ashamed of the Gospel, may it produce some good fruit!<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
