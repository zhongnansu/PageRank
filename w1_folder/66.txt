<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Caulking,_USAF.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2245" data-file-width="3000" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Caulking%2C_USAF.jpg/220px-Caulking%2C_USAF.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Caulking%2C_USAF.jpg/330px-Caulking%2C_USAF.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Caulking%2C_USAF.jpg/440px-Caulking%2C_USAF.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Caulking,_USAF.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Man applying caulk to <a href="/wiki/Baseboard" title="Baseboard">baseboard</a></div></div></div>
<p><b>Caulk</b> or (less frequently) <b>caulking</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is a material used to <a href="/wiki/Seal_(mechanical)" title="Seal (mechanical)">seal</a> joints or seams against leakage in various structures and piping. 
</p><p>The oldest form of caulk consisted of fibrous materials driven into the wedge-shaped seams between boards on <a href="/wiki/Boat_building#Wood" title="Boat building">wooden boats or ships</a>. <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">Cast iron</a> <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_sewer" title="Sanitary sewer">sewerage</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_pipe" title="Water pipe">pipe</a> were formerly caulked in a similar way. Riveted seams in <a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">ships</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boiler" title="Boiler">boilers</a> were formerly sealed by hammering the <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Modern caulking compounds are flexible sealing compounds used to close up gaps in <a href="/wiki/Building" title="Building">buildings</a> and other structures against <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dust" title="Dust">dust</a>, <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, or as a component in <a href="/wiki/Firestop" title="Firestop">firestopping</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Tunnel" title="Tunnel">tunnelling</a> industry, caulking is the sealing of joints in segmental <a href="/wiki/Precast_concrete" title="Precast concrete">precast concrete</a> <a href="/wiki/Tunnel" title="Tunnel">tunnels</a>, commonly by using <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_uses">Historical uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caulk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historical uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wooden_shipbuilding">Wooden shipbuilding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caulk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Wooden shipbuilding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Caulked_hull_timbers,_Spry,_Blists_Hill.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Caulked_hull_timbers%2C_Spry%2C_Blists_Hill.jpg/220px-Caulked_hull_timbers%2C_Spry%2C_Blists_Hill.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Caulked_hull_timbers%2C_Spry%2C_Blists_Hill.jpg/330px-Caulked_hull_timbers%2C_Spry%2C_Blists_Hill.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Caulked_hull_timbers%2C_Spry%2C_Blists_Hill.jpg/440px-Caulked_hull_timbers%2C_Spry%2C_Blists_Hill.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Caulked_hull_timbers,_Spry,_Blists_Hill.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dried-out caulking on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Severn_trow" title="Severn trow">Severn trow</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Severn_trow,_Spry&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Severn trow, Spry (page does not exist)"><i>Spry</i></a>, now displayed on shore</div></div></div>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MaryRose-caulking_tools2.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2397" data-file-width="3027" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/MaryRose-caulking_tools2.JPG/220px-MaryRose-caulking_tools2.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/MaryRose-caulking_tools2.JPG/330px-MaryRose-caulking_tools2.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/MaryRose-caulking_tools2.JPG/440px-MaryRose-caulking_tools2.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MaryRose-caulking_tools2.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A caulking mallet, tar pot and a piece of petrified tar found on board the 16th century <a href="/wiki/Carrack" title="Carrack">carrack</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Mary_Rose" title="Mary Rose">Mary Rose</a></i></div></div></div>
<p>Traditional caulking (also spelled calking) on wooden vessels uses <a href="/wiki/Fiber" title="Fiber">fibers</a> of cotton and <a href="/wiki/Oakum" title="Oakum">oakum</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hemp" title="Hemp">hemp</a> fiber soaked in pine <a href="/wiki/Tar" title="Tar">tar</a>). These fibers are driven into the wedge-shaped seam between planks, with a caulking mallet and a broad <a href="/wiki/Chisel" title="Chisel">chisel</a>-like tool called a caulking iron. The caulking is then covered over with a <a href="/wiki/Putty" title="Putty">putty</a>, in the case of hull seams, or else in deck seams with melted pine <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(resin)" title="Pitch (resin)">pitch</a>, in a process referred to as paying, or "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calefaction" title="Calefaction">calefaction</a>"
</p><p>Modern marine sealants are frequently used now in place of the pitch, or even to supplant the <a href="/wiki/Oakum" title="Oakum">oakum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a> itself.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iron_or_steel_shipbuilding">Iron or steel shipbuilding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caulk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Iron or steel shipbuilding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In riveted steel or iron <a href="/wiki/Shipbuilding" title="Shipbuilding">ship construction</a>, caulking was a process of rendering seams watertight by driving a thick, blunt <a href="/wiki/Chisel" title="Chisel">chisel</a>-like tool into the plating adjacent to the seam. This had the effect of displacing the metal into a close fit with the adjoining piece.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Originally done by hand much like wooden vessel caulking, pneumatic tools were later employed. With the advent of electric-arc <a href="/wiki/Welding" title="Welding">welding</a> for ship construction, steel ship caulking was rendered obsolete.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Boilermaking">Boilermaking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caulk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Boilermaking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Caulking of iron and steel, of the same type described above for ship's hulls, was also used by <a href="/wiki/Boilermaker" title="Boilermaker">boilermakers</a> in the era of riveted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steam_boiler" title="Steam boiler">boilers</a> to make the joints watertight and steamtight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_use_in_construction">Modern use in construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caulk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Modern use in construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To caulk in the building trades is to close up joints and gaps in buildings. Caulking provides thermal insulation, controls water penetration and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noise_mitigation" title="Noise mitigation">reduces noise transmission</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Caulking_underneath_bathroom_sink.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="441" data-file-width="320" height="234" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Caulking_underneath_bathroom_sink.jpg/170px-Caulking_underneath_bathroom_sink.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Caulking_underneath_bathroom_sink.jpg/255px-Caulking_underneath_bathroom_sink.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0b/Caulking_underneath_bathroom_sink.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Caulking_underneath_bathroom_sink.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Silicone-based caulk on this upturned bathroom sink will spread smoothly, sealing the gap, when the sink is turned over and installed.</div></div></div>
<p>This is mostly done with ready-mixed construction chemicals sold as caulk such as <a href="/wiki/Silicone" title="Silicone">silicone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polyurethane" title="Polyurethane">polyurethane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polysulfide" title="Polysulfide">polysulfide</a>, sylil-terminated-polyether or polyurethane and acrylic sealant.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Application">Application<span id="Caulking_gun"></span><span id="Caulk_cartridge"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caulk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Application">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Caulking.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2025" data-file-width="2558" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Caulking.jpg/220px-Caulking.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Caulking.jpg/330px-Caulking.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Caulking.jpg/440px-Caulking.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Caulking.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Silicone" title="Silicone">Silicone</a> caulking extruded from a caulking gun</div></div></div>
<p>For bulk use, caulk is generally distributed in disposable cartridges, which are rigid cylindrical cardboard or plastic tubes with an applicator tip at one end, and a movable plunger at the far end. These are used in caulking guns, which typically have a trigger connected to a rod which pushes the plunger, and has a <a href="/wiki/Ratchet_(device)" title="Ratchet (device)">ratchet</a> to prevent backlash. The push rod may also be actuated by a motor or by compressed air. Similar mechanisms are used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grease_gun" title="Grease gun">grease guns</a>.
</p><p>For smaller applications, caulk may be distributed in squeezable <a href="/wiki/Tube_(container)" title="Tube (container)">tubes</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Backer_rod">Backer rod</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caulk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Backer rod">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Backer rod, also called backer material or back-up rod, is a flexible <a href="/wiki/Foam" title="Foam">foam</a> product used behind caulking to increase elasticity, reduce consumption, force the caulking into contact with the sides of the joint creating a better bond, determine the thickness of the caulking, and define the cross-section hour-glass shape of the caulk. The backer rod also acts as a bond breaker to keep the caulking from sticking to the bottom of the opening—called a three-sided bond—with the caulk only adhering to the sides of the opening in an hour-glass shape it can flex more easily and is less likely to tear. Backer rods can also be used to reduce consumption of the caulking by filling part of the joints.
</p><p>Backer rod is often round and rope-like in shape but many special types and shaped such as square, rectangular and D-shapes are available. High temperature backer rods are available. It is available in different diameters and firmness, and in polyethylene and polyurethane materials. Backer rod also comes in open-cell and closed-cell types of foam.
</p><p>Open-cell foam is porous so it will let gasses through which could otherwise cause blistering of the sealant. Also, open-cell backer rod allows air to get to the back side of the sealant which accelerates curing when used with air-cured sealants such as silicone. Open-cell rod is more compressible than closed-cell foam and should be 25% larger diameter than the joint.
</p><p>Closed-cell foam does not absorb water and is impermeable.  Closed-cell rods are less compressible and <i>should not</i> be compressed more than 25%. Closed-cell rod will also lose firmness and out-gas if damaged during installation or overcompressed or at sharp bends. The gasses cannot pass through this backer rod and can deform, weaken, and even cause holes (leaks) in the sealant as it escapes. Out-gassing is the reason open-cell backer rod was developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_efficiency">Energy efficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caulk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Energy efficiency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Consumer_Federation_of_America" title="Consumer Federation of America">Consumer Federation of America</a>, sealing unwanted leaks around homes is an excellent way to cut home energy costs and decrease the household carbon footprint.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Also, sealing cracks and crevices around homes puts less strain on home appliances and can save time, money and hassle by preventing major repairs. Additionally, increasing the lifetime of homes and appliances also puts less waste and pollution into landfills.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Preventing_infestation">Preventing infestation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caulk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Preventing infestation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sealing cracks and crevices prevents ingress by rodents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
