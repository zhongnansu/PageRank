<p>The <b>Sleipner gas field</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_gas_field" title="Natural gas field">natural gas field</a> in the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>, about 250 kilometres (160 mi) west of <a href="/wiki/Stavanger" title="Stavanger">Stavanger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>.  Two parts of the field are in production, Sleipner West (proven in 1974), and Sleipner East (1981).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oil-gas1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-oil-gas1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oil-gas2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-oil-gas2-2">[2]</a></sup>  The field produces natural gas and light oil condensates from sandstone structures about 2,500 metres (8,200 ft) below sea level. It is operated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statoil" title="Statoil">Statoil</a>.  The field is named after the steed <a href="/wiki/Sleipnir" title="Sleipnir">Sleipnir</a> in <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">Norse mythology</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reserves_and_production">Reserves and production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sleipner_gas_field&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Reserves and production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of the end of 2005, the estimated recoverable reserves for the Sleipner West and East fields were 51.6 billion cubic metres of natural gas, 4.4 million tonnes of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_gas_liquids" title="Natural gas liquids">natural gas liquids</a>, and 3.9 million cubic metres of condensates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oil-gas1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-oil-gas1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oil-gas2_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-oil-gas2-2">[2]</a></sup>  Daily production of the field in 2008 was 300 thousand barrels per day (48<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>3</sup> m<sup>3</sup>/d) equivalents, 36 million cubic metres of natural gas per day, and 14,000 cubic metres of condensate per day.
</p><p>Sleipner field consists of four platforms.  The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sleipner_A_offshore_platform" title="Sleipner A offshore platform">Sleipner A platform</a> is located on the Sleipner East and the Sleipner B platform is located on the Sleipner West. Sleipner B is operated remotely from the Sleipner A via an <a href="/wiki/Umbilical_cable" title="Umbilical cable">umbilical cable</a>.  The Sleipner T <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> treatment platform is linked physically to the Sleipner A platform by a bridge and to the Sleipner B wellhead platform by 12.5-kilometre (7.8 mi) carbon dioxide flow line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters181209_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-reuters181209-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ieaghg_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ieaghg-4">[4]</a></sup>  The Sleipner Riser platform, serving the <a href="/wiki/Langeled_pipeline" title="Langeled pipeline">Langeled</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zeepipe" title="Zeepipe">Zeepipe</a> pipelines, is located on the Sleipner East field.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_capture_and_storage_project">Carbon capture and storage project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sleipner_gas_field&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Carbon capture and storage project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Sleipner West field is used as a facility for <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a> (CCS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnn_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-cnn-6">[6]</a></sup>  It is the world's first offshore CCS plant, operative since October 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-statoil_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-statoil-7">[7]</a></sup>  The Sleipner West field has up to 9% CO2, Norway only allows 2.5% CO2 before imposing production penalties which may have been NOK 1 million/day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Carbon dioxide is treated on the Sleipner T treatment platform. After that carbon dioxide is transported to the Sleipner A platform where it is injected into the Utsira formation through a dedicated well ca. 1000 meters under the seabed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ieaghg_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ieaghg-4">[4]</a></sup>  Norwegian natural gas pipelines' operator <a href="/wiki/Gassco" title="Gassco">Gassco</a> had proposed to build a 240-kilometre (150 mi) carbon dioxide pipeline from <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A5rst%C3%B8" title="Kårstø">Kårstø</a> to transport carbon dioxide from the now decommissioned <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A5rst%C3%B8_Power_Station" title="Kårstø Power Station">Kårstø power station</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bloomberg191109_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-bloomberg191109-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
