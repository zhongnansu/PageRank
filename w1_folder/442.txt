<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Blackdamp</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Blackdamp</b> (also known as <b>stythe</b> or <b>choke damp</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Asphyxiant_gas" title="Asphyxiant gas">asphyxiant</a>, reducing the available <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> content of air to a level incapable of sustaining human or animal life.  It is not a single gas but a mixture of unbreathable gases left after oxygen is removed from the air and typically consists of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_vapour" title="Water vapour">water vapour</a>.  The suffix <i>damp</i> is believed to derive from the German word for vapours (<i>"Dampf"</i>). The word <i>damp</i> is used in similar mining terms such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_damp" title="White damp">white damp</a> (<a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>), <a href="/wiki/Firedamp" title="Firedamp">fire damp</a> (typically <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stink_damp" title="Stink damp">stink damp</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources">Sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackdamp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Blackdamp is encountered in enclosed environments such as mines, sewers, wells, tunnels and ships' holds. It occurs with particular frequency in abandoned or poorly ventilated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_mine" title="Coal mine">coal mines</a>. Coal, once exposed to the air of a mine, naturally begins absorbing oxygen and exuding carbon dioxide and water vapor. The amount of blackdamp exuded by a mine varies based on a number of factors, including the temperature (coal releases more carbon dioxide in the warmer months), the amount of exposed coal, and the type of coal, although all mines with exposed coal produce gas.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hazards">Hazards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackdamp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hazards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Blackdamp is considered a particularly pernicious type of <a href="/wiki/Damp_(mining)" title="Damp (mining)">damp</a> (especially in a historical context), due to its omnipresence where exposed coal is found, and slow onset of symptoms. It produces no obvious odor (unlike the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a> of stinkdamp), is constantly being reintroduced to the air (instead of being released in pockets from actively mined sections), and does not require <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> in order to be released (unlike <a href="/wiki/Whitedamp" title="Whitedamp">whitedamp</a> or <a href="/wiki/Afterdamp" title="Afterdamp">afterdamp</a>). Many of the initial symptoms of oxygen deprivation (dizziness, light-headedness, drowsiness and poor coordination) are relatively innocuous and can easily be mistaken for simple fatigue, given the physically strenuous job of coal mining. The time between the onset of initial symptoms and the start of frank <a href="/wiki/Asphyxia" title="Asphyxia">asphyxiation</a> (and rapid unconsciousness) can be as short as seconds. Thus, if the warning signs are missed, a large number of miners can be rapidly incapacitated in the same short period of time, leaving no one to summon help.
</p><p>In addition to the danger inside the mine, blackdamp can be "exhaled" in large quantities from mines (especially long-abandoned coal mines with few outlets for escaping gas) during sudden changes in atmospheric pressure, potentially causing asphyxiation on the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disasters">Disasters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackdamp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Disasters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:111px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mine_safety_lamp.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="313" data-file-width="109" height="313" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/a/a4/Mine_safety_lamp.jpg" width="109"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mine_safety_lamp.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Modern flame safety lamp used in mines, manufactured by Koehler</div></div></div>
<p>The gas mixture has been responsible for many deaths among underground workers, especially miners. For example, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hartley_Colliery_Disaster" title="Hartley Colliery Disaster">Hartley Colliery Disaster</a> when the entire population of a colliery in north-east England were trapped when the beam of an engine suddenly broke and fell down the single shaft, blocking it with debris. It entombed 204 men and boys, who could not be rescued in time, and so suffocated in the blackdamp atmosphere, and died.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Detection_and_countermeasures">Detection and countermeasures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackdamp&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Detection and countermeasures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Historically, the <a href="/wiki/Domestic_canary" title="Domestic canary">domestic canary</a> was used as an early warning against any asphyxiating gases.
</p><p>In active mining operations, the threat from blackdamp is addressed with proper mineshaft ventilation as well as various detection methods, typically using <a href="/wiki/Safety_lamp" title="Safety lamp">miner's safety lamps</a> or hand-held <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_gas_detector" title="Electronic gas detector">electronic gas detectors</a>. The safety lamp is merely a specially designed lantern with a flame that is designed to automatically extinguish itself at an oxygen concentration of approximately 18% (normal atmospheric concentration of oxygen is c. 21%). This low detection threshold gives miners an unmistakable warning and allows them to escape before any potentially incapacitating effects are felt.
</p>
