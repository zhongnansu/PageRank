<p><b>CiteSeer<sup>x</sup></b> (originally called <b>CiteSeer</b>) is a public <a href="/wiki/Search_engine_(computing)" title="Search engine (computing)">search engine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Digital_library" title="Digital library">digital library</a> for scientific and academic papers, primarily in the fields of <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Information_science" title="Information science">information science</a>. CiteSeer holds a United States patent # 6289342, titled "<i>Autonomous citation indexing and literature browsing using citation context</i>," granted on September 11, 2001. Stephen R. Lawrence, C. Lee Giles, Kurt D. Bollacker are the inventors of this patent assigned to NEC Laboratories America, Inc. This patent was filed on May 20, 1998, which has its roots (Priority) to January 5, 1998. A continuation patent was also granted to the same inventors and also assigned to NEC Labs on this invention i.e. US Patent # 6738780 granted on May 18, 2004 and was filed on May 16, 2001. CiteSeer is considered as a predecessor of academic search tools such as <a href="/wiki/Google_Scholar" title="Google Scholar">Google Scholar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Academic_Search" title="Microsoft Academic Search">Microsoft Academic Search</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> CiteSeer-like engines and archives usually only harvest documents from publicly available websites and do not crawl publisher websites. For this reason, authors whose documents are freely available are more likely to be represented in the index.
</p><p>CiteSeer's goal is to improve the dissemination and access of academic and scientific literature. As a non-profit service that can be freely used by anyone, it has been considered as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_access_(publishing)" title="Open access (publishing)">open access</a> movement that is attempting to change <a href="/wiki/Academic_publishing" title="Academic publishing">academic and scientific publishing</a> to allow greater access to scientific literature. CiteSeer freely provided <a href="/wiki/Open_Archives_Initiative" title="Open Archives Initiative">Open Archives Initiative</a> <a href="/wiki/Metadata" title="Metadata">metadata</a> of all indexed documents and links indexed documents when possible to other sources of metadata such as <a href="/wiki/DBLP" title="DBLP">DBLP</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ACM_Portal" title="ACM Portal">ACM Portal</a>. To promote <a href="/wiki/Open_data" title="Open data">open data</a>, <b>CiteSeer<sup>x</sup></b> shares its data for non-commercial purposes under a Creative Commons license.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CC-license_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-CC-license-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The name can be construed to have at least two explanations. As a pun, a 'sightseer' is a tourist who looks at the sights, so a 'cite seer' would be a researcher who looks at cited papers. Another is a 'seer' is a prophet and a 'cite seer' is a prophet of citations. CiteSeer changed its name to ResearchIndex at one point and then changed it back.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CiteSeerX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CiteSeer_and_CiteSeer.IST">CiteSeer and CiteSeer.IST</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CiteSeerX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: CiteSeer and CiteSeer.IST">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CiteSeer was created by researchers <a href="/wiki/Lee_Giles" title="Lee Giles">Lee Giles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Bollacker" title="Kurt Bollacker">Kurt Bollacker</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steve_Lawrence_(computer_scientist)" title="Steve Lawrence (computer scientist)">Steve Lawrence</a> in 1997 while they were at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NEC_Research_Institute" title="NEC Research Institute">NEC Research Institute</a> (now <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NEC_Laboratories_America" title="NEC Laboratories America">NEC Labs</a>), <a href="/wiki/Princeton,_New_Jersey" title="Princeton, New Jersey">Princeton, New Jersey</a>, USA. CiteSeer's goal was to actively crawl and harvest academic and scientific documents on the web and use autonomous <a href="/wiki/Citation_index" title="Citation index">citation indexing</a> to permit querying by citation or by document, ranking them by <a href="/wiki/Citation_impact" title="Citation impact">citation impact</a>. At one point, it was called ResearchIndex.
</p><p>CiteSeer became public in 1998 and had many new features unavailable in academic search engines at that time. These included:
</p>
<ul><li>Autonomous Citation Indexing automatically created a citation index that can be used for literature search and evaluation.</li>
<li>Citation statistics and related documents were computed for all articles cited in the database, not just the indexed articles.</li>
<li>Reference linking allowing browsing of the database using citation links.</li>
<li>Citation context showed the context of citations to a given paper, allowing a researcher to quickly and easily see what other researchers have to say about an article of interest.</li>
<li>Related documents were shown using citation and word based measures and an active and continuously updated bibliography is shown for each document.</li></ul>
<p>After NEC, in 2004 it was hosted as CiteSeer.IST on the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a> at the College of Information Sciences and Technology, The <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_State_University" title="Pennsylvania State University">Pennsylvania State University</a>, and had over 700,000 documents. For enhanced access, performance and research, similar versions of CiteSeer were supported at universities such as the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Z%C3%BCrich" title="University of Zürich">University of Zürich</a> and the <a href="/wiki/National_University_of_Singapore" title="National University of Singapore">National University of Singapore</a>. However, these versions of CiteSeer proved difficult to maintain and are no longer available. Because CiteSeer only indexes freely available papers on the web and does not have access to publisher metadata, it returns fewer citation counts than sites, such as <a href="/wiki/Google_Scholar" title="Google Scholar">Google Scholar</a>, that have publisher metadata.  
</p><p>CiteSeer had not been comprehensively updated since 2005 due to limitations in its architecture design. It had a representative sampling of research documents in computer and information science but was limited in coverage because it was limited to papers that are publicly available, usually at an author's homepage, or those submitted by an author. To overcome some of these limitations, a modular and open source architecture for CiteSeer was designed - CiteSeer<sup>x</sup>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CiteSeerx">CiteSeer<sup>x</sup></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CiteSeerX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: CiteSeerx">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>CiteSeer<sup>x</sup></b> replaced CiteSeer and all queries to CiteSeer were redirected. CiteSeer<sup>x</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about-page_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-about-page-2">[2]</a></sup> is a public <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Search_engine" title="Search engine">search engine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Digital_library" title="Digital library">digital library</a> and <a href="/wiki/Disciplinary_repository" title="Disciplinary repository">repository</a> for scientific and academic papers primarily with a focus on <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Information_science" title="Information science">information science</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about-page_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-about-page-2">[2]</a></sup> However, recently CiteSeer<sup>x</sup> has been expanding into other scholarly domains such as economics, physics and others. Released in 2008, it was loosely based on the previous CiteSeer search engine and digital library and is built with a new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_source" title="Open source">open source</a> infrastructure, SeerSuite, and new algorithms and their implementations. It was developed by researchers Dr. Isaac Councill and Dr. C. <a href="/wiki/Lee_Giles" title="Lee Giles">Lee Giles</a> at <a href="/wiki/Penn_State_College_of_Information_Sciences_and_Technology" title="Penn State College of Information Sciences and Technology">the College of Information Sciences and Technology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_State_University" title="Pennsylvania State University">Pennsylvania State University</a>. It continues to support the goals outlined by CiteSeer to actively crawl and harvest academic and scientific documents on the public web and to use a citation inquery by citations and ranking of documents by the impact of citations. Currently, Lee Giles, Prasenjit Mitra, Susan Gauch, Min-Yen Kan, Pradeep Teregowda, Juan Pablo Fernández Ramírez, Pucktada Treeratpituk, Jian Wu, Douglas Jordan, Steve Carman, Jack Carroll, Jim Jansen, and Shuyi Zheng are or have been actively involved in its development. Recently, a table search feature was introduced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It has been funded by the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a>, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Research" title="Microsoft Research">Microsoft Research</a>.
</p><p>CiteSeer<sup>x</sup> continues to be rated as one of the world's top repositories and was rated number 1 in July 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It currently has over 6 million documents with nearly 6 million unique authors and 120 million citations.
</p><p>CiteSeer<sup>x</sup> also shares its software, data, databases and metadata with other researchers, currently by <a href="/wiki/Amazon_S3" title="Amazon S3">Amazon S3</a> and by <a href="/wiki/Rsync" title="Rsync">rsync</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Its new modular open source architecture and software (available previously on <a href="/wiki/SourceForge" title="SourceForge">SourceForge</a> but now on <a href="/wiki/GitHub" title="GitHub">GitHub</a>) is built on <a href="/wiki/Apache_Solr" title="Apache Solr">Apache Solr</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Apache_Software_Foundation" title="Apache Software Foundation">Apache</a> and open source tools which allows it to be a testbed for new algorithms in document harvesting, ranking, indexing, and information extraction.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_features">Current features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CiteSeerX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Current features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Automated_information_extraction">Automated information extraction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CiteSeerX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Automated information extraction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CiteSeer<sup>x</sup> uses automated information extraction tools, usually built on machine learning methods such ParsCit, to extract scholarly document metadata such as title, authors, abstract, citations, etc. As such, there are sometime errors in authors and titles. Other academic search engines have similar errors.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Focused_crawling">Focused crawling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CiteSeerX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Focused crawling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CiteSeer<sup>x</sup> crawls publicly available scholarly documents primarily from author webpages and other open resources, and does not have access to publisher metadata. As such citation counts in CiteSeer<sup>x</sup> are usually less than those in Google Scholar and Microsoft Academic Search who have access to publisher metadata.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CiteSeerX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CiteSeer<sup>x</sup> has nearly 1 million users worldwide based on unique IP addresses and has millions of hits daily. Annual downloads of document PDFs was nearly 200 million for 2015.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Data">Data</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CiteSeerX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Data">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CiteSeer<sup>x</sup> data is regularly shared under a <a href="/wiki/Creative_Commons_license" title="Creative Commons license">Creative Commons BY-NC-SA license</a> with researchers worldwide and has been and is used in many experiments and competitions.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_SeerSuite-based_search_engines">Other SeerSuite-based search engines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CiteSeerX&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other SeerSuite-based search engines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The CiteSeer model had been extended to cover academic documents in business with <a href="/wiki/SmealSearch" title="SmealSearch">SmealSearch</a> and in e-business with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=EBizSearch&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="EBizSearch (page does not exist)">eBizSearch</a>.  However, these were not maintained by their sponsors. An older version of both of these could be once found at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=BizSeer.IST&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="BizSeer.IST (page does not exist)">BizSeer.IST</a> but is no longer in service.
</p><p>Other Seer-like search and repository systems have been built for chemistry, <a href="/wiki/ChemXSeer" title="ChemXSeer">Chem<sub>X</sub>Seer</a> and for archaeology, ArchSeer. Another had been built for robots.txt file search, <a href="/wiki/BotSeer" title="BotSeer">BotSeer</a>. All of these are built on the open source tool <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=SeerSuite&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="SeerSuite (page does not exist)">SeerSuite</a>, which uses the open source indexer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lucene" title="Lucene">Lucene</a>.
</p>
