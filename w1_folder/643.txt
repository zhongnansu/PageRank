<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Cabin pressurization</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Cabin pressure" redirects here. For other uses, see <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Cabin_Pressure_(disambiguation)" title="Cabin Pressure (disambiguation)">Cabin Pressure (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Boeing_737_fuselage_train_hull_3473.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2232" data-file-width="3968" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Boeing_737_fuselage_train_hull_3473.jpg/220px-Boeing_737_fuselage_train_hull_3473.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Boeing_737_fuselage_train_hull_3473.jpg/330px-Boeing_737_fuselage_train_hull_3473.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Boeing_737_fuselage_train_hull_3473.jpg/440px-Boeing_737_fuselage_train_hull_3473.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Boeing_737_fuselage_train_hull_3473.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An airliner fuselage, such as this <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737" title="Boeing 737">Boeing 737</a>, forms a cylindrical <a href="/wiki/Pressure_vessel" title="Pressure vessel">pressure vessel</a></div></div></div>
<p><b>Cabin pressurization</b> is a process in which conditioned air is pumped into the <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_cabin" title="Aircraft cabin">cabin</a> of an aircraft or <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a>, in order to create a safe and comfortable environment for passengers and crew flying at high altitudes. For aircraft, this air is usually <a href="/wiki/Bleed_air" title="Bleed air">bled off</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbine engines</a> at the compressor stage, and for spacecraft, it is carried in high-pressure, often <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">cryogenic</a> tanks. The air is cooled, humidified, and mixed with recirculated air if necessary, before it is distributed to the cabin by one or more <a href="/wiki/Environmental_control_system_(aircraft)" title="Environmental control system (aircraft)">environmental control systems</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The cabin pressure is regulated by the outflow valve.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Need_for_cabin_pressurization">Need for cabin pressurization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cabin_pressurization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Need for cabin pressurization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pxctl.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2816" data-file-width="2112" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Pxctl.jpg/170px-Pxctl.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Pxctl.jpg/255px-Pxctl.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Pxctl.jpg/340px-Pxctl.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pxctl.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The pressurization controls on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_737-800" title="Boeing 737-800">Boeing 737-800</a></div></div></div>
<p>Pressurization becomes increasingly necessary at altitudes above 10,000 feet (3,000 m) above <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a> to protect crew and passengers from the risk of a number of physiological problems caused by the low outside air pressure above that altitude. For private aircraft operating in the US, crew members are required to use oxygen masks if the cabin altitude stays above 12,500 ft for more than 30 minutes, or if the cabin altitude reaches 14,000 ft at any time. At altitudes above 15,000 ft, passengers are required to be provided oxygen masks as well. On commercial aircraft, the cabin altitude must be maintained at 8,000 ft or less. Pressurization of the cargo hold is also required to prevent damage to pressure-sensitive goods that might leak, expand, burst or be crushed on re-pressurization. The principal physiological problems are listed below.
</p>
<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(medical)" title="Hypoxia (medical)">Hypoxia</a></dt>
<dd>The lower <a href="/wiki/Partial_pressure" title="Partial pressure">partial pressure</a> of oxygen at altitude reduces the <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_alveolus" title="Pulmonary alveolus">alveolar</a> oxygen tension in the lungs and subsequently in the brain, leading to sluggish thinking, dimmed vision, loss of consciousness, and ultimately death. In some individuals, particularly those with heart or lung disease, symptoms may begin as low as 5,000 feet (1,500 m), although most passengers can tolerate altitudes of 8,000 feet (2,400 m) without ill effect. At this altitude, there is about 25% less oxygen than there is at sea level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-02_calc_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-02_calc-2">[2]</a></sup></dd>
<dd>Hypoxia may be addressed by the administration of supplemental oxygen, either through an <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_mask" title="Oxygen mask">oxygen mask</a> or through a <a href="/wiki/Nasal_cannula" title="Nasal cannula">nasal cannula</a>. Without pressurization, sufficient oxygen can be delivered up to an altitude of about 40,000 feet (12,000 m). This is because a person who is used to living at sea level needs about 0.20 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">bar</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Partial_pressure_of_oxygen" title="Partial pressure of oxygen">partial oxygen pressure</a> to function normally and that pressure can be maintained up to about 40,000 feet (12,000 m) by increasing the <a href="/wiki/Mole_fraction" title="Mole fraction">mole fraction</a> of oxygen in the air that is being breathed. At 40,000 feet (12,000 m), the ambient air pressure falls to about 0.2 bar, at which maintaining a minimum partial pressure of oxygen of 0.2 bar requires breathing 100% oxygen using an <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_mask" title="Oxygen mask">oxygen mask</a>.</dd>
<dd>Emergency oxygen supply masks in the passenger compartment of airliners do not need to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxygen_masks" title="Oxygen masks">pressure-demand masks</a> because most flights stay below 40,000 feet (12,000 m). Above that altitude the partial pressure of oxygen will fall below 0.2 bar even at 100% oxygen and some degree of cabin pressurization or rapid descent will be essential to avoid the risk of hypoxia.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Altitude_sickness" title="Altitude sickness">Altitude sickness</a></dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Hyperventilation" title="Hyperventilation">Hyperventilation</a>, the body's most common response to hypoxia, does help to partially restore the partial pressure of oxygen in the blood, but it also causes <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) to out-gas, raising the blood pH and inducing <a href="/wiki/Alkalosis" title="Alkalosis">alkalosis</a>. Passengers may experience fatigue, <a href="/wiki/Nausea" title="Nausea">nausea</a>, headaches, sleeplessness, and (on extended flights) even <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulmonary_oedema" title="Pulmonary oedema">pulmonary oedema</a>. These are the same symptoms that mountain climbers experience, but the limited duration of powered flight makes the development of pulmonary oedema unlikely. Altitude sickness may be controlled by a full <a href="/wiki/Pressure_suit" title="Pressure suit">pressure suit</a> with helmet and faceplate, which completely envelops the body in a pressurized environment; however, this is impractical for commercial passengers.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Decompression_sickness" title="Decompression sickness">Decompression sickness</a></dt>
<dd>The low partial pressure of gases, principally nitrogen (N<sub>2</sub>) but including all other gases, may cause dissolved gases in the bloodstream to precipitate out, resulting in <i>gas embolism</i>, or bubbles in the bloodstream. The mechanism is the same as that of compressed-air divers on ascent from depth. Symptoms may include the early symptoms of "the bends"—tiredness, forgetfulness, headache, stroke, thrombosis, and subcutaneous itching—but rarely the full symptoms thereof. Decompression sickness may also be controlled by a full-pressure suit as for altitude sickness.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Barotrauma" title="Barotrauma">Barotrauma</a></dt>
<dd>As the aircraft climbs or descends, passengers may experience discomfort or acute pain as gases trapped within their bodies expand or contract. The most common problems occur with air trapped in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_ear" title="Middle ear">middle ear</a> (aerotitus) or paranasal sinuses by a blocked <a href="/wiki/Eustachian_tube" title="Eustachian tube">Eustachian tube</a> or sinuses. Pain may also be experienced in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_gastrointestinal_tract" title="Human gastrointestinal tract">gastrointestinal tract</a> or even the teeth (<a href="/wiki/Barodontalgia" title="Barodontalgia">barodontalgia</a>). Usually these are not severe enough to cause actual trauma but can result in soreness in the ear that persists after the flight and can exacerbate or precipitate pre-existing medical conditions, such as <a href="/wiki/Pneumothorax" title="Pneumothorax">pneumothorax</a>.</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cabin_altitude">Cabin altitude</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cabin_pressurization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cabin altitude">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CabinAltitudeQF744.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2160" data-file-width="3840" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/CabinAltitudeQF744.jpg/220px-CabinAltitudeQF744.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/CabinAltitudeQF744.jpg/330px-CabinAltitudeQF744.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/CabinAltitudeQF744.jpg/440px-CabinAltitudeQF744.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CabinAltitudeQF744.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An empty bottle, sealed at 37,000ft, is crushed on descent to sea level, compared with one in its original state.</div></div></div>
<p>The pressure inside the cabin is technically referred to as the <i>equivalent effective cabin altitude</i> or more commonly as the <i>cabin altitude</i>. This is defined as the equivalent altitude above <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">mean sea level</a> having the same <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> according to a standard atmospheric model such as the <a href="/wiki/International_Standard_Atmosphere" title="International Standard Atmosphere">International Standard Atmosphere</a>. Thus a cabin altitude of zero would have the pressure found at mean sea level, which is taken to be 101.325 kilopascals (14.696 psi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aeromech.usyd.edu.au_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-aeromech.usyd.edu.au-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aircraft">Aircraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cabin_pressurization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aircraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Airliner" title="Airliner">airliners</a>, cabin altitude during flight is kept above sea level in order to reduce stress on the pressurized part of the <a href="/wiki/Fuselage" title="Fuselage">fuselage</a>; this stress is proportional to the difference in pressure inside and outside the cabin. In a typical commercial passenger flight, the cabin altitude is programmed to rise gradually from the altitude of the airport of origin to a regulatory maximum of 8,000 ft (2,400 m). This cabin altitude is maintained while the aircraft is cruising at its maximum altitude and then reduced gradually during descent until the cabin pressure matches the ambient air pressure at the destination.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Keeping the cabin altitude below 8,000 ft (2,400 m) generally prevents significant <a href="/wiki/Hypoxic_hypoxia" title="Hypoxic hypoxia">hypoxia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Altitude_sickness" title="Altitude sickness">altitude sickness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Decompression_sickness" title="Decompression sickness">decompression sickness</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Barotrauma" title="Barotrauma">barotrauma</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">Federal Aviation Administration</a> (FAA) regulations in the U.S. mandate that under normal operating conditions, the cabin altitude may not exceed this limit at the maximum operating altitude of the aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This mandatory maximum cabin altitude does not eliminate all physiological problems; passengers with conditions such as <a href="/wiki/Pneumothorax" title="Pneumothorax">pneumothorax</a> are advised not to fly until fully healed, and people suffering from a cold or other infection may still experience pain in the ears and sinuses.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The rate of change of cabin altitude strongly affects comfort as humans are sensitive to pressure changes in the <a href="/wiki/Inner_ear" title="Inner ear">inner ear</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paranasal_sinuses" title="Paranasal sinuses">sinuses</a> and this has to be managed carefully. <a href="/wiki/Scuba_diving" title="Scuba diving">Scuba</a> divers flying within the "no fly" period after a dive are at risk of <a href="/wiki/Decompression_sickness" title="Decompression sickness">decompression sickness</a> because the accumulated nitrogen in their bodies can form bubbles when exposed to reduced cabin pressure.
</p><p>The cabin altitude of the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_767" title="Boeing 767">Boeing 767</a> is typically about 7,000 feet (2,100 m) when cruising at 37,000 feet (11,000 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This is typical for older jet airliners. A design goal for many, but not all, newer aircraft is to provide a lower cabin altitude than older designs. This can be beneficial for passenger comfort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flightglobal_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-flightglobal-7">[7]</a></sup> For example, the <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Global_Express" title="Bombardier Global Express">Bombardier Global Express</a> business jet can provide a cabin altitude of 4,500 ft (1,400 m) when cruising at 41,000 feet (12,000 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-xrs_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-xrs-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bombardier_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-bombardier-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ecs_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ecs-10">[10]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emivest_SJ30" title="Emivest SJ30">Emivest SJ30</a> business jet can provide a sea-level cabin altitude when cruising at 41,000 feet (12,000 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> One study of 8 flights in <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A380" title="Airbus A380">Airbus A380</a> aircraft found a median cabin pressure altitude of 6,128 feet (1,868 m), and 65 flights in <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747-400" title="Boeing 747-400">Boeing 747-400</a> aircraft found a median cabin pressure altitude of 5,159 feet (1,572 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ers_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-ers-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Before 1996, approximately 6,000 large commercial transport airplanes were type-certificated to fly up to 45,000 ft (14,000 m) without having to meet high-altitude special conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In 1996, the FAA adopted Amendment 25-87, which imposed additional high-altitude cabin pressure specifications for new-type aircraft designs. Aircraft certified to operate above 25,000 ft (7,600 m) "must be designed so that occupants will not be exposed to cabin pressure altitudes in excess of 15,000 ft (4,600 m) after any probable failure condition in the pressurization system".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FARs,_14_CFR,_Part_25,_Section_841_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FARs,_14_CFR,_Part_25,_Section_841-15">[15]</a></sup> In the event of a decompression that results from "any failure condition not shown to be extremely improbable", the plane must be designed such that occupants will not be exposed to a cabin altitude exceeding 25,000 ft (7,600 m) for more than 2 minutes, nor to an altitude exceeding 40,000 ft (12,000 m) at any time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FARs,_14_CFR,_Part_25,_Section_841_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-FARs,_14_CFR,_Part_25,_Section_841-15">[15]</a></sup> In practice, that new <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Regulations" title="Federal Aviation Regulations">Federal Aviation Regulations</a> amendment imposes an operational <a href="/wiki/Ceiling_(aeronautics)" title="Ceiling (aeronautics)">ceiling</a> of 40,000 ft (12,000 m) on the majority of newly designed commercial aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Exemption_No._8695_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Exemption_No._8695-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Aircraft manufacturers can apply for a relaxation of this rule if the circumstances warrant it. In 2004, <a href="/wiki/Airbus" title="Airbus">Airbus</a> acquired an FAA exemption to allow the cabin altitude of the A380 to reach 43,000 ft (13,000 m) in the event of a decompression incident and to exceed 40,000 ft (12,000 m) for one minute. This allows the A380 to operate at a higher altitude than other newly designed civilian aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Exemption_No._8695_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Exemption_No._8695-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spacecraft">Spacecraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cabin_pressurization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Spacecraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Russian engineers used an air-like nitrogen/oxygen mixture, kept at a cabin altitude near zero at all times, in their 1961 <a href="/wiki/Vostok_(spacecraft)" title="Vostok (spacecraft)">Vostok</a>, 1964 <a href="/wiki/Voskhod_(spacecraft)" title="Voskhod (spacecraft)">Voskhod</a>, and 1967 to present <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_(spacecraft)" title="Soyuz (spacecraft)">Soyuz</a> spacecraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> This requires a heavier <a href="/wiki/Space_vehicle" title="Space vehicle">space vehicle</a> design, because the spacecraft cabin structure must withstand the stress of 14.7 pounds per square inch (1 bar) against the vacuum of space, and also because an inert nitrogen mass must be carried. Care must also be taken to avoid <a href="/wiki/Decompression_sickness" title="Decompression sickness">decompression sickness</a> when cosmonauts perform <a href="/wiki/Extravehicular_activity" title="Extravehicular activity">extravehicular activity</a>, as current soft <a href="/wiki/Space_suit" title="Space suit">space suits</a> are pressurized with pure oxygen at relatively low pressure in order to provide reasonable flexibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>By contrast, the United States used a pure oxygen atmosphere for its 1961 <a href="/wiki/Project_Mercury" title="Project Mercury">Mercury</a>, 1965 <a href="/wiki/Project_Gemini" title="Project Gemini">Gemini</a>, and 1967 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo_spacecraft" title="Apollo spacecraft">Apollo</a> spacecraft, mainly in order to avoid decompression sickness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Mercury used a cabin altitude of 24,800 feet (7,600 m) (5.5 pounds per square inch (0.38 bar));<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Gemini used an altitude of 25,700 feet (7,800 m) (5.3 psi (0.37 bar));<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and Apollo used 27,000 feet (8,200 m) (5.0 psi (0.34 bar))<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> in space. This allowed for a lighter space vehicle design. Before launch, the pressure was kept at slightly higher than sea level at a constant 5.3 psi (0.37 bar) above ambient for Gemini, and 2 psi (0.14 bar) above sea level at launch for Apollo), and transitioned to the space cabin altitude during ascent. However, the high pressure pure oxygen atmosphere proved to be a fatal fire hazard in Apollo, contributing to the deaths of the entire crew of <a href="/wiki/Apollo_1" title="Apollo 1">Apollo 1</a> during a 1967 ground test. After this, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> revised its procedure to use a 40% nitrogen/60% oxygen mix at zero cabin altitude at launch, but kept the low-pressure pure oxygen in space.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo program</a>, the United States used air-like cabin atmospheres<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (December 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> for <a href="/wiki/Skylab" title="Skylab">Skylab</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_orbiter" title="Space Shuttle orbiter">Space Shuttle orbiter</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanics">Mechanics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cabin_pressurization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mechanics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pressurization is achieved by the design of an airtight fuselage engineered to be pressurized with a source of compressed air and controlled by an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_control_system" title="Environmental control system">environmental control system</a> (ECS). The most common source of compressed air for pressurization is <a href="/wiki/Bleed_air" title="Bleed air">bleed air</a> extracted from the compressor stage of a <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbine</a> engine, from a low or intermediate stage and also from an additional high stage; the exact stage can vary depending on engine type. By the time the cold outside air has reached the bleed air valves, it is at a very high pressure and has been heated to around 200 <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">°C</a> (392 <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">°F</a>). The control and selection of high or low bleed sources is fully automatic and is governed by the needs of various pneumatic systems at various stages of flight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Engineering_Aspects_of_Cabin_Air_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Engineering_Aspects_of_Cabin_Air-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>The part of the bleed air that is directed to the ECS is then expanded to bring it to cabin pressure, which cools it. A final, suitable temperature is then achieved by adding back heat from the hot compressed air via a <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">heat exchanger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Air_cycle_machine" title="Air cycle machine">air cycle machine</a> known as the packs system. In some larger airliners, hot trim air can be added downstream of air conditioned air coming from the packs if it is needed to warm a section of the cabin that is colder than others.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Outflow.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2112" data-file-width="2816" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Outflow.jpg/220px-Outflow.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Outflow.jpg/330px-Outflow.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Outflow.jpg/440px-Outflow.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Outflow.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Outflow and pressure relief valve on a <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737_Next_Generation" title="Boeing 737 Next Generation">Boeing 737-800</a></div></div></div>
<p>At least two engines provide compressed bleed air for all the plane's pneumatic systems, to provide full <a href="/wiki/Redundancy_(engineering)" title="Redundancy (engineering)">redundancy</a>. Compressed air is also obtained from the <a href="/wiki/Auxiliary_power_unit" title="Auxiliary power unit">auxiliary power unit</a> (APU), if fitted, in the event of an emergency and for cabin air supply on the ground before the main engines are started. Most modern commercial aircraft today have fully redundant, duplicated electronic controllers for maintaining pressurization along with a manual back-up control system.
</p><p>All exhaust air is dumped to atmosphere via an outflow valve, usually at the rear of the fuselage. This valve controls the cabin pressure and also acts as a safety relief valve, in addition to other safety relief valves. If the automatic pressure controllers fail, the pilot can manually control the cabin pressure valve, according to the backup emergency procedure checklist. The automatic controller normally maintains the proper cabin pressure altitude by constantly adjusting the outflow valve position so that the cabin altitude is as low as practical without exceeding the maximum pressure differential limit on the fuselage. The pressure differential varies between aircraft types, typical values are between 7.8 <a href="/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch" title="Pounds per square inch">psi</a> (54 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">kPa</a>) and 9.4 <a href="/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch" title="Pounds per square inch">psi</a> (65 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">kPa</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Differential_Pressure_Characteristics_of_Aircraft_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Differential_Pressure_Characteristics_of_Aircraft-26">[26]</a></sup> At 39,000 feet (12,000 m), the cabin pressure would be automatically maintained at about 6,900 feet (2,100 m) (450 feet (140 m) lower than Mexico City), which is about 11.5 psi (79 kPa) of atmosphere pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Engineering_Aspects_of_Cabin_Air_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Engineering_Aspects_of_Cabin_Air-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some aircraft, such as the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_787_Dreamliner" title="Boeing 787 Dreamliner">Boeing 787 Dreamliner</a>, have re-introduced electric compressors previously used on piston-engined airliners to provide pressurization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AERO_QTR_406_787_from_the_ground_up_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-AERO_QTR_406_787_from_the_ground_up-27">[27]</a></sup> The use of electric compressors increases the electrical generation load on the engines and introduces a number of stages of energy transfer; therefore, it is unclear whether this increases the overall efficiency of the aircraft air handling system. It does, however, remove the danger of <a href="/wiki/Fume_event" title="Fume event">chemical contamination of the cabin</a>, simplify engine design, avert the need to run high pressure pipework around the aircraft, and provide greater design flexibility.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unplanned_decompression">Unplanned decompression</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cabin_pressurization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Unplanned decompression">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Uncontrolled_decompression" title="Uncontrolled decompression">Uncontrolled decompression</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Passenger_oxygen_mask_dsc06035.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="745" data-file-width="511" height="248" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Passenger_oxygen_mask_dsc06035.jpg/170px-Passenger_oxygen_mask_dsc06035.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Passenger_oxygen_mask_dsc06035.jpg/255px-Passenger_oxygen_mask_dsc06035.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Passenger_oxygen_mask_dsc06035.jpg/340px-Passenger_oxygen_mask_dsc06035.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Passenger_oxygen_mask_dsc06035.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Passenger oxygen mask deployment</div></div></div>
<p>Unplanned loss of cabin pressure at altitude is rare but has resulted in a <a href="/wiki/Uncontrolled_decompression#Notable_decompression_accidents_and_incidents" title="Uncontrolled decompression">number of fatal accidents</a>. Failures range from sudden, catastrophic loss of airframe integrity (explosive decompression) to slow leaks or equipment malfunctions that allow cabin pressure to drop undetected to levels that can lead to unconsciousness or severe performance degradation of the aircrew.
</p><p>Any failure of cabin pressurization above 10,000 feet (3,000 m) requires an emergency descent to 8,000 feet (2,400 m) or the closest to that while maintaining the Minimum Safe Altitude (MSA), and the deployment of an <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_mask" title="Oxygen mask">oxygen mask</a> for each seat. The oxygen systems have sufficient oxygen for all on board and give the pilots adequate time to descend to below 8,000 ft (2,400 m). Without emergency oxygen, <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(medical)" title="Hypoxia (medical)">hypoxia</a> may lead to loss of consciousness and a subsequent loss of control of the aircraft. The <a href="/wiki/Time_of_useful_consciousness" title="Time of useful consciousness">time of useful consciousness</a> varies according to altitude. As the pressure falls the cabin air temperature may also plummet to the ambient outside temperature with a danger of <a href="/wiki/Hypothermia" title="Hypothermia">hypothermia</a> or <a href="/wiki/Frostbite" title="Frostbite">frostbite</a>.
</p><p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jet_fighter" title="Jet fighter">jet fighter</a> aircraft, the small size of the <a href="/wiki/Cockpit" title="Cockpit">cockpit</a> means that any decompression will be very rapid and would not allow the pilot time to put on an oxygen mask. Therefore, fighter jet pilots and aircrew are required to wear oxygen masks at all times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>On June 30, 1971, the crew of <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_11" title="Soyuz 11">Soyuz 11</a>, Soviet cosmonauts <a href="/wiki/Georgy_Dobrovolsky" title="Georgy Dobrovolsky">Georgy Dobrovolsky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vladislav_Volkov" title="Vladislav Volkov">Vladislav Volkov</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Viktor_Patsayev" title="Viktor Patsayev">Viktor Patsayev</a> were killed after the cabin vent valve accidentally opened before atmospheric re-entry. There had been no indication of trouble until the recovery team opened the capsule and found the dead crew.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-time-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ea_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-ea-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cabin_pressurization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The aircraft that pioneered pressurized cabin systems include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Packard-Le_P%C3%A8re_LUSAC-11" title="Packard-Le Père LUSAC-11">Packard-Le Père LUSAC-11</a>, (1920, a modified French design, not actually pressurized but with an enclosed, oxygen enriched cockpit)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Engineering_Division_USD-9A" title="Engineering Division USD-9A">Engineering Division USD-9A</a>, a modified <a href="/wiki/Airco_DH.9A" title="Airco DH.9A">Airco DH.9A</a> (1921 – the first aircraft to fly with the addition of a pressurized cockpit module)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Junkers_Ju_49" title="Junkers Ju 49">Junkers Ju 49</a> (1931 – a German experimental aircraft purpose-built to test the concept of cabin pressurization)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Farman_F.1000" title="Farman F.1000">Farman F.1000</a> (1932 – a French record breaking pressurized cockpit, experimental aircraft)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chizhevski_BOK-1" title="Chizhevski BOK-1">Chizhevski BOK-1</a> (1936 – a Russian experimental aircraft)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lockheed_XC-35" title="Lockheed XC-35">Lockheed XC-35</a> (1937 – an American pressurized aircraft. Rather than a pressure capsule enclosing the cockpit, the <a href="/wiki/Monocoque" title="Monocoque">monocoque</a> fuselage skin was the pressure vessel.)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Renard_R.35" title="Renard R.35">Renard R.35</a> (1938 – the first pressurized piston airliner, which crashed on first flight)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_307" title="Boeing 307">Boeing 307</a> (1938 – the first pressurized airliner to enter commercial service)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Constellation" title="Lockheed Constellation">Lockheed Constellation</a> (1943 – the first pressurized airliner in wide service)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Avro_Tudor" title="Avro Tudor">Avro Tudor</a> (1946 – first British pressurized airliner)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Comet" title="De Havilland Comet">de Havilland Comet</a> (British, Comet 1 1949 – the first jetliner, Comet 4 1958 – resolving the Comet 1 problems)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tupolev_Tu-144" title="Tupolev Tu-144">Tupolev Tu-144</a> and <a href="/wiki/Concorde" title="Concorde">Concorde</a> (1968 USSR and 1969 Anglo-French respectively – first to operate at very high altitude)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/SyberJet_SJ30" title="SyberJet SJ30">SyberJet SJ30</a> (2005) First civilian business jet to certify 12.0 psi pressurization system allowing for a sea level cabin at 41,000 ft (12,000 m).</li></ul>
<p>In the late 1910s, attempts were being made to achieve higher and higher altitudes. In 1920, flights well over 37,000 ft (11,000 m) were first achieved by test pilot Lt. <a href="/wiki/John_A._Macready" title="John A. Macready">John A. Macready</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Packard-Le_P%C3%A8re_LUSAC-11" title="Packard-Le Père LUSAC-11">Packard-Le Père LUSAC-11</a> biplane at <a href="/wiki/McCook_Field" title="McCook Field">McCook Field</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dayton,_Ohio" title="Dayton, Ohio">Dayton, Ohio</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cornelisse_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cornelisse-31">[31]</a></sup> The flight was possible by releasing stored oxygen into the cockpit, which was released directly into an enclosed cabin and not to an oxygen mask, which was developed later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cornelisse_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cornelisse-31">[31]</a></sup> With this system flights nearing 40,000 ft (12,000 m) were possible, but the lack of atmospheric pressure at that altitude caused the pilot's heart to enlarge visibly, and many pilots reported health problems from such high altitude flights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cornelisse_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cornelisse-31">[31]</a></sup> Some early airliners had oxygen masks for the passengers for routine flights.
</p><p>In 1921, a Wright-Dayton USD-9A reconnaissance biplane was modified with the addition of a completely enclosed air-tight chamber that could be pressurized with air forced into it by small external turbines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cornelisse_31-3"><a href="#cite_note-Cornelisse-31">[31]</a></sup> The chamber had a hatch only 22 in (0.56 m) in diameter that would be sealed by the pilot at 3,000 ft (910 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cornelisse_31-4"><a href="#cite_note-Cornelisse-31">[31]</a></sup> The chamber contained only one instrument, an altimeter, while the conventional cockpit instruments were all mounted outside the chamber, visible through five small portholes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cornelisse_31-5"><a href="#cite_note-Cornelisse-31">[31]</a></sup> The first attempt to operate the aircraft was again made by Lt. John A. McCready, who discovered that the turbine was forcing air into the chamber faster than the small release valve provided could release it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cornelisse_31-6"><a href="#cite_note-Cornelisse-31">[31]</a></sup> As a result, the chamber quickly over pressurized, and the flight was abandoned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cornelisse_31-7"><a href="#cite_note-Cornelisse-31">[31]</a></sup> A second attempt had to be abandoned when the pilot discovered at 3,000 ft (910 m) that he was too short to close the chamber hatch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cornelisse_31-8"><a href="#cite_note-Cornelisse-31">[31]</a></sup> The first successful flight was finally made by test pilot Lt. Harrold Harris, making it the world's first flight by a pressurized aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cornelisse_31-9"><a href="#cite_note-Cornelisse-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first airliner with a pressurized cabin was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_307" title="Boeing 307">Boeing 307</a> Stratoliner, built in 1938, prior to <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, though only ten were produced. The 307's "pressure compartment was from the nose of the aircraft to a pressure <a href="/wiki/Bulkhead_(partition)" title="Bulkhead (partition)">bulkhead</a> in the aft just forward of the horizontal stabilizer."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:B-8_winter_helmet_%26_A-14_oxygen_mask_(1944).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="681" data-file-width="667" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/B-8_winter_helmet_%26_A-14_oxygen_mask_%281944%29.jpg/220px-B-8_winter_helmet_%26_A-14_oxygen_mask_%281944%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/B-8_winter_helmet_%26_A-14_oxygen_mask_%281944%29.jpg/330px-B-8_winter_helmet_%26_A-14_oxygen_mask_%281944%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/B-8_winter_helmet_%26_A-14_oxygen_mask_%281944%29.jpg/440px-B-8_winter_helmet_%26_A-14_oxygen_mask_%281944%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:B-8_winter_helmet_%26_A-14_oxygen_mask_(1944).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>World War II era flying helmet and oxygen mask</div></div></div>
<p>World War II was a catalyst for aircraft development. Initially, the piston aircraft of World War II, though they often flew at very high altitudes, were not pressurized and relied on oxygen masks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> This became impractical with the development of larger bombers where crew were required to move about the cabin and this led to the first bomber with cabin pressurization (though restricted to crew areas), the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_B-29_Superfortress" title="Boeing B-29 Superfortress">Boeing B-29 Superfortress</a>. The control system for this was designed by <a href="/wiki/Garrett_AiResearch" title="Garrett AiResearch">Garrett AiResearch Manufacturing Company</a>, drawing in part on licensing of patents held by Boeing for the Stratoliner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Post-war piston airliners such as the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Constellation" title="Lockheed Constellation">Lockheed Constellation</a> (1943) extended the technology to civilian service. The piston engined airliners generally relied on electrical compressors to provide pressurized cabin air. Engine supercharging and cabin pressurization enabled planes like the <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-6" title="Douglas DC-6">Douglas DC-6</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-7" title="Douglas DC-7">Douglas DC-7</a>, and the Constellation to have certified service ceilings from 24,000 ft (7,300 m) to 28,400 ft (8,700 m). Designing a pressurized fuselage to cope with that altitude range was within the engineering and metallurgical knowledge of that time. The introduction of jet airliners required a significant increase in cruise altitudes to the 30,000–41,000 ft (9,100–12,500 m) range, where jet engines are more fuel efficient. That increase in cruise altitudes required far more rigorous engineering of the fuselage, and in the beginning not all the engineering problems were fully understood.
</p><p>The world’s first commercial jet airliner was the British <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Comet" title="De Havilland Comet">de Havilland Comet</a> (1949) designed with a service ceiling of 36,000 ft (11,000 m). It was the first time that a large diameter, pressurized fuselage with windows had been built and flown at this altitude. Initially, the design was very successful but <a href="/wiki/South_African_Airways_Flight_201#Official_investigation" title="South African Airways Flight 201">two catastrophic airframe failures in 1954</a> resulting in the total loss of the aircraft, passengers and crew grounded what was then the entire world jet airliner fleet. Extensive investigation and groundbreaking engineering analysis of the wreckage led to a number of very significant engineering advances that solved the basic problems of pressurized fuselage design at altitude. The critical problem proved to be a combination of an inadequate understanding of the effect of progressive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal_fatigue" title="Metal fatigue">metal fatigue</a> as the fuselage undergoes repeated stress cycles coupled with a misunderstanding of how aircraft skin stresses are redistributed around openings in the fuselage such as windows and rivet holes.
</p><p>The critical engineering principles concerning metal fatigue learned from the Comet 1 program<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> were applied directly to the design of the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_707" title="Boeing 707">Boeing 707</a> (1957) and all subsequent jet airliners. One immediately noticeable legacy of the Comet disasters is the oval windows on every jet airliner; the metal fatigue cracks that destroyed the Comets were initiated by the small radius corners on the Comet 1’s almost square windows. The Comet fuselage was redesigned and the Comet 4 (1958) went on to become a successful airliner, pioneering the first transatlantic jet service, but the program never really recovered from these disasters and was overtaken by the Boeing 707.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Concorde" title="Concorde">Concorde</a> had to deal with particularly high pressure differentials because it flew at unusually high altitude (up to 60,000 feet (18,000 m)) and maintained a cabin altitude of 6,000 ft (1,800 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> This made the aircraft significantly heavier and contributed to the high cost of a flight. The Concorde also had smaller cabin windows than most other commercial passenger aircraft in order to slow the rate of decompression if a window failed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nunn_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-nunn-37">[37]</a></sup> The high cruising altitude also required the use of high pressure oxygen and <a href="/wiki/Diving_regulator#Demand_valve" title="Diving regulator">demand valves</a> at the emergency masks unlike the <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_mask" title="Oxygen mask">continuous-flow masks</a> used in conventional airliners.
</p><p>The designed operating cabin altitude for new aircraft is falling and this is expected to reduce any remaining physiological problems.
</p>
