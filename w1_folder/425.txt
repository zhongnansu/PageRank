<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Hypercapnia</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none"> Abnormally high tissue carbon dioxide levels </div>

<p><b>Hypercapnia</b>, also known as <b>hypercarbia</b> and <b>CO<sub>2</sub> retention</b>, is a condition of abnormally elevated <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) levels in the blood. Carbon dioxide is a <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gaseous</a> product of the <a href="/wiki/Human_body" title="Human body">body's</a> <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a> and is normally expelled through the <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">lungs</a>.
</p><p>Hypercapnia normally triggers a reflex which increases breathing and access to <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> (O<sub>2</sub>), such as arousal and turning the head during sleep. A failure of this reflex can be fatal, for example as a contributory factor in <a href="/wiki/Sudden_infant_death_syndrome" title="Sudden infant death syndrome">sudden infant death syndrome</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kinney_et_al_2009_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kinney_et_al_2009-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hypercapnia is the opposite of <a href="/wiki/Hypocapnia" title="Hypocapnia">hypocapnia</a>, the state of having abnormally reduced levels of carbon dioxide in the blood.  Hypercapnia is from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>hyper</i> = "above" or "too much" and <i>kapnos</i> = "<a href="/wiki/Smoke" title="Smoke">smoke</a>".
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Signs_and_symptoms">Signs and symptoms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypercapnia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Signs and symptoms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Symptoms and signs of early hypercapnia include flushed skin, full <a href="/wiki/Pulse" title="Pulse">pulse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tachypnea" title="Tachypnea">tachypnea</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dyspnea" title="Dyspnea">dyspnea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Premature_ventricular_contraction" title="Premature ventricular contraction">extrasystoles</a>, muscle twitches, hand flaps, reduced neural activity, and possibly raised <a href="/wiki/Blood_pressure" title="Blood pressure">blood pressure</a>. According to other sources, symptoms of mild hypercapnia might include headache, confusion and lethargy. Hypercapnia can induce increased cardiac output, an elevation in arterial blood pressure, and a propensity toward <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arrhythmias" title="Arrhythmias">arrhythmias</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stapczynski_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stapczynski-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morgan_et_al_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morgan_et_al-5">[5]</a></sup> Hypercapnia may increase pulmonary capillary resistance.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In severe hypercapnia (generally PaCO<sub>2</sub> greater than 10 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">kPa</a> or 75 <a href="/wiki/Millimeter_of_mercury" title="Millimeter of mercury">mmHg</a>), symptomatology progresses to disorientation, <a href="/wiki/Panic" title="Panic">panic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hyperventilation" title="Hyperventilation">hyperventilation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convulsions" title="Convulsions">convulsions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Unconsciousness" title="Unconsciousness">unconsciousness</a>, and eventually <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">death</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lambertson_1971_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lambertson_1971-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glatte_et_al_1967_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Glatte_et_al_1967-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypercapnia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hypercapnia is generally caused by <a href="/wiki/Hypoventilation" title="Hypoventilation">hypoventilation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_disease" title="Respiratory disease">lung disease</a>, or diminished <a href="/wiki/Consciousness" title="Consciousness">consciousness</a>. It may also be caused by exposure to environments containing abnormally high concentrations of carbon dioxide, such as from volcanic or geothermal activity, or by <a href="/wiki/Rebreather" title="Rebreather">rebreathing</a> exhaled <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. It can also be an initial effect of administering supplemental oxygen on a patient with sleep apnea. In this situation the hypercapnia can also be accompanied by <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_acidosis" title="Respiratory acidosis">respiratory acidosis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dement_et_al_2000_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dement_et_al_2000-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnosis">Diagnosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypercapnia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Diagnosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Laboratory_values">Laboratory values</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypercapnia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Laboratory values">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hypercapnia is generally defined as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_gas" title="Blood gas">blood gas</a> carbon dioxide level over 45 mmHg. Since carbon dioxide is in equilibrium with <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a> in the blood, hypercapnia can drive serum pH down, resulting in  respiratory acidosis. Clinically, the effect of hypercapnia on pH is estimated using the ratio of the arterial pressure of carbon dioxide to the concentration of bicarbonate ion, P<sub>a</sub>CO<sub>2</sub>/[HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>].
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="During_diving">During diving</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypercapnia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: During diving">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Normal respiration in divers results in <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_alveolus" title="Pulmonary alveolus">alveolar</a> <a href="/wiki/Hypoventilation" title="Hypoventilation">hypoventilation</a> resulting in inadequate CO<sub>2</sub> elimination or hypercapnia. Lanphier's work at the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy_Experimental_Diving_Unit" title="United States Navy Experimental Diving Unit">US Navy Experimental Diving Unit</a> answered the question, "Why don't divers breathe enough?":<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usn_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-usn-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Higher inspired oxygen (PiO<sub>2</sub>) at 4 atm (400 kPa) accounted for not more than 25% of the elevation in end tidal CO<sub>2</sub> (etCO<sub>2</sub>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-etCO2_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-etCO2-10">[10]</a></sup> above values found at the same work rate when breathing air just below the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AD0784151_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-AD0784151-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AD0728247_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-AD0728247-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AD0206734_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-AD0206734-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AD0725851_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-AD0725851-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>Increased work of breathing accounted for most of the elevation of PaCO<sub>2</sub> (<a href="/wiki/Alveolar_gas_equation" title="Alveolar gas equation">alveolar gas equation</a>) in exposures above 1 atm (100 kPa), as indicated by the results when <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> was substituted for <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> at 4 atm (400 kPa).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AD0784151_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-AD0784151-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AD0728247_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-AD0728247-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AD0206734_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-AD0206734-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AD0725851_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-AD0725851-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>Inadequate ventilatory response to exertion was indicated by the fact that, despite resting values in the normal range, PetCO<sub>2</sub> rose markedly with exertion even when the divers breathed air at a depth of only a few feet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AD0784151_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-AD0784151-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AD0728247_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-AD0728247-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AD0206734_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-AD0206734-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AD0725851_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-AD0725851-14">[14]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_dioxide_retention">Carbon dioxide retention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypercapnia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Carbon dioxide retention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A variety of reasons exists for carbon dioxide not being expelled completely when the diver exhales:
</p>
<ul><li>The diver is exhaling into a vessel that does not allow all the CO<sub>2</sub> to escape to the environment, such as a long <a href="/wiki/Snorkeling" title="Snorkeling">snorkel</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full-face_diving_mask" title="Full-face diving mask">full-face diving mask</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Diving_helmet" title="Diving helmet">diving helmet</a>, and the diver then reinhales from that vessel, causing increased <a href="/wiki/Dead_space_(physiology)" title="Dead space (physiology)">dead space</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AD0725851_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-AD0725851-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_scrubber" title="Carbon dioxide scrubber">carbon dioxide scrubber</a> in the diver's <a href="/wiki/Rebreather" title="Rebreather">rebreather</a> is failing to remove sufficient carbon dioxide from the loop (higher inspired CO<sub>2</sub>).</li>
<li>The diver is overexercising, producing excess carbon dioxide due to elevated metabolic activity.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Breathing_gas" title="Breathing gas">breathing gas</a> is higher at depth, so the effort required to fully inhale and exhale has increased, making breathing more difficult and less efficient (high <a href="/wiki/Work_of_breathing" title="Work of breathing">work of breathing</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usn_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-usn-9">[9]</a></sup> The higher gas density also causes gas mixing within the lung to be less efficient, thus increasing the dead space .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AD0725851_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-AD0725851-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>The diver is deliberately <a href="/wiki/Hypoventilation" title="Hypoventilation">hypoventilating</a>, known as "skip breathing".</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Skip_breathing">Skip breathing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypercapnia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Skip breathing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Skip breathing is a controversial technique to conserve <a href="/wiki/Breathing_gas" title="Breathing gas">breathing gas</a> when using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open-circuit_scuba" title="Open-circuit scuba">open-circuit scuba</a>, which consists of briefly holding one's breath between inhalation and exhalation (i.e., "skipping" a breath). It leads to CO<sub>2</sub> not being exhaled efficiently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cheshire2001_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cheshire2001-15">[15]</a></sup> The risk of burst lung (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulmonary_barotrauma" title="Pulmonary barotrauma">pulmonary barotrauma</a> of ascent) is increased if the breath is held while ascending.  It is particularly counterproductive with a <a href="/wiki/Rebreather" title="Rebreather">rebreather</a>, where the act of breathing pumps the gas around the "loop", pushing carbon dioxide through the scrubber and mixing freshly injected oxygen.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rebreathers">Rebreathers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypercapnia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Rebreathers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In closed-circuit <a href="/wiki/Scuba_set" title="Scuba set">SCUBA</a> (rebreather) diving, exhaled carbon dioxide must be removed from the breathing system, usually by a <a href="/wiki/Scrubber" title="Scrubber">scrubber</a> containing a solid chemical compound with a high affinity for CO<sub>2</sub>, such as soda lime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rebreather2.0_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-rebreather2.0-16">[16]</a></sup> If not removed from the system, it may be reinhaled, causing an increase in the inhaled concentration.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="MRI">MRI</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypercapnia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: MRI">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The hypercapnic state is routinely used to calibrate blood oxygen-level dependent <a href="/wiki/Functional_magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Functional magnetic resonance imaging">functional magnetic resonance imaging</a> (BOLD fMRI), a modality that is sensitive to changes in blood oxygenation. However, this calibration crucially relies on the assumption that hypercapnia has no effect on neuronal function, which is a matter of debate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ing_2014_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ing_2014-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names">Names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypercapnia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The spelling "hypercapnea" is occasionally seen in published medical articles (44 results in a PubMed search in 2016), but it is not entered in major dictionaries and is not tied to any <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymology</a> that involves the <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-pnea#Suffix" title="wikt:-pnea">-pnea</a></i> suffix. It is a misspelling by writers who misunderstand that the word <i>hypercapnia</i> does not end in the same suffix that <i><a href="/wiki/Apnea" title="Apnea">apnea</a></i> does.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
