<p><b>Germanium dioxide</b>, also called <b>germanium oxide</b> and <b>germania</b>, is an <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the chemical formula <a href="/wiki/Germanium" title="Germanium">Ge</a><a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">O</a><sub>2</sub>.  It is the main commercial source of germanium. It also forms as a <a href="/wiki/Passivation_(chemistry)" title="Passivation (chemistry)">passivation layer</a> on pure germanium in contact with atmospheric oxygen.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Germanium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The two predominant polymorphs of GeO<sub>2</sub> are hexagonal and tetragonal. Hexagonal GeO<sub>2</sub> has the same structure as β-quartz, with germanium having <a href="/wiki/Coordination_number" title="Coordination number">coordination number</a> 4.  Tetragonal GeO<sub>2</sub> (the mineral <a href="/wiki/Argutite" title="Argutite">argutite</a>) has the <a href="/wiki/Rutile" title="Rutile">rutile</a>-like structure seen in <a href="/wiki/Stishovite" title="Stishovite">stishovite</a>.  In this motif, germanium has the coordination number 6.  An amorphous (glassy) form of GeO<sub>2</sub> is similar to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fused_silica" title="Fused silica">fused silica</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Germanium dioxide can be prepared in both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystalline" title="Crystalline">crystalline</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amorphous" title="Amorphous">amorphous</a> forms. At ambient pressure the amorphous structure is formed by a network of GeO<sub>4</sub> tetrahedra. At elevated pressure up to approximately 9 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPa" title="GPa">GPa</a> the germanium average <a href="/wiki/Coordination_number" title="Coordination number">coordination number</a> steadily increases from 4 to around 5 with a corresponding increase in the Ge-O bond distance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Drewitt2010_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Drewitt2010-2">[2]</a></sup> At higher pressures, up to approximately 15 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPa" title="GPa">GPa</a>, the germanium <a href="/wiki/Coordination_number" title="Coordination number">coordination number</a> increases to 6 and the dense network structure is composed of GeO<sub>6</sub> octahedra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guthrie_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guthrie-3">[3]</a></sup> When the pressure is subsequently reduced, the structure reverts to the tetrahedral form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Drewitt2010_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Drewitt2010-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guthrie_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guthrie-3">[3]</a></sup>  At high pressure, the rutile form converts to an orthorhombic CaCl<sub>2</sub> form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Germanium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Heating germanium dioxide with powdered <a href="/wiki/Germanium" title="Germanium">germanium</a> at 1000 °C forms <a href="/wiki/Germanium_monoxide" title="Germanium monoxide">germanium monoxide</a> (GeO).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The hexagonal (d = 4.29 g/cm3) form of germanium dioxide is more soluble than the rutile (d = 6.27 g/cm3) form and dissolves to form germanic acid, H<sub>4</sub>GeO<sub>4</sub> or Ge(OH)<sub>4</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-5">[5]</a></sup> GeO<sub>2</sub> is only slightly soluble in acid but dissolves more readily in alkali to give <a href="/wiki/Germanate" title="Germanate">germanates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>In contact with <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>, it releases the volatile and corrosive <a href="/wiki/Germanium_tetrachloride" title="Germanium tetrachloride">germanium tetrachloride</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Germanium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive index</a> (1.7) and <a href="/wiki/Dispersion_(optics)" title="Dispersion (optics)">optical dispersion</a> properties of germanium dioxide makes it useful as an optical material for <a href="/wiki/Wide-angle_lens" title="Wide-angle lens">wide-angle lenses</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Optical_microscope" title="Optical microscope">optical microscope</a> <a href="/wiki/Objective_(optics)" title="Objective (optics)">objective</a> <a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)" title="Lens (optics)">lenses</a>, and for the core of fiber-optic lines. See <a href="/wiki/Optical_fiber" title="Optical fiber">Optical fiber</a> for specifics on the manufacturing process. Both Germanium and its glass oxide, GeO<sub>2</sub> are transparent to the <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> spectrum. The glass can be manufactured into IR windows and lenses, used for <a href="/wiki/Night_vision_device" title="Night vision device">night-vision</a> technology in the military, luxury vehicles,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Thermographic_camera" title="Thermographic camera">Thermographic cameras</a>. GeO<sub>2</sub> is preferred over other IR transparent glasses because it is mechanically strong and therefore preferred for rugged military usage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>A mixture of silicon dioxide and germanium dioxide ("silica-germania") is used as an optical material for <a href="/wiki/Optical_fiber" title="Optical fiber">optical fibers</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_waveguide" title="Optical waveguide">optical waveguides</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-8">[8]</a></sup> Controlling the ratio of the elements allows precise control of refractive index. Silica-germania glasses have lower viscosity and higher refractive index than pure silica. Germania replaced <a href="/wiki/Titanium_dioxide" title="Titanium dioxide">titania</a> as the silica dopant for silica fiber, eliminating the need for subsequent heat treatment, which made the fibers brittle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Germanium dioxide is also used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a> in production of <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene_terephthalate" title="Polyethylene terephthalate">polyethylene terephthalate</a> resin,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thiele_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thiele-10">[10]</a></sup> and for production of other germanium compounds. It is used as a feedstock for production of some <a href="/wiki/Phosphor" title="Phosphor">phosphors</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semiconductor_material" title="Semiconductor material">semiconductor materials</a>.
</p><p>Germanium dioxide is used in <a href="/wiki/Algaculture" title="Algaculture">algaculture</a> as an inhibitor of unwanted <a href="/wiki/Diatom" title="Diatom">diatom</a> growth in algal cultures, since contamination with the comparatively fast-growing diatoms often inhibits the growth of or outcompetes the original algae strains. GeO<sub>2</sub> is readily taken up by diatoms and leads to silicon being substituted by germanium in biochemical processes within the diatoms, causing a significant reduction of the diatoms' growth rate or even their complete elimination, with little effect on non-diatom algal species. For this application, the concentration of germanium dioxide typically used in the culture medium is between 1 and 10 mg/l, depending on the stage of the contamination and the species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Andersen_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Andersen-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicity_and_medical">Toxicity and medical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Germanium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Toxicity and medical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Germanium dioxide has low toxicity, but in higher doses it is <a href="/wiki/Nephrotoxicity" title="Nephrotoxicity">nephrotoxic</a>.
</p><p>Germanium dioxide is used as a germanium supplement in some questionable <a href="/wiki/Dietary_supplement" title="Dietary supplement">dietary supplements</a> and "miracle cures".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>  High doses of these resulted in several cases of germanium poisonings.
</p>
