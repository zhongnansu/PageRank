<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Heterotroph</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Auto-and_heterotrophs.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3167" data-file-width="2722" height="349" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Auto-and_heterotrophs.png/300px-Auto-and_heterotrophs.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Auto-and_heterotrophs.png/450px-Auto-and_heterotrophs.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Auto-and_heterotrophs.png/600px-Auto-and_heterotrophs.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Auto-and_heterotrophs.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cycle between <a href="/wiki/Autotroph" title="Autotroph">autotrophs</a> and <a class="mw-selflink selflink">heterotrophs</a>. Autotrophs use light, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>), and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> and complex organic compounds, mainly through the process of <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> (green arrow). Both types of organisms use such compounds via <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">cellular respiration</a> to both generate <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a> and again form CO<sub>2</sub> and water (two red arrows).</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>heterotroph</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'h' in 'hi'">h</span><span title="/ɛ/: 'e' in 'dress'">ɛ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ər/: 'er' in 'letter'">ər</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span></span>, <wbr/>-<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span></span>/</a></span></span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Ancient Greek ἕτερος <i>héteros</i> = "other" plus <i>trophe</i> = "nutrition") is an <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organism</a> that cannot produce its own food, relying instead on the intake of nutrition from other sources of organic carbon, mainly plant or animal matter. In the food chain, heterotrophs are secondary and tertiary consumers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-essential_microbiology_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-essential_microbiology-3">[3]</a></sup> Ninety-five percent or more of all types of living organisms are heterotrophic, including all <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a> and some <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protists" title="Protists">protists</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cell_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-cell-4">[4]</a></sup> The term heterotroph arose in <a href="/wiki/Microbiology" title="Microbiology">microbiology</a> in 1946 as part of a classification of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microorganisms" title="Microorganisms">microorganisms</a> based on their type of <a href="/wiki/Nutrition" title="Nutrition">nutrition</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The term is now used in many fields, such as <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> in describing the <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a>.
</p><p>Heterotrophs may be subdivided according to their energy source. If the heterotroph uses chemical energy, it is a <a href="/wiki/Chemotroph" title="Chemotroph">chemoheterotroph</a> (e.g., humans and mushrooms). If it uses light for energy, then it is a <a href="/wiki/Photoheterotroph" title="Photoheterotroph">photoheterotroph</a> (e.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_non-sulfur_bacteria" title="Green non-sulfur bacteria">green non-sulfur bacteria</a>).
</p><p>Heterotrophs represent one of the two mechanisms of nutrition (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trophic_levels" title="Trophic levels">trophic levels</a>), the other being <a href="/wiki/Autotroph" title="Autotroph">autotrophs</a> (auto = self, troph = nutrition). Autotrophs use energy from <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photoautotroph" title="Photoautotroph">photoautotrophs</a>) or inorganic compounds (<a href="/wiki/Lithoautotroph" title="Lithoautotroph">lithoautotrophs</a>) to convert inorganic <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> to organic carbon compounds and energy to sustain their life. Comparing the two in layman's terms, heterotrophs (such as animals) eat either autotrophs (such as plants) or other heterotrophs, or both.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Detritivore" title="Detritivore">Detritivores</a> are heterotrophs which obtain <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrients</a> by consuming <a href="/wiki/Detritus" title="Detritus">detritus</a> (decomposing plant and animal parts as well as <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">feces</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saprotroph" title="Saprotroph">Saprotrophs</a> (also called lysotrophs) are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemoheterotroph" title="Chemoheterotroph">chemoheterotrophs</a> that use <a href="/wiki/Extracellular_digestion" title="Extracellular digestion">extracellular digestion</a> in processing decayed organic matter. It is a term most often associated with <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungi</a>. The process is most often facilitated through the <a href="/wiki/Active_transport" title="Active transport">active transport</a> of such materials through <a href="/wiki/Endocytosis" title="Endocytosis">endocytosis</a> within the internal mycelium and its constituent <a href="/wiki/Hypha" title="Hypha">hyphae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-advanced_biology_principles_1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-advanced_biology_principles_1-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heterotroph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Heterotrophs can be <a href="/wiki/Organotroph" title="Organotroph">organotrophs</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lithotroph" title="Lithotroph">lithotrophs</a>. Organotrophs exploit reduced carbon compounds as electron sources, like carbohydrates, fats, and proteins from plants and animals. On the other hand, lithotrophs use inorganic compounds, such as ammonium, nitrite, and sulfur to obtain electron sources. Another way of classifying different heterotrophs is by assigning them as <a href="/wiki/Chemotroph" title="Chemotroph">chemotrophs</a> or <a href="/wiki/Phototroph" title="Phototroph">phototrophs</a>. Phototrophs utilize light to obtain energy and carry out metabolic processes, whereas chemotrophs use the energy obtained by the oxidation of chemicals from their environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Photoorganoheterotrophs, such as <a href="/wiki/Rhodospirillaceae" title="Rhodospirillaceae">Rhodospirillaceae</a> and purple non-sulfur bacteria synthesize organic compounds using sunlight coupled with oxidation of organic substances, including <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>, elemental <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thiosulfate" title="Thiosulfate">thiosulfate</a>, and molecular <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>.
They use organic compounds to build structures. They do not fix carbon dioxide and apparently do not have the <a href="/wiki/Light-independent_reactions#Calvin_Cycle" title="Light-independent reactions">Calvin cycle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-botany_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-botany-9">[9]</a></sup> Chemolithoheterotrophs like the <i>Oceanithermus profundus</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> obtain energy from the oxidation of inorganic compounds. <a href="/wiki/Mixotroph" title="Mixotroph">Mixotrophs</a> (or facultative chemolithotroph) can use either carbon dioxide or organic carbon as the carbon source, meaning that mixotrophs have the ability to use both heterotrophic methods as well as autotrophic methods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biogeochemistry_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-biogeochemistry-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prokaryotes_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-prokaryotes-12">[12]</a></sup>
Although mixotrophs have the ability to grow both under both heterotrophic and autotrophic conditions, <i>C. vulgaris</i> have higher biomass and lipid productivity when growing under heterotrophic conditions compared to autotrophic conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Heterotrophs, by consuming reduced carbon compounds, are able to use all the energy that they obtain from food for growth and reproduction, unlike autotrophs, which must use some of their energy for carbon fixation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-botany_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-botany-9">[9]</a></sup> Both heterotrophs and autotrophs alike are usually dependent on the metabolic activities of other organisms for nutrients other than carbon, including nitrogen, phosphorus, and sulfur, and can die from lack of food that supplies these nutrients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> This applies not only to animals and fungi but also to bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-botany_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-botany-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heterotroph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Consumer_(food_chain)" title="Consumer (food chain)">Consumer (food chain)</a></div>
<p>Many heterotrophs are <a href="/wiki/Organotroph" title="Organotroph">chemoorganoheterotrophs</a> that use organic carbon (e.g. glucose) as their carbon source, and organic chemicals (e.g. carbohydrates, lipids, proteins) as their energy and electron sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Heterotrophs function as <a href="/wiki/Consumer_(food_chain)" title="Consumer (food chain)">consumers in food chain</a>: they obtain these nutrients from <a href="/wiki/Saprotrophic_nutrition" title="Saprotrophic nutrition">saprotrophic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parasitic_nutrition" title="Parasitic nutrition">parasitic</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Holozoic_nutrition" title="Holozoic nutrition">holozoic nutrients</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> They break down complex organic compounds (e.g., carbohydrates, fats, and proteins) produced by autotrophs into simpler compounds (e.g., carbohydrates into <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a>, fats into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatty_acids" title="Fatty acids">fatty acids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glycerol" title="Glycerol">glycerol</a>, and proteins into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amino_acids" title="Amino acids">amino acids</a>). They release energy by oxidizing carbon and hydrogen atoms present in carbohydrates, lipids, and proteins to carbon dioxide and water, respectively.
</p><p>They can catabolize organic compounds by respiration, fermentation, or both. <a href="/wiki/Fermentation" title="Fermentation">Fermenting</a> heterotrophs are either facultative or obligate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anaerobes" title="Anaerobes">anaerobes</a> that carry out fermentation in low oxygen environments, in which the production of ATP is commonly coupled with <a href="/wiki/Substrate-level_phosphorylation" title="Substrate-level phosphorylation">substrate-level phosphorylation</a> and the production of end products (e.g. alcohol, CO2, sulfide).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">[17]</a></sup> These products can then serve as the substrates for other bacteria in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Anaerobic_digest&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Anaerobic digest (page does not exist)">anaerobic digest</a>, and be converted into CO<sub>2</sub> and CH<sub>4</sub>, which is an important step for the carbon cycle for removing organic fermentation products from anaerobic environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">[17]</a></sup> Heterotrophs can undergo <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">respiration</a>, in which ATP production is coupled with <a href="/wiki/Oxidative_phosphorylation" title="Oxidative phosphorylation">oxidative phosphorylation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-18">[18]</a></sup> This leads to the release of oxidized carbon wastes such as CO<sub>2</sub> and reduced wastes like H<sub>2</sub>O, H<sub>2</sub>S, or N<sub>2</sub>O into the atmosphere. Heterotrophic microbes’ respiration and fermentation account for a large portion of the release of CO<sub>2</sub> into the atmosphere, making it available for autotrophs as a source of nutrient and plants as a cellulose synthesis substrate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Respiration in heterotrophs is often accompanied by <a href="/wiki/Mineralization_(biology)" title="Mineralization (biology)">mineralization</a>, the process of converting organic compounds to inorganic forms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-19">[19]</a></sup> When the organic nutrient source taken in by the heterotroph contains essential elements such as N, S, P in addition to C, H, and O, they are often removed first to proceed with the oxidation of organic nutrient and production of ATP via respiration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-19">[19]</a></sup> S and N in organic carbon source are transformed into H<sub>2</sub>S and NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup> through desulfurylation and <a href="/wiki/Deamination" title="Deamination">deamination</a>, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-:1-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-:2-18">[18]</a></sup> Heterotrophs also allow for <a href="/wiki/Dephosphorylation" title="Dephosphorylation">dephosphorylation</a> as part of <a href="/wiki/Decomposition" title="Decomposition">decomposition</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-:2-18">[18]</a></sup> The conversion of N and S from organic form to inorganic form is a critical part of the <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_cycle" title="Nitrogen cycle">nitrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_cycle" title="Sulfur cycle">sulfur cycle</a>. H<sub>2</sub>S formed from desulfurylation is further oxidized by lithotrophs and phototrophs while NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup> formed from deamination is further oxidized by lithotrophs to the forms available to plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_19-4"><a href="#cite_note-:1-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_18-4"><a href="#cite_note-:2-18">[18]</a></sup> Heterotrophs’ ability to mineralize essential elements is critical to plant survival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_18-5"><a href="#cite_note-:2-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Most <a href="/wiki/Opisthokont" title="Opisthokont">opisthokonts</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prokaryotes" title="Prokaryotes">prokaryotes</a> are heterotrophic; in particular, all animals and fungi are heterotrophs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cell_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-cell-4">[4]</a></sup> Some animals, such as <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a>, form <a href="/wiki/Symbiosis" title="Symbiosis">symbiotic</a> relationships with autotrophs and obtain organic carbon in this way. Furthermore, some <a href="/wiki/Parasitic_plant" title="Parasitic plant">parasitic plants</a> have also turned fully or partially heterotrophic, while <a href="/wiki/Carnivorous_plant" title="Carnivorous plant">carnivorous plants</a> consume animals to augment their nitrogen supply while remaining autotrophic.
</p><p>Animals are classified as heterotrophs by ingestion, fungi are classified as heterotrophs by absorption.
