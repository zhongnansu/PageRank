<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Coal liquefaction</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Coal liquefaction</b> is a process of converting <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> into liquid hydrocarbons: <a href="/wiki/Liquid_fuel" title="Liquid fuel">liquid fuels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Petrochemical" title="Petrochemical">petrochemicals</a>. This process is often known as "Coal to X", where X can be many different hydrocarbon-based products. However, the most common process chain is "Coal to Liquid Fuels" (CTL).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_background">Historical background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_liquefaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historical background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Coal liquefaction is an old technique that originally was developed at the beginning of the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hook&amp;Aleklett_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hook&amp;Aleklett-1">[1]</a></sup> The best known CTL process is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fischer-Tropsch" title="Fischer-Tropsch">Fischer-Tropsch</a> (FT) synthesis, named after the inventors <a href="/wiki/Franz_Joseph_Emil_Fischer" title="Franz Joseph Emil Fischer">Franz Fischer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hans_Tropsch" title="Hans Tropsch">Hans Tropsch</a> from the Kaiser Wilhelm Institute in the 1920s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davis&amp;Occelli_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davis&amp;Occelli-2">[2]</a></sup>  The FT synthesis is the basis for indirect coal liquefaction (ICL) technology. <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Bergius" title="Friedrich Bergius">Friedrich Bergius</a>, also a German chemist, invented direct coal liquefaction (DCL) as a way to convert lignite into synthetic oil in 1913.
</p><p>Coal liquefaction became an integral part of the German industry and helped to fuel its military during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Coal liquefaction was an important part of <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a>'s four-year plan of 1936.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stranges_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stranges-3">[3]</a></sup> In the mid 1930s, companies like <a href="/wiki/IG_Farben" title="IG Farben">IG Farben</a> and Ruhrchemie initiated industrial production of synthetic fuels derived from coal. This led to the construction of twelve DCL plants using hydrogenation and nine ICL plants using Fischer-Tropsch synthesis by the end of World War II. In total, CTL provided 92% of Germany’s air fuel and over 50% of its petroleum supply in the 1940s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hook&amp;Aleklett_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hook&amp;Aleklett-1">[1]</a></sup> The DCL and ICL plants effectively complemented each other rather than competed. The reason for this is that coal hydrogenation yields high quality gasoline for aviation and motors, while FT synthesis chiefly produced high quality diesel, lubrication oil, and waxes together with some smaller amounts of lower quality motor gasoline. The DCL plants were also more developed as <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a> - the only coal available in many parts of Germany - worked better with hydrogenation than with FT synthesis. After the war, Germany had to abandon its synthetic fuel production as it was prohibited by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Potsdam_conference" title="Potsdam conference">Potsdam conference</a> in 1945.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stranges_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stranges-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>South Africa developed its own CTL technology in the 1950s. The South African Coal, Oil and Gas Corporation (<a href="/wiki/Sasol" title="Sasol">Sasol</a>) was founded in 1950 as part of industrialization process that the South African government considered essential for continued economic development and autonomy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> However, South Africa had no domestic oil reserves and this made the country very vulnerable to disruption of supplies coming from outside, albeit for different reasons at different times. Sasol was a successful way to protect the country’s balance of payment against the increasing dependence on foreign oil. For years its principal product was synthetic fuel and this business enjoyed significant government protection in South Africa during the <a href="/wiki/Apartheid" title="Apartheid">apartheid</a> years for its contribution to domestic <a href="/wiki/Energy_security" title="Energy security">energy security</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Although it was generally much more expensive to produce oil from coal than from natural petroleum, the political as well as economic importance of achieving as much independence as possible in this sphere was sufficient to overcome any objections. Early attempts to attract private capital, foreign or domestic, were unsuccessful, and it was only with state support that the coal liquefaction could start. CTL continued to play a vital part in South Africa's national economy, providing around 30% of its domestic fuel demand. The <a href="/wiki/Democratization" title="Democratization">democratization</a> in the 1990s has made Sasol search for products that could prove more competitive in the global marketplace, and as of the new millennium Sasol is focusing primarily on its petrochemical business, as well as on efforts to convert natural gas into crude oil (<a href="/wiki/Gas_to_liquids" title="Gas to liquids">GTL</a>) usings its expertise in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fischer-Tropsch_synthesis" title="Fischer-Tropsch synthesis">Fischer-Tropsch synthesis</a>.
</p><p>CTL technologies have steadily improved since the Second World War. Technical development has resulted in a variety of systems capable of handling a wide array of coal types. However, only a few enterprises based on generating liquid fuels from coal have been undertaken, most of them based on ICL-technology. The most successful one is the South African company <a href="/wiki/Sasol" title="Sasol">Sasol</a>. CTL also received new interest in the early-2000s as a possible mitigation option for reducing oil dependence as rising oil prices and concerns over <a href="/wiki/Peak_oil" title="Peak oil">peak oil</a> made planners rethink existing supply chains for liquid fuels.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods">Methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_liquefaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Specific liquefaction technologies generally fall into two categories: direct (DCL) and indirect liquefaction (ICL) processes. Direct processes are based on approaches such as <a href="/wiki/Carbonization" title="Carbonization">carbonization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">pyrolysis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-handbook2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-handbook2-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Indirect liquefaction processes generally involve gasification of coal to a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, often known as synthetic gas or simply <a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">syngas</a>, and then using a suitable process such as <a href="/wiki/Fischer%E2%80%93Tropsch_process" title="Fischer–Tropsch process">Fischer–Tropsch process</a> to convert the syngas mixture into liquid hydrocarbons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>In contrast, direct liquefaction processes convert coal into liquids directly without having to rely on intermediate steps by breaking down the organic structure of coal with application of solvents or catalysts in a high pressure and temperature environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Since liquid hydrocarbons generally have a higher hydrogen-carbon molar ratio than coals, either hydrogenation or carbon-rejection processes must be employed in both ICL and DCL technologies.
</p><p>At industrial scales (i.e. thousands of barrels/day) a coal liquefaction plant typically requires multibillion-dollar capital investments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hook_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-hook-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pyrolysis_and_carbonization_processes">Pyrolysis and carbonization processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_liquefaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pyrolysis and carbonization processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A number of carbonization processes exist. The carbonization conversion typically occurs through <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">pyrolysis</a> or <a href="/wiki/Destructive_distillation" title="Destructive distillation">destructive distillation</a>. It produces condensable <a href="/wiki/Coal_tar" title="Coal tar">coal tar</a>, oil and water vapor, non-condensable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthetic_gas" title="Synthetic gas">synthetic gas</a>, and a solid residue-<a href="/wiki/Char" title="Char">char</a>.
</p><p>One typical example of carbonization is the <a href="/wiki/Karrick_process" title="Karrick process">Karrick process</a>. In this low-temperature <a href="/wiki/Carbonization" title="Carbonization">carbonization</a> process, coal is heated at 680 °F (360 °C) to 1,380 °F (750 °C) in the absence of air. These temperatures optimize the production of coal tars richer in lighter hydrocarbons than normal coal tar. However, any produced liquids are mostly a by-product and the main product is semi-coke - a solid and smokeless fuel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hook&amp;Aleklett_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hook&amp;Aleklett-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The COED Process, developed by <a href="/wiki/FMC_Corporation" title="FMC Corporation">FMC Corporation</a>, uses a <a href="/wiki/Fluidized_bed" title="Fluidized bed">fluidized bed</a> for processing, in combination with increasing temperature, through four stages of pyrolysis. Heat is transferred by hot gases produced by combustion of part of the produced char. A modification of this process, the COGAS Process, involves the addition of gasification of char.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lee3_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-lee3-10">[10]</a></sup> The TOSCOAL Process, an analogue to the <a href="/wiki/TOSCO_II_process" title="TOSCO II process">TOSCO II oil shale retorting process</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lurgi-Ruhrgas_process" title="Lurgi-Ruhrgas process">Lurgi-Ruhrgas process</a>, which is also used for the <a href="/wiki/Shale_oil_extraction" title="Shale oil extraction">shale oil extraction</a>, uses hot recycled solids for the heat transfer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lee3_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-lee3-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Liquid yields of pyrolysis and Karrick processes are generally too low for practical use for synthetic liquid fuel production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ekinci2002_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ekinci2002-11">[11]</a></sup> Furthermore, the resulting liquids are generally of low quality and require further treatment before they can be usable as motor fuels. Resulting coal tars and oils from pyrolysis are then further processed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrotreating" title="Hydrotreating">hydrotreating</a> to remove <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> species, after which they are finally processed into liquid fuels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lee3_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-lee3-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>In summary, there is little possibility that this process will yield economically viable volumes of liquid fuel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hook_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-hook-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrogenation_processes">Hydrogenation processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_liquefaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hydrogenation processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>After <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> several plants based on this technology were built in Germany; these plants were extensively used during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> to supply Germany with fuel and lubricants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The Kohleoel Process, developed in Germany by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RAG_Aktiengesellschaft" title="RAG Aktiengesellschaft">Ruhrkohle</a> and <a href="/wiki/VEBA" title="VEBA">VEBA</a>, was used in the demonstration plant with the capacity of 200 ton of <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a> per day, built in <a href="/wiki/Bottrop" title="Bottrop">Bottrop</a>, Germany. This plant operated from 1981 to 1987. In this process, coal is mixed with a recycle solvent and iron catalyst. After preheating and pressurizing, H<sub>2</sub> is added. The process takes place in a tubular reactor at the pressure of 300 bar and at the temperature of 470 °C (880 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dti_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-dti-13">[13]</a></sup>  This process was also explored by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SASOL" title="SASOL">SASOL</a> in South Africa.
</p><p>In 1970-1980s, Japanese companies <a href="/wiki/JFE_Holdings" title="JFE Holdings">Nippon Kokan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sumitomo_Metal_Industries" title="Sumitomo Metal Industries">Sumitomo Metal Industries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Heavy_Industries" title="Mitsubishi Heavy Industries">Mitsubishi Heavy Industries</a> developed the NEDOL process. In this process, coal is mixed with a recycled solvent and a synthetic iron-based catalyst; after preheating, H<sub>2</sub> is added. The reaction takes place in a tubular reactor at a temperature between 430 °C (810 °F) and 465 °C (870 °F) at the pressure 150-200 bar.  The produced oil has low quality and requires intensive upgrading.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dti_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-dti-13">[13]</a></sup>  H-Coal process, developed by Hydrocarbon Research, Inc., in 1963, mixes pulverized coal with recycled liquids, hydrogen and catalyst in the ebullated bed reactor. Advantages of this process are that dissolution and oil upgrading are taking place in the single reactor, products have high H/C ratio, and a fast reaction time, while the main disadvantages are high gas yield (this is basically a thermal cracking process), high hydrogen consumption, and limitation of oil usage only as a boiler oil because of impurities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lee3_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-lee3-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The SRC-I and SRC-II (Solvent Refined Coal) processes were developed by <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Oil" title="Gulf Oil">Gulf Oil</a> and implemented as pilot plants in the United States in the 1960s and 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dti_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-dti-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Nuclear Utility Services Corporation developed hydrogenation process which was patented by Wilburn C. Schroeder in 1976. The process involved dried, pulverized coal mixed with roughly 1wt% <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum" title="Molybdenum">molybdenum</a> catalysts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-handbook2_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-handbook2-6">[6]</a></sup>  Hydrogenation occurred by use of high temperature and pressure <a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">synthesis gas</a> produced in a separate gasifier. The process ultimately yielded a synthetic crude product, <a href="/wiki/Naphtha" title="Naphtha">Naphtha</a>, a limited amount of C<sub>3</sub>/C<sub>4</sub> gas, light-medium weight liquids (C<sub>5</sub>-C<sub>10</sub>) suitable for use as fuels, small amounts of NH<sub>3</sub> and significant amounts of CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>  Other single-stage hydrogenation processes are the Exxon Donor Solvent Process, the Imhausen High-pressure Process, and the Conoco Zinc Chloride Process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dti_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-dti-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are also a number of two-stage direct liquefaction processes; however, after the 1980s only the Catalytic Two-stage Liquefaction Process, modified from the H-Coal Process; the Liquid Solvent Extraction Process by <a href="/wiki/British_Coal" title="British Coal">British Coal</a>; and the Brown Coal Liquefaction Process of Japan have been developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dti_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-dti-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Shenhua_Group" title="Shenhua Group">Shenhua</a>, a Chinese coal mining company, decided in 2002 to build a direct liquefaction plant in Erdos, Inner Mongolia (<a href="/wiki/Erdos_CTL" title="Erdos CTL">Erdos CTL</a>), with barrel capacity of 20 thousand barrels per day (3.2<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>3</sup> m<sup>3</sup>/d) of liquid products including diesel oil, liquefied petroleum gas (LPG) and naphtha (petroleum ether). First tests were implemented at the end of 2008. A second and longer test campaign was started in October 2009. In 2011, Shenhua Group reported that the direct liquefaction plant had been in continuous and stable operations since November 2010, and that Shenhua had made 800 million yuan ($125.1 million) in earnings before taxes in the first six months of 2011 on the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Chevron Corporation developed a process invented by Joel W. Rosenthal called the Chevron Coal Liquefaction Process (CCLP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>  It is unique due the close-coupling of the non-catalytic dissolver and the catalytic <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hydroprocessing&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hydroprocessing (page does not exist)">hydroprocessing</a> unit.  The oil produced had properties that were unique when compared to other coal oils;  it was lighter and had far fewer heteroatom impurities.  The process was scaled-up to the 6 ton per day level, but not proven commercially.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indirect_conversion_processes">Indirect conversion processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_liquefaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Indirect conversion processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fischer-Tropsch_process" title="Fischer-Tropsch process">Fischer-Tropsch process</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gas_to_liquids" title="Gas to liquids">Gas to liquids</a></div>
<p>Indirect coal liquefaction (ICL) processes operate in two stages. In the first stage, coal is converted into <a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">syngas</a> (a purified mixture of CO and H<sub>2</sub> gas). In the second stage, the syngas is converted into light hydrocarbons using one of three main processes: Fischer-Tropsch synthesis, <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">Methanol</a> synthesis with subsequent conversion to <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petrochemicals" title="Petrochemicals">petrochemicals</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Methanation" title="Methanation">methanation</a>. Fischer-Tropsch is the oldest of the ICL processes.
</p><p>In methanol synthesis processes <a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">syngas</a> is converted to <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>, which is subsequently polymerized into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkanes" title="Alkanes">alkanes</a> over a <a href="/wiki/Zeolite" title="Zeolite">zeolite</a> catalyst. This process, under the moniker MTG (MTG for "Methanol To Gasoline"), was developed by <a href="/wiki/Mobil" title="Mobil">Mobil</a> in the early 1970s, and is being tested at a demonstration plant by <a href="/wiki/Jincheng_Anthracite_Mining_Group" title="Jincheng Anthracite Mining Group">Jincheng Anthracite Mining Group</a> (JAMG) in Shanxi, China. Based on this methanol synthesis, China has also developed a strong <a class="external text" href="http://worldctx.com/documentation/coal-to-chemicals/" rel="nofollow">coal-to-chemicals</a> industry, with outputs such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olefins" title="Olefins">olefins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">MEG</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_ether" title="Dimethyl ether">DME</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aromatic" title="Aromatic">aromatics</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Methanation" title="Methanation">Methanation</a> reaction converts syngas to substitute <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> (SNG). The Great Plains Gasification Plant in Beulah, North Dakota is a coal-to-SNG facility producing 160 million cubic feet per day of SNG, and has been in operation since 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Several <a class="external text" href="http://worldctx.com/documentation/coal-to-sng/" rel="nofollow">coal-to-SNG</a> plants are in operation or in project in China, South Korea and India.
</p><p>The above instances of commercial plants based on indirect coal liquefaction processes, as well as many others not listed here including those in planning stages and under construction, are tabulated in the Gasification Technologies Council's World Gasification Database.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GTC_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-GTC-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_considerations">Environmental considerations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_liquefaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Environmental considerations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_the_coal_industry" title="Environmental impact of the coal industry">Environmental impact of the coal industry</a></div>
<p>Typically coal liquefaction processes are associated with significant CO<sub>2</sub> emissions from the gasification process or as well as from generation of necessary process heat and electricity inputs to the liquefaction reactors,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hook_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-hook-9">[9]</a></sup> thus releasing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gases</a> that can contribute to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropogenic_global_warming" title="Anthropogenic global warming">anthropogenic global warming</a>. Especially if coal liquefaction is conducted without any <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a> technologies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> There are technically feasible low-emission configurations of CTL plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>High water consumption in water-gas shift or methane steam reforming reactions is another adverse environmental effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hook_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-hook-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Pyrolysis of coal produces <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbons" title="Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons">polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons</a>, which are known <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogens</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> On the other hand, synthetic fuels produced by indirect coal liquefaction processes tend to be 'cleaner' than naturally occurring crudes, as heteroatom (e.g. sulfur) compounds are not synthesized or are excluded from the final product.
</p><p>CO<sub>2</sub> emission control at <a href="/wiki/Erdos_CTL" title="Erdos CTL">Erdos CTL</a>, an Inner Mongolian plant with a <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a> demonstration project, involves injecting CO<sub>2</sub> into the saline aquifer of Erdos Basin, at a rate of 100,000 tonnes per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Independent_sources" title="Wikipedia:Independent sources"><span title="This claim needs a reference to a independent, third-party source. (August 2014)">third-party source needed</span></a></i>]</sup> As of late October 2013, an accumulated amount of 154,000 tonnes of CO<sub>2</sub>  had been injected since 2010, which reached or exceeded the design value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Independent_sources" title="Wikipedia:Independent sources"><span title="This claim needs a reference to a independent, third-party source. (August 2014)">third-party source needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Ultimately, coal liquefaction-derived fuels will be judged relative to targets established for low-<a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions fuels.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style_(words_to_watch)#Editorializing" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style (words to watch)"><span title="A statement that draws an independent conclusion. (August 2014)">editorializing</span></a></i>]</sup> For example, in the United States, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renewable_Fuel_Standard" title="Renewable Fuel Standard">Renewable Fuel Standard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Low-carbon_fuel_standard" title="Low-carbon fuel standard">Low-carbon fuel standard</a> such as enacted in the State of California reflect an increasing demand for low carbon-footprint fuels. Also, legislation in the United States has restricted the military's use of alternative liquid fuels to only those demonstrated to have life-cycle GHG emissions less than or equal to those of their conventional petroleum-based equivalent, as required by Section 526 of the Energy Independence and Security Act (EISA) of 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_and_development_of_coal_liquefaction">Research and development of coal liquefaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_liquefaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Research and development of coal liquefaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The United States military has an active program to promote alternative fuels use,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and utilizing vast domestic U.S. coal reserves to produce fuels through coal liquefaction would have obvious economic and security advantages. But with their higher carbon footprint, fuels from coal liquefaction face the significant challenge of reducing life-cycle GHG emissions to competitive levels, which demands continued research and development of liquefaction technology to increase efficiency and reduce emissions. A number of avenues of research &amp; development will need to be pursued, including:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">Carbon capture and storage</a> including <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">enhanced oil recovery</a> and developmental CCS methods to offset emissions from both synthesis and utilization of liquid fuels from coal,</li>
<li>Coal/biomass/natural gas feedstock blends for coal liquefaction: Utilizing carbon-neutral biomass and hydrogen-rich natural gas as co-feeds in coal liquefaction processes has significant potential for bringing fuel products' life-cycle GHG emissions into competitive ranges,</li>
<li>Hydrogen from renewables: the hydrogen demand of coal liquefaction processes might be supplied through renewable energy sources including wind, solar, and biomass, significantly reducing the emissions associated with traditional methods of hydrogen synthesis (such as steam methane reforming or char gasification), and</li>
<li>Process improvements such as intensification of the Fischer-Tropsch process, hybrid liquefaction processes, and more efficient <a href="/wiki/Air_separation" title="Air separation">air separation</a> technologies needed for production of oxygen (e.g. ceramic membrane-based oxygen separation).</li></ul>
<p>Since 2014, the U.S. Department of Energy and the Department of Defense have been collaborating on supporting new research and development in the area of coal liquefaction to produce military-specification liquid fuels, with an emphasis on jet fuel, which would be both cost-effective and in accordance with EISA Section 526.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Projects underway in this area are described under the U.S. Department of Energy National Energy Technology Laboratory's <a class="external text" href="http://www.netl.doe.gov/research/coal/energy-systems/fuels/coal-and-biomass-to-liquids/advanced-fuels-synthesis-index/" rel="nofollow">Advanced Fuels Synthesis R&amp;D area in the Coal and Coal-Biomass to Liquids Program</a>.
</p><p>Every year, a researcher or developer in coal conversion is rewarded by the industry in receiving the <a class="external text" href="http://worldctx.com/world-ctx-award/" rel="nofollow">World Carbon To X Award</a>. The 2016 Award recipient is Mr. Jona Pillay, Executive director for Gasification &amp; CTL, Jindal Steel &amp; Power Ltd (India). The 2017 Award recipient is Dr. Yao Min, Deputy General Manager of Shenhua Ningxia Coal Group (China).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>In terms of commercial development, coal conversion is experiencing a strong acceleration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Geographically, most active projects and recently commissioned operations are located in Asia, mainly in China, while U.S. projects have been delayed or canceled due to the development of shale gas and shale oil.
</p>

<li id="cite_note-dti-13"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-dti_13-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-dti_13-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-dti_13-2"><sup><i><b>c</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-dti_13-3"><sup><i><b>d</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-dti_13-4"><sup><i><b>e</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">The SRC-I pilot plant operated at Fort Lewis Wash in the 1970s but was not able to overcome lack of solvent balance problems (continual imports of solvent containing polynuclear aromatics were necessary). A SRC-I demonstration plant was scheduled to be built at Newman, KY but was cancelled in 1981. Based on 1913 work by Bergius it had been noted that certain minerals in coal ash had a mild catalytic activity, and this led to design work on a SRC-II demonstration plant to be built at Morgantown, WV. This too was cancelled in 1981. It appeared based on the work done so far to be desirable to separate the coal-dissolution and catalytic-hydrogenation functions to obtain a greater yield of <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_crude" title="Synthetic crude">synthetic crude</a> oil; this was accomplished in a small+scale pilot plant at Wilsonville, AL during 1981-85. The plant also included a critical-solvent deasher to recover a maximum amount of usable liquid product. In a commercial plant, the deasher underflow containing unreacted carbonaceous matter would be gasified to provide hydrogen to drive the process. This program ended in 1985 and the plant was scrapped.
