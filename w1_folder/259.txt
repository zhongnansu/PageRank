<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Carbon-based fuel</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Carbon-based fuel</b> is any <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a>   principally from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidation</a> or burning of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>.   Carbon-based fuels are of two main kinds, <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuels</a>  and <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>. Whereas biofuels are derived from recent-growth organic matter<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and are typically harvested, as with logging of forests and cutting of corn, fossil fuels are of prehistoric origin<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and are extracted from the ground, the principal fossil fuels being <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">oil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>.
</p><p>From an economic policy perspective, an important distinction between biofuels and fossil fuels is that only the former is <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_energy" title="Sustainable energy">sustainable</a> or <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable</a>. 
</p><p>Whereas we can continue to obtain energy from biofuels indefinitely in principle, the Earth's reserves of fossil fuels was determined millions of years ago<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and is therefore fixed as far as our foreseeable future is concerned.  The great variability in the ease of extraction of fossil fuels however makes its endgame scenario one of increasing prices over one or more centuries rather than of abrupt exhaustion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>From the perspective of climate and <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, biofuels and fossil fuels have in common that they contribute to the production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_carbon_dioxide" title="Atmospheric carbon dioxide">atmospheric carbon dioxide</a>, which has emerged in recent decades as the fastest-changing <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a>, whose principal impacts are <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a>.  However biofuels actively participate in the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a> today by photosynthesizing carbon dioxide, unlike fossil fuels whose participation was long ago, and can therefore in principle bring atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> into an equilibrium not possible with the continued use of fossil fuel.  But in practice <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> is a slow process, and the additional fuel produced by artificial methods of accelerating it such as application of fertilizer tends to be offset by the energy consumed by the accelerating processes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> to a degree currently under active debate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  In contrast the speed of photosynthesis is immaterial for fossil fuels because they had millions of years in which to accumulate. Burning of both fossil fuels and biofuels usually also produces <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>, which is toxic and can kill a person after mixing with the haemoglobin of the blood, increasing its concentration in the body. Biofuels and fossil fuels may also produce many other <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollutants</a> depending on the contents of the fuel.
</p>
