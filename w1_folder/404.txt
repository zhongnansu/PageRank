<p>The <b>light-independent reactions</b>, or <b>dark reactions</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> are <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reactions</a> that convert <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">compounds</a> into <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a>. These reactions occur in the <a href="/wiki/Stroma_(fluid)" title="Stroma (fluid)">stroma</a>, the fluid-filled area of a <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplast</a> outside the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thylakoid_membranes" title="Thylakoid membranes">thylakoid membranes</a>. These reactions take the products (<a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NADPH" title="NADPH">NADPH</a>) of light-dependent reactions and perform further chemical processes on them.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coupling_to_other_Metabolic_Pathways">Coupling to other Metabolic Pathways <span id="Calvin_Cycle"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Light-independent_reactions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Coupling to other Metabolic Pathways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>These reactions are closely coupled to the thylakoid electron transport chain as reducing power provided by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NADPH" title="NADPH">NADPH</a> produced in the <a href="/wiki/Photosystem_I" title="Photosystem I">photosystem I</a> is actively needed. The process of <a href="/wiki/Photorespiration" title="Photorespiration">photorespiration</a>, also known as C2 cycle, is also coupled to the dark reactions, as it results from an alternative reaction of the <a href="/wiki/RuBisCO" title="RuBisCO">RuBisCO</a> enzyme, and its final byproduct is also another glyceraldehyde-3-P.
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:452px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Calvin-cycle4.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="766" data-file-width="836" height="412" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Calvin-cycle4.svg/450px-Calvin-cycle4.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Calvin-cycle4.svg/675px-Calvin-cycle4.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Calvin-cycle4.svg/900px-Calvin-cycle4.svg.png 2x" width="450"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Calvin-cycle4.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Overview of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calvin_cycle" title="Calvin cycle">Calvin cycle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_fixation" title="Carbon fixation">carbon fixation</a></div></div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Calvin cycle</b>, <b>Calvin–Benson–Bassham (CBB) cycle</b>, <b>reductive pentose phosphate cycle</b> or <b>C3 cycle</b> is a series of <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemical</a> <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">redox</a> reactions that take place in the <a href="/wiki/Stroma_(fluid)" title="Stroma (fluid)">stroma</a> of <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplast</a> in <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a>.
</p><p>The cycle was discovered by <a href="/wiki/Melvin_Calvin" title="Melvin Calvin">Melvin Calvin</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Bassham" title="James Bassham">James Bassham</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Benson" title="Andrew Benson">Andrew Benson</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> by using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbon-14" title="Carbon-14">carbon-14</a>.
</p><p>Photosynthesis occurs in two stages in a cell. In the first stage, light-dependent reactions capture the energy of light and use it to make the energy-storage and transport molecules <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NADPH" title="NADPH">NADPH</a>. The Calvin cycle uses the energy from short-lived electronically excited carriers to convert <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> into <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> that can be used by the organism (and by animals that feed on it). This set of reactions is also called <i>carbon fixation</i>. The key <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzyme</a> of the cycle is called <a href="/wiki/RuBisCO" title="RuBisCO">RuBisCO</a>. In the following biochemical equations, the chemical species (phosphates and carboxylic acids) exist in equilibria among their various ionized states as governed by the <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a>.
</p><p>The enzymes in the Calvin cycle are functionally equivalent to most enzymes used in other metabolic pathways such as  <a href="/wiki/Gluconeogenesis" title="Gluconeogenesis">gluconeogenesis</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pentose_phosphate_pathway" title="Pentose phosphate pathway">pentose phosphate pathway</a>, but they are found in the chloroplast stroma instead of the cell <a href="/wiki/Cytosol" title="Cytosol">cytosol</a>, separating the reactions. They are activated in the light (which is why the name "dark reaction" is misleading), and also by products of the light-dependent reaction. These regulatory functions prevent the Calvin cycle from being respired to carbon dioxide. Energy (in the form of ATP) would be wasted in carrying out these reactions that have no <a href="/wiki/Primary_production" title="Primary production">net productivity</a>.
</p><p>The sum of reactions in the Calvin cycle is the following:
</p>
<dl><dd>3 <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> + 6 <a href="/wiki/Nicotinamide_adenine_dinucleotide_phosphate" title="Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate">NADPH</a> + 6 H<sup>+</sup> + 9 <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a> → <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate" title="Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate">glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate</a> (G3P)  + 6 <a href="/wiki/Nicotinamide_adenine_dinucleotide_phosphate" title="Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate">NADP<sup>+</sup></a> + 9 <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_diphosphate" title="Adenosine diphosphate">ADP</a> + 3 <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O</span> + 8 P<sub>i</sub>   (P<sub>i</sub> = inorganic <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a>)</dd></dl>
<p>Hexose (six-carbon) sugars are not a product of the Calvin cycle. Although many texts list a product of photosynthesis as <span class="chemf nowrap">C<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span>H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">12</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span>, this is mainly a convenience to counter the equation of respiration, where six-carbon sugars are oxidized in mitochondria. The carbohydrate products of the Calvin cycle are three-carbon sugar phosphate molecules, or "triose phosphates", namely, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate" title="Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate">glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate</a> (G3P).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Steps">Steps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Light-independent_reactions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Steps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the first stage of the Calvin cycle, a CO<sub>2</sub> molecule is incorporated into one of two three-carbon molecules (<a href="/wiki/Glyceraldehyde_3-phosphate" title="Glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate">glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate</a> or G3P), where it uses up two molecules of <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a> and two molecules of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NADPH" title="NADPH">NADPH</a>, which had been produced in the light-dependent stage. The three steps involved are:
</p>

<ol><li>The enzyme <a href="/wiki/RuBisCO" title="RuBisCO">RuBisCO</a> catalyses the carboxylation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate" title="Ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate">ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate</a>, RuBP, a 5-carbon compound, by carbon dioxide (a total of 6 carbons) in a two-step reaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The product of the first step is enediol-enzyme complex that can capture <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> or <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>. Thus, enediol-enzyme complex is the real carboxylase/oxygenase. The <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> that is captured by enediol in second step produces an unstable six-carbon compound called 2-carboxy 3-keto 1,5-biphosphoribotol (or 3-keto-2-carboxyarabinitol 1,5-bisphosphate) that immediately splits into 2 molecules of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3-phosphoglycerate" title="3-phosphoglycerate">3-phosphoglycerate</a>, or 3-PGA, a 3-carbon compound<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> (also: 3-phosphoglyceric acid, PGA, 3PGA).</li>
<li>The enzyme <a href="/wiki/Phosphoglycerate_kinase" title="Phosphoglycerate kinase">phosphoglycerate kinase</a> catalyses the phosphorylation of 3-PGA by <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a> (which was produced in the light-dependent stage). <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1,3-Bisphosphoglycerate" title="1,3-Bisphosphoglycerate">1,3-Bisphosphoglycerate</a> (1,3BPGA, glycerate-1,3-bisphosphate) and <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_diphosphate" title="Adenosine diphosphate">ADP</a> are the products. (However, note that two 3-PGAs are produced for every <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> that enters the cycle, so this step utilizes two <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a> per <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> fixed.)</li>
<li>The enzyme <a href="/wiki/Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate_dehydrogenase_(NADP%2B)_(phosphorylating)" title="Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (NADP+) (phosphorylating)">glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate dehydrogenase</a> catalyses the <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduction</a> of 1,3BPGA by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NADPH" title="NADPH">NADPH</a> (which is another product of the light-dependent stage). <a href="/wiki/Glyceraldehyde_3-phosphate" title="Glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate">Glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate</a> (also called G3P, GP, TP, PGAL, GAP) is produced, and the NADPH itself is oxidized and becomes NADP<sup>+</sup>. Again, two NADPH are utilized per <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> fixed.</li></ol>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:537px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Calvin_cycle_step_3.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="250" data-file-width="713" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Calvin_cycle_step_3.svg/535px-Calvin_cycle_step_3.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Calvin_cycle_step_3.svg/803px-Calvin_cycle_step_3.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Calvin_cycle_step_3.svg/1070px-Calvin_cycle_step_3.svg.png 2x" width="535"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Calvin_cycle_step_3.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Regeneration stage of the Calvin cycle</div></div></div>
<p>The next stage in the Calvin cycle is to regenerate RuBP. Five G3P molecules produce three RuBP molecules, using up three molecules of ATP. Since each CO<sub>2</sub> molecule produces two G3P molecules, three CO<sub>2</sub> molecules produce six G3P molecules, of which five are used to regenerate RuBP, leaving a net gain of one G3P molecule per three CO<sub>2</sub> molecules (as would be expected from the number of carbon atoms involved).
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Calvin_cycle.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="586" data-file-width="820" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Calvin_cycle.svg/220px-Calvin_cycle.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Calvin_cycle.svg/330px-Calvin_cycle.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Calvin_cycle.svg/440px-Calvin_cycle.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Calvin_cycle.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Simplified C3 cycle with structural formulas</div></div></div>
<p>The regeneration stage can be broken down into steps.
</p>
<ol><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triose_phosphate_isomerase" title="Triose phosphate isomerase">Triose phosphate isomerase</a> converts all of the G3P reversibly into <a href="/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone_phosphate" title="Dihydroxyacetone phosphate">dihydroxyacetone phosphate</a> (DHAP), also a 3-carbon molecule.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aldolase" title="Aldolase">Aldolase</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase" title="Fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase">fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase</a> convert a G3P and a DHAP into <a href="/wiki/Fructose_6-phosphate" title="Fructose 6-phosphate">fructose 6-phosphate</a> (6C). A phosphate ion is lost into solution.</li>
<li>Then fixation of another <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> generates two more G3P.</li>
<li>F6P has two carbons removed by <a href="/wiki/Transketolase" title="Transketolase">transketolase</a>, giving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erythrose-4-phosphate" title="Erythrose-4-phosphate">erythrose-4-phosphate</a>. The two carbons on <a href="/wiki/Transketolase" title="Transketolase">transketolase</a> are added to a G3P, giving the ketose <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xylulose-5-phosphate" title="Xylulose-5-phosphate">xylulose-5-phosphate</a> (Xu5P).</li>
<li>E4P and a DHAP (formed from one of the G3P from the second <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> fixation) are converted into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sedoheptulose-1,7-bisphosphate" title="Sedoheptulose-1,7-bisphosphate">sedoheptulose-1,7-bisphosphate</a> (7C) by aldolase enzyme.</li>
<li>Sedoheptulose-1,7-bisphosphatase (one of only three enzymes of the Calvin cycle that are unique to plants) cleaves <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sedoheptulose-1,7-bisphosphate" title="Sedoheptulose-1,7-bisphosphate">sedoheptulose-1,7-bisphosphate</a> into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sedoheptulose-7-phosphate" title="Sedoheptulose-7-phosphate">sedoheptulose-7-phosphate</a>, releasing an inorganic phosphate ion into solution.</li>
<li>Fixation of a third <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> generates two more G3P. The ketose S7P has two carbons removed by <a href="/wiki/Transketolase" title="Transketolase">transketolase</a>, giving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ribose-5-phosphate" title="Ribose-5-phosphate">ribose-5-phosphate</a> (R5P), and the two carbons remaining on <a href="/wiki/Transketolase" title="Transketolase">transketolase</a> are transferred to one of the G3P, giving another Xu5P. This leaves one G3P as the product of fixation of 3 <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>, with generation of three pentoses that can be converted to Ru5P.</li>
<li>R5P is converted into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ribulose-5-phosphate" title="Ribulose-5-phosphate">ribulose-5-phosphate</a> (Ru5P, RuP) by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphopentose_isomerase" title="Phosphopentose isomerase">phosphopentose isomerase</a>. Xu5P is converted into RuP by <a href="/wiki/Phosphopentose_epimerase" title="Phosphopentose epimerase">phosphopentose epimerase</a>.</li>
<li>Finally, <a href="/wiki/Phosphoribulokinase" title="Phosphoribulokinase">phosphoribulokinase</a> (another plant-unique enzyme of the pathway) phosphorylates RuP into RuBP, ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate, completing the Calvin <i>cycle</i>. This requires the input of one ATP.</li></ol>
<p>Thus, of six G3P produced, five are used to make three RuBP (5C) molecules (totaling 15 carbons), with only one G3P available for subsequent conversion to hexose. This requires nine ATP molecules and six NADPH molecules per three <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> molecules. The equation of the overall Calvin cycle is shown diagrammatically below.
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:582px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Calvin_cycle_overall.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="250" data-file-width="775" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Calvin_cycle_overall.svg/580px-Calvin_cycle_overall.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Calvin_cycle_overall.svg/870px-Calvin_cycle_overall.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Calvin_cycle_overall.svg/1160px-Calvin_cycle_overall.svg.png 2x" width="580"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Calvin_cycle_overall.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The overall equation of the Calvin cycle (black circles represent carbon atoms)</div></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/RuBisCO" title="RuBisCO">RuBisCO</a> also reacts competitively with <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> instead of <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> in <a href="/wiki/Photorespiration" title="Photorespiration">photorespiration</a>. The rate of photorespiration is higher at high temperatures. Photorespiration turns RuBP into 3-PGA and 2-phosphoglycolate, a 2-carbon molecule that can be converted via glycolate and glyoxalate to glycine. Via the glycine cleavage system and tetrahydrofolate, two glycines are converted into serine +<span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>. Serine can be converted back to 3-phosphoglycerate. Thus, only 3 of 4 carbons from two phosphoglycolates can be converted back to 3-PGA. It can be seen that photorespiration has very negative consequences for the plant, because, rather than fixing <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>, this process leads to loss of <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>. <a href="/wiki/C4_carbon_fixation" title="C4 carbon fixation">C4 carbon fixation</a> evolved to circumvent photorespiration, but can occur only in certain plants native to very warm or tropical climates—corn, for example.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Products">Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Light-independent_reactions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The immediate products of one turn of the Calvin cycle are 2 glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate (G3P) molecules, 3 ADP, and 2 NADP<sup>+</sup>. (ADP and NADP<sup>+</sup> are not really "products."  They are regenerated and later used again in the <a href="/wiki/Light-dependent_reactions" title="Light-dependent reactions">Light-dependent reactions</a>). Each G3P molecule is composed of 3 carbons. For the Calvin cycle to continue, RuBP (ribulose 1,5-bisphosphate) must be regenerated. So, 5 out of 6 carbons from the 2 G3P molecules are used for this purpose. Therefore, there is only 1 net carbon produced to play with for each turn. To create 1 surplus G3P requires 3 carbons, and therefore 3 turns of the Calvin cycle. To make one glucose molecule (which can be created from 2 G3P molecules) would require 6 turns of the Calvin cycle. Surplus G3P can also be used to form other carbohydrates such as starch, sucrose, and cellulose, depending on what the plant needs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Light-dependent_regulation">Light-dependent regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Light-independent_reactions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Light-dependent regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Light-dependent_reactions" title="Light-dependent reactions">Light-dependent reactions</a></div>
<p>These reactions do not occur in the dark or at night. There is a light-dependent regulation of the cycle enzymes, as the third step requires reduced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NADP" title="NADP">NADP</a>.
</p><p>There are two regulation systems at work when the cycle must be turned on or off: the <a href="/wiki/Thioredoxin" title="Thioredoxin">thioredoxin</a>/<a href="/wiki/Ferredoxin" title="Ferredoxin">ferredoxin</a> activation system, which activates some of the cycle enzymes; and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RuBisCo" title="RuBisCo">RuBisCo</a> enzyme activation, active in the Calvin cycle, which involves its own activase.
</p><p>The thioredoxin/ferredoxin system activates the enzymes glyceraldehyde-3-P dehydrogenase, glyceraldehyde-3-P phosphatase, fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase, sedoheptulose-1,7-bisphosphatase, and ribulose-5-phosphatase kinase, which are key points of the process. This happens when light is available, as the ferredoxin protein is reduced in the <a href="/wiki/Photosystem_I" title="Photosystem I">photosystem I</a> complex of the thylakoid electron chain when electrons are circulating through it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Ferredoxin then binds to and reduces the thioredoxin protein, which activates the cycle enzymes by severing a <a href="/wiki/Cystine" title="Cystine">cystine</a> bond found in all these enzymes. This is a dynamic process as the same bond is formed again by other proteins that deactivate the enzymes. The implications of this process are that the enzymes remain mostly activated by day and are deactivated in the dark when there is no more reduced ferredoxin available.
</p><p>The enzyme RuBisCo has its own, more complex activation process. It requires that a specific <a href="/wiki/Lysine" title="Lysine">lysine</a> amino acid be carbamylated to activate the enzyme. This lysine binds to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RuBP" title="RuBP">RuBP</a> and leads to a non-functional state if left uncarbamylated. A specific activase enzyme, called <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=RuBisCo_activase&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="RuBisCo activase (page does not exist)">RuBisCo activase</a>, helps this carbamylation process by removing one proton from the lysine and making the binding of the carbon dioxide molecule possible. Even then the RuBisCo enzyme is not yet functional, as it needs a magnesium ion bound to the lysine to function. This magnesium ion is released from the thylakoid lumen when the inner pH drops due to the active pumping of protons from the electron flow. RuBisCo activase itself is activated by increased concentrations of <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a> in the stroma caused by its <a href="/wiki/Phosphorylation" title="Phosphorylation">phosphorylation</a>.
</p>
