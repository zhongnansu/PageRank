<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Lignite</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>

<p><b>Lignite</b>, often referred to as <b>brown coal</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-brit-1">[1]</a></sup> is a soft, brown, <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustible,</a> <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rock</a> formed from naturally compressed <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a>. It is considered the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_rank" title="Coal rank">lowest rank</a> of <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> due to its relatively low <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_content" title="Heat content">heat content</a>. It has a carbon content around 60–70 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-brit-1">[1]</a></sup> It is mined all around the world and is used almost exclusively as a fuel for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steam-electric_power_generation" title="Steam-electric power generation">steam-electric power generation</a>, but is also mined for its <a href="/wiki/Germanium" title="Germanium">germanium</a> content in China.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lignite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lignite_mining_in_Western_North_Dakota.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="917" data-file-width="1474" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Lignite_mining_in_Western_North_Dakota.jpg/220px-Lignite_mining_in_Western_North_Dakota.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Lignite_mining_in_Western_North_Dakota.jpg/330px-Lignite_mining_in_Western_North_Dakota.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Lignite_mining_in_Western_North_Dakota.jpg/440px-Lignite_mining_in_Western_North_Dakota.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lignite_mining_in_Western_North_Dakota.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lignite mining in Western <a href="/wiki/North_Dakota" title="North Dakota">North Dakota</a>, United States (<i>circa</i> 1945)</div></div></div>
<p>Lignite is brownish-black in color and has a carbon content around 60–70 percent, a high <a href="/wiki/Coal_assay#Moisture" title="Coal assay">inherent moisture content</a> sometimes as high as 75 percent,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brit_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-brit-1">[1]</a></sup> and an <a href="/wiki/Coal_assay#Ash" title="Coal assay">ash</a> content ranging from 6–19 percent compared with 6–12 percent for <a href="/wiki/Bituminous_coal" title="Bituminous coal">bituminous coal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Garzweiler.strip.mine.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1213" data-file-width="1828" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Garzweiler.strip.mine.jpg/220px-Garzweiler.strip.mine.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Garzweiler.strip.mine.jpg/330px-Garzweiler.strip.mine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Garzweiler.strip.mine.jpg/440px-Garzweiler.strip.mine.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Garzweiler.strip.mine.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strip_mining" title="Strip mining">Strip mining</a> lignite at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tagebau_Garzweiler" title="Tagebau Garzweiler">Tagebau Garzweiler</a> in Germany</div></div></div>
<p>The energy content of lignite ranges from 10 to 20 MJ/kg (9–17 million <a href="/wiki/British_thermal_unit" title="British thermal unit">BTU</a> per <a href="/wiki/Short_ton" title="Short ton">short ton</a>) on a moist, mineral-matter-free basis. The energy content of lignite consumed in the United States averages <span class="nowrap">15 MJ/kg</span> (13 million BTU/ton), on the as-received basis (i.e., containing both inherent moisture and mineral matter). The energy content of lignite consumed in Victoria, Australia, averages <span class="nowrap">8.4 MJ/kg</span> (7.3 million BTU/ton).
</p><p>Lignite has a high content of volatile matter which makes it easier to convert into gas and liquid petroleum products than higher-ranking coals. Unfortunately, its high moisture content and susceptibility to <a href="/wiki/Spontaneous_combustion" title="Spontaneous combustion">spontaneous combustion</a> can cause problems in transportation and storage. It is now known that efficient processes which remove latent moisture locked within the structure of brown coal will relegate the risk of spontaneous combustion to the same level as black coal, transform the calorific value of brown coal to a <a href="/wiki/Black_coal_equivalent" title="Black coal equivalent">black coal equivalent</a> fuel, and significantly reduce the emissions profile of 'densified' brown coal to a level similar to or better than most black coals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> However, removing the moisture increases the cost of the final lignite fuel.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lignite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lom_%C4%8CSA_Most_Czech_Republic_2016_7.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1617" data-file-width="3000" height="119" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Lom_%C4%8CSA_Most_Czech_Republic_2016_7.jpg/220px-Lom_%C4%8CSA_Most_Czech_Republic_2016_7.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Lom_%C4%8CSA_Most_Czech_Republic_2016_7.jpg/330px-Lom_%C4%8CSA_Most_Czech_Republic_2016_7.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Lom_%C4%8CSA_Most_Czech_Republic_2016_7.jpg/440px-Lom_%C4%8CSA_Most_Czech_Republic_2016_7.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lom_%C4%8CSA_Most_Czech_Republic_2016_7.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Layer of lignite for mining in <a href="/wiki/Lom_%C4%8CSA" title="Lom ČSA">Lom ČSA, Czech Republic</a></div></div></div>
<p>Because of its low <a href="/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">energy density</a> and typically high moisture content, brown coal is inefficient to transport and is not traded extensively on the world market compared with higher coal grades. It is often burned in power stations near the mines, such as in Australia's <a href="/wiki/Latrobe_Valley" title="Latrobe Valley">Latrobe Valley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Luminant" title="Luminant">Luminant</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Monticello_Steam_Electric_Station" title="Monticello Steam Electric Station">Monticello plant</a> in Texas. Primarily because of latent high moisture content and low energy density of brown coal, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_emissions" title="Carbon dioxide emissions">carbon dioxide emissions</a> from traditional brown-coal-fired plants are generally much higher per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">megawatt</a> generated than for comparable black-coal plants, with the world's highest-emitting plant being <a href="/wiki/Hazelwood_Power_Station" title="Hazelwood Power Station">Hazelwood Power Station</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> until its closure in March 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The operation of traditional brown-coal plants, particularly in combination with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strip_mining" title="Strip mining">strip mining</a>, can be politically contentious due to environmental concerns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2014, about 12 percent of <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Germany" title="Energy in Germany">Germany's energy</a> and, specifically, 27 percent of Germany's electricity came from lignite power plants,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> while in 2014 in <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Greece" title="Energy in Greece">Greece</a>, lignite provided about 50 percent of its power needs.
</p><p>An environmentally beneficial use of lignite can be found in its use in cultivation and distribution of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_control" title="Biological control">biological control</a> microbes that suppress plant disease causing microbes. The carbon enriches the <a href="/wiki/Soil_organic_matter" title="Soil organic matter">organic matter in the soil</a> while the biological control microbes provide an alternative to chemical pesticides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Reaction with <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_ammonium_cation" title="Quaternary ammonium cation">quaternary amine</a> forms a product called amine-treated lignite (ATL), which is used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drilling_mud" title="Drilling mud">drilling mud</a> to reduce fluid loss during drilling.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lignite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pendeloque_en_lignite_Marsoulas_MHNT.PRE.2012.0.6.95.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4428" data-file-width="5516" height="177" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Pendeloque_en_lignite_Marsoulas_MHNT.PRE.2012.0.6.95.jpg/220px-Pendeloque_en_lignite_Marsoulas_MHNT.PRE.2012.0.6.95.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Pendeloque_en_lignite_Marsoulas_MHNT.PRE.2012.0.6.95.jpg/330px-Pendeloque_en_lignite_Marsoulas_MHNT.PRE.2012.0.6.95.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Pendeloque_en_lignite_Marsoulas_MHNT.PRE.2012.0.6.95.jpg/440px-Pendeloque_en_lignite_Marsoulas_MHNT.PRE.2012.0.6.95.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pendeloque_en_lignite_Marsoulas_MHNT.PRE.2012.0.6.95.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Pendant in lignite (<a href="/wiki/Jet_(lignite)" title="Jet (lignite)">jet</a>) from the <a href="/wiki/Magdalenian" title="Magdalenian">Magdalenian</a> culture</div></div></div>
<p>Lignite begins as an accumulation of partially decayed plant material, or <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a>. Burial by other sediments results in increasing temperature, depending on the local <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_gradient" title="Geothermal gradient">geothermal gradient</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tectonics" title="Tectonics">tectonic setting</a>, and increasing pressure. This causes compaction of the material and loss of some of the water and volatile matter (primarily <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>). This process, called <a href="/wiki/Coal#Formation" title="Coal">coalification</a>, concentrates the carbon content, and thus the heat content, of the material. Deeper burial and the passage of time result in further expulsion of moisture and volatile matter, eventually transforming the material into higher-rank coals such as bituminous and anthracite coal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Lignite deposits are typically younger than higher-ranked coals, with the majority of them having formed during the <a href="/wiki/Tertiary" title="Tertiary">Tertiary</a> period.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Resources">Resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lignite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lignite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Latrobe_Valley" title="Latrobe Valley">Latrobe Valley</a> in <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, contains estimated reserves of some 65 billion tonnes of brown coal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gov_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gov-11">[11]</a></sup> The deposit is equivalent to 25 percent of known world reserves. The coal seams are up to 100 metres thick, with multiple coal seams often giving virtually continuous brown coal thickness of up to 230 metres. Seams are covered by very little <a href="/wiki/Overburden" title="Overburden">overburden</a> (10 to 20 metres).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gov_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gov-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lignite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lignite can be separated into two types. The first is xyloid lignite or <a href="/wiki/Fossil_wood" title="Fossil wood">fossil wood</a> and the second form is the compact lignite or perfect lignite.
</p><p>Although xyloid lignite may sometimes have the tenacity and the appearance of ordinary wood, it can be seen that the combustible woody tissue has experienced a great modification. It is reducible to a fine powder by <a href="/wiki/Trituration" title="Trituration">trituration</a>, and if submitted to the action of a weak solution of <a href="/wiki/Potash" title="Potash">potash</a>, it yields a considerable quantity of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humic_acid" title="Humic acid">humic acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mackie_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mackie-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Leonardite" title="Leonardite">Leonardite</a> is an oxidized form of lignite, which also contains high levels of humic acid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Jet_(lignite)" title="Jet (lignite)">Jet</a> is a hardened, <a href="/wiki/Gemstone" title="Gemstone">gem-like</a> form of lignite used in various types of jewelry.
</p>
