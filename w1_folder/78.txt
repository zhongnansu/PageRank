<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Carbon lock-in</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Carbon lock-in</b> refers to the self-perpetuating <a href="/wiki/Inertia" title="Inertia">inertia</a> created by large <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_system" title="Electric power system">energy systems</a> that inhibits public and private efforts to introduce <a href="/wiki/Alternative_energy" title="Alternative energy">alternative energy</a> technologies. Related to the concept of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lock-in_(decision-making)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lock-in (decision-making) (page does not exist)">technological lock-in</a>, the concept is most used in relation to the challenge of altering the current <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_infrastructure" title="Energy infrastructure">energy infrastructure</a> to respond to global <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.
</p><p>The concept and term was first coined by Gregory C. Unruh in a 1999 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Fletcher_School_of_Law_and_Diplomacy" title="The Fletcher School of Law and Diplomacy">Fletcher School</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tufts_University" title="Tufts University">Tufts University</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doctoral_thesis" title="Doctoral thesis">doctoral thesis</a> entitled “Escaping Carbon Lock-In.” It has since gained popularity in climate change policy discussions, especially those focused on preventing the <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">globalization</a> of carbon lock-in to rapidly industrializing countries like <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.
</p><p>The source of carbon lock-in inertia in energy systems arises from the co-evolution of large interdependent technological networks and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_institution" title="Social institution">social institutions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cultural_practice" title="Cultural practice">cultural practices</a> that support and benefit from system growth. The growth of the system is fostered by increasing <a href="/wiki/Returns_to_scale" title="Returns to scale">returns to scale</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction">Introduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_lock-in&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Introduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to Unruh:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>…industrial economies have been locked into fossil fuel-based energy systems through a process of technological and institutional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Co-evolution" title="Co-evolution">co-evolution</a> driven by path-dependent increasing returns to scale. It is asserted that this condition, termed carbon lock-in, creates persistent <a href="/wiki/Market_(economics)" title="Market (economics)">market</a> and <a href="/wiki/Policy" title="Policy">policy</a> failures that can inhibit the diffusion of carbon-saving technologies despite their apparent environmental and economic advantages.</p><div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>Gregory C. Unruh, Understanding carbon lock-in (2000)</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>The concept emerged in response to what is termed the “<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_policy" title="Climate policy">climate policy</a> paradox,” which recognizes that there is substantial <a href="/wiki/Scientific_consensus" title="Scientific consensus">scientific consensus</a> that climate change is a real and present threat to humans and other species uniquely adapted to current climatic conditions. Similarly there is evidence that technologies exist which can lower the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_intensity" title="Carbon intensity">carbon intensity</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic_activity" title="Economic activity">economic activity</a> in a cost-effective manner, including <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">energy efficiency</a> innovations as well as some <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> applications. The existence of these apparent “win-win” no-regrets opportunities for society to act on climate concerns creates a <a href="/wiki/Paradox" title="Paradox">paradox</a>. If such technologies exist, and they are cost effective and help minimize <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_forcing" title="Climate forcing">climate-forcing</a> emissions, why aren’t they diffusing more rapidly? The conjecture is that industrial economies have become locked into fossil fuel technologies by past <a href="/wiki/Investment" title="Investment">investments</a> and policy decisions, the effects of positive feedback on increasing returns, and the economic growth of energy infrastructure.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="A_Co-Evolutionary_Process">A Co-Evolutionary Process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_lock-in&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: A Co-Evolutionary Process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carbon lock-in emerges over time as <a href="/wiki/Energy_development" title="Energy development">energy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Economic_development" title="Economic development">economic development</a> in industrialized countries has proceeded. The carbon lock-in framework builds hierarchically from individual technological artifacts, usually manufactured by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/For-profit" title="For-profit">for-profit</a> organizations, to technological systems of interdependent artifacts. As these systems grow, they begin to have important societal implications drawing in government regulation of the system’s growth and development. The government’s involvement with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/System_management" title="System management">system management</a>, be it for safety, universal service or other national interests, institutionalizes the system and signals the emergence of a techno-institutional complex.
</p><p>Over time <a href="/wiki/Consumer" title="Consumer">consumers</a> and the public adapt their lifestyles to the capabilities of the technology and the system becomes embedded in society. Examples of this process can be seen in the growth of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">automobile</a>-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transportation" title="Transportation">transportation</a> systems and fossil-fuel powered energy systems.
</p><p>It is this co-evolutionary <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedback</a> development process that creates the lock-in condition and associated barriers to the diffusion of <a href="/wiki/Alternative_technology" title="Alternative technology">alternative technologies</a>, even those with known superior environmental performance characteristics. A 2007 <a href="/wiki/Oak_Ridge_National_Laboratory" title="Oak Ridge National Laboratory">Oak Ridge National Laboratory</a> report entitled “Carbon Lock-In: Barriers to Deploying Climate Change Mitigation Technologies” (sponsored by the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Climate_Change_Technology_Program" title="U.S. Climate Change Technology Program">U.S. Climate Change Technology Program</a>, CCTP) classifies three major types of carbon lock-in barriers: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cost_effectiveness" title="Cost effectiveness">cost effectiveness</a>, financial/legal and <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_property" title="Intellectual property">intellectual property</a> barriers. Escaping the lock-in condition requires overcoming these barriers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Globalizing_Carbon_Lock-In">Globalizing Carbon Lock-In</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_lock-in&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Globalizing Carbon Lock-In">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:442px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EricksonEtAl_2015_AssessingCarbonLockin_EnvironmentalResearchLetters.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1063" data-file-width="1720" height="272" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/EricksonEtAl_2015_AssessingCarbonLockin_EnvironmentalResearchLetters.jpg/440px-EricksonEtAl_2015_AssessingCarbonLockin_EnvironmentalResearchLetters.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/EricksonEtAl_2015_AssessingCarbonLockin_EnvironmentalResearchLetters.jpg/660px-EricksonEtAl_2015_AssessingCarbonLockin_EnvironmentalResearchLetters.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/EricksonEtAl_2015_AssessingCarbonLockin_EnvironmentalResearchLetters.jpg/880px-EricksonEtAl_2015_AssessingCarbonLockin_EnvironmentalResearchLetters.jpg 2x" width="440"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EricksonEtAl_2015_AssessingCarbonLockin_EnvironmentalResearchLetters.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>According to an assessment proposed by Erickson et al. (2015), coal power plants, because of long lifetime, gas power, which may soon be overbuilt, and vehicles with internal combustion engines, because of strong techno-institutional effects, over-commit the highest amounts of carbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The carbon lock-in concept has gained more attention as China’s rapid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_economics" title="Industrial economics">industrial economic development</a> has progressed. The concern is that if China pursues the same fossil-fuel driven economic development models of established industrial counties, building out extensive automobile-based infrastructures and fossil-fuel powered energy systems, they will lock-in persistent and growing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emissions" title="Greenhouse gas emissions">greenhouse gas emissions</a> well into the future. The same arguments can be extended to all rapidly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrialization" title="Industrialization">industrializing</a> countries including India. This concern is arising as <a href="/wiki/Scientific_evidence" title="Scientific evidence">scientific evidence</a> is indicating that current emission growth must be stopped and global emissions reduced by upwards of 60% if humanity is to prevent substantial unwanted climate disruption.
</p><p>Recent studies by <a href="/wiki/Steven_J._Davis" title="Steven J. Davis">Steven J. Davis</a> and co-authors have quantified the future CO2 emissions that can be expected to be produced by current energy infrastructure <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and the magnitude of lock-in related to power plants being built each year in China and elsewhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
