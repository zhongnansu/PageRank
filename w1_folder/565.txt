<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Trihalomethane</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Trihalomethanes</b> (<b>THM</b>s) are <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compounds</a> in which three of the four hydrogen atoms of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> (CH<sub>4</sub>) are replaced by <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogen</a> atoms.  Many trihalomethanes find uses in industry as <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvents</a> or <a href="/wiki/Refrigerant" title="Refrigerant">refrigerants</a>.  THMs are also environmental pollutants, and many are considered <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogenic</a>. Trihalomethanes with all the same halogen atoms are called <b>haloforms</b>. Several of these are easy to prepare through the <a href="/wiki/Haloform_reaction" title="Haloform reaction">haloform reaction</a>.
</p><p>Trihalomethanes were the subject of first drinking water regulations issued after passage of the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Safe_Drinking_Water_Act" title="Safe Drinking Water Act">Safe Drinking Water Act</a> in 1974.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_reactions">Chemical reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trihalomethane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Chemical reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Haloforms
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_uses">Industrial uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trihalomethane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Industrial uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Only chloroform has significant applications of the haloforms.  In the predominant application, chloroform is required for the production of tetrafluoroethylene, precursor to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teflon" title="Teflon">teflon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Chloroform is fluorinated by reaction with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">hydrogen fluoride</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Chlorodifluoromethane" title="Chlorodifluoromethane">chlorodifluoromethane</a> (R-22). Pyrolysis of chlorodifluoromethane (at 550-750 °C) yields TFE, with <a href="/wiki/Difluorocarbene" title="Difluorocarbene">difluorocarbene</a> as an intermediate.
</p>
<dl><dd>CHCl<sub>3</sub> + 2 HF → CHClF<sub>2</sub> + 2 HCl</dd>
<dd>2 CHClF<sub>2</sub> → C<sub>2</sub>F<sub>4</sub> + 2 HCl</dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Refrigerants">Refrigerants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trihalomethane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Refrigerants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trifluoromethane" title="Trifluoromethane">Trifluoromethane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chlorodifluoromethane" title="Chlorodifluoromethane">chlorodifluoromethane</a> are both used as <a href="/wiki/Refrigerant" title="Refrigerant">refrigerants</a>.  Trihalomethanes released to the environment break down faster than <a href="/wiki/Haloalkane" title="Haloalkane">chlorofluorocarbons</a> (CFCs), thereby doing much less damage to the <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a> .  Chlorodifluoromethane is a refrigerant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HCFC" title="HCFC">HCFC</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrochlorofluorocarbon" title="Hydrochlorofluorocarbon">hydrochlorofluorocarbon</a>, while fluoroform is an HFC, or <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluorocarbon" title="Hydrofluorocarbon">hydrofluorocarbon</a>. Fluoroform is not ozone depleting.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solvents">Solvents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trihalomethane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Solvents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Chloroform is a common <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvent</a> in organic chemistry.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_pollutants">Water pollutants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trihalomethane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Water pollutants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Trihalomethanes are formed as a by-product predominantly when <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> is used to <a href="/wiki/Water_purification" title="Water purification">disinfect drinking water</a>. They are generally referred to as <a href="/wiki/Disinfection_by-product" title="Disinfection by-product">disinfection by-products</a>. They result from the reaction of chlorine or bromine with <a href="/wiki/Organic_matter" title="Organic matter">organic matter</a> present in the water being treated. The THMs produced have been associated through epidemiological studies with some adverse health effects. Many governments set limits on the amount permissible in drinking water. However, trihalomethanes are only one group of many hundreds of possible disinfection by-products—the vast majority of which are not monitored—and it has not yet been clearly demonstrated which of these are the most plausible candidate for causation of these health effects. In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">EPA</a> limits the total concentration of the four chief constituents  (<a href="/wiki/Chloroform" title="Chloroform">chloroform</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bromoform" title="Bromoform">bromoform</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bromodichloromethane" title="Bromodichloromethane">bromodichloromethane</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dibromochloromethane" title="Dibromochloromethane">dibromochloromethane</a>), referred to as total trihalomethanes (TTHM), to 80 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_billion" title="Parts per billion">parts per billion</a> in treated water.
</p><p>Traces of <a href="/wiki/Chloroform" title="Chloroform">chloroform</a> is also formed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swimming_pools" title="Swimming pools">swimming pools</a> that are disinfected with <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hypochlorite" title="Hypochlorite">hypochlorite</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Haloform_reaction" title="Haloform reaction">haloform reaction</a> with organic substances (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Urine" title="Urine">urine</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sweat" title="Sweat">sweat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hair" title="Hair">hair</a> and <a href="/wiki/Skin" title="Skin">skin</a> particles). Although it possible to inhale THMs, the U.S. EPA, has determined that this exposure is minimal compared to that from consumption. In swimmers, uptake of THMs is greatest via the skin with dermal absorption accounting for 80% of THM uptake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lindstrom1997_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lindstrom1997-3">[3]</a></sup> Exercising in a chlorinated pool increases the toxicity of a "safe" chlorinated pool atmosphere<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Drobnic1996_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Drobnic1996-4">[4]</a></sup> with toxic effects of chlorine byproducts greater in young swimmers than older swimmers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aiking1994_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aiking1994-5">[5]</a></sup>
Studies in adolescents have shown an inverse relationship between serum testosterone levels and the amount of time spent in public pools. Chlorination by-products have been linked as a probable cause.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nickmilder2011_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nickmilder2011-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
