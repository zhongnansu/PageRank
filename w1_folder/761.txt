<p>The <i><b>Sunday Herald</b></i> was a Scottish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunday_editions" title="Sunday editions">Sunday newspaper</a>, published between 7 February 1999 and 2 September 2018. Originally a <a href="/wiki/Broadsheet" title="Broadsheet">broadsheet</a>, it was published in <a href="/wiki/Compact_(newspaper)" title="Compact (newspaper)">compact</a> format from 20 November 2005. The paper was known for having combined a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centre-left" title="Centre-left">centre-left</a> stance with support for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_devolution" title="Scottish devolution">Scottish devolution</a>, and later <a href="/wiki/Scottish_independence" title="Scottish independence">Scottish independence</a>. The last edition of the newspaper was published on 2 September 2018 and was replaced with Sunday editions of <a href="/wiki/The_Herald_(Glasgow)" title="The Herald (Glasgow)"><i>The Herald</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/The_National_(Scotland)" title="The National (Scotland)"><i>The National</i></a><i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></i>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Circulation">Circulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunday_Herald&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Circulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In July 2012, the decision was made by the newspapers' publishers to classify both the Sunday Herald as a "regional" title, thus exempting it from the monthly   biennially.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Between July and December 2013, the <i>Sunday Herald</i> sold an average of 23,907 copies, down 7.5% on the 12 months previous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> After declaring support for <a href="/wiki/Scottish_independence" title="Scottish independence">Scottish independence</a>, The <i>Sunday Herald</i> received a huge increase in sales, with circulation in September 2014 up 111% year on year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>By 2017 circulation had fallen significantly to 18,387 and in August 2018 staff were told they would now be expected to work on the Glasgow Herald too, with the potential for the two titles to be combined at some point in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_profile">History and profile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunday_Herald&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History and profile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In early 1998 the Scottish Media Group (SMG), then led by chairman <a href="/wiki/Gus_Macdonald" title="Gus Macdonald">Gus Macdonald</a>, decided to create a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunday_editions" title="Sunday editions">Sunday sister</a> for its existing national morning title <i><a href="/wiki/The_Herald_(Glasgow)" title="The Herald (Glasgow)">The Herald</a></i>, because the <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a>-based media group was losing advertising revenue to rival newspaper publishers every Sunday. In March 1998 the media company's board appointed <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Jaspan" title="Andrew Jaspan">Andrew Jaspan</a>, then the publisher and managing director of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Big_Issue" title="The Big Issue">The Big Issue</a></i> and a former editor of <i>Scotland on Sunday</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Scotsman" title="The Scotsman">The Scotsman</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">The Observer</a></i> to examine the business case for launching a new Sunday title. In October 1998 SMG (now known as <a href="/wiki/STV_Group_plc" title="STV Group plc">STV Group plc</a>), which also owns the broadcaster <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/STV_(TV_network)" title="STV (TV network)">STV</a>, committed to putting £10 million behind the new paper's launch.
</p><p>The paper was awarded the European Newspaper of the Year in the category of weekend paper by the European Newspaper Congress in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <i>Sunday Herald</i> was the only Scottish newspaper to back a vote for independence in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_independence_referendum,_2014" title="Scottish independence referendum, 2014">Scottish independence referendum, 2014</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Jaspan.27s_launch_team"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Jaspan's_launch_team">Jaspan's launch team</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunday_Herald&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Jaspan's launch team">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jaspan assembled a launch team including former <a href="/wiki/Hue_and_Cry_(band)" title="Hue and Cry (band)">Hue &amp; Cry</a> singer <a href="/wiki/Pat_Kane" title="Pat Kane">Pat Kane</a>, TV producer and presenter <a href="/wiki/Muriel_Gray" title="Muriel Gray">Muriel Gray</a> and <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> political commentator <a href="/wiki/Iain_Macwhirter" title="Iain Macwhirter">Iain Macwhirter</a> and designer Simon Cunningham. Other former BBC television and radio journalists who joined the title included <a href="/wiki/Lesley_Riddoch" title="Lesley Riddoch">Lesley Riddoch</a>, Torcuil Crichton and Pennie Taylor. A number of former <i><a href="/wiki/The_Scotsman" title="The Scotsman">Scotsman</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Scotland_on_Sunday" title="Scotland on Sunday">Scotland on Sunday</a></i> staff also joined the new paper, as did several journalists from <i>The Big Issue</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> Scottish edition including Neil Mackay, David Milne and Iain S Bruce.
</p><p>The <i>Sunday Herald</i> was launched as a seven-section newspaper on 7 February 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It was advertised with the slogan "No ordinary Sunday".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The use of the word "fuck" in the first edition of the magazine alienated older and more <a href="/wiki/Social_conservatism" title="Social conservatism">conservative</a> readers, but the paper quickly won a following among more liberal-minded Scots. It also won a raft of awards for its journalism, design and photography, in the UK and internationally, and secured the former archbishop <a href="/wiki/Richard_Holloway" title="Richard Holloway">Richard Holloway</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/On_the_Waterfront" title="On the Waterfront">On the Waterfront</a></i> scriptwriter <a href="/wiki/Budd_Schulberg" title="Budd Schulberg">Budd Schulberg</a> as regular contributors. Its web version gained a large readership in the United States because of its consistent anti-<a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> and anti-<a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a> line.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sale_to_Newsquest">Sale to Newsquest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunday_Herald&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sale to Newsquest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After having over-paid for acquisitions during the dot-com era, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SMG_plc" title="SMG plc">Scottish Media Group</a> was in serious financial trouble by 2002. The company decided to sell its publishing arm, whose assets included <i>The Herald</i>, <i>Sunday Herald</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Evening_Times" title="Evening Times">Evening Times</a></i> and magazines including <i>Scottish Farmer</i>, <i>Boxing News</i> and <i>The Strad</i> and a public <a href="/wiki/Auction" title="Auction">auction</a>, accompanied by a heated public debated, ensued.
</p><p>When it looked like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barclay_brothers" title="Barclay brothers">Barclay brothers</a>, owners of rival papers <i>The Scotsman</i> and <i>Scotland on Sunday</i>, were set to become the publishing group's owners, questions were raised in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a>. Had Sir David and Sir Frederick Barclay and <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Neil" title="Andrew Neil">Andrew Neil</a> succeeded in acquiring the fledgling <i>Sunday Herald</i>, they would have closed it down to give a clear run to their own <i>Scotland on Sunday</i> title, and merged <i>The Herald</i> with <i>The Scotsman</i>. That their goals were anti-competitive was confirmed when an unsigned leader written by Jaspan making these claims went unchallenged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Determined to prevent the paper being acquired by <a href="/wiki/Tax_exile" title="Tax exile">tax exiles</a> with no sympathy for its centre-left ethos, Jaspan led a campaign to keep it out of their hands. This included lobbying senior <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party (UK)</a> politicians at their September 2002 conference in <a href="/wiki/Blackpool" title="Blackpool">Blackpool</a>.
</p><p>The campaign proved successful, with even the <a href="/wiki/Financial_Times" title="Financial Times">Financial Times</a> questioning whether it was right for the Barclay twins to have a monopoly of quality papers published in Scotland. The <i>Sunday Herald</i> and related titles were sold instead to <a href="/wiki/Newsquest" title="Newsquest">Newsquest</a> (a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gannett" title="Gannett">Gannett</a> company) for £216 million. This was cleared by the UK <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Trade_and_Industry_(United_Kingdom)" title="Department of Trade and Industry (United Kingdom)">Department of Trade and Industry</a> in March 2003, partly because it was persuaded the papers would keep their <a href="/wiki/Editorial_independence" title="Editorial independence">editorial independence</a> under Gannett's ownership and because of Gannett's creation of a new Scottish division to run the acquired papers from Glasgow. The DTI report said: "We do not expect the transfer adversely to affect the current editorial freedom, the current editorial stance, content or quality of the SMG titles, accurate presentation of news or freedom of expression." The deal completed on 5 April 2003.
</p><p>Jaspan resigned in 2004 to become editor of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Age" title="The Age">The Age</a></i> in Melbourne, Australia. <a href="/wiki/Richard_Walker_(editor)" title="Richard Walker (editor)">Richard Walker</a> was appointed as his successor. Walker, a former production journalist on both the <i>Daily Record</i> and <i>Scotland on Sunday</i> had been with the title since its launch and had served as deputy to Jaspan for five years.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Walker_years">The Walker years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunday_Herald&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Walker years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Walker took the <i>Sunday Herald</i> <a href="/wiki/Tabloid_(newspaper_format)" title="Tabloid (newspaper format)">tabloid</a> in November 2005 which brought a temporary uplift in circulation. Sales settled at 58,000 (source: <a href="/wiki/Audit_Bureau_of_Circulations_(UK)" title="Audit Bureau of Circulations (UK)">Audit Bureau of Circulations</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and readership at 195,000 (source: National Readership Survey<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>). The week before the <i>Sunday Herald</i> was launched in February 1999, the Barclays' <i>Scotland on Sunday</i> sold more than 130,000 copies. This has since plummeted to c.46,000, about 50% higher than the circulation (June 2012 ABCs) of the Sunday Herald (26,074 weekly).
</p><p>Walker was behind the launch of the blog site Sundayheraldtalk.com<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> in September 2006..
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxigate">Taxigate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunday_Herald&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Taxigate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In April 2006 the <i>Sunday Herald</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> Scottish political editor, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Hutcheon" title="Paul Hutcheon">Paul Hutcheon</a>, won both Political Journalist of the Year and Journalist of the Year in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Press_Awards" title="Scottish Press Awards">Scottish Press Awards</a> for articles revealing that <a href="/wiki/David_McLetchie" title="David McLetchie">David McLetchie</a>, leader of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_Conservative_and_Unionist_Party" title="Scottish Conservative and Unionist Party">Scottish Conservative and Unionist Party</a>, had abused taxpayers' money to pay for taxi fares for legal and party work. Hutcheon made use of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_Freedom_of_Information_Act" title="Scottish Freedom of Information Act">Scottish Freedom of Information Act</a> to establish his case, which ultimately led to McLetchie resigning both as Conservative leader and as a partner in Edinburgh law firm Tods Murray.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Super_injunctions">Super injunctions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sunday_Herald&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Super injunctions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 22 May 2011, the paper became the first mainstream UK publication to name a person involved with a super injunction. In <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CTB_v_News_Group_Newspapers" title="CTB v News Group Newspapers">CTB v News Group Newspapers</a></i> the claimant, a <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">footballer</a> previously known only as CTB, was identified by publishing as its front page an image of <a href="/wiki/Ryan_Giggs" title="Ryan Giggs">Ryan Giggs</a> whose eyes are covered with a black bar which features the word "censored".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_superinjunction_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc_superinjunction-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The paper argued that the injunction was not valid in Scotland which is a separate jurisdiction and only applicable to England,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_superinjunction_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbc_superinjunction-16">[16]</a></sup> however one legal opinion suggests that the Scottish news outlet may be in breach an English injunction due to a House of Lords ruling in the 1987 <a href="/wiki/Spycatcher" title="Spycatcher">Spycatcher</a> case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
