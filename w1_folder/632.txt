<p><b>Paleoclimatology</b> (in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_spelling" title="British spelling">British spelling</a>, <b>palaeoclimatology</b>) is the study of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">changes</a> in <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> taken on the scale of the entire <a href="/wiki/History_of_Earth" title="History of Earth">history of Earth</a>. It uses a variety of <a href="/wiki/Proxy_(climate)" title="Proxy (climate)">proxy</a> methods from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_sciences" title="Earth sciences">Earth</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_sciences" title="Life sciences">life sciences</a> to obtain data previously preserved within things such as <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rocks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediments</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tree_ring" title="Tree ring">tree rings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mollusc_shell" title="Mollusc shell">shells</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microfossil" title="Microfossil">microfossils</a>. It then uses the records to determine the past states of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s various climate regions and its <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmospheric</a> system. Studies of past changes in the environment and biodiversity often reflect on the current situation, specifically the impact of climate on <a href="/wiki/Extinction_event" title="Extinction event">mass extinctions</a> and biotic recovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SahneyBenton2008RecoveryFromProfoundExtinction_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-SahneyBenton2008RecoveryFromProfoundExtinction-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paleoclimatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/History_of_climate_change_science" title="History of climate change science">History of climate change science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Historical_climatology" title="Historical climatology">Historical climatology</a></div>
<p>The scientific study field of paleoclimate began to form in the early 19th century, when discoveries about glaciations and natural changes in Earth's past climate helped to understand the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>.The first observations which had a real scientific basis were probably those by John Hardcastle in New Zealand, in the 1880s. He noted that the <a href="/wiki/Loess" title="Loess">loess</a> deposits at Timaru in the South Island recorded changes in climate; he called the loess a 'climate register'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reconstructing_ancient_climates">Reconstructing ancient climates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paleoclimatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reconstructing ancient climates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Paleoclimatologists employ a wide variety of techniques to deduce ancient climates.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ice">Ice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paleoclimatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mountain <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a> and the polar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_caps" title="Ice caps">ice caps</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_sheets" title="Ice sheets">ice sheets</a> provide much data in paleoclimatology. Ice-coring projects in the ice caps of <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> have yielded data going back several hundred thousand years, over 800,000 years in the case of the <a href="/wiki/European_Project_for_Ice_Coring_in_Antarctica" title="European Project for Ice Coring in Antarctica">EPICA</a> project.
</p>
<dl><dd><ul><li>Air trapped within fallen <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a> becomes encased in tiny bubbles as the snow is compressed into ice in the glacier under the weight of later years' snow. The trapped air has proven a tremendously valuable source for direct measurement of the composition of air from the time the ice was formed.</li>
<li>Layering can be observed because of seasonal pauses in ice accumulation and can be used to establish chronology, associating specific depths of the core with ranges of time.</li>
<li>Changes in the layering thickness can be used to determine changes in precipitation or temperature.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Oxygen-18" title="Oxygen-18">Oxygen-18</a> quantity changes (<a href="/wiki/%CE%9418O" title="Δ18O"><span class="texhtml">δ</span><sup>18</sup>O</a>) in ice layers represent changes in average ocean surface temperature. Water molecules containing the heavier O-18 evaporate at a higher temperature than water molecules containing the normal <a href="/wiki/Oxygen-16" title="Oxygen-16">Oxygen-16</a> isotope. The ratio of O-18 to O-16 will be higher as temperature increases. It also depends on other factors such as the water's salinity and the volume of water locked up in ice sheets. Various cycles in those isotope ratios have been detected.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pollen#Pollen_in_the_fossil_record" title="Pollen">Pollen</a> has been observed in the ice cores and can be used to understand which plants were present as the layer formed. Pollen is produced in abundance and its distribution is typically well understood. A pollen count for a specific layer can be produced by observing the total amount of pollen categorized by type (shape) in a controlled sample of that layer. Changes in plant frequency over time can be plotted through statistical analysis of pollen counts in the core. Knowing which plants were present leads to an understanding of precipitation and temperature, and types of fauna present. <a href="/wiki/Palynology" title="Palynology">Palynology</a> includes the study of pollen for these purposes.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Volcanic_ash" title="Volcanic ash">Volcanic ash</a> is contained in some layers, and can be used to establish the time of the layer's formation. Each volcanic event distributed ash with a unique set of properties (shape and color of particles, chemical signature). Establishing the ash's source will establish a range of time to associate with layer of ice.</li></ul></dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dendroclimatology">Dendroclimatology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paleoclimatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dendroclimatology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dendroclimatology" title="Dendroclimatology">Dendroclimatology</a></div>
<p>Climatic information can be obtained through an understanding of changes in tree growth. Generally, trees respond to changes in climatic variables by speeding up or slowing down growth, which in turn is generally reflected by a greater or lesser thickness in growth rings. Different species, however, respond to changes in climatic variables in different ways. A tree-ring record is established by compiling information from many living trees in a specific area.
</p><p>Older intact wood that has escaped decay can extend the time covered by the record by matching the ring depth changes to contemporary specimens. By using that method, some areas have tree-ring records dating back a few thousand years. Older wood not connected to a contemporary record can be dated generally with radiocarbon techniques. A tree-ring record can be used to produce information regarding precipitation, temperature, hydrology, and fire corresponding to a particular area.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sedimentary_content">Sedimentary content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paleoclimatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sedimentary content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On a longer time scale, geologists must refer to the sedimentary record for data.
</p>
<dl><dd><ul><li>Sediments, sometimes lithified to form rock, may contain remnants of preserved vegetation, animals, plankton, or <a href="/wiki/Palynology" title="Palynology">pollen</a>, which may be characteristic of certain climatic zones.</li>
<li>Biomarker molecules such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkenones" title="Alkenones">alkenones</a> may yield information about their temperature of formation.</li>
<li>Chemical signatures, particularly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mg/Ca" title="Mg/Ca">Mg/Ca</a> ratio of <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a> in <a href="/wiki/Foraminifera" title="Foraminifera">Foraminifera</a> tests, can be used to reconstruct past temperature.</li>
<li>Isotopic ratios can provide further information. Specifically, the <a href="/wiki/%CE%9418O" title="Δ18O"><span class="texhtml">δ</span><sup>18</sup>O</a> record responds to changes in temperature and ice volume, and the <a href="/wiki/%CE%9413C" title="Δ13C"><span class="texhtml">δ</span><sup>13</sup>C</a> record reflects a range of factors, which are often difficult to disentangle.</li></ul></dd></dl>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Core%2BRepository%2Bcore_samples2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="300" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Core%2BRepository%2Bcore_samples2.jpg/220px-Core%2BRepository%2Bcore_samples2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e7/Core%2BRepository%2Bcore_samples2.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Core%2BRepository%2Bcore_samples2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sea floor core sample labelled to identify the exact spot on the sea floor where the sample was taken. Sediments from nearby locations can show significant differences in chemical and biological composition.</div></div></div>
<dl><dt><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sedimentary_facies" title="Sedimentary facies">Sedimentary facies</a></dt>
<dd>On a longer time scale, the rock record may show signs of <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level rise</a> and fall, and features such as <a href="/wiki/Dune#Lithified_dunes" title="Dune">"fossilised" sand dunes</a> can be identified. Scientists can get a grasp of long term climate by studying <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rock</a> going back billions of years. The division of earth history into separate periods is largely based on visible changes in sedimentary rock layers that demarcate major changes in conditions. Often, they include major shifts in climate.</dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sclerochronology">Sclerochronology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paleoclimatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sclerochronology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl><dt>Corals (see also <a href="/wiki/Sclerochronology" title="Sclerochronology">sclerochronology</a>)</dt>
<dd>Coral "rings" are similar to tree rings except that they respond to different things, such as the water temperature, freshwater influx, pH changes, and wave action. From there, certain equipment can be used to derive the sea surface temperature and water salinity from the past few centuries. The δ<sup>18</sup>O of <a href="/wiki/Coralline_algae" title="Coralline algae">coralline</a> red algae provides a useful proxy of the combined sea surface temperature and sea surface salinity at high latitudes and the tropics, where many traditional techniques are limited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Geology2008_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Geology2008-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cobb_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cobb-4">[4]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Landscapes_and_landforms">Landscapes and landforms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paleoclimatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Landscapes and landforms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Within <a href="/wiki/Climatic_geomorphology" title="Climatic geomorphology">climatic geomorphology</a> one approach is to study <a href="/wiki/Relict_(geology)" title="Relict (geology)">relict landforms</a> to infer ancient climates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gutix2_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gutix2-5">[5]</a></sup> Being often concerned about past climates climatic geomorphology is considered sometimes to be a theme of <a href="/wiki/Historical_geology" title="Historical geology">historical geology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Climatic geomorphology is of limited use to study recent (<a href="/wiki/Quaternary" title="Quaternary">Quaternary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a>) large climate changes since there are seldom discernible in the geomorphological record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goudie2004_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goudie2004-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations">Limitations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paleoclimatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Limitations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A multinational consortium, the <a href="/wiki/European_Project_for_Ice_Coring_in_Antarctica" title="European Project for Ice Coring in Antarctica">European Project for Ice Coring in Antarctica</a> (EPICA), has drilled an ice core in Dome C on the East Antarctic ice sheet and retrieved ice from roughly 800,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The international ice core community has, under the auspices of International Partnerships in Ice Core Sciences (IPICS), defined a priority project to obtain the oldest possible ice core record from Antarctica, an ice core record reaching back to or towards 1.5 million years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Page_1_1_International_Partnerships_in_Ice_Core_Sciences_(IPICS)_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Page_1_1_International_Partnerships_in_Ice_Core_Sciences_(IPICS)-9">[9]</a></sup> The deep marine record, the source of most isotopic data, exists only on oceanic plates, which are eventually subducted: the oldest remaining material is <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=200">200</a> million years</span> old. Older sediments are also more prone to corruption by <a href="/wiki/Diagenesis" title="Diagenesis">diagenesis</a>. Resolution and confidence in the data decrease over time.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_climate_events_in_Earth_history">Notable climate events in Earth history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paleoclimatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Notable climate events in Earth history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Earliest_atmosphere">Earliest atmosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paleoclimatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Earliest atmosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Paleoatmosphere" title="Paleoatmosphere">first atmosphere</a> would have consisted of gases in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_nebula#Formation_of_planets" title="Solar nebula">solar nebula</a>, primarily <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>. In addition, there would probably have been simple <a href="/wiki/Hydride" title="Hydride">hydrides</a> such as those now found in gas giants like <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a>, notably <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> vapor, <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>. As the solar nebula dissipated, the gases would have escaped, partly driven off by the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zahnle_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zahnle-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_atmosphere">Second atmosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paleoclimatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Second atmosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The next atmosphere, consisting largely of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, and inert gases, was produced by outgassing from <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">volcanism</a>, supplemented by gases produced during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_heavy_bombardment" title="Late heavy bombardment">late heavy bombardment</a> of Earth by huge <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asteroids" title="Asteroids">asteroids</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zahnle_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zahnle-10">[10]</a></sup> A major part of carbon dioxide emissions were soon dissolved in water and built up carbonate sediments.
</p><p>Water-related sediments have been found dating from as early as 3.8 billion years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> About 3.4 billion years ago, nitrogen was the major part of the then stable "second atmosphere". An influence of life has to be taken into account rather soon in the history of the atmosphere because hints of early life forms have been dated to as early as 3.5 billion years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The fact that it is not perfectly in line with the 30% lower solar radiance (compared to today) of the early Sun has been described as the "<a href="/wiki/Faint_young_Sun_paradox" title="Faint young Sun paradox">faint young Sun paradox</a>".
</p><p>The geological record, however, shows a continually relatively warm surface during the complete early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperature_record" title="Temperature record">temperature record</a> of Earth with the exception of one cold glacial phase about 2.4 billion years ago. In the late <a href="/wiki/Archean" title="Archean">Archaean</a> eon, an oxygen-containing atmosphere began to develop, apparently from photosynthesizing <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">cyanobacteria</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Great_Oxygenation_Event" title="Great Oxygenation Event">Great Oxygenation Event</a>) which have been found as <a href="/wiki/Stromatolite" title="Stromatolite">stromatolite</a> fossils from 2.7 billion years ago. The early basic carbon isotopy (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotope_ratio" title="Isotope ratio">isotope ratio</a> proportions) was very much in line with what is found today, suggesting that the fundamental features of the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a> were established as early as 4 billion years ago.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Third_atmosphere">Third atmosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paleoclimatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Third atmosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The constant rearrangement of continents by <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a> influences the long-term evolution of the atmosphere by transferring carbon dioxide to and from large continental carbonate stores. Free oxygen did not exist in the atmosphere until about 2.4 billion years ago, during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Oxygenation_Event" title="Great Oxygenation Event">Great Oxygenation Event</a>, and its appearance is indicated by the end of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Banded_iron_formations" title="Banded iron formations">banded iron formations</a>. Until then, any oxygen produced by photosynthesis was consumed by oxidation of reduced materials, notably iron. Molecules of free oxygen did not start to accumulate in the atmosphere until the rate of production of oxygen began to exceed the availability of reducing materials. That point was a shift from a <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reducing</a> atmosphere to an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidizing" title="Oxidizing">oxidizing</a> atmosphere. O<sub>2</sub> showed major variations until reaching a steady state of more than 15% by the end of the Precambrian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The following time span was the <a href="/wiki/Phanerozoic" title="Phanerozoic">Phanerozoic</a> eon, during which oxygen-breathing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metazoan" title="Metazoan">metazoan</a> life forms began to appear.
</p><p>The amount of oxygen in the atmosphere has fluctuated over the last 600 million years, reaching a peak of 35%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beerling2007_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beerling2007-14">[14]</a></sup> during the <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a> period, significantly higher than today's 21%. Two main processes govern changes in the atmosphere: plants <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">use carbon dioxide from the atmosphere</a>, releasing oxygen and the breakdown of <a href="/wiki/Pyrite" title="Pyrite">pyrite</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic_eruption" title="Volcanic eruption">volcanic eruptions</a> release <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> into the atmosphere, which oxidizes and hence reduces the amount of oxygen in the atmosphere. However, volcanic eruptions also release carbon dioxide, which plants can convert to oxygen. The exact cause of the variation of the amount of oxygen in the atmosphere is not known. Periods with much oxygen in the atmosphere are associated with rapid development of animals. Today's atmosphere contains 21% oxygen, which is high enough for rapid development of animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_during_geological_ages">Climate during geological ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paleoclimatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Climate during geological ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Huronian_glaciation" title="Huronian glaciation">Huronian glaciation</a>, is the first known glaciation in Earth's history, and lasted from 2400-2100 million years ago.</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogenian_glaciation" title="Cryogenian glaciation">Cryogenian glaciation</a> lasted from 720-635 million years ago.</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precambrian_climate">Precambrian climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paleoclimatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Precambrian climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Precambrian" title="Precambrian">Precambrian</a></div>
<p>The climate of the late Precambrian showed some major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glaciation" title="Glaciation">glaciation</a> events spreading over much of the earth. At this time the continents were bunched up in the <a href="/wiki/Rodinia" title="Rodinia">Rodinia</a> supercontinent. Massive deposits of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tillite" title="Tillite">tillites</a> and anomalous <a href="/wiki/Isotopic_signature" title="Isotopic signature">isotopic signatures</a> are found, which gave rise to the <a href="/wiki/Snowball_Earth" title="Snowball Earth">Snowball Earth</a> hypothesis. As the <a href="/wiki/Proterozoic" title="Proterozoic">Proterozoic Eon</a> drew to a close, the Earth started to warm up. By the dawn of the Cambrian and the Phanerozoic, life forms were abundant in the <a href="/wiki/Cambrian_explosion" title="Cambrian explosion">Cambrian explosion</a> with average global temperatures of about 22 °<a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">C</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phanerozoic_climate">Phanerozoic climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paleoclimatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Phanerozoic climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:452px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Phanerozoic_Climate_Change.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="520" data-file-width="726" height="322" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Phanerozoic_Climate_Change.png/450px-Phanerozoic_Climate_Change.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Phanerozoic_Climate_Change.png/675px-Phanerozoic_Climate_Change.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9c/Phanerozoic_Climate_Change.png 2x" width="450"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Phanerozoic_Climate_Change.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>500 million years of climate change</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Phanerozoic" title="Phanerozoic">Phanerozoic</a></div>
<p>Major drivers for the preindustrial ages have been variations of the sun, volcanic ashes and exhalations, relative movements of the earth towards the sun, and tectonically induced effects as for major sea currents, watersheds, and ocean oscillations. In the early Phanerozoic, increased atmospheric carbon dioxide concentrations have been linked to driving or amplifying increased global temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Royer et al. 2004<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dana_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dana-17">[17]</a></sup> found a climate sensitivity for the rest of the Phanerozoic which was calculated to be similar to today's modern range of values.
</p><p>The difference in global mean temperatures between a fully glacial Earth and an ice free Earth is estimated at approximately 10 °C, though far larger changes would be observed at high latitudes and smaller ones at low latitudes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> One requirement for the development of large scale ice sheets seems to be the arrangement of continental land masses at or near the poles. The constant rearrangement of continents by <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a> can also shape long-term climate evolution. However, the presence or absence of land masses at the poles is not sufficient to guarantee glaciations or exclude polar ice caps. Evidence exists of past warm periods in Earth's climate when polar land masses similar to <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> were home to <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a> forests rather than ice sheets.
</p><p>The relatively warm local minimum between <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> goes along with an increase of subduction and mid-ocean ridge volcanism <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> due to the breakup of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pangea" title="Pangea">Pangea</a> <a href="/wiki/Supercontinent" title="Supercontinent">supercontinent</a>.
</p><p>Superimposed on the long-term evolution between hot and cold climates have been many short-term fluctuations in climate similar to, and sometimes more severe than, the varying glacial and interglacial states of the present <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a>. Some of the most severe fluctuations, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleocene-Eocene_Thermal_Maximum" title="Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum">Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum</a>, may be related to rapid climate changes due to sudden collapses of natural <a href="/wiki/Methane_clathrate" title="Methane clathrate">methane clathrate</a> reservoirs in the oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>A similar, single event of induced severe climate change after a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorite_impact" title="Meteorite impact">meteorite impact</a> has been proposed as reason for the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_extinction_event" title="Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event">Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event</a>. Other major thresholds are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Permian-Triassic_extinction_event" title="Permian-Triassic extinction event">Permian-Triassic</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ordovician-Silurian_extinction_events" title="Ordovician-Silurian extinction events">Ordovician-Silurian extinction events</a> with various reasons suggested.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quaternary_climate">Quaternary climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paleoclimatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Quaternary climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

Blue curve is temperature,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> red curve is atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and brown curve is dust fluxes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Note length of glacial-interglacial cycles averages ~100,000 years.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Quaternary" title="Quaternary">Quaternary</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_large-scale_temperature_reconstructions_of_the_last_2,000_years" title="List of large-scale temperature reconstructions of the last 2,000 years">List of large-scale temperature reconstructions of the last 2,000 years</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Quaternary" title="Quaternary">Quaternary</a> sub-era includes the current climate. There has been a cycle of <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice ages</a> for the past 2.2–2.1 million years (starting before the Quaternary in the late <a href="/wiki/Neogene" title="Neogene">Neogene</a> Period).
</p><p>Note in the graphic on the right the strong 120,000-year periodicity of the cycles, and the striking asymmetry of the curves. This asymmetry is believed to result from complex interactions of feedback mechanisms. It has been observed that ice ages deepen by progressive steps, but the recovery to interglacial conditions occurs in one big step.
</p>

<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_forcing" title="Climate forcing">climate forcing</a> is the difference of radiant energy (<a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a>) received by the Earth and the outgoing <a href="/wiki/Outgoing_longwave_radiation" title="Outgoing longwave radiation">longwave radiation</a> back to space. The radiative forcing is quantified based on the CO<sub>2</sub> amount in the <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a>, in units of watts per square meter to the Earth's surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Dependent on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiative_balance" title="Radiative balance">radiative balance</a> of incoming and outgoing energy, the Earth either warms up or cools down. Earth radiative balance originates from changes in solar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation">insolation</a> and the concentrations of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gases</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerosols" title="Aerosols">aerosols</a>. Climate change may be due to internal processes in Earth sphere's and/or following external forcings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internal_processes_and_forcings">Internal processes and forcings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paleoclimatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Internal processes and forcings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Earth's climate system involves the study of the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cryosphere" title="Cryosphere">cryosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and the sum of these processes from Earth sphere's is considered the processes affecting the climate. Greenhouse gases act as the internal forcing of the climate system. Particular interests in climate science and paleoclimatology focuses on the study of Earth <a href="/wiki/Climate_sensitivity" title="Climate sensitivity">climate sensitivity</a>, in response to the sum of forcings.
</p><p>Examples:

<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">Thermohaline circulation</a> (Hydrosphere)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">Life</a> (Biosphere)</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="External_forcings">External forcings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paleoclimatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: External forcings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">Milankovitch cycles</a> determine Earth distance and position to the Sun. The solar insolation is the total amount of solar radiation received by Earth.</li>
<li>Volcanic eruptions are considered an external forcing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC_AR5_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC_AR5-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li>Human changes of the composition of the atmosphere or land use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC_AR5_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC_AR5-27">[27]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanisms">Mechanisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paleoclimatology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Mechanisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On timescales of millions of years, the uplift of mountain ranges and subsequent <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a> processes of rocks and soils and the <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subduction</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic_plates" title="Tectonic plates">tectonic plates</a>, are an important part of the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The weathering <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Co2_sequestration" title="Co2 sequestration">sequesters CO<sub>2</sub></a>, by the reaction of minerals with chemicals (especially <a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">silicate</a> weathering with CO<sub>2</sub>) and thereby removing CO<sub>2</sub> from the atmosphere and reducing the radiative forcing. The opposite effect is <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">volcanism</a>, responsible for the natural <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>, by emitting CO<sub>2</sub> into the atmosphere, thus affecting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glaciation" title="Glaciation">glaciation</a> (Ice Age) cycles. <a href="/wiki/James_Hansen" title="James Hansen">James Hansen</a> suggested that humans emit CO<sub>2</sub> 10,000 times faster than natural processes have done in the past.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">Ice sheet</a> dynamics and continental positions (and linked vegetation changes) have been important factors in the long term evolution of the earth's climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> There is also a close correlation between CO<sub>2</sub> and temperature, where CO<sub>2</sub> has a strong control over global temperatures in Earth history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
