<p>The <b>California Air Resources Board</b> (<b>CARB</b> or <b>ARB</b>) is the "clean air agency" in the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_California" title="Government of California">government of California</a>. Established in 1967 when then-governor <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> signed the Mulford-Carrell Act, combining the Bureau of Air Sanitation and the Motor Vehicle Pollution Control Board, CARB is a department within the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_(government)" title="Cabinet (government)">cabinet</a>-level <a href="/wiki/California_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="California Environmental Protection Agency">California Environmental Protection Agency</a>.
</p><p>The stated goals of CARB include attaining and maintaining healthy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_quality" title="Air quality">air quality</a>; protecting the public from exposure to toxic air contaminants; and providing innovative approaches for complying with air pollution rules and regulations. CARB has also been instrumental in driving innovation throughout the global automotive industry through programs such as its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ZEV_mandate" title="ZEV mandate">ZEV mandate</a>.
</p><p>One of CARB's responsibilities is to define <a href="/wiki/United_States_emission_standards" title="United States emission standards">vehicle emissions standards</a>. California is the only state permitted to issue emissions standards under the federal <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)" title="Clean Air Act (United States)">Clean Air Act</a>, subject to a waiver from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a>. Other states may choose to follow CARB or federal standards but may not set their own.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=California_Air_Resources_Board&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>CARB's governing board is made up of 14 members, soon to be 16 with 2 members being non-voting.
</p><p>Six of the governor-appointed board members are chosen from regional <a href="/wiki/List_of_California_air_districts" title="List of California air districts">air pollution control or air quality management districts</a>, including one each from<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Bay_Area_Air_Quality_Management_District" title="Bay Area Air Quality Management District">Bay Area AQMD</a> (San Francisco Bay Area), currently John Gioia</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/San_Diego_County,_California" title="San Diego County, California">San Diego County</a> APCD, currently Ron Roberts</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/San_Joaquin_Valley" title="San Joaquin Valley">San Joaquin Valley</a> APCD, currently Alexander Sherriffs, M.D.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/South_Coast_Air_Quality_Management_District" title="South Coast Air Quality Management District">South Coast AQMD</a>, currently Judy Mitchell</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Sacramento_metropolitan_area" title="Sacramento metropolitan area">Sacramento-area</a> district: Sacramento Metropolitan AQMD, Yolo-Solano AQMD, Placer County APCD, Feather River AQMD, or El Dorado County AQMD, currently Phil Serna</li></ul>
<p>Three governor-appointed board members are experts in <a href="/wiki/Automotive_engineering" title="Automotive engineering">automotive engineering</a>, currently Dan Sperling; science, agriculture, or law, currently John Eisenhut; and medicine, currently John R. Balmes, M.D.. The governor's three remaining appointees are members of the public, including an expert in air pollution control or one of the fields mentioned above.
</p><p>The two legislature-appointed board members work directly with communities affected by air pollution. They are currently Diane Takvorian and Dean Florez, appointed by the Assembly and Senate respectively.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_Quality_Planning_and_Science_Division">Air Quality Planning and Science Division</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=California_Air_Resources_Board&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Air Quality Planning and Science Division">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:THE_LOS_ANGELES_REACTIVE_POLLUTANT_PROGRAM_(LARPP),_IS_A_UNIQUE,_MULTI-AGENCY_AIR_POLLUTION_RESEARCH_STUDY_SPONSORED..._-_NARA_-_552386.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2036" data-file-width="3000" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/THE_LOS_ANGELES_REACTIVE_POLLUTANT_PROGRAM_%28LARPP%29%2C_IS_A_UNIQUE%2C_MULTI-AGENCY_AIR_POLLUTION_RESEARCH_STUDY_SPONSORED..._-_NARA_-_552386.jpg/250px-THE_LOS_ANGELES_REACTIVE_POLLUTANT_PROGRAM_%28LARPP%29%2C_IS_A_UNIQUE%2C_MULTI-AGENCY_AIR_POLLUTION_RESEARCH_STUDY_SPONSORED..._-_NARA_-_552386.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/THE_LOS_ANGELES_REACTIVE_POLLUTANT_PROGRAM_%28LARPP%29%2C_IS_A_UNIQUE%2C_MULTI-AGENCY_AIR_POLLUTION_RESEARCH_STUDY_SPONSORED..._-_NARA_-_552386.jpg/375px-THE_LOS_ANGELES_REACTIVE_POLLUTANT_PROGRAM_%28LARPP%29%2C_IS_A_UNIQUE%2C_MULTI-AGENCY_AIR_POLLUTION_RESEARCH_STUDY_SPONSORED..._-_NARA_-_552386.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/THE_LOS_ANGELES_REACTIVE_POLLUTANT_PROGRAM_%28LARPP%29%2C_IS_A_UNIQUE%2C_MULTI-AGENCY_AIR_POLLUTION_RESEARCH_STUDY_SPONSORED..._-_NARA_-_552386.jpg/500px-THE_LOS_ANGELES_REACTIVE_POLLUTANT_PROGRAM_%28LARPP%29%2C_IS_A_UNIQUE%2C_MULTI-AGENCY_AIR_POLLUTION_RESEARCH_STUDY_SPONSORED..._-_NARA_-_552386.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:THE_LOS_ANGELES_REACTIVE_POLLUTANT_PROGRAM_(LARPP),_IS_A_UNIQUE,_MULTI-AGENCY_AIR_POLLUTION_RESEARCH_STUDY_SPONSORED..._-_NARA_-_552386.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>California Air Resources Board Laboratory, Los Angeles, in 1973</div></div></div>
<p>The division assesses the extent of California's air quality problems and the progress being made to abate them, coordinates statewide development of clean air plans and maintains databases pertinent to air quality and emissions. The division's technical support work provides a basis for clean air plans and CARB's regulatory programs. This support includes management and interpretation of emission inventories, air quality data, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorological" title="Meteorological">meteorological</a> data and of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_quality_modeling" title="Air quality modeling">air quality modeling</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PTSD_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-PTSD-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Air Quality Planning and Science Division has five branches:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Emission_inventory" title="Emission inventory">Emission Inventory</a> Branch</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_dispersion_modeling" title="Atmospheric dispersion modeling">Modeling</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">Meteorology</a> Branch</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_quality" title="Air quality">Air Quality</a> Data Branch</li>
<li>Air Quality &amp; <a href="/wiki/Transportation_planning" title="Transportation planning">Transportation Planning</a> Branch</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_pollution_dispersion_terminology" title="Air pollution dispersion terminology">Mobile Source</a> Analysis Branch</li></ul>
<h4><span id="Atmospheric_Modeling_.26_Support_Section"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_Modeling_&amp;_Support_Section">Atmospheric Modeling &amp; Support Section</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=California_Air_Resources_Board&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Atmospheric Modeling &amp; Support Section">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Atmospheric Modeling &amp; Support Section is one of three sections within the Modeling &amp; Meteorology Branch. The other two sections are the Regional Air Quality Modeling Section and the Meteorology Section.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PTSD_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-PTSD-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The air quality and <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_dispersion_modeling" title="Atmospheric dispersion modeling">atmospheric pollution dispersion models</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> routinely used by this Section include a number of the models recommended by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">U.S. Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA). The section uses models which were either developed by CARB or whose development was funded by CARB, such as:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/CALPUFF" title="CALPUFF">CALPUFF</a> – Originally developed by the Sigma Research Company (SRC) under contract to CARB. Currently maintained by the TRC Solution Company under contract to the U.S. EPA.</li>
<li>CALGRID – Developed by CARB and currently maintained by CARB.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>SARMAP – Developed by CARB and currently maintained by CARB.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_reducing_greenhouse_gases">Role in reducing greenhouse gases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=California_Air_Resources_Board&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Role in reducing greenhouse gases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_California" title="Climate change in California">Climate change in California</a></div>
<p>The California Air Resources Board is charged with implementing California's comprehensive suite of policies to reduce emissions of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>. In part due to the efforts of CARB,  California has successfully decoupled greenhouse gas emissions from economic growth, and achieved its goal of reducing emissions to 1990 levels four years earlier than the target date of 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CARBrelease_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-CARBrelease-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_Fuel_Vehicle_Incentive_Program">Alternative Fuel Vehicle Incentive Program</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=California_Air_Resources_Board&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Alternative Fuel Vehicle Incentive Program">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Alternative Fuel Vehicle Incentive Program (also known as Fueling Alternatives) is funded by the California Air Resources Board (CARB), offered throughout the State of California and administered by the California Center for Sustainable Energy (CCSE).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CARBFuelAlt_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-CARBFuelAlt-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="California_zero-emissions_vehicle">California zero-emissions vehicle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=California_Air_Resources_Board&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: California zero-emissions vehicle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The CARB ZEV program was enacted by the California government to promote the use of zero emission vehicles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Union_of_Concerned_Scientists_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Union_of_Concerned_Scientists-12">[12]</a></sup> The program goal is to reduce the pervasive air pollution affecting the main metropolitan areas in the state, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, where prolonged pollution episodes are frequent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TwoBillion_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-TwoBillion-13">[13]</a></sup> The first ruling was the 1990 Low-Emission Vehicle (LEV I) Program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TwoBillion_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-TwoBillion-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CARB2_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-CARB2-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first definition has its origin in the California ZEV rule, adopted as part of the 1990 Low-Emission Vehicle (LEV I) Program mandated by CARB.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TwoBillion_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-TwoBillion-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CARB2_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-CARB2-14">[14]</a></sup> The ZEV regulation has evolved and been modified several times since 1990, and several new partial or low-emission categories were created and defined as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CARB2_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-CARB2-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CARB3_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-CARB3-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boschert_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boschert-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AboutZEV_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-AboutZEV-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_Emission_Vehicle" title="Low Emission Vehicle">LEV (Low Emission Vehicle)</a>: The least stringent emission standard for all new cars sold in California beyond 2004.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultra_Low_Emission_Vehicle" title="Ultra Low Emission Vehicle">ULEV (Ultra Low Emission Vehicle)</a>: 50% cleaner than the average new 2003 model year vehicle.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Super_Ultra_Low_Emission_Vehicle" title="Super Ultra Low Emission Vehicle">SULEV (Super Ultra Low Emission Vehicle)</a>: These vehicles emit substantially lower levels of hydrocarbons, carbon monoxide, oxides of nitrogen and particulate matter than conventional vehicles. They are 90% cleaner than the average new 2003 model year vehicle.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Partial_zero-emissions_vehicle" title="Partial zero-emissions vehicle">PZEV (Partial Zero Emission Vehicle)</a>: Meets SULEV tailpipe standards, has a 15-year / 150,000 mile warranty, and zero evaporative emissions. These vehicles are 80% cleaner than the average 2002 model year car.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PZEV" title="PZEV">AT PZEV (Advanced Technology PZEV)</a>: These are advanced technology vehicles that meet PZEV standards and include ZEV enabling technology. They are 80% cleaner than the average 2002 model year car.</li>
<li>ZEV (Zero Emission Vehicle): Zero tailpipe emissions, and 98% cleaner than the average new 2003 model year vehicle.</li></ul>
<p>The Low-Emission Vehicle Program is currently under revision to define modified ZEV regulations for 2015 models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CARB2_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-CARB2-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CARB4_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-CARB4-19">[19]</a></sup> CARB estimates the ZEV program will result in 15% ZEV sales by 2025. The share remained at 3% between 2014 and 2016. Battery vehicles receive 3 or 4 credits, while fuel cell cars receive 9. As of  2016<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=California_Air_Resources_Board&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, a credit has a market value of $3-4,000, and some automakers have more credits than required.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> CARB voted unanimously in March 2017 to require automakers to average 54.5 mpg for new cars in 2025.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Low-carbon_fuel_standard">Low-carbon fuel standard</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=California_Air_Resources_Board&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Low-carbon fuel standard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Low-carbon_fuel_standard" title="Low-carbon fuel standard">Low-carbon fuel standard</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Low-carbon_fuel_standard#California_Low-Carbon_Fuel_Standard" title="Low-carbon fuel standard">Low-Carbon Fuel Standard</a> (LCFS) requires <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">oil refineries</a> and distributors to ensure that the mix of fuel they sell in the Californian market meets the established declining targets for <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions measured in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CO2-equivalent" title="CO2-equivalent">CO2-equivalent</a> <a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">grams</a> per unit of fuel energy sold for transport purposes. The 2007 Governor's LCFS directive calls for a reduction of at least 10% in the carbon intensity of California's transportation fuels by 2020. These reductions include not only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tailpipe" title="Tailpipe">tailpipe</a> emissions but also all other associated emissions from production, distribution and use of transport fuels within the state. Therefore, California LCFS considers the fuel's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_cycle_assessment" title="Life cycle assessment">full life cycle</a>, also known as the "well to wheels" or "seed to wheels" efficiency of transport fuels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TwoBillion_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-TwoBillion-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CARB01_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-CARB01-23">[23]</a></sup> The standard is aimed to reduce the state’s dependence on petroleum, create a market for clean transportation technology, and stimulate the production and use of alternative, low-carbon fuels in California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ARBproposal_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-ARBproposal-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>On April 23, 2009, CARB approved the specific rules for the LCFS that will go into effect in January 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFG042409_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-SFG042409-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT042409_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT042409-26">[26]</a></sup> The rule proposal prepared by its technical staff was approved by a 9-1 vote, to set the 2020 maximum carbon intensity reference value to 86 <a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">grams</a> of carbon dioxide released per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megajoule" title="Megajoule">megajoule</a> of energy produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ARBproposal_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-ARBproposal-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNICA01_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNICA01-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="PHEV_Research_Center">PHEV Research Center</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=California_Air_Resources_Board&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: PHEV Research Center">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PHEV_Research_Center" title="PHEV Research Center">PHEV Research Center</a></div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PHEV_Research_Center" title="PHEV Research Center">PHEV Research Center</a> was launched with funding from the California Air Resources Board.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=California_Air_Resources_Board&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>CARB's Goods Movement Emission Reduction Plan (GMERP) has been criticized by a coalition of California truck owners, farmers, construction contractors and business and community leaders. The coalition has estimated that "applying the new emissions regulations to the 2.3 million or so diesel trucks that move goods throughout California could cost at least $8 billion".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
