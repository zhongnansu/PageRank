<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Artery</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>An <b>artery</b> (plural <b>arteries</b>) (from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i> </i>ἀρτηρία<i> (artēria)</i>, meaning 'windpipe, artery')<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Blood_vessel" title="Blood vessel">blood vessel</a> that takes <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a>  away from the heart to all parts of the body (tissues, lungs, etc). Most arteries carry oxygenated blood; the two exceptions are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulmonary_arteries" title="Pulmonary arteries">pulmonary</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Umbilical_artery" title="Umbilical artery">umbilical arteries</a>, which carry deoxygenated blood to the organs that oxygenate it. The <a href="/wiki/Effective_arterial_blood_volume" title="Effective arterial blood volume">effective arterial blood volume</a> is that <a href="/wiki/Extracellular_fluid" title="Extracellular fluid">extracellular fluid</a> which fills the arterial system.
</p><p>The arteries are part of the <a href="/wiki/Circulatory_system" title="Circulatory system">circulatory system</a>, which is responsible for the delivery of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrients</a> to all cells, as well as the removal of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and waste products, the maintenance of optimum <a href="/wiki/PH#Living_systems" title="PH">blood pH</a>, and the circulation of proteins and cells of the <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune system</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Developed_country" title="Developed country">developed countries</a>, the two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leading_causes_of_death" title="Leading causes of death">leading causes of death</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">myocardial infarction</a> (heart attack), and <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a>, may each directly result from an arterial system that has been slowly and progressively compromised by years of deterioration (usually clogged by cholesterol).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Blausen_0055_ArteryWallStructure.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2500" data-file-width="2500" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Blausen_0055_ArteryWallStructure.png/220px-Blausen_0055_ArteryWallStructure.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Blausen_0055_ArteryWallStructure.png/330px-Blausen_0055_ArteryWallStructure.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Blausen_0055_ArteryWallStructure.png/440px-Blausen_0055_ArteryWallStructure.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Blausen_0055_ArteryWallStructure.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Microscopic anatomy of an artery.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Artery.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1637" data-file-width="1914" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Artery.png/220px-Artery.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Artery.png/330px-Artery.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Artery.png/440px-Artery.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Artery.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cross-section of a human artery</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Arterial_tree" title="Arterial tree">Arterial tree</a></div>
<p>The anatomy of arteries can be separated into <a href="/wiki/Gross_anatomy" title="Gross anatomy">gross anatomy</a>, at the <a href="/wiki/Macroscopic_scale" title="Macroscopic scale">macroscopic level</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Histology" title="Histology">microanatomy</a>, which must be studied with a <a href="/wiki/Microscope" title="Microscope">microscope</a>. The arterial system of the <a href="/wiki/Human_body" title="Human body">human body</a> is divided into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systemic_circulation" title="Systemic circulation">systemic arteries</a>, carrying blood from the heart to the whole body, and <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_artery" title="Pulmonary artery">pulmonary arteries</a>, carrying deoxygenated blood from the <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">lungs</a>.
</p><p>The outermost layer of an artery (or vein) is known as the <a href="/wiki/Tunica_externa" title="Tunica externa">tunica externa</a>, also known as <i>tunica adventitia</i>, and is composed of <a href="/wiki/Collagen" title="Collagen">collagen</a> fibers and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elastic_tissue" title="Elastic tissue">elastic tissue</a> - with the largest arteries containing <a href="/wiki/Vasa_vasorum" title="Vasa vasorum">vasa vasorum</a> (small blood vessels that supply large blood vessels)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup>. Inside this layer is the <a href="/wiki/Tunica_media" title="Tunica media">tunica media</a>, or <i>media</i>, which is made up of <a href="/wiki/Smooth_muscle" title="Smooth muscle">smooth muscle</a> cells, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elastic_tissue" title="Elastic tissue">elastic tissue</a> (also called <i>connective tissue proper</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Collagen" title="Collagen">collagen</a> fibres<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup>. The innermost layer, which is in direct contact with the flow of blood, is the <a href="/wiki/Tunica_intima" title="Tunica intima">tunica intima</a>, commonly called the <i>intima</i>. The elastic tissue allows the artery to bend and fit through places in the body. This layer is mainly made up of <a href="/wiki/Endothelium" title="Endothelium">endothelial cells</a> (and a supporting layer of <a href="/wiki/Elastin" title="Elastin">elastin</a> rich <a href="/wiki/Collagen" title="Collagen">collagen</a> in elastic arteries). The hollow internal cavity in which the blood flows is called the <a href="/wiki/Lumen_(anatomy)" title="Lumen (anatomy)">lumen</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Arterial formation begins and ends when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endothelial_cell" title="Endothelial cell">endothelial cells</a> begin to express arterial specific genes, such as <a href="/wiki/EPH_receptor_B2" title="EPH receptor B2">ephrin B2</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arterial_System_en.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="842" data-file-width="595" height="311" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Arterial_System_en.svg/220px-Arterial_System_en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Arterial_System_en.svg/330px-Arterial_System_en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Arterial_System_en.svg/440px-Arterial_System_en.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arterial_System_en.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Arteries form part of the human <a href="/wiki/Circulatory_system" title="Circulatory system">circulatory system</a></div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Circulatory_system" title="Circulatory system">Circulatory system</a></div>
<p>Arteries form part of the <a href="/wiki/Circulatory_system" title="Circulatory system">circulatory system</a>. They carry <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> that is oxygenated after it has been pumped from the <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a>. <a href="/wiki/Coronary_circulation" title="Coronary circulation">Coronary arteries</a> also aid the heart in pumping blood. Arteries carry oxygenated blood away from the heart to the tissues, except for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulmonary" title="Pulmonary">pulmonary</a> arteries, which carry blood to the lungs for oxygenation (usually <a href="/wiki/Vein" title="Vein">veins</a> carry deoxygenated blood to the heart but the <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_vein" title="Pulmonary vein">pulmonary veins</a> carry oxygenated blood as well).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  There are two types of unique arteries. The <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_artery" title="Pulmonary artery">pulmonary artery</a> carries blood from the heart to the <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">lungs</a>, where it receives oxygen. It is unique because the blood in it is not "oxygenated", as it has not yet passed through the lungs. The other unique artery is the <a href="/wiki/Umbilical_artery" title="Umbilical artery">umbilical artery</a>, which carries deoxygenated blood from a fetus to its mother.
</p><p>Arteries have a <a href="/wiki/Blood_pressure" title="Blood pressure">blood pressure</a> higher than other parts of the circulatory system. The pressure in arteries varies during the <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_cycle" title="Cardiac cycle">cardiac cycle</a>. It is highest when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systole_(medicine)" title="Systole (medicine)">heart contracts</a> and lowest when <a href="/wiki/Diastole" title="Diastole">heart relaxes</a>. The variation in pressure produces a <a href="/wiki/Pulse" title="Pulse">pulse</a>, which can be felt in different areas of the body, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radial_pulse" title="Radial pulse">radial pulse</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arterioles" title="Arterioles">Arterioles</a> have the greatest collective influence on both local blood flow and on overall blood pressure. They are the primary "adjustable nozzles" in the blood system, across which the greatest pressure drop occurs. The combination of heart output (<a href="/wiki/Cardiac_output" title="Cardiac output">cardiac output</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systemic_vascular_resistance" title="Systemic vascular resistance">systemic vascular resistance</a>, which refers to the collective resistance of all of the body's <a href="/wiki/Arteriole" title="Arteriole">arterioles</a>, are the principal determinants of arterial blood pressure at any given moment.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systemic_circulation" title="Systemic circulation">Systemic arteries</a> are the arteries (including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peripheral_arteries" title="Peripheral arteries">peripheral arteries</a>), of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systemic_circulation" title="Systemic circulation">systemic circulation</a>, which is the part of the <a href="/wiki/Circulatory_system" title="Circulatory system">cardiovascular system</a> that carries <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygenated</a> <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> away from the heart, to the <a href="/wiki/Human_body" title="Human body">body</a>, and returns deoxygenated blood back to the heart. Systemic arteries can be subdivided into two types—muscular and elastic—according to the relative compositions of elastic and muscle tissue in their tunica media as well as their size and the makeup of the internal and external elastic lamina. The larger arteries (&gt;10 mm diameter) are generally elastic and the smaller ones (0.1–10 mm) tend to be muscular. Systemic arteries deliver blood to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arterioles" title="Arterioles">arterioles</a>, and then to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capillaries" title="Capillaries">capillaries</a>, where nutrients and gases are exchanged.
</p><p>After travelling from the <a href="/wiki/Aorta" title="Aorta">aorta</a>, blood travels through peripheral arteries into smaller arteries called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arterioles" title="Arterioles">arterioles</a>, and eventually to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capillaries" title="Capillaries">capillaries</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arterioles" title="Arterioles">Arterioles</a> help in regulating <a href="/wiki/Blood_pressure" title="Blood pressure">blood pressure</a> by the variable contraction of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smooth_muscle_tissue" title="Smooth muscle tissue">smooth muscle</a> of their walls, and deliver blood to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capillaries" title="Capillaries">capillaries</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aorta">Aorta</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Aorta">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Aorta" title="Aorta">aorta</a> is the root <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systemic_circulation" title="Systemic circulation">systemic</a> artery (i.e., main artery). In humans, it receives blood directly from the left <a href="/wiki/Ventricle_(heart)" title="Ventricle (heart)">ventricle</a> of the heart via the <a href="/wiki/Aortic_valve" title="Aortic valve">aortic valve</a>. As the aorta branches, and these arteries branch in turn, they become successively smaller in diameter, down to the <a href="/wiki/Arteriole" title="Arteriole">arterioles</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Arteriole" title="Arteriole">arterioles</a> supply <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capillaries" title="Capillaries">capillaries</a>, which in turn empty into <a href="/wiki/Venule" title="Venule">venules</a>. The very first branches off of the aorta are the <a href="/wiki/Coronary_circulation" title="Coronary circulation">coronary arteries</a>, which supply blood to the heart muscle itself. These are followed by the branches off the aortic arch, namely the <a href="/wiki/Brachiocephalic_artery" title="Brachiocephalic artery">brachiocephalic artery</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left_common_carotid" title="Left common carotid">left common carotid</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Subclavian_artery" title="Subclavian artery">left subclavian</a> arteries.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Capillaries">Capillaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Capillaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capillaries" title="Capillaries">Capillaries</a></div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capillaries" title="Capillaries">capillaries</a> are the smallest of the blood vessels and are part of the <a href="/wiki/Microcirculation" title="Microcirculation">microcirculation</a>. The microvessels have a width of a single cell in diameter to aid in the fast and easy <a href="/wiki/Diffusion#Diffusion_vs._bulk_flow" title="Diffusion">diffusion</a> of gases, sugars and nutrients to surrounding tissues. Capillaries have no <a href="/wiki/Smooth_muscle" title="Smooth muscle">smooth muscle</a> surrounding them and have a diameter less than that of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_blood_cells" title="Red blood cells">red blood cells</a>; a red blood cell is typically 7 micrometers outside diameter, capillaries typically 5 micrometers inside diameter. The red blood cells must distort in order to pass through the capillaries.
</p><p>These small diameters of the capillaries provide a relatively large surface area for the exchange of gases and nutrients.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_significance">Clinical significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Clinical significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Atherosclerosis_diagram.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="451" data-file-width="450" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Atherosclerosis_diagram.png/220px-Atherosclerosis_diagram.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Atherosclerosis_diagram.png/330px-Atherosclerosis_diagram.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Atherosclerosis_diagram.png/440px-Atherosclerosis_diagram.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Atherosclerosis_diagram.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diagram showing the effects of <a href="/wiki/Atherosclerosis" title="Atherosclerosis">atherosclerosis</a> on an artery.</div></div></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systemic_circulation" title="Systemic circulation">Systemic</a> arterial pressures, are generated by the forceful contractions of the heart's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left_ventricle" title="Left ventricle">left ventricle</a>. High <a href="/wiki/Blood_pressure" title="Blood pressure">blood pressure</a> is a factor in causing arterial damage. Healthy resting arterial pressures, are relatively low, mean systemic pressures typically being under 100 <a href="/wiki/Millimeter_of_mercury" title="Millimeter of mercury">mmHg</a> (1.9 <a href="/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch" title="Pounds per square inch">psi</a>; 13 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">kPa</a>) above surrounding <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> (about 760 mmHg, 14.7 psi, 101 kPa at sea level). To withstand and adapt to the pressures within, arteries are surrounded by varying thicknesses of <a href="/wiki/Smooth_muscle" title="Smooth muscle">smooth muscle</a> which have extensive elastic and inelastic <a href="/wiki/Connective_tissue" title="Connective tissue">connective tissues</a>. The pulse pressure, being the difference between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systole_(medicine)" title="Systole (medicine)">systolic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diastole" title="Diastole">diastolic</a> pressure, is determined primarily by the amount of blood ejected by each heart beat, <a href="/wiki/Stroke_volume" title="Stroke volume">stroke volume</a>, versus the volume and elasticity of the major arteries.
</p><p>A  <a href="/wiki/Blood_squirt" title="Blood squirt">blood squirt</a> also known as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arterial_gush" title="Arterial gush">arterial gush</a> is the effect when an artery is <a href="/wiki/Cutting" title="Cutting">cut</a> due to the higher arterial pressures. Blood is spurted out at a rapid, intermittent rate, that coincides with the heartbeat. The amount of <a href="/wiki/Exsanguination" title="Exsanguination">blood loss</a> can be copious, can occur very rapidly, and be life-threatening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Over time, factors such as elevated arterial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_sugar" title="Blood sugar">blood sugar</a> (particularly as seen in <a href="/wiki/Diabetes_mellitus" title="Diabetes mellitus">diabetes mellitus</a>), <a href="/wiki/Lipoprotein" title="Lipoprotein">lipoprotein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cholesterol" title="Cholesterol">cholesterol</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_blood_pressure" title="High blood pressure">high blood pressure</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stress_(psychological)" title="Stress (psychological)">stress</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_smoking" title="Tobacco smoking">smoking</a>, are all implicated in damaging both the <a href="/wiki/Endothelium" title="Endothelium">endothelium</a> and walls of the arteries, resulting in <a href="/wiki/Atherosclerosis" title="Atherosclerosis">atherosclerosis</a>. Atherosclerosis is a disease marked by the hardening of arteries. This is caused by an <a href="/wiki/Atheroma" title="Atheroma">atheroma</a> or plaque in the artery wall and is a build-up of cell debris, that contain <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipids</a>, (cholesterol and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatty_acids" title="Fatty acids">fatty acids</a>), <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and a variable amount of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fibrous_connective_tissue" title="Fibrous connective tissue">fibrous connective tissue</a>.
</p><p>Accidental intraarterial injection either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iatrogenic" title="Iatrogenic">iatrogenically</a> or through recreational drug use can cause symptoms such as intense pain, <a href="/wiki/Paresthesia" title="Paresthesia">paresthesia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Necrosis" title="Necrosis">necrosis</a>. It usually causes permanent damage to the limb;  often <a href="/wiki/Amputation" title="Amputation">amputation</a> is necessary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Among the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greeks" title="Ancient Greeks">Ancient Greeks</a>, the arteries were considered to be "air holders" that were responsible for the transport of air to the tissues and were connected to the <a href="/wiki/Trachea" title="Trachea">trachea</a>. This was as a result of finding the arteries of cadavers devoid of blood.
</p><p>In medieval times, it was recognized that arteries carried a fluid, called "spiritual blood" or "vital spirits", considered to be different from the contents of the <a href="/wiki/Vein" title="Vein">veins</a>. This theory went back to <a href="/wiki/Galen" title="Galen">Galen</a>. In the late medieval period, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertebrate_trachea" title="Vertebrate trachea">trachea</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ligaments" title="Ligaments">ligaments</a> were also called "arteries".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/William_Harvey" title="William Harvey">William Harvey</a> described and popularized the modern concept of the circulatory system and the roles of arteries and veins in the 17th century.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Alexis_Carrel" title="Alexis Carrel">Alexis Carrel</a> at the beginning of the 20th century first described the technique for vascular suturing and anastomosis and successfully performed many <a href="/wiki/Organ_transplantation" title="Organ transplantation">organ transplantations</a> in animals; he thus actually opened the way to modern <a href="/wiki/Vascular_surgery" title="Vascular surgery">vascular surgery</a> that was before limited to vessels permanent ligation.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theodor_Kocher" title="Theodor Kocher">Theodor Kocher</a> reported that <a href="/wiki/Atherosclerosis" title="Atherosclerosis">atherosclerosis</a> often developed in patients who had undergone a <a href="/wiki/Thyroidectomy" title="Thyroidectomy">thyroidectomy</a> and suggested that <a href="/wiki/Hypothyroidism" title="Hypothyroidism">hypothyroidism</a> favors atherosclerosis, which was, in 1900s autopsies, seen more frequently in iodine-deficient Austrians compared to Icelanders, who are not deficient in <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a>. Turner reported the effectiveness of <a href="/wiki/Iodide" title="Iodide">iodide</a> and dried extracts of <a href="/wiki/Thyroid" title="Thyroid">thyroid</a> in the prevention of atherosclerosis in laboratory rabbits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
