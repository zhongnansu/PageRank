<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Economies of scale</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Economies_of_scale.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="600" height="275" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Economies_of_scale.svg/330px-Economies_of_scale.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Economies_of_scale.svg/495px-Economies_of_scale.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Economies_of_scale.svg/660px-Economies_of_scale.svg.png 2x" width="330"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Economies_of_scale.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>As quantity of production increases from Q to Q2, the average cost of each unit decreases from C to C1. LRAC is the long run average cost</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Microeconomics" title="Microeconomics">microeconomics</a>, <b>economies of scale</b> are the cost advantages that  enterprises obtain due to their scale of operation (typically measured by amount of output produced), with cost per unit of output decreasing with increasing scale.
</p><p>Economies of scale apply to a variety of organizational and business situations and at various levels, such as a business or manufacturing unit, plant or an entire enterprise. When average costs start falling as output increases, then economies of scale are occurring. 
Some economies of scale, such as capital cost of manufacturing facilities and friction loss of transportation and industrial equipment, have a <a href="/wiki/Economies_of_scale#Physical_and_engineering_basis" title="Economies of scale">physical or engineering basis</a>.
</p><p>Another source of scale economies is the possibility of purchasing inputs at a lower per-unit cost when they are purchased in large quantities.
</p><p>The economic concept dates back to <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a> and the idea of obtaining larger production returns through the use of division of labor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Diseconomies_of_scale" title="Diseconomies of scale">Diseconomies of scale</a> are the opposite.
</p><p>Economies of scale often have limits, such as passing the optimum design point where costs per additional unit begin to increase.  Common limits include exceeding the nearby raw material supply, such as wood in the lumber, <a href="/wiki/Pulp_and_paper_industry" title="Pulp and paper industry">pulp and paper industry</a>. A common limit for low cost per unit weight commodities is saturating the regional market, thus having to ship product uneconomical distances. Other limits include using energy less efficiently or having a higher defect rate.
</p><p>Large producers are usually efficient at long runs of a product grade (a commodity) and find it costly to switch grades frequently.  They will therefore avoid specialty grades even though they have higher margins.  Often smaller (usually older) manufacturing facilities remain viable by changing from commodity grade production to specialty products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economies_of_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The simple meaning of economies of scale is doing things more efficiently with increasing size or speed of operation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Economies of scale often rely on <a href="/wiki/Fixed_cost" title="Fixed cost">fixed costs</a>, which are constant and don't vary with output, and <a href="/wiki/Variable_cost" title="Variable cost">variable costs</a>, which can be affected with the amount of output. In wholesale and retail distribution, increasing the speed of operations, such as order fulfillment, lowers the cost of both fixed and <a href="/wiki/Working_capital" title="Working capital">working capital</a>. Other common sources of economies of scale are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Purchase" title="Purchase">purchasing</a> (bulk buying of materials through long-term contracts), managerial (increasing the specialization of managers), financial (obtaining lower-<a href="/wiki/Interest" title="Interest">interest</a> charges when borrowing from banks and having access to a greater range of financial instruments), <a href="/wiki/Marketing" title="Marketing">marketing</a> (spreading the cost of advertising over a greater range of output in <a href="/wiki/Media_market" title="Media market">media markets</a>), and technological (taking advantage of <a href="/wiki/Returns_to_scale" title="Returns to scale">returns to scale</a> in the production function). Each of these factors reduces the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_run_average_cost" title="Long run average cost">long run average costs</a> (LRAC) of production by shifting the <a href="/wiki/Cost_curve" title="Cost curve">short-run average total cost (SRATC) curve</a> down and to the right.
</p><p>Economies of scale is a practical concept that may explain real-world phenomena such as patterns of international trade or the number of firms in a market. The exploitation of economies of scale helps explain why companies grow large in some industries. It is also a justification for <a href="/wiki/Free_trade" title="Free trade">free trade</a> policies, since some economies of scale may require a larger market than is possible within a particular country—for example, it would not be efficient for <a href="/wiki/Liechtenstein" title="Liechtenstein">Liechtenstein</a> to have its own carmaker if they only sold to their local market. A lone carmaker may be profitable, but even more so if they exported cars to global markets in addition to selling to the local market. Economies of scale also play a role in a "<a href="/wiki/Natural_monopoly" title="Natural monopoly">natural monopoly</a>". There is a distinction between two types of economies of scale: internal and external. An industry that exhibits an internal economy of scale is one where the costs of production fall when the number of firms in the industry drops, but the remaining firms increase their production to match previous levels. Conversely, an industry exhibits an external economy of scale when costs drop due to the introduction of more firms, thus allowing for more efficient use of specialized services and machinery. 
</p><p>The management thinker and translator of the <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Production_System" title="Toyota Production System">Toyota Production System</a> for service, Professor <a href="/wiki/John_Seddon" title="John Seddon">John Seddon</a>, argues that attempting to create economies by increasing scale is powered by myth in the service sector. Instead, he believes that economies will come from improving the flow of a service, from the first receipt of a customer’s demand to the eventual satisfaction of that demand. In trying to manage and reduce unit costs, firms often raise total costs by creating <a href="/wiki/Failure_demand" title="Failure demand">failure demand</a>. Seddon claims that arguments for an economy of scale are a mix of (a) the plausibly obvious and (b) a little hard data, brought together to produce two broad assertions, for which there is little hard factual evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_and_engineering_basis">Physical and engineering basis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economies_of_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical and engineering basis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some of the economies of scale recognized in engineering have a physical basis, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Square-cube_law" title="Square-cube law">square-cube law</a>, by which the surface of a vessel increases by the square of the dimensions while the volume increases by the cube.  This law has a direct effect on the capital cost of such things as buildings, factories, pipelines, ships and airplanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>In structural engineering, the strength of <a href="/wiki/Beam_(structure)" title="Beam (structure)">beams</a> increases with the cube of the thickness.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">Drag</a> loss of vehicles like aircraft or ships generally increases less than proportional with increasing cargo volume, although the physical details can be quite complicated. Therefore, making them larger usually results in less fuel consumption per ton of cargo at a given speed.
</p><p>Heat losses from industrial processes vary per unit of volume for pipes, tanks and other vessels in a relationship somewhat similar to the square-cube law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Capital_and_operating_cost">Capital and operating cost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economies_of_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Capital and operating cost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Overall costs of capital projects are known to be subject to economies of scale.  A crude estimate is that if the capital cost for a given sized piece of equipment is known, changing the size will change the capital cost by the 0.6 power of the capacity ratio (the point six power rule).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>In estimating capital cost, it typically requires an insignificant amount of labor, and possibly not much more in materials, to install a larger capacity electrical wire or pipe having significantly greater capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The cost of a unit of capacity of many types of equipment, such as electric motors, centrifugal pumps, diesel and gasoline engines, decreases as size increases.  Also, the efficiency increases with size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Crew_size_and_other_operating_costs_for_ships.2C_trains_and_airplanes"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Crew_size_and_other_operating_costs_for_ships,_trains_and_airplanes">Crew size and other operating costs for ships, trains and airplanes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economies_of_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Crew size and other operating costs for ships, trains and airplanes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Operating crew size for ships, airplanes, trains, etc., does not increase in direct proportion to capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>  (Operating crew consists of pilots, co-pilots, navigators, etc. and does not include passenger service personnel.)  Many aircraft models were significantly lengthened or "stretched" to increase payload.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many manufacturing facilities, especially those making bulk materials like chemicals, refined petroleum products, cement and paper, have labor requirements that are not greatly influenced by changes in plant capacity.  This is because labor requirements of automated processes tend to be based on the complexity of the operation rather than production rate, and many manufacturing facilities have nearly the same basic number of processing steps and pieces of equipment, regardless of production capacity.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economical_use_of_byproducts">Economical use of byproducts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economies_of_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Economical use of byproducts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a> noted that large scale manufacturing allowed economical use of products that would otherwise be waste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>  Marx cited the chemical industry as an example, which today along with petrochemicals, remains highly dependent on turning various residual reactant streams into salable products.  In the pulp and paper industry it is economical to burn bark and fine wood particles to produce <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Process_steam&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Process steam (page does not exist)">process steam</a> and to recover the spent pulping chemicals for conversion back to a usable form.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economies_of_scale_and_returns_to_scale">Economies of scale and returns to scale</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economies_of_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Economies of scale and returns to scale">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Economies of scale is related to and can easily be confused with the theoretical economic notion of returns to scale.  Where economies of scale refer to a firm's costs, returns to scale describe the relationship between inputs and outputs in a long-run (all inputs variable) production function. A production function has <i>constant</i> returns to scale if increasing all inputs by some proportion results in output increasing by that same proportion. Returns are <i>decreasing</i> if, say, doubling inputs results in less than double the output, and <i>increasing</i> if more than double the output.  If a mathematical function is used to represent the production function, and if that production function is <a href="/wiki/Homogeneous_function" title="Homogeneous function">homogeneous</a>, returns to scale are represented by the degree of homogeneity of the function.  Homogeneous production functions with constant returns to scale are first degree homogeneous, increasing returns to scale are represented by degrees of homogeneity greater than one, and decreasing returns to scale by degrees of homogeneity less than one.
</p><p>If the firm is a perfect competitor in all input markets, and thus the per-unit prices of all its inputs are unaffected by how much of the inputs the firm purchases, then it can be shown that at a particular level of output, the firm has economies of scale if and only if it has increasing returns to scale, has diseconomies of scale if and only if it has decreasing returns to scale, and has neither economies nor diseconomies of scale if it has constant returns to scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>  In this case, with <a href="/wiki/Perfect_competition" title="Perfect competition">perfect competition</a> in the output market the long-run equilibrium will involve all firms operating at the minimum point of their long-run average cost curves (i.e., at the borderline between economies and diseconomies of scale).
</p><p>If, however, the firm is not a perfect competitor in the input markets, then the above conclusions are modified.  For example, if there are increasing returns to scale in some range of output levels, but the firm is so big in one or more input markets that increasing its purchases of an input drives up the input's per-unit cost, then the firm could have diseconomies of scale in that range of output levels.  Conversely, if the firm is able to get bulk discounts of an input, then it could have economies of scale in some range of output levels even if it has decreasing returns in production in that output range.
</p><p>The literature assumed that due to the competitive nature of <a href="/wiki/Reverse_auction" title="Reverse auction">reverse auctions</a>, and in order to compensate for lower prices and lower margins, suppliers seek higher volumes to maintain or increase the total revenue. Buyers, in turn, benefit from the lower transaction costs and economies of scale that result from larger volumes. In part as a result, numerous studies have indicated that the procurement volume must be sufficiently high to provide sufficient profits to attract enough suppliers, and provide buyers with enough savings to cover their additional costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShalevAsbjornsen2010_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-ShalevAsbjornsen2010-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, surprisingly enough, Shalev and Asbjornse found, in their research based on 139 reverse auctions conducted in the public sector by public sector buyers, that the higher auction volume, or economies of scale, did not lead to better success of the auction. They found that auction volume did not correlate with competition, nor with the number of bidders, suggesting that auction volume does not promote additional competition. They noted, however, that their data included a wide range of products, and the degree of competition in each market varied significantly, and offer that further research on this issue should be conducted to determine whether these findings remain the same when purchasing the same product for both small and high volumes. Keeping competitive factors constant, increasing auction volume may further increase competition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ShalevAsbjornsen2010_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-ShalevAsbjornsen2010-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distributional_consequences">Distributional consequences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economies_of_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Distributional consequences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In his 1844 <i><a href="/wiki/Economic_and_Philosophic_Manuscripts_of_1844" title="Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts of 1844">Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a> observes that economies of scale have historically been associated with an increasing concentration of private wealth and have been used to justify such concentration. Marx points out that concentrated private ownership of large-scale economic enterprises is a historically contingent fact, and not essential to the nature of such enterprises. In the case of agriculture, for example, Marx calls attention to the <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sophistry" title="wikt:sophistry">sophistical</a> nature of the arguments used to justify the system of concentrated ownership of land:
</p>
<dl><dd>As for large landed property, its defenders have always sophistically identified the economic advantages offered by large-scale agriculture with large-scale landed property, as if it were not precisely as a result of the abolition of property that this advantage, for one thing, received its greatest possible extension, and, for another, only then would be of social benefit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marx1844_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-marx1844-19">[19]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>Instead of concentrated private ownership of land, Marx recommends that economies of scale should instead be realized by <a href="/wiki/Cooperative" title="Cooperative">associations</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>Association, applied to land, shares the economic advantage of large-scale landed property, and first brings to realization the original tendency inherent in land-division, namely, equality. In the same way association re-establishes, now on a rational basis, no longer mediated by serfdom, overlordship and the silly mysticism of property, the intimate ties of man with the earth, for the earth ceases to be an object of huckstering, and through free labor and free enjoyment becomes once more a true personal property of man.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marx1844_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-marx1844-19">[19]</a></sup></dd></dl>
