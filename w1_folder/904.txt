<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Physisorption</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Physisorption</b>, also called <b>physical adsorption</b>, is a process in which the electronic structure of the atom or molecule is barely perturbed upon <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ConceptsinSurfacePhysics_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ConceptsinSurfacePhysics-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction">Introduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physisorption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Introduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The fundamental interacting force of physisorption is caused by <a href="/wiki/Van_der_Waals_force" title="Van der Waals force">van der Waals force</a>. Even though the interaction energy is very weak (~10–100 meV), physisorption plays an important role in nature.  For instance, the van der Waals attraction between surfaces and foot-hairs of <a href="/wiki/Gecko" title="Gecko">geckos</a> provides the remarkable ability to climb up vertical walls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  Van der Waals forces originate from the interactions between induced, permanent or transient electric dipoles.
</p><p>In comparison with <a href="/wiki/Chemisorption" title="Chemisorption">chemisorption</a>, in which the electronic structure of bonding atoms or molecules is changed and covalent or ionic bonds form, physisorption, generally speaking, can only be observed in the environment of low temperature (thermal energy at room temperature ~26 meV) and the absence of the relatively strong chemisorptions.  In practice, the categorisation of a particular adsorption as physisorption or chemisorption depends principally on the <a href="/wiki/Binding_energy" title="Binding energy">binding energy</a> of the adsorbate to the substrate.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modeling_by_image_charge">Modeling by image charge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physisorption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Modeling by image charge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physisorption_potential">Physisorption potential</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physisorption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Physisorption potential">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Physisorption_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="960" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Physisorption_2.jpg/400px-Physisorption_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Physisorption_2.jpg/600px-Physisorption_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Physisorption_2.jpg/800px-Physisorption_2.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Physisorption_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fig. 2. Calculated physisorption potential energy for He adsorbed on various <a href="/wiki/Jellium" title="Jellium">jellium</a> metal surfaces. Note that the weak van der Waals attraction forms shallow wells with energy about few meV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kohn_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kohn-5">[5]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Even though the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Van_der_Waals_interaction" title="Van der Waals interaction">van der Waals interaction</a> is attractive, as the adsorbed atom moves closer to the surface the wavefunction of electron starts to overlap with that of the surface atoms.  Further the energy of the system will increase due to the orthogonality of wavefunctions of the approaching atom and surface atoms.
</p><p>This <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pauli_exclusion" title="Pauli exclusion">Pauli exclusion</a> and repulsion are particularly strong for atoms with closed valence shells that dominate the surface interaction.  As a result, the minimum energy of physisorption must be found by the balance between the long-range van der Waals attraction and short-range <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pauli_repulsion" title="Pauli repulsion">Pauli repulsion</a>.  For instance, by separating the total interaction of physisorption into two contributions- a short-range term depicted by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hartree%E2%80%93Fock" title="Hartree–Fock">Hartree–Fock</a> theory and a long-range van der Waals attraction, the equilibrium position of physisorption for rare gases adsorbed on <a href="/wiki/Jellium" title="Jellium">jellium</a> substrate can be determined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kohn_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kohn-5">[5]</a></sup>  Fig. 2 shows the physisorption potential energy of He adsorbed on Ag, Cu, and Au substrates which are described by the <a href="/wiki/Jellium" title="Jellium">jellium</a> model with different densities of smear-out background positive charges.  It can be found that the weak van der Waals interaction leads to shallow attractive energy wells (&lt;10 meV).  One of the experimental methods for exploring physisorption potential energy is the scattering process, for instance, inert gas atoms scattered from metal surfaces.  Certain specific features of the interaction potential between scattered atoms and surface can be extracted by analyzing the experimentally determined angular distribution and cross sections of the scattered particles.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_with_chemisorption">Comparison with chemisorption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Physisorption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Comparison with chemisorption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>Physisorption is a general phenomenon and occurs in any solid/fluid or solid/gas system. <a href="/wiki/Chemisorption" title="Chemisorption">Chemisorption</a> is characterized by chemical specificity.</li>
<li>In physisorption, perturbation of the electronic states of adsorbent and adsorbate is minimal.  For chemisorption, changes in the electronic states may be detectable by suitable physical means.</li>
<li>Typical binding energy of physisorption is about 10–100 meV.  Chemisorption usually forms bonding with energy of 1–10 eV.</li>
<li>The elementary step in physisorption from a gas phase does not involve an activation energy.  Chemisorption often involves an activation energy.</li>
<li>For physisorption, under appropriate conditions, gas phase molecules can form multilayer adsorption.  In chemisorption, molecules are adsorbed on the surface by valence bonds and only form monolayer adsorption.</li></ul>
