<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Amorphous carbonia</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Amorphous carbonia</b>, also called <b>a-carbonia</b> or <b>a-CO<sub>2</sub></b>, is an exotic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amorphous" title="Amorphous">amorphous</a> solid form of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> that is analogous to amorphous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica_glass" title="Silica glass">silica glass</a>. It was first made in the laboratory in 2006 by subjecting <a href="/wiki/Dry_ice" title="Dry ice">dry ice</a> to high pressures (40-48 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigapascal" title="Gigapascal">gigapascal</a>, or 400,000 to 480,000 <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmospheres</a>), in a <a href="/wiki/Diamond_anvil_cell" title="Diamond anvil cell">diamond anvil cell</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-google-1">[1]</a></sup> Amorphous carbonia is not stable at ordinary pressures—it quickly reverts to normal CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>While normally carbon dioxide forms <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_crystal" title="Molecular crystal">molecular crystals</a>, where individual molecules are bound by <a href="/wiki/Van_der_Waals_force" title="Van der Waals force">Van der Waals forces</a>, in amorphous carbonia a <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">covalently bound</a> three-dimensional network of atoms is formed, in a structure analogous to <a href="/wiki/Silicon_dioxide" title="Silicon dioxide">silicon dioxide</a> or <a href="/wiki/Germanium_dioxide" title="Germanium dioxide">germanium dioxide</a> glass.
</p><p>Mixtures of a-carbonia and a-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">silica</a> may be a prospective very hard and stiff glass material stable at room temperature. Such glass may serve as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protective_coating" title="Protective coating">protective coatings</a>, e.g. in <a href="/wiki/Microelectronics" title="Microelectronics">microelectronics</a>.
</p><p>The discovery has implications for <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">astrophysics</a>, as interiors of <a href="/wiki/Gas_giant" title="Gas giant">massive planets</a> may contain amorphous solid carbon dioxide.
</p>
