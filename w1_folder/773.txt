<p>In <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, a <b>service</b> is a transaction in which no physical goods are transferred from the seller to the buyer. The benefits of such a service are held to be demonstrated by the buyer's willingness to make the exchange. Public services are those that society (nation state, fiscal union, region) as a whole pays for. Using <a href="/wiki/Resource" title="Resource">resources</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skill" title="Skill">skill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ingenuity" title="Ingenuity">ingenuity</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Experience" title="Experience">experience</a>, service providers benefit service consumers. Service is intangible in nature.
</p>

<h2><span id="Three_I.27s"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Three_I's">Three I's</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Service_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Three I's">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Services can be described in terms of I's.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intangibility">Intangibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Service_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Intangibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Services are by definition intangible. They are not manufactured, transported or stocked.
</p><p>Services cannot be stored for a future use. They are produced and consumed simultaneously.
</p><p>Services are perishable in two regards:
</p>
<ul><li>Service-relevant resources, processes and systems are assigned for service delivery during a specific period in time. If the service consumer does not request and consume the service during this period, the related resources may go unused. From the perspective of the service provider, this is a lost <a href="/wiki/Business_opportunity" title="Business opportunity">business opportunity</a> if no other use for those resources is available. Examples: A hairdresser serves another client. An empty seat on an airplane cannot be filled after departure.</li>
<li>When the service has been completely rendered to the consumer, this particular service irreversibly vanishes. Example: a passenger has been transported to the destination.</li></ul>
<p>The service provider must deliver the service at the time of service consumption. The service is not manifested in a physical object that is independent of the provider. The service consumer is also inseparable from service delivery. Examples: The service consumer must sit in the hairdresser's chair, or in the airplane seat. Correspondingly, the hairdresser or the pilot must be in the shop or plane, respectively, to deliver the service.
</p>
<h3><span id="Inconsistency_.28variability.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Inconsistency_(variability)">Inconsistency (variability)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Service_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Inconsistency (variability)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Each service is unique. It can never be exactly repeated as the time, location, circumstances, conditions, current configurations and/or as signed resources are different for the next delivery, even if the same service consumer requests the same service. Many services are regarded as heterogeneous and are typically modified for each service consumer or each service contextual . Example: The taxi service which transports the service consumer from home to work is different from the taxi service which transports the same service consumer from work to home – another point in time, the other direction, possibly another route, probably another taxi driver and cab. Another and more common term for this is heterogeneity.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Involvement">Involvement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Service_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Involvement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Both service provider and service consumer participate in the service providers
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Service_quality">Service quality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Service_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Service quality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mass_generation" title="Mass generation">Mass generation</a> and delivery of services must be mastered for a service provider to expand. This can be seen as a problem of <a href="/wiki/Service_quality" title="Service quality">service quality</a>. Both inputs and outputs to the processes involved providing services are highly variable, as are the relationships between these processes, making it difficult to maintain consistent service quality. Many services involve variable human activity, rather than a precisely determined process; exceptions include <a href="/wiki/Public_utility" title="Public utility">utilities</a>. The human factor is often the key success factor in service provision. Demand can vary by <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">season</a>, <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">time</a> of day, <a href="/wiki/Business_cycle" title="Business cycle">business cycle</a>, etc. Consistency is necessary to create enduring business relationships.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Specification">Specification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Service_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Specification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Any service can be clearly and completely, consistently and concisely specified by means of standard attributes that conform to the <a href="/wiki/MECE_principle" title="MECE principle">MECE principle</a> (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive).
</p>
<ul><li>Service consumer benefits – (set of) benefits that are triggerable, consumable and effectively utilizable for any authorized service consumer and that are rendered upon request. These benefits must be described in terms that are meaningful to consumers.</li>
<li>Service-specific functional parameters – parameters that are essential to the respective service and that describe the important dimension(s) of the <a href="/wiki/Servicescape" title="Servicescape">servicescape</a>, the service output or the service outcome, e.g. whether the passenger sits in an aisle or window seat.</li>
<li>Service delivery point – the physical location and/or logical interface where the benefits of the service are rendered to the consumer. At this point the service delivery preparation can be assessed and delivery can be monitored and controlled.</li>
<li>Service consumer count – the number of consumers that are enabled to consume a service.</li>
<li>Service delivery readiness time – the moments when the service is available and all the specified service elements are available at the delivery point</li>
<li>Service consumer support times – the moments when the support team ("service desk") is available. The service desk is the Single Point of Contact (SPoC) for service inquiries. At those times, the service desk can be reached via commonly available communication methods (phone, web, etc.)</li>
<li>Service consumer support language – the language(s) spoken by the service desk.</li>
<li>Service fulfillment target – the provider's promise to deliver the service, expressed as the ratio of the count of successful service deliveries to the count of service requests by a single consumer or consumer group over some time period.</li>
<li>Service impairment duration – the maximum allowable interval between the first occurrence of a service impairment and the full resumption and completion of the service delivery.</li>
<li>Service delivery duration – the maximum allowable period for effectively rendering all service benefits to the consumer.</li>
<li>Service delivery unit – the scope/number of action(s) that constitute a delivered service.Serves as the reference object for the Service Delivering Price, for all service costs as well as for charging and billing.</li>
<li>Service delivery price – the amount of money the customer pays to receive a service. Typically, the price includes a service access price that qualifies the consumer to request the service and a service consumption price for each delivered service.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Delivery">Delivery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Service_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Delivery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:StarbucksVaughanMills.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1325" data-file-width="2000" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/StarbucksVaughanMills.JPG/220px-StarbucksVaughanMills.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/StarbucksVaughanMills.JPG/330px-StarbucksVaughanMills.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/StarbucksVaughanMills.JPG/440px-StarbucksVaughanMills.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:StarbucksVaughanMills.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Coffee house - a type of service delivery.</div></div></div>
<p>The delivery of a service typically involves six factors:
</p>
<ul><li>Service provider (workers and managers)</li>
<li>Equipment used to provide the service (e.g. vehicles, cash registers, technical systems, computer systems)</li>
<li>Physical facilities (e.g. buildings, parking, waiting rooms)</li>
<li>Service consumer</li>
<li>Other customers at the service delivery location</li>
<li>Customer contact</li></ul>
<p>The service encounter is defined as all activities involved in the service delivery process. Some service managers use the term "moment of truth" to indicate that point in a service encounter where interactions are most intense.
</p><p>Many <a href="/wiki/List_of_business_theorists" title="List of business theorists">business theorists</a> view service provision as a performance or act (sometimes humorously referred to as <i>dramalurgy</i>, perhaps in reference to <a href="/wiki/Dramaturgy" title="Dramaturgy">dramaturgy</a>). The location of the service delivery is referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Stage_(theatre)" title="Stage (theatre)">stage</a> and the objects that facilitate the service process are called <a href="/wiki/Theatrical_property" title="Theatrical property">props</a>. A script is a sequence of <a href="/wiki/Behavior" title="Behavior">behaviors</a> followed by those involved, including the client(s). Some service <a href="/wiki/Drama" title="Drama">dramas</a> are tightly scripted, others are more <a href="/wiki/Ad_libitum" title="Ad libitum">ad lib</a>. Role congruence occurs when each <a href="/wiki/Actor" title="Actor">actor</a> follows a script that harmonizes with the <a href="/wiki/Role" title="Role">roles</a> played by the other actors.
</p><p>In some service industries, especially health care, dispute resolution and social services, a popular concept is the idea of the caseload, which refers to the total number of patients, clients, litigants, or claimants for which a given employee is responsible. Employees must balance the needs of each individual case against the needs of all other current cases as well as their own needs.
</p><p>Under <a href="/wiki/English_law" title="English law">English law</a>, if a service provider is induced to deliver services to a <a href="/wiki/Dishonesty" title="Dishonesty">dishonest</a> client by a deception, this is an offence under the <a href="/wiki/Theft_Act_1978" title="Theft Act 1978">Theft Act 1978</a>.
</p><p>Lovelock used the number of delivery sites (whether single or multiple) and the method of delivery to classify services in a 2 x 3 matrix. Then implications are that the convenience of receiving the service is the lowest when the customer has to come to the service and must use a single or specific outlet. Convenience increases (to a point) as the number of service points increase.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Service-commodity_goods_continuum">Service-commodity goods continuum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Service_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Service-commodity goods continuum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Service-goods_continuum.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="220" height="400" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9c/Service-goods_continuum.png" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Service-goods_continuum.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Service-Commodity Goods continuum</div></div></div>
<p>The distinction between a good and a service remains disputed. The perspective in the late-eighteenth and early-nineteenth centuries focused on creation and possession of wealth. Classical economists contended that goods were objects of value over which ownership rights could be established and exchanged. Ownership implied tangible possession of an object that had been acquired through purchase, barter or gift from the producer or previous owner and was legally identifiable as the property of the current owner.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a>’s famous book, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wealth_of_Nations" title="The Wealth of Nations">The Wealth of Nations</a></i>, published in <a href="/wiki/1776" title="1776">1776</a>, distinguished between the outputs of what he termed "productive" and "unproductive" labor. The former, he stated, produced goods that could be stored after production and subsequently exchanged for money or other items of value. The latter, however useful or necessary, created services that perished at the time of production and therefore did not contribute to wealth. Building on this theme, French economist Jean-Baptiste Say argued that production and consumption were inseparable in services, coining the term "immaterial products" to describe them.
</p><p>Most modern business theorists describe a continuum with pure service on one terminal point and pure <a href="/wiki/Commodity" title="Commodity">commodity good</a> on the other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Most <a href="/wiki/Product_(business)" title="Product (business)">products</a> fall between these two extremes. For example, a <a href="/wiki/Restaurant" title="Restaurant">restaurant</a> provides a physical good (the <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a>), but also provides services in the form of ambience, the setting and clearing of the table, etc. And although some utilities actually deliver physical goods — like water utilities that deliver water — utilities are usually treated as services.
</p><p>In a narrower sense, service refers to <a href="/wiki/Quality_(business)" title="Quality (business)">quality</a> of <a href="/wiki/Customer_service" title="Customer service">customer service</a>: the measured appropriateness of assistance and support provided to a customer. This particular usage occurs frequently in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retailing" title="Retailing">retailing</a>.
</p>
