<p>In <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a>, <b>amines</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span></span>, <wbr/><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <span style="font-size:85%;">UK also</span> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/eɪ/: 'a' in 'face'">eɪ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> are <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">compounds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Functional_group" title="Functional group">functional groups</a> that contain a <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">basic</a> <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Lone_pair" title="Lone pair">lone pair</a>. Amines are formally <a href="/wiki/Derivative_(chemistry)" title="Derivative (chemistry)">derivatives</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>, wherein one or more <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> atoms have been replaced by a <a href="/wiki/Substituent" title="Substituent">substituent</a> such as an <a href="/wiki/Alkyl" title="Alkyl">alkyl</a> or <a href="/wiki/Aryl" title="Aryl">aryl</a> group<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> (these may respectively be called alkylamines and arylamines; amines in which both types of substituent are attached to one nitrogen atom may be called alkylarylamines). Important amines include <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biogenic_amine" title="Biogenic amine">biogenic amines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trimethylamine" title="Trimethylamine">trimethylamine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aniline" title="Aniline">aniline</a>; see <a href="/wiki/Category:Amines" title="Category:Amines">Category:Amines</a> for a list of amines. <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">Inorganic</a> derivatives of <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> are also called amines, such as <a href="/wiki/Chloramine" title="Chloramine">chloramine</a> (NClH<sub>2</sub>); see <a href="/wiki/Category:Inorganic_amines" title="Category:Inorganic amines">Category:Inorganic amines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The substituent -NH<sub>2</sub> is called an amino group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OChemSmith3E_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-OChemSmith3E-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Compounds with a nitrogen atom attached to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonyl" title="Carbonyl">carbonyl</a> group, thus having the structure R–CO–NR′R″, are called <a href="/wiki/Amide" title="Amide">amides</a> and have different chemical properties from amines.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_of_amines">Classification of amines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification of amines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Amines can be classified according to the nature and number of substituents on nitrogen.  <b>Aliphatic amines</b> contain only H and alkyl substituents. <b><a href="/wiki/Aromatic_amine" title="Aromatic amine">Aromatic amines</a></b> have the nitrogen atom connected to an <a href="/wiki/Aromaticity" title="Aromaticity">aromatic</a> ring.
</p><p>Amines, alkyl and aryl alike, are organized into three subcategories <a href="#amino_group_connectivity_table"><small>(see table)</small></a> based on the number of carbon atoms adjacent to the nitrogen:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OChemSmith3E_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-OChemSmith3E-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Primary (1°) amines</b>—Primary amines arise when one of three hydrogen atoms in ammonia is replaced by an <a href="/wiki/Alkyl" title="Alkyl">alkyl</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aromatic" title="Aromatic">aromatic</a> group. Important primary alkyl amines include, <a href="/wiki/Methylamine" title="Methylamine">methylamine</a>, most <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a>,  and the <a href="/wiki/Buffering_agent" title="Buffering agent">buffering agent</a> <a href="/wiki/Tris" title="Tris">tris</a>, while primary aromatic amines include <a href="/wiki/Aniline" title="Aniline">aniline</a>.</li>
<li><b>Secondary (2°) amines</b>—Secondary amines have two organic substituents (alkyl, aryl or both) bound to the nitrogen together with one hydrogen. Important representatives include <a href="/wiki/Dimethylamine" title="Dimethylamine">dimethylamine</a>, while an example of an aromatic amine would be <a href="/wiki/Diphenylamine" title="Diphenylamine">diphenylamine</a>.</li>
<li><b>Tertiary (3°) amines</b>—In tertiary amines, nitrogen has three organic substituents. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Trimethylamine" title="Trimethylamine">trimethylamine</a>, which has a distinctively fishy smell, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EDTA" title="EDTA">EDTA</a>.</li></ul>
<p>A fourth subcategory is determined by the connectivity of the substituents attached to the nitrogen:
</p>
<ul><li><b>Cyclic amines</b>—<a href="/wiki/Cyclic_compound" title="Cyclic compound">Cyclic</a> amines are either secondary or tertiary amines. Examples of cyclic amines include the 3-membered ring <a href="/wiki/Aziridine" title="Aziridine">aziridine</a> and the six-membered ring <a href="/wiki/Piperidine" title="Piperidine">piperidine</a>. <i>N</i>-methylpiperidine and <i>N</i>-phenylpiperidine are examples of cyclic tertiary amines.</li></ul>
<p>It is also possible to have four organic substituents on the nitrogen. These species are not amines but are <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_ammonium_cation" title="Quaternary ammonium cation">quaternary ammonium cations</a> and have a charged nitrogen center.  Quaternary ammonium salts exist with many kinds of anions.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming_conventions">Naming conventions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Naming conventions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Amines are named in several ways. Typically, the compound is given the prefix "amino-" or the suffix: "-amine". The prefix "<i>N</i>-" shows substitution on the nitrogen atom. An organic compound with multiple amino groups is called a diamine, triamine, tetraamine and so forth.
</p><p>Systematic names for some common amines:
</p>
<table border="0" style="margin:auto;">
<tbody><tr style="vertical-align:top; text-align:center;">
<td>Lower amines are named with the suffix <i>-amine</i>.<br/>
<p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Methylamine.svg"><img alt="Methylamine.svg" data-file-height="675" data-file-width="790" height="85" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Methylamine.svg/100px-Methylamine.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Methylamine.svg/150px-Methylamine.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Methylamine.svg/200px-Methylamine.svg.png 2x" width="100"/></a><br/>
<b>methylamine</b>
</p>
</td>
<td>Higher amines have the prefix <i>amino</i> as a functional group. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bonding" title="Hydrogen bonding">Hydrogen bonding</a> significantly influences the properties of primary and secondary amines. For example, <a href="/wiki/Methylamine" title="Methylamine">methyl</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethylamine" title="Ethylamine">ethyl</a> amines are gases under standard conditions, whereas the corresponding <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methyl</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethyl</a> alcohols are liquids. Amines possess a characteristic ammonia smell, liquid amines have a distinctive "fishy" smell.
</p><p>The nitrogen atom features a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lone_electron_pair" title="Lone electron pair">lone electron pair</a> that can bind H<sup>+</sup> to form an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ammonium_ion" title="Ammonium ion">ammonium ion</a> R<sub>3</sub>NH<sup>+</sup>. The lone electron pair is represented in this article by a two dots above or next to the N. The water <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">solubility</a> of simple amines is enhanced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bonding" title="Hydrogen bonding">hydrogen bonding</a> involving these lone electron pairs.  Typically salts of ammonium compounds exhibit the following order of solubility in water: primary ammonium (<span class="chemf nowrap">RNH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>) &gt; secondary ammonium (<span class="chemf nowrap">R<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>) &gt; tertiary ammonium (R<sub>3</sub>NH<sup>+</sup>). Small aliphatic amines display significant solubility in many <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvents</a>, whereas those with large substituents are lipophilic. Aromatic amines, such as <a href="/wiki/Aniline" title="Aniline">aniline</a>, have their lone pair electrons <a href="/wiki/Conjugated_system" title="Conjugated system">conjugated</a> into the benzene ring, thus their tendency to engage in hydrogen bonding is diminished. Their boiling points are high and their solubility in water is low.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spectroscopic_identification">Spectroscopic identification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Spectroscopic identification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Typically the presence of an amine functional group is deduced by a combination of techniques, including mass spectrometry as well as NMR and IR spectroscopies.  <sup>1</sup>H NMR signals for amines disappear upon treatment of the sample with D<sub>2</sub>O.  In their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrared_spectrum" title="Infrared spectrum">infrared spectrum</a> <a href="/wiki/Amine#Connectivity" title="Amine">primary amines</a> exhibit two N-H bands, whereas <a href="/wiki/Amine#Connectivity" title="Amine">secondary amines</a> exhibit only one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OChemSmith3E_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-OChemSmith3E-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alkyl_amines">Alkyl amines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Alkyl amines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Alkyl amines characteristically feature tetrahedral nitrogen centers.  C-N-C and C-N-H angles approach the idealized angle of 109°.  C-N distances are slightly shorter than C-C distances.  The energy barrier for the <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_inversion" title="Nitrogen inversion">nitrogen inversion</a> of the stereocenter is about 7 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kcal/mol" title="Kcal/mol">kcal/mol</a> for a trialkylamine. The interconversion has been compared to the inversion of an open umbrella into a strong wind.
</p><p>Amines of the type NHRR′ and NRR′R″ are <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)" title="Chirality (chemistry)">chiral</a>: the nitrogen center bears four substituents counting the lone pair. Because of the low barrier to inversion, amines of the type NHRR′ cannot be obtained in optical purity.  For chiral tertiary amines, NRR′R″ can only be resolved when the R, R′, and R″ groups are constrained in cyclic structures such as N-substituted <a href="/wiki/Aziridine" title="Aziridine">aziridines</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quaternary_ammonium_salt" title="Quaternary ammonium salt">quaternary ammonium salts</a> are resolvable).
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aromatic_amines">Aromatic amines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aromatic amines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In aromatic amines ("anilines"), nitrogen is often nearly planar owing to conjugation of the lone pair with the aryl substituent.  The C-N distance is correspondingly shorter.  In aniline, the C-N distance is the same as the C-C distances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The basicity of amines depends on:
</p>
<ol><li>The electronic properties of the substituents (alkyl groups enhance the basicity, aryl groups diminish it).</li>
<li>The degree of solvation of the protonated amine, which includes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steric_hindrance" title="Steric hindrance">steric hindrance</a> by the groups on nitrogen.</li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electronic_effects">Electronic effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Electronic effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Owing to inductive effects, the basicity of an amine might be expected to increase with the number of alkyl groups on the amine.  Correlations are complicated owing to the effects of solvation which are opposite the trends for inductive effects. Solvation effects also dominate the basicity of aromatic amines (anilines). For anilines, the lone pair of electrons on nitrogen delocalises into the ring, resulting in decreased basicity.  Substituents on the aromatic ring, and their positions relative to the amine group, also affect basicity as seen in the table.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solvation_effects">Solvation effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Solvation effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Solvation significantly affects the basicity of amines.  N-H groups strongly interact with water, especially in ammonium ions. Consequently, the basicity of ammonia is enhanced by 10<sup>11</sup> by solvation. The intrinsic basicity of amines, i.e. the situation where solvation is unimportant, has been evaluated in the gas phase.  In the gas phase, amines exhibit the basicities predicted from the electron-releasing effects of the organic substituents.  Thus tertiary amines are more basic than secondary amines, which are more basic than primary amines, and finally ammonia is least basic.  The order of pK<sub>b</sub>'s (basicities in water) does not follow this order.  Similarly aniline is more basic than ammonia in the gas phase, but ten thousand times less so in aqueous solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>In aprotic polar solvents such as <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfoxide" title="Dimethyl sulfoxide">DMSO</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dimethylformamide" title="Dimethylformamide">DMF</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Acetonitrile" title="Acetonitrile">acetonitrile</a>  the energy of solvation is not as high as in protic polar solvents like water and methanol.  For this reason, the basicity of amines in these aprotic solvents is almost solely governed by the electronic effects.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis">Synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alkylation">Alkylation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Alkylation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The most industrially significant amines are prepared from ammonia by <a href="/wiki/Alkylation" title="Alkylation">alkylation</a> with alcohols:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>ROH + NH<sub>3</sub> → RNH<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Unlike the reaction of amines with alkyl halides, the industrial method is <a href="/wiki/Green_chemistry" title="Green chemistry">green</a> insofar that the coproduct is water.  The reaction of amines and ammonia with alkyl halides is used for synthesis in the laboratory:
</p>
<dl><dd>RX + 2 R′NH<sub>2</sub> → RR′NH + [RR′NH<sub>2</sub>]X</dd></dl>
<p>Such reactions, which are most useful for alkyl iodides and bromides, are rarely employed because the degree of alkylation is difficult to control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-5">[5]</a></sup> Selectivity can be improved via the <a href="/wiki/Del%C3%A9pine_reaction" title="Delépine reaction">Delépine reaction</a>, although this is rarely employed on an industrial scale.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reductive_routes">Reductive routes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Reductive routes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Via the process of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrile" title="Nitrile">nitriles</a> are reduced to amines using hydrogen in the presence of a nickel catalyst. Reactions are sensitive to acidic or alkaline conditions, which can cause hydrolysis of the –CN group. LiAlH<sub>4</sub> is more commonly employed for the reduction of nitriles on the laboratory scale. Similarly, LiAlH<sub>4</sub> reduces <a href="/wiki/Amide" title="Amide">amides</a> to amines. Many amines are produced from aldehydes and ketones via <a href="/wiki/Reductive_amination" title="Reductive amination">reductive amination</a>, which can either proceed catalytically or stoichiometrically.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Aniline" title="Aniline">Aniline</a> (C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>NH<sub>2</sub>) and its derivatives are prepared by reduction of the nitroaromatics. In industry, hydrogen is the preferred reductant, whereas, in the laboratory, tin and iron are often employed.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Alkylation.2C_acylation.2C_and_sulfonation"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Alkylation,_acylation,_and_sulfonation">Alkylation, acylation, and sulfonation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Alkylation, acylation, and sulfonation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Aside from their basicity, the dominant reactivity of amines is their <a href="/wiki/Nucleophilic_acyl_substitution" title="Nucleophilic acyl substitution">nucleophilicity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Most primary amines are good <a href="/wiki/Ligand" title="Ligand">ligands</a> for metal ions to give <a href="/wiki/Coordination_complex" title="Coordination complex">coordination complexes</a>. Amines are alkylated by alkyl halides. <a href="/wiki/Acyl_chloride" title="Acyl chloride">Acyl chlorides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Organic_acid_anhydride" title="Organic acid anhydride">acid anhydrides</a> react with primary and secondary amines to form <a href="/wiki/Amide" title="Amide">amides</a> (the "<a href="/wiki/Schotten%E2%80%93Baumann_reaction" title="Schotten–Baumann reaction">Schotten–Baumann reaction</a>").
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Amide_formation_from_amine.svg" title="Amide formation"><img alt="Amide formation" data-file-height="158" data-file-width="512" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Amide_formation_from_amine.svg/512px-Amide_formation_from_amine.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Amide_formation_from_amine.svg/768px-Amide_formation_from_amine.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Amide_formation_from_amine.svg/1024px-Amide_formation_from_amine.svg.png 2x" width="512"/></a></div></div>
<p>Similarly, with sulfonyl chlorides, one obtains <a href="/wiki/Sulfonamide" title="Sulfonamide">sulfonamides</a>. This transformation, known as the <a href="/wiki/Hinsberg_reaction" title="Hinsberg reaction">Hinsberg reaction</a>, is a chemical test for the presence of amines.
</p><p>Because amines are basic, they neutralize <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a> to form the corresponding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ammonium_salt" title="Ammonium salt">ammonium salts</a> R<sub>3</sub>NH<sup>+</sup>. When formed from carboxylic acids and primary and secondary amines, these salts thermally dehydrate to form the corresponding <a href="/wiki/Amide" title="Amide">amides</a>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conversion_to_imines">Conversion to imines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Conversion to imines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alkylimino-de-oxo-bisubstitution" title="Alkylimino-de-oxo-bisubstitution">Imine formation</a> is an important reaction. Primary amines react with <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">ketones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aldehyde" title="Aldehyde">aldehydes</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Imine" title="Imine">imines</a>. In the case of <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">formaldehyde</a> (R′ = H), these products typically exist as cyclic <a href="/wiki/Trimer_(chemistry)" title="Trimer (chemistry)">trimers</a>.
</p>
<dl><dd>RNH<sub>2</sub> + R′<sub>2</sub>C=O → R′<sub>2</sub>C=NR + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Reduction of these imines gives secondary amines:
</p>
<dl><dd>R′<sub>2</sub>C=NR + H<sub>2</sub> → R′<sub>2</sub>CH–NHR</dd></dl>
<p>Similarly, secondary amines react with ketones and aldehydes to form <a href="/wiki/Enamine" title="Enamine">enamines</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>R<sub>2</sub>NH + R′(R″CH<sub>2</sub>)C=O → R″CH=C(NR<sub>2</sub>)R′ + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_activity">Biological activity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Biological activity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Amines are ubiquitous in biology. The breakdown of <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a> releases amines, famously in the case of decaying fish which smell of <a href="/wiki/Trimethylamine" title="Trimethylamine">trimethylamine</a>. Many <a href="/wiki/Neurotransmitter" title="Neurotransmitter">neurotransmitters</a> are amines, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epinephrine" title="Epinephrine">epinephrine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norepinephrine" title="Norepinephrine">norepinephrine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dopamine" title="Dopamine">dopamine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serotonin" title="Serotonin">serotonin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Histamine" title="Histamine">histamine</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protonated" title="Protonated">Protonated</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amino_group" title="Amino group">amino groups</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">–NH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>) are the most common positively charged moieties in <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>, specifically in the amino acid <a href="/wiki/Lysine" title="Lysine">lysine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The anionic polymer <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> is typically bound to various amine-rich proteins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Additionally, the terminal charged primary ammonium on lysine forms <a href="/wiki/Salt_bridge_(protein_and_supramolecular)" title="Salt bridge (protein and supramolecular)">salt bridges</a> with <a href="/wiki/Carboxylate" title="Carboxylate">carboxylate</a> groups of other amino acids in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polypeptide" title="Polypeptide">polypeptides</a>, which is one of the primary influences on the three-dimensional structures of proteins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Application_of_amines">Application of amines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Application of amines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dyes">Dyes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Dyes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Primary aromatic amines are used as a starting material for the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Azo_dye" title="Azo dye">azo dyes</a>. It reacts with nitrous acid to form diazonium salt, which can undergo coupling reaction to form an azo compound. As azo-compounds are highly coloured, they are widely used in dyeing industries, such as:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Methyl_orange" title="Methyl orange">Methyl orange</a></li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Direct_brown_138&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Direct brown 138 (page does not exist)">Direct brown 138</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunset_yellow" title="Sunset yellow">Sunset yellow</a> FCF</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ponceau_4R" title="Ponceau 4R">Ponceau</a></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Drugs">Drugs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Drugs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many drugs are designed to mimic or to interfere with the action of natural amine <a href="/wiki/Neurotransmitter" title="Neurotransmitter">neurotransmitters</a>, exemplified by the amine drugs:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chlorpheniramine" title="Chlorpheniramine">Chlorpheniramine</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Antihistamine" title="Antihistamine">antihistamine</a> that helps to relieve allergic disorders due to cold, hay fever, itchy skin, insect bites and stings.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chlorpromazine" title="Chlorpromazine">Chlorpromazine</a> is a tranquilizer that sedates without inducing sleep. It is used to relieve anxiety, excitement, restlessness or even mental disorder.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ephedrine" title="Ephedrine">Ephedrine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phenylephrine" title="Phenylephrine">phenylephrine</a>, as amine hydrochlorides, are used as decongestants.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Amphetamine" title="Amphetamine">Amphetamine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methamphetamine" title="Methamphetamine">methamphetamine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Methcathinone" title="Methcathinone">methcathinone</a> are psychostimulant amines that are listed as controlled substances by the US <a href="/wiki/Drug_Enforcement_Administration" title="Drug Enforcement Administration">DEA</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Amitriptyline" title="Amitriptyline">Amitriptyline</a>, <a href="/wiki/Imipramine" title="Imipramine">imipramine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lofepramine" title="Lofepramine">lofepramine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Clomipramine" title="Clomipramine">clomipramine</a> are <a href="/wiki/Tricyclic_antidepressant" title="Tricyclic antidepressant">tricyclic antidepressants</a> and tertiary amines.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nortriptyline" title="Nortriptyline">Nortriptyline</a>, <a href="/wiki/Desipramine" title="Desipramine">desipramine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Amoxapine" title="Amoxapine">amoxapine</a> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tricyclic_antidepressants" title="Tricyclic antidepressants">tricyclic antidepressants</a> and secondary amines. (The tricyclics are grouped by the nature of the final amine group on the side chain.)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Substituted_tryptamine" title="Substituted tryptamine">Substituted tryptamines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Substituted_phenethylamine" title="Substituted phenethylamine">phenethylamines</a> are key basic structures for a large variety of <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_drug" title="Psychedelic drug">psychedelic drugs</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Opiate" title="Opiate">Opiate</a> <a href="/wiki/Analgesic" title="Analgesic">analgesics</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Morphine" title="Morphine">morphine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Codeine" title="Codeine">codeine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Heroin" title="Heroin">heroin</a> are <a href="/wiki/Tertiary_(chemistry)" title="Tertiary (chemistry)">tertiary</a> amines.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_treatment">Gas treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Gas treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Aqueous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monoethanolamine" title="Monoethanolamine">monoethanolamine</a> (MEA), diglycolamine (DGA), <a href="/wiki/Diethanolamine" title="Diethanolamine">diethanolamine</a> (DEA), diisopropanolamine (DIPA) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N-methyl-diethanolamine" title="N-methyl-diethanolamine">methyldiethanolamine</a> (MDEA) are widely used industrially for removing <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a> (H<sub>2</sub>S) from natural gas and refinery process streams. They may also be used to remove CO<sub>2</sub> from combustion gases and <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">flue gases</a> and may have potential for abatement of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>. Related processes are known as <a href="/wiki/Amine_gas_treating" title="Amine gas treating">sweetening</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Low molecular weight simple amines, such as <a href="/wiki/Ethylamine" title="Ethylamine">ethylamine</a>, are only weakly toxic with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LD50" title="LD50">LD50</a> between 100 and 1000 mg/kg. They are skin irritants, especially as some are easily absorbed through the skin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-5">[5]</a></sup>  Amines are a broad class of compounds, and more complex members of the class can be extremely bioactive, for example <a href="/wiki/Strychnine" title="Strychnine">strychnine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heroin" title="Heroin">heroin</a>.
</p>
