<p><b>Tuscany</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ʌ/: 'u' in 'cut'">ʌ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling"><span style="font-size:90%">TUSK</span>-ə-nee</i></a>; <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <i lang="it">Toscana</i>, <small>pronounced </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Italian" title="Help:IPA/Italian">[toˈskaːna]</a></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_Italy" title="Regions of Italy">region</a> in <a href="/wiki/Central_Italy" title="Central Italy">central Italy</a> with an area of about 23,000 square kilometres (8,900 square miles) and a population of about 3.8 million inhabitants (2013). The regional capital is <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a> (<i>Firenze</i>).
</p><p>Tuscany is known for its landscapes, traditions, history, artistic legacy, and its influence on <a href="/wiki/High_culture" title="High culture">high culture</a>. It is regarded as the birthplace of the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance" title="Italian Renaissance">Italian Renaissance</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  and has been home to many figures influential in the history of art and science, and contains well-known museums such as the <a href="/wiki/Uffizi" title="Uffizi">Uffizi</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pitti_Palace" title="Pitti Palace">Pitti Palace</a>. Tuscany produces <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wines</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Chianti" title="Chianti">Chianti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vino_Nobile_di_Montepulciano" title="Vino Nobile di Montepulciano">Vino Nobile di Montepulciano</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morellino_di_Scansano" title="Morellino di Scansano">Morellino di Scansano</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brunello_di_Montalcino" title="Brunello di Montalcino">Brunello di Montalcino</a>. Having a strong linguistic and cultural identity, it is sometimes considered "a nation within a nation".
</p><p>Tuscany is traditionally a popular destination in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, and the main tourist destinations by number of tourist arrivals in 2014 were <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">Pisa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montecatini_Terme" title="Montecatini Terme">Montecatini Terme</a>, <a href="/wiki/Castiglione_della_Pescaia" title="Castiglione della Pescaia">Castiglione della Pescaia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grosseto" title="Grosseto">Grosseto</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ilgiunco.net_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ilgiunco.net-5">[5]</a></sup> The village of <a href="/wiki/Castiglione_della_Pescaia" title="Castiglione della Pescaia">Castiglione della Pescaia</a> is the most visited seaside destination in the region,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ilgiunco.net_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-ilgiunco.net-5">[5]</a></sup> with seaside tourism accounting for approximately 40% of tourist arrivals in Tuscany. Additionally, <a href="/wiki/Siena" title="Siena">Siena</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lucca" title="Lucca">Lucca</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Chianti" title="Chianti">Chianti</a> region, <a href="/wiki/Versilia" title="Versilia">Versilia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Val_d%27Orcia" title="Val d'Orcia">Val d'Orcia</a> are also internationally renowned and particularly popular spots among travellers.
</p><p>Seven Tuscan localities have been designated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Sites</a>: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historic_centre_of_Florence" title="Historic centre of Florence">historic centre of Florence</a> (1982); the <a href="/wiki/Piazza_dei_Miracoli" title="Piazza dei Miracoli">Cathedral square of Pisa</a> (1987); the historical centre of <a href="/wiki/San_Gimignano" title="San Gimignano">San Gimignano</a> (1990); the historical centre of <a href="/wiki/Siena" title="Siena">Siena</a> (1995); the historical centre of <a href="/wiki/Pienza" title="Pienza">Pienza</a> (1996); the <a href="/wiki/Val_d%27Orcia" title="Val d'Orcia">Val d'Orcia</a> (2004), and the <a href="/wiki/Medici_villas" title="Medici villas">Medici Villas and Gardens</a> (2013). Tuscany has over 120 protected <a href="/wiki/Nature_reserve" title="Nature reserve">nature reserves</a>, making Tuscany and its capital Florence popular tourist destinations that attract millions of tourists every year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 2012, the city of Florence was the world's 89th most visited city, with over 1.834 million arrivals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuscany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Toscana_Thornthwaite.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="402" data-file-width="378" height="213" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Toscana_Thornthwaite.png/200px-Toscana_Thornthwaite.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Toscana_Thornthwaite.png/300px-Toscana_Thornthwaite.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/19/Toscana_Thornthwaite.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Toscana_Thornthwaite.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Climate#Thornthwaite" title="Climate">Thornthwaite climate classification</a> of Tuscany  <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r853265166">.mw-parser-output div.columns-2 div.column{float:left;width:50%;min-width:300px}.mw-parser-output div.columns-3 div.column{float:left;width:33.3%;min-width:200px}.mw-parser-output div.columns-4 div.column{float:left;width:25%;min-width:150px}.mw-parser-output div.columns-5 div.column{float:left;width:20%;min-width:120px}</style><table class="multicol" role="presentation" style=";border-spacing:0;background:transparent;"> <tbody><tr style="vertical-align:top;"> <td style="width:15.0em;;;"> <span style="margin:0; <p>Roughly triangular in shape, Tuscany borders the regions of <a href="/wiki/Liguria" title="Liguria">Liguria</a> to the northwest, <a href="/wiki/Emilia-Romagna" title="Emilia-Romagna">Emilia-Romagna</a> to the north, <a href="/wiki/Marche" title="Marche">Marche</a> to the northeast, <a href="/wiki/Umbria" title="Umbria">Umbria</a> to the east and <a href="/wiki/Lazio" title="Lazio">Lazio</a> to the southeast. The <a href="/wiki/Comune" title="Comune">comune</a> (municipality) of <a href="/wiki/Badia_Tedalda" title="Badia Tedalda">Badia Tedalda</a>, in the Tuscan <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Arezzo" title="Province of Arezzo">Province of Arezzo</a>, has an exclave named Ca' Raffaello within Marche.
</p><p>Tuscany has a western coastline on the <a href="/wiki/Ligurian_Sea" title="Ligurian Sea">Ligurian Sea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tyrrhenian_Sea" title="Tyrrhenian Sea">Tyrrhenian Sea</a>, among which is the <a href="/wiki/Tuscan_Archipelago" title="Tuscan Archipelago">Tuscan Archipelago</a>, of which the largest island is <a href="/wiki/Elba" title="Elba">Elba</a>. Tuscany has an area of approximately 22,993 square kilometres (8,878 sq mi). Surrounded and crossed by major mountain chains, and with few (but fertile) plains, the region has a relief that is dominated by hilly country used for <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>. Hills make up nearly two-thirds (66.5%) of the region's total area, covering 15,292 square kilometres (5,904 sq mi), and mountains (of which the highest are the <a href="/wiki/Apennine_Mountains" title="Apennine Mountains">Apennines</a>), a further 25%, or 5,770 square kilometres (2,230 sq mi). Plains occupy 8.4% of the total area—1,930 square kilometres (750 sq mi)—mostly around the valley of the <a href="/wiki/Arno" title="Arno">Arno</a>. Many of Tuscany's largest cities lie on the banks of the Arno, including the capital <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Empoli" title="Empoli">Empoli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">Pisa</a>.
</p><p>The climate is fairly mild in the coastal areas, and is harsher and rainy in the interior, with considerable fluctuations in temperature between winter and summer,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ite1geo_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ite1geo-8">[8]</a></sup> giving the region a soil-building active freeze-thaw cycle, in part accounting for the region's once having served as a key breadbasket of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">ancient Rome</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuscany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Tuscany" title="History of Tuscany">History of Tuscany</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Appennini_and_Villanovan_cultures">Appennini and Villanovan cultures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuscany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Appennini and Villanovan cultures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Apennine_culture" title="Apennine culture">Apennine culture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Villanovan_culture" title="Villanovan culture">Villanovan culture</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ossuari_biconici_villanoviani_da_necropoli_di_casal_di_lanza_(vulci),_IX-VIII_sec_ac._01.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2536" data-file-width="3288" height="185" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Ossuari_biconici_villanoviani_da_necropoli_di_casal_di_lanza_%28vulci%29%2C_IX-VIII_sec_ac._01.JPG/240px-Ossuari_biconici_villanoviani_da_necropoli_di_casal_di_lanza_%28vulci%29%2C_IX-VIII_sec_ac._01.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Ossuari_biconici_villanoviani_da_necropoli_di_casal_di_lanza_%28vulci%29%2C_IX-VIII_sec_ac._01.JPG/360px-Ossuari_biconici_villanoviani_da_necropoli_di_casal_di_lanza_%28vulci%29%2C_IX-VIII_sec_ac._01.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Ossuari_biconici_villanoviani_da_necropoli_di_casal_di_lanza_%28vulci%29%2C_IX-VIII_sec_ac._01.JPG/480px-Ossuari_biconici_villanoviani_da_necropoli_di_casal_di_lanza_%28vulci%29%2C_IX-VIII_sec_ac._01.JPG 2x" width="240"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ossuari_biconici_villanoviani_da_necropoli_di_casal_di_lanza_(vulci),_IX-VIII_sec_ac._01.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cinerary urns of the <a href="/wiki/Villanovan_culture" title="Villanovan culture">Villanovan culture</a></div></div></div>
<p>The pre-Etruscan history of the area in the late <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Ages</a> parallels that of the early <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Greeks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barker_5_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barker_5-10">[10]</a></sup> The Tuscan area was inhabited by peoples of the so-called <a href="/wiki/Apennine_culture" title="Apennine culture">Apennine culture</a> in the late <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_millennium_BC" title="Second millennium BC">second millennium BC</a> (roughly 1350–1150 BC) who had trading relationships with the <a href="/wiki/Minoan_civilization" title="Minoan civilization">Minoan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mycenaean_Greece" title="Mycenaean Greece">Mycenaean</a> civilizations in the <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Sea" title="Aegean Sea">Aegean Sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barker_5_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barker_5-10">[10]</a></sup> Following this, the <a href="/wiki/Villanovan_culture" title="Villanovan culture">Villanovan culture</a> (1100–700 BC) saw Tuscany, and the rest of Etruria, taken over by <a href="/wiki/Chiefdom" title="Chiefdom">chiefdoms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barker_5_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Barker_5-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/City-state" title="City-state">City-states</a> developed in the late Villanovan (paralleling Greece and the Aegean) before "Orientalization" occurred and the Etruscan civilization rose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barker_5_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Barker_5-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etruscans">Etruscans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuscany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Etruscans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Etruscan_civilization" title="Etruscan civilization">Etruscan civilization</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chimera_d%27arezzo,_fi,_03.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2037" data-file-width="2368" height="206" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Chimera_d%27arezzo%2C_fi%2C_03.JPG/240px-Chimera_d%27arezzo%2C_fi%2C_03.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Chimera_d%27arezzo%2C_fi%2C_03.JPG/360px-Chimera_d%27arezzo%2C_fi%2C_03.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Chimera_d%27arezzo%2C_fi%2C_03.JPG/480px-Chimera_d%27arezzo%2C_fi%2C_03.JPG 2x" width="240"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chimera_d%27arezzo,_fi,_03.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <i><a href="/wiki/Chimera_of_Arezzo" title="Chimera of Arezzo">Chimera of Arezzo</a></i>, Etruscan bronze, 400 BC</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Etruscan_civilization" title="Etruscan civilization">Etruscans</a> (Latin: <i>Tusci</i>) created the first major <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilization</a> in this region, large enough to establish a <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transport</a> infrastructure, to implement <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a> and to produce vibrant art.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_2_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jones_2-11">[11]</a></sup> The Etruscans lived in the area of <a href="/wiki/Etruria" title="Etruria">Etruria</a> well into prehistory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barker_5_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-Barker_5-10">[10]</a></sup> The civilization grew to fill the area between the Arno and <a href="/wiki/Tiber" title="Tiber">Tiber</a> from the eighth century BCE, reaching its peak during the seventh and sixth centuries B.C., finally succumbing to the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a> by the first century BCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barker_1_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barker_1-12">[12]</a></sup> Throughout their existence, they lost territory (in <a href="/wiki/Campania" title="Campania">Campania</a>) to <a href="/wiki/Magna_Graecia" title="Magna Graecia">Magna Graecia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carthage" title="Carthage">Carthage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_2_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jones_2-11">[11]</a></sup> Despite being seen as distinct in its manners and customs by <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">contemporary Greeks</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barker_4_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barker_4-13">[13]</a></sup> the cultures of Greece, and later <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Rome</a>, influenced the civilization to a great extent. One reason for its eventual demise<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barker_1_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barker_1-12">[12]</a></sup> was this increasing absorption by surrounding cultures, including the adoption of the Etruscan upper class by the Romans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_2_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jones_2-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Romans">Romans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuscany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Romans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Soon after absorbing Etruria (to the north, northeast, east and a strip to the south), Rome established the cities of <a href="/wiki/Lucca" title="Lucca">Lucca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">Pisa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siena" title="Siena">Siena</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>, endowed the area with new technologies and development, and ensured peace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_2_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Jones_2-11">[11]</a></sup> These developments included extensions of existing roads, introduction of aqueducts and sewers, and the construction of many buildings, both public and private. However, many of these structures have been destroyed by erosion due to weather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_2_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-Jones_2-11">[11]</a></sup> The Roman civilization in the West of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a> and later <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> collapsed in the fifth century, and the region fell briefly to <a href="/wiki/Barbarian" title="Barbarian">barbarians</a> migrating through the Empire from <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Goths</a> (Eastern - <a href="/wiki/Ostrogoths" title="Ostrogoths">Ostrogoths</a> and Western - <a href="/wiki/Visigoths" title="Visigoths">Visigoths</a>), then was re-conquered by the revived <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Roman_Empire" title="Eastern Roman Empire">Eastern Roman Empire</a> (later <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a>) under the strong Emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Justinian" title="Justinian">Justinian</a>. In the years following 572, the <a href="/wiki/Lombards" title="Lombards">Lombards</a> arrived and designated <a href="/wiki/Lucca" title="Lucca">Lucca</a> the capital of their subsequent <a href="/wiki/Tuscia" title="Tuscia">Tuscia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_2_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-Jones_2-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_period">Medieval period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuscany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Medieval period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Battle_of_Montaperti.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="564" data-file-width="642" height="211" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Battle_of_Montaperti.jpg/240px-Battle_of_Montaperti.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Battle_of_Montaperti.jpg/360px-Battle_of_Montaperti.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Battle_of_Montaperti.jpg/480px-Battle_of_Montaperti.jpg 2x" width="240"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Battle_of_Montaperti.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Montaperti" title="Battle of Montaperti">Battle of Montaperti</a>, 1260</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/March_of_Tuscany" title="March of Tuscany">March of Tuscany</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pilgrim" title="Pilgrim">Pilgrims</a> travelling along the <a href="/wiki/Via_Francigena" title="Via Francigena">Via Francigena</a> between <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> and <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> brought wealth and development during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval_period" title="Medieval period">medieval period</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_2_11-6"><a href="#cite_note-Jones_2-11">[11]</a></sup> The food and shelter required by these travellers fuelled the growth of communities around churches and taverns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_2_11-7"><a href="#cite_note-Jones_2-11">[11]</a></sup> The conflict between the <a href="/wiki/Guelphs_and_Ghibellines" title="Guelphs and Ghibellines">Guelphs and Ghibellines</a>, factions supporting the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papacy" title="Papacy">Papacy</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> in central and northern Italy during the 12th and 13th centuries, split the Tuscan people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_2_11-8"><a href="#cite_note-Jones_2-11">[11]</a></sup> The two factions gave rise to several powerful and rich <a href="/wiki/Medieval_commune" title="Medieval commune">medieval communes</a> in Tuscany: <a href="/wiki/Arezzo" title="Arezzo">Arezzo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>, Lucca, <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">Pisa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Siena" title="Siena">Siena</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_2_11-9"><a href="#cite_note-Jones_2-11">[11]</a></sup> Balance between these communes was ensured by the assets they held: Pisa, a port; Siena, banking; and Lucca, banking and silk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_3_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jones_3-14">[14]</a></sup> But by the time of the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>, Florence had become the cultural capital of Tuscany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_3_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jones_3-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>One family that benefitted from Florence's growing wealth and power was the ruling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medici_family" title="Medici family">Medici family</a>. Its scion <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_de%27_Medici" title="Lorenzo de' Medici">Lorenzo de' Medici</a> was one of the most famous of the Medici. The legacy of his influence is visible today in the prodigious expression of art and architecture in Florence. His famous descendant <a href="/wiki/Catherine_de%27_Medici" title="Catherine de' Medici">Catherine de' Medici</a> married Prince Henry (later <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_France" title="Henry II of France">King Henry II</a>) of France in 1533.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a> epidemic hit Tuscany starting in 1348.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> It eventually killed 70% of the Tuscan population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> According to Melissa Snell, "Florence lost a third of its population in the first six months of the plague, and from 45% to 75% of its population in the first year."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1630, Florence and Tuscany were once again ravaged by the <a href="/wiki/1629%E2%80%9331_Italian_plague" title="1629–31 Italian plague">plague</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renaissance">Renaissance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuscany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Renaissance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Renaissance" title="The Renaissance">The Renaissance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance" title="Italian Renaissance">Italian Renaissance</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Botticelli-primavera.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3236" data-file-width="4926" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Botticelli-primavera.jpg/240px-Botticelli-primavera.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Botticelli-primavera.jpg/360px-Botticelli-primavera.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Botticelli-primavera.jpg/480px-Botticelli-primavera.jpg 2x" width="240"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Botticelli-primavera.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Primavera_(painting)" title="Primavera (painting)">Primavera</a></i> (1482) by <a href="/wiki/Sandro_Botticelli" title="Sandro Botticelli">Botticelli</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hanging_and_burning_of_Girolamo_Savonarola_in_Florence.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="881" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Hanging_and_burning_of_Girolamo_Savonarola_in_Florence.jpg/240px-Hanging_and_burning_of_Girolamo_Savonarola_in_Florence.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Hanging_and_burning_of_Girolamo_Savonarola_in_Florence.jpg/360px-Hanging_and_burning_of_Girolamo_Savonarola_in_Florence.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Hanging_and_burning_of_Girolamo_Savonarola_in_Florence.jpg/480px-Hanging_and_burning_of_Girolamo_Savonarola_in_Florence.jpg 2x" width="240"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hanging_and_burning_of_Girolamo_Savonarola_in_Florence.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hanging and burning of <a href="/wiki/Girolamo_Savonarola" title="Girolamo Savonarola">Girolamo Savonarola</a> in Piazza della Signoria in <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a> 1498 - Painting depicting Renaissance Florence</div></div></div>
<p>Tuscany, especially <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>, is regarded as the birthplace of the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>. Though "Tuscany" remained a linguistic, cultural and geographic conception, rather than a political reality, in the 15th century, Florence extended its dominion in Tuscany through the annexation of Arezzo in 1384, the purchase of Pisa in 1405 and the suppression of a local resistance there (1406). <a href="/wiki/Livorno" title="Livorno">Livorno</a> was bought in 1421 and became the harbour of Florence.
</p><p>From the leading city of Florence, the republic was from 1434 onward dominated by the increasingly monarchical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medici" title="Medici">Medici</a> family. Initially, under <a href="/wiki/Cosimo_de%27_Medici" title="Cosimo de' Medici">Cosimo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Piero_di_Cosimo_de%27_Medici" title="Piero di Cosimo de' Medici">Piero the Gouty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_de%27_Medici" title="Lorenzo de' Medici">Lorenzo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Piero_the_Unfortunate" title="Piero the Unfortunate">Piero the Unfortunate</a>, the forms of the republic were retained and the Medici ruled without a title, usually without even a formal office. These rulers presided over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florentine_Renaissance" title="Florentine Renaissance">Florentine Renaissance</a>. There was a return to the republic from 1494 to 1512, when first <a href="/wiki/Girolamo_Savonarola" title="Girolamo Savonarola">Girolamo Savonarola</a> then <a href="/wiki/Piero_Soderini" title="Piero Soderini">Piero Soderini</a> oversaw the state. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_Giovanni_de%27_Medici" title="Cardinal Giovanni de' Medici">Cardinal Giovanni de' Medici</a> retook the city with Spanish forces in 1512, before going to Rome to become <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_X" title="Pope Leo X">Pope Leo X</a>. Florence was dominated by a series of papal proxies until 1527 when the citizens declared the republic again, only to have it taken from them again in 1530 after a siege by an Imperial and Spanish army. At this point <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_VII" title="Pope Clement VII">Pope Clement VII</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V</a> appointed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alessandro_de%27_Medici" title="Alessandro de' Medici">Alessandro de' Medici</a> as the first formal hereditary ruler.
</p><p>The Sienese commune was not incorporated into Tuscany until 1555, and during the 15th century Siena enjoyed a cultural 'Sienese Renaissance' with its own more conservative character. Lucca remained an independent republic until 1847 when it became part of <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Tuscany" title="Grand Duchy of Tuscany">Grand Duchy of Tuscany</a> by the will of its people. <a href="/wiki/Piombino" title="Piombino">Piombino</a> and other strategic towns constituted the tiny <a href="/wiki/State_of_the_Presidi" title="State of the Presidi">State of the <i>Presidi</i></a> under Spanish control.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_era">Modern era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuscany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Modern era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Tuscany" title="Grand Duchy of Tuscany">Grand Duchy of Tuscany</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GrhToscane.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="559" data-file-width="606" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/GrhToscane.jpg/240px-GrhToscane.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/GrhToscane.jpg/360px-GrhToscane.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/GrhToscane.jpg/480px-GrhToscane.jpg 2x" width="240"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GrhToscane.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Tuscany" title="Grand Duchy of Tuscany">Grand Duchy of Tuscany</a></div></div></div>
<p>In the 16th century, the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Medici" title="House of Medici">Medicis</a>, rulers of Florence, annexed the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Siena" title="Republic of Siena">Republic of Siena</a>, creating the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Tuscany" title="Grand Duchy of Tuscany">Grand Duchy of Tuscany</a>. The Medici family became extinct in 1737 with the death of <a href="/wiki/Gian_Gastone_de%27_Medici,_Grand_Duke_of_Tuscany" title="Gian Gastone de' Medici, Grand Duke of Tuscany">Gian Gastone</a>, and Tuscany was transferred to <a href="/wiki/Francis_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Francis I, Holy Roman Emperor">Francis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duke_of_Lorraine" title="Duke of Lorraine">Duke of Lorraine</a> and husband of Austrian Empress <a href="/wiki/Maria_Theresa" title="Maria Theresa">Maria Theresa</a>, who let the country be ruled by his son. The dynasty of the Lorena ruled Tuscany until 1860, with the exception of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleonic_period" title="Napoleonic period">Napoleonic period</a>, when most of the country was annexed to the <a href="/wiki/First_French_Empire" title="First French Empire">French Empire</a>. After the <a href="/wiki/Second_Italian_War_of_Independence" title="Second Italian War of Independence">Second Italian War of Independence</a>, a revolution evicted the last Grand Duke, and after a plebiscite Tuscany became part of the new <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy" title="Kingdom of Italy">Kingdom of Italy</a>. From 1864 to 1870 Florence became the second capital of the kingdom.
</p><p>Under <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito Mussolini</a>, the area came under the dominance of local <a href="/wiki/National_Fascist_Party" title="National Fascist Party">Fascist</a> leaders such as <a href="/wiki/Dino_Perrone_Compagni" title="Dino Perrone Compagni">Dino Perrone Compagni</a> (from Florence), and <a href="/wiki/Costanzo_Ciano" title="Costanzo Ciano">Costanzo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galeazzo_Ciano" title="Galeazzo Ciano">Galeazzo Ciano</a> (from <a href="/wiki/Livorno" title="Livorno">Livorno</a>). Following the fall of Mussolini and the armistice of 8 September 1943, Tuscany became part of the Nazi-controlled <a href="/wiki/Italian_Social_Republic" title="Italian Social Republic">Italian Social Republic</a>, and was conquered almost totally by the Anglo-American forces during summer 1944. Following the end of the Social Republic, and the transition from the Kingdom to the modern <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian Republic</a>, Tuscany once more flourished as a cultural centre of Italy. After the establishment of regional autonomy in 1975, Tuscany has always been ruled by centre-left governments.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuscany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg"><img alt="[icon]" data-file-height="31" data-file-width="44" height="14" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" width="20"/></a></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs expansion</b>. <small>You can help by <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tuscany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=">adding to it</a>.</small> <small><i>(December 2009)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Tuscany has an immense cultural and artistic heritage, expressed in the region's churches, palaces, art galleries, museums, villages and piazzas. Many of these artifacts are found in the main cities, such as <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siena" title="Siena">Siena</a>, but also in smaller villages scattered around the region, such as <a href="/wiki/San_Gimignano" title="San Gimignano">San Gimignano</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Art">Art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuscany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:192px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:David_von_Michelangelo.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2046" data-file-width="1068" height="364" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/David_von_Michelangelo.jpg/190px-David_von_Michelangelo.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/David_von_Michelangelo.jpg/285px-David_von_Michelangelo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/David_von_Michelangelo.jpg/380px-David_von_Michelangelo.jpg 2x" width="190"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:David_von_Michelangelo.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Michelangelo%27s_David" title="Michelangelo's David">Michelangelo's David</a></div></div></div>
<p>Tuscany has a unique artistic legacy, and Florence is one of the world's most important water-colour centres, even so that it is often nicknamed the "art palace of Italy" (the region is also believed to have the largest concentration of Renaissance art and architecture in the world).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Painters such as <a href="/wiki/Cimabue" title="Cimabue">Cimabue</a> and <a href="/wiki/Giotto" title="Giotto">Giotto</a>, the fathers of Italian painting, lived in Florence and Tuscany, as well as Arnolfo and <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Pisano" title="Andrea Pisano">Andrea Pisano</a>, renewers of architecture and sculpture; <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Brunelleschi" title="Filippo Brunelleschi">Brunelleschi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Donatello" title="Donatello">Donatello</a> and <a href="/wiki/Masaccio" title="Masaccio">Masaccio</a>, forefathers of the Renaissance; <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_Ghiberti" title="Lorenzo Ghiberti">Ghiberti</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Della_Robbia" title="Della Robbia">Della Robbias</a>, <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Lippi" title="Filippo Lippi">Filippo Lippi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fra_Angelico" title="Fra Angelico">Angelico</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Botticelli" title="Botticelli">Botticelli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paolo_Uccello" title="Paolo Uccello">Paolo Uccello</a>, and the universal genius of <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>The region contains numerous museums and art galleries, many housing some of the world's most precious works of art. Such museums include the <a href="/wiki/Uffizi" title="Uffizi">Uffizi</a>, which keeps Botticelli's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Birth_of_Venus" title="The Birth of Venus">The Birth of Venus</a></i>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pitti_Palace" title="Pitti Palace">Pitti Palace</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Bargello" title="Bargello">Bargello</a>, to name a few. Most of the frescos, sculptures and paintings in Tuscany are held in the region's abundant churches and cathedrals, such as <a href="/wiki/Florence_Cathedral" title="Florence Cathedral">Florence Cathedral</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siena_Cathedral" title="Siena Cathedral">Siena Cathedral</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pisa_Cathedral" title="Pisa Cathedral">Pisa Cathedral</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collegiata_di_San_Gimignano" title="Collegiata di San Gimignano">Collegiata di San Gimignano</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Art_schools">Art schools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuscany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Art schools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:192px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pietro_lorenzetti,_compianto_(dettaglio)_basilica_inferiore_di_assisi_(1310-1329).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="500" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Pietro_lorenzetti%2C_compianto_%28dettaglio%29_basilica_inferiore_di_assisi_%281310-1329%29.jpg/190px-Pietro_lorenzetti%2C_compianto_%28dettaglio%29_basilica_inferiore_di_assisi_%281310-1329%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Pietro_lorenzetti%2C_compianto_%28dettaglio%29_basilica_inferiore_di_assisi_%281310-1329%29.jpg/285px-Pietro_lorenzetti%2C_compianto_%28dettaglio%29_basilica_inferiore_di_assisi_%281310-1329%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Pietro_lorenzetti%2C_compianto_%28dettaglio%29_basilica_inferiore_di_assisi_%281310-1329%29.jpg/380px-Pietro_lorenzetti%2C_compianto_%28dettaglio%29_basilica_inferiore_di_assisi_%281310-1329%29.jpg 2x" width="190"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pietro_lorenzetti,_compianto_(dettaglio)_basilica_inferiore_di_assisi_(1310-1329).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A painting from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sienese_school" title="Sienese school">Sienese school</a> by <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Lorenzetti" title="Pietro Lorenzetti">Pietro Lorenzetti</a></div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florentine_school" title="Florentine school">Florentine school</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sienese_school" title="Sienese school">Sienese school</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lucchese_School" title="Lucchese School">Lucchese School</a></div>
<p>In the medieval period and in the Renaissance, there were four main Tuscan art schools which competed against each other: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florentine_School" title="Florentine School">Florentine School</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sienese_School" title="Sienese School">Sienese School</a>, the Pisan School and the <a href="/wiki/Lucchese_School" title="Lucchese School">Lucchese School</a>.
</p>
<ul><li>The Florentine School refers to artists in, from or influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Realism_(arts)" title="Realism (arts)">naturalistic</a> style developed in the 14th century, largely through the efforts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giotto_di_Bondone" title="Giotto di Bondone">Giotto di Bondone</a>, and in the 15th century the leading school of the world. Some of the best known artists of the Florentine School are <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Brunelleschi" title="Filippo Brunelleschi">Brunelleschi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Donatello" title="Donatello">Donatello</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fra_Angelico" title="Fra Angelico">Fra Angelico</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Botticelli" title="Botticelli">Botticelli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Lippi" title="Filippo Lippi">Lippi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Masolino_da_Panicale" title="Masolino da Panicale">Masolino</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Masaccio" title="Masaccio">Masaccio</a>.</li>
<li>The Sienese School of painting flourished in <a href="/wiki/Siena" title="Siena">Siena</a> between the 13th and 15th centuries and for a time rivaled Florence, though it was more conservative, being inclined towards the decorative beauty and elegant grace of late <a href="/wiki/Gothic_art" title="Gothic art">Gothic art</a>. Its most important representatives include <a href="/wiki/Duccio" title="Duccio">Duccio</a>, whose work shows Byzantine influence; his pupil <a href="/wiki/Simone_Martini" title="Simone Martini">Simone Martini</a>; <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Lorenzetti" title="Pietro Lorenzetti">Pietro</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ambrogio_Lorenzetti" title="Ambrogio Lorenzetti">Ambrogio Lorenzetti</a>; <a href="/wiki/Domenico_di_Bartolo" title="Domenico di Bartolo">Domenico</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taddeo_di_Bartolo" title="Taddeo di Bartolo">Taddeo di Bartolo</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Stefano_di_Giovanni" title="Stefano di Giovanni">Sassetta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matteo_di_Giovanni" title="Matteo di Giovanni">Matteo di Giovanni</a>. Unlike the naturalistic Florentine art, there is a mystical streak in Sienese art,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> characterized by a common focus on miraculous events, distortions of time and place, and often dreamlike coloration, with less attention to proportions. In the 16th century the Mannerists <a href="/wiki/Domenico_di_Pace_Beccafumi" title="Domenico di Pace Beccafumi">Beccafumi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Il_Sodoma" title="Il Sodoma">Il Sodoma</a> worked there. While Baldassare Peruzzi was born and trained in Siena, his major works and style reflect his long career in Rome. The economic and political decline of Siena by the 16th century, and its eventual subjugation by Florence, largely checked the development of Sienese painting, although it also meant that many Sienese works in churches and public buildings were not discarded or destroyed by new paintings or rebuilding. Siena remains a remarkably well-preserved Italian late-Medieval town.</li>
<li>The Lucchese School, also known as the School of Lucca and as the Pisan-Lucchese School, was a school of painting and sculpture that flourished in the 11th and 12th centuries in the western and southern part of the region, with an important center in <a href="/wiki/Volterra" title="Volterra">Volterra</a>.  The art is mostly anonymous. Although not as elegant or delicate as the Florentine School, Lucchese works are remarkable for their monumentality.</li></ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuscany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tuscan_dialect" title="Tuscan dialect">Tuscan dialect</a></div>
<p>Apart from standard <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tuscan_dialect" title="Tuscan dialect">Tuscan dialect</a> (<i>dialetto toscano</i>) is spoken in Tuscany. The Italian language is a "literary version" of Tuscan. It became the language of culture for all the people of Italy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-language-history_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-language-history-23">[23]</a></sup> thanks to the prestige of the masterpieces of <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante Alighieri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petrarch" title="Petrarch">Petrarch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Boccaccio" title="Giovanni Boccaccio">Giovanni Boccaccio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Machiavelli" title="Niccolò Machiavelli">Niccolò Machiavelli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Guicciardini" title="Francesco Guicciardini">Francesco Guicciardini</a>. It would later become the official language of all the <a href="/wiki/List_of_historic_states_of_Italy" title="List of historic states of Italy">Italian states</a> and of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy" title="Kingdom of Italy">Kingdom of Italy</a>, when it was formed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-language-history_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-language-history-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuscany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GiacomoPuccini.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1428" data-file-width="946" height="272" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/GiacomoPuccini.jpg/180px-GiacomoPuccini.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/GiacomoPuccini.jpg/270px-GiacomoPuccini.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/GiacomoPuccini.jpg/360px-GiacomoPuccini.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GiacomoPuccini.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Giacomo_Puccini" title="Giacomo Puccini">Giacomo Puccini</a></div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Tuscany" title="Music of Tuscany">Music of Tuscany</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Florence" title="Music of Florence">Music of Florence</a></div>
<p>Tuscany has a rich ancient and modern musical tradition, and has produced numerous composers and musicians, including <a href="/wiki/Giacomo_Puccini" title="Giacomo Puccini">Giacomo Puccini</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Mascagni" title="Pietro Mascagni">Pietro Mascagni</a>. Florence is the main musical centre of Tuscany. The city was at the heart of much of the Western musical tradition. It was there that the <a href="/wiki/Florentine_Camerata" title="Florentine Camerata">Florentine Camerata</a> convened in the mid-16th century and experimented with setting tales of <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a> to music and staging, resulting in the first <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">operas</a>, fostering the further development of the operatic form, and the later developments of separate "classical" forms such as the <a href="/wiki/Symphony" title="Symphony">symphony</a>.
</p><p>There are numerous musical centres in Tuscany. <a href="/wiki/Arezzo" title="Arezzo">Arezzo</a> is indelibly connected with the name of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guido_d%27Arezzo" title="Guido d'Arezzo">Guido d'Arezzo</a>, the 11th-century monk who invented modern <a href="/wiki/Musical_notation" title="Musical notation">musical notation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Solf%C3%A8ge" title="Solfège">do-re-mi</a> system of naming notes of the scale; <a href="/wiki/Lucca" title="Lucca">Lucca</a> hosted possibly the greatest Italian composer of <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romanticism</a>, Giacomo Puccini; and <a href="/wiki/Siena" title="Siena">Siena</a> is well known for the <a href="/wiki/Accademia_Musicale_Chigiana" title="Accademia Musicale Chigiana">Accademia Musicale Chigiana</a>, an organization that currently sponsors major musical activities such as the Siena Music Week and the Alfredo Casella International Composition Competition. Other important musical centres in Tuscany include <a href="/wiki/Livorno" title="Livorno">Livorno</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">Pisa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grosseto" title="Grosseto">Grosseto</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuscany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Altichiero,_ritratto_di_Francesco_Petrarca.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2088" data-file-width="1772" height="212" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Altichiero%2C_ritratto_di_Francesco_Petrarca.jpg/180px-Altichiero%2C_ritratto_di_Francesco_Petrarca.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Altichiero%2C_ritratto_di_Francesco_Petrarca.jpg/270px-Altichiero%2C_ritratto_di_Francesco_Petrarca.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Altichiero%2C_ritratto_di_Francesco_Petrarca.jpg/360px-Altichiero%2C_ritratto_di_Francesco_Petrarca.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Altichiero,_ritratto_di_Francesco_Petrarca.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tuscan poet and literary figure <a href="/wiki/Petrarch" title="Petrarch">Petrarch</a></div></div></div>
<p>Several famous writers and poets are from Tuscany, most notably Florentine author <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante Alighieri</a>. Tuscany's literary scene particularly thrived in the 13th century and the Renaissance.
</p><p>In Tuscany, especially in the Middle Ages, popular love poetry existed. A school of imitators of the Sicilians was led by <a href="/wiki/Dante_da_Maiano" title="Dante da Maiano">Dante da Maiano</a>, but its literary originality took another line — that of humorous and satirical poetry. The democratic form of government created a style of poetry which stood strongly against the medieval mystic and chivalrous style. Devout invocation of God or of a lady came from the <a href="/wiki/Cloister" title="Cloister">cloister</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">castle</a>; in the streets of the cities everything that had gone before was treated with ridicule or biting <a href="/wiki/Sarcasm" title="Sarcasm">sarcasm</a>. <a href="/wiki/Folg%C3%B3re_da_San_Gimignano" title="Folgóre da San Gimignano">Folgóre da San Gimignano</a> laughs when in his sonnets he tells a party of Sienese youths the occupations of every month in the year, or when he teaches a party of Florentine lads the pleasures of every day in the week. Cenne della Chitarra laughs when he parodies Folgore's sonnets. The sonnets of Rustico di Filippo are half-fun and half-satire, as is the work of <a href="/wiki/Cecco_Angiolieri" title="Cecco Angiolieri">Cecco Angiolieri</a> of Siena, the oldest humorist we know, a far-off precursor of <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Rabelais" title="François Rabelais">François Rabelais</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michel_de_Montaigne" title="Michel de Montaigne">Michel de Montaigne</a>.
</p><p>Another type of poetry also began in Tuscany. Guittone d'Arezzo made art abandon chivalry and <a href="/wiki/Provence" title="Provence">Provençal</a> forms for national motives and Latin forms. He attempted political poetry, and although his work is often obscure, he prepared the way for the Bolognese school. <a href="/wiki/Bologna" title="Bologna">Bologna</a> was the city of science, and <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophical</a> poetry appeared there. <a href="/wiki/Guido_Guinizelli" title="Guido Guinizelli">Guido Guinizelli</a> was the poet after the new fashion of the art. In his work the ideas of <a href="/wiki/Chivalry" title="Chivalry">chivalry</a> are changed and enlarged. Only those whose heart is pure can be blessed with true love, regardless of class. He refuted the traditional credo of <a href="/wiki/Courtly_love" title="Courtly love">courtly love</a>, for which love is a subtle philosophy only a few chosen knights and princesses could grasp. Love is blind to blasons but not to a good heart when it finds one: when it succeeds it is the result of the spiritual, not physical affinity between two souls. Guinizzelli's democratic view can be better understood in the light of the greater equality and freedom enjoyed by the city-states of the center-north and the rise of a middle class eager to legitimise itself in the eyes of the old nobility, still regarded with respect and admiration but in fact dispossessed of its political power. Guinizelli's <i><a href="/wiki/Canzone" title="Canzone">Canzoni</a></i> make up the bible of <a href="/wiki/Dolce_Stil_Novo" title="Dolce Stil Novo">Dolce Stil Novo</a>, and one in particular, "Al cor gentil" ("To a Kind Heart") is considered the manifesto of the new movement which would bloom in Florence under <a href="/wiki/Guido_Cavalcanti" title="Guido Cavalcanti">Cavalcanti</a>, Dante and their followers. His poetry has some of the faults of the school of d'Arezzo. Nevertheless, he marks a great development in the history of Italian art, especially because of his close connection with Dante's <a href="/wiki/Lyric_poetry" title="Lyric poetry">lyric poetry</a>.
</p><p>In the 13th century, there were several major <a href="/wiki/Allegory" title="Allegory">allegorical poems</a>. One of these is by <a href="/wiki/Brunetto_Latini" title="Brunetto Latini">Brunetto Latini</a>, who was a close friend of Dante. His <i>Tesoretto</i> is a short poem, in seven-syllable verses, rhyming in couplets, in which the author professes to be lost in a wilderness and to meet with a lady, who represents Nature, from whom he receives much instruction. We see here the vision, the allegory, the instruction with a moral object, three elements which we shall find again in the <i>Divine Comedy</i>. Francesco da Barberino, a learned lawyer who was secretary to <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishops</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Judge" title="Judge">judge</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Notary_public" title="Notary public">notary</a>, wrote two little allegorical poems, the <i>Documenti d'amore</i> and <i>Del reggimento e dei costumi delle donne</i>. The poems today are generally studied not as literature, but for historical context. A fourth allegorical work was the <i>Intelligenza</i>, which is sometimes attributed to Compagni, but is probably only a translation of French poems.
</p><p>In the 15th century, <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_humanism" title="Renaissance humanism">humanist</a> and publisher <a href="/wiki/Aldus_Manutius" title="Aldus Manutius">Aldus Manutius</a> published the Tuscan poets Petrarch and Dante Alighieri (<i><a href="/wiki/Divine_Comedy" title="Divine Comedy">Divine Comedy</a></i>), creating the model for what became a standard for modern Italian.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cuisine">Cuisine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuscany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Cuisine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Salumi_e_vino_lucchese.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="490" data-file-width="600" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Salumi_e_vino_lucchese.JPG/230px-Salumi_e_vino_lucchese.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Salumi_e_vino_lucchese.JPG/345px-Salumi_e_vino_lucchese.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Salumi_e_vino_lucchese.JPG/460px-Salumi_e_vino_lucchese.JPG 2x" width="230"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Salumi_e_vino_lucchese.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An assortment of Tuscan foods (from Lucca): various wine and cheese, and different sorts of salamis and hams</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuscan_cuisine" title="Tuscan cuisine">Tuscan cuisine</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tuscan_wine" title="Tuscan wine">Tuscan wine</a></div>
<p>Simplicity is central to the <a href="/wiki/Italian_cuisine" title="Italian cuisine">Tuscan cuisine</a>. <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">Legumes</a>, bread, cheese, vegetables, mushrooms and fresh fruit are used. <a href="/wiki/Olive_oil" title="Olive oil">Olive oil</a> is made up of Moraiolo, <a href="/wiki/Leccino" title="Leccino">Leccino</a> and Frantoiano olives. White <a href="/wiki/Truffle" title="Truffle">truffles</a> from <a href="/wiki/San_Miniato" title="San Miniato">San Miniato</a> appear in October and November. Beef of the highest quality comes from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chiana_Valley" title="Chiana Valley">Chiana Valley</a>, specifically a breed known as <a href="/wiki/Chianina" title="Chianina">Chianina</a> used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florentine_steak" title="Florentine steak">Florentine steak</a>. The indigenous <a href="/wiki/Cinta_Senese" title="Cinta Senese">Cinta Senese</a> breed of <a href="/wiki/Pork" title="Pork">pork</a> is also produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Wine is a famous and common produce of Tuscany. <a href="/wiki/Chianti" title="Chianti">Chianti</a> is arguably the most well-known internationally. So many British tourists come to the area where Chianti wine is produced that this specific area has been nicknamed "<a href="/wiki/Chiantishire" title="Chiantishire">Chiantishire</a>".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Postage_stamps">Postage stamps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuscany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Postage stamps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tuscany6.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1134" data-file-width="971" height="117" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Tuscany6.jpg/100px-Tuscany6.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Tuscany6.jpg/150px-Tuscany6.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Tuscany6.jpg/200px-Tuscany6.jpg 2x" width="100"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tuscany6.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>4 crazie stamp from 1851</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Postage_stamps_and_postal_history_of_Tuscany" title="Postage stamps and postal history of Tuscany">Postage stamps and postal history of Tuscany</a></div>
<p>Between 1851 and 1860, the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Tuscany" title="Grand Duchy of Tuscany">Grand Duchy of Tuscany</a>, an independent Italian state until 1859 when it joined the <a href="/wiki/United_Provinces_of_Central_Italy" title="United Provinces of Central Italy">United Provinces of Central Italy</a>, produced two postage stamp issues which are among the most prized <a href="/wiki/Classic_stamp" title="Classic stamp">classic stamp</a> issues of the world, and include the most valuable Italian stamp. The Grand Duchy of Tuscany was an independent Italian state from 1569 to 1859, but was occupied by <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> from 1808 to 1814. The Duchy comprised most of the present area of Tuscany, and its capital was Florence. In December 1859, the Grand Duchy officially ceased to exist, being joined to the duchies of <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Modena_and_Reggio" title="Duchy of Modena and Reggio">Modena</a> and <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Parma" title="Duchy of Parma">Parma</a> to form the United Provinces of Central Italy, which was annexed by the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Sardinia" title="Kingdom of Sardinia">Kingdom of Sardinia</a> a few months later in March 1860. In 1862 it became part of Italy, and joined the Italian postal system.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuscany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Panorama_vicino_certaldo.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2346" data-file-width="3737" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Panorama_vicino_certaldo.jpg/230px-Panorama_vicino_certaldo.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Panorama_vicino_certaldo.jpg/345px-Panorama_vicino_certaldo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Panorama_vicino_certaldo.jpg/460px-Panorama_vicino_certaldo.jpg 2x" width="230"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Panorama_vicino_certaldo.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Vineyards in the <a href="/wiki/Chianti" title="Chianti">Chianti</a> region</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">Agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuscany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The subsoil in Tuscany is relatively rich in mineral resources, with iron ore, copper, mercury and <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a> mines, the famous <i>soffioni</i> (<a href="/wiki/Fumarole" title="Fumarole">fumarole</a>) at <a href="/wiki/Larderello" title="Larderello">Larderello</a>, and the vast marble mines in <a href="/wiki/Versilia" title="Versilia">Versilia</a>. Although its share is falling all the time, agriculture still contributes to the region's economy. In the region's inland areas cereals, potatoes, olives and grapes are grown. The swamplands, which used to be marshy, now produce vegetables, rice, tobacco, beets and sunflowers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ite1geo_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-ite1geo-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industry">Industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuscany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The industrial sector is dominated by mining, given the abundance of underground resources. Also of note are textiles, chemicals/pharmaceuticals, metalworking and steel, glass and ceramics, clothing and printing/publishing sectors. Smaller areas specialising in manufacturing and craft industries are found in the hinterland: the leather and footwear area in the south-west part of the province of Florence, the hot-house plant area in <a href="/wiki/Pistoia" title="Pistoia">Pistoia</a>, the ceramics and textile industries in the <a href="/wiki/Prato" title="Prato">Prato</a> area, scooters and motorcycles in <a href="/wiki/Pontedera" title="Pontedera">Pontedera</a>, and the processing of timber for the manufacture of wooden furniture in the <a href="/wiki/Cascina" title="Cascina">Cascina</a> area. The heavy industries (mining, steel and mechanical engineering) are concentrated along the coastal strip (<a href="/wiki/Livorno" title="Livorno">Livorno</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">Pisa</a> areas), where there are also important chemical industries. Also of note are the marble (<a href="/wiki/Carrara" title="Carrara">Carrara</a> area) and paper industries (<a href="/wiki/Lucca" title="Lucca">Lucca</a> area).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ite1geo_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-ite1geo-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tourism">Tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuscany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cala_Grande_Argentario.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Cala_Grande_Argentario.jpg/220px-Cala_Grande_Argentario.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Cala_Grande_Argentario.jpg/330px-Cala_Grande_Argentario.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Cala_Grande_Argentario.jpg/440px-Cala_Grande_Argentario.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cala_Grande_Argentario.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Maremma" title="Maremma">Maremma</a> encapsulates the most visited seaside destinations in Tuscany. Above, the Tuscan littoral of <a href="/wiki/Monte_Argentario" title="Monte Argentario">Monte Argentario</a></div></div></div>
<p>Tuscany is a traditionally popular destination in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, and the main tourist destinations by number of tourist arrivals are <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">Pisa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montecatini_Terme" title="Montecatini Terme">Montecatini Terme</a>, <a href="/wiki/Castiglione_della_Pescaia" title="Castiglione della Pescaia">Castiglione della Pescaia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grosseto" title="Grosseto">Grosseto</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ilgiunco.net_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-ilgiunco.net-5">[5]</a></sup> Additionally, the <a href="/wiki/Chianti" title="Chianti">Chianti</a> region, <a href="/wiki/Versilia" title="Versilia">Versilia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Val_d%27Orcia" title="Val d'Orcia">Val d'Orcia</a> are also internationally renowned and particularly popular spots among travellers.
</p><p>As far as seaside tourism is concerned, which represents 40% of tourist arrivals in the region, <a href="/wiki/Castiglione_della_Pescaia" title="Castiglione della Pescaia">Castiglione della Pescaia</a>'s sea has been repeatedly nominated as <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>'s best sea by the Italian non-governmental environmentalist organisation <a href="/wiki/Legambiente" title="Legambiente">Legambiente</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legambiente.it_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-legambiente.it-25">[25]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Castiglione_della_Pescaia" title="Castiglione della Pescaia">Castiglione</a> gained the first place in the most recent ranking too, published in 2015. The town is also the most visited seaside destination in Tuscany, and fourth most visited overall, with circa 1.3 million tourist arrivals recorded in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legambiente.it_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-legambiente.it-25">[25]</a></sup> Other popular seaside destination are <a href="/wiki/Grosseto" title="Grosseto">Grosseto</a> (second most popular after Castiglione), <a href="/wiki/Orbetello" title="Orbetello">Orbetello</a> (third most popular), <a href="/wiki/Monte_Argentario" title="Monte Argentario">Monte Argentario</a>, <a href="/wiki/Viareggio" title="Viareggio">Viareggio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elba" title="Elba">Elba</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giglio_Island" title="Giglio Island">Giglio Island</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fashion">Fashion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuscany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Fashion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fashion_in_Florence" title="Fashion in Florence">Fashion in Florence</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fashion_designers_of_Florence" title="Fashion designers of Florence">Fashion designers of Florence</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Via_de_Tornabuoni_08.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2112" data-file-width="2816" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Via_de_Tornabuoni_08.JPG/220px-Via_de_Tornabuoni_08.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Via_de_Tornabuoni_08.JPG/330px-Via_de_Tornabuoni_08.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Via_de_Tornabuoni_08.JPG/440px-Via_de_Tornabuoni_08.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Via_de_Tornabuoni_08.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Via_de%27_Tornabuoni" title="Via de' Tornabuoni">Via de' Tornabuoni</a> in Florence, the city's top fashion and shopping street, contains some of the world's most luxurious clothing and jewelry houses, such as <a href="/wiki/Cartier_(jeweler)" title="Cartier (jeweler)">Cartier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salvatore_Ferragamo_S.p.A." title="Salvatore Ferragamo S.p.A.">Ferragamo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gucci" title="Gucci">Gucci</a>, <a href="/wiki/Versace" title="Versace">Versace</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bulgari" title="Bulgari">Bulgari</a>, to name a few.</div></div></div>
<p>The fashion and <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">textile</a> industry are the pillars of the Florentine economy. In the 15th century, Florentines were working with luxury textiles such as wool and silk. Today the greatest designers in Europe utilize the textile industry in Tuscany, and especially Florence.
</p><p>Italy has one of the strongest textile industries in Europe, accounting for approximately one quarter of European production. Its turnover is over 25 billion euros. It is the third largest supplier of clothing after China and Japan. The Italian fashion industry generates 60% of its turnover abroad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The population density of Tuscany, with 161 inhabitants per square kilometre (420/sq mi) in 2008, is below the national average (198.8/km<sup>2</sup> or 515/sq mi). This is due to the low population density of the provinces of Arezzo, Siena, and especially Grosseto (50/km<sup>2</sup> or 130/sq mi). The highest density is found in the province of Prato (675/km<sup>2</sup> or 1,750/sq mi), followed by the provinces of Pistoia, Livorno, Florence and Lucca, peaking in the cities of Florence (more than 3,500/km<sup>2</sup> or 9,100/sq mi), Livorno, Prato, Viareggio, Forte dei Marmi and Montecatini Terme (all with a population density of more than 1,000/km<sup>2</sup> or 2,600/sq mi). The territorial distribution of the population is closely linked to the socio-cultural and, more recently, economic and industrial development of Tuscany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ite1geo_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-ite1geo-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Accordingly, the least densely populated areas are those where the main activity is agriculture, unlike the others where, despite the presence of a number of large industrial complexes, the main activities are connected with tourism and associated services, alongside many small firms in the leather, glass, paper and clothing sectors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ite1geo_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-ite1geo-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Italians" title="Italians">Italians</a> make up 93% of the total population. Starting from the 1980s, the region has attracted a large flux of immigrants, particularly from <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. There is also a significant community of British and American residents. As of  2008<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tuscany&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the Italian national institute of statistics ISTAT estimated that 275,149 foreign-born immigrants live in Tuscany, equal to 7% of the total regional population.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_and_politics">Government and politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tuscany&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Government and politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Tuscany" title="Politics of Tuscany">Politics of Tuscany</a></div>
<p>Tuscany is a stronghold of the center-left <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(Italy)" title="Democratic Party (Italy)">Democratic Party</a> (PD), forming with <a href="/wiki/Emilia-Romagna" title="Emilia-Romagna">Emilia-Romagna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Umbria" title="Umbria">Umbria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marche" title="Marche">Marche</a> the so-called Italian political "Red Quadrilateral". Since 1970, Tuscany has been continuously governed by the Socialist-Communist or PD-led governments. At the <a href="/wiki/Italian_general_election,_2013" title="Italian general election, 2013">February 2013 elections</a>, Tuscany gave more than 40% of its votes to <a href="/wiki/Pier_Luigi_Bersani" title="Pier Luigi Bersani">Pier Luigi Bersani</a>, and only 20.7% to <a href="/wiki/Silvio_Berlusconi" title="Silvio Berlusconi">Silvio Berlusconi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_2014_(Italy)" title="European Parliament election, 2014 (Italy)">2014 European elections</a>, Tuscany gave 56.4% of its votes to <a href="/wiki/Matteo_Renzi" title="Matteo Renzi">Matteo Renzi</a>'s center-left Democratic Party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Tuscany was one of only three regions to vote YES in the <a href="/wiki/Italian_constitutional_referendum,_2016" title="Italian constitutional referendum, 2016">2016 Italian constitutional referendum</a>.
</p>
