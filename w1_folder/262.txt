<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Kerosene</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Kerogen" title="Kerogen">Kerogen</a>.</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Kerosene_(disambiguation)" title="Kerosene (disambiguation)">Kerosene (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kerosene_bottle.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="614" data-file-width="237" height="440" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Kerosene_bottle.jpg/170px-Kerosene_bottle.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/27/Kerosene_bottle.jpg 1.5x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kerosene_bottle.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An Australian kerosene bottle, containing blue-dyed kerosene</div></div></div>
<p><b>Kerosene</b>, also known as <b>paraffin</b>, <b>lamp oil</b>, and <b>coal oil</b> (an obsolete term), is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combustibility" title="Combustibility">combustible</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a> liquid which is derived from <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a>. It is widely used as a <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a> in industry as well as households. Its name derives from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el">κηρός</span> (<i>keros</i>) meaning <a href="/wiki/Wax" title="Wax">wax</a>, and was registered as a trademark by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian" title="Canadian">Canadian</a> geologist and inventor <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Pineo_Gesner" title="Abraham Pineo Gesner">Abraham Gesner</a> in 1854 before evolving into a <a href="/wiki/Generic_trademark" title="Generic trademark">genericized trademark</a>. It is sometimes spelled <b>kerosine</b> in scientific and industrial usage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The term <b>kerosene</b> is common in much of <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> while the term <b>paraffin</b> (or a closely related variant) is used in <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>, eastern <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, and in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and (a variant of) the term petroleum in <a href="/wiki/Croatian_language" title="Croatian language">Croatian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">Czech</a>, <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Danish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Estonian_language" title="Estonian language">Estonian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a>, <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latvian_language" title="Latvian language">Latvian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serbian_language" title="Serbian language">Serbian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovak_language" title="Slovak language">Slovak</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slovenian_language" title="Slovenian language">Slovenian</a>. In some of these languages the term <i>kerosine</i> refers instead to <a href="/wiki/Jet_fuel" title="Jet fuel">jet fuel</a>. The term <b>lamp oil</b>, or the equivalent in the local languages, is common in the majority of <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. <a href="/wiki/Liquid_paraffin_(drug)" title="Liquid paraffin (drug)">Liquid paraffin</a> (called mineral oil in the US) is a more viscous and highly refined product which is used as a laxative. <a href="/wiki/Paraffin_wax" title="Paraffin wax">Paraffin wax</a> is a waxy solid extracted from petroleum.
</p><p>Kerosene is widely used to power <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">jet engines</a> of aircraft (<a href="/wiki/Jet_fuel" title="Jet fuel">jet fuel</a>) and some <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine" title="Rocket engine">rocket engines</a> and is also commonly used as a cooking and lighting fuel and for fire toys such as <a href="/wiki/Poi_(performance_art)#Fire_poi" title="Poi (performance art)">poi</a>. In parts of Asia, kerosene is sometimes used as fuel for small <a href="/wiki/Outboard_motor" title="Outboard motor">outboard motors</a> or even <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle" title="Motorcycle">motorcycles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> World total kerosene consumption for all purposes is equivalent to about 1.2 million barrels (50 million U.S. gallons; 42 million imperial gallons; 190 million liters) per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> A jet engine requires a <i>burning</i>, rather than exploding, fuel. In <a href="/wiki/Otto_engine" title="Otto engine">Otto engines</a>, used in most cars, petrol or diesel is mixed with air and as vapour explodes in smaller proportions in the cylinders. Whilst kerosene in the combustion chamber of a jet engine (after an initial electrical spark) becomes ignited and then continues to burn. (And as the jet engine's inlet area is larger than its exhaust area, this causes the flame to draw air through the engine. Fans in the front and generators behind the combustion chamber then enforces efficiency.)
</p><p>To prevent confusion between kerosene and the much more flammable and volatile <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>, some jurisdictions regulate markings or colorings for containers used to store or dispense kerosene. For example, in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Commonwealth of Pennsylvania</a> requires that portable containers used at retail service stations be colored blue, as opposed to red (for <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>) or yellow (for <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel fuel</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kerosene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kerosene is a low <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a>, clear liquid formed from hydrocarbons obtained from the <a href="/wiki/Fractional_distillation" title="Fractional distillation">fractional distillation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> between 150 and 275 °C (300 and 525 °F), resulting in a mixture with a <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> of 0.78–0.81 g/cm<sup>3</sup> (0.45–0.47 oz/cu in) composed of carbon chains that typically contain between 10 and 16 <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> per <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecule</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> It is miscible in petroleum solvents but <a href="/wiki/Miscibility" title="Miscibility">immiscible</a> in water.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Society_for_Testing_and_Materials" title="American Society for Testing and Materials">American Society for Testing and Materials</a> standard specification D-3699-78 recognizes two grades of kerosene: grades 1-K (less than 0.04% sulfur by weight) and 2-K (0.3% sulfur by weight). 1-K grade kerosene burns cleaner with fewer deposits, fewer toxins, and less frequent maintenance than 2-K grade kerosene, and is the preferred grade of kerosene for indoor kerosene heaters and stoves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Regardless of crude oil source or processing history, kerosene's major components are branched and straight chain <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">alkanes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naphthene" title="Naphthene">naphthenes</a> (cycloalkanes), which normally account for at least 70% by volume. <a href="/wiki/Aromatic_hydrocarbon" title="Aromatic hydrocarbon">Aromatic hydrocarbons</a> in this boiling range, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkylbenzene" title="Alkylbenzene">alkylbenzenes</a> (single ring) and alkylnaphthalenes (double ring), do not normally exceed 25% by volume of kerosene streams. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olefin" title="Olefin">Olefins</a> are usually not present at more than 5% by volume.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Flash_point" title="Flash point">flash point</a> of kerosene is between 37 and 65 °C (100 and 150 °F), and its <a href="/wiki/Autoignition_temperature" title="Autoignition temperature">autoignition temperature</a> is 220 °C (428 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Pour_point" title="Pour point">pour point</a> of kerosene depends on grade, with commercial aviation fuel standardized at −47 °C (−53 °F).
</p><p>1-K grade kerosene freezes around −40 °C (−40 °F, 233 K).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Heat_of_combustion" title="Heat of combustion">Heat of combustion</a> of kerosene is similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel fuel</a>; its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lower_heating_value" title="Lower heating value">lower heating value</a> is  43.1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megajoule" title="Megajoule">MJ</a>/<a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kg</a> (around 18,500 <a href="/wiki/British_thermal_unit" title="British thermal unit">Btu</a>/<a href="/wiki/Pound_(mass)" title="Pound (mass)">lb</a>), and its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Higher_heating_value" title="Higher heating value">higher heating value</a> is 46.2 MJ/kg (19,900 Btu/lb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United Kingdom, two grades of heating oil are defined. BS 2869 Class C1 is the lightest grade used for lanterns, camping stoves, wick heaters, and mixed with gasoline in some vintage combustion engines as a substitute for <a href="/wiki/Tractor_vaporising_oil" title="Tractor vaporising oil">tractor vaporising oil</a>. BS 2869 Class C2 is a heavier distillate, which is used as domestic heating oil. Premium kerosene is usually sold in 5-or-20-liter (1.1 or 4.4 imp gal; 1.3 or 5.3 U.S. gal) containers from hardware, camping and garden stores and is often dyed purple.  Standard kerosene is usually dispensed in bulk by a tanker and is undyed.
</p><p>National and international standards define the properties of several grades of kerosene used for <a href="/wiki/Jet_fuel" title="Jet fuel">jet fuel</a>. Flash point and freezing point properties are of particular interest for operation and safety; the standards also define additives for control of <a href="/wiki/Static_electricity" title="Static electricity">static electricity</a> and other purposes.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kerosene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The process of distilling crude oil/petroleum into kerosene, as well as other hydrocarbon compounds, was first written about in the 9th century by the Persian scholar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhazes" title="Rhazes">Rāzi</a> (or Rhazes). In his <i>Kitab al-Asrar</i> (<i>Book of Secrets</i>), the physician and chemist Razi described two methods for the production of kerosene, termed <i>naft abyad</i> ("white naphtha"), using an apparatus called an <a href="/wiki/Alembic" title="Alembic">alembic</a>. One method used <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> as an <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(chemistry)" title="Absorption (chemistry)">absorbent</a>, whereas the other method used <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">ammonium chloride</a> (<i>sal ammoniac</i>). The distillation process was repeated until most of the volatile hydrocarbon fractions had been removed and the final product was perfectly clear and safe to burn. Kerosene was also produced during the same period from <a href="/wiki/Oil_shale" title="Oil shale">oil shale</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bitumen" title="Bitumen">bitumen</a> by heating the rock to extract the oil, which was then distilled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> During the medieval Chinese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" title="Ming Dynasty">Ming Dynasty</a>, the Chinese made use of kerosene through extracting and purifying petroleum and then converted it into lamp fuel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The Chinese made use of petroleum and kerosene for lighting lamps and heating homes as early as 1500 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Illuminating_oil_from_coal_and_oil_shale">Illuminating oil from coal and oil shale</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kerosene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Illuminating oil from coal and oil shale">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although "coal oil" was well known by industrial chemists at least as early as the 1700s as a byproduct of making coal gas and coal tar, it burned with a smoky flame that prevented its use for indoor illumination. In cities, much indoor illumination was provided by piped-in <a href="/wiki/Coal_gas" title="Coal gas">coal gas</a>, but outside the cities, and for spot lighting within the cities, the lucrative market for fueling indoor lamps was supplied by <a href="/wiki/Whale_oil" title="Whale oil">whale oil</a>, specifically that from <a href="/wiki/Sperm_whale" title="Sperm whale">sperm whales</a>, which burned brighter and cleaner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Canadians" title="Canadians">Canadian</a> <a href="/wiki/Geologist" title="Geologist">geologist</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abraham_Gesner" title="Abraham Gesner">Abraham Gesner</a> claimed that in 1846, he had given a public demonstration in <a href="/wiki/Charlottetown" title="Charlottetown">Charlottetown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prince_Edward_Island" title="Prince Edward Island">Prince Edward Island</a> of a new process he had discovered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[note 1]</a></sup>  He heated coal in a <a href="/wiki/Retort" title="Retort">retort</a>, and distilled from it a clear, thin fluid that he showed made an excellent lamp fuel. He coined the name "kerosene" for his fuel, a contraction of <i>keroselaion</i>, meaning <i>wax-oil</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-russell_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-russell-19">[18]</a></sup> The cost of extracting kerosene from coal was high.
</p><p>Gesner recalled from his extensive knowledge of <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a>'s geology a naturally occurring <a href="/wiki/Asphalt" title="Asphalt">asphaltum</a> called <a href="/wiki/Albertite" title="Albertite">albertite</a>. He was blocked from using it by the New Brunswick coal conglomerate because they had coal extraction rights for the province, and he lost a court case when their experts claimed albertite was a form of coal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> In 1854, Gesner moved to <a href="/wiki/Newtown_Creek" title="Newtown Creek">Newtown Creek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>. There, he secured backing from a group of businessmen. They formed the North American Gas Light Company, to which he assigned his patents.
</p><p>Despite clear priority of discovery, Gesner did not obtain his first kerosene patent until 1854, two years after <a href="/wiki/James_Young_(chemist)" title="James Young (chemist)">James Young</a>'s United States patent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> Gesner's method of purifying the distillation products appears to have been superior to Young's, resulting in a cleaner and better-smelling fuel. Manufacture of kerosene under the Gesner patents began in New York in 1854 and later in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>—being distilled from <a href="/wiki/Bituminous_coal" title="Bituminous coal">bituminous coal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oil_shale" title="Oil shale">oil shale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-russell_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-russell-19">[18]</a></sup> Gesner registered the word "Kerosene" as a trademark in 1854, and for several years, only the North American Gas Light Company and the Downer Company (to which Gesner had granted the right) were allowed to call their lamp oil "Kerosene" in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1848, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scottish</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">chemist</a> <a href="/wiki/James_Young_(chemist)" title="James Young (chemist)">James Young</a> experimented with oil discovered seeping in a coal mine as a source of lubricating oil and illuminating fuel. When the seep became exhausted, he experimented with the dry distillation of coal, especially the resinous "boghead coal" (<a href="/wiki/Torbanite" title="Torbanite">torbanite</a>). He extracted a number of useful liquids from it, one of which he named <i>paraffine oil</i> because at low temperatures, it congealed into a substance that resembled paraffin wax. Young took out a patent on his process and the resulting products in 1850, and built the first truly commercial oil-works in the world at <a href="/wiki/Bathgate" title="Bathgate">Bathgate</a> in 1851, using oil extracted from locally mined torbanite, shale, and bituminous coal. In 1852, he took out a United States patent for the same invention. These patents were subsequently upheld in both countries in a series of lawsuits, and other producers were obliged to pay him royalties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-russell_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-russell-19">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kerosene_from_petroleum">Kerosene from petroleum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kerosene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Kerosene from petroleum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1851, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samuel_Martin_Kier" title="Samuel Martin Kier">Samuel Martin Kier</a> began selling lamp oil to local miners, under the name "Carbon Oil". He distilled this by a process of his own invention from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a>. He also invented a new lamp to burn his product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pioneer_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pioneer-24">[23]</a></sup>  He has been dubbed the <i>Grandfather of the American Oil Industry</i> by historians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McInnis_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-McInnis-25">[24]</a></sup>  Since the 1840s, Kier's <a href="/wiki/Salt_well" title="Salt well">salt wells</a> were becoming fouled with <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a>.  At first, Kier simply dumped the useless oil into the nearby <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Main_Line_Canal" title="Pennsylvania Main Line Canal">Pennsylvania Main Line Canal</a>, but later he began experimenting with several distillates of the crude oil, along with a chemist from eastern Pennsylvania.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harper_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harper-26">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ignacy_%C5%81ukasiewicz" title="Ignacy Łukasiewicz">Ignacy Łukasiewicz</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Polish</a> pharmacist residing in <a href="/wiki/Lviv" title="Lviv">Lviv</a>, and his <a href="/wiki/Hungarians" title="Hungarians">Hungarian</a> partner <a class="extiw" href="https://pl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jan_Zeh" title="pl:Jan Zeh">Jan Zeh</a> had been experimenting with different distillation techniques, trying to improve on Gesner's kerosene process, but using <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">oil</a> from a local <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_seep" title="Petroleum seep">petroleum seep</a>. Many people knew of his work, but paid little attention to it. On the night of 31 July 1853, doctors at the local hospital needed to perform an emergency operation, virtually impossible by candlelight. They therefore sent a messenger for Łukasiewicz and his new lamps. The lamp burned so brightly and cleanly that the hospital officials ordered several lamps plus a large supply of fuel. Łukasiewicz realized the potential of his work and quit the pharmacy to find a business partner, and then travelled to <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a> to register his technique with the government. Łukasiewicz moved to the <a href="/wiki/Gorlice" title="Gorlice">Gorlice</a> region of Poland in 1854, and sank several wells across southern Poland over the following decade, setting up a refinery near <a href="/wiki/Jas%C5%82o" title="Jasło">Jasło</a> in 1859.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>The petroleum discovery at the <a href="/wiki/Drake_Well" title="Drake Well">Drake Well</a> in western Pennsylvania in 1859 caused a great deal of public excitement and investment drilling in new wells, not only in Pennsylvania, but also in Canada, where petroleum had been discovered at <a href="/wiki/Oil_Springs,_Ontario" title="Oil Springs, Ontario">Oil Springs, Ontario</a> in 1858, and southern Poland, where Ignacy Łukasiewicz had been distilling lamp oil from petroleum seeps since 1852. The increased supply of petroleum allowed oil refiners to entirely side-step the oil-from-coal patents of both Young and Gesner, and produce illuminating oil from petroleum without paying royalties to anyone. As a result, the illuminating oil industry in the United States completely switched over to petroleum in the 1860s. The petroleum-based illuminating oil was widely sold as Kerosene, and the trade name soon lost its proprietary status, and became the lower-case generic product "kerosene".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup> Because Gesner’s original Kerosene had been also known as "coal oil," generic kerosene from petroleum was commonly called "coal oil" in some parts of the United States well into the 20th century.
</p><p>In the United Kingdom, manufacturing oil from coal (or oil shale) continued into the early 20th century, although increasingly overshadowed by petroleum oils.
</p><p>As kerosene production increased, whaling declined. The American whaling fleet, which had been steadily growing for 50 years, reached its all-time peak of 199 ships in 1858. By 1860, just two years later, the fleet had dropped to 167 ships. The Civil War cut into American whaling temporarily, but only 105 whaling ships returned to sea in 1866, the first full year of peace, and that number dwindled until only 39 American ships set out to hunt whales in 1876.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup> Kerosene, made first from coal and oil shale, then from petroleum, had largely taken over whaling’s lucrative market in lamp oil.
</p><p>Electric lighting started displacing kerosene as an illuminant in the late 19th century, especially in urban areas. However, kerosene remained the predominant commercial end-use for petroleum refined in the United States until 1909, when it was exceeded by motor fuels. The rise of the gasoline-powered automobile in the early 20th century created a demand for the lighter hydrocarbon fractions, and refiners invented methods to increase the output of gasoline, while decreasing the output of kerosene. In addition, some of the heavier hydrocarbons that previously went into kerosene were incorporated into diesel fuel. Kerosene kept some market share by being increasingly used in stoves and portable heaters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2013, kerosene made up about 0.1 percent by volume of petroleum refinery output in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use">Use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kerosene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="As_fuel">As fuel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kerosene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: As fuel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Heating_and_lighting">Heating and lighting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kerosene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Heating and lighting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>At one time the fuel, also known as heating oil in the UK and Ireland, was widely used in <a href="/wiki/Kerosene_lamp" title="Kerosene lamp">kerosene lamps</a> and lanterns. Although it replaced <a href="/wiki/Whale_oil" title="Whale oil">whale oil</a>, the 1873 edition of <i>Elements of Chemistry</i> said, "The vapor of this substance [kerosene] mixed with air is as explosive as gunpowder."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup> This may have been due to the common practice of adulterating kerosene with cheaper but more volatile hydrocarbon mixtures, such as <a href="/wiki/Naphtha" title="Naphtha">naphtha</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup>
Kerosene was  a significant fire risk; in 1880, nearly two of every five New York City fires were caused by defective kerosene lamps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">less-developed countries</a> kerosene is an important source of energy for cooking and lighting. It is used as a <a href="/wiki/Cooking" title="Cooking">cooking</a> fuel in <a href="/wiki/Portable_stove" title="Portable stove">portable stoves</a> for <a href="/wiki/Backpacking_(wilderness)" title="Backpacking (wilderness)">backpackers</a>. As a heating fuel, it is often used in portable stoves, and is sold in some <a href="/wiki/Filling_station" title="Filling station">filling stations</a>. It is sometimes used as a heat source during power failures.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kerosene_truck_Aichi_Japan.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1768" data-file-width="2880" height="135" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Kerosene_truck_Aichi_Japan.jpg/220px-Kerosene_truck_Aichi_Japan.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Kerosene_truck_Aichi_Japan.jpg/330px-Kerosene_truck_Aichi_Japan.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Kerosene_truck_Aichi_Japan.jpg/440px-Kerosene_truck_Aichi_Japan.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kerosene_truck_Aichi_Japan.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A truck delivering kerosene in Japan</div></div></div>
<p>Kerosene is widely used in Japan as a home heating fuel for portable and installed kerosene heaters. In Japan, kerosene can be readily bought at any filling station or be delivered to homes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nagoya_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nagoya-35">[34]</a></sup>
In the United Kingdom and Ireland, kerosene is often used as a heating fuel in areas not connected to a <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">gas</a> pipeline network. It is used less for cooking, with <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_petroleum_gas" title="Liquefied petroleum gas">LPG</a> being preferred because it is easier to light.  Kerosene is often the fuel of choice for range cookers such as <a href="/wiki/Rayburn_range" title="Rayburn range">Rayburn</a>. Additives such as RangeKlene can be put into kerosene to ensure that it burns cleaner and produces less soot when used in range cookers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Amish" title="Amish">Amish</a>, who generally abstain from the use of electricity, rely on kerosene for lighting at night.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kerosene_Storage_Tank.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Kerosene_Storage_Tank.jpg/220px-Kerosene_Storage_Tank.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Kerosene_Storage_Tank.jpg/330px-Kerosene_Storage_Tank.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Kerosene_Storage_Tank.jpg/440px-Kerosene_Storage_Tank.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kerosene_Storage_Tank.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Kerosene Storage Tank</div></div></div>
<p>More ubiquitous in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, <a href="/wiki/Kerosene_heater" title="Kerosene heater">kerosene space heaters</a> were often built into kitchen ranges, and kept many farm and fishing families warm and dry through the winter. At one time, citrus growers used a <a href="/wiki/Smudge_pot" title="Smudge pot">smudge pot</a> fueled by kerosene to create a pall of thick smoke over a grove in an effort to prevent freezing temperatures from damaging crops. "<a href="/wiki/Salamander_heater" title="Salamander heater">Salamanders</a>" are kerosene space heaters used on construction sites to dry out building materials and to warm workers. Before the days of electrically lighted road barriers, highway construction zones were marked at night by kerosene fired, pot-bellied torches. Most of these uses of kerosene created thick black smoke because of the low temperature of combustion.
</p><p>A notable exception, discovered in the early 19th century, is the use of a <a href="/wiki/Gas_mantle" title="Gas mantle">gas mantle</a> mounted above the wick on a kerosene lamp. Looking like a delicate woven bag above the woven cotton wick, the mantle is a residue of mineral materials (mostly <a href="/wiki/Thorium_dioxide" title="Thorium dioxide">thorium dioxide</a>), heated to <a href="/wiki/Incandescence" title="Incandescence">incandescence</a> by the flame from the wick. The <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cerium" title="Cerium">cerium</a> oxide combination produces both a whiter light and a greater fraction of the energy in the form of visible light than a <a href="/wiki/Black_body" title="Black body">black body</a> at the same temperature would.  These types of lamps are still in use today in areas of the world without electricity, because they give a much better light than a simple wick-type lamp does.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Recently, a multipurpose lantern that doubles as a cook stove has been introduced in India in areas with no electricity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cooking">Cooking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kerosene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cooking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rippingille_Albion_Lamp_Coy_c1900_advert.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1643" data-file-width="1082" height="258" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Rippingille_Albion_Lamp_Coy_c1900_advert.jpg/170px-Rippingille_Albion_Lamp_Coy_c1900_advert.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Rippingille_Albion_Lamp_Coy_c1900_advert.jpg/255px-Rippingille_Albion_Lamp_Coy_c1900_advert.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Rippingille_Albion_Lamp_Coy_c1900_advert.jpg/340px-Rippingille_Albion_Lamp_Coy_c1900_advert.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rippingille_Albion_Lamp_Coy_c1900_advert.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Advertisement for an oil stove, from the Albion Lamp Company, Birmingham, England, c. 1900</div></div></div>
<p>In countries such as India and Nigeria, kerosene is the main fuel used for cooking, especially by the poor, and kerosene stoves have replaced traditional wood-based cooking appliances. As such, increase in the price of kerosene can have a major political and environmental consequence. The Indian government subsidizes the fuel to keep the price very low, to around 15 U.S. cents per liter as of February 2007, as lower prices discourage dismantling of forests for cooking fuel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup> In Nigeria an attempt by the government to remove a fuel subsidy that includes kerosene met with strong opposition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>Kerosene is used as a fuel in <a href="/wiki/Portable_stove" title="Portable stove">portable stoves</a>, especially in <a href="/wiki/Primus_stove" title="Primus stove">Primus stoves</a> invented in 1892. Portable kerosene stoves earn a reputation of reliable and durable stove in everyday use, and perform especially well under adverse conditions. In outdoor activities and mountaineering, a decisive advantage of <a href="/wiki/Portable_stove#Pressurized_liquid_fuel_stoves" title="Portable stove">pressurized kerosene stoves</a> over <a href="/wiki/Portable_stove#Gas_cartridge_stoves" title="Portable stove">gas cartridge stoves</a> is their particularly high thermal output and their ability to operate at very low temperature in winter or at high altitude.  Wick stoves like Perfection's or wickless like Boss continue to be used by the Amish and off grid living and in natural disasters where there is no power available.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kerosene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the mid-20th century, kerosene or <a href="/wiki/Tractor_vaporising_oil" title="Tractor vaporising oil">tractor vaporising oil</a> (TVO) was used as a cheap fuel for tractors. The engine would start on gasoline, then switch over to kerosene once the engine warmed up.  A heat valve on the manifold would route the exhaust gases around the intake pipe, heating the kerosene to the point where it was vaporized and could be ignited by an <a href="/wiki/Electric_spark" title="Electric spark">electric spark</a>.
</p><p>In Europe following the Second World War, automobiles were modified similarly to run on kerosene rather than gasoline, which they would have to import and pay heavy taxes on. Besides additional piping and the switch between fuels, the head gasket was replaced by a much thicker one to diminish the compression ratio (making the engine less powerful and less efficient, but able to run on kerosene). The necessary equipment was sold under the trademark "Econom".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corporation1951_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Corporation1951-40">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the fuel crisis of the 1970s, Saab-Valmet developed and series-produced the <a href="/wiki/Saab_99" title="Saab 99">Saab 99</a> Petro that ran on kerosene, <a href="/wiki/Turpentine" title="Turpentine">turpentine</a> or gasoline. The project, codenamed "Project Lapponia", was headed by Simo Vuorio, and towards the end of the 1970s, a working prototype was produced based on the Saab 99 GL. The car was designed to run on two fuels. Gasoline was used for cold starts and when extra power was needed, but normally it ran on kerosene or turpentine. The idea was that the gasoline could be made from peat using the <a href="/wiki/Fischer%E2%80%93Tropsch_process" title="Fischer–Tropsch process">Fischer–Tropsch process</a>. Between 1980 and 1984, 3,756 Saab 99 Petros and 2,385 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Talbot_Horizon" title="Talbot Horizon">Talbot Horizons</a> (a version of the Chrysler Horizon that integrated many Saab components) were made. One reason to manufacture kerosene-fueled cars was that in Finland kerosene was less heavily taxed than gasoline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>Kerosene is used to fuel smaller-horsepower outboard motors built by Yamaha Motors, Suzuki Marine, and Tohatsu. Primarily used on small fishing craft, these are dual-fuel engines that start on gasoline and then transition to kerosene once the engine reaches optimum <a href="/wiki/Operating_temperature" title="Operating temperature">operating temperature</a>.  Multiple fuel Evinrude and Mercury Racing engines also burn kerosene, as well as jet fuel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>Today, kerosene is mainly used in <a href="/wiki/Jet_fuel" title="Jet fuel">fuel for jet engines</a> in several grades. One highly refined form of the fuel is known as <a href="/wiki/RP-1" title="RP-1">RP-1</a>, and is often burned with <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">liquid oxygen</a> as rocket fuel. These fuel grade kerosenes meet specifications for <a href="/wiki/Smoke_point" title="Smoke point">smoke points</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freeze_point" title="Freeze point">freeze points</a>.  The combustion reaction can be approximated as follows, with the molecular formula C<sub>12</sub>H<sub>26</sub> (<a href="/wiki/Dodecane" title="Dodecane">dodecane</a>):
</p><p>2 C<sub>12</sub>H<sub>26</sub>(<i>l</i>) + 37 O<sub>2</sub>(<i>g</i>) → 24 CO<sub>2</sub>(<i>g</i>) + 26 H<sub>2</sub>O(<i>g</i>); <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy" title="Enthalpy">∆<i>H</i>˚</a> = -7513 <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">kJ</a>
</p><p>In the initial phase of liftoff, the <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a> launch vehicle was powered by the reaction of liquid oxygen with RP-1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ebbing_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ebbing-43">[42]</a></sup>  For the five 6.4 meganewton sea-level thrust <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-1_(rocket_engine)" title="F-1 (rocket engine)">F-1</a> rocket engines of the Saturn V, burning together, the reaction generated roughly 1.62 × 10<sup>11</sup> <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">watts</a> (J/s) (162 gigawatt) or 217 million horsepower.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ebbing_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ebbing-43">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>Kerosene is sometimes used as an additive in Diesel fuel to prevent gelling or waxing in cold temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Ultra-low sulfur kerosene</i> is a custom-blended fuel used by the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Transit_Authority" title="New York City Transit Authority">New York City Transit Authority</a> to power its bus fleet. The transit agency started using this fuel in 2004, prior to the widespread adoption of <a href="/wiki/Ultra-low-sulfur_diesel" title="Ultra-low-sulfur diesel">ultra-low-sulfur diesel</a>, which has since become the standard. In 2008, the suppliers of the custom fuel failed to tender for a renewal of the transit agency's contract, leading to a negotiated contract at a significantly increased cost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/JP-8" title="JP-8">JP-8</a>, (for "Jet Propellant 8") a kerosene-based fuel, is used by the United States military as a replacement in diesel fueled vehicles and for powering aircraft. JP-8 is also used by the U.S. military and its NATO allies as a fuel for heaters, stoves, tanks and as a replacement for diesel fuel in the engines of nearly all tactical ground vehicles and electrical generators.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_chemistry">In chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kerosene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: In chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kerosene is used as a diluent in the <a href="/wiki/PUREX" title="PUREX">PUREX</a> extraction process, but it is increasingly being supplanted by <a href="/wiki/Dodecane" title="Dodecane">dodecane</a>.
In <a href="/wiki/X-ray_crystallography" title="X-ray crystallography">X-ray crystallography</a>, kerosene can be used to store crystals. When a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrated" title="Hydrated">hydrated</a> crystal is left in air, <a href="/wiki/Dehydration" title="Dehydration">dehydration</a> may occur slowly. This makes the color of the crystal become dull. Kerosene can keep air from the crystal.
</p><p>It can be also used to prevent air from re-dissolving in a boiled liquid,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KeroseneMiscellaneous_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-KeroseneMiscellaneous-46">[45]</a></sup> and to store alkali metals such as  <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rubidium" title="Rubidium">rubidium</a> (with the exception of lithium, which is less dense than kerosene, causing it to float).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_entertainment">In entertainment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kerosene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: In entertainment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kerosene is often used in the entertainment industry for fire performances, such as <a href="/wiki/Fire_breathing" title="Fire breathing">fire breathing</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poi_(juggling)" title="Poi (juggling)">fire juggling or poi</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fire_dancing" title="Fire dancing">fire dancing</a>. Because of its low flame temperature when burnt in free air, the risk is lower should the performer come in contact with the flame. Kerosene is generally not recommended as fuel for indoor fire dancing, as it produces an unpleasant (to some) odor, which becomes poisonous in sufficient concentration. <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">Ethanol</a> was sometimes used instead, but the flames it produces look less impressive, and its lower <a href="/wiki/Flash_point" title="Flash point">flash point</a> poses a high risk.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_industry">In industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kerosene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: In industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As a petroleum product miscible with many industrial liquids, kerosene can be used as both a solvent, able to remove other petroleum products, such as chain grease, and as a <a href="/wiki/Cutting_fluid" title="Cutting fluid">lubricant</a>, with less risk of combustion when compared to using <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>. It can also be used as a cooling agent in metal production and treatment (oxygen-free conditions).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the petroleum industry, kerosene is often used as a synthetic <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a> for corrosion experiments to simulate crude oil in field conditions.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Others">Others</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kerosene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Others">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kerosene can be applied topically to hard-to-remove mucilage or adhesive left by stickers on a glass surface (such as in show windows of stores).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KeroseneMiscellaneous_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-KeroseneMiscellaneous-46">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>It can be used to remove candle wax that has dripped onto a glass surface; it is recommended that the excess wax be scraped off prior to applying kerosene via a soaked cloth or tissue paper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KeroseneMiscellaneous_46-2"><a href="#cite_note-KeroseneMiscellaneous-46">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>It can be used to clean bicycle and motorcycle chains of old lubricant before relubrication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KeroseneMiscellaneous_46-3"><a href="#cite_note-KeroseneMiscellaneous-46">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>It can also be used to thin oil based paint used in fine art. Some artists even use it to clean their brushes; however, it leaves the bristles greasy to the touch.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicity">Toxicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kerosene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Toxicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ingestion" title="Ingestion">Ingestion</a> of kerosene is harmful or fatal. Kerosene is sometimes recommended as a folk remedy for killing <a href="/wiki/Head_louse" title="Head louse">head lice</a>, but health agencies warn against this as it can cause burns and serious illness. A kerosene shampoo can even be fatal if fumes are inhaled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>People can be exposed to kerosene in the workplace by breathing it in, swallowing it, skin contact, and eye contact. The US <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health" title="National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health">National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health</a> (NIOSH) has set a <a href="/wiki/Recommended_exposure_limit" title="Recommended exposure limit">recommended exposure limit</a> of 100 mg/m<sup>3</sup> over an 8-hour workday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kerosene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">
<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-18"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-18">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">In his book of 1861 and its second edition of 1865, Gesner claimed to have demonstrated <i>liquid</i> kerosene – an "oil" – in 1846 during his public lectures on Prince Edward's Island.  
<ul><li>Gesner, Abraham (1861) <a class="external text" href="https://archive.org/stream/apracticaltreat02gesngoog#page/n16/mode/2up" rel="nofollow"><i>A Practical Treatise on Coal, Petroleum, and Other Distilled Oils.</i></a>  New York, New York, USA:  Bailliere Brothers, p. 9.</li>
<li>Gesner, Abraham ; Gesner, George Weltden (1865) <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=KTkKAAAAIAAJ&amp;pg=PA9#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow"><i>A Practical Treatise on Coal, Petroleum, and Other Distilled Oils</i></a>, 2nd ed., New York, New York, USA:  Bailliere Brothers, p. 9.</li></ul>
However, John Butt characterized Gesner's book as " … a piece of propaganda designed to get people to believe that he had been constantly interested in inventing burning oil from 1846 to 1854."  Butt also stated that "No independent documentary proof has ever been produced to support Gesner's claim."   Furthermore, "He [Gesner] omitted to mention that kerosene had first been used to describe an illuminating gas."
<ul><li>Butt, John (1963) <a class="external text" href="http://theses.gla.ac.uk/3894/1/1963ButtPhD.pdf" rel="nofollow">"James Young, Scottish Industrialist and Philanthropist,"</a> Ph.D. thesis (University of Glasgow, Scotland, UK), p. 227.</li></ul>
As late as 1850, Gesner promoted his "kerosene" as an illuminating <i>gas</i>:  
<ul><li>In his U.S. patent of 1850, Gesner called the product of his distillations an "illuminating gas", not an oil:  Gesner, Abraham <a class="external text" href="http://pdfpiw.uspto.gov/.piw?docid=00007052" rel="nofollow">"Manufacture of illuminating-gas from bitumen"</a> U.S. Patent no. 7,052 (issued: 29 January 1850).</li>
<li>In his prospectus of 1850, Gesner repeatedly identified "kerosene" as a <i>gas</i>, not an oil:  Gesner, Abraham (1850) <a class="external text" href="https://babel.hathitrust.org/cgi/pt?id=aeu.ark:/13960/t9960mm6j;view=1up;seq=7" rel="nofollow">"Prospectus of Gesner's patent kerosene gas, obtained from bitumen, asphaltum, or mineral pitch."</a> New York, New York, USA:  Trehern &amp; Williamson.</li></ul>
</span></li>
</ol></div></div>
