<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Polymorphism (materials science)</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>In <a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">materials science</a>, <b>polymorphism</b> is the ability of a solid material to exist in more than one form or <a href="/wiki/Crystal_structure" title="Crystal structure">crystal structure</a>. Polymorphism can potentially be found in any crystalline material including <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metals</a>, and is related to <a href="/wiki/Allotropy" title="Allotropy">allotropy</a>, which refers to <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical elements</a>. The complete morphology of a material is described by polymorphism and other variables such as <a href="/wiki/Crystal_habit" title="Crystal habit">crystal habit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amorphous_solid" title="Amorphous solid">amorphous fraction</a> or <a href="/wiki/Crystallographic_defect" title="Crystallographic defect">crystallographic defects</a>. Polymorphism is relevant to the fields of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical" title="Pharmaceutical">pharmaceuticals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agrochemical" title="Agrochemical">agrochemicals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigments</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dyestuff" title="Dyestuff">dyestuffs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">foods</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Explosive" title="Explosive">explosives</a>.
</p><p>When polymorphism exists as a result of a difference in crystal packing, it is called <b>packing polymorphism</b>. Polymorphism can also result from the existence of different <a href="/wiki/Conformational_isomerism" title="Conformational isomerism">conformers</a> of the same molecule in <b>conformational polymorphism</b>. In <b>pseudopolymorphism</b> the different crystal types are the result of <a href="/wiki/Mineral_hydration" title="Mineral hydration">hydration</a> or <a href="/wiki/Solvation" title="Solvation">solvation</a>. This is more correctly referred to as <b>solvomorphism</b> as different solvates have different chemical formulae. An example of an organic polymorph is <a href="/wiki/Glycine" title="Glycine">glycine</a>, which is able to form <a href="/wiki/Monoclinic_crystal_system" title="Monoclinic crystal system">monoclinic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hexagonal_crystal_family" title="Hexagonal crystal family">hexagonal crystals</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">Silica</a> is known to form many polymorphs, the most important of which are; <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">α-quartz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">β-quartz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tridymite" title="Tridymite">tridymite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cristobalite" title="Cristobalite">cristobalite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coesite" title="Coesite">coesite</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stishovite" title="Stishovite">stishovite</a>. A classical example is the pair of minerals, <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">aragonite</a>, both forms of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>.
</p><p>An analogous phenomenon for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amorphous" title="Amorphous">amorphous</a> materials is <a href="/wiki/Polyamorphism" title="Polyamorphism">polyamorphism</a>, when a substance can take on several different amorphous modifications.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polymorphism_(materials_science)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In terms of <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>, there are two types of polymorphic behaviour. For a monotropic system, a plot of the free energy of the various polymorphs against temperature do not cross before all polymorphs melt—in other words, any transition from one polymorph to another below melting point will be irreversible. For an enantiotropic system, a plot of the free energy against temperature shows a crossing point threshold before the various melting points.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It may also be possible to revert interchangeably between the two polymorphs by heating or cooling, or through physical contact with a lower energy polymorph.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Energy-Temperature_diagrams_of_a_system_exhibiting_two_polymorphic_forms_with_enantiotropic_behaviour_(left)_and_monotropic_behaviour_(right)..jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="518" data-file-width="1019" height="112" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Energy-Temperature_diagrams_of_a_system_exhibiting_two_polymorphic_forms_with_enantiotropic_behaviour_%28left%29_and_monotropic_behaviour_%28right%29..jpg/220px-Energy-Temperature_diagrams_of_a_system_exhibiting_two_polymorphic_forms_with_enantiotropic_behaviour_%28left%29_and_monotropic_behaviour_%28right%29..jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Energy-Temperature_diagrams_of_a_system_exhibiting_two_polymorphic_forms_with_enantiotropic_behaviour_%28left%29_and_monotropic_behaviour_%28right%29..jpg/330px-Energy-Temperature_diagrams_of_a_system_exhibiting_two_polymorphic_forms_with_enantiotropic_behaviour_%28left%29_and_monotropic_behaviour_%28right%29..jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Energy-Temperature_diagrams_of_a_system_exhibiting_two_polymorphic_forms_with_enantiotropic_behaviour_%28left%29_and_monotropic_behaviour_%28right%29..jpg/440px-Energy-Temperature_diagrams_of_a_system_exhibiting_two_polymorphic_forms_with_enantiotropic_behaviour_%28left%29_and_monotropic_behaviour_%28right%29..jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Energy-Temperature_diagrams_of_a_system_exhibiting_two_polymorphic_forms_with_enantiotropic_behaviour_(left)_and_monotropic_behaviour_(right)..jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Solid phase transitions which transform reversibly without passing through the liquid or gaseous phases are called enantiotropic. In contrast, if the modifications are not convertible under these conditions, the system is monotropic. Experimental data are used to differentiate between enantiotropic and monotropic transitions and energy/temperature semi-quantitative diagrams can be drawn by applying several rules, principally the heat-of-transition rule, the heat-of-fusion rule and the density rule. These rules enable the deduction of the relative positions of the H and Gisobars in the E/T diagram. [1]</div></div></div>
<p>The first observation of polymorphism in organic materials is attributed to <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_W%C3%B6hler" title="Friedrich Wöhler">Friedrich Wöhler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Justus_von_Liebig" title="Justus von Liebig">Justus von Liebig</a> when in 1832 they examined<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> a boiling solution of <a href="/wiki/Benzamide" title="Benzamide">benzamide</a>: upon cooling, the benzamide initially crystallised as silky needles, but when standing these were slowly replaced by rhombic crystals. Present-day analysis<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> identifies three polymorphs for benzamide: the least stable one, formed by flash cooling is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthorhombic" title="Orthorhombic">orthorhombic</a> form II. This type is followed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monoclinic" title="Monoclinic">monoclinic</a> form III (observed by Wöhler/Liebig). The most stable form is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monoclinic" title="Monoclinic">monoclinic</a> form I. The hydrogen bonding mechanisms are the same for all three phases; however, they differ strongly in their pi-pi interactions.
</p><p>Polymorphs have different stabilities and may spontaneously convert from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metastable" title="Metastable">metastable</a> form (unstable form) to the <a href="/wiki/Stable" title="Stable">stable</a> form at a particular temperature. Most polymorphs of organic molecules only differ by a few kJ/mol in lattice energy. Approximately 50% of known polymorph pairs differ by less than 2 kJ/mol and stability differences of more than 10 kJ/mol are rare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> They also exhibit different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melting_points" title="Melting points">melting points</a>, solubilities (which affect the dissolution rate of <a href="/wiki/Drug" title="Drug">drug</a> and consequently its bioavailability in the body), <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a> crystal and <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">diffraction</a> patterns.
</p><p>Various conditions in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystallisation" title="Crystallisation">crystallisation</a> process is the main reason responsible for the development of different polymorphic forms. These conditions include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">Solvent</a> effects (the packing of crystal may be different in polar and nonpolar solvents)</li>
<li>Certain impurities inhibiting growth pattern and favour the growth of a metastable polymorphs</li>
<li>The level of supersaturation from which material is crystallised (in which generally the higher the concentration above the <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">solubility</a>, the more likelihood of metastable formation)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">Temperature</a> at which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystallisation" title="Crystallisation">crystallisation</a> is carried out</li>
<li>Geometry of covalent bonds (differences leading to conformational polymorphism)</li>
<li>Change in stirring conditions</li></ul>
<p>Despite the potential implications, polymorphism is not always well understood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seddon_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Seddon-5">[5]</a></sup> In 2006 a new crystal form of <a href="/wiki/Maleic_acid" title="Maleic acid">maleic acid</a> was discovered 124 years after the first crystal form was studied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Maleic acid is a chemical manufactured on a very large scale in the chemical industry and is a salt forming component in medicine. The new crystal type is produced when a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Co-crystal" title="Co-crystal">co-crystal</a> of <a href="/wiki/Caffeine" title="Caffeine">caffeine</a> and maleic acid (2:1) is dissolved in <a href="/wiki/Chloroform" title="Chloroform">chloroform</a> and when the solvent is allowed to evaporate slowly. Whereas form I has <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monoclinic" title="Monoclinic">monoclinic</a> <a href="/wiki/Space_group" title="Space group">space group</a> <i>P</i>2<sub>1</sub>/<i>c</i>, the new form has space group <i>Pc</i>. Both polymorphs consist of sheets of molecules connected through <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bond" title="Hydrogen bond">hydrogen bonding</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxylic acid</a> groups; but, in form I, the sheets alternate with respect of the net <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_dipole_moment" title="Molecular dipole moment">dipole moment</a>, whereas, in form II, the sheets are oriented in the same direction.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/1,3,5-Trinitrobenzene" title="1,3,5-Trinitrobenzene">1,3,5-Trinitrobenzene</a> is more than 125 years old and was used as an <a href="/wiki/Explosive" title="Explosive">explosive</a> before the arrival of the safer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2,4,6-trinitrotoluene" title="2,4,6-trinitrotoluene">2,4,6-trinitrotoluene</a>. Only one crystal form of 1,3,5-trinitrobenzene was known in the space group <i>Pbca</i>. In 2004, a second polymorph was obtained in the space group <i>Pca</i>2<sub>1</sub> when the compound was crystallised in the presence of an additive, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Trisindane&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Trisindane (page does not exist)">trisindane</a>. This experiment shows that additives can induce the appearance of polymorphic forms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Walter_McCrone" title="Walter McCrone">Walter McCrone</a> has stated that "every compound has different polymorphic forms, and that, in general, the number of forms known for a given compound is proportional to the time and money spent in research on that compound." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seddon_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Seddon-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Ostwald.27s_rule"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Ostwald's_rule">Ostwald's rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polymorphism_(materials_science)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ostwald's rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Ostwald's rule</b> or <b>Ostwald's step rule</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
conceived by <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Ostwald" title="Wilhelm Ostwald">Wilhelm Ostwald</a>, states that in general it is not the most stable but the least stable polymorph that crystallises first. See for examples the aforementioned benzamide, <a href="/wiki/Dolomite" title="Dolomite">dolomite</a> or <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>, which on sublimation first forms the less stable white and then the more stable red <a href="/wiki/Allotropy" title="Allotropy">allotrope</a>.
</p><p>Ostwald suggested that the solid first formed on crystallisation of a solution or a melt would be the least stable polymorph. This can be explained on the basis of irreversible thermodynamics, structural relationships, or a combined consideration of statistical thermodynamics and structural variation with temperature. Ostwald's rule is not a universal law but is only a possible tendency in nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_binary_metal_oxides">In binary metal oxides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polymorphism_(materials_science)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In binary metal oxides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_pharmaceuticals">In pharmaceuticals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polymorphism_(materials_science)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In pharmaceuticals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Polymorphism is important in the development of <a href="/wiki/Active_ingredient" title="Active ingredient">pharmaceutical ingredients</a>. Many <a href="/wiki/Medication" title="Medication">drugs</a> receive <a href="/wiki/Regulation" title="Regulation">regulatory approval</a> for only a single crystal form or polymorph. In a classic <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a> case the pharmaceutical company <a href="/wiki/GlaxoSmithKline" title="GlaxoSmithKline">GlaxoSmithKline</a> defended its patent for the polymorph type II of the active ingredient in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zantac" title="Zantac">Zantac</a> against competitors while that of the polymorph type I had already expired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Polymorphism in drugs can also have direct medical implications. <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">Medicine</a> is often administered orally as a crystalline solid and <a href="/wiki/Solvation" title="Solvation">dissolution</a> rates depend on the exact crystal form of a polymorph. Polymorphic purity of drug samples can be checked using techniques such as powder X-ray diffraction, IR/Raman
spectroscopy, and utilizing the differences in their optical properties in some cases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the case of the antiviral drug <a href="/wiki/Ritonavir" title="Ritonavir">ritonavir</a>, not only was one polymorph virtually inactive compared to the alternative crystal form, but the inactive polymorph was subsequently found to convert the active polymorph into the inactive form on contact, due to its lower energy and greater stability making spontaneous interconversion energetically favourable. Even a speck of the lower energy polymorph could convert large stockpiles of ritonavir into the medically useless inactive polymorph, and this caused major issues with production which ultimately were only solved by reformulating the medicine into gelcaps and tablets, rather than the original capsules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cefdinir" title="Cefdinir">Cefdinir</a> is a drug appearing in 11 patents from 5 pharmaceutical companies in which a total of 5 different polymorphs are described. The original inventor <i>Fujisawa</i> now <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astellas" title="Astellas">Astellas</a> (with US partner <a href="/wiki/Abbott_Laboratories" title="Abbott Laboratories">Abbott</a>) extended the original patent covering a <a href="/wiki/Suspension_(chemistry)" title="Suspension (chemistry)">suspension</a> with a new <a href="/wiki/Anhydrous" title="Anhydrous">anhydrous</a> formulation. Competitors in turn patented <a href="/wiki/Hydrate" title="Hydrate">hydrates</a> of the drug with varying water content, which were described with only basic techniques such as <a href="/wiki/Infrared_spectroscopy" title="Infrared spectroscopy">infrared spectroscopy</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/XRPD" title="XRPD">XRPD</a>, a practice criticised in one review<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> because these techniques at the most suggest a different crystal structure but are unable to specify one; however, given the recent advances in XRPD, it is perfectly feasible to obtain the structure of a polymorph of a drug, even if there is no single crystal available for that polymorphic form. These techniques also tend to overlook chemical impurities or even co-components. Abbott researchers realised this the hard way when, in one patent application, it was ignored that their new cefdinir crystal form was, in fact, that of a <a href="/wiki/Pyridinium" title="Pyridinium">pyridinium</a> salt. The review also questioned whether the polymorphs offered any advantages to the existing drug: something clearly demanded in a new patent.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acetylsalicylic_acid" title="Acetylsalicylic acid">Acetylsalicylic acid</a> has an elusive second polymorph that was first discovered by Vishweshwar et al.;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> fine structural details were given by Bond et al.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> A new crystal type was found after attempted co-crystallization of aspirin and <a href="/wiki/Levetiracetam" title="Levetiracetam">levetiracetam</a> from hot <a href="/wiki/Acetonitrile" title="Acetonitrile">acetonitrile</a>. In form I, two aspirin molecules form centrosymmetric <a href="/wiki/Dimer_(chemistry)" title="Dimer (chemistry)">dimers</a> through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acetyl" title="Acetyl">acetyl</a> groups with the (acidic) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methyl" title="Methyl">methyl</a> proton to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonyl" title="Carbonyl">carbonyl</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bond" title="Hydrogen bond">hydrogen bonds</a>, and, in form II, each aspirin molecule forms the same hydrogen bonds, but then with two neighbouring molecules instead of one. With respect to the hydrogen bonds formed by the <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxylic acid</a> groups, both polymorphs form identical dimer structures. The aspirin polymorphs contain identical 2-dimensional sections and are therefore more precisely described as polytypes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Paracetamol powder has poor compression properties; this poses difficulty in making tablets, so a new polymorph of <a href="/wiki/Paracetamol" title="Paracetamol">paracetamol</a> was found which is more compressible.</li>
<li>Due to differences in solubility of polymorphs, one polymorph may be more active therapeutically than another polymorph of same drug.</li>
<li>Cortisone acetate exists in at least five different polymorphs, four of which are unstable in water and change to a stable form.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Carbamazepine" title="Carbamazepine">Carbamazepine</a> (used in epilepsy and trigeminal neuralgia) beta-polymorph developed from solvent of high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dielectric_constant" title="Dielectric constant">dielectric constant</a> ex aliphatic <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a>, whereas alpha polymorph crystallized from solvents of low dielectric constant such as carbon tetrachloride.</li>
<li>Estrogen and <a href="/wiki/Chloramphenicol" title="Chloramphenicol">chloramphenicol</a> also show polymorphism.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disappearing_polymorphs">Disappearing polymorphs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polymorphism_(materials_science)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Disappearing polymorphs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Crystal polymorphs can disappear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seddon_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Seddon-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> There have been cases of laboratories growing crystals of a particular structure and when they try to recreate this, the original crystal structure isn't created but a new crystal structure is. Also, findings of one crystal structure intermittently polymorphing over time into another have been recorded. The drug <a href="/wiki/Paroxetine" title="Paroxetine">paroxetine</a> was subject to a lawsuit that hinged on such a pair of polymorphs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> An example is known when a so-called "disappeared" polymorph re-appeared after 40 years. These so-called "disappearing" polymorphs are most likely metastable kinetic forms.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Polytypism">Polytypism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polymorphism_(materials_science)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Polytypism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Polytypes</b> are a special case of polymorphs, where multiple <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Close-packed" title="Close-packed">close-packed</a> crystal structures differ in one dimension only.  Polytypes have identical close-packed planes, but differ in the <a href="/wiki/Stacking_fault" title="Stacking fault"> stacking sequence</a> in the third dimension perpendicular to these planes.  <a href="/wiki/Polymorphs_of_silicon_carbide" title="Polymorphs of silicon carbide">Silicon carbide</a> (SiC) has more than 170 known polytypes, although most are rare.  All the polytypes of SiC have virtually the same <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gibbs_free_energy" title="Gibbs free energy">Gibbs free energy</a>.  The 
most common SiC polytypes are shown in Table 1.

<p>A second group of materials with different polytypes are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transition_metal_dichalcogenides" title="Transition metal dichalcogenides">transition metal dichalcogenides</a>, layered materials such as molybdenum disulfide (MoS<sub>2</sub>). For these materials the polytypes have more distinct effects on material properties, e.g. for MoS<sub>2</sub>, the 1T polytype is metallic in character, while the 2H form is more semiconducting<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>.
Another example is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tantalum_disulfide" title="Tantalum disulfide">Tantalum disulfide</a>, where the common 1T as well as 2H polytypes occur, but also more complex 'mixed coordination' types such as 4Hb and 6R, where the trigonal prismatic and the octahedral geometry layers are mixed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Here, the 1T polytype exhibits a <a href="/wiki/Charge_density_wave" title="Charge density wave">charge density wave</a>, with distinct influence on the conductivity as a function of temperature, while the 2H polytype exhibits <a href="/wiki/Superconductivity" title="Superconductivity">superconductivity</a>.
</p><p>ZnS and CdI<sub>2</sub> are also polytypical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> It has been suggested that this type of polymorphism is due to kinetics where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Screw_dislocation" title="Screw dislocation">screw dislocations</a> rapidly reproduce partly disordered sequences in a periodic fashion.
</p>
