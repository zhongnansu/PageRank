<p><b>Fayalite</b> (Fe<sub>2</sub>SiO<sub>4</sub>; commonly abbreviated to <b>Fa</b>), also called iron chrysolite, is the <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>-rich end-member of the <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">olivine</a> solid-solution series. In common with all minerals in the <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">olivine group</a>, fayalite crystallizes in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthorhombic" title="Orthorhombic">orthorhombic</a> system (<a href="/wiki/Space_group" title="Space group">space group</a> <i>Pbnm</i>) with cell parameters <i>a</i> 4.82 Å, <i>b</i> 10.48 Å and <i>c</i> 6.09 Å.
</p><p>Fayalite forms solid solution series with the magnesium olivine endmember <a href="/wiki/Forsterite" title="Forsterite">forsterite</a> (Mg<sub>2</sub>SiO<sub>4</sub>) and also with the <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a> rich olivine endmember <a href="/wiki/Tephroite" title="Tephroite">tephroite</a> (Mn<sub>2</sub>SiO<sub>4</sub>).
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Atomic_structure_of_olivine_1.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="258" data-file-width="300" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Atomic_structure_of_olivine_1.png/220px-Atomic_structure_of_olivine_1.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cc/Atomic_structure_of_olivine_1.png 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Atomic_structure_of_olivine_1.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The atomic scale structure of fayalite looking along the A axis. Oxygen is shown in red, silicon in pink, and iron in blue. A projection of the unit cell is shown by the black rectangle.</div></div></div>
<p>Iron rich olivine is a relatively common constituent of acidic and <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_earth_metal" title="Alkaline earth metal">alkaline</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Igneous" title="Igneous">igneous</a> rocks such as volcanic <a href="/wiki/Obsidian" title="Obsidian">obsidians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhyolite" title="Rhyolite">rhyolites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trachyte" title="Trachyte">trachytes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phonolite" title="Phonolite">phonolites</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plutonic" title="Plutonic">plutonic</a> quartz <a href="/wiki/Syenite" title="Syenite">syenites</a> where it is associated with <a href="/wiki/Amphibole" title="Amphibole">amphiboles</a>.   Its main occurrence is in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultramafic" title="Ultramafic">ultramafic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic" title="Volcanic">volcanic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plutonic" title="Plutonic">plutonic</a> rocks and less commonly in <a href="/wiki/Felsic" title="Felsic">felsic</a> plutonic rocks and rarely in <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a> <a href="/wiki/Pegmatite" title="Pegmatite">pegmatite</a>. It also occurs in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lithophysae" title="Lithophysae">lithophysae</a> in <a href="/wiki/Obsidian" title="Obsidian">obsidian</a>.  It also occurs in medium-grade thermally <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphosed</a> iron-rich sediments and in impure carbonate rocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Handbook_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Handbook-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fayalite is stable with quartz at low pressures, whereas more magnesian olivine is not, because of the reaction olivine + <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a> = <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthopyroxene" title="Orthopyroxene">orthopyroxene</a>. Iron stabilizes the olivine + quartz pair. The pressure and compositional dependence of the reaction can be used to calculate constraints on pressures at which assemblages of olivine + quartz formed.
</p><p>Fayalite can also react with <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Magnetite" title="Magnetite">magnetite</a> + quartz: the three minerals together make up the "FMQ" oxygen buffer. The reaction is used to control the <a href="/wiki/Fugacity" title="Fugacity">fugacity</a> of oxygen in laboratory experiments. It can also be used to calculate the fugacity of oxygen recorded by mineral assemblages in metamorphic and igneous processes.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fayalite-pV.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="540" data-file-width="720" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Fayalite-pV.svg/220px-Fayalite-pV.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Fayalite-pV.svg/330px-Fayalite-pV.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Fayalite-pV.svg/440px-Fayalite-pV.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fayalite-pV.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Molar volume vs. pressure at room temperature</div></div></div>
<p>At high pressure, fayalite undergoes a phase transition to ahrensite, the iron-bearing analogue of <a href="/wiki/Ringwoodite" title="Ringwoodite">ringwoodite</a>, i.e., contrary to forsterite there is no intermediate form analogous to <a href="/wiki/Wadsleyite" title="Wadsleyite">wadsleyite</a>; under the conditions prevailing in the upper mantle of the Earth, the transition would occur at ca. 6–7 GPa, i.e., at substantially lower pressure than the phase transitions of forsterite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Presnall_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Presnall-4">[4]</a></sup> In high-pressure experiments, the transformation may be delayed, so that it may remain stable to pressures of almost 35 GPa (see fig.), at which point it may become amorphous rather than take on a crystalline structure such as ahrensite.
</p><p>The name <i>fayalite</i> is derived from <a href="/wiki/Faial_Island" title="Faial Island">Faial</a> (Fayal) Island in the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> where it was first described in 1840.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mindat_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mindat-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
