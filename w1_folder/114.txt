<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Unit of measurement</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>unit of measurement</b> is a definite <a href="/wiki/Magnitude_(mathematics)" title="Magnitude (mathematics)">magnitude</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Quantity" title="Quantity">quantity</a>, defined and adopted by convention or by law, that is used as a standard for measurement of the same kind of quantity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Any other quantity of that kind can be expressed as a multiple of the unit of measurement.
</p><p>For example, a <a href="/wiki/Length" title="Length">length</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Physical_quantity" title="Physical quantity">physical quantity</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metre</a> is a unit of length that represents a definite predetermined length. When we say 10 metres (or 10 m), we actually mean 10 times the definite predetermined length called "metre".
Measurement is a process of determining how large or small a physical quantity is as compared to a basic reference quantity of the same kind.
</p><p>The definition, agreement, and practical use of units of measurement have played a crucial role in human endeavour from early ages up to the present. A multitude of <a href="/wiki/System_of_measurement" title="System of measurement">systems of units</a> used to be very common. Now there is a global standard, the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a> (SI), the modern form of the <a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">metric system</a>.
</p><p>In trade, <b>weights and measures</b> is often a subject of governmental regulation, to ensure fairness and transparency. The <a href="/wiki/International_Bureau_of_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Bureau of Weights and Measures">International Bureau of Weights and Measures</a> (BIPM) is tasked with ensuring worldwide uniformity of measurements and their traceability to the International System of Units (SI).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Metrology" title="Metrology">Metrology</a> is the science of developing nationally and internationally accepted units of measurement.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> and metrology, units are standards for <a href="/wiki/Measurement" title="Measurement">measurement</a> of physical quantities that need clear definitions to be useful. <a href="/wiki/Reproducibility" title="Reproducibility">Reproducibility</a> of experimental results is central to the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific method</a>. A standard system of units facilitates this. Scientific systems of units are a refinement of the concept of weights and measures historically developed for commercial purposes.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">Science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a> often use larger and smaller units of measurement than those used in everyday life. The judicious selection of the units of measurement can aid researchers in <a href="/wiki/Problem_solving" title="Problem solving">problem solving</a> (see, for example, <a href="/wiki/Dimensional_analysis" title="Dimensional analysis">dimensional analysis</a>).
</p><p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_sciences" title="Social sciences">social sciences</a>, there are no standard units of measurement and the theory and practice of measurement is studied in <a href="/wiki/Psychometrics" title="Psychometrics">psychometrics</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_conjoint_measurement" title="Theory of conjoint measurement">theory of conjoint measurement</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unit_of_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_measurement" title="History of measurement">History of measurement</a></div>
<p>A unit of measurement is a standardised <a href="/wiki/Quantity" title="Quantity">quantity</a> of a physical property, used as a factor to express occurring quantities of that property. Units of measurement were among the earliest tools invented by humans. Primitive societies needed rudimentary measures for many tasks: constructing dwellings of an appropriate size and shape, fashioning clothing, or bartering food or raw materials.
</p><p>The earliest known uniform systems of measurement seem to have all been created sometime in the <a href="/wiki/4th_millennium_BC" title="4th millennium BC">4th</a> and <a href="/wiki/3rd_millennium_BC" title="3rd millennium BC">3rd millennia BC</a> among the ancient peoples of <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Egypt</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Indus_Valley_Civilisation" title="Indus Valley Civilisation">Indus Valley</a>, and perhaps also <a href="/wiki/Elam" title="Elam">Elam</a> in <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Persia</a> as well.
</p><p>Weights and measures are mentioned in the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> (Leviticus 19:35–36). It is a commandment to be honest and have fair  measures.
</p><p>In the <i><a href="/wiki/Magna_Carta" title="Magna Carta">Magna Carta</a></i> of 1215 (The Great Charter) with the seal of <a href="/wiki/John,_King_of_England" title="John, King of England">King John</a>, put before him by the Barons of England, King John agreed in Clause 35 "There shall be one measure of wine throughout our whole realm, and one measure of ale and one measure of corn—namely, the London quart;—and one width of dyed and russet and hauberk cloths—namely, two ells below the selvage..."
</p><p>As of the 21st Century, multiple unit systems are used all over the world such as the United States Customary System, the British Customary System, and the International System. However, the United States is the only industrialized country that has not yet completely converted to the Metric System. The systematic effort to develop a universally acceptable system of units dates back to 1790 when the French National Assembly charged the French Academy of Sciences to come up such a unit system. This system was the precursor to the metric system which was quickly developed in France but did not take on universal acceptance until 1875 when The Metric Convention Treaty was signed by 17 nations. After this treaty was signed, a General Conference of Weights and Measures (CGPM) was established. The CGPM produced the current SI system which was adopted in 1954 at the 10th conference of weights and measures. Currently, the United States is a dual-system society which uses both the SI system and the US Customary system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Systems_of_units">Systems of units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unit_of_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Systems of units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/System_of_measurement" title="System of measurement">System of measurement</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_systems">Traditional systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unit_of_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Traditional systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Historically many of the systems of measurement which had been in use were to some extent based on the dimensions of the human body. As a result, units of measure could vary not only from location to location, but from person to person.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metric_systems">Metric systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unit_of_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Metric systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">Metric systems</a> of units have <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_metric_system" title="History of the metric system">evolved since the adoption of the original metric system</a> in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> in 1791. The current international standard metric system is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI" title="SI">International System of Units</a> (abbreviated to SI). An important feature of modern systems is <a href="/wiki/Standardization" title="Standardization">standardization</a>. Each unit has a universally recognized size.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tabella_conversione_metrica_1860_MG_7771.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2494" data-file-width="2438" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Tabella_conversione_metrica_1860_MG_7771.jpg/220px-Tabella_conversione_metrica_1860_MG_7771.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Tabella_conversione_metrica_1860_MG_7771.jpg/330px-Tabella_conversione_metrica_1860_MG_7771.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Tabella_conversione_metrica_1860_MG_7771.jpg/440px-Tabella_conversione_metrica_1860_MG_7771.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tabella_conversione_metrica_1860_MG_7771.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An example of <a href="/wiki/Metrication" title="Metrication">metrication</a> in 1860 when Tuscany became part of modern Italy (ex. one "libbra" = 339.54 grams)</div></div></div>
<p>Both the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_units" title="Imperial units">imperial units</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_customary_units" title="US customary units">US customary units</a> derive from earlier <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_unit" title="English unit">English units</a>. Imperial units were mostly used in the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">British Commonwealth</a> and the former <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>. US customary units are still the main system of measurement used in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> despite Congress having legally authorised metric measure on 28 July 1866.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Some steps towards US <a href="/wiki/Metrication" title="Metrication">metrication</a> have been made, particularly the redefinition of basic US and imperial units to derive exactly from SI units. Since the <a href="/wiki/International_yard_and_pound" title="International yard and pound">international yard and pound</a> agreement of 1959 the US and imperial inch is now defined as exactly <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6998254000000000000♠</span>0.0254 <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">m</a></span>, and the US and imperial avoirdupois pound is now defined as exactly <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6999453592370000000♠</span>453.592<span style="margin-left:.25em;">37</span> <a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">g</a></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_systems">Natural systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unit_of_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Natural systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While the above systems of units are based on arbitrary unit values, formalised as standards, some unit values occur naturally in science. Systems of units based on these are called <a href="/wiki/Natural_units" title="Natural units">natural units</a>. Similar to natural units, <a href="/wiki/Atomic_units" title="Atomic units">atomic units</a> (au) are a convenient <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/System_of_units" title="System of units">system of units</a> of measurement used in <a href="/wiki/Atomic_physics" title="Atomic physics">atomic physics</a>.
</p><p>Also a great number of <a href="/wiki/List_of_unusual_units_of_measurement" title="List of unusual units of measurement">unusual and non-standard units</a> may be encountered. These may include the <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass">solar mass</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7030200000000000000♠</span>2<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>30</sup> kg</span>), the <a href="/wiki/TNT_equivalent" title="TNT equivalent">megaton</a> (the energy released by detonating one million tons of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trinitrotoluene" title="Trinitrotoluene">trinitrotoluene</a>, TNT) and the <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">electronvolt</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_control_of_weights_and_measures">Legal control of weights and measures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unit_of_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legal control of weights and measures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Weights_and_Measures_Act" title="Weights and Measures Act">Weights and Measures Act</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trading_standards" title="Trading standards">Trading standards</a></div>
<p>To reduce the incidence of retail fraud, many national <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Statutes" title="Statutes">statutes</a> have standard definitions of weights and measures that may be used (hence "statute measure"), and these are verified by legal officers.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Informal_comparison_to_familiar_concepts">Informal comparison to familiar concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unit_of_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Informal comparison to familiar concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_unusual_units_of_measurement" title="List of unusual units of measurement">List of unusual units of measurement</a></div>
<p>In informal settings, a quantity may be described as multiples of that of a familiar entity, which can be easier to contextualise than a value in a formal unit system. For instance, a publication may describe an area in a foreign country as a number of multiples of the area of a region local to the readership. The propensity for certain concepts to be used frequently can give rise to loosely defined "systems" of units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Base_and_derived_units">Base and derived units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unit_of_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Base and derived units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Different systems of units are based on different choices of a set of <a href="/wiki/Base_unit_(measurement)" title="Base unit (measurement)">base units</a>.
The most widely used system of units is the International System of Units, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI" title="SI">SI</a>. There are seven <a href="/wiki/SI_base_unit" title="SI base unit">SI base units</a>. All <a href="/wiki/SI_derived_unit" title="SI derived unit">other SI units</a> can be derived from these base units.
</p><p>For most quantities a unit is necessary to communicate values of that physical quantity. For example, conveying to someone a particular length without using some sort of unit is impossible, because a length cannot be described without a reference used to make sense of the value given.
</p><p>But not all quantities require a unit of their own. Using physical laws, units of quantities can be expressed as combinations of units of other quantities. Thus only a small set of units is required. These units are taken as the <i>base units</i>. Other units are <i>derived units</i>. Derived units are a matter of convenience, as they can be expressed in terms of basic units. Which units are considered base units is a matter of choice.
</p><p>The base units of SI are actually not the smallest set possible. Smaller sets have been defined. For example, there are unit sets in which the <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">electric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a> have the same unit.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This is based on physical laws that show that electric and magnetic field are actually different manifestations of the same phenomenon.
</p>

<p>A distinction should be made between units and standards. A unit is fixed by its definition, and is independent of physical conditions such as temperature. By contrast, a standard is a physical realization of a unit, and realizes that unit only under certain physical conditions. For example, the metre is a unit, while a metal bar is a standard. One metre is the same length regardless of temperature, but a metal bar will be exactly one metre long only at a certain temperature.
</p><p>There are certain rules that have to be used when dealing with units:
</p>
<ul><li>Treat units algebraically. Only add like terms. When a unit is divided by itself, the division yields a unitless one. When two different units are multiplied or divided, the result is a new unit, referred to by the combination of the units. For instance, in SI, the unit of speed is metres per second (m/s). See <a href="/wiki/Dimensional_analysis" title="Dimensional analysis">dimensional analysis</a>. A unit can be multiplied by itself, creating a unit with an exponent (e.g. m<sup>2</sup>/s<sup>2</sup>). Put simply, units obey the laws of indices. (See <a href="/wiki/Exponentiation" title="Exponentiation">Exponentiation</a>.)</li>
<li>Some units have special names, however these should be treated like their equivalents. For example, one newton (N) is equivalent to 1 kg⋅m/s<sup>2</sup>. Thus a quantity may have several unit designations, for example: the unit for <a href="/wiki/Surface_tension" title="Surface tension">surface tension</a> can be referred to as either N/m (newtons per metre) or kg/s<sup>2</sup> (kilograms per second squared). Whether these designations are equivalent is disputed amongst metrologists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emerson_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emerson-7">[7]</a></sup></li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Real-world_implications">Real-world implications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unit_of_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Real-world implications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One example of the importance of agreed units is the failure of the <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> <a href="/wiki/Mars_Climate_Orbiter" title="Mars Climate Orbiter">Mars Climate Orbiter</a>, which was accidentally destroyed on a mission to Mars in September 1999 instead of entering orbit due to miscommunications about the value of forces: different computer programs used different units of measurement (<a href="/wiki/Newton_(unit)" title="Newton (unit)">newton</a> versus <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pound_force" title="Pound force">pound force</a>). Considerable amounts of effort, time, and money were wasted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mixups_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-mixups-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 15 April 1999, <a href="/wiki/Korean_Air" title="Korean Air">Korean Air</a> cargo <a href="/wiki/Korean_Air_Cargo_Flight_6316" title="Korean Air Cargo Flight 6316">flight 6316</a> from <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">Shanghai</a> to <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a> was lost due to the crew confusing tower instructions (in metres) and altimeter readings (in feet). Three crew and five people on the ground were killed. Thirty-seven were injured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1983, a Boeing 767 (which came to be known as the <a href="/wiki/Gimli_Glider" title="Gimli Glider">Gimli Glider</a>) ran out of fuel in mid-flight because of two mistakes in figuring the fuel supply of <a href="/wiki/Air_Canada" title="Air Canada">Air Canada</a>'s first aircraft to use metric measurements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This accident was the result of both confusion due to the simultaneous use of metric and Imperial measures and confusion of mass and volume measures.
</p>
