<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Monoculture</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">The agricultural practice of producing a single crop at a time</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For computers running identical software, see <a href="/wiki/Monoculture_(computer_science)" title="Monoculture (computer science)">Monoculture (computer science)</a>. For the sociological concept, see <a href="/wiki/Monoculturalism" title="Monoculturalism">Monoculturalism</a>.</div>
<p><b>Monoculture</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agricultural</a> practice of producing or growing a single <a href="/wiki/Crop" title="Crop">crop</a>, plant, or livestock species, variety, or breed in a field or farming system at a time. <a href="/wiki/Polyculture" title="Polyculture">Polyculture</a>, where more than one crop is grown in the same space at the same time, is the alternative to monoculture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Monoculture is widely used in both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_farming" title="Industrial farming">industrial farming</a> and <a href="/wiki/Organic_farming" title="Organic farming">organic farming</a> and has allowed increased efficiency in planting and harvest.
</p><p>Continuous monoculture, or <a href="/wiki/Monocropping" title="Monocropping">monocropping</a>, where the same species is grown year after year,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> can lead to the quicker buildup of <a href="/wiki/Pest_(organism)" title="Pest (organism)">pests</a> and <a href="/wiki/Disease" title="Disease">diseases</a>, and then rapid spread where a uniform crop is susceptible to a <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogen</a>. The practice has been criticized for its environmental effects and for putting the food supply chain <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">at risk</a>. Diversity can be added both in time, as with a crop rotation or sequence, or in space, with a <a href="/wiki/Polyculture" title="Polyculture">polyculture</a>.  
</p>

<p>Oligoculture has been suggested to describe a crop rotation of just a few crops, as is practiced by several regions of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The term monoculture is frequently applied for other uses to describe any group dominated by a single variety, e.g. social <a href="/wiki/Monoculturalism" title="Monoculturalism">monoculturalism</a>, or in the field of musicology to describe the dominance of the American and British music-industries in Western <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop music</a>, or in the field of computer science to describe a <a href="/wiki/Monoculture_(computer_science)" title="Monoculture (computer science)">group of computers</a> all running identical software.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_use">Land use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monoculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Land use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term is used in <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> and describes the practice of planting the same <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivar</a> over an extended area. Each cultivar has the same standardized planting, maintenance and harvesting requirements resulting in greater yields and lower costs.
</p><p>It also is beneficial because a crop can be tailor-planted for a location that has special problems – like <a href="/wiki/Soil_salinity" title="Soil salinity">soil salt</a> or <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a> or a short growing season.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>When a crop is matched to its well-managed environment, a monoculture can produce higher yields than a polyculture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In the last 40 years, modern practices such as monoculture planting and the use of synthesized <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a> have reduced the amount of additional land needed to produce food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MillerSpoolman2008_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-MillerSpoolman2008-6">[6]</a></sup> However, planting the same crop in the same place each year depletes the nutrients from the earth that the plant relies on and leaves soil weak and unable to support healthy plant growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monocrops_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-monocrops-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gardening_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-gardening-8">[8]</a></sup> Because soil structure and quality is so poor, farmers are forced to use chemical fertilizers to encourage plant growth and fruit production. These fertilizers, in turn, disrupt the natural makeup of the soil and contribute further to nutrient depletion. Monocropping also creates the spread of pests and diseases, which have to be treated with yet more chemicals. The effects of monocropping on the environment are severe when pesticides and fertilizers make their way into ground water or become airborne, creating pollution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monocrops_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-monocrops-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gardening_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-gardening-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forestry">Forestry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monoculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Forestry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Forestry" title="Forestry">forestry</a>, monoculture refers to the planting of one species of tree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Monoculture plantings provide greater yields<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and more efficient harvesting than natural stands of trees. Single-species stands of trees are often the natural way trees grow, but the stands show a diversity in tree sizes, with dead trees mixed with mature and young trees. In forestry, monoculture stands that are planted and harvested as a unit provide limited resources for wildlife that depend on dead trees and openings, since all the trees are the same size; they are most often harvested by <a href="/wiki/Clearcutting" title="Clearcutting">clearcutting</a>, which drastically alters the habitat. The mechanical harvesting of trees can compact soils, which can adversely affect understory growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Single-species planting also causes trees to be more vulnerable when they are infected with a pathogen, or attacked by insects,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Richardson2000_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Richardson2000-11">[11]</a></sup> or affected by adverse environmental conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lawns_and_animals">Lawns and animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monoculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lawns and animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Examples of monoculture include <a href="/wiki/Lawn" title="Lawn">lawns</a> and most fields of wheat or corn. The term is also used where a single breed of farm animal is raised in large-scale <a href="/wiki/Concentrated_animal_feeding_operation" title="Concentrated animal feeding operation">concentrated animal feeding operations</a> (CAFOs). In the United States, <a href="/wiki/The_Livestock_Conservancy" title="The Livestock Conservancy">The Livestock Conservancy</a> was formed to protect nearly 200 endangered livestock breeds from going extinct, largely due to the increased reliance on just a handful of highly specialized breeds.
</p><p><br/>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disease">Disease</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monoculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Disease">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Crops used in agriculture are usually single strains that have been <a href="/wiki/Plant_breeding" title="Plant breeding">bred</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (May 2018)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> for high yield and for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disease_resistance" title="Disease resistance">resistance to certain common diseases</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Given that all plants in a monoculture are genetically similar, if a disease strikes to which they have no resistance, it can destroy entire populations of crops. <a href="/wiki/Polyculture" title="Polyculture">Polyculture</a>, the mixing of different crops, reduces the likelihood that one or more of the crops will be resistant to any particular <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogen</a>. Studies have shown that planting a mixture of crop strains in the same field can combat disease effectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LetterstoNature2000_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-LetterstoNature2000-13">[13]</a></sup> Ending monocultures grown under disease conditions by introducing crop diversity has greatly increased yields. In one study in China, the planting of several varieties of <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a> in the same field increased yields of non-resistant strains by 89% compared to non-resistant strains grown in monoculture, largely because of a dramatic (94%) decrease in the incidence of disease, making <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a> less necessary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> As of  2009<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Monoculture&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Wheat_leaf_rust" title="Wheat leaf rust"> wheat leaf-rust</a> fungus occasioned a great deal of worry internationally, having already decimated wheat crops in Uganda and Kenya, and having started to make inroads into Asia as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Given the very genetically similar strains of much of the world's wheat crops following the <a href="/wiki/Green_Revolution" title="Green Revolution">Green Revolution</a>, the impacts of such diseases threaten agricultural production worldwide.
</p><p>In Ireland, exclusive use of one variety of potato, the "lumper", led to the <a href="/wiki/Great_Famine_(Ireland)" title="Great Famine (Ireland)"> Great Famine</a> of 1845-1849. Lumpers provided inexpensive food to feed the Irish masses. Potatoes were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vegetative_propagation" title="Vegetative propagation">propagated vegetatively</a> with little to no genetic variation. 
When <i><a href="/wiki/Phytophthora_infestans" title="Phytophthora infestans">Phytophthora infestans</a></i> arrived in Ireland from the Americas in 1845, the lumper had no resistance to the disease, leading to the nearly complete failure of the potato crop across Ireland. Had the farmers used multiple varieties of potato, the famine might not have occurred.
</p><p>Many of today's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Livestock_industry" title="Livestock industry">livestock production systems</a> rely on just a handful of highly specialized breeds. Focusing heavily on a single trait (output) may come at the expense of other desirable traits - such as <a href="/wiki/Fecundity" title="Fecundity"> fertility</a>, resistance to disease, <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Vigor_(disambiguation)" title="Vigor (disambiguation)"> vigor</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mothering" title="Mothering">mothering</a> instincts. In the early 1990s a few <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holstein_cow" title="Holstein cow">Holstein calves</a> were observed to grow poorly and died in the first 6 months of life. They were all found to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homozygous" title="Homozygous">homozygous</a> for a mutation in the gene that caused <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bovine_Leukocyte_Adhesion_Deficiency&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bovine Leukocyte Adhesion Deficiency (page does not exist)">Bovine Leukocyte Adhesion Deficiency</a>. This mutation was found at a high frequency in Holstein populations worldwide. (15% among bulls in the US, 10% in Germany, and 16% in Japan.) Resdearchers studying the pedigrees of affected and carrier animals tracked the source of the mutation to a single bull that was widely used in the industry. Note that in 1990 there were approximately 4 million Holstein cattle in the US, making the affected population around 600,000 animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Humans rely heavily on a relatively small number of food crops and farm animals for food.  If disease hits a major food crop - as happened during the 19th-century Irish potato famine - food supplies for large populations could come under threat.  Maintaining and increasing <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> in agriculture could help safeguard world food-supplies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sociology">Sociology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monoculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sociology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Monocultural land-use can have effects at the level of <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Seasonal peaks in <a href="/wiki/Harvest" title="Harvest">harvesting</a> activity can place demands on labor availability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polyculture">Polyculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monoculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Polyculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Polyculture" title="Polyculture">Polyculture</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Environmental_movement" title="Environmental movement">environmental movement</a> seeks to change popular culture by redefining the "perfect lawn" to be something other than a turf monoculture, and seeks <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_policy" title="Agricultural policy">agricultural policy</a> that provides greater encouragement for more diverse cropping systems. <a href="/wiki/Local_food" title="Local food">Local food</a> systems may also encourage growing multiple species and a wide variety of crops at the same time and same place. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heirloom_gardening" title="Heirloom gardening">Heirloom gardening</a> and raising heritage livestock breeds have come about largely as a reaction against monocultures in agriculture.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
