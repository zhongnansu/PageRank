<p><b>Social policy</b> is <a href="/wiki/Policy" title="Policy">policy</a> usually within a governmental or political setting, such as the <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state" title="Welfare state">welfare state</a> and study of <a href="/wiki/Social_services" title="Social services">social services</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Social policy consists of guidelines, principles, legislation and activities that affect the living conditions conducive to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_welfare" title="Human welfare">human welfare</a>, such as a person's <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a>. The Department of Social Policy at the <a href="/wiki/London_School_of_Economics" title="London School of Economics">London School of Economics</a> defines social policy as "an interdisciplinary and applied subject concerned with the analysis of societies' responses to social need", which seeks to foster in its students a capacity to understand theory and evidence drawn from a wide range of social science disciplines, including economics, sociology, psychology, geography, history, law, philosophy and political science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The Malcolm Wiener Center for Social Policy at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> describes social policy as "public policy and practice in the areas of <a href="/wiki/Health_policy" title="Health policy">health care</a>, human services, <a href="/wiki/Criminal_justice" title="Criminal justice">criminal justice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_inequality" title="Social inequality">inequality</a>, <a href="/wiki/Education_policy" title="Education policy">education</a>, and labor".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Social policy might also be described as actions that affect the well-being of members of a society through shaping the distribution of and access to goods and resources in that society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Social policy often deals with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wicked_problems" title="Wicked problems">wicked problems</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The discussion of "social policy" in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> can also apply to governmental policy on social issues such as tackling <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racism</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/LGBT" title="LGBT">LGBT</a> issues (such as <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage" title="Same-sex marriage">same-sex marriage</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and the legal status of <a href="/wiki/Abortion" title="Abortion">abortion</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guns" title="Guns">guns</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Euthanasia" title="Euthanasia">euthanasia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recreational_drugs" title="Recreational drugs">recreational drugs</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Prostitution" title="Prostitution">prostitution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Social_policy&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span> <small><i>(May 2015)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The earliest example of direct intervention by government in human welfare dates back to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umar_ibn_al-Khatt%C4%81b" title="Umar ibn al-Khattāb">Umar ibn al-Khattāb</a>'s rule as the second <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caliph" title="Caliph">caliph</a> of Islam in the 6th century. He used <i><a href="/wiki/Zakat" title="Zakat">zakat</a></i> collections and also other governmental resources to establish pensions, income support, child benefits, and various stipends for people of the non-Muslim community<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.
</p><p>In the West, proponents of scientific social planning such as the sociologist <a href="/wiki/Auguste_Comte" title="Auguste Comte">Auguste Comte</a>, and social researchers, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_Booth_(philanthropist)" title="Charles Booth (philanthropist)">Charles Booth</a>, contributed to the emergence of social policy in the first industrialised countries following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a>. Surveys of poverty exposing the brutal conditions in the urban <a href="/wiki/Slum" title="Slum">slum</a> conurbations of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victorian_Britain" title="Victorian Britain">Victorian Britain</a> supplied the pressure leading to changes such as the <a href="/wiki/Decline_and_abolition_of_the_poor_law_system" title="Decline and abolition of the poor law system">decline and abolition of the poor law system</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liberal_welfare_reforms" title="Liberal welfare reforms">Liberal welfare reforms</a>. Other significant examples in the development of social policy are the <a href="/wiki/Otto_von_Bismarck#Social_legislation" title="Otto von Bismarck">Bismarckian welfare state</a> in 19th century <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_Security_(United_States)#History" title="Social Security (United States)">social security policies in the United States</a> introduced under the rubric of the <a href="/wiki/New_Deal" title="New Deal">New Deal</a> between 1933 and 1935, and the <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service_Act_1946" title="National Health Service Act 1946">National Health Service Act 1946</a> in Britain.
</p><p>Social policy in the 21st century is complex and in each state it is subject to <a href="/wiki/Local_government" title="Local government">local</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_government" title="Central government">national governments</a>, as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supranational" title="Supranational">supranational</a> political influence. For example, membership of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> is conditional on member states' adherence to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Law_of_the_European_Union#Social_chapter" title="Law of the European Union">Social Chapter</a> of European Union law <span class="clarify-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_laws" title="International laws">international laws</a>.</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Clarify" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="This passage needs to be better explained. (May 2015)">clarify</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Welfare_State" title="Welfare State">Welfare State</a> and <a href="/wiki/Welfare" title="Welfare">Welfare</a></div>
<p>Social policy is an academic discipline focusing on the systematic evaluation of societies' responses to social need. It was established in the early-to-mid part of the 20th century as a complement to <a href="/wiki/Social_work" title="Social work">social work</a> studies. One can reasonably argue there is not a single clear and comprehensive definition of social policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> This is probably because social policy is more an area of study than a discipline, and also because the meaning of social policy has constantly evolved over time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> For this reason, the founding fathers of the discipline (mainly sociologists, such as <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Humphrey_Marshall" title="Thomas Humphrey Marshall">Thomas Humphrey Marshall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Titmuss" title="Richard Titmuss">Titmuss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Townsend_(sociologist)" title="Peter Townsend (sociologist)">Peter Townsend (sociologist)</a> and historians, such as <a href="/wiki/Asa_Briggs" title="Asa Briggs">Asa Briggs</a>) mainly defined the domain by looking at its aims: to reduce <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a>, insure against social risks, provide  <a href="/wiki/Equal_opportunity" title="Equal opportunity">equal opportunity</a> for all, enhance <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a>, and foster the expansion of social citizenship and social rights.
</p><p>Scholars studied and categorized social security systems on the basis of their 'modes of intervention' (universalism, social insurance, social assistance). Of course, each welfare state system includes a mixture of these elements, but certain systems are geared toward universal principles, i.e. Sweden and Denmark. Others emphasize social insurance mechanisms, i.e. Germany and France. Still other focus on social assistance for the poor, i.e. the United Kingdom .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Social policy aims to improve human welfare and to meet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_need" title="Human need">human needs</a> for education, health, housing and economic security. Important areas of social policy are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wellbeing" title="Wellbeing">wellbeing</a> and welfare, poverty reduction,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Social_security" title="Social security">social security</a>, <a href="/wiki/Justice" title="Justice">justice</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unemployment_insurance" title="Unemployment insurance">unemployment insurance</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Living_conditions" title="Living conditions">living conditions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Animal_rights" title="Animal rights">animal rights</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pensions" title="Pensions">pensions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Health_care" title="Health care">health care</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_housing" title="Social housing">social housing</a>, family policy, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_care" title="Social care">social care</a>, <a href="/wiki/Child_protection" title="Child protection">child protection</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_exclusion" title="Social exclusion">social exclusion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Education_policy" title="Education policy">education policy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">crime</a> and <a href="/wiki/Criminal_justice" title="Criminal justice">criminal justice</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urban_development" title="Urban development">urban development</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contract_labour" title="Contract labour">labor</a> issues.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_social_policy">United States social policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: United States social policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:William_Jennings_Bryan,_1860-1925.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1438" data-file-width="1080" height="213" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/William_Jennings_Bryan%2C_1860-1925.jpg/160px-William_Jennings_Bryan%2C_1860-1925.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/William_Jennings_Bryan%2C_1860-1925.jpg/240px-William_Jennings_Bryan%2C_1860-1925.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/William_Jennings_Bryan%2C_1860-1925.jpg/320px-William_Jennings_Bryan%2C_1860-1925.jpg 2x" width="160"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:William_Jennings_Bryan,_1860-1925.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Secretary_of_State" title="U.S. Secretary of State">U.S. Secretary of State</a> <a href="/wiki/William_Jennings_Bryan" title="William Jennings Bryan">William Jennings Bryan</a> was the first major U.S. political figure to incorporate formal social policy into official government decisions, a champion of <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">social justice</a>.  Bryan is pictured in 1908.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Socialsecurityheadquarters.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2055" data-file-width="3137" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Socialsecurityheadquarters.jpg/220px-Socialsecurityheadquarters.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Socialsecurityheadquarters.jpg/330px-Socialsecurityheadquarters.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Socialsecurityheadquarters.jpg/440px-Socialsecurityheadquarters.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Socialsecurityheadquarters.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Social_Security_Administration" title="Social Security Administration">Social Security Administration</a> headquarters is in Woodlawn, Maryland.</div></div></div>
<p>Religious, racial, ideological, scientific and philosophical movements and ideas have historically influenced American social policy, for example, <a href="/wiki/John_Calvin" title="John Calvin">John Calvin</a> and his idea of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-destination" title="Pre-destination">pre-destination</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> Values of hard work and <a href="/wiki/Individualism" title="Individualism">individualism</a>. Moreover, <a href="/wiki/Social_Darwinism" title="Social Darwinism">Social Darwinism</a> helped mold America's ideas of <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Survival_of_the_fittest" title="Survival of the fittest">survival of the fittest</a></i> mentality. The Catholic Church's social teaching has also been considerably influential to the development of social policy.
</p><p>United States politicians who have favored increasing government observance of social policy often do not frame their proposals around typical notions of welfare or benefits; instead, in cases like <i><a href="/wiki/Medicare_(United_States)" title="Medicare (United States)">Medicare</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Medicaid" title="Medicaid">Medicaid</a></i>, President <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lyndon_Johnson" title="Lyndon Johnson">Lyndon Johnson</a> presented a package called the <a href="/wiki/Great_Society" title="Great Society">Great Society</a> that framed a larger vision around <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a>. <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">Insurance</a> has been a growing policy topic, and a recent example of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Health_care_law" title="Health care law">health care law</a> as social policy is the <a href="/wiki/Patient_Protection_and_Affordable_Care_Act" title="Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act">Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act</a> formed by the 111th U.S. Congress and signed into law by President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democrat</a>, on March 23, 2010.
</p><p>Moreover, former president <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a>'s ground breaking <a href="/wiki/New_Deal" title="New Deal">New Deal</a> is a paragon example of Social Policy that focused predominantly on a program of providing work and stimulating the economy through public spending on projects, rather than on cash payment. The programs were in response to the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> affecting the United States in the 1930s.
</p><p>An American <a href="/wiki/Education_policy" title="Education policy">education policy</a> proposal from a <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a> president is the <a href="/wiki/No_Child_Left_Behind_Act" title="No Child Left Behind Act">No Child Left Behind Act</a>. This Act took effect on January 8, 2002, and was put in place to raise standards in education so different individuals can have better educational outcomes. The No Child Left Behind Act requires every state to <a href="/wiki/Standards-based_education_reform_in_the_United_States" title="Standards-based education reform in the United States">assess students on basic skills</a> to receive federal funding. However, this Act did not create a national standard since each state develops their own set of standards and assessments. Critics of most all social policies point toward a depiction of a <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state" title="Welfare state">welfare state</a> on the make of a Hobbesian <a href="/wiki/Leviathan_(Hobbes_book)" title="Leviathan (Hobbes book)">Leviathan</a>.
</p>
