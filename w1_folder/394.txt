<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Alkalinity</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">The capacity of water to resist changes in pH that would make the water more acidic</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Alkali" title="Alkali">alkali</a> or <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">base (chemistry)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WOA05_GLODAP_pd_ALK_AYool.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1133" data-file-width="1612" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/WOA05_GLODAP_pd_ALK_AYool.png/200px-WOA05_GLODAP_pd_ALK_AYool.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/WOA05_GLODAP_pd_ALK_AYool.png/300px-WOA05_GLODAP_pd_ALK_AYool.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/WOA05_GLODAP_pd_ALK_AYool.png/400px-WOA05_GLODAP_pd_ALK_AYool.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WOA05_GLODAP_pd_ALK_AYool.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sea surface alkalinity (from the <a href="/wiki/Global_Ocean_Data_Analysis_Project" title="Global Ocean Data Analysis Project">GLODAP</a> <a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">climatology</a>).</div></div></div>
<p><b>Alkalinity</b> (from Arabic "<i>al-qalī</i>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) is the capacity of water to resist changes in <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> that would make the water more <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acidic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> (It should not be confused with <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">basicity</a> which is an absolute measurement on the pH scale.) Alkalinity is the strength of a <a href="/wiki/Buffer_solution" title="Buffer solution">buffer solution</a> composed of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weak_acid" title="Weak acid">weak acids</a> and their <a href="/wiki/Conjugate_acid" title="Conjugate acid">conjugate bases</a>. It is measured by titrating the solution with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monoprotic_acid" title="Monoprotic acid">monoprotic acid</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HCl" title="HCl">HCl</a> until its pH changes abruptly, or it reaches a known endpoint where that happens. Alkalinity is expressed in units of meq/L  (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milliequivalents" title="Milliequivalents">milliequivalents</a> per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liter" title="Liter">liter</a>), which corresponds to the amount of monoprotic acid added as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titrant" title="Titrant">titrant</a> in millimoles per liter.
</p><p>Although alkalinity is primarily a term used by oceanographers it is also used by hydrologists. For instance, measuring alkalinity is important in determining a stream's ability to neutralize acidic pollution from <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">rainfall</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a>. It is one of the best measures of the sensitivity of the stream to acid inputs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> There can be long-term changes in the alkalinity of streams and rivers in response to human disturbances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10.1021/es401046s_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-10.1021/es401046s-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alkalinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1884, Professor <a href="/wiki/William_Dittmar" title="William Dittmar"> Wilhelm (William) Dittmar</a> of Anderson College, now the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Strathclyde" title="University of Strathclyde">University of Strathclyde</a>, analysed 77 pristine seawater samples from around the world brought back by the <a href="/wiki/Challenger_expedition" title="Challenger expedition">Challenger expedition</a>. He found that in seawater the major ions were in a fixed ratio, confirming the hypothesis of <a href="/wiki/Johan_Georg_Forchhammer" title="Johan Georg Forchhammer">Johan Georg Forchhammer</a>, that is now known as the Principle of Constant Proportions. However, there was one exception. Dittmar found that the concentration of calcium was slightly greater in the deep ocean, and named this increase alkalinity.
</p><p>1884 was also the year when <a href="/wiki/Svante_Arrhenius" title="Svante Arrhenius">Svante Arrhenius</a> submitted his PhD theses in which he advocated the existence of <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> in solution, and defined acids as <a href="/wiki/Hydronium" title="Hydronium">hydronium</a> ion donors and bases as <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxide</a> ions donors. For that work, he received the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1903.
</p><p>Thus Dittmar's alkalinity is the hydronium <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">cations</a> which exist to balance electrically the increase in calcium <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">anions</a> in deep ocean water, although now the meaning alkalinity has expanded.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Simplified_summary">Simplified summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alkalinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Simplified summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Alkalinity roughly refers to the amount of bases in a solution that can be converted to uncharged species by a strong acid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>  The cited author, James Drever, provides an equation expressed in terms of molar equivalents, which means the number of moles of each ion type multiplied by (the absolute value of) the charge of the ion.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (February 2015)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata"> – <a href="/wiki/Talk:Alkalinity#Dubious" title="Talk:Alkalinity">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> For example, 1 mole of HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>1−</sup> in solution represents 1 molar equivalent, while 1 mole of CO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup> is 2 molar equivalents because twice as many H<sup>+</sup> ions would be necessary to balance the charge. The total charge of a solution always equals zero.
</p><p>Quoting from page 52, "Ions such as Na<sup>+</sup>, K<sup>+</sup>, Ca<sup>2+</sup>, Mg<sup>2+</sup>, Cl<sup> −</sup>, SO<sub>4</sub><sup>2−</sup>, and NO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup> can be regarded as "<i>conservative</i>" in the sense that their concentrations are unaffected by changes in the pH, pressure, or temperature (within the ranges normally encountered near the earth's surface and assuming no precipitation or dissolution of solid phases, or biological transformations)."
</p><p>On the left-hand side of the equation is the sum of conservative cations minus the sum of conservative anions. Balancing this on the right side is the sum of the anions that could be neutralized by added H<sup>+</sup> ions (non-conservative anions) minus H<sup>+</sup> ions already present, as indicated by the pH.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="&quot;What about other cations? Are all cations except H^+ 'conservative ' ?&quot; (February 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> All numbers are molar equivalents.
</p><p>This right side term is called <b>total alkalinity</b>.  It is, quoting Drever, "formally defined as the equivalent sum of the bases that are titratable with strong acid (Stumm and Morgan, 1981)".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  The listing of ions shown on the right in Drever was "<i>m</i><sub>HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup></sub> + 2<i>m</i><sub>CO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup></sub> + <i>m</i><sub>B(OH)<sub>4</sub><sup>−</sup></sub> + <i>m</i><sub>H<sub>3</sub>(SiO)<sub>4</sub><sup>−</sup></sub> + <i>m</i><sub>HS<sup>−</sup></sub> + <i>m</i><sub><i>organic anions</i></sub> + <i>m</i><sub>OH<sup>−</sup></sub> - <i>m</i><sub>H<sup>+</sup></sub>".  Total alkalinity is measured by adding a strong acid until all the anions listed above are converted to uncharged species.  The total alkalinity is not (much) affected by temperature, pressure, or pH, though the values of individual constituents are, mostly being conversions between HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup> and CO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup>.
</p><p>Drever further notes that in most natural waters, all anions except HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup> and CO<sub>3</sub><sup>−2</sup> have low concentrations.  Thus <i><b>carbonate alkalinity</b></i>, which is equal to <i>m</i><sub>HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup></sub> + 2<i>m</i><sub>CO<sub>3</sub><sup>−2</sup></sub> is also approximately equal to the total alkalinity.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Detailed_description">Detailed description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alkalinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Detailed description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Alkalinity</b> or <b>A<sub>T</sub></b> measures the ability of a solution to neutralize <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Equivalence_point" title="Equivalence point">equivalence point</a> of carbonate or bicarbonate. The alkalinity is equal to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stoichiometric" title="Stoichiometric">stoichiometric</a> sum of the <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">bases</a> in solution.  In the natural environment <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonate_alkalinity" title="Carbonate alkalinity">carbonate alkalinity</a> tends to make up most of the total alkalinity due to the common occurrence and dissolution of <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonate</a> rocks and presence of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> in the atmosphere. Other common natural components that can contribute to alkalinity include <a href="/wiki/Borate" title="Borate">borate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">silicate</a>, dissolved <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Conjugate_acid" title="Conjugate acid">conjugate bases</a> of some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_acids" title="Organic acids">organic acids</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfate</a>. Solutions produced in a laboratory may contain a virtually limitless number of bases that contribute to alkalinity. Alkalinity is usually given in the unit mEq/L (milliequivalent per liter). Commercially, as in the swimming pool industry, alkalinity might also be given in parts per million of equivalent calcium carbonate (ppm CaCO<sub>3</sub>).
</p><p>Alkalinity is sometimes incorrectly used interchangeably with <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">basicity</a>.   For example, the addition of CO<sub>2</sub> lowers the pH of a solution. This increase reduces the basicity; however, the alkalinity remains unchanged (<a href="/wiki/Alkalinity#Addition_of_CO2" title="Alkalinity">see example below</a>).For total alkalinity testing, N/10 H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> is used by hydrologists along with phenolphthalein indicator.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theoretical_treatment">Theoretical treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alkalinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Theoretical treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In typical <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> or <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a>, the measured alkalinity is set equal to:
</p>
<dl><dd>A<sub>T</sub> = [HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>]<sub>T</sub> + 2[CO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup>]<sub>T</sub> + [B(OH)<sub>4</sub><sup>−</sup>]<sub>T</sub> + [OH<sup>−</sup>]<sub>T</sub> + 2[PO<sub>4</sub><sup>3−</sup>]<sub>T</sub> + [HPO<sub>4</sub><sup>2−</sup>]<sub>T</sub> + [SiO(OH)<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>]<sub>T</sub> − [H<sup>+</sup>]<sub>sws</sub> − [HSO<sub>4</sub><sup>−</sup>]</dd></dl>
<p>(Subscript T indicates the total concentration of the species in the solution as measured. This is opposed to the free concentration, which takes into account the significant amount of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ion_pair" title="Ion pair">ion pair</a> interactions that occur in seawater.)
</p><p>Alkalinity can be measured by titrating a sample with a strong acid until all the buffering capacity of the aforementioned ions above the pH of bicarbonate or carbonate is consumed. This point is functionally set to pH 4.5. At this point, all the bases of interest have been protonated to the zero level species, hence they no longer cause alkalinity. In the carbonate system the bicarbonate ions [HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>] and the carbonate ions [CO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup>] have become converted to carbonic acid [H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>] at this pH. This pH is also called the CO<sub>2</sub> equivalence point where the major component in water is dissolved CO<sub>2</sub> which is converted to H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> in an aqueous solution. There are no strong acids or bases at this point. Therefore, the alkalinity is modeled and quantified with respect to the CO<sub>2</sub> equivalence point. Because the alkalinity is measured with respect to the CO<sub>2</sub> equivalence point, the dissolution of CO<sub>2</sub>, although it adds acid and dissolved inorganic carbon, does not change the alkalinity. In natural conditions, the dissolution of basic rocks and addition of ammonia [NH<sub>3</sub>] or organic amines leads to the addition of base to natural waters at the CO<sub>2</sub> equivalence point. The dissolved base in water increases the pH and titrates an equivalent amount of CO<sub>2</sub> to bicarbonate ion and carbonate ion. At equilibrium, the water contains a certain amount of alkalinity contributed by the concentration of weak acid anions. Conversely, the addition of acid converts weak acid anions to CO<sub>2</sub> and continuous addition of strong acids can cause the alkalinity to become less than zero.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> For example, the following reactions take place during the addition of acid to a typical seawater solution:
</p>
<dl><dd>B(OH)<sub>4</sub><sup>−</sup> + H<sup>+</sup> → B(OH)<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>OH<sup>−</sup> + H<sup>+</sup> → H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>PO<sub>4</sub><sup>−3</sup> + 2H<sup>+</sup> → H<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>4</sub><sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<dl><dd>HPO<sub>4</sub><sup>−2</sup> + H<sup>+</sup> → H<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>4</sub><sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<dl><dd>[SiO(OH)<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>] + H<sup>+</sup> → [Si(OH)<sub>4</sub><sup>0</sup>]</dd></dl>
<p>It can be seen from the above protonation reactions that most bases consume one proton (H<sup>+</sup>) to become a neutral species, thus increasing alkalinity by one per equivalent. CO<sub>3</sub><sup>−2</sup> however, will consume two protons before becoming a zero level species (CO<sub>2</sub>), thus it increases alkalinity by two per mole of CO<sub>3</sub><sup>−2</sup>. [H<sup>+</sup>] and [HSO<sub>4</sub><sup>−</sup>] decrease alkalinity, as they act as sources of protons. They are often represented collectively as [H<sup>+</sup>]<sub>T</sub>.
</p><p>Alkalinity is typically reported as mg/L <i>as</i> CaCO<sub>3</sub>.  (The conjunction "as" is appropriate in this case because the alkalinity results from a mixture of ions but is reported "as if" all of this is due to CaCO<sub>3</sub>.)  This can be converted into milliEquivalents per Liter (mEq/L) by dividing by 50 (the approximate <a href="/wiki/Molar_mass" title="Molar mass">MW</a> of CaCO<sub>3</sub>/2).
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sum_of_contributing_species">Sum of contributing species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alkalinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sum of contributing species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Addition_of_CO2">Addition of CO<sub>2</sub></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alkalinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Addition of CO2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Addition (or removal) of CO<sub>2</sub> to a solution does not change its alkalinity, since the net reaction produces the same number of equivalents of positively contributing species (H<sup>+</sup>) as negative contributing species (HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup> and/or CO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup>). Adding CO<sub>2</sub> to the solution lowers its pH, but does not affect alkalinity.
</p><p>At all pH values:
</p>
<dl><dd>CO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O ⇌ HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup> + H<sup>+</sup></dd></dl>
<p>Only at high (basic) pH values:
</p>
<dl><dd>HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup> + H<sup>+</sup> ⇌ CO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup> + 2H<sup>+</sup></dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dissolution_of_carbonate_rock">Dissolution of carbonate rock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alkalinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Dissolution of carbonate rock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Addition of CO<sub>2</sub> to a solution in contact with a solid can (over time) affect the alkalinity, especially for carbonate minerals in contact with groundwater or seawater . The dissolution (or precipitation) of carbonate rock has a strong influence on the alkalinity. This is because carbonate rock is composed of CaCO<sub>3</sub> and its dissociation will add Ca<sup>+2</sup> and CO<sub>3</sub><sup>−2</sup> into solution. Ca<sup>+2</sup> will not influence alkalinity, but CO<sub>3</sub><sup>−2</sup> will increase alkalinity by 2 units.  Increased dissolution of carbonate rock by acidification from acid rain and mining has contributed to increased alkalinity concentrations in some major rivers throughout the Eastern U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10.1021/es401046s_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-10.1021/es401046s-4">[4]</a></sup> The following reaction shows how acid rain, containing sulfuric acid, can have the effect of increasing river alkalinity by increasing the amount of bicarbonate ion:
</p>
<dl><dd>2CaCO<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> → 2Ca<sup>+2</sup> + 2HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup> + SO<sub>4</sub><sup>−2</sup></dd></dl>
<p>Another way of writing this is:
</p>
<dl><dd>CaCO<sub>3</sub> + H<sup>+</sup> ⇌ Ca<sup>+2</sup> + HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<p>The lower the pH, the higher the concentration of bicarbonate will be. This shows how a lower pH can lead to higher alkalinity if the amount of bicarbonate produced is greater than the amount of H<sup>+</sup> remaining after the reaction. This is the case since the amount of acid in the rainwater is low. If this alkaline groundwater later comes into contact with the atmosphere, it can lose CO<sub>2</sub>, precipitate carbonate, and thereby become less alkaline again. When carbonate minerals, water, and the atmosphere are all in equilibrium, the reversible reaction
</p>
<dl><dd>CaCO<sub>3</sub> + 2H<sup>+</sup> ⇌ Ca<sup>+2</sup> + CO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>shows that pH will be related to calcium ion concentration, with lower pH going with higher calcium ion concentration. In this case, the higher the pH, the more bicarbonate and carbonate ion there will be, in contrast to the paradoxical situation described above, where one does not have equilibrium with the atmosphere.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceanic_alkalinity">Oceanic alkalinity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alkalinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Oceanic alkalinity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Processes_that_increase_alkalinity">Processes that increase alkalinity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alkalinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Processes that increase alkalinity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are many methods of alkalinity generation in the ocean.  Perhaps the most well known is the dissolution of CaCO<sub>3</sub> (calcium carbonate, which is a component of coral reefs) to form Ca<sup>2+</sup> and CO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup> (carbonate).  The carbonate ion has the potential to absorb two hydrogen ions.  Therefore, it causes a net increase in ocean alkalinity.  Calcium carbonate dissolution is an indirect result of ocean pH lowering.  It can cause great damage to <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reef</a> ecosystems, but has a relatively low effect on the total alkalinity (A<sub>T</sub>) in the ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-8">[8]</a></sup> Lowering of pH due to absorption of CO<sub>2</sub> actually raises the alkalinity by causing dissolution of carbonates.
</p><p>Anaerobic degradation processes, such as denitrification and sulfate reduction, have a much greater impact on oceanic alkalinity.  Denitrification and sulfate reduction occur in the deep ocean, where there is an absence of oxygen.  Both of these processes consume hydrogen ions and releases quasi-inert gases (N<sub>2</sub> or H<sub>2</sub>S), which eventually escape into the atmosphere.  This consumption of H<sup>+</sup> increases the alkalinity.  It has been estimated that anaerobic degradation could be as much as 60% of the total oceanic alkalinity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Processes_that_decrease_alkalinity">Processes that decrease alkalinity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alkalinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Processes that decrease alkalinity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Anaerobic processes generally increase alkalinity.  Conversely, aerobic degradation can decrease A<sub>T</sub>.  This process occurs in portions of the ocean where oxygen is present (surface waters).  It results in dissolved organic matter and the production of hydrogen ions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-8">[8]</a></sup> An increase in H<sup>+</sup> clearly decreases alkalinity.  However, the dissolved organic matter may have base functional groups that can consume these hydrogen ions and negate their effect on alkalinity.  Therefore, aerobic degradation has a relatively low impact on the overall oceanic alkalinity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>All of these aforementioned methods are chemical processes.  However, physical processes can also serve to affect A<sub>T</sub>.  The melting of polar ice caps is a growing concern that can serve to decrease oceanic alkalinity.  If the ice were to melt, then the overall volume of the ocean would increase.  Because alkalinity is a concentration value (mol/L), increasing the volume would theoretically serve to decrease A<sub>T</sub>.  However, the actual effect would be much more complicated than this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_temporal_variability">Global temporal variability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alkalinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Global temporal variability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Researchers have shown oceanic alkalinity to vary over time.  Because A<sub>T</sub> is calculated from the ions in the ocean, a change in the chemical composition would alter alkalinity.  One way this can occur is through ocean acidification.  However, oceanic alkalinity is relatively stable, so significant changes can only occur over long time scales (i.e. hundreds to thousands of years).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> As a result, seasonal and annual variability is generally very low.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spatial_variability">Spatial variability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alkalinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Spatial variability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Researchers have also shown alkalinity to vary depending on location.  Local A<sub>T</sub> can be affected by two main mixing patterns: current and river.  Current dominated mixing occurs close to the shore in areas with strong water flow.  In these areas, alkalinity trends follow current and have a segmented relationship with salinity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>River dominated mixing also occurs close to the shore; it is strongest close to the mouth of a large river (i.e. the Mississippi or Amazon).  Here, the rivers can act as either a source or a sink of alkalinity.  A<sub>T</sub> follows the outflow of the river and has a linear relationship with salinity.  This mixing pattern is most important in late winter and spring, because snowmelt increases the river’s outflow.  As the season progresses into summer, river processes are less significant, and current mixing can become the dominant process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Oceanic alkalinity also follows general trends based on latitude and depth.  It has been shown that A<sub>T</sub> is often inversely proportional to sea surface temperature (SST).  Therefore, it generally increases with high latitudes and depths.  As a result, upwelling areas (where water from the deep ocean is pushed to the surface) also have higher alkalinity values.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
