<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Petra Nova</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>Petra Nova</b> project is a coal-energy-sector/ clean-energy project designed to reduce carbon emissions from one of the boilers of a <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_power_station" title="Fossil fuel power station">coal burning power plant</a> in <a href="/wiki/Thompsons,_Texas" title="Thompsons, Texas">Thompsons, Texas</a>.  It is a multi-million dollar project taken up by <a href="/wiki/NRG_Energy" title="NRG Energy">NRG Energy</a> and <a href="/wiki/JXTG_Nippon_Oil_%26_Energy" title="JXTG Nippon Oil &amp; Energy">JX Nippon Oil</a> to retrofit one of the boilers at their <a href="/wiki/WA_Parish_Generating_Station" title="WA Parish Generating Station">WA Parish Generating Station</a> with a post-combustion <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_capture" title="Carbon capture">carbon capture</a> treatment system to treat a portion of the atmospheric exhaust emissions from the retrofitted boiler.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The plant entered commercial service in 1977, and the new carbon emissions reduction system was first put into operation on January 10, 2017. The project is designed to annually capture approximately 33% of the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) (or 1.6 million tonnes) emissions from the plant's boiler #8.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The carbon dioxide gas is captured at 99% purity, and is then compressed and piped about 82 miles to the West Ranch Oil Field, where it is used for <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">enhanced oil recovery</a>. The oil field had previously been producing 300 barrels of oil per day.  With the new injection of high pressure carbon dioxide into the field, the oil production of the field was increased by a factor of 50 to 15,000 barrels per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup> This project is expected to run for at least another 20 years. To satisfy the Clean Coal Power Initiative requirements, the Texas Bureau of Economic Geology has put a monitoring plan in place to keep track of the injection and movement of the CO<sub>2</sub> beneath the surface and in the rock structures at West Ranch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology">Technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Petra_Nova&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Petra Nova carbon emissions reduction system uses an <a href="/wiki/Amine_gas_treating" title="Amine gas treating">amine-based</a> <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(chemistry)" title="Absorption (chemistry)">absorption</a> system, or the KM CDR Process (Kansai Mitsubishi Carbon Dioxide Recovery). This process was developed by <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi" title="Mitsubishi">Mitsubishi</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kansai_Electric_Power" title="Kansai Electric Power">Kansai Electric Power</a> and utilizes a high performing proprietary solvent called KS-1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The CO<sub>2</sub> is removed from the exhaust gas through a basic absorber-stripper system. The gaseous CO<sub>2</sub> is then compressed to a <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_carbon_dioxide" title="Supercritical carbon dioxide">supercritical liquid</a>. The CO<sub>2</sub> leaving the carbon capture plant is over 99% pure and is sent 82 miles through 12-inch diameter pipes to their end location of the West Ranch oil field, where it is used for <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">enhanced oil recovery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup> The carbon dioxide from the Petra Nova Initiative will eventually end up in sandstone in the Frio Formation of the West Ranch oil field. It will remain about 5,000 feet underground and cover more than 4,000 acres of ground area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">Economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Petra_Nova&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Petra Nova carbon emissions reduction system costed approximately $1 billion to install, and received a nearly $190 million grant from the U.S. Government<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> under the Clean Coal Initiative, as well as a $250 million loan from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_government" title="Japanese government">Japanese government</a>. The increased oil recovery in the adjacent oil field was expected to result in a net savings. However, when the project was first proposed oil prices were very high (at $100 per barrel) and it was assumed that they would not drop. As of 2017, the current oil price is about $50 per barrel and therefore a net loss has resulted regarding the oil production at the field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
