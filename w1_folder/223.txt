<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Organolithium reagent</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Organolithium reagents</b> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organometallic" title="Organometallic">organometallic</a> compounds that contain <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> – <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a> bonds. They are important reagents in <a href="/wiki/Organic_synthesis" title="Organic synthesis">organic synthesis</a>, and are frequently used to transfer the organic group or the lithium atom to the substrates in synthetic steps, through nucleophilic addition or simple deprotonation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-analytical_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-analytical-1">[1]</a></sup> Organolithium reagents are used in industry as an initiator for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anionic_polymerization" title="Anionic polymerization">anionic polymerization</a>, which leads to the production of various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elastomers" title="Elastomers">elastomers</a>. They have also been applied in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asymmetric_synthesis" title="Asymmetric synthesis">asymmetric synthesis</a> in the pharmaceutical industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orglipharm_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-orglipharm-2">[2]</a></sup> Due to the large difference in <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">electronegativity</a> between the carbon atom and the lithium atom, the C-Li bond is highly <a href="/wiki/Ionization" title="Ionization">ionic</a>. Owing to the polar nature of the C-Li bond, organolithium reagents are good <a href="/wiki/Nucleophile" title="Nucleophile">nucleophiles</a> and strong bases. For laboratory organic synthesis, many organolithium reagents are commercially available in solution form. These reagents are highly reactive, and are sometimes <a href="/wiki/Pyrophoricity" title="Pyrophoricity">pyrophoric</a>. </p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Glasbottles_containing_Butyllithium,_Buli.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="532" data-file-width="800" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Glasbottles_containing_Butyllithium%2C_Buli.jpg/220px-Glasbottles_containing_Butyllithium%2C_Buli.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Glasbottles_containing_Butyllithium%2C_Buli.jpg/330px-Glasbottles_containing_Butyllithium%2C_Buli.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Glasbottles_containing_Butyllithium%2C_Buli.jpg/440px-Glasbottles_containing_Butyllithium%2C_Buli.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Glasbottles_containing_Butyllithium,_Buli.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Glass bottles containing butyllithium</div></div></div>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_development">History and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organolithium_reagent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Studies of organolithium reagents began in the 1930 and were pioneered by <a href="/wiki/Karl_Ziegler" title="Karl Ziegler">Karl Ziegler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georg_Wittig" title="Georg Wittig">Georg Wittig</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Henry_Gilman" title="Henry Gilman">Henry Gilman</a>. In comparison with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grignard_reagents" title="Grignard reagents">Grignard reagents</a>, organolithium reagents can often perform the same reactions with increased rates and higher yields, such as in the case of metalation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisch2002_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eisch2002-3">[3]</a></sup>
Since then, organolithium reagents have overtaken Grignard reagents in usage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chemorgli_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-chemorgli-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organolithium_reagent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although simple alkyllithium species are often represented as monomer RLi, they exist as aggregates (<a href="/wiki/Oligomer" title="Oligomer">oligomers</a>) or polymers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leadstructure_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-leadstructure-5">[5]</a></sup> 
The degree of aggregation depends on the organic substituent and the presence of other ligands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reich_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reich-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strohmann_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strohmann-7">[7]</a></sup> These structures have been elucidated by a variety of methods, notably <sup>6</sup>Li, <sup>7</sup>Li, and <sup>13</sup>C <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NMR_spectroscopy" title="NMR spectroscopy">NMR spectroscopy</a> and X-ray diffraction analysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-analytical_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-analytical-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Computational_chemistry" title="Computational chemistry">Computational chemistry</a> supports these assignments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leadstructure_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-leadstructure-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nature_of_carbon-lithium_bond">Nature of carbon-lithium bond</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organolithium_reagent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nature of carbon-lithium bond">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:97px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Structure_4.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1190" data-file-width="1130" height="100" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Structure_4.png/95px-Structure_4.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Structure_4.png/142px-Structure_4.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Structure_4.png/190px-Structure_4.png 2x" width="95"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Structure_4.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Delocalized electron density in allyllithium reagents</div></div></div>
<p>The relative <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">electronegativities</a> of carbon and lithium suggest that the C-Li bond will be highly polar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gopakomar_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-gopakomar-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-streiwieser_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-streiwieser-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bickelhaupt_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-bickelhaupt-10">[10]</a></sup>
However, certain organolithium compounds possess properties such as solubility in nonpolar solvents that complicate the issue.
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gopakomar_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-gopakomar-8">[8]</a></sup> While most data suggest the C-Li bond to be essentially ionic, there has been debate as to whether a small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Covalent" title="Covalent">covalent</a> character exists in the C-Li bond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-streiwieser_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-streiwieser-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bickelhaupt_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-bickelhaupt-10">[10]</a></sup>  One estimate puts the percentage of ionic character of alkyllithium compounds at 80 to 88%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>In allyl lithium compounds, the lithium cation coordinates to the face of the carbon π bond in an η<sup>3</sup> fashion instead of a localized, carbanionic center, thus, allyllithiums are often less aggregated than alkyllithiums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reich_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reich-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fraenkelallyllithium_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fraenkelallyllithium-12">[12]</a></sup> In aryllithium complexes, the lithium cation coordinates to a single carbanion center through a Li-C σ type bond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reich_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Reich-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fraenkel_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fraenkel-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solid_state_structure">Solid state structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organolithium_reagent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Solid state structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:272px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Building_block_of_alkyllithium_aggregates.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4975" data-file-width="6300" height="213" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Building_block_of_alkyllithium_aggregates.png/270px-Building_block_of_alkyllithium_aggregates.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Building_block_of_alkyllithium_aggregates.png/405px-Building_block_of_alkyllithium_aggregates.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Building_block_of_alkyllithium_aggregates.png/540px-Building_block_of_alkyllithium_aggregates.png 2x" width="270"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Building_block_of_alkyllithium_aggregates.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tetrahedron and octahedron metal cores formed by aggregation of the Li3 triangle - carbanion coordinate complex<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leadstructure_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-leadstructure-5">[5]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Like other species consisting of polar subunits, organolithium species aggregate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strohmann_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Strohmann-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Power_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Power-14">[14]</a></sup>
Formation of aggregates is influenced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrostatic" title="Electrostatic">electrostatic</a> interactions, the coordination between lithium and surrounding solvent molecules or polar additives, and steric effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strohmann_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Strohmann-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>A basic building block toward constructing more complex structures is a carbanionic center interacting with a Li<sub>3</sub> triangle in an η- <sub>3</sub> fashion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leadstructure_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-leadstructure-5">[5]</a></sup>
In simple alkyllithium reagents, these triangles aggregate to form tetrahedron or octahedron structures. For example, methyllithium, ethyllithium and <i>tert</i>-butyllithium all exist in the tetramer [RLi]<sub>4</sub>. Methyllithium exists as tetramers in a <a href="/wiki/Cubane-type_cluster" title="Cubane-type cluster">cubane-type cluster</a> in the solid state, with four lithium centers forming a tetrahedron. Each methanide in the tetramer in methyllithium can have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agostic" title="Agostic">agostic</a> interaction with lithium cations in adjacent tetramers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leadstructure_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-leadstructure-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strohmann_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Strohmann-7">[7]</a></sup>
Ethyllithium and <i>tert</i>-butyllithium, on the other hand, do not exhibit this interaction, and are thus soluble in non-polar hydrocarbon solvents. Another class of alkyllithium adopts hexameric structures, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N-butyllithium" title="N-butyllithium"><i>n</i>-butyllithium</a>, isopropyllithium, and cyclohexanyllithium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leadstructure_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-leadstructure-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dimerliamide.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="521" data-file-width="821" height="159" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Dimerliamide.jpg/250px-Dimerliamide.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Dimerliamide.jpg/375px-Dimerliamide.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Dimerliamide.jpg/500px-Dimerliamide.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dimerliamide.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>LDA dimer with THF coordinated to Li cations</div></div></div>
<p>Common lithium amides, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Lithium_bis(trimethylsilyl)amide" title="Lithium bis(trimethylsilyl)amide">lithium bis(trimethylsilyl)amide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lithium_diisopropylamide" title="Lithium diisopropylamide">lithium diisopropylamide</a>, are also subject to aggregation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williard,_P._G.;_Salvino,_J._M._1993_1–3_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Williard,_P._G.;_Salvino,_J._M._1993_1–3-15">[15]</a></sup> Lithium amides adopt polymeric-ladder type structures in non-coordinating solvent in the solid state, and they generally exist as dimers in ethereal solvents. In the presence of strongly donating ligands, tri- or tetrameric lithium centers are formed. 
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liamides_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Liamides-16">[16]</a></sup>
For example, LDA exists primarily as dimers in THF.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williard,_P._G.;_Salvino,_J._M._1993_1–3_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Williard,_P._G.;_Salvino,_J._M._1993_1–3-15">[15]</a></sup> The structures of common lithium amides, such as lithium diisopropylamide (LDA) and lithium hexamethyldisilazide (LiHMDS) have been extensively studied by Collum and coworkers using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NMR_spectroscopy" title="NMR spectroscopy">NMR spectroscopy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collumamides_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Collumamides-17">[17]</a></sup>
Another important class of reagents is silyllithiums, extensively used in the synthesis of organometallic complexes and polysilane <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dendrimers" title="Dendrimers">dendrimers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strohmann_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Strohmann-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lithiosilanes_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-lithiosilanes-18">[18]</a></sup>
In the solid state, in contrast with alkyllithium reagents, most silyllithiums tend to form monomeric structures coordinated with solvent molecules such as THF, and only a few silyllithiums have been characterized as higher aggregates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strohmann_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-Strohmann-7">[7]</a></sup>
This difference can arise from the method of preparation of silyllithiums, the steric hindrance caused by the bulky alkyl substituents on silicon, and the less polarized nature of Si-Li bonds. The addition of strongly donating ligands, such as TMEDA and (-)-<a href="/wiki/Sparteine" title="Sparteine">sparteine</a>, can displace coordinating solvent molecules in silyllithiums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strohmann_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-Strohmann-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_structure">Solution structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organolithium_reagent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Solution structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Relying solely on the structural information of organolithium aggregates obtained in the solid state from crystal structures has certain limits, as it is possible for organolithium reagents to adopt different structures in reaction solution environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reich_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Reich-6">[6]</a></sup> Also, in some cases the crystal structure of an organolithium species can be difficult to isolate. Therefore, studying the structures of organolithium reagents, and the lithium-containing intermediates in solution form is extremely useful in understanding the reactivity of these reagents.
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solutionenolate_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-solutionenolate-19">[19]</a></sup> NMR spectroscopy has emerged as a powerful tool for the studies of organolithium aggregates in solution. For alkyllithium species, C-Li <i>J</i> coupling can often used to determine the number of lithium interacting with a carbanion center, and whether these interactions are static or dynamic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reich_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Reich-6">[6]</a></sup> Separate NMR signals can also differentiate the presence of multiple aggregates from a common monomeric unit. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reich103_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-reich103-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>The structures of organolithium compounds are affected by the presence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_base" title="Lewis base">Lewis bases</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Tetrahydrofuran" title="Tetrahydrofuran">tetrahydrofuran</a> (THF), <a href="/wiki/Diethyl_ether" title="Diethyl ether">diethyl ether</a> (Et<sub>2</sub>O), tetramethylethylene diamine (TMEDA) or <a href="/wiki/Hexamethylphosphoramide" title="Hexamethylphosphoramide">hexamethylphosphoramide</a> (HMPA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leadstructure_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-leadstructure-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Methyllithium" title="Methyllithium">Methyllithium</a> is a special case, in which solvation with ether, or polar additive HMPA does not deaggregate the tetrameric structure in the solid state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strohmann_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-Strohmann-7">[7]</a></sup> On the other hand, THF deaggregates hexameric butyl lithium: the tetramer is the main species, and ΔG for interconversion between tetramer and dimer is around 11 kcal/mol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McGarrity_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-McGarrity-21">[21]</a></sup> TMEDA can also chelate to the lithium cations in <i>n</i>-butyllithium and form solvated dimers such as [(TMEDA) LiBu-n)]<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leadstructure_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-leadstructure-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reich_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-Reich-6">[6]</a></sup> Phenyllithium has been shown to exist as a distorted tetramer in the crystallized ether solvate, and as a mixture of dimer and tetramer in ether solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reich_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-Reich-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_reactivity">Structure and reactivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organolithium_reagent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Structure and reactivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As the structures of organolithium reagents change according to their chemical environment, so do their reactivity and selectivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strohmann_7-8"><a href="#cite_note-Strohmann-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whatsgoingon_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-whatsgoingon-22">[22]</a></sup>
One question surrounding the structure-reactivity relationship is whether there exists a correlation between the degree of aggregation and the reactivity of organolithium reagents. It was originally proposed that lower aggregates such as monomers are more reactive in alkyllithiums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strohmann44_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strohmann44-23">[23]</a></sup> 
However, reaction pathways in which dimer or other oligomers are the reactive species have also been discovered,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strohmann24b_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strohmann24b-24">[24]</a></sup> and for lithium amides such as LDA, dimer-based reactions are common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collumldareview_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Collumldareview-25">[25]</a></sup> 
A series of solution kinetics studies of LDA - mediated reactions suggest that lower aggregates of enolates do not necessarily lead to higher reactivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collumamides_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Collumamides-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Also, some Lewis bases increase reactivity of organolithium compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chalk_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chalk-26">[26]</a></sup>
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reich2_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reich2-27">[27]</a></sup>
However, whether these additives function as strong chelating ligands, and how the observed increase in reactivity relates to structural changes in aggregates caused by these additives are not always clear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chalk_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chalk-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reich2_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reich2-27">[27]</a></sup>
For example, TMEDA increases rates and efficiencies in many reactions involving organolithium reagents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strohmann_7-9"><a href="#cite_note-Strohmann-7">[7]</a></sup> Toward alkyllithium reagents, TMEDA functions as a donor ligand, reduces the degree of aggregation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leadstructure_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-leadstructure-5">[5]</a></sup> and increases the nucleophilicity of these species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williard_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Williard-28">[28]</a></sup>
However, TMEDA does not always function as a donor ligand to lithium cation, especially in the presence of anionic oxygen and nitrogen centers. For example, it only weakly interacts with LDA and LiHMDS even in hydrocarbon solvents with no competing donor ligands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collumtmedagood_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Collumtmedagood-29">[29]</a></sup>
In imine lithiation, while THF acts as a strong donating ligand to LiHMDS, the weakly coordinating TMEDA readily dissociates from LiHMDS, leading to the formation of LiHMDS dimers that is the more reactive species. Thus, in the case of LiHMDS, TMEDA does not increase reactivity by reducing aggregation state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Collumimine_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Collumimine-30">[30]</a></sup> Also, as opposed to simple alkyllithium compounds, TMEDA does not deaggregate lithio-acetophenolate in THF solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reich_6-8"><a href="#cite_note-Reich-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seebach_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Seebach-31">[31]</a></sup>
The addition of HMPA to lithium amides such as LiHMDS and LDA often results in a mixture of dimer/monomer aggregates in THF. However, the ratio of dimer/monomer species does not change with increased concentration of HMPA, thus, the observed increase in reactivity is not the result of deaggregation. The mechanism of how these additives increase reactivity is still being researched.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whatsgoingon_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-whatsgoingon-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactivity_and_applications">Reactivity and applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organolithium_reagent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reactivity and applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The C-Li bond in organolithium reagents is highly polarized. As a result, the carbon attracts most of the <a href="/wiki/Electron_density" title="Electron density">electron density</a> in the bond and resembles a carbanion. Thus, organolithium reagents are strongly basic and nucleophilic. Some of the most common applications of organolithium reagents in synthesis include their use as nucleophiles, strong bases for deprotonation, initiator for polymerization, and starting material for the preparation of other organometallic compounds.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="As_nucleophile">As nucleophile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organolithium_reagent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: As nucleophile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbolithiation_reactions">Carbolithiation reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organolithium_reagent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Carbolithiation reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>As nucleophiles, organolithium reagents undergo carbolithiation reactions, whereby the carbon-lithium bond adds across a carbon-carbon double or triple bond, forming new organolithium species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Intracarbolithiation_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Intracarbolithiation-32">[32]</a></sup> This reaction is the most widely employed reaction of organolithium compounds. Carbolithiation is key in anionic polymerization processes, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N-butyllithium" title="N-butyllithium"><i>n</i>-butyllithium</a> is used as a catalyst to initiate the polymerization of <a href="/wiki/Styrene" title="Styrene">styrene</a>, butadiene, or isoprene or mixtures thereof.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hsieh_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hsieh-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anionicpolymer_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-anionicpolymer-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Anionic_polymerization_of_styrene_initiated_by_sec-BuLi.png" title="Anionic polymerization of styrene initiated by sec-butyllithium"><img alt="Anionic polymerization of styrene initiated by sec-butyllithium" data-file-height="6578" data-file-width="8625" height="343" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Anionic_polymerization_of_styrene_initiated_by_sec-BuLi.png/450px-Anionic_polymerization_of_styrene_initiated_by_sec-BuLi.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Anionic_polymerization_of_styrene_initiated_by_sec-BuLi.png/675px-Anionic_polymerization_of_styrene_initiated_by_sec-BuLi.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Anionic_polymerization_of_styrene_initiated_by_sec-BuLi.png/900px-Anionic_polymerization_of_styrene_initiated_by_sec-BuLi.png 2x" width="450"/></a></div></div></dd></dl>
<p>Another application that takes advantage of this reactivity is the formation of carbocyclic and heterocyclic coumpounds by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intramolecular" title="Intramolecular">intramolecular</a> carbolithiation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Intracarbolithiation_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Intracarbolithiation-32">[32]</a></sup> As a form of anionic cyclization, intramolecular carbolithiation reactions offer several advantages over <a href="/wiki/Radical_cyclization" title="Radical cyclization">radical cyclization</a>. First, it is possible for the product cyclic organolithium species to react with electrophiles, whereas it is often difficult to trap a radical intermediate of the corresponding structure. Secondly, anionic cyclizations are often more regio- and stereospecific than radical cyclization, particularly in the case of 5-hexenyllithiums. Intramolecular carbolithiation allows addition of the alkyl-, <a href="/wiki/Vinyllithium" title="Vinyllithium">vinyllithium</a> to triple bonds and mono-alkyl substituted double bonds. Aryllithiums can also undergo addition if a 5-membered ring is formed. The limitations of intramolecular carbolithiation include difficulty of forming 3 or 4-membered rings, as the intermediate cyclic organolithium species often tend to undergo ring-openings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Intracarbolithiation_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-Intracarbolithiation-32">[32]</a></sup> Below is an example of intramolecular carbolithiation reaction. The lithium species derived from the lithium-halogen exchange cyclized to form the vinyllithium through 5-exo-trig ring closure. The vinyllithium species further reacts with electrophiles and produce functionalized cyclopentylidene compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-intra18_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-intra18-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Intramolecular_carbolithiation%27.png" title="A sample stereoselective intramolecular carbolithiation reaction"><img alt="A sample stereoselective intramolecular carbolithiation reaction" data-file-height="4520" data-file-width="8928" height="238" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Intramolecular_carbolithiation%27.png/470px-Intramolecular_carbolithiation%27.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Intramolecular_carbolithiation%27.png/705px-Intramolecular_carbolithiation%27.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Intramolecular_carbolithiation%27.png/940px-Intramolecular_carbolithiation%27.png 2x" width="470"/></a></div></div></dd></dl>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Addition_to_carbonyl_compounds">Addition to carbonyl compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organolithium_reagent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Addition to carbonyl compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Nucleophilic organolithium reagents can add to electrophilic carbonyl double bonds to form carbon-carbon bonds. They can react with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aldehydes" title="Aldehydes">aldehydes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ketones" title="Ketones">ketones</a> to produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcohols" title="Alcohols">alcohols</a>. The addition proceeds mainly via polar addition, in which the nucleophilic organolithium species attacks from the equatorial direction, and produces the axial alcohol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carey_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carey-36">[36]</a></sup> Addition of lithium salts such as LiClO<sub>4</sub> can improve the stereoselectivity of the reaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ashby_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ashby-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LiClO4_increase_selectivity.png" title="LiClO4 increase selectivity of t BuLi"><img alt="LiClO4 increase selectivity of t BuLi" data-file-height="2157" data-file-width="9316" height="116" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/LiClO4_increase_selectivity.png/500px-LiClO4_increase_selectivity.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/LiClO4_increase_selectivity.png/750px-LiClO4_increase_selectivity.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/LiClO4_increase_selectivity.png/1000px-LiClO4_increase_selectivity.png 2x" width="500"/></a></div></div></dd></dl>
<p>When the ketone is sterically hindered, using Grignard reagents often leads to reduction of the carbonyl group instead of addition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carey_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Carey-36">[36]</a></sup> However, alkyllithium reagents are less likely to reduce the ketone, and may be used to synthesize substituted alcohols.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yamatakaddorgli_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yamatakaddorgli-38">[38]</a></sup> Below is an example of ethyllithium addition to adamantone to produce tertiary alcohol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adamantone_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-adamantone-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Li_add_to_adamantone.png" title="Li add to adamantone"><img alt="Li add to adamantone" data-file-height="3518" data-file-width="6095" height="202" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Li_add_to_adamantone.png/350px-Li_add_to_adamantone.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Li_add_to_adamantone.png/525px-Li_add_to_adamantone.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Li_add_to_adamantone.png/700px-Li_add_to_adamantone.png 2x" width="350"/></a></div></div></dd></dl>
<p>Organolithium reagents are also better than Grignard reagents in their ability to react with carboxylic acids to form ketones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carey_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-Carey-36">[36]</a></sup> This reaction can be optimized by carefully controlling the amount of organolithium reagent addition, or using trimethylsilyl chloride to quench excess lithium reagent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carey114_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-carey114-40">[40]</a></sup> A more common way to synthesize ketones is through the addition of organolithium reagents to Weinreb amides (N-methoxy-N-methyl amides). This reaction provides ketones when the organolithium reagents is used in excess, due to chelation of the lithium ion between the N-methoxy oxygen and the carbonyl oxygen, which forms a tetrahedral intermediate that collapses upon acidic work up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weinreb_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-weinreb-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Li_add_to_weinreb.png" title="Li add to weinreb"><img alt="Li add to weinreb" data-file-height="1481" data-file-width="8490" height="78" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Li_add_to_weinreb.png/450px-Li_add_to_weinreb.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Li_add_to_weinreb.png/675px-Li_add_to_weinreb.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Li_add_to_weinreb.png/900px-Li_add_to_weinreb.png 2x" width="450"/></a></div></div></dd></dl>
<p>Organolithium reagents can also react with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> to form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acids" title="Carboxylic acids">carboxylic acids</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-liaddtocbx_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-liaddtocbx-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the case of <a href="/wiki/Enone" title="Enone">enone</a> substrates, where two sites of nucleophilic addition are possible (1,2 addition to the carbonyl carbon or 1,4 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conjugate_addition" title="Conjugate addition">conjugate addition</a> to the β carbon), most highly reactive organolithium species favor the 1,2 addition, however, there are several ways to propel organolithium reagents to undergo conjugate addition. First, since the 1,4 adduct is the likely to be the more thermodynamically favorable species, conjugate addition can be achieved through equilibration (isomerization of the two product), especially when the lithium nucleophile is weak and 1,2 addition is reversible. Secondly, adding donor ligands to the reaction forms heteroatom-stabilized lithium species which favors 1,4 conjugate addition. In one example, addition of low-level of HMPA to the solvent favors the 1,4 addition. In the absence of donor ligand, lithium cation is closely coordinated to the oxygen atom, however, when the lithium cation is solvated by HMPA, the coordination between carbonyl oxygen and lithium ion is weakened. This method generally cannot be used to affect the regioselectivity of alkyl- and aryllithium reagents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14conjugate_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-14conjugate-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12vs14_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-12vs14-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1,4vs1,2_addition1.svg" title="1,4vs1,2 addition"><img alt="1,4vs1,2 addition" data-file-height="317" data-file-width="448" height="340" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/1%2C4vs1%2C2_addition1.svg/480px-1%2C4vs1%2C2_addition1.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/1%2C4vs1%2C2_addition1.svg/720px-1%2C4vs1%2C2_addition1.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/1%2C4vs1%2C2_addition1.svg/960px-1%2C4vs1%2C2_addition1.svg.png 2x" width="480"/></a></div></div></dd></dl>
<p>Organolithium reagents can also perform enantioselective nucleophilic addition to carbonyl and its derivatives, often in the presence of chiral ligands. This reactivity is widely applied in the industrial syntheses of pharmaceutical compounds. An example is the Merck and Dupont synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Efavirenz" title="Efavirenz">Efavirenz</a>, a potent <a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">HIV</a> reverse transcriptase inhibitor. Lithium acetylide is added to a prochiral ketone to yield a chiral alcohol product. The structure of the active reaction intermediate was determined by NMR spectroscopy studies in the solution state and X-ray crystallography of the solid state to be a cubic 2:2 tetramer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MerckDupont_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-MerckDupont-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Merck_synthesis_of_Efavirenz.png" title="Merck synthesis of Efavirenz"><img alt="Merck synthesis of Efavirenz" data-file-height="7174" data-file-width="12843" height="391" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Merck_synthesis_of_Efavirenz.png/700px-Merck_synthesis_of_Efavirenz.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Merck_synthesis_of_Efavirenz.png/1050px-Merck_synthesis_of_Efavirenz.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Merck_synthesis_of_Efavirenz.png/1400px-Merck_synthesis_of_Efavirenz.png 2x" width="700"/></a></div></div></dd></dl>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="SN2_type_reactions">S<sub>N</sub>2 type reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organolithium_reagent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: SN2 type reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Organolithium reagents can serve as nucleophiles and carry out S<sub>N</sub>2 type reactions with alkyl or allylic halides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inversionSn2_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-inversionSn2-46">[46]</a></sup>
Although they are considered more reactive than Grignards reactions in alkylation, their use is still limited due to competing side reactions such as radical reactions or metal-halogen exchange. Most organolithium reagents used in a alkylations are more stabilized, less basic, and less aggregated, such as heteroatom stabilized, aryl- or allyllithium reagents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reich_6-9"><a href="#cite_note-Reich-6">[6]</a></sup> HMPA has been shown to increase reaction rate and product yields, and the reactivity of aryllithium reagents is often enhanced by the addition of potassium alkoxides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carey_36-3"><a href="#cite_note-Carey-36">[36]</a></sup> Organolithium reagents can also carry out nucleophilic attacks with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epoxides" title="Epoxides">epoxides</a> to form alcohols.
</p>
<dl><dd><div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SN2_inversion_with_benzyllithium.png" title="SN2 inversion with benzyllithium"><img alt="SN2 inversion with benzyllithium" data-file-height="2986" data-file-width="10319" height="159" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/SN2_inversion_with_benzyllithium.png/550px-SN2_inversion_with_benzyllithium.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/SN2_inversion_with_benzyllithium.png/825px-SN2_inversion_with_benzyllithium.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/SN2_inversion_with_benzyllithium.png/1100px-SN2_inversion_with_benzyllithium.png 2x" width="550"/></a></div></div></dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="As_base">As base</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organolithium_reagent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: As base">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Organolithium reagents provide a wide range of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basicity" title="Basicity">basicity</a>. <a href="/wiki/Tert-Butyllithium" title="Tert-Butyllithium"><i>tert</i>-Butyllithium</a>, with three weakly electron donating alkyl groups, is the strongest base commercially available (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PKa" title="PKa">pKa</a> = 53).  As a result, the acidic protons on -OH, -NH and -SH are often protected in the presence of organolithium reagents. Some commonly used lithium bases are alkyllithium species such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N-butyllithium" title="N-butyllithium"><i>n</i>-butyllithium</a> and lithium dialkylamides (LiNR<sub>2</sub>). Reagents with bulky R groups such as lithium diisopropylamide (LDA) and lithium bis(trimethylsilyl)amide (LiHMDS) are often sterically hindered for nucleophilic addition, and are thus more selective toward deprotonation. Lithium dialkylamides (LiNR<sub>2</sub>) are widely used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enolate" title="Enolate">enolate</a> formation and <a href="/wiki/Aldol" title="Aldol">aldol</a> reaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reich3_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reich3-47">[47]</a></sup> The reactivity and selectivity of these bases are also influenced by solvents and other counter ions.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Metalation">Metalation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organolithium_reagent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Metalation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Metalation with organolithium reagents, also known as <b>lithiation</b> or lithium-hydrogen exchange, is achieved when an organolithium reagent, most commonly an alkyllithium, abstracts a proton and forms a new organolithium species. 
</p>

<p>Common metalation reagents are the butyllithiums. <i>tert</i>-Butyllithium and <i>sec-</i>butyllithium are generally more reactive and have better selectivity than <i>n</i>-butyllithium, however, they are also more expensive and difficult to handle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reich3_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reich3-47">[47]</a></sup> Metalation is a common way of preparing versatile organolithium reagents. The position of metalation is mostly controlled by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acidity" title="Acidity">acidity</a> of the C-H bond. Lithiation often occurs at a position α to electron withdrawing groups, since they are good at stabilizing the electron-density of the anion. Directing groups on aromatic compounds and <a href="/wiki/Heterocyclic_compound" title="Heterocyclic compound">heterocycles</a> provide regioselective sites of metalation; directed ortho metalation is an important class of metalation reactions. Metalated sulfones, acyl groups and α-metalated amides are important intermediates in chemistry synthesis. Metalation of allyl ether with alkyllithium or LDA forms an anion α to the oxygen, and can proceed to <a href="/wiki/2,3-Wittig_rearrangement" title="2,3-Wittig rearrangement">2,3-Wittig rearrangement</a>. Addition of donor ligands such as TMEDA and HMPA can increase metalation rate and broaden substrate scope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leroux_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leroux-48">[48]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chiral" title="Chiral">Chiral</a> organolithium reagents can be accessed through asymmetric metalation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asymmetalation_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-asymmetalation-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Directed_ortho_metalation2.png" title="Directed ortho metalation"><img alt="Directed ortho metalation" data-file-height="1164" data-file-width="9258" height="63" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Directed_ortho_metalation2.png/500px-Directed_ortho_metalation2.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Directed_ortho_metalation2.png/750px-Directed_ortho_metalation2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Directed_ortho_metalation2.png/1000px-Directed_ortho_metalation2.png 2x" width="500"/></a></div></div></dd></dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Directed_ortho_metalation" title="Directed ortho metalation">Directed ortho metalation</a> is an important tool in the synthesis of regiospecific substituted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aromatic" title="Aromatic">aromatic</a> compounds. This approach to lithiation and subsequent quenching of the intermediate lithium species with electrophile is often better than the electrophilic aromatic substitution due to its high regioselectivity. This reaction proceeds through deprotonation by organolithium reagents at the positions α to the direct metalation group (DMG) on the aromatic ring. The DMG is often a functional group containing a <a href="/wiki/Heteroatom" title="Heteroatom">heteroatom</a> that is Lewis basic, and can coordinate to the Lewis-acidic lithium cation. This generates a complex-induced proximity effect, which directs deprotonation at the α position to form an aryllithium species that can further react with electrophiles. Some of the most effective DMGs are amides, <a href="/wiki/Carbamate" title="Carbamate">carbamates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfone" title="Sulfone">sulfones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfonamide" title="Sulfonamide">sulfonamides</a>. They are strong electron-withdrawing groups that increase the acidity of alpha-protons on the aromatic ring. In the presence of two DMGs, metalation often occurs ortho to the stronger directing group, though mixed products are also observed. A number of heterocycles that contain acidic protons can also undergo ortho-metalation. However, for electron-poor heterocycles, lithium amide bases such as LDA are generally used, since alkyllithium has been observed to perform addition to the electron-poor heterocycles rather than deprotonation. In certain transition metal-arene complexes, such as ferrocene, the transition metal attracts electron-density from the arene, thus rendering the aromatic protons more acidic, and ready for ortho-metalation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clayden_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clayden-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Superbases">Superbases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organolithium_reagent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Superbases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Superbase" title="Superbase">superbase</a></div>
<p>Addition of potassium alkoxide to alkyllithium greatly increases the basicity of organolithium species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schlosser2_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schlosser2-51">[51]</a></sup> The most common "superbase" can be formed by addition of KOtBu to butyllithium, often abbreviated as "LiCKOR" reagents. These "superbases" are highly reactive and often stereoselective reagents. In the example below, the LiCKOR base generates a stereospecific crotylboronate species through metalation and subsequent lithium-metalloid exchange.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roush_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roush-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p>

<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Asymmetric_metalation">Asymmetric metalation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organolithium_reagent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Asymmetric metalation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Enantioenriched organlithium species can be obtained through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asymmetric_synthesis" title="Asymmetric synthesis">asymmetric</a> metalation of prochiral substrates. Asymmetric induction requires the presence of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chiral" title="Chiral">chiral</a> ligand such as (-)-<a href="/wiki/Sparteine" title="Sparteine">sparteine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asymmetalation_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-asymmetalation-49">[49]</a></sup> The enantiomeric ratio of the chiral lithium species is often influenced by the differences in rates of deprotonation. In the example below, treatment of <i>N</i>-Boc-<i>N</i>-benzylamine with <i>n</i>-butyllithium in the presence of (-)-sparteine affords one enantiomer of the product with high <a href="/wiki/Enantiomeric_excess" title="Enantiomeric excess">enantiomeric excess</a>. Transmetalation with trimethyltinchloride affords the opposite enantiomer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NBocbenzylamines_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-NBocbenzylamines-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Enolate_formation">Enolate formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organolithium_reagent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Enolate formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Lithium <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enolate" title="Enolate">enolates</a> are formed through deprotonation of a C-H bond α to the carbonyl group by an organolithium species. Lithium enolates are widely used as nucleophiles in carbon-carbon bond formation reactions such as <a href="/wiki/Aldol_condensation" title="Aldol condensation">aldol condensation</a> and alkylation. They are also an important intermediate in the formation of <a href="/wiki/Silyl_enol_ether" title="Silyl enol ether">silyl enol ether</a>.
</p>
<dl><dd><div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Li_enolate_2.png" title="Sample aldol reaction with lithium enolate"><img alt="Sample aldol reaction with lithium enolate" data-file-height="1961" data-file-width="6213" height="125" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Li_enolate_2.png/397px-Li_enolate_2.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Li_enolate_2.png/596px-Li_enolate_2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Li_enolate_2.png/793px-Li_enolate_2.png 2x" width="397"/></a></div></div></dd></dl>
<p>Lithium enolate formation can be generalized as an acid-base reaction, in which the relatively acidic proton α to the carbonyl group (pK =20-28 in DMSO) reacts with organolithium base. Generally, strong, non-nucleophilic bases, especially lithium amides such LDA, LiHMDS and LiTMP are used. THF and DMSO are common solvents in lithium enolate reactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-valnotenolate_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-valnotenolate-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>The stereochemistry and mechanism of enolate formation have gained much interest in the chemistry community. Many factors influence the outcome of enolate stereochemistry, such as steric effects, solvent, polar additives, and types of organolithium bases. Among the many models used to explain and predict the selectivity in stereochemistry of lithium enolates is the Ireland model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ireland_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ireland-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>In this assumption, a monomeric LDA reacts with the carbonyl substrate and form a cyclic Zimmerman-Traxler type transition state. The (E)-enolate is favored due to an unfavorable <i>syn-pentane</i> interaction in the (Z)-enolate transition state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-valnotenolate_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-valnotenolate-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ireland_model_enolate1.svg" title="Ireland model for lithium enolate stereoselectivity. In this example, the (E) enolate is favored."><img alt="Ireland model for lithium enolate stereoselectivity. In this example, the (E) enolate is favored." data-file-height="319" data-file-width="532" height="324" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Ireland_model_enolate1.svg/540px-Ireland_model_enolate1.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Ireland_model_enolate1.svg/810px-Ireland_model_enolate1.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Ireland_model_enolate1.svg/1080px-Ireland_model_enolate1.svg.png 2x" width="540"/></a></div></div></dd></dl>
<p>Addition of polar additives such as HMPA or DMPU favors the formation of (Z) enolates. The Ireland model argues that these donor ligands coordinate to the lithium cations, as a result, carbonyl oxygen and lithium interaction is reduced, and the transition state is not as tightly bound as a six-membered chair. The percentage of (Z) enolates also increases when lithium bases with bulkier side chains (such as LiHMDS) are used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-valnotenolate_54-2"><a href="#cite_note-valnotenolate-54">[54]</a></sup> However, the mechanism of how these additives reverse stereoselectivity is still being debated.
</p><p>There have been some challenges to the Ireland model, as it depicts the lithium species as a monomer in the transition state. In reality, a variety of lithium aggregates are often observed in solutions of lithium enolates, and depending on specific substrate, solvent and reaction conditions, it can be difficult to determine which aggregate is the actual reactive species in solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-valnotenolate_54-3"><a href="#cite_note-valnotenolate-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lithium-halogen_exchange">Lithium-halogen exchange</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organolithium_reagent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Lithium-halogen exchange">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The mechanism of lithium-halogen exchange is still debated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baileyreview_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-baileyreview-57">[57]</a></sup>
One possible pathway involves a nucleophilic mechanism that generates a reversible “ate-complex” intermediate. Farnham and Calabrese were able to isolate “ate-complex” lithium bis(pentafluorophenyl) iodinate complexed with TMEDA and obtain an X-ray crystal structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atecomplex_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-atecomplex-58">[58]</a></sup>
The "ate-complex" further reacts with electrophiles and provides pentafluorophenyl iodide and C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>Li.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atecomplex_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-atecomplex-58">[58]</a></sup> A number of kinetic studies also support a nucleophilic pathway in which the carbanion on the lithium species attacks the halogen atom on the aryl halide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rogers_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rogers-59">[59]</a></sup>
Another possible mechanism involves single electron transfer and the generation of radicals. In reactions of secondary and tertiary alkyllithium and alkyl halides, radical species were detected by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EPR_spectroscopy" title="EPR spectroscopy">EPR spectroscopy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fischerradical_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fischerradical-60">[60]</a></sup>
However, whether these radicals are reaction intermediates is not definitive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baileyreview_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-baileyreview-57">[57]</a></sup> The mechanistic studies of lithium-halogen exchange is also complicated by the formation of aggregates of organolithium species.
</p><p>The rate of lithium halogen exchange is extremely fast. It is usually faster than nucleophilic addition and can sometimes exceed the rate of proton transfer. In the example below, the exchange between lithium and primary iodide is almost instantaneous, and outcompetes proton transfer from methanol to <i>tert</i>-butyllithium. The major alkene product is formed in over 90% yield.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bailey_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bailey-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fast_li-x_exchange%27.png" title="Rapid lithium iodide exchange"><img alt="Rapid lithium iodide exchange" data-file-height="1423" data-file-width="9146" height="81" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Fast_li-x_exchange%27.png/520px-Fast_li-x_exchange%27.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Fast_li-x_exchange%27.png/780px-Fast_li-x_exchange%27.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Fast_li-x_exchange%27.png/1040px-Fast_li-x_exchange%27.png 2x" width="520"/></a></div></div>
<p>Lithium-halogen exchange is very useful in preparing new organolithium reagents. Exchange rates usually follow the trend I &gt; Br &gt; Cl. Alkyl- and arylfluoride are generally unreactive toward organolithium reagents. Lithium halogen exchange is kinetically controlled, and the rate of exchange is primarily influenced by the stabilities of the carbanion intermediates (sp &gt; sp2 &gt; sp3) of the organolithium reagents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carey_36-4"><a href="#cite_note-Carey-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leroux_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-Leroux-48">[48]</a></sup> For example, the more basic tertiary organolithium reagents (usually <i>n</i>-butyllithium, <i>sec</i>-butyllithium or <i>tert</i>-butyllithium) are the most reactive, and will react with primary alkyl halide (usually bromide or iodide) to form the more stable organolithium species. Therefore, lithium halogen exchange is most frequently used to prepare vinyl-, aryl- and primary alkyllithium reagents. Lithium halogen exchange is also facilitated when alkoxy groups or heteroatoms are present to stabilize the carbanion, and this method is especially useful for the preparation of functionalized lithium reagents which cannot tolerate the harsher conditions required for reduction with lithium metal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leroux_48-2"><a href="#cite_note-Leroux-48">[48]</a></sup> Substrates such as vinyl halides usually undergo lithium-halogen exchange with retention of the stereochemistry of the double bond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seebach2_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Seebach2-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Retention_of_stereochem.png" title="Retention of stereochem"><img alt="Retention of stereochem" data-file-height="2667" data-file-width="9320" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Retention_of_stereochem.png/500px-Retention_of_stereochem.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Retention_of_stereochem.png/750px-Retention_of_stereochem.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Retention_of_stereochem.png/1000px-Retention_of_stereochem.png 2x" width="500"/></a></div></div></dd></dl>
<p>Below is an example of the use of lithium-halogen exchange in the synthesis of morphine. Here, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N-butyllithium" title="N-butyllithium"><i>n</i>-butyllithium</a> is used to perform lithium-halogen exchange with bromide. The nucleophilic carbanion center quickly undergoes carbolithiation to the double bond, generating an anion stabilized by the adjacent sulfone group. An intramolecular S<sub>N</sub>2 reaction by the anion forms the cyclic backbone of morphine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morphinelix_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-morphinelix-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Synthesis_of_morphine.png" title="synthesis of morphine lithium halogen exchange"><img alt="synthesis of morphine lithium halogen exchange" data-file-height="8755" data-file-width="13521" height="421" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Synthesis_of_morphine.png/650px-Synthesis_of_morphine.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Synthesis_of_morphine.png/975px-Synthesis_of_morphine.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Synthesis_of_morphine.png/1300px-Synthesis_of_morphine.png 2x" width="650"/></a></div></div>
<p>Lithium halogen exchange is a crucial part of Parham cyclization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parham_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-parham-64">[64]</a></sup> In this reaction, an aryl halide (usually iodide or bromide) exchanges with organolithium to form a lithiated arene species. If the arene bears a side chain with an electrophillic moiety, the carbanion attached to the lithium will perform intramolecular nucleophilic attack and cyclize. This reaction is a useful strategy for heterocycle formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parhamheterocycles_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-parhamheterocycles-65">[65]</a></sup> In the example below, Parham cyclization was used to in the cyclization of an isocyanate to form isoindolinone, which was then converted to a nitrone. The nitrone species further reacts with radicals, and can be used as "spin traps" to study biological radical processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mitospin_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-mitospin-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Parham_cyclization_in_MitoSpin%27.png" title="Parham cyclization in MitoSpin"><img alt="Parham cyclization in MitoSpin" data-file-height="2918" data-file-width="12481" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Parham_cyclization_in_MitoSpin%27.png/580px-Parham_cyclization_in_MitoSpin%27.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Parham_cyclization_in_MitoSpin%27.png/870px-Parham_cyclization_in_MitoSpin%27.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Parham_cyclization_in_MitoSpin%27.png/1160px-Parham_cyclization_in_MitoSpin%27.png 2x" width="580"/></a></div></div></dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transmetalation">Transmetalation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organolithium_reagent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Transmetalation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

Organocopper, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organotin" title="Organotin">organotin</a>, organosilicon, organoboron, organophosphorus, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organocerium" title="Organocerium">organocerium</a> and organosulfur compounds are frequently prepared by reacting organolithium reagents with appropriate electrophiles.

<p>Common types of transmetalation include Li/Sn, Li/Hg, and Li/Te exchange, which are fast at low temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reich3_47-2"><a href="#cite_note-Reich3-47">[47]</a></sup> The advantage of Li/Sn exchange is that the tri-alkylstannane precursors undergo few side reactions, as the resulting n-Bu<sub>3</sub>Sn byproducts are unreactive toward alkyllithium reagents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reich3_47-3"><a href="#cite_note-Reich3-47">[47]</a></sup> In the following example, vinylstannane, synthesized from terminal alkyne, forms vinyllithium through transmetalation with n-BuLi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stannane_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-stannane-67">[67]</a></sup>

The resulting organocuprates are generally less reactive toward aldehydes and ketones than organolithium reagents or Grignard reagents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pathwayofcuprate_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-pathwayofcuprate-69">[69]</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organolithium_reagent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most simple alkyllithium reagents, and common lithium amides are commercially available in a variety of solvents and concentrations. Organolithium reagents can also be prepared in the laboratory. Below are some common methods for preparing organolithium reagents.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction_with_lithium_metal">Reaction with lithium metal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organolithium_reagent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Reaction with lithium metal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Reduction of alkyl halide with metallic lithium can afford simple alkyl and aryl organolithium reagents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carey_36-5"><a href="#cite_note-Carey-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>

<p>Industrial preparation of organolithium reagents is achieved using this method by treating the alkyl chloride with metal lithium containing 0.5-2% <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>. The conversion is highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermic</a>. The sodium initiates the radical pathway and increases the rate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schlosser_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schlosser-70">[70]</a></sup> The reduction proceeds via a radical pathway. Below is an example of the preparation of a functionalized lithium reagent using reduction with lithium metal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reductLi_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-reductLi-71">[71]</a></sup> Sometimes, lithium metal in the form of fine powders are used in the reaction with certain catalysts such as <a href="/wiki/Naphthalene" title="Naphthalene">naphthalene</a> or 4,4’-di-t-butylbiphenyl (DTBB). Another substrate that can be reduced with lithium metal to generate alkyllithium reagents is sulfides. Reduction of sulfides is useful in the formation of functionalized organolithium reagents such as alpha-lithio ethers, sulfides, and silanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohen_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metalation_2">Metalation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organolithium_reagent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Metalation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>See <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Organolithium_reagent:Metalation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Organolithium reagent:Metalation (page does not exist)">Metalation</a>.(under Reactivity and applications)
</p><p>A second method of preparing organolithium reagents is a metalation (lithium hydrogen exchange). The relative acidity of hydrogen atoms controls the position of lithiation.
</p><p>This is the most common method for preparing alkynyllithium reagents, because the terminal hydrogen bound to the <i>sp</i> carbon is very acidic and easily deprotonated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carey_36-6"><a href="#cite_note-Carey-36">[36]</a></sup> For aromatic compounds, the position of lithiation is also determined by the directing effect of substituent groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Snieckus_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-Snieckus-73">[73]</a></sup> Some of the most effective directing substituent groups are alkoxy, amido, sulfoxide, sulfonyl. Metalation often occurs at the position ortho to these substituents. In heteroaromatic compounds, metalation usually occurs at the position ortho to the heteroatom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carey_36-7"><a href="#cite_note-Carey-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Snieckus_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-Snieckus-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lithium_halogen_exchange">Lithium halogen exchange</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organolithium_reagent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Lithium halogen exchange">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>See lithium-halogen exchange (under Reactivity and applications)
</p><p>A third method to prepare organolithium reagents is through lithium halogen exchange.
</p><p><i>tert-</i>butyllithium or <i>n-</i>butyllithium are the most commonly used reagents for generating new organolithium species through lithium halogen exchange. Lithium-halogen exchange is mostly used to convert aryl and alkenyl iodides and bromides with <i>sp2</i> carbons to the corresponding organolithium compounds. The reaction is extremely fast, and often proceed at -60 to -120 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leroux_48-3"><a href="#cite_note-Leroux-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transmetalation_2">Transmetalation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organolithium_reagent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Transmetalation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>See transmetalation (under Reactivity and applications)
</p><p>The fourth method to prepare organolithium reagents is through transmetalation. This method can be used for preparing vinyllithium.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shapiro_reaction">Shapiro reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organolithium_reagent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Shapiro reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Shapiro_reaction" title="Shapiro reaction">Shapiro reaction</a>, two equivalents of strong alkyllithium base react with p-tosylhydrazone compounds to produce the vinyllithium, or upon quenching, the olefin product.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Handling">Handling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organolithium_reagent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Handling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Organolithium compounds are highly reactive species and require specialized handling techniques. They are often corrosive, flammable, and sometimes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrophoric" title="Pyrophoric">pyrophoric</a> (spontaneous ignition when exposed to oxygen or moisture).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Letchford_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-Letchford-74">[74]</a></sup> Alkyllithium reagents can also undergo thermal decomposition to form the corresponding alkyl species and lithium hydride.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gellert_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gellert-75">[75]</a></sup>  Organolithium reagents are typically stored below 10 °C. Reactions are conducted using <a href="/wiki/Air-free_technique" title="Air-free technique">air-free techniques</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Letchford_74-1"><a href="#cite_note-Letchford-74">[74]</a></sup> The concentration of alkyllithium reagents is often determined by <a href="/wiki/Titration" title="Titration">titration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup>
</p><p>Organolithium reagents react, often slowly, with ethers, which nonetheless are often used as solvents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p>
