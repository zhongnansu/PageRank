<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Emission intensity</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comparisons_of_life-cycle_greenhouse_gas_emissions" title="Comparisons of life-cycle greenhouse gas emissions">Comparisons of life-cycle greenhouse gas emissions</a></div>
<p>An <b>emission intensity</b> (also <b>carbon intensity</b>, <b>C.I.</b>) is the emission rate of a given <a href="/wiki/Pollutant" title="Pollutant">pollutant</a> relative to the intensity of a specific activity, or an industrial production process; for example <a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">grams</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> released per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megajoule" title="Megajoule">megajoule</a> of energy produced, or the ratio of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions produced to <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a> (GDP). Emission intensities are used to derive estimates of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_pollutant" title="Air pollutant">air pollutant</a> or greenhouse gas emissions based on the amount of fuel <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combusted</a>, the number of animals in <a href="/wiki/Animal_husbandry" title="Animal husbandry">animal husbandry</a>, on industrial production levels, distances traveled or similar activity data. Emission intensities may also be used to compare the environmental impact of different fuels or activities. In some case the related terms <b>emission factor</b> and <b>carbon intensity</b> are used interchangeably. The jargon used can be different, for different fields/industrial sectors; normally the term "carbon" excludes other pollutants, such as particulate emissions. One commonly used figure is <b>carbon intensity per kilowatt-hour</b> (<b>CIPK</b>), which is used to compare emissions from different sources of electrical power.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methodologies">Methodologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emission_intensity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Methodologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Different methodologies can be used to assess the carbon intensity of a process. Among the most used methodologies there are:
</p>
<ul><li>The whole <a href="/wiki/Life-cycle_assessment" title="Life-cycle assessment">life-cycle assessment</a> (LCA): this includes not only the carbon emissions due to a specific process, but also those due to the production and end-of-life of materials, plants and machineries used for the considered process. This is a quite complex method, requiring a big set of variables.</li>
<li>The well-to-wheels (WTW), commonly used in the Energy and Transport sectors: this is a simplified LCA considering the emissions of the process itself, the emissions due to the extraction and refining of the material (or fuel) used in the process (also "Upstream emissions", but excluding the emissions due to the production and end-of-life of plants and machineries. This methodology is used, in the U.S.A., by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GREET_model" title="GREET model">GREET model</a> and in Europe in the <a class="external text" href="http://iet.jrc.ec.europa.eu/about-jec/jec-well-wheels-analyses-wtw" rel="nofollow">JEC WTW</a>.</li>
<li>WTW-LCA hybrid methods, trying to fill in the gap between the WTW and LCA methods. In example, for an Electric Vehicle, an hybrid method considering also the GHG due to the manufacturing and the end of life of the battery gives GHG emissions 10-13% higher, compared to the WTW  <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>Methods not considering LCA aspects but only the emissions occurring during a specific process; i.e. just the combustion of a fuel in a power plant, without considering the Upstream emissions. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Different calculation methods can lead to different results. The results can largely vary also for different geographic regions and timeframes (see, in example, <a class="external text" href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1361920916307933" rel="nofollow">how C.I. of electricity varies, for different European countries, and how varied in a few years</a>: from 2009 to 2013 the C.I. of electricity in the European Union fell on average by 20%,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> So while comparing different values of Carbon Intensity it is important to correctly consider all the boundary conditions (or initial hypotheses) considered for the calculations. For example, Chinese oil fields emit between 1.5 to more than 40 g of CO<sub>2</sub>eq per MJ with about 90% of all fields emitting 1.5–13.5 g CO<sub>2</sub>eq.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Masnadi_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Masnadi-4">[4]</a></sup> Such highly skewed carbon intensity patterns necessitate disaggregation of seemingly homogenous emission activities and proper consideration of many factors for understanding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Höök2018_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Höök2018-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Estimating_emissions">Estimating emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emission_intensity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Estimating emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Emission factors assume a linear relation between the intensity of the activity and the emission resulting from this activity:
</p><p><i>Emission<sub>pollutant</sub> = Activity * Emission Factor<sub>pollutant</sub></i>
</p><p>Intensities are also used in projecting possible future scenarios such as those used in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="International Panel on Climate Change">IPCC</a> assessments, along with projected future changes in population, economic activity and energy technologies. The interrelations of these variables is treated under the so-called <a href="/wiki/Kaya_identity" title="Kaya identity">Kaya identity</a>.
</p><p>The level of uncertainty of the resulting estimates depends significantly on the source category and the pollutant. Some examples: 
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">Carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) emissions from the combustion of fuel can be estimated with a high degree of certainty regardless of how the fuel is used as these emissions depend almost exclusively on the <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> content of the fuel, which is generally known with a high degree of precision. The same is true for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulphur_dioxide" title="Sulphur dioxide">sulphur dioxide</a> (SO<sub>2</sub>), since sulphur contents of fuels are also generally well known. Both carbon and sulphur are almost completely oxidized during combustion and all carbon and sulphur atoms in the fuel will be present in the <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">flue gases</a> as CO<sub>2</sub> and SO<sub>2</sub> respectively.</li>
<li>In contrast, the levels of other air pollutants and non-CO<sub>2</sub> greenhouse gas emissions from combustion depend on the precise technology applied when fuel is combusted. These emissions are basically caused by either incomplete combustion of a small fraction of the fuel (<a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Non-methane_volatile_organic_compound" title="Non-methane volatile organic compound">non-methane volatile organic compounds</a>) or by complicated chemical and physical processes during the combustion and in the smoke stack or tailpipe. Examples of these are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">particulates</a>, NO<sub>x</sub>, a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a>, NO, and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide" title="Nitrogen dioxide">nitrogen dioxide</a>, NO<sub>2</sub>).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">Nitrous oxide</a> (N<sub>2</sub>O) emissions from agricultural soils are highly uncertain because they depend very much on both the exact conditions of the soil, the application of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertilizers" title="Fertilizers">fertilizers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorological</a> conditions.</li></ul>

<p>In 2009 CO<sub>2</sub> intensity of GDP in the OECD countries reduced by 2.9%  and amounted to 0.33 kCO<sub>2</sub>/$05p in the OECD countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> ("$05p" = 2005 US dollars, using purchasing power parities). The USA posted a higher ratio of 0.41 kCO<sub>2</sub>/$05p while Europe showed the largest drop in CO<sub>2</sub> intensity compared to the previous year (−3.7%). CO<sub>2</sub> intensity continued to be roughly higher in non-OECD countries. Despite a slight improvement, China continued to post a high CO<sub>2</sub> intensity (0.81 kCO<sub>2</sub>/$05p). CO<sub>2</sub> intensity in Asia rose by 2% during 2009 since energy consumption continued to develop at a strong pace. Important ratios were also observed in countries in CIS and the Middle East.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_intensity_in_Europe">Carbon intensity in Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emission_intensity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Carbon intensity in Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Total CO<sub>2</sub> emissions from energy use were 5% below their 1990 level in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Over the period 1990–2007, CO<sub>2</sub> emissions from energy use have decreased on average by 0.3%/year although the economic activity (GDP) increased by 2.3%/year. After dropping until 1994 (−1.6%/year), the CO<sub>2</sub> emissions have increased steadily (0.4%/year on average) until 2003 and decreased slowly again since (on average by 0.6%/year). Total CO<sub>2</sub> emissions per capita decreased from 8.7 t in 1990 to 7.8 t in 2007, that is to say a decrease by 10%.
Almost 40% of the reduction in CO<sub>2</sub> intensity is due to increased use of energy carriers with lower emission factors
Total CO<sub>2</sub> emissions per unit of GDP, the “CO<sub>2</sub> intensity”, decreased more rapidly than energy intensity: by 2.3%/year and 1.4%/year, respectively, on average between 1990 and 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Commodity_Exchange_Bratislava" title="Commodity Exchange Bratislava">Commodity Exchange Bratislava</a> (CEB) has calculated carbon intensity for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voluntary_Emissions_Reduction" title="Voluntary Emissions Reduction">Voluntary Emissions Reduction</a> projects carbon intensity in 2012 to be 0,343 tn/MWh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Emission_factors_for_greenhouse_gas_inventory_reporting">Emission factors for greenhouse gas inventory reporting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emission_intensity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Emission factors for greenhouse gas inventory reporting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of the most important uses of emission factors is for the reporting of national <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_inventory" title="Greenhouse gas inventory">greenhouse gas inventories</a> under the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change</a> (UNFCCC). The so-called <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change#Annex_I_countries" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">Annex I Parties</a> to the UNFCCC have to annually report their national total emissions of greenhouse gases in a formalized reporting format, defining the source categories and fuels that must be included.
</p><p>The UNFCCC has accepted the <b>Revised 1996 IPCC Guidelines for National Greenhouse Gas Inventories</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> developed and published by the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC) as the emission estimation methods that must be used by the parties to the convention to ensure transparency, completeness, consistency, comparability and accuracy of the national greenhouse gas inventories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> These IPCC Guidelines are the primary source for default emission factors. Recently IPCC has published the <b>2006 IPCC Guidelines for National Greenhouse Gas Inventories</b>. These and many more greenhouse gas emission factors can be found on IPCC's Emission Factor Database.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Commercially applicable organisational greenhouse gas emission factors can be found on the search engine, EmissionFactors.com.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Particularly for non-CO<sub>2</sub> emissions, there is often a high degree of uncertainty associated with these emission factors when applied to individual countries. In general, the use of country-specific emission factors would provide more accurate estimates of emissions than the use of the default emission factors. According to the IPCC, if an activity is a major source of emissions for a country ('key source'), it is 'good practice' to develop a country-specific emission factor for that activity.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Emission_factors_for_air_pollutant_inventory_reporting">Emission factors for air pollutant inventory reporting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emission_intensity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Emission factors for air pollutant inventory reporting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNECE" title="UNECE">United Nations Economic Commission for Europe</a> and the EU <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Emission_Ceilings_Directive" title="National Emission Ceilings Directive">National Emission Ceilings Directive</a> (2016) require countries to produce annual National Air Pollution Emission Inventories under the provisions of the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_Long-Range_Transboundary_Air_Pollution" title="Convention on Long-Range Transboundary Air Pollution">Convention on Long-Range Transboundary Air Pollution</a> (CLRTAP). 
</p><p>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=European_Monitoring_and_Evaluation_Programme&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="European Monitoring and Evaluation Programme (page does not exist)">European Monitoring and Evaluation Programme</a> (EMEP) Task Force of the <a href="/wiki/European_Environment_Agency" title="European Environment Agency">European Environment Agency</a> has developed methods to estimate emissions and the associated emission factors for air pollutants, which have been published in the EMEP/CORINAIR Emission Inventory Guidebook.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> on Emission Inventories and Projections TFEIP <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Intensity_targets">Intensity targets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emission_intensity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Intensity targets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GHG_intensity_2000.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="477" data-file-width="940" height="203" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/GHG_intensity_2000.svg/400px-GHG_intensity_2000.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/GHG_intensity_2000.svg/600px-GHG_intensity_2000.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/GHG_intensity_2000.svg/800px-GHG_intensity_2000.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GHG_intensity_2000.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Greenhouse gas intensity in 2000 including land-use change</div></div></div>
<p>The U.S. plans to cut carbon intensity per dollar of GDP by 18% by 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> This has been criticised by the World Resources Institute as this approach does not ensure absolute reductions if GDP grows faster than intensity declines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>From 1990 to 2000, the carbon intensity of the U.S. economy declined by 17%, yet total emissions increased by 14%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bfr_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-bfr-26">[26]</a></sup> In 2002, the U.S. National Environmental trust labelled carbon intensity, "a bookkeeping trick which allows the administration to do nothing about global warming while unsafe levels of emissions continue to rise."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-net.org_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-net.org-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
