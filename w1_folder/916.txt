<p>A <b>petroleum reservoir</b> or <b>oil and gas reservoir</b> is a subsurface pool of <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a> contained in <a href="/wiki/Porosity" title="Porosity">porous</a> or fractured <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rock_formation" title="Rock formation">rock formations</a>. Petroleum reservoirs are broadly classified as <b>conventional</b> and <b><a href="/wiki/Unconventional_oil" title="Unconventional oil">unconventional</a></b> reservoirs. In case of conventional reservoirs, the naturally occurring hydrocarbons, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a> or <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, are trapped by overlying rock formations with lower <a href="/wiki/Permeability_(earth_sciences)" title="Permeability (earth sciences)">permeability</a>. While in unconventional reservoirs the rocks have high <a href="/wiki/Porosity" title="Porosity">porosity</a> and low permeability which keeps the hydrocarbons trapped in place, therefore not requiring a <a href="/wiki/Caprock" title="Caprock">cap rock</a>. Reservoirs are found using <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon_exploration" title="Hydrocarbon exploration">hydrocarbon exploration</a> methods.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oil_field">Oil field</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Petroleum_reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Oil field">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Oil_wells_just_offshore_at_Summerland,_California,_c.1915.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="872" data-file-width="1512" height="127" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Oil_wells_just_offshore_at_Summerland%2C_California%2C_c.1915.jpg/220px-Oil_wells_just_offshore_at_Summerland%2C_California%2C_c.1915.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Oil_wells_just_offshore_at_Summerland%2C_California%2C_c.1915.jpg/330px-Oil_wells_just_offshore_at_Summerland%2C_California%2C_c.1915.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Oil_wells_just_offshore_at_Summerland%2C_California%2C_c.1915.jpg/440px-Oil_wells_just_offshore_at_Summerland%2C_California%2C_c.1915.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Oil_wells_just_offshore_at_Summerland,_California,_c.1915.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An oil field with dozens of wells. <p>A region with an abundance of <a href="/wiki/Oil_well" title="Oil well">oil wells</a> extracting <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> (crude oil) from below ground. Because the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_reservoir" title="Oil reservoir">oil reservoirs</a> typically extend over a large area, possibly several hundred kilometres across, full exploitation entails multiple wells scattered across the area. In addition, there may be exploratory wells probing the edges, pipelines to transport the oil elsewhere, and support facilities.
</p><p>Because an oil field may be remote from civilization, establishing a field is often an extremely complicated exercise in <a href="/wiki/Logistics" title="Logistics">logistics</a>. This goes beyond requirements for drilling, to include associated infrastructure. For instance, workers require housing to allow them to work onsite for months or years. In turn, housing and equipment require electricity and water. In cold regions, pipelines may need to be heated. Also, excess <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> may be <a href="/wiki/Gas_flare" title="Gas flare">burned off</a> if there is no way to make use of it—which requires a furnace, chimney and pipes to carry it from the well to the furnace.
</p><p>Thus, the typical oil field resembles a small, self-contained town in the midst of a landscape dotted with <a href="/wiki/Drilling_rig" title="Drilling rig">drilling rigs</a> or the pump jacks, which are known as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nodding_donkey" title="Nodding donkey">nodding donkeys</a>" because of their bobbing arm. Several companies, such as <a href="/wiki/Hill_International" title="Hill International">Hill International</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bechtel_Corporation" title="Bechtel Corporation">Bechtel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Esso" title="Esso">Esso</a>, <a href="/wiki/Weatherford_International" title="Weatherford International">Weatherford International</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schlumberger_Limited" title="Schlumberger Limited">Schlumberger Limited</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baker_Hughes" title="Baker Hughes">Baker Hughes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Halliburton" title="Halliburton">Halliburton</a>, have organizations that specialize in the large-scale construction of the <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a> and providing specialized services required to operate a field profitably.
</p><p>More than 40,000 oil fields are scattered around the globe, on land and offshore. The largest are the <a href="/wiki/Ghawar_Field" title="Ghawar Field">Ghawar Field</a> in <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burgan_Field" title="Burgan Field">Burgan Field</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwait</a>, with more than 60 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1000000000_(number)" title="1000000000 (number)">billion</a> <a href="/wiki/Barrel_(unit)" title="Barrel (unit)">barrels</a> <span class="nowrap">(9.5×10<sup>9</sup> m<sup>3</sup>)</span> estimated in each. Most oil fields are much smaller. According to the US Department of Energy (Energy Information Administration), as of 2003 the US alone had over 30,000 oil fields.
</p><p>In the modern age, the location of oil fields with proven <a href="/wiki/Oil_reserves" title="Oil reserves">oil reserves</a> is a key underlying factor in many <a href="/wiki/Geopolitics" title="Geopolitics">geopolitical</a> conflicts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The term "oilfield" is also used as a shorthand to refer to the entire <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_industry" title="Petroleum industry">petroleum industry</a>. However, it is more accurate to divide the oil industry into three sectors:  upstream (crude production from wells and separation of water from oil), midstream (pipeline and tanker transport of crude) and downstream (refining and marketing of refined products).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_field">Gas field</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Petroleum_reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Gas field">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

The Offshore Support Vessel <i>Toisa Perseus</i> is shown in the foreground, illustrating part of the complex logistics of offshore oil and gas exploration and production.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">Natural gas</a> originates by the same geological <a href="/wiki/Cracking_(chemistry)" title="Cracking (chemistry)">thermal cracking</a> process that converts <a href="/wiki/Kerogen" title="Kerogen">kerogen</a> to <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a>.  As a consequence, oil and natural gas are often found together.  In common usage, deposits rich in oil are known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_field" title="Oil field">oil fields</a>, and deposits rich in natural gas are called natural gas fields. 
</p><p>In general, organic sediments buried in depths of 1,000 m to 6,000 m (at temperatures of 60 °<a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">C</a> to 150 °C) generate oil, while sediments buried deeper and at higher temperatures generate natural gas. The deeper the source, the "drier" the gas (that is, the smaller the proportion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_gas_condensate" title="Natural gas condensate">condensates</a> in the gas).  Because both oil and natural gas are lighter than water, they tend to rise from their sources until they either <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_seep" title="Petroleum seep">seep</a> to the surface or are trapped by a non-permeable stratigraphic trap.  They can be extracted from the trap by drilling.
</p><p>The largest natural gas field is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Pars_/_North_Dome_Gas-Condensate_field" title="South Pars / North Dome Gas-Condensate field">South Pars/Asalouyeh</a> gas field, which is shared between <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> and <a href="/wiki/Qatar" title="Qatar">Qatar</a>. The second largest natural gas field is the <a href="/wiki/Urengoy_gas_field" title="Urengoy gas field">Urengoy gas field</a>, and the third largest is the <a href="/wiki/Yamburg_gas_field" title="Yamburg gas field">Yamburg gas field</a>, both in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>.
</p><p>Like oil, natural gas is often found underwater in offshore gas fields such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Sea_Oil" title="North Sea Oil">North Sea</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corrib_Gas_Field" title="Corrib Gas Field">Corrib Gas Field</a> off <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, and near <a href="/wiki/Sable_Island" title="Sable Island">Sable Island</a>. The technology to extract and transport offshore natural gas is different from land-based fields. It uses a few, very large <a href="/wiki/Offshore_drilling" title="Offshore drilling">offshore drilling</a> rigs, due to the cost and logistical difficulties in working over water.
</p><p>Rising gas prices in the early 21st century encouraged drillers to revisit fields that previously were not considered economically viable. For example, in 2008 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freeport_McMoRan" title="Freeport McMoRan">McMoran Exploration</a> passed a drilling depth of over 32,000 feet (9754 m) (the deepest test well in the history of gas production) at the Blackbeard site in the Gulf of Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exxon_Mobil" title="Exxon Mobil">Exxon Mobil</a>'s drill rig there had reached 30,000 feet by 2006 without finding gas, before it abandoned the site.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Petroleum_reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil">Crude oil</a> is found in all oil reservoirs formed in the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a> from the remains of once-living things. Evidence indicates that millions of years of heat and <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> changed the remains of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microscopic" title="Microscopic">microscopic</a> <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animal</a> into oil and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>.
</p><p>Roy Nurmi, an interpretation adviser for <a href="/wiki/Schlumberger" title="Schlumberger">Schlumberger</a> oil field services company, described the process as follows:
</p>
<blockquote><p>Plankton and algae, proteins and the life that's floating in the sea, as it dies, falls to the bottom, and these organisms are going to be the source of our oil and gas. When they're buried with the accumulating sediment and reach an adequate temperature, something above 50 to 70 °C they start to cook. This transformation, this change, changes them into the liquid hydrocarbons that move and migrate, will become our oil and gas reservoir.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quote_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-quote-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>In addition to the <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_ecosystem" title="Aquatic ecosystem">aquatic</a> environment, which is usually a sea, but might also be a <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reef</a> or <a href="/wiki/Algal_mat" title="Algal mat">algal mat</a>, the formation of an oil or gas reservoir also requires a <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_basin" title="Sedimentary basin">sedimentary basin</a> that passes through four steps:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whatis_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-whatis-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Deep burial under sand and mud.</li>
<li>Pressure cooking.</li>
<li>Hydrocarbon migration from the source to the reservoir rock.</li>
<li>Trapping by impermeable rock.</li></ul>
<p>Timing is also an important consideration; it is suggested that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ohio_River_Valley" title="Ohio River Valley">Ohio River Valley</a> could have had as much oil as the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> at one time, but that it escaped due to a lack of traps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whatis_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-whatis-4">[4]</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>, on the other hand, endured millions of years of sea level changes that successfully resulted in the formation of more than 150 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oilfield" title="Oilfield">oilfields</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-northsea_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-northsea-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the process is generally the same, various environmental factors lead to the creation of a wide variety of reservoirs. Reservoirs exist anywhere from the land surface to 30,000 ft (9,000 m) below the surface and are a variety of shapes, sizes and ages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-variety_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-variety-6">[6]</a></sup> In recent years igneous reservoirs have become an important new field of oil exploration, especially in <a href="/wiki/Trachyte" title="Trachyte">trachyte</a> and <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basalt</a> formations. These two types of reservoirs differ in oil content and physical properties like <a href="/wiki/Fracture_(geology)" title="Fracture (geology)">fracture</a> connectivity, pore connectivity, and rock <a href="/wiki/Porosity" title="Porosity">porosity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Traps">Traps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Petroleum_reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Traps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A trap forms when the <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a> forces driving the upward migration of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrocarbons" title="Hydrocarbons">hydrocarbons</a> through a <a href="/wiki/Permeability_(earth_sciences)" title="Permeability (earth sciences)">permeable</a> rock cannot overcome the <a href="/wiki/Capillary_action" title="Capillary action">capillary forces</a> of a sealing medium.  The timing of trap formation relative to that of petroleum generation and migration is crucial to ensuring a reservoir can form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Petroleum_geology" title="Petroleum geology">Petroleum geologists</a> broadly classify traps into three categories that are based on their geological characteristics: the <a href="/wiki/Structural_trap" title="Structural trap">structural trap</a>, the stratigraphic trap and the far less common <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrodynamic_trap&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hydrodynamic trap (page does not exist)">hydrodynamic trap</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The trapping mechanisms for many petroleum reservoirs have characteristics from several categories and can be known as a combination trap.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Structural_traps">Structural traps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Petroleum_reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Structural traps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:92px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Structural_Trap_(Anticlinal).svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="413" data-file-width="640" height="58" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Structural_Trap_%28Anticlinal%29.svg/90px-Structural_Trap_%28Anticlinal%29.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Structural_Trap_%28Anticlinal%29.svg/135px-Structural_Trap_%28Anticlinal%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Structural_Trap_%28Anticlinal%29.svg/180px-Structural_Trap_%28Anticlinal%29.svg.png 2x" width="90"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Structural_Trap_(Anticlinal).svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fold (structural) trap</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:92px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Structural_Trap_Fault.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="413" data-file-width="640" height="58" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Structural_Trap_Fault.svg/90px-Structural_Trap_Fault.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Structural_Trap_Fault.svg/135px-Structural_Trap_Fault.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Structural_Trap_Fault.svg/180px-Structural_Trap_Fault.svg.png 2x" width="90"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Structural_Trap_Fault.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fault (structural) trap</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Structural_trap" title="Structural trap">Structural traps</a> are formed as a result of changes in the structure of the subsurface due to processes such as folding and faulting, leading to the formation of <a href="/wiki/Dome_(geology)" title="Dome (geology)">domes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anticline" title="Anticline">anticlines</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geological_fold" title="Geological fold">folds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>  Examples of this kind of trap are an <a href="/wiki/Structural_trap#Anticlinal_trap" title="Structural trap">anticline trap</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> a <a href="/wiki/Structural_trap#Fault_trap" title="Structural trap">fault trap</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Structural_trap#Salt_dome_trap" title="Structural trap">salt dome trap</a>. (see <a href="/wiki/Salt_dome" title="Salt dome">salt dome</a>)
</p><p>They are more easily delineated and more prospective than their stratigraphic counterparts, with the majority of the world's petroleum reserves being found in structural traps.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Stratigraphic_traps">Stratigraphic traps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Petroleum_reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Stratigraphic traps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Stratigraphy" title="Stratigraphy">Stratigraphic</a> traps</i> are formed as a result of lateral and vertical variations in the thickness, texture, <a href="/wiki/Porosity" title="Porosity">porosity</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lithology" title="Lithology">lithology</a> of the reservoir rock.  Examples of this type of trap are an <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Unconformity_trap&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Unconformity trap (page does not exist)">unconformity trap</a>, a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lens_trap&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lens trap (page does not exist)">lens trap</a> and a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Reef_trap&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Reef trap (page does not exist)">reef trap</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrodynamic_traps">Hydrodynamic traps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Petroleum_reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Hydrodynamic traps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrodynamic_traps&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hydrodynamic traps (page does not exist)">Hydrodynamic traps</a> are a far less common type of trap.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>  They are caused by the differences in water pressure, that are associated with water flow, creating a tilt of the hydrocarbon-water contact.
</p>
<h4><span id="Seal.2F_cap_rock"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Seal/_cap_rock">Seal/ cap rock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Petroleum_reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Seal/ cap rock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The seal is a fundamental part of the trap that prevents hydrocarbons from further upward migration.
</p><p>A capillary seal is formed when the <a href="/wiki/Capillary_pressure" title="Capillary pressure">capillary pressure</a> across the pore throats is greater than or equal to the buoyancy pressure of the migrating hydrocarbons.  They do not allow fluids to migrate across them until their integrity is disrupted, causing them to leak.  There are two types of capillary seal <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> whose classifications are based on the preferential mechanism of leaking: the hydraulic seal and the membrane seal.
</p><p>The membrane seal will leak whenever the pressure differential across the seal exceeds the threshold displacement pressure, allowing fluids to migrate through the pore spaces in the seal.  It will leak just enough to bring the pressure differential below that of the displacement pressure and will reseal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>The hydraulic seal occurs in rocks that have a significantly higher displacement pressure such that the pressure required for tension fracturing is actually lower than the pressure required for fluid displacement – for example, in evaporites or very tight shales.   The rock will <a href="/wiki/Fracture" title="Fracture">fracture</a> when the pore pressure is greater than both its minimum stress and its tensile strength then reseal when the pressure reduces and the fractures close.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Estimating_reserves">Estimating reserves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Petroleum_reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Estimating reserves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the discovery of a reservoir, a petroleum engineer will seek to build a better picture of the accumulation.  In a simple textbook example of a uniform reservoir, the first stage is to conduct a seismic survey to determine the possible size of the trap.  Appraisal wells can be used to determine the location of oil–water contact and with it the height of the oil bearing sands.  Often coupled with seismic data, it is possible to estimate the volume of oil bearing reservoir.
</p><p>The next step is to use information from appraisal wells to estimate the porosity of the rock.  The porosity, or the percentage of the total volume that contains fluids rather than solid rock, is 20–35% or less.  It can give information on the actual capacity.  Laboratory testing can determine the characteristics of the reservoir fluids, particularly the expansion factor of the oil, or how much the oil expands when brought from the high pressure and high temperature of the reservoir to a "stock tank" at the surface.
</p><p>With such information, it is possible to estimate how many "stock tank" <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barrel_(volume)" title="Barrel (volume)">barrels</a> of oil are located in the reservoir.  Such oil is called the <a href="/wiki/Oil_in_place" title="Oil in place">stock tank oil initially in place (STOIIP)</a>.  As a result of studying factors such as the permeability of the rock (how easily fluids can flow through the rock) and possible drive mechanisms, it is possible to estimate the recovery factor, or what proportion of oil in place can be reasonably expected to be produced. The recovery factor is commonly 30–35%, giving a value for the recoverable reserves.
</p><p>The difficulty is that reservoirs are not uniform.  They have variable porosities and permeabilities and may be compartmentalised, with fractures and faults breaking them up and complicating fluid flow. For this reason, <a href="/wiki/Reservoir_modeling" title="Reservoir modeling">computer modeling</a> of economically viable reservoirs is often carried out. <a href="/wiki/Geologist" title="Geologist">Geologists</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geophysicist" title="Geophysicist">geophysicists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reservoir_engineering" title="Reservoir engineering">reservoir engineers</a> work together to build a model which allows <a href="/wiki/Reservoir_simulation" title="Reservoir simulation">simulation</a> of the flow of fluids in the reservoir, leading to an improved estimate of reserves.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Petroleum_reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To obtain the contents of the oil reservoir, it is usually necessary to <a href="/wiki/Oil_well" title="Oil well">drill</a> into the Earth's crust, although surface oil seeps exist in some parts of the world, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Brea_tar_pits" title="La Brea tar pits">La Brea tar pits</a> in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, and numerous seeps in <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Drive_mechanisms">Drive mechanisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Petroleum_reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Drive mechanisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A virgin reservoir may be under sufficient pressure to push hydrocarbons to surface. As the fluids are produced, the pressure will often decline, and production will falter. The reservoir may respond to the withdrawal of fluid in a way that tends to maintain the pressure.  Artificial drive methods may be necessary.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution-gas_drive">Solution-gas drive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Petroleum_reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Solution-gas drive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This mechanism (also known as depletion drive) depends on the associated gas of the oil. The virgin reservoir may be entirely liquid, but will be expected to have gaseous hydrocarbons in solution due to the pressure. As the reservoir depletes, the pressure falls below the <a href="/wiki/Bubble_point" title="Bubble point">bubble point</a>, and the gas comes out of solution to form a gas cap at the top. This gas cap pushes down on the liquid helping to maintain pressure.
</p><p>This occurs when the natural gas is in a cap below the oil. When the well is drilled the lowered pressure above means that the oil expands. As the pressure is reduced it reaches bubble point and subsequently the gas bubbles drive the oil to the surface. The bubbles then reach critical saturation and flow together as a single gas phase. Beyond this point and below this pressure the gas phase flows out more rapidly than the oil because of its lowered viscosity. More free gas is produced and eventually the energy source is depleted. In some cases depending on the geology the gas may migrate to the top of the oil and form a secondary gas cap.
</p><p>Some energy may be supplied by water, gas in water, or compressed rock. These are usually minor contributions with respect to hydrocarbon expansion.
</p><p>By properly managing the production rates, greater benefits can be had from solution-gas drives. Secondary recovery involves the injection of gas or water to maintain reservoir pressure. The gas/oil ratio and the oil production rate are stable until the reservoir pressure drops below the bubble point when critical gas saturation is reached. When the gas is exhausted, the gas/oil ratio and the oil rate drops, the reservoir pressure has been reduced and the reservoir energy exhausted.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_cap_drive">Gas cap drive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Petroleum_reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Gas cap drive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In reservoirs already having a gas cap (the virgin pressure is already below bubble point), the gas cap expands with the depletion of the reservoir, pushing down on the liquid sections applying extra pressure.
</p><p>This is present in the reservoir if there is more gas than can be dissolved in the reservoir. The gas will often migrate to the crest of the structure. It is compressed on top of the oil reserve, as the oil is produced the cap helps to push the oil out. Over time the gas cap moves down and infiltrates the oil and eventually the well will begin to produce more and more gas until it produces only gas. It is best to manage the gas cap effectively; that is, placing the oil wells such that the gas cap will not reach them until the maximum amount of oil is produced. Also a high production rate may cause the gas to migrate downward into the production interval. In this case over time the reservoir pressure depletion is not as steep as in the case of solution based gas drive. In this case the oil rate will not decline as steeply but will depend also on the placement of the well with respect to the gas cap.
</p><p>As with other drive mechanisms, water or gas injection can be used to maintain reservoir pressure. When a gas cap is coupled with water influx the recovery mechanism can be highly efficient.
</p>
<h3><span id="Aquifer_.28water.29_drive"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Aquifer_(water)_drive">Aquifer (water) drive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Petroleum_reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Aquifer (water) drive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Water (usually salty) may be present below the hydrocarbons.  Water, as with all liquids, is compressible to a small degree.  As the hydrocarbons are depleted, the reduction in pressure in the reservoir allows the water to expand slightly.  Although this unit expansion is minute, if the aquifer is large enough this will translate into a large increase in volume, which will push up on the hydrocarbons, maintaining pressure.
</p><p>With a water-drive reservoir the decline in reservoir pressure is very slight; in some cases the reservoir pressure may remain unchanged. The gas/oil ratio also remains stable. The oil rate will remain fairly stable until the water reaches the well. In time, the water cut will increase and the well will be watered out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>The water may be present in an <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifer</a> (but rarely one replenished with surface water). This water gradually replaces the volume of oil and gas that is produced out of the well, given that the production rate is equivalent to the aquifer activity. That is, the aquifer is being replenished from some natural water influx. If the water begins to be produced along with the oil, the recovery rate may become uneconomical owing to the higher lifting and water disposal costs.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_and_gas_injection">Water and gas injection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Petroleum_reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Water and gas injection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Water_injection_(oil_production)" title="Water injection (oil production)">Water injection (oil production)</a></div>
<p>If the natural drives are insufficient, as they very often are, then the pressure can be artificially maintained by injecting water into the aquifer or gas into the gas cap.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gravity_drainage">Gravity drainage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Petroleum_reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Gravity drainage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The force of gravity will cause the oil to move downward of the gas and upward of the water. If vertical permeability exists then recovery rates may be even better.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_and_gas_condensate_reservoirs">Gas and gas condensate reservoirs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Petroleum_reservoir&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Gas and gas condensate reservoirs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These occur if the reservoir conditions allow the hydrocarbons to exist as a gas. Retrieval is a matter of gas expansion. Recovery from a closed reservoir (i.e., no water drive) is very good, especially if bottom hole pressure is reduced to a minimum (usually done with compressors at the well head). Any produced liquids are light coloured to colourless, with a gravity higher than 45 API. Gas cycling is the process where dry gas is injected and produced along with condensed liquid.
</p>
