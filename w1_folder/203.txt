<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Dipole</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about the electromagnetic phenomenon. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Dipole_(disambiguation)" title="Dipole (disambiguation)">dipole (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:VFPt_Dipole_field.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="700" height="214" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/VFPt_Dipole_field.svg/250px-VFPt_Dipole_field.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/VFPt_Dipole_field.svg/375px-VFPt_Dipole_field.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/VFPt_Dipole_field.svg/500px-VFPt_Dipole_field.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:VFPt_Dipole_field.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The magnetic field of a sphere with a north magnetic pole at the top and a  south magnetic pole at the bottom. By comparison, <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth's magnetic field">Earth</a> has a <i>south</i> magnetic pole near its north geographic pole and a <i>north</i> magnetic pole near its south pole.</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetism" title="Electromagnetism">electromagnetism</a>, there are two kinds of <b>dipoles</b>:
</p>
<ul><li>An <a href="/wiki/Electric_dipole_moment" title="Electric dipole moment">electric dipole</a> is a separation of positive and negative charges. The simplest example of this is a pair of <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">electric charges</a> of equal magnitude but opposite sign, separated by some (usually small) distance. A permanent electric dipole is called an <a href="/wiki/Electret" title="Electret">electret</a>.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_dipole" title="Magnetic dipole">magnetic dipole</a> is a closed circulation of <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric current</a>. A simple example of this is a single loop of wire with some constant current through it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Dipoles can be characterized by their dipole moment, a vector quantity. For the simple electric dipole given above, the <a href="/wiki/Electric_dipole_moment" title="Electric dipole moment">electric dipole moment</a> points from the negative charge towards the positive charge, and has a magnitude equal to the strength of each charge times the separation between the charges. (To be precise: for the definition of the dipole moment, one should always consider the "dipole limit", where, for example, the distance of the generating charges should <i>converge</i> to 0 while simultaneously, the charge strength should <i>diverge</i> to infinity in such a way that the product remains a positive constant.)
</p><p>For the current loop, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_dipole_moment" title="Magnetic dipole moment">magnetic dipole moment</a> points through the loop (according to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right_hand_grip_rule" title="Right hand grip rule">right hand grip rule</a>), with a magnitude equal to the current in the loop times the area of the loop.
</p><p>In addition to current loops, the <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a>, among other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fundamental_particle" title="Fundamental particle">fundamental particles</a>, has a magnetic dipole moment. That is because it generates a <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a> that is identical to that generated by a very small current loop. However, the electron's magnetic moment is not due to a current loop, but is instead an <a href="/wiki/Intrinsic_and_extrinsic_properties" title="Intrinsic and extrinsic properties">intrinsic</a> property of the electron.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It is also possible that the electron has an <i>electric</i> dipole moment although it has not yet been observed (see <a href="/wiki/Electron_electric_dipole_moment" title="Electron electric dipole moment">electron electric dipole moment</a> for more information).
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dipole_Contour.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="449" data-file-width="558" height="201" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Dipole_Contour.svg/250px-Dipole_Contour.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Dipole_Contour.svg/375px-Dipole_Contour.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Dipole_Contour.svg/500px-Dipole_Contour.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dipole_Contour.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Contour plot of the <a href="/wiki/Electrostatics#Electrostatic_potential" title="Electrostatics">electrostatic potential</a> of a horizontally oriented electrical dipole of finite size. Strong colors indicate highest and lowest potential (where the opposing charges of the dipole are located).</div></div></div>
<p>A permanent magnet, such as a bar magnet, owes its magnetism to the intrinsic magnetic dipole moment of the electron. The two ends of a bar magnet are referred to as poles (not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_monopole" title="Magnetic monopole">monopoles</a>), and may be labeled "north" and "south". In terms of the Earth's magnetic field, they are respectively "north-seeking" and "south-seeking" poles: if the magnet were freely suspended in the Earth's magnetic field, the north-seeking pole would point towards the north and the south-seeking pole would point towards the south. The dipole moment of the bar magnet points from its magnetic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_pole" title="South pole">south</a> to its magnetic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_pole" title="North pole">north pole</a>. The north pole of a bar magnet in a <a href="/wiki/Compass" title="Compass">compass</a> points north. However, that means that Earth's geomagnetic north pole is the <i>south</i> pole (south-seeking pole) of its dipole moment and vice versa.
</p><p>The only known mechanisms for the creation of magnetic dipoles are by current loops or quantum-mechanical <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">spin</a> since the existence of <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_monopole" title="Magnetic monopole">magnetic monopoles</a> has never been experimentally demonstrated.
</p><p>The term comes from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">δίς</span> (<i>dis</i>), "twice"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">πόλος</span> (<i>polos</i>), "axis".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dipole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:VFPt_dipole_electric.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="600" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/VFPt_dipole_electric.svg/250px-VFPt_dipole_electric.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/VFPt_dipole_electric.svg/375px-VFPt_dipole_electric.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/VFPt_dipole_electric.svg/500px-VFPt_dipole_electric.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:VFPt_dipole_electric.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Electric field lines of two opposing charges separated by a finite distance.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:VFPt_dipole_magnetic2.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="600" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/VFPt_dipole_magnetic2.svg/250px-VFPt_dipole_magnetic2.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/VFPt_dipole_magnetic2.svg/375px-VFPt_dipole_magnetic2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/VFPt_dipole_magnetic2.svg/500px-VFPt_dipole_magnetic2.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:VFPt_dipole_magnetic2.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Magnetic field lines of a ring current of finite diameter.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:VFPt_dipole_point.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="600" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/VFPt_dipole_point.svg/250px-VFPt_dipole_point.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/VFPt_dipole_point.svg/375px-VFPt_dipole_point.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/VFPt_dipole_point.svg/500px-VFPt_dipole_point.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:VFPt_dipole_point.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Field lines of a point dipole of any type, electric, magnetic, acoustic, etc.</div></div></div>
<p>A <i>physical dipole</i> consists of two equal and opposite point charges: in the literal sense, two poles. Its field at large distances (i.e., distances large in comparison to the separation of the poles) depends almost entirely on the dipole moment as defined above. A <i>point (electric) dipole</i> is the limit obtained by letting the separation tend to 0 while keeping the dipole moment fixed. The field of a point dipole has a particularly simple form, and the order-1 term in the <a href="/wiki/Multipole_expansion" title="Multipole expansion">multipole expansion</a> is precisely the point dipole field.
</p><p>Although there are no known <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_monopole" title="Magnetic monopole">magnetic monopoles</a> in nature, there are magnetic dipoles in the form of the quantum-mechanical <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">spin</a> associated with particles such as <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> (although the accurate description of such effects falls outside of classical electromagnetism). A theoretical magnetic <i>point dipole</i> has a magnetic field of exactly the same form as the electric field of an electric point dipole. A very small current-carrying loop is approximately a magnetic point dipole; the magnetic dipole moment of such a loop is the product of the current flowing in the loop and the (vector) area of the loop.
</p><p>Any configuration of charges or currents has a 'dipole moment', which describes the dipole whose field is the best approximation, at large distances, to that of the given configuration.  This is simply one term in the multipole expansion when the total charge ("monopole moment") is 0—as it <i>always</i> is for the magnetic case, since there are no magnetic monopoles.  The dipole term is the dominant one at large distances:  Its field falls off in proportion to <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;"><i>r</i><sup>3</sup></span></span>, as compared to <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;"><i>r</i><sup>4</sup></span></span> for the next (<a href="/wiki/Quadrupole" title="Quadrupole">quadrupole</a>) term and higher powers of <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;"><i>r</i></span></span> for higher terms, or <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;"><i>r</i><sup>2</sup></span></span> for the monopole term.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Molecular_dipoles">Molecular dipoles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dipole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Molecular dipoles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Chemical_polarity" title="Chemical polarity">chemical polarity</a></div>
<p>Many <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> have such dipole moments due to non-uniform distributions of positive and negative charges on the various atoms.  Such is the case with polar compounds like <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">hydrogen fluoride</a> (HF), where <a href="/wiki/Electron_density" title="Electron density">electron density</a> is shared unequally between atoms. Therefore, a molecule's dipole is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_dipole" title="Electric dipole">electric dipole</a> with an inherent electric field which should not be confused with a <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_dipole" title="Magnetic dipole">magnetic dipole</a> which generates a magnetic field.
</p><p>The physical chemist <a href="/wiki/Peter_Debye" title="Peter Debye">Peter J. W. Debye</a> was the first scientist to study molecular dipoles extensively, and, as a consequence, dipole moments are measured in units named <i><a href="/wiki/Debye" title="Debye">debye</a></i> in his honor.
</p><p>For molecules there are three types of dipoles:
</p>
<dl><dt>Permanent dipoles</dt>
<dd>These occur when two atoms in a molecule have substantially different <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">electronegativity</a>: One atom attracts electrons more than another, becoming more negative, while the other atom becomes more positive. A molecule with a permanent dipole moment is called a <i>polar</i> molecule. See <a href="/wiki/Intermolecular_force#Dipole-dipole_interactions" title="Intermolecular force">dipole–dipole attractions</a>.</dd>
<dt>Instantaneous dipoles</dt>
<dd>These occur due to chance when <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> happen to be more concentrated in one place than another in a <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecule</a>, creating a temporary dipole. See <a href="/wiki/London_dispersion_force" title="London dispersion force">instantaneous dipole</a>.</dd>
<dt>Induced dipoles</dt>
<dd>These can occur when one molecule with a permanent dipole repels another molecule's electrons, <i>inducing</i> a dipole moment in that molecule. A molecule is <i>polarized</i> when it carries an induced dipole. See <a href="/wiki/Intermolecular_force#Debye_(induced_dipole)_force" title="Intermolecular force">induced-dipole attraction</a>.</dd></dl>
<p>More generally, an induced dipole of <i>any</i> polarizable charge distribution <i>ρ</i> (remember that a molecule has a charge distribution) is caused by an electric field external to <i>ρ</i>. This field may, for instance, originate from an ion or polar molecule in the vicinity of <i>ρ</i> or may be macroscopic (e.g., a molecule between the plates of a charged <a href="/wiki/Capacitor" title="Capacitor">capacitor</a>). The size of the induced dipole moment is equal to the product of the strength of the external field and the dipole <a href="/wiki/Polarizability" title="Polarizability">polarizability</a> of <i>ρ</i>.
</p><p>Dipole moment values can be obtained from measurement of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dielectric_constant" title="Dielectric constant">dielectric constant</a>. Some typical gas phase values in <a href="/wiki/Debye" title="Debye">debye</a> units are:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>: 0</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>: 0.112 D</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>: 0.53 D</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Phosgene" title="Phosgene">phosgene</a>: 1.17 D</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a>: 1.85 D</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide" title="Hydrogen cyanide">hydrogen cyanide</a>: 2.98 D</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cyanamide" title="Cyanamide">cyanamide</a>: 4.27 D</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Potassium_bromide" title="Potassium bromide">potassium bromide</a>: 10.41 D</li></ul>

<p>Potassium bromide (KBr) has one of the highest dipole moments because it is an <a href="/wiki/Ionic_compound" title="Ionic compound">ionic compound</a> that exists as a molecule in the gas phase.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:H2O_2D_labelled.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="350" data-file-width="800" height="70" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/H2O_2D_labelled.svg/160px-H2O_2D_labelled.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/H2O_2D_labelled.svg/240px-H2O_2D_labelled.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/H2O_2D_labelled.svg/320px-H2O_2D_labelled.svg.png 2x" width="160"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:H2O_2D_labelled.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The bent molecule H<sub>2</sub>O has a net dipole. The two bond dipoles do not cancel.</div></div></div>
<p>The overall dipole moment of a molecule may be approximated as a <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_vector#Addition_and_subtraction" title="Euclidean vector">vector sum</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bond_dipole_moment" title="Bond dipole moment">bond dipole moments</a>. As a vector sum it depends on the relative orientation of the bonds, so that from the dipole moment information can be deduced about the <a href="/wiki/Molecular_geometry" title="Molecular geometry">molecular geometry</a>.
</p><p>
For example, the zero dipole of CO<sub>2</sub> implies that the two C=O bond dipole moments cancel so that the molecule must be linear. For H<sub>2</sub>O the O−H bond moments do not cancel because the molecule is bent. For ozone (O<sub>3</sub>) which is also a bent molecule, the bond dipole moments are not zero even though the O−O bonds are between similar atoms. This agrees with the Lewis structures for the resonance forms of ozone which show a positive charge on the central oxygen atom. </p><center><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ozone-resonance-Lewis-2D.png" title="Resonance Lewis structures of the ozone molecule"><img alt="Resonance Lewis structures of the ozone molecule" data-file-height="295" data-file-width="1100" height="107" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Ozone-resonance-Lewis-2D.png/400px-Ozone-resonance-Lewis-2D.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Ozone-resonance-Lewis-2D.png/600px-Ozone-resonance-Lewis-2D.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Ozone-resonance-Lewis-2D.png/800px-Ozone-resonance-Lewis-2D.png 2x" width="400"/></a></center>

<p>An example in organic chemistry of the role of geometry in determining dipole moment is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cis-trans_isomerism" title="Cis-trans isomerism"><i>cis</i> and <i>trans</i> isomers</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1,2-dichloroethene" title="1,2-dichloroethene">1,2-dichloroethene</a>. In the <i>cis</i> isomer the two polar C−Cl bonds are on the same side of the C=C double bond and the molecular dipole moment is 1.90 D. In the <i>trans</i> isomer, the dipole moment is zero because the two C−Cl bonds are on opposite sides of the C=C and cancel (and the two bond moments for the much less polar C−H bonds also cancel).
</p><p>Another example of the role of molecular geometry is <a href="/wiki/Boron_trifluoride" title="Boron trifluoride">boron trifluoride</a>, which has three polar bonds with a difference in <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">electronegativity</a> greater than the traditionally cited threshold of 1.7 for <a href="/wiki/Ionic_bonding" title="Ionic bonding">ionic bonding</a>. However, due to the equilateral triangular distribution of the fluoride ions about the boron cation center, the molecule <b>as a whole</b> does not exhibit any identifiable pole: one cannot construct a plane that divides the molecule into a net negative part and a net positive part.
</p>

<p>In the case of open-shell atoms with degenerate  energy levels, one could define a dipole moment by the aid of the first-order <a href="/wiki/Stark_effect" title="Stark effect">Stark effect</a>. This gives a non-vanishing dipole (by definition proportional to a non-vanishing first-order Stark shift) only if some of the wavefunctions belonging to the degenerate energies have opposite <a href="/wiki/Parity_(physics)" title="Parity (physics)">parity</a>; i.e., have different behavior under inversion. This is a rare occurrence, but happens for the excited H-atom, where 2s and 2p states are "accidentally" degenerate (see article <a href="/wiki/Laplace%E2%80%93Runge%E2%80%93Lenz_vector#Quantum_mechanics_of_the_hydrogen_atom" title="Laplace–Runge–Lenz vector">Laplace–Runge–Lenz vector</a> for the origin of this degeneracy) and have opposite parity (2s is even and 2p is odd).
