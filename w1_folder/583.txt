<p><b>Asphyxia</b> or <b>asphyxiation</b> is a condition of severely deficient supply of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Human_body" title="Human body">body</a> that arises from abnormal <a href="/wiki/Breathing" title="Breathing">breathing</a>. An example of asphyxia is <a href="/wiki/Choking" title="Choking">choking</a>. Asphyxia causes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generalized_hypoxia" title="Generalized hypoxia">generalized hypoxia</a>, which affects primarily the tissues and organs. There are many circumstances that can induce asphyxia, all of which are characterized by an inability of an individual to acquire sufficient oxygen through breathing for an extended period of time. Asphyxia can cause coma or death.
</p><p>In 2015 about 9.8 million cases of unintentional suffocation occurred which resulted in 35,600 deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GBD2015Pre_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-GBD2015Pre-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GBD2015De_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-GBD2015De-2">[2]</a></sup> The word asphyxia is from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">α-</span> "without" and <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">σφύξις</span> <i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek language text">sphyxis</i>, "squeeze" (throb of heart).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cause">Cause</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asphyxia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Cause">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Situations that can cause asphyxia include but are not limited to: the constriction or obstruction of airways, such as from <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laryngospasm" title="Laryngospasm">laryngospasm</a>, or simple blockage from the presence of foreign materials; from being in environments where oxygen is not readily accessible: such as underwater, in a low oxygen atmosphere, or in a vacuum; environments where sufficiently oxygenated air is present, but cannot be adequately breathed because of air contamination such as excessive smoke.
</p><p>Other causes of oxygen deficiency include 
but are not limited to:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Acute_respiratory_distress_syndrome" title="Acute respiratory distress syndrome">Acute respiratory distress syndrome</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide_poisoning" title="Carbon monoxide poisoning">Carbon monoxide inhalation</a>, such as that from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Car_exhaust" title="Car exhaust">car exhaust</a> and the smoke's emission from a lighted <a href="/wiki/Cigarette" title="Cigarette">cigarette</a>: carbon monoxide has a higher affinity than oxygen to the <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a> in the blood's red blood corpuscles, bonding with it tenaciously, and, in the process, displacing oxygen and preventing the blood from transporting oxygen around the body</li>
<li>Contact with certain chemicals, including <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_agent" title="Pulmonary agent">pulmonary agents</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Phosgene" title="Phosgene">phosgene</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Blood_agent" title="Blood agent">blood agents</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide" title="Hydrogen cyanide">hydrogen cyanide</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Drowning" title="Drowning">Drowning</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Drug_overdose" title="Drug overdose">Drug overdose</a></li>
<li>Exposure to extreme low pressure or <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a> to the pattern (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_exposure" title="Space exposure">space exposure</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hanging" title="Hanging">Hanging</a>, whether suspension or short drop hanging</li>
<li>Self-induced <a href="/wiki/Hypocapnia" title="Hypocapnia">hypocapnia</a> by <a href="/wiki/Hyperventilation" title="Hyperventilation">hyperventilation</a>, as in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shallow_water_blackout" title="Shallow water blackout">shallow water</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deep_water_blackout" title="Deep water blackout">deep water blackout</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Choking_game" title="Choking game">choking game</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Inert_gas_asphyxiation" title="Inert gas asphyxiation">Inert gas asphyxiation</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congenital_central_hypoventilation_syndrome" title="Congenital central hypoventilation syndrome">Congenital central hypoventilation syndrome</a>, or primary alveolar hypoventilation, a disorder of the autonomic nervous system in which a patient must consciously breathe; although it is often said that persons with this disease will die if they fall asleep, this is not usually the case</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Respiratory_disease" title="Respiratory disease">Respiratory diseases</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sleep_apnea" title="Sleep apnea">Sleep apnea</a></li>
<li>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seizure" title="Seizure">seizure</a> which stops breathing activity</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Strangling" title="Strangling">Strangling</a></li>
<li>Breaking the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wind_pipe" title="Wind pipe">wind pipe</a>.</li>
<li>Prolonged exposure to <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine gas</a></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Smothering">Smothering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asphyxia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Smothering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Smother" redirects here. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Smother_(disambiguation)" title="Smother (disambiguation)">Smother (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>Smothering is the mechanical obstruction of the flow of air from the environment into the mouth and/or nostrils, for instance, by covering the mouth and nose with a hand, pillow, or a plastic bag.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferris_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ferris-4">[4]</a></sup> Smothering can be either partial or complete, where partial indicates that the person being smothered is able to inhale some air, although less than required. In a normal situation, smothering requires at least partial obstruction of both the nasal cavities and the mouth to lead to asphyxia. Smothering with the hands or chest is used in some <a href="/wiki/Combat_sport" title="Combat sport">combat sports</a> to distract the opponent, and create openings for <a href="/wiki/Transition_(grappling)" title="Transition (grappling)">transitions</a>, as the opponent is forced to react to the smothering.
</p><p>In some cases, when performing certain routines, smothering is combined with simultaneous compressive asphyxia. One example is overlay, in which an adult accidentally rolls over onto an infant during <a href="/wiki/Co-sleeping" title="Co-sleeping">co-sleeping</a>, an accident that often goes unnoticed and is mistakenly thought to be <a href="/wiki/Sudden_infant_death_syndrome" title="Sudden infant death syndrome">sudden infant death syndrome</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ferris_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ferris-4">[4]</a></sup> Other accidents involving a similar mechanism are <a href="/wiki/Cave-in" title="Cave-in">cave-ins</a> or when an individual is buried in sand or grain.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Homicide" title="Homicide">homicidal</a> cases, the term burking is often ascribed to a killing method that involves simultaneous smothering and compression of the torso.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dimaio_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-dimaio-5">[5]</a></sup> The term "burking" comes from the method <a href="/wiki/Burke_and_Hare_murders" title="Burke and Hare murders">William Burke</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Hare_(murderer)" title="William Hare (murderer)">William Hare</a> used to kill their victims during the <a href="/wiki/Burke_and_Hare_murders" title="Burke and Hare murders">West Port murders</a>. They killed the usually intoxicated victims by sitting on their chests and suffocating them by putting a hand over their nose and mouth, while using the other hand to push the victim's jaw up. The corpses had no visible injuries, and were supplied to medical schools for money.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Compressive_asphyxia">Compressive asphyxia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asphyxia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Compressive asphyxia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Positional_asphyxia" title="Positional asphyxia">Positional asphyxia</a></div>
<p>Compressive asphyxia (also called chest compression) is mechanically limiting expansion of the lungs by compressing the torso, hence interfering with breathing. Compressive asphyxia occurs when the chest or abdomen is compressed <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location#Usage_in_human_anatomy" title="Anatomical terms of location">posteriorly</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jones_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-jones-7">[7]</a></sup> In accidents, the term <a href="/wiki/Traumatic_asphyxia" title="Traumatic asphyxia">traumatic asphyxia</a> or crush asphyxia usually refers to compressive asphyxia resulting from being crushed or pinned under a large weight or force. An example of traumatic asphyxia includes cases where an individual has been using a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jack_(mechanical)" title="Jack (mechanical)">car-jack</a> to repair a car from below, and is crushed under the weight of the vehicle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dimaio_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-dimaio-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pythonidae" title="Pythonidae">Pythons</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anacondas" title="Anacondas">anacondas</a>, and other constrictor snakes kill through compressive asphyxia. In cases of co-sleeping ("overlay"), the weight of an adult or large child may compress an infant's chest, preventing proper expansion of the chest. Risk factors include large or obese adults, parental fatigue or impairment (sedation by drugs or alcohol) of the co-sleeping adult and a small shared sleeping space (for example, both adult and infant sharing a couch).
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Stampede#Human_stampedes" title="Stampede">fatal crowd disasters</a>, compressive asphyxia from being crushed against the crowd causes the large part of the deaths, rather than <a href="/wiki/Blunt_trauma" title="Blunt trauma">blunt trauma</a> from trampling. This is what occurred at the <a href="/wiki/1971_Ibrox_disaster" title="1971 Ibrox disaster">Ibrox disaster</a> in 1971, where 66 <a href="/wiki/Rangers_F.C." title="Rangers F.C.">Rangers</a> fans died; the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1979_The_Who_concert_disaster" title="1979 The Who concert disaster">1979 The Who concert disaster</a> where 11 died; the <a href="/wiki/Luzhniki_disaster" title="Luzhniki disaster">Luzhniki disaster</a> in 1982, when 66 <a href="/wiki/FC_Spartak_Moscow" title="FC Spartak Moscow">FC Spartak Moscow</a> fans died; and at the <a href="/wiki/Hillsborough_disaster" title="Hillsborough disaster">Hillsborough disaster</a> in 1989, 96 <a href="/wiki/Liverpool_F.C." title="Liverpool F.C.">Liverpool</a> fans were crushed to death in an overcrowded terrace, 95 of the 96 victims died from compressive asphyxia, with 93 dying directly from it and 2 others dying from related complications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In confined spaces, people push and lean against each other; evidence from bent steel railings in several fatal crowd accidents have shown horizontal forces over 4500 N (equivalent to a weight of approximately 450 kg, or 1014 lbs). In cases where people have stacked up on each other forming a human pile, estimations have been made of around 380 kg (838 lbs) of compressive weight in the lowest layer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>"Positional" or "restraint" asphyxia is when a person is restrained and left alone prone, such as in a police vehicle, and is unable to reposition himself or herself in order to breathe. The death can be in the vehicle, or following loss of consciousness to be followed by death while in a coma, having presented with anoxic brain damage. The asphyxia can be caused by facial compression, neck compression, or chest compression. This occurs mostly during restraint and handcuffing situations by law enforcement, including psychiatric incidents. The weight of the restraint(s) doing the compression may contribute to what is attributed to positional asphyxia. Therefore, passive deaths following custody restraint that are presumed to be the result of positional asphyxia may actually be examples of asphyxia occurring during the restraint process.
</p><p>Chest compression is also featured in various <a href="/wiki/Grappling" title="Grappling">grappling</a> combat sports, where it is sometimes called wringing. Such techniques are used either to tire the opponent or as complementary or distractive moves in combination with <a href="/wiki/Grappling_hold#Pinning_hold" title="Grappling hold">pinning holds</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ohlenkamp_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ohlenkamp-10">[10]</a></sup> or sometimes even as <a href="/wiki/Grappling_hold#Submission_hold" title="Grappling hold">submission holds</a>. Examples of chest compression include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knee-on-stomach_position" title="Knee-on-stomach position">knee-on-stomach position</a>; or techniques such as leg scissors (also referred to as body scissors and in <a href="/wiki/Bud%C5%8D" title="Budō">budō</a> referred to as <i>do-jime</i>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> 胴絞, "trunk strangle" or "body triangle")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ijf_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-ijf-12">[12]</a></sup> where a participant wraps his or her legs around the opponent's midsection and squeezes them together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pressing_(execution)" title="Pressing (execution)">Pressing</a> is a form of <a href="/wiki/Torture" title="Torture">torture</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Execution" title="Execution">execution</a> that works through asphyxia e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burking" title="Burking">burking</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Perinatal_asphyxia">Perinatal asphyxia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asphyxia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Perinatal asphyxia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Perinatal_asphyxia" title="Perinatal asphyxia">Perinatal asphyxia</a></div>
<p>Perinatal asphyxia is the medical condition resulting from deprivation of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(medical)" title="Hypoxia (medical)">hypoxia</a>) to a newborn infant long enough to cause apparent harm. It results most commonly from a drop in maternal <a href="/wiki/Blood_pressure" title="Blood pressure">blood pressure</a> or interference during <a href="/wiki/Childbirth" title="Childbirth">delivery</a> with blood flow to the infant's <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a>. This can occur as a result of inadequate <a href="/wiki/Circulatory_system" title="Circulatory system">circulation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Perfusion" title="Perfusion">perfusion</a>, impaired respiratory effort, or inadequate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ventilation_(physiology)" title="Ventilation (physiology)">ventilation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davis_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davis-14">[14]</a></sup> There has long been a scientific debate over whether <a href="/wiki/Neonatal_resuscitation" title="Neonatal resuscitation">newborn infants with asphyxia should be resuscitated</a> with 100% oxygen or normal air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davis_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Davis-14">[14]</a></sup> It has been demonstrated that high concentrations of oxygen lead to generation of oxygen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_radicals" title="Free radicals">free radicals</a>, which have a role in <a href="/wiki/Reperfusion_injury" title="Reperfusion injury">reperfusion injury</a> after asphyxia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Research by <a href="/wiki/Ola_Didrik_Saugstad" title="Ola Didrik Saugstad">Ola Didrik Saugstad</a> and others led to new international guidelines on newborn resuscitation in 2010, recommending the use of normal air instead of 100% oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
