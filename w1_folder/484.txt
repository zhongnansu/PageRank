<p><b>JSTOR</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/dʒ/: 'j' in 'jam'">dʒ</span><span title="/eɪ/: 'a' in 'face'">eɪ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ɔːr/: 'ar' in 'war'">ɔːr</span></span>/</a></span></span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> short for <i>Journal Storage</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Digital_library" title="Digital library">digital library</a> founded in 1995. Originally containing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digitizing" title="Digitizing">digitized</a> back issues of <a href="/wiki/Academic_journal" title="Academic journal">academic journals</a>, it now also includes books and primary sources, and current issues of journals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSTOR_glance_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-JSTOR_glance-4">[4]</a></sup> It provides <a href="/wiki/Full-text_search" title="Full-text search">full-text searches</a> of almost 2,000 journals. As of 2013, more than 8,000 institutions in more than 160 countries had access to JSTOR;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Annual_Summary_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Annual_Summary-5">[5]</a></sup> most access is by subscription, but some of the site's <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">public domain</a> content is available at no cost to anyone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> JSTOR's revenue was $86 million in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NonProfitExplorer_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-NonProfitExplorer-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=JSTOR&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/William_G._Bowen" title="William G. Bowen">William G. Bowen</a>, president of <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a> from 1972 to 1988,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> founded JSTOR in 1995. JSTOR originally was conceived as a solution to one of the problems faced by libraries, especially <a href="/wiki/Research_library" title="Research library">research and university libraries</a>, due to the increasing number of academic journals in existence. Most libraries found it prohibitively expensive in terms of cost and space to maintain a comprehensive collection of journals. By digitizing many journal titles, JSTOR allowed libraries to outsource the storage of journals with the confidence that they would remain available long-term. Online access and full-text search ability improved access dramatically.
</p><p>Bowen initially considered using CD-ROMs for distribution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Ira_Fuchs" title="Ira Fuchs">Ira Fuchs</a>, Princeton University's vice-president for Computing and Information Technology, convinced Bowen that CD-ROM was becoming an increasingly outdated technology and that network distribution could eliminate redundancy and increase accessibility. (For example, all Princeton's administrative and academic buildings were networked by 1989; the student dormitory network was completed in 1994; and campus networks like the one at Princeton were, in turn, linked to larger networks such as <a href="/wiki/BITNET" title="BITNET">BITNET</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>.) JSTOR was initiated in 1995 at seven different library sites, and originally encompassed ten economics and history journals. JSTOR access improved based on feedback from its initial sites, and it became a fully searchable index accessible from any ordinary <a href="/wiki/Web_browser" title="Web browser">web browser</a>. Special software was put in place to make pictures and graphs clear and readable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>With the success of this limited project, Bowen and Kevin Guthrie, then-president of JSTOR, wanted to expand the number of participating journals. They met with representatives of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society of London</a> and an agreement was made to digitize the <i><a href="/wiki/Philosophical_Transactions_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society">Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society</a></i> dating from its beginning in 1665. The work of adding these volumes to JSTOR was completed by December 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Andrew_W._Mellon_Foundation" title="Andrew W. Mellon Foundation">Andrew W. Mellon Foundation</a> funded JSTOR initially. Until January 2009 JSTOR operated as an independent, self-sustaining <a href="/wiki/Nonprofit_organization" title="Nonprofit organization">nonprofit organization</a> with offices in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Ann_Arbor,_Michigan" title="Ann Arbor, Michigan">Ann Arbor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>. Then JSTOR merged with the nonprofit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ithaka_Harbors,_Inc." title="Ithaka Harbors, Inc.">Ithaka Harbors, Inc.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>—a nonprofit organization founded in 2003 and "dedicated to helping the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academic_community" title="Academic community">academic community</a> take full advantage of rapidly advancing information and networking technologies".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-About_Ithaka_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-About_Ithaka-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Content">Content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=JSTOR&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>JSTOR content is provided by more than 900 publishers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Annual_Summary_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Annual_Summary-5">[5]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Bibliographic_database" title="Bibliographic database">database</a> contains more than 1,900 journal titles,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Annual_Summary_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Annual_Summary-5">[5]</a></sup> in more than 50 disciplines. Each object is uniquely identified by an integer value, starting at <code>1</code>.
</p><p>In addition to the main site, the JSTOR labs group operates an open service that allows access to the contents of the archives for the purposes of corpus analysis at its <i>Data for Research</i> service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This site offers a search facility with graphical indication of the article coverage and loose integration into the main JSTOR site. Users may create focused sets of articles and then request a dataset containing word and <a href="/wiki/N-gram" title="N-gram">n-gram</a> frequencies and basic metadata. They are notified when the dataset is ready and may download it in either <a href="/wiki/XML" title="XML">XML</a> or <a href="/wiki/Comma-separated_values" title="Comma-separated values">CSV</a> formats. The service does not offer full-text, although academics may request that from JSTOR, subject to a non-disclosure agreement.
</p><p>JSTOR Plant Science<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> is available in addition to the main site. JSTOR Plant Science provides access to content such as plant type specimens, taxonomic structures, scientific literature, and related materials and aimed at those researching, teaching, or studying botany, biology, ecology, environmental, and conservation studies. The materials on JSTOR Plant Science are contributed through the Global Plants Initiative (GPI)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and are accessible only to JSTOR and GPI members. Two partner networks are contributing to this: the African Plants Initiative, which focuses on plants from Africa, and the Latin American Plants Initiative, which contributes plants from Latin America.
</p><p>JSTOR launched its Books at JSTOR program in November 2012, adding 15,000 current and backlist books to its site. The books are linked with reviews and from citations in journal articles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>In September 2014, JSTOR launched <i>JSTOR Daily</i>, an online magazine meant to bring academic research to a broader audience. Posted articles are generally based on JSTOR entries, and some entries provide the backstory to current events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Access">Access</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=JSTOR&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Access">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>JSTOR is licensed mainly to academic institutions, public libraries, research institutions, museums, and schools. More than 7,000 institutions in more than 150 countries have access.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSTOR_glance_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-JSTOR_glance-4">[4]</a></sup> JSTOR has been running a pilot program of allowing subscribing institutions to provide access to their alumni, in addition to current students and staff. The Alumni Access Program officially launched in January 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Individual subscriptions also are available to certain journal titles through the journal publisher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Every year, JSTOR blocks 150 million attempts by non-subscribers to read articles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Inquiries have been made about the possibility of making JSTOR <a href="/wiki/Open_access" title="Open access">open access</a>. According to Harvard Law professor <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Lessig" title="Lawrence Lessig">Lawrence Lessig</a>, JSTOR had been asked "how much would it cost to make this available to the whole world, how much would we need to pay you? The answer was $250 million".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aaron_Swartz_incident">Aaron Swartz incident</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=JSTOR&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Aaron Swartz incident">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_v._Aaron_Swartz" title="United States v. Aaron Swartz">United States v. Aaron Swartz</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Swartz#JSTOR" title="Aaron Swartz">Aaron Swartz § JSTOR</a></div>
<p>In late 2010 and early 2011, Internet activist <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Swartz" title="Aaron Swartz">Aaron Swartz</a> used <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">MIT</a>'s data network to bulk-download a substantial portion of JSTOR's collection of academic journal articles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSTORstatement_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-JSTORstatement-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HuffPost_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-HuffPost-22">[22]</a></sup> When the bulk-download was discovered, a video camera was placed in the room to film the mysterious visitor and the relevant computer was left untouched. Once video was captured of the visitor, the download was stopped and Swartz was identified. Rather than pursue a civil lawsuit against him, in June 2011 they reached a settlement wherein he surrendered the downloaded data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSTORstatement_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-JSTORstatement-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HuffPost_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-HuffPost-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>The following month, federal authorities charged Swartz with several "<a href="/wiki/Data_theft" title="Data theft">data theft</a>"–related crimes, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wire_fraud" title="Wire fraud">wire fraud</a>, computer fraud, unlawfully obtaining information from a <a href="/wiki/Computer_security" title="Computer security">protected computer</a>, and recklessly damaging a protected computer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bilton_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bilton-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-open-access-adv_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-open-access-adv-24">[24]</a></sup> Prosecutors in the case claimed that Swartz acted with the intention of making the papers available on <a href="/wiki/Peer-to-peer_file_sharing" title="Peer-to-peer file sharing">P2P file-sharing sites</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HuffPost_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-HuffPost-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feds:_Harvard_fellow_hacked_millions_of_papers_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Feds:_Harvard_fellow_hacked_millions_of_papers-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Swartz surrendered to authorities, pleaded not guilty to all counts, and was released on $100,000 bail. In September 2012, U.S. attorneys increased the number of charges against Swartz from four to thirteen, with a possible penalty of 35 years in prison and $1 million in fines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JusticeGov_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-JusticeGov-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wired_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wired-27">[27]</a></sup> The case still was pending when Swartz committed <a href="/wiki/Suicide" title="Suicide">suicide</a> in January 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Prosecutors dropped the charges after his suicide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations">Limitations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=JSTOR&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Limitations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The availability of most journals on JSTOR is controlled by a "<a href="/wiki/Embargo_(academic_publishing)" title="Embargo (academic publishing)">moving wall</a>", which is an agreed-upon delay between the current volume of the journal and the latest volume available on JSTOR. This time period is specified by agreement between JSTOR and the publisher of the journal, which usually is three to five years. Publishers may request that the period of a "moving wall" be changed or request discontinuation of coverage. Formerly, publishers also could request that the "moving wall" be changed to a "fixed wall"—a specified date after which JSTOR would not add new volumes to its database. As of  November 2010<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=JSTOR&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, "fixed wall" agreements were still in effect with three publishers of 29 journals made available online through sites controlled by the publishers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wall_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wall-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2010, JSTOR started adding current issues of certain journals through its Current Scholarship Program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Increasing_public_access">Increasing public access</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=JSTOR&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Increasing public access">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Beginning September 6, 2011, JSTOR made public domain content available at no charge to the public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSTOR_EJC_announcement_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-JSTOR_EJC_announcement-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LibraryJournal_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-LibraryJournal-33">[33]</a></sup> This "Early Journal Content" program constitutes about 6% of JSTOR's total content, and includes over 500,000 documents from more than 200 journals that were published before 1923 in the United States, and before 1870 in other countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSTOR_EJC_announcement_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-JSTOR_EJC_announcement-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LibraryJournal_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-LibraryJournal-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSTOR_EJC_about_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-JSTOR_EJC_about-34">[34]</a></sup> JSTOR stated that it had been working on making this material free for some time. The Swartz controversy and Greg Maxwell's protest <a href="/wiki/Torrent_file" title="Torrent file">torrent</a> of the same content led JSTOR to "press ahead" with the initiative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSTOR_EJC_announcement_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-JSTOR_EJC_announcement-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LibraryJournal_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-LibraryJournal-33">[33]</a></sup> As of 2017, JSTOR does not have plans to extend it to other public domain content, stating that "We do not believe that just because something is in the public domain, it can always be provided for free".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>In January 2012, JSTOR started a pilot program, "Register &amp; Read", offering limited no-cost access (not <a href="/wiki/Open_access" title="Open access">open access</a>) to archived articles for individuals who register for the service. At the conclusion of the pilot, in January 2013, JSTOR expanded Register &amp; Read from an initial 76 publishers to include about 1,200 journals from over 700 publishers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Registered readers may read up to six articles online every calendar month, but may not print or download PDFs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>JSTOR is conducting a <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:JSTOR" title="Wikipedia:JSTOR">pilot program with Wikipedia</a>, whereby established editors are given reading privileges through the Wikipedia Library, as with a university library.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use">Use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=JSTOR&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2012, JSTOR users performed nearly 152 million searches, with more than 113 million article views and 73.5 million article downloads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Annual_Summary_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Annual_Summary-5">[5]</a></sup> JSTOR has been used as a resource for linguistics research to investigate trends in language use over time and also to analyze gender differences in scholarly publishing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lexicology_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lexicology-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
