<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Methane monooxygenase</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Methane monooxygenase</b>, or MMO, is an enzyme capable of oxidizing the C-H bond in <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> as well as other <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">alkanes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Methane monooxygenase belongs to the class of <a href="/wiki/Oxidoreductase" title="Oxidoreductase">oxidoreductase</a> enzymes (<a href="/wiki/Enzyme_Commission_number" title="Enzyme Commission number">EC</a> <a class="external text" href="https://enzyme.expasy.org/EC/1.14.13.25" rel="nofollow">1.14.13.25</a>).
</p><p>There are two well-studied forms of MMO: the soluble form (sMMO) and the particulate form (pMMO). The active site in sMMO contains a di-iron center bridged by an oxygen atom (Fe-O-Fe), whereas the active site in pMMO utilizes copper, although some propose that pMMO also uses iron. Structures of both proteins have been determined by X-ray crystallography; however, the location and mechanism of the active site in pMMO is still poorly understood and is an area of active research.
</p><p>The particulate methane monooxygenase and related ammonia monooxygenase are integral membrane proteins, occurring in methanotrophs and ammonia oxidisers,respectively, which are thought to be related.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PUB00016963_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-PUB00016963-2">[2]</a></sup> These enzymes have a relatively wide substrate specificity and can catalyse the oxidation of a range of substrates including ammonia, methane, halogenated hydrocarbons, and aromatic molecules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PUB00016964_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-PUB00016964-3">[3]</a></sup> These enzymes are composed of 3 subunits - A (<a href="/wiki/InterPro" title="InterPro">InterPro</a>:<i> <a class="external text" href="https://www.ebi.ac.uk/interpro/IEntry?ac=IPR003393" rel="nofollow">IPR003393</a></i>), B (<a href="/wiki/InterPro" title="InterPro">InterPro</a>:<i> <a class="external text" href="https://www.ebi.ac.uk/interpro/IEntry?ac=IPR006833" rel="nofollow">IPR006833</a></i>) and C (<a href="/wiki/InterPro" title="InterPro">InterPro</a>:<i> <a class="external text" href="https://www.ebi.ac.uk/interpro/IEntry?ac=IPR006980" rel="nofollow">IPR006980</a></i>) - and contain various metal centers, including copper. Particulate methane monooxygenase from <i><a href="/wiki/Methylococcus_capsulatus" title="Methylococcus capsulatus">Methylococcus capsulatus</a></i> is an ABC homotrimer, which contains mononuclear and dinuclear copper metal centers, and a third metal center containing a metal ion whose identity in vivo is not certain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PUB00016965_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-PUB00016965-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The A subunit from <i>Methylococcus capsulatus</i> (Bath) resides primarily within the membrane and consists of 7 transmembrane helices and a beta-hairpin, which interacts with the soluble region of the B subunit. A conserved glutamate residue is thought to contribute to a metal center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PUB00016965_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-PUB00016965-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Methane monooxygenases are found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methanotrophic" title="Methanotrophic">methanotrophic</a> <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, a class of bacteria that exist at the interface of aerobic (oxygen-containing) and anaerobic (oxygen-devoid) environments. One of the more widely studied bacteria of this type is <i>Methylococcus capsulatus</i> (Bath).  This bacterium was discovered in the <a href="/wiki/Hot_spring" title="Hot spring">hot springs</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bath,_England" title="Bath, England">Bath, England</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BathSpringDiscovery_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-BathSpringDiscovery-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span id="Soluble_methane_monooxygenase_.28MMO.29_systems"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Soluble_methane_monooxygenase_(MMO)_systems">Soluble methane monooxygenase (MMO) systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methane_monooxygenase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Soluble methane monooxygenase (MMO) systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Methanotrophic bacteria play an essential role of cycling carbon through anaerobic sediments. The chemistry behind the cycling takes a chemically inert hydrocarbon, methane, and converts it to a more active species, <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>. Other hydrocarbons are oxidized by MMOs, so a new hydroxylation catalyst based on the understanding of MMO systems could possibly make a more efficient use of the world supply of natural gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NatureMMO_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-NatureMMO-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>This is a classic monooxygenase reaction in which two reducing equivalents from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NAD(P)H" title="NAD(P)H">NAD(P)H</a> are utilized to split the O-O bond of O2. One atom is reduced to water by a 2 e- reduction and the second is incorporated into the substrate to yield methanol:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMOcat_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-MMOcat-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>CH<sub>4</sub> + O<sub>2</sub> + NAD(P)H + H<sup>+</sup> -&gt; CH<sub>3</sub>OH + NAD(P)<sup>+</sup> + H<sub>2</sub>O
</p><p>Two forms of MMO have been found: soluble and particulate. The best characterized forms of soluble MMO contains three protein components: hydroxylase, the β unit, and the reductase. Each of which is necessary for effective substrate hydroxylation and NADH oxidation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMOcat_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-MMOcat-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methane_monooxygenase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MMOstates.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="525" data-file-width="1369" height="84" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/MMOstates.JPG/220px-MMOstates.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/MMOstates.JPG/330px-MMOstates.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/MMOstates.JPG/440px-MMOstates.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MMOstates.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The resting, oxidized, and reduced state of the diiron core.</div></div></div>
<p>X-ray crystallography of the MMO shows that it is a dimer formed of three subunits, α2β2γ2. With 2.2 A resolution, the crystallography shows that MMO is a relatively flat molecule with the dimensions of 60 x 100 x 120 A. In addition, there is a wide canyon running along the dimer interface with an opening in the center of the molecule. Most of the protomers involves helices from the α and β subunits with no participation from the γ subunit. Also, the interactions with the protomers resembles ribonucleotide reductase R2 protein dimer interaction, resembling a heart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMOheart1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-MMOheart1-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMOheart2_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-MMOheart2-9">[9]</a></sup> Each iron has a six coordinate octahedral environment. The dinuclear iron centers are positioned in the α subunit. Each iron atoms are also coordinated to a histidine δN atom, Fe 1 to a His 147 and Fe 2 to His 246, Fe 1 is a ligated to a monodentate carboxylate, Glu 114, a semi bridging caboxylate, Glu 144, and a water molecule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NatureMMO_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-NatureMMO-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The substrate must bind near the active site in order for the reaction to take place. Near to the iron centers, there are hydrophobic pockets. It is thought that here the methane binds and is held until needed. From the X-ray crystallography, there is no direct path to these packets. However, a slight conformation change in the Phe 188 or The 213 side-chains could allow access.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NatureMMO_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-NatureMMO-6">[6]</a></sup> This conformational change could be triggered by the binding of a coupling protein and the activase.
</p><p>Upon reduction, one of the carboxylate ligands undergoes a “1,2 carboxylate” shift from behind a terminal monodentate ligand to a bridging ligand for the two irons, with the second oxygen coordinated to Fe 2. In the reduced form of MMOH<sub>red</sub>, the ligand environment for the Fe effectively becomes five coordinated, a form that permits the cluster to activate dioxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMOcat_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-MMOcat-7">[7]</a></sup> The two irons are at this point oxidized to FeIV and have changed from low-spin <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferromagnetic" title="Ferromagnetic">ferromagnetic</a> to high-spin antiferromagnetic.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_catalytic_cycle_and_mechanism">Proposed catalytic cycle and mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methane_monooxygenase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Proposed catalytic cycle and mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MMOcycle.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="525" data-file-width="500" height="525" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/06/MMOcycle.JPG" width="500"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption">The proposed catalytic Cycle for MMO.</div></div></div>
<p>From the MMOH<sub>red</sub>, the diiron centers react with the O<sub>2</sub> to form intermediate P. This intermediate is a peroxide species where the oxygens are bound symmetrically, suggested by spectroscopic studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMOcat13_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-MMOcat13-10">[10]</a></sup> However, the structure is not known. Intermediate P then converts to intermediate Q, which was proposed to contain two antiferromagnetically coupled high-spin FeIV centers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMOcat_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-MMOcat-7">[7]</a></sup> This compound Q with its diamond core is critical to the oxidizing species for MMO.
</p><p>There are two mechanisms suggested for the reaction between compound Q and the alkane: radical and nonradical. The radical mechanism starts with abstraction of the hydrogen atom from the substrate to form QH (the rate determining step), hydroxyl bridged compound Q and the free alkyl radical. The nonradical mechanism implies a concerted pathway, occurring via a four-center transition state and leading to a “hydrido-alkyl-Q” compound. As of 1999, the research suggests that the methane oxidation proceeds via a bound-radical mechanism.
</p><p>It was suggested that the transition state for the radical mechanism involves a torsion motion of the hydroxyl OH ligand before the methyl radical can add to the bridging hydroxyl ligand to form the alcohol. As the radical approaches, the H atom of the alkane leave the coplanar tricoordinate O environment and bends upward to create a tetrahedral tetracoordinate O environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMOcat_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-MMOcat-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The final step for this reaction is the elimination of the alcohol and the regeneration of the catalysts. There are a few ways in which this can occur. It could be a stepwise mechanism that starts with the elimination of the alcohol and an intermediate Fe-O-Fe core, and the latter can eliminate the water and regenerate the enzyme through a 2e- reduction. On the other hand, it can start with a 2e- reduction process of bridging the O1 atom to give a water molecule, followed by elimination of the alcohol and regeneration of the enzyme. In addition, it is possible that there is a concerted mechanism whereby the elimination of the methanol occurs spontaneously with 2e- reduction of the bridging O1 center and regeneration of the catalyst.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MMOcat_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-MMOcat-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
