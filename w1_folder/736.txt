<p><b>Torness nuclear power station</b> was the last of the United Kingdom's <a href="/wiki/Generation_II_reactor" title="Generation II reactor">second generation nuclear power plants</a> to be commissioned. Construction of this facility began in 1980 for the then <a href="/wiki/South_of_Scotland_Electricity_Board" title="South of Scotland Electricity Board">South of Scotland Electricity Board</a> (SSEB) and it was commissioned in 1988. Torness nuclear power station is located approximately 30 miles (50 km) east of <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a> at Torness Point near <a href="/wiki/Dunbar" title="Dunbar">Dunbar</a> in East Lothian, Scotland. It is a local landmark, highly visible from the main A1 road and <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_Main_Line" title="East Coast Main Line">East Coast Main Line</a> railway.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Torness_Nuclear_Power_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After extensive discussions with the <a href="/wiki/Local_planning_authority" title="Local planning authority">local planning authority</a> and more than twenty other interested organisations, the <a href="/wiki/South_of_Scotland_Electricity_Board" title="South of Scotland Electricity Board">SSEB</a> sought approval of the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Scotland" title="Secretary of State for Scotland">Secretary of State for Scotland</a> in 1973 for Torness as a site for a nuclear power station. A public exhibition was held at Dunbar in February 1974 to explain the Board's proposals, and in June 1974, a public inquiry was held.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>There was widespread public opposition to the building of a nuclear plant at Torness. Diverse campaigning groups came together to highlight the environmental and human cost of nuclear power stations. In May 1978 4000 people marched from Dunbar to occupy the Torness site. Many of them signed a declaration to “take all nonviolent steps necessary to prevent the construction of a nuclear power station at Torness”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The SSEB submitted designs for four types of reactor then being considered by HM Government for the next stage of the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Nuclear power in the United Kingdom">UK civil nuclear programme</a>: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advanced_gas-cooled_reactor" title="Advanced gas-cooled reactor">advanced gas-cooled reactor</a> (AGR), the Steam Generating Heavy Water Reactor (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SGHWR" title="SGHWR">SGHWR</a>), the Light Water Reactor (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LWR" title="LWR">LWR</a>) and the High Temperature Reactor (HTR). In February 1975, the Secretary of State for Scotland granted the SSEB statutory consent for the location of future nuclear power stations and, after review of the four alternative reactor types, consent was given on 24 May 1978 for construction of the AGR station.
</p><p>The construction, which was undertaken by a consortium known as National Nuclear Corporation ('NNC'),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> began in 1980. The reactors were supplied by NNC and the turbines by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Electric_Company_plc" title="General Electric Company plc">GEC</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plants_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-plants-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Torness was the last of the United Kingdom's second generation nuclear power plants to be commissioned (25 May 1988).
The station consists of two advanced gas-cooled reactors (AGR) capable of producing a peak rating of 1364 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MWe" title="MWe">MWe</a>. Upon deregulation of the United Kingdom's electricity generation market it passed to the state-owned <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Nuclear" title="Scottish Nuclear">Scottish Nuclear</a>, privatised as part of <a href="/wiki/British_Energy" title="British Energy">British Energy</a> which was sold to the French company <a href="/wiki/%C3%89lectricit%C3%A9_de_France" title="Électricité de France">EDF</a> in January 2009, and incorporated in the latter's UK subsidiary <a href="/wiki/EDF_Energy" title="EDF Energy">EDF Energy</a>. It is expected to operate until 2030.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plant_design">Plant design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Torness_Nuclear_Power_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Plant design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Torness_Power_Station_from_the_east.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2664" data-file-width="4831" height="121" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/11/Torness_Power_Station_from_the_east.jpg/220px-Torness_Power_Station_from_the_east.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/11/Torness_Power_Station_from_the_east.jpg/330px-Torness_Power_Station_from_the_east.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/11/Torness_Power_Station_from_the_east.jpg/440px-Torness_Power_Station_from_the_east.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Torness_Power_Station_from_the_east.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The station viewed from the east</div></div></div>
<p>Torness shares its design with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heysham_Power_Station#Heysham_2" title="Heysham Power Station">Heysham 2</a> nuclear power station. The station was designed by NNC, a company created from the gradual amalgamation of five consortia that were formed in the 1950s and 1960s to build the UK's commercial nuclear power stations. NNC is now <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AMEC" title="AMEC">AMEC Nuclear UK Ltd</a>.
</p><p>The graphite-moderated, <a href="/wiki/Gas-cooled_reactor" title="Gas-cooled reactor">gas-cooled</a> design was proven at the WAGR – the Windscale experimental <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advanced_gas-cooled_reactor" title="Advanced gas-cooled reactor">AGR</a> facility and is a significant evolution of the <a href="/wiki/Magnox" title="Magnox">Magnox</a> reactor designs.
The entire UK commercial reactor programme shares a common heritage with, and was built on operating experience gained from the early PIPPA reactors at <a href="/wiki/Sellafield#Calder_Hall_nuclear_power_station" title="Sellafield">Calder Hall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chapelcross_nuclear_power_station" title="Chapelcross nuclear power station">Chapelcross</a> and prior to that, the Windscale piles.
</p><p>When first operated Torness probably had the most sophisticated and complex computerised control system for a nuclear power station worldwide, and a far more sophisticated than earlier members of the advanced gas-cooled reactor fleet. Over 70 <a href="/wiki/Ferranti_Argus" title="Ferranti Argus">Ferranti Argus 700</a> computers are used in the control and instrumentation systems, which included Digital Direct Control (DDC) of the reactors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iaea-199507_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-iaea-199507-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wjhill-19900508_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-wjhill-19900508-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crash_of_an_RAF_Tornado_near_the_site_-_November_1999">Crash of an RAF Tornado near the site - November 1999</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Torness_Nuclear_Power_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Crash of an RAF Tornado near the site - November 1999">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In November 1999 an <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">RAF</a> <a href="/wiki/Panavia_Tornado" title="Panavia Tornado">Panavia Tornado</a> crashed into the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> less than 1 km from the power station following an engine failure. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK_Ministry_of_Defence" title="UK Ministry of Defence">UK Ministry of Defence</a> commended the two crew members for demonstrating "exceptional levels of airmanship and awareness in the most adverse of conditions", because they ensured that the Tornado was clear of the power station before abandoning the aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Failure_of_a_gas_circulator_.E2.80.93_May_2002"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Failure_of_a_gas_circulator_–_May_2002">Failure of a gas circulator – May 2002</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Torness_Nuclear_Power_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Failure of a gas circulator – May 2002">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This was thought, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forensic_evidence" title="Forensic evidence">forensic evidence</a>, to be linked to the development of an unexpected <a href="/wiki/Fatigue_(material)" title="Fatigue (material)">fatigue</a>-related crack in part of the impeller. In August, another gas circulator on the other Torness reactor showed signs of increasing vibration and was promptly shut down by the operators. Its subsequent disassembly revealed a fully developed fatigue related crack in a similar position to the first failure, but the prompt shutdown had prevented consequential damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Unplanned_power_excursion_.E2.80.93_December_2005"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Unplanned_power_excursion_–_December_2005">Unplanned power excursion – December 2005</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Torness_Nuclear_Power_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Unplanned power excursion – December 2005">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An unplanned power increase on Reactor 2 at Torness during the night shift of 30 December 2005. Operators responded to the event by taking corrective action to restore normal core reactivity levels. Station and Company investigations identified that improvement to the training of operators covering reactivity fundamentals is appropriate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Shutdown_after_drumscreen_blockage_by_seaweed_.E2.80.93_August_2006"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Shutdown_after_drumscreen_blockage_by_seaweed_–_August_2006">Shutdown after drumscreen blockage by seaweed – August 2006</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Torness_Nuclear_Power_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Shutdown after drumscreen blockage by seaweed – August 2006">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Complete blockage by seaweed of the main cooling water intake drum-screens is an initiating event considered in the Station Safety Report (SSR). The event resulted in supplies of main cooling water being lost for a period. As a consequence, water supplies to the reactor seawater (RSW) system, which provides a safety role, were lost for a time on one reactor and restricted on the other. The station responded to the event by shutting down both reactors within 70 minutes of receiving the first indication of impaired main cooling water flow and provided adequate post trip cooling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Shutdown_after_sea-water_intake_blockage_by_jellyfish_.E2.80.93_June_2011"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Shutdown_after_sea-water_intake_blockage_by_jellyfish_–_June_2011">Shutdown after sea-water intake blockage by jellyfish – June 2011</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Torness_Nuclear_Power_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Shutdown after sea-water intake blockage by jellyfish – June 2011">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 28 June 2011<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> both reactors were manually shut down due to reduced flow of sea water at intakes by a large mass of <a href="/wiki/Jellyfish" title="Jellyfish">jellyfish</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
