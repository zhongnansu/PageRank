<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Overshoot (population)</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Population_dynamics" title="Population dynamics">population dynamics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Population_ecology" title="Population ecology">population ecology</a>, <b>overshoot</b> occurs when a population temporarily exceeds the long term <a href="/wiki/Carrying_capacity" title="Carrying capacity">carrying capacity</a> of its environment. The environment usually has mechanisms in place to prevent overshoot. For example, plants are only able to regenerate and regrow a few times after being consumed before completely dying off. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The consequence of overshoot is called a collapse, a crash or a die-off in which there is a decline in <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a>. The entire sequence or trajectory undergone by the population and its environment together is often termed 'overshoot-and-collapse'.
</p><p>Overshoot can occur due to lag effects.  <a href="/wiki/Reproduction" title="Reproduction">Reproduction</a> rates may remain high relative to the <a href="/wiki/Mortality_rate" title="Mortality rate">death rate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eaec_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-eaec-2">[2]</a></sup> Entire <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a> may be severely affected and sometimes reduced to less-<a href="/wiki/Complexity" title="Complexity">complex</a> states due to prolonged overshoot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geap_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-geap-3">[3]</a></sup>  The eradication of disease can trigger overshoot when a population suddenly exceeds the land's carrying capacity. An example of this occurred on the <a href="/wiki/Horn_of_Africa" title="Horn of Africa">Horn of Africa</a> when <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">smallpox</a> was eliminated. A region that had supported around 1 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pastoralists" title="Pastoralists">pastoralists</a> for centuries was suddenly expected to support 14 million people. The result was <a href="/wiki/Overgrazing" title="Overgrazing">overgrazing</a>, which led to <a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion" title="Soil erosion">soil erosion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lkp_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-lkp-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Humans">Humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overshoot_(population)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Human_overpopulation" title="Human overpopulation">Human overpopulation</a></div>
<p>An attempt to apply this concept to human experience is <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_R._Catton,_Jr.#Overshoot:_The_Ecological_Basis_of_Revolutionary_Change" title="William R. Catton, Jr.">Overshoot: The Ecological Basis of Revolutionary Change</a>,</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_R._Catton,_Jr." title="William R. Catton, Jr.">William R. Catton, Jr.</a>, which updates the 1974 book,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Limits_to_Growth" title="The Limits to Growth">The Limits to Growth</a></i>. The <a href="/wiki/Club_of_Rome" title="Club of Rome">Club of Rome</a> study <i><a href="/wiki/The_Limits_to_Growth" title="The Limits to Growth">The Limits to Growth</a></i> used a computer model which was based on overshoot and collapse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acab_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-acab-7">[7]</a></sup> The Limits to Growth simulation showed in several scenarios how overshoot could happen based on an assumption of hard limits to resources, but did not deal with questions of substitution of limited resources with <a href="/wiki/Renewable_resource" title="Renewable resource">renewable resources</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Depending on your <a href="/wiki/World_view" title="World view">world view</a>, some believe, including Catton, that the Earth's population has already overshot its carrying capacity, by both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Overpopulation_(biology)" title="Overpopulation (biology)">overpopulation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Overconsumption" title="Overconsumption">overconsumption</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> This may include <a href="/wiki/Doomer" title="Doomer">doomers</a> and those within various <a href="/wiki/Degrowth" title="Degrowth">degrowth</a> movements.  An alternative view is <a href="/wiki/Cornucopian" title="Cornucopian">cornucopian</a> including those who believe matter and energy will be obtained from beyond Earth.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measuring_human_overshoot">Measuring human overshoot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overshoot_(population)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Measuring human overshoot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Overshoot_(population)&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span> <small><i>(June 2013)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Globally, we can measure how much the human economy demands against what the biosphere can renew.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> One accounting approach is the <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a>. Footprint accounting measures the demand on <a href="/wiki/Biocapacity" title="Biocapacity">biocapacity</a>, i.e., area of biologically productive land and water required to produce the ecological services (including resource regeneration and waste absorption) for a given population. Biocapacity encompasses the biologically productive capacity of all of the planet's surface areas – cropland, grazing land, forest, fresh water etc. Footprint accounts include non-renewable resources like fossil fuels and other minerals in as far as their use puts a demand on biocapacity. Some demands are difficult to measure and are not included in the accounts – hence the results tend to underestimate dependency. Ecological footprint and biocapacity are measured in global hectares (hectares with world average biological productivity). Thus increased productivity would reduce dependency; while increased population or consumption per head would increase it.
</p><p>The percentage of an area's footprint supported from its renewable resources shows its overall resource dependence within or above its areas' regeneration rate. A percentage over 100 percent indicates local overuse or net dependence on ecosystems elsewhere.  Similarly, the percentage of an area’s population supported from its renewable resources is its sustainable population at current consumption levels with current technology; the remaining percentage is its overshoot population. A country's biocapacity deficit increases as either their population or their per capita consumption grows; and faster if both grow.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overshoot_Index_of_various_countries">Overshoot Index of various countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overshoot_(population)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Overshoot Index of various countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A detailed list of countries and their main figures to compute the Overshoot (overpopulation) index has been computed by The Optimum Population Trust. All source data is from <a class="external text" href="http://www.footprintnetwork.org/images/uploads/Ecological_Footprint_Atlas_2010.pdf" rel="nofollow">The Ecological Footprint Atlas 2010</a>, based on 2007 figures, produced by the <a href="/wiki/Global_Footprint_Network" title="Global Footprint Network">Global Footprint Network</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Countries with populations of under one million have been omitted. Global Footprint Network’s data are largely from UN sources; their methodology<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> is still admittedly imprecise, but is subject to continuous refinement.
