<p><b>Monosaccharides</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/aɪ/: 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span></span>, <wbr/><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>-/</a></span></span>, from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BC%CF%8C%CE%BD%CE%BF%CF%82" title="wikt:μόνος">monos</a></i>: single, <i>sacchar</i>: sugar), also called <b>simple sugars</b>, are the simplest form of <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a> and the most basic units of <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> They cannot be further <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolyzed</a> to simpler <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compounds</a>. The general formula is <span class="chemf nowrap">C<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"><i>n</i></sub></span>H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2<i>n</i></sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"><i>n</i></sub></span></span>. They are  usually <a href="/wiki/Transparency_and_translucency" title="Transparency and translucency">colorless</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soluble" title="Soluble">soluble</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystalline</a> solids. Some monosaccharides have a <a href="/wiki/Sweetness" title="Sweetness">sweet taste</a>. 
</p><p>Examples of monosaccharides include <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> (dextrose), <a href="/wiki/Fructose" title="Fructose">fructose</a> (levulose), and <a href="/wiki/Galactose" title="Galactose">galactose</a>. Monosaccharides are the building blocks of <a href="/wiki/Disaccharide" title="Disaccharide">disaccharides</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lactose" title="Lactose">lactose</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Polysaccharide" title="Polysaccharide">polysaccharides</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a>). Each <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> atom that supports a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl" title="Hydroxyl">hydroxyl</a> group (so, all of the carbons except for the primary and terminal carbon) is <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)" title="Chirality (chemistry)">chiral</a>, giving rise to a number of <a href="/wiki/Isomer" title="Isomer">isomeric</a> forms, all with the same chemical formula. For instance, galactose and glucose are both <a href="/wiki/Aldohexose" title="Aldohexose">aldohexoses</a>, but have different physical structures and chemical properties.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_nomenclature">Structure and nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monosaccharide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure and nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With few exceptions (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Deoxyribose" title="Deoxyribose">deoxyribose</a>), monosaccharides have this <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formula</a>: (CH<sub>2</sub>O)<sub><i>x</i></sub>, where conventionally <i>x</i> ≥ 3.   Monosaccharides can be classified by the number <i>x</i> of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> atoms they contain:  <a href="/wiki/Triose" title="Triose">triose</a> (3), <a href="/wiki/Tetrose" title="Tetrose">tetrose</a> (4), <a href="/wiki/Pentose" title="Pentose">pentose</a> (5), <a href="/wiki/Hexose" title="Hexose">hexose</a> (6), <a href="/wiki/Heptose" title="Heptose">heptose</a> (7), and so on.
</p><p>The most important monosaccharide, glucose, is a <a href="/wiki/Hexose" title="Hexose">hexose</a>. Examples of heptoses include the <a href="/wiki/Ketose" title="Ketose">ketoses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mannoheptulose" title="Mannoheptulose">mannoheptulose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sedoheptulose" title="Sedoheptulose">sedoheptulose</a>. Monosaccharides with eight or more carbons are rarely observed as they are quite unstable. In <a href="/wiki/Aqueous_solution" title="Aqueous solution">aqueous solutions</a> monosaccharides exist as rings if they have more than four carbons.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Linear-chain_monosaccharides">Linear-chain monosaccharides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monosaccharide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Linear-chain monosaccharides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Simple monosaccharides have a linear and unbranched carbon skeleton with one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonyl" title="Carbonyl">carbonyl</a> (C=O) <a href="/wiki/Functional_group" title="Functional group">functional group</a>, and one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl" title="Hydroxyl">hydroxyl</a> (OH) group on each of the remaining carbon <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a>. Therefore, the molecular structure of a simple monosaccharide can be written as H(CHOH)<sub><i>n</i></sub>(C=O)(CHOH)<sub><i>m</i></sub>H, where <i>n</i> + 1 + <i>m</i> = <i>x</i>; so that its elemental formula is C<sub><i>x</i></sub>H<sub>2<i>x</i></sub>O<sub><i>x</i></sub>.
</p><p>By convention, the carbon atoms are numbered from 1 to <i>x</i> along the backbone, starting from the end that is closest to the C=O group. Monosaccharides are the simplest units of carbohydrates and the simplest form of sugar.
</p><p>If the carbonyl is at position 1 (that is, <i>n</i> or <i>m</i> is zero), the molecule begins with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formyl" title="Formyl">formyl</a> group H(C=O)− and is technically an <a href="/wiki/Aldehyde" title="Aldehyde">aldehyde</a>.  In that case, the compound is termed an <a href="/wiki/Aldose" title="Aldose">aldose</a>. Otherwise, the molecule has a <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">keto</a> group, a carbonyl −(C=O)− between two carbons; then it is formally a <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">ketone</a>, and is termed a <a href="/wiki/Ketose" title="Ketose">ketose</a>.  Ketoses of biological interest usually have the carbonyl at position 2.
</p><p>The various classifications above can be combined, resulting in names such as "aldohexose" and "ketotriose".
</p><p>A more general nomenclature for open-chain monosaccharides combines a Greek prefix to indicate the number of carbons (tri-, tetr-, pent-, hex-, etc.) with the suffixes "-ose" for aldoses and "-ulose" for ketoses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In the latter case, if the carbonyl is not at position 2, its position is then indicated by a numeric infix.  So, for example, H(C=O)(CHOH)<sub>4</sub>H is pentose, H(CHOH)(C=O)(CHOH)<sub>3</sub>H is pentulose, and H(CHOH)<sub>2</sub>(C=O)(CHOH)<sub>2</sub>H is pent-3-ulose.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Open-chain_stereoisomers">Open-chain stereoisomers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monosaccharide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Open-chain stereoisomers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Two monosaccharides with equivalent <a href="/wiki/Molecular_graph" title="Molecular graph">molecular graphs</a> (same chain length and same carbonyl position) may still be distinct <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stereoisomer" title="Stereoisomer">stereoisomers</a>, whose molecules differ in the three-dimensional arrangement of the <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">bonds</a> of certain atoms.  This happens only if the molecule contains a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stereogenic_center" title="Stereogenic center">stereogenic center</a>, specifically a carbon atom that is <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)" title="Chirality (chemistry)">chiral</a> (connected to four distinct molecular sub-structures).  Those four bonds can have any of two configurations in space distinguished by their <a href="/wiki/Handedness" title="Handedness">handedness</a>.  In a simple open-chain monosaccharide, every carbon is chiral except the first and the last atoms of the chain, and (in ketoses) the carbon with the keto group.
</p><p>For example, the triketose H(CHOH)(C=O)(CHOH)H (glycerone, <a href="/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone" title="Dihydroxyacetone">dihydroxyacetone</a>) has no stereogenic center, and therefore exists as a single stereoisomer.  The other triose, the aldose H(C=O)(CHOH)<sub>2</sub>H (<a href="/wiki/Glyceraldehyde" title="Glyceraldehyde">glyceraldehyde</a>), has one chiral carbon — the central one, number 2 — which is bonded to groups −H, −OH, −C(OH)H<sub>2</sub>, and −(C=O)H.  Therefore, it exists as two stereoisomers whose molecules are mirror images of each other (like a left and a right glove).  Monosaccharides with four or more carbons may contain multiple chiral carbons, so they typically have more than two stereoisomers.  The number of distinct stereoisomers with the same diagram is bounded by 2<sup><i>c</i></sup>, where <i>c</i> is the total number of chiral carbons.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Fischer_projection" title="Fischer projection">Fischer projection</a> is a systematic way of drawing the <a href="/wiki/Skeletal_formula" title="Skeletal formula">skeletal formula</a> of an acyclic monosaccharide so that the handedness of each chiral carbon is well specified.  Each stereoisomer of a simple open-chain monosaccharide can be identified by the positions (right or left) in the Fischer diagram of the chiral hydroxyls (the hydroxyls attached to the chiral carbons).
</p><p>Most stereoisomers are themselves chiral (distinct from their mirror images).  In the Fischer projection, two mirror-image isomers differ by having the positions of all chiral hydroxyls reversed right-to-left. Mirror-image isomers are chemically identical in non-chiral environments, but usually have very different biochemical properties and occurrences in nature.
</p><p>While most stereoisomers can be arranged in pairs of mirror-image forms, there are some non-chiral stereoisomers that are identical to their mirror images, in spite of having chiral centers.  This happens whenever the molecular graph is symmetrical, as in the 3-ketopentoses H(CHOH)<sub>2</sub>(CO)(CHOH)<sub>2</sub>H, and the two halves are mirror images of each other.  In that case, mirroring is equivalent to a half-turn rotation. For this reason, there are only three distinct 3-ketopentose stereoisomers, even though the molecule has two chiral carbons.
</p><p>Distinct stereoisomers that are not mirror-images of each other usually have different chemical properties, even in non-chiral environments.  Therefore, each mirror pair and each non-chiral stereoisomer may be given a specific <a href="/wiki/Monosaccharide_nomenclature" title="Monosaccharide nomenclature">monosaccharide name</a>.  For example, there are 16 distinct aldohexose stereoisomers, but the name "glucose" means a specific pair of mirror-image aldohexoses. In the Fischer projection, one of the two glucose isomers has the hydroxyl at left on C3, and at right on C4 and C5; while the other isomer has the reversed pattern.  These specific monosaccharide names have conventional three-letter abbreviations, like "Glu" for glucose and "Thr" for <a href="/wiki/Threose" title="Threose">threose</a>.
</p><p>Generally, a monosaccharide with <i>n</i> asymmetrical carbons has 2<sup><i>n</i></sup> stereoisomers. The number of open chain stereoisomers for an aldose monosaccharide is larger by one than that of a ketose monosaccharide of the same length.  Every ketose will have 2<sup>(<i>n</i>−3)</sup> stereoisomers where <i>n</i> &gt; 2 is the number of carbons.  Every aldose will have 2<sup>(<i>n</i>−2)</sup> stereoisomers where <i>n</i> &gt; 2 is the number of carbons.
These are also referred to as epimers which have the different arrangement of −OH and −H groups at the asymmetric or chiral carbon atoms (this does not apply to those carbons having the carbonyl functional group).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Configuration_of_monosaccharides">Configuration of monosaccharides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monosaccharide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Configuration of monosaccharides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like many chiral molecules, the two stereoisomers of glyceraldehyde will gradually rotate the <a href="/wiki/Optical_rotation" title="Optical rotation">polarization direction</a> of linearly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polarized_light" title="Polarized light">polarized light</a> as it passes through it, even in solution.  The two stereoisomers are identified with the prefixes <small>D</small>- and <small>L</small>-, according to the sense of rotation: <small>D</small>-glyceraldehyde is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dextrorotatory" title="Dextrorotatory">dextrorotatory</a> (rotates the polarization axis clockwise), while <small>L</small>-glyceraldehyde is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Levorotatory" title="Levorotatory">levorotatory</a> (rotates it counterclockwise).
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DL-Glucose.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="293" data-file-width="361" height="243" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/DL-Glucose.svg/300px-DL-Glucose.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/DL-Glucose.svg/450px-DL-Glucose.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/DL-Glucose.svg/600px-DL-Glucose.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DL-Glucose.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>D- and L-glucose</div></div></div>
<p>The <small>D</small>- and <small>L</small>- prefixes are also used with other monosaccharides, to distinguish two particular stereoisomers that are mirror-images of each other. For this purpose, one considers the chiral carbon that is furthest removed from the C=O group. Its four bonds must connect to −H, −OH, −C(OH)H, and the rest of the molecule.  If the molecule can be rotated in space so that the directions of those four groups match those of the analog groups in <small>D</small>-glyceraldehyde's C2, then the isomer receives the <small>D</small>- prefix. Otherwise, it receives the <small>L</small>- prefix.
</p><p>In the Fischer projection, the <small>D</small>- and <small>L</small>- prefixes specifies the configuration at the carbon atom that is second from bottom: <small>D</small>- if the hydroxyl is on the right side, and <small>L</small>- if it is on the left side.
</p><p>Note that the <small>D</small>- and <small>L</small>- prefixes do not indicate the direction of rotation of polarized light, which is a combined effect of the arrangement at all chiral centers.  However, the two enantiomers will always rotate the light in opposite directions, by the same amount. See also <a href="/wiki/Absolute_configuration#By_configuration:_D-_and_L-" title="Absolute configuration"><span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">d/l</span> system</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cyclisation_of_Monosaccharides">Cyclisation of Monosaccharides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monosaccharide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cyclisation of Monosaccharides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A monosaccharide often switches from the acyclic (open-chain) form to a <a href="/wiki/Cyclic_compound" title="Cyclic compound">cyclic</a> form, through a <a href="/wiki/Nucleophilic_addition" title="Nucleophilic addition">nucleophilic addition</a> reaction between the carbonyl group and one of the hydroxyls of the same molecule.  The reaction creates a ring of carbon atoms closed by one bridging <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> atom.  The resulting molecule has an <a href="/wiki/Hemiacetal" title="Hemiacetal">hemiacetal</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hemiketal" title="Hemiketal">hemiketal</a> group, depending on whether the linear form was an aldose or a ketose.  The reaction is easily reversed, yielding the original open-chain form.
</p><p>In these cyclic forms, the ring usually has <b>5</b> or <b>6</b> atoms.  These forms are called <a href="/wiki/Furanose" title="Furanose">furanoses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pyranose" title="Pyranose">pyranoses</a>, respectively — by analogy with <a href="/wiki/Furan" title="Furan">furan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pyran" title="Pyran">pyran</a>, the simplest compounds with the same carbon-oxygen ring (although they lack the double bonds of these two molecules).  For example, the aldohexose <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> may form a hemiacetal linkage between the hydroxyl on carbon 1 and the oxygen on carbon 4, yielding a molecule with a 5-membered ring, called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glucofuranose" title="Glucofuranose">glucofuranose</a>.  The same reaction can take place between carbons 1 and 5 to form a molecule with a 6-membered ring, called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glucopyranose" title="Glucopyranose">glucopyranose</a>.  Cyclic forms with a 7-atom ring (the same of <a href="/wiki/Oxepane" title="Oxepane">oxepane</a>), rarely encountered, are called <a href="/wiki/Heptose" title="Heptose">heptoses</a>.
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Existence_in_solution.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="240" data-file-width="774" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Existence_in_solution.png/500px-Existence_in_solution.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Existence_in_solution.png/750px-Existence_in_solution.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/09/Existence_in_solution.png 2x" width="500"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Existence_in_solution.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Conversion between the furanose, acyclic, and pyranose forms of D-glucose.</div></div></div></div>

<p>For many monosaccharides (including glucose), the cyclic forms predominate, in the solid state and in solutions, and therefore the same name commonly is used for the open- and closed-chain isomers.  Thus, for example, the term "glucose" may signify glucofuranose, glucopyranose, the open-chain form, or a mixture of the three.
</p><p>Cyclization creates a new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stereogenic" title="Stereogenic">stereogenic</a> center at the carbonyl-bearing carbon.  The −OH group that replaces the carbonyl's oxygen may end up in two distinct positions relative to the ring's midplane.  Thus each open-chain monosaccharide yields two cyclic isomers (<a href="/wiki/Anomer" title="Anomer">anomers</a>), denoted by the prefixes α- and β-.  The molecule can change between these two forms by a process called <a href="/wiki/Mutarotation" title="Mutarotation">mutarotation</a>, that consists in a reversal of the ring-forming reaction followed by another ring formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Haworth_projection">Haworth projection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monosaccharide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Haworth projection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The stereochemical structure of a cyclic monosaccharide can be represented in a <a href="/wiki/Haworth_projection" title="Haworth projection">Haworth projection</a>. In this diagram, the α-isomer for the <a href="/wiki/Pyranose" title="Pyranose">pyranose</a> form of a D-aldohexose has the -OH of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anomeric_carbon" title="Anomeric carbon">anomeric carbon</a> below the plane of the carbon atoms, while the β-isomer has the -OH of the anomeric carbon above the plane. Pyranoses typically adopt a chair conformation, similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Cyclohexane" title="Cyclohexane">cyclohexane</a>. In this conformation, the α-isomer has the -OH of the anomeric carbon in an axial position, whereas the β-isomer has the OH- of the anomeric carbon in equatorial position (considering D-aldohexose sugars).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
