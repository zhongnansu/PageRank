<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hestem%C3%B8j.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="650" data-file-width="802" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Hestem%C3%B8j.jpg/220px-Hestem%C3%B8j.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Hestem%C3%B8j.jpg/330px-Hestem%C3%B8j.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Hestem%C3%B8j.jpg/440px-Hestem%C3%B8j.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hestem%C3%B8j.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Animal manure is often a mixture of animal <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">feces</a> and bedding straw, as in this example from a <a href="/wiki/Stable" title="Stable">stable</a></div></div></div>
<p><b>Manure</b> is <a href="/wiki/Organic_matter" title="Organic matter">organic matter</a>, mostly derived from animal <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">feces</a> except in the case of <a href="/wiki/Green_manure" title="Green manure">green manure</a>, which can be used as <a href="/wiki/Organic_fertilizer" title="Organic fertilizer">organic fertilizer</a> in <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>. Manures contribute to the fertility of the <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> by adding <a href="/wiki/Organic_matter" title="Organic matter">organic matter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nutrient#Nutrients_and_the_environment" title="Nutrient">nutrients</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, that are utilised by <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungi</a> and other organisms in the soil. Higher organisms then feed on the  fungi  and bacteria in a chain of life that comprises the <a href="/wiki/Soil_food_web" title="Soil food web">soil food web</a>.  
</p><p>In the past, the term "manure" included inorganic <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a>, but this usage is now very rare.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span></h2>
<p>There are three main classes of manures used in <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> management:
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Animal_manure">Animal manure</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Manure_pools_in_Haikou_01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2212" data-file-width="4000" height="122" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Manure_pools_in_Haikou_01.jpg/220px-Manure_pools_in_Haikou_01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Manure_pools_in_Haikou_01.jpg/330px-Manure_pools_in_Haikou_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Manure_pools_in_Haikou_01.jpg/440px-Manure_pools_in_Haikou_01.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Manure_pools_in_Haikou_01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cement reservoirs, one new, and one containing cow manure mixed with water. This is common in rural <a href="/wiki/Hainan" title="Hainan">Hainan</a> Province, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Most animal manure consists of <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">feces</a>. Common forms of animal manure include farmyard manure (FYM) or farm slurry (liquid manure). FYM also contains plant material (often straw), which has been used as bedding for animals and has absorbed the feces and <a href="/wiki/Urine" title="Urine">urine</a>. Agricultural manure in liquid form, known as <a href="/wiki/Slurry" title="Slurry">slurry</a>, is produced by more intensive livestock rearing systems where concrete or slats are used, instead of straw bedding. Manure from different animals has different qualities and requires different application rates when used as fertilizer. For example <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pig" title="Pig">pigs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sheep" title="Sheep">sheep</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chicken" title="Chicken">chickens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey_(bird)" title="Turkey (bird)">turkeys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbits</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Guano" title="Guano">guano</a> from <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabirds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">bats</a> all have different properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> For instance, sheep manure is high in nitrogen and potash, while pig manure is relatively low in both. Horses mainly eat grass and a few weeds so horse manure can contain grass and weed seeds, as horses do not digest seeds the way that cattle do. Cattle manure is a good source of nitrogen as well as organic carbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bernal_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bernal-2">[2]</a></sup>Chicken litter, coming from a bird, is very concentrated in nitrogen and phosphate and is prized for both properties."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bernal_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bernal-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lustosa_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lustosa-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Animal manures may be adulterated or contaminated with other animal products, such as <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shoddy" title="Shoddy">shoddy</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Hair" title="Hair">hair</a>), <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">feathers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bone</a>. Livestock feed can be mixed with the manure due to spillage. For example, chickens are often fed <a href="/wiki/Meat_and_bone_meal" title="Meat and bone meal">meat and bone meal</a>, an animal product, which can end up becoming mixed with chicken litter.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_manure">Human manure</span></h4>
<p>Some people refer to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_excreta" title="Human excreta">human excreta</a> as human manure, and the word "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humanure" title="Humanure">humanure</a>" has also been used. Just like animal manure, it can be applied as a soil conditioner (<a href="/wiki/Reuse_of_excreta" title="Reuse of excreta">reuse of excreta</a> in agriculture). <a href="/wiki/Sewage_sludge" title="Sewage sludge">Sewage sludge</a> is a material that contains human excreta, as it is generated after mixing excreta with water and treatment of the <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a> in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment_plant" title="Sewage treatment plant">sewage treatment plant</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Compost">Compost</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Compost" title="Compost">Compost</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NRCSAR02028_-_Arkansas_(239)(NRCS_Photo_Gallery).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1500" data-file-width="2100" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/NRCSAR02028_-_Arkansas_%28239%29%28NRCS_Photo_Gallery%29.jpg/220px-NRCSAR02028_-_Arkansas_%28239%29%28NRCS_Photo_Gallery%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/NRCSAR02028_-_Arkansas_%28239%29%28NRCS_Photo_Gallery%29.jpg/330px-NRCSAR02028_-_Arkansas_%28239%29%28NRCS_Photo_Gallery%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/NRCSAR02028_-_Arkansas_%28239%29%28NRCS_Photo_Gallery%29.jpg/440px-NRCSAR02028_-_Arkansas_%28239%29%28NRCS_Photo_Gallery%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NRCSAR02028_-_Arkansas_(239)(NRCS_Photo_Gallery).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Compost containing turkey manure and wood chips from bedding material is dried and then applied to pastures for fertilizer.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Compost" title="Compost">Compost</a> is the decomposed remnants of organic materials.  It is usually of plant origin, but often includes some animal dung or bedding.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_manure">Green manure</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Green_manure" title="Green manure">Green manures</a> are crops grown for the express purpose of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plow" title="Plow">plowing</a> them in, thus increasing fertility through the incorporation of nutrients and organic matter into the soil.  <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">Leguminous plants</a> such as clover are often used for this, as they <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixing" title="Nitrogen fixing">fix nitrogen</a> using <i><a href="/wiki/Rhizobia" title="Rhizobia">Rhizobia</a></i> bacteria in specialized <a href="/wiki/Root_nodule" title="Root nodule">nodes</a> in the root structure.
</p><p>Other types of plant matter used as manure include the contents of the <a href="/wiki/Rumen" title="Rumen">rumens</a> of slaughtered <a href="/wiki/Ruminant" title="Ruminant">ruminants</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spent_grain" title="Spent grain">spent grain</a> (left over from <a href="/wiki/Brewing" title="Brewing">brewing</a> <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Seaweed" title="Seaweed">seaweed</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses_of_manure">Uses of manure</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Reuse_of_excreta" title="Reuse of excreta">Reuse of excreta</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Animal_manure_2">Animal manure</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Misthaufen16.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="608" data-file-width="430" height="240" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Misthaufen16.JPG/170px-Misthaufen16.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Misthaufen16.JPG/255px-Misthaufen16.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Misthaufen16.JPG/340px-Misthaufen16.JPG 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Misthaufen16.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Manure on a wall.</div></div></div>
<p>Animal manure, such as <a href="/wiki/Chicken_manure" title="Chicken manure">chicken manure</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cow_dung" title="Cow dung">cow dung</a>, has been used for centuries as a fertilizer for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Farming" title="Farming">farming</a>. It can improve the soil structure (aggregation) so that the soil holds more nutrients and water, and therefore becomes more fertile.  Animal manure also encourages soil microbial activity which promotes the soil's trace mineral supply, improving plant nutrition.  It also contains some nitrogen and other nutrients that assist the growth of plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Manures with a particularly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unpleasant_odor" title="Unpleasant odor">unpleasant odor</a> (such as slurries from <a href="/wiki/Intensive_farming" title="Intensive farming">intensive pig farming</a>) are usually knifed (injected) directly into the soil to reduce release of the odor. Manure from pigs and cattle is usually spread on fields using a <a href="/wiki/Manure_spreader" title="Manure spreader">manure spreader</a>. Due to the relatively lower level of proteins in vegetable matter, <a href="/wiki/Herbivore" title="Herbivore">herbivore</a> manure has a milder smell than the dung of <a href="/wiki/Carnivore" title="Carnivore">carnivores</a> or <a href="/wiki/Omnivore" title="Omnivore">omnivores</a>. However, herbivore slurry that has undergone anaerobic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">fermentation</a> may develop more unpleasant odors, and this can be a problem in some agricultural regions.  Poultry droppings are harmful to plants when fresh, but after a period of <a href="/wiki/Compost" title="Compost">composting</a> are valuable fertilizers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Manure is also commercially composted and bagged and sold as a soil amendment.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2018, Austrian scientists offered a method of <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a> production from <a href="/wiki/Elephant" title="Elephant">elephant</a> and cow manure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Issues">Issues</span></h2>
<p>Any quantity of manure may be a source of <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a> or food spoilage organisms which may be carried by <a href="/wiki/Fly" title="Fly">flies</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rodents" title="Rodents">rodents</a> or a range of other vector organisms and cause disease or put food safety at risk.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Livestock_antibiotics">Livestock antibiotics</span></h3>
<p>In 2007, a <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota" title="University of Minnesota">University of Minnesota</a> study<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UMinn_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-UMinn-8">[8]</a></sup> indicated that foods such as corn, lettuce, and potatoes have been found to accumulate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibacterial" title="Antibacterial">antibiotics</a> from soils spread with animal manure that contains these drugs.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Organic_food" title="Organic food">Organic foods</a> may be much more or much less likely to contain antibiotics, depending on their sources and treatment of manure. For instance, by Soil Association Standard 4.7.38, most organic arable farmers either have their own supply of manure (which would, therefore, not normally contain drug residues) or else rely on green manure crops for the extra fertility (if any nonorganic manure is used by organic farmers, then it usually has to be rotted or composted to degrade any residues of drugs and eliminate any pathogenic bacteria—Standard 4.7.38, Soil Association organic farming standards). On the other hand, as found in the University of Minnesota study, the non-usage of artificial fertilizers, and resulting exclusive use of manure as fertilizer, by organic farmers can result in significantly greater accumulations of antibiotics in organic foods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UMinn_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-UMinn-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
