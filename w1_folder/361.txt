<p>The <b>American Veterinary Medical Association</b> (<b>AVMA</b>), founded in 1863, is a not-for-profit association representing more than 91,000<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veterinarian" title="Veterinarian">veterinarians</a> working in private and corporate practice, government, industry, academia, and uniformed services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-History-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The AVMA provides information resources, continuing education opportunities, publications, and discounts on personal and professional products, programs, and services. The AVMA indicates that it <a href="/wiki/Lobbying" title="Lobbying">lobbies</a> for animal friendly legislation within a framework that supports the use of animals for human purposes (e.g., food, fiber, research, companionship).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Positions_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Positions-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Education" title="United States Department of Education">United States Department of Education</a> has designated the AVMA Council on Education as the accrediting body for the 30<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AVMA_Accredited_Veterinary_Colleges_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-AVMA_Accredited_Veterinary_Colleges-4">[4]</a></sup> schools of veterinary medicine in the United States. In this capacity, the AVMA develops and maintains educational standards for these institutions to ensure the qualifications and competency of graduates of veterinary schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Accreditation_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Accreditation-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The AVMA publishes the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Journal_of_the_American_Veterinary_Medical_Association" title="Journal of the American Veterinary Medical Association">Journal of the American Veterinary Medical Association</a></i> and the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=American_Journal_of_Veterinary_Research&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="American Journal of Veterinary Research (page does not exist)">American Journal of Veterinary Research</a></i>.
</p><p>The AVMA's veterinary student organization is the <a href="/wiki/Student_American_Veterinary_Medical_Association" title="Student American Veterinary Medical Association">Student American Veterinary Medical Association</a> (SAVMA).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Veterinary_Medical_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The American Veterinary Medical Association (AVMA) was founded in 1863, when 40 delegates representing seven states met for a convention in New York. Originally named the United States Veterinary Medical Association, the USVMA was renamed the AVMA in 1889.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-About_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-About-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>By 1913, the AVMA consisted of 1,650 members, with membership open only to graduates of accredited veterinary schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-About_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-About-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Today, the AVMA has more than 91,000 members engaged in a wide variety of work. In addition to treating pets, veterinarians work in a number of fields, such as public health, agriculture, food safety, academics, and the military.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-History-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="AVMA_policy">AVMA policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Veterinary_Medical_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: AVMA policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The AVMA produces policies in response to member requests and stakeholder interest. These statements are general and aim to encourage improvement based on the best available scientific evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Policies_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Policies-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2005, the AVMA changed its policy on pregnant sow housing, stating that "given the number of variables and large variation in performance within both group and stall systems for pregnant sows, no one system is clearly better than others under all conditions and according to all criteria of animal welfare".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Housing_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Housing-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The AVMA's policy was adopted after a comprehensive review by a multi-disciplinary, multi-perspective task force of experts that produced an accompanying review of housing for pregnant sows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Review_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Review-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The AVMA has voted on several proposals to take a formal stand against the forced feeding of birds to make <a href="/wiki/Foie_gras" title="Foie gras">foie gras</a>. Although foie gras has been banned in many countries in Europe, as well as in the U.S. state of California, because of an absence of science specifically addressing the welfare aspects of foie gras production, as well as conflicting opinions among its membership, the AVMA opted not to take a stand either for or against foie gras. The AVMA has published a welfare implications of foie gras production backgrounder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Backgrounder_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Backgrounder-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legislation">Legislation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Veterinary_Medical_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Legislation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>AVMA supported the <a href="/wiki/Veterinary_Medicine_Mobility_Act_of_2014" title="Veterinary Medicine Mobility Act of 2014">Veterinary Medicine Mobility Act of 2014</a>, a law that amended the <a href="/wiki/Controlled_Substances_Act" title="Controlled Substances Act">Controlled Substances Act</a> (CSA) to clarify that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veterinarian" title="Veterinarian">veterinarians</a> are not required to have separate registrations to dispense <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Controlled_substances" title="Controlled substances">controlled substances</a> outside of their principal place of business, such as when treating animals on a <a href="/wiki/Farm" title="Farm">farm</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1528sum_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-1528sum-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbo1528_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-cbo1528-12">[12]</a></sup> AVMA argued that "the CSA must be amended so that our nation's animals do not suffer unnecessarily."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AVMASupport_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-AVMASupport-13">[13]</a></sup> Due to an interpretation of the law by the <a href="/wiki/Drug_Enforcement_Administration" title="Drug Enforcement Administration">Drug Enforcement Administration</a>, veterinarians were not allowed to travel to their off-site animal patients with controlled substances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAVMCsupport_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-AAVMCsupport-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_Accreditation">Academic Accreditation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Veterinary_Medical_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Academic Accreditation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Two bodies within AVMA are responsible for veterinary education accreditation: the AVMA Council on Education (COE) and the Committee on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paraveterinary_workers#Veterinary_nurse_and_technician" title="Paraveterinary workers">Veterinary Technician</a> Education and Activities (CVTEA). The former is responsible for accreditation of veterinary colleges and the latter veterinary technology programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AVMA_Center_for_Veterinary_Education_Accreditation_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-AVMA_Center_for_Veterinary_Education_Accreditation-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p><br/>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="AVMA_Accredited_Veterinary_Technology_Programs">AVMA Accredited Veterinary Technology Programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Veterinary_Medical_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: AVMA Accredited Veterinary Technology Programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The AVMA accredits <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veterinary_technician" title="Veterinary technician">veterinary technician</a> programs in all U.S. states except for Arkansas, Montana, and Washington D.C. It also accredits one program in Canada and a number of distance learning programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Programs_Accredited_by_the_AVMA_CVTEA_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Programs_Accredited_by_the_AVMA_CVTEA-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Specialists_in_veterinary_medicine">Specialists in veterinary medicine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_Veterinary_Medical_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Specialists in veterinary medicine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>A veterinary specialist, as recognized by the AVMA, is a graduate veterinarian who has successfully completed the process of board certification in an AVMA-recognized veterinary specialty organization (ie, board or college). To become board certified, a veterinarian must have extensive post-graduate training and experience and pass a credential review and examinations set by the given specialty organization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Specialists_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Specialists-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
