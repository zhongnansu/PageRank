<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Acid gas</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Acid gas</b> is a particular typology of <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> or any other <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> <a href="/wiki/Mixture" title="Mixture">mixture</a> containing significant quantities of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a> (H<sub>2</sub>S), <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>), or similar <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acidic</a> gases.
</p><p>The terms <i>acid gas</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Sour_gas" title="Sour gas">sour gas</a></i> are often incorrectly treated as synonyms. Strictly speaking, a sour gas is any gas that specifically contains hydrogen sulfide in significant amounts; an acid gas is any gas that contains significant amounts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acidic" title="Acidic">acidic</a> gases such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) or hydrogen sulfide. Thus, carbon dioxide by itself is an acid gas but not a sour gas.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Processing_and_safety">Processing and safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acid_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Processing and safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before a raw natural gas containing hydrogen sulfide and/or carbon dioxide can be used, the raw gas must be treated to reduce impurities to acceptable levels and this is commonly done with an <a href="/wiki/Amine_gas_treating" title="Amine gas treating">amine gas treating</a> process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The removed H<sub>2</sub>S is most often subsequently converted to by-product elemental <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Claus_process" title="Claus process">Claus process</a> or alternatively converted to valuable <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WSA_Process" title="WSA Process">WSA Process</a> unit.
</p><p>Processes within oil refineries or <a href="/wiki/Natural-gas_processing" title="Natural-gas processing">natural-gas processing</a> plants that remove <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercaptan" title="Mercaptan">mercaptans</a> and/or hydrogen sulfide are commonly referred to as 'sweetening' processes because they result in products which no longer have the sour, foul odors of mercaptans and hydrogen sulfide.
</p><p>Hydrogen sulfide is a <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#Toxicity" title="Hydrogen sulfide">toxic</a> gas. It also restricts the materials that can be used for <a href="/wiki/Piping" title="Piping">piping</a> and other equipment for handling sour gas, as many metals are sensitive to <a href="/wiki/Sulfide_stress_cracking" title="Sulfide stress cracking">sulfide stress cracking</a>.
</p><p>Carbon dioxide at concentrations of 7% to 10.1% cause dizziness, headache, visual and hearing dysfunction, and unconsciousness within a few minutes to an hour. Concentrations above 17% are lethal when exposed for more than one minute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
