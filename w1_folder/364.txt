<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Keeling Curve</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Keeling" redirects here. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Keeling_(disambiguation)" title="Keeling (disambiguation)">Keeling (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:KeelingCurve-5-26-18.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="500" height="132" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/KeelingCurve-5-26-18.jpg/220px-KeelingCurve-5-26-18.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/KeelingCurve-5-26-18.jpg/330px-KeelingCurve-5-26-18.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/KeelingCurve-5-26-18.jpg/440px-KeelingCurve-5-26-18.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:KeelingCurve-5-26-18.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Keeling Curve</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Keeling Curve</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chart" title="Chart">graph</a> that plots the ongoing change in concentration of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Carbon dioxide in Earth's atmosphere">carbon dioxide in Earth's atmosphere</a> since the 1950s. It is based on continuous measurements taken at the <a href="/wiki/Mauna_Loa_Observatory" title="Mauna Loa Observatory">Mauna Loa Observatory</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> that began under the supervision of <a href="/wiki/Charles_David_Keeling" title="Charles David Keeling">Charles David Keeling</a>. Keeling's measurements showed the first significant evidence of rapidly increasing carbon dioxide levels in the atmosphere. Many scientists credit Keeling's graph with first bringing the world's attention to the current increase of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Charles David Keeling, of <a href="/wiki/Scripps_Institution_of_Oceanography" title="Scripps Institution of Oceanography">Scripps Institution of Oceanography</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UC_San_Diego" title="UC San Diego">UC San Diego</a>, was the first person to make frequent regular measurements of the atmospheric carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>) concentration, taking readings at the <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">South Pole</a> and in Hawaii from 1958 onwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kahele_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kahele-2">[2]</a></sup>  According to Dr <a href="/wiki/Naomi_Oreskes" title="Naomi Oreskes">Naomi Oreskes</a>, Professor, History of Science at Harvard University, it is one of the most important scientific works of the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clidis_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-clidis-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Measurements of carbon dioxide concentrations in the atmosphere had been taken prior to the Mauna Loa measurements, but on an ad-hoc basis across a variety of locations. <a href="/wiki/Guy_Stewart_Callendar" title="Guy Stewart Callendar">Guy Stewart Callendar</a> had shown a steady increase in concentrations since the 19th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fleming_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fleming-4">[4]</a></sup> Keeling had perfected the measurement techniques and observed "strong <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_cycle" title="Diurnal cycle">diurnal</a> behavior with steady values of about 310 ppm in the afternoon" at three locations: <a href="/wiki/Big_Sur" title="Big Sur">Big Sur</a> near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monterey" title="Monterey">Monterey</a>, rain forests of the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Peninsula" title="Olympic Peninsula">Olympic Peninsula</a>, and high mountain forests in <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> By measuring the ratio of two <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_carbon" title="Isotopes of carbon">isotopes of carbon</a>, Keeling attributed the diurnal change to respiration from local plants and soils, with afternoon values representative of the "free atmosphere". By 1960, Keeling and his group had determined that the measurement records from California, Antarctica, and Hawaii were long enough to see not just the diurnal and seasonal variations, but also a year-on-year increase that roughly matched the amount of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a> burned per year. In the article that made him famous, Keeling observed: "at the South Pole the observed rate of increase is nearly that to be expected from the combustion of fossil fuel".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mauna_Loa_measurements">Mauna Loa measurements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keeling_Curve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mauna Loa measurements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Due to funding cuts in the mid-1960s, Keeling was forced to abandon continuous monitoring efforts at the South Pole, but he scraped together enough money to maintain operations at Mauna Loa, which have continued to the present day <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rewards_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-rewards-7">[7]</a></sup> alongside the monitoring program by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">NOAA</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esrl.noaa.gov_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-esrl.noaa.gov-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The measurements collected at Mauna Loa show a steady increase in mean atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentration from about 315 parts per million by volume (<a href="/wiki/Parts-per_notation" title="Parts-per notation">ppmv</a>) in 1958 to 401 ppmv as of April 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MaunaMonthly_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-MaunaMonthly-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> This increase in atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> is  due to the <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>, and has been accelerating in recent years. Since carbon dioxide is a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a>, this has significant implications for <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. Measurements of carbon dioxide concentration in ancient <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_bubble" title="Air bubble">air bubbles</a> trapped in polar <a href="/wiki/Ice_core" title="Ice core">ice cores</a> show that mean atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentration has historically been between 275 and 285 ppmv during the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> epoch (9,000 BCE onwards), but started rising sharply at the beginning of the nineteenth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neftel_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Neftel-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Keeling and collaborators made measurements on the incoming ocean breeze and above the <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology)" title="Inversion (meteorology)">thermal inversion layer</a> to minimize local contamination from volcanic vents. In addition, the data are <a href="/wiki/Normalization_(statistics)" title="Normalization (statistics)">normalized</a> to negate any influence from local contamination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Measurements at many other isolated sites have confirmed the long-term trend shown by the Keeling Curve,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> though no sites have a record as long as Mauna Loa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KeelingWhorfContinuous_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-KeelingWhorfContinuous-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Keeling Curve also shows a cyclic variation of about 5 ppmv in each year corresponding to the seasonal change in uptake of CO<sub>2</sub> by the world's land vegetation. Most of this vegetation is in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" title="Northern hemisphere">Northern hemisphere</a>, since this is where most of the land is located. From a maximum in May, the level decreases during the northern spring and summer as new <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plant</a> growth takes carbon dioxide out of the atmosphere through <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>. After reaching a minimum in September, the level rises again in the northern fall and winter as plants and leaves die off and decay, releasing the gas back into the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The impact of green  plankton material in the worlds oceans, which may actually be responsible for taking up to 60% of the carbon dioxide out of the atmosphere through photosynthesis is yet to be fathomed though.
</p><p>Due in part to the significance of Keeling's findings,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rewards_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-rewards-7">[7]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">NOAA</a> began monitoring CO<sub>2</sub> levels worldwide in the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esrl.noaa.gov_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-esrl.noaa.gov-8">[8]</a></sup> Today, CO<sub>2</sub> levels are monitored at about 100 sites around the globe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Carbon dioxide measurements at the Mauna Loa observatory in Hawaii are made with a type of infrared spectrophotometer first called a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capnograph" title="Capnograph">capnograph</a> by its inventor, <a href="/wiki/John_Tyndall" title="John Tyndall">John Tyndall</a>, in 1864 but now known as a <a href="/wiki/Nondispersive_infrared_sensor" title="Nondispersive infrared sensor">nondispersive infrared sensor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The way in which the infrared analyzer data was obtained was through pen traces on a strip chart recorder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Currently (May, 2013) several laser-based sensors are being added to run concurrently with the IR spectrophotometer at Scripps, while <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">NOAA</a> measurements at Mauna Loa use <a href="/wiki/Nondispersive_infrared_sensor" title="Nondispersive infrared sensor">nondispersive infrared sensor</a> tightly calibrated using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WMO" title="WMO">WMO</a> standards as the main reference for the curve.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Multiple other sensors and technologies are also used at Mauna Loa to augment these measurements.
</p><p>Charles David Keeling died in 2005.  Supervision of the measuring project was taken over by his son, <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Keeling" title="Ralph Keeling">Ralph Keeling</a>, a professor of geochemistry at Scripps Oceanography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> On the 50th anniversary of the beginning of the Mauna Loa CO<sub>2</sub>  record, Ralph Keeling published an article detailing the history of his father's project and the many updates that have occurred in its half-century of existence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>When C.D. Keeling published his 1960 paper, there did not appear to be any evidence of oceanic absorption. Keeling conjectured that this might be attributable to fluctuations in the causation of the seasonal variation, within the small number of years since his measurements had begun. However it is now understood that allowance should be made for about 50% absorption by the oceanic sinks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Keeling father and son have contributed to the work which has since yielded a much fuller understanding of the relation between atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> and the entirety of the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a>, including the effects of land and ocean sinks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>On May 9, 2013, the daily mean concentration of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere measured at Mauna Loa surpassed 400 parts per million (ppm) for the first time since measurements began in 1958.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Estimates of carbon dioxide during previous geologic eras suggest that CO<sub>2</sub> has not been around this level for 2 to 4 million years, since the <a href="/wiki/Pliocene_climate" title="Pliocene climate">mid-Pliocene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montaigne_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Montaigne-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2015 the Keeling Curve was designated a <a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Chemical_Landmarks" title="National Historic Chemical Landmarks">National Historic Chemical Landmark</a> by the American Chemical Society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>  Commemorative plaques are installed at the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration's Mauna Loa Observatory and the Scripps Institution of Oceanography at the University of California, San Diego.
</p>
