<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">In situ resource utilization</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:In-Situ_Resource_Utilization_Testbed.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="387" data-file-width="324" height="299" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/In-Situ_Resource_Utilization_Testbed.gif/250px-In-Situ_Resource_Utilization_Testbed.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d1/In-Situ_Resource_Utilization_Testbed.gif 1.5x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:In-Situ_Resource_Utilization_Testbed.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>ISRU <a href="/wiki/Water-gas_shift_reaction" title="Water-gas shift reaction">reverse water gas shift</a> testbed (NASA KSC)</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">space exploration</a>, <b>in situ resource utilization</b> (<b>ISRU</b>) is defined as <i>"the collection, processing, storing and use of materials encountered in the course of human or robotic space exploration that replace materials that would otherwise be brought from Earth."</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
ISRU is the practice of leveraging resources found or manufactured on other <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">astronomical objects</a> (the Moon, Mars, asteroids, etc.) to fulfill or enhance the requirements and capabilities of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_mission" title="Space mission">space mission</a>.
</p><p>ISRU can provide materials for <a href="/wiki/Life_support_system" title="Life support system">life support</a>, <a href="/wiki/Propellant" title="Propellant">propellants</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Construction_material" title="Construction material">construction materials</a>, and energy to a spacecraft <a href="/wiki/Payload" title="Payload">payloads</a> or space exploration crews.
It is now very common for <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> and robotic planetary surface mission to harness the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a> found <i><a href="/wiki/In_situ" title="In situ">in situ</a></i> in the form of <a href="/wiki/Solar_panels_on_spacecraft" title="Solar panels on spacecraft">solar panels</a>. The use of ISRU for material production has not yet been implemented in a space mission, though several field tests in the late 2000s demonstrated various lunar ISRU techniques in a relevant environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>ISRU has long been considered as a possible avenue for reducing the mass and cost of space exploration architectures, in that it may be a way to drastically reduce the amount of payload that must be launched from Earth in order to explore a given <a href="/wiki/Planetary_body" title="Planetary body">planetary body</a>.
According to <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>, "in-situ resource utilisation will enable the affordable establishment of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_exploration" title="Extraterrestrial exploration">extraterrestrial exploration</a> and operations by minimizing the materials carried from Earth."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ_resource_utilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water">Water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ_resource_utilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the context of ISRU water is most often sought directly as fuel or as feedstock for fuel production. Applications include its use in life support either directly by drinking, for <a href="/wiki/Space_farming" title="Space farming">growing food</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water" title="Electrolysis of water">producing oxygen</a>, or numerous other industrial processes. All of which require a ready supply of water in the environment and the equipment to extract it. Such <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_water" title="Extraterrestrial water">extraterrestrial water</a> has been discovered in a variety of forms throughout the solar system, and a number of potential water extraction technologies have been investigated. For water that is chemically bound to <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">regolith</a>, solid ice, or some manner of permafrost, sufficient heating can recover the water. However this is not as easy as it appears because ice and permafrost can often be harder than plain rock, necessitating laborious mining operations. Where there is some level of atmosphere, such as on Mars, water can be extracted directly from the air using a simple process such as <a href="/wiki/WAVAR" title="WAVAR">WAVAR</a>. Another possible source of water is deep aquifers kept warm by Mars's latent geological heat, which can be tapped to provide both water and geothermal power.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rocket_propellant">Rocket propellant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ_resource_utilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rocket propellant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rocket propellant from <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a> has also been proposed for the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, mainly from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_ice" title="Lunar ice">ice that has been found at the poles</a>. The likely difficulties include working at extremely low temperatures and extraction from the <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">regolith</a>. Most schemes <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water" title="Electrolysis of water">electrolyse the water</a> and form <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> and liquify and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogenic" title="Cryogenic">cryogenically</a> store them. This requires large amounts of equipment and power to achieve. Alternatively it is possible to simply heat the water in a nuclear or solar <a href="/wiki/Thermal_rocket" title="Thermal rocket">thermal rocket</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> which seems to give very much more <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> delivered to <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a> (LEO) in spite of the much lower <a href="/wiki/Specific_impulse" title="Specific impulse">specific impulse</a>, for a given amount of equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Monopropellant" title="Monopropellant">monopropellant</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a> (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>) can be made from water on <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> and the Moon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum" title="Aluminum">Aluminum</a> as well as other metals have been proposed for use as rocket propellant made using lunar resources,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hepp_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hepp-7">[7]</a></sup> and proposals include reacting the aluminum with water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> For Mars, methane propellant can be manufactured via the <a href="/wiki/Sabatier_reaction" title="Sabatier reaction">Sabatier</a> process.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_cell_production">Solar cell production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ_resource_utilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Solar cell production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It has long been suggested that <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">solar cells</a> could be produced from the materials present in lunar soil. Silicon, aluminium, and glass, three of the primary materials required for solar cell production, are found in high concentrations in lunar soil and can be utilised to produce solar cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In fact, the native vacuum on the lunar surface provides an excellent environment for direct vacuum deposition of thin-film materials for solar cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Solar arrays produced on the lunar surface can be used to support lunar surface operations as well as satellites off the lunar surface. Solar arrays produced on the lunar surface may prove more cost effective than solar arrays produced and shipped from Earth, but this trade depends heavily on the location of the particular application in question.
</p><p>Another potential application of lunar-derived solar arrays is providing power to Earth. In its original form, known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_power_satellite" title="Solar power satellite">solar power satellite</a>, the proposal was intended as an alternate power source for <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. Solar cells would be shipped to Earth orbit and assembled, the power being transmitted to Earth via microwave beams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>  Despite much work on the cost of such a venture, the uncertainty lay in the cost and complexity of fabrication procedures on the lunar surface.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metals_for_construction_or_return_to_Earth">Metals for construction or return to Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ_resource_utilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Metals for construction or return to Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Asteroid_mining" title="Asteroid mining">Asteroid mining</a> could also involve extraction of metals for construction material in space, which may be more cost-effective than bringing such material up out of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s deep gravity well, or that of any other large body like the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>.  Metallic asteroids contain huge amounts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siderophile_element" title="Siderophile element">siderophilic metals</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Precious_metal" title="Precious metal">precious metals</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Building_materials">Building materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ_resource_utilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Building materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The colonisation of planets or moons will require obtaining local building materials, such as <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">regolith</a>. For example, studies employing artificial Mars soil mixed with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epoxy_resin" title="Epoxy resin">epoxy resin</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetraethoxysilane" title="Tetraethoxysilane">tetraethoxysilane</a>, produce high enough values of strength, resistance, and flexibility parameters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Locations">Locations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ_resource_utilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Locations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mars">Mars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ_resource_utilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Mars_Direct" title="Mars Direct">Mars Direct</a></div>
<p>ISRU research for Mars is focused primarily on providing <a href="/wiki/Rocket_propellant" title="Rocket propellant">rocket propellant</a> for a return trip to Earth — either for a manned or a sample return mission — or for use as fuel on Mars. Many of the proposed techniques utilise the well-characterised <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Mars" title="Atmosphere of Mars">atmosphere of Mars</a> as feedstock.  Since this can be easily simulated on Earth, these proposals are relatively simple to implement, though it is by no means certain that NASA or the ESA will favour this approach over a more conventional direct mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>A typical proposal for ISRU is the use of a <a href="/wiki/Sabatier_reaction" title="Sabatier reaction">Sabatier reaction</a>, <span class="nowrap">CO<sub>2</sub> + 4H<sub>2</sub> → CH<sub>4</sub> + 2H<sub>2</sub>O</span>, in order to produce methane on the Martian surface, to be used as a propellant.  Oxygen is liberated from the water by <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water" title="Electrolysis of water">electrolysis</a>, and the hydrogen recycled back into the Sabatier reaction.  The usefulness of this reaction is that—as of  2008<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=In_situ_resource_utilization&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, when the availability of water on Mars was less scientifically demonstrated—only the hydrogen (which is light) was thought to need to be brought from Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of  2016<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=In_situ_resource_utilization&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, <a href="/wiki/SpaceX" title="SpaceX">SpaceX</a> is currently <a href="/wiki/New_product_development" title="New product development">developing</a> the technology for a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SpaceX_Mars_propellant_plant" title="SpaceX Mars propellant plant">Mars propellant plant</a> that will use a variation on what is described in the previous paragraph.  Rather than transporting hydrogen from Earth to use in making the methane and oxygen, they intend to mine the requisite water from subsurface <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">water ice</a> that is now known to be abundant across much of the Martian surface, produce and then <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_natural_gas" title="Liquefied natural gas">store</a> the post-Sabatier reactants, and then use it as propellant for return flights of their <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interplanetary_Spaceship" title="Interplanetary Spaceship">Interplanetary Spaceship</a></i> no earlier than 2023.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spacex-itspresentation201609_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-spacex-itspresentation201609-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfi20160927_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-sfi20160927-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>A similar reaction proposed for Mars is the reverse <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_gas_shift_reaction" title="Water gas shift reaction">water gas shift reaction</a>,  <span class="nowrap">CO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub> → CO + H<sub>2</sub>O</span>. This reaction takes place rapidly in the presence of an iron-chrome <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a> at 400 Celsius,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and has been implemented in an Earth-based testbed by NASA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Again, oxygen is recycled from the water by <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water" title="Electrolysis of water">electrolysis</a>, and the reaction only needs a small amount of hydrogen from Earth.  The net result of this reaction is the production of oxygen, to be used as the oxidizer component of rocket fuel.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Moon">Moon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ_resource_utilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Moon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Footprint.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="639" data-file-width="650" height="246" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Footprint.gif/250px-Footprint.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Footprint.gif/375px-Footprint.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Footprint.gif/500px-Footprint.gif 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Footprint.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Footprint in lunar <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">regolith</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The Moon possesses abundant raw materials that are potentially relevant to a hierarchy of future applications, beginning with the use of lunar materials to facilitate human activities on the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> itself and progressing to the use of lunar resources to underpin a future industrial capability within the Earth-Moon system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>The lunar highland material <a href="/wiki/Anorthite" title="Anorthite">anorthite</a> can be used as <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ore</a>. Smelters can produce pure aluminium, calcium metal, oxygen and silica glass from anorthite. Raw anorthite is also good for making fiberglass and other glass and ceramic products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mining_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-mining-36">[36]</a></sup> One particular processing technique is to use <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a> brought from Earth as <a href="/wiki/Potassium_fluoride" title="Potassium fluoride">potassium fluoride</a> to separate the raw materials from the lunar rocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Over twenty different methods have been proposed for <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> extraction on the Moon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hepp_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hepp-7">[7]</a></sup>  Oxygen is often found in iron rich lunar minerals and glasses as <a href="/wiki/Iron_oxide" title="Iron oxide">iron oxide</a>. The oxygen can be extracted by heating the material to temperatures above 900 °C and exposing it to hydrogen gas. The basic equation is: FeO + H<sub>2</sub> → Fe + H<sub>2</sub>O. This process has recently been made much more practical by the discovery of significant amounts of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>-containing <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">regolith</a> near the <a href="/wiki/Moon#Presence_of_water" title="Moon">Moon's poles</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clementine_spacecraft" title="Clementine spacecraft">Clementine spacecraft</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>Lunar materials may also be valuable for other uses.  It has also been proposed to use lunar regolith as a general construction material,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> through processing techniques such as <a href="/wiki/Sintering" title="Sintering">sintering</a>, hot-pressing, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquification" title="Liquification">liquification</a>, and the cast <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basalt</a> method. Cast basalt is used on Earth for construction of, for example, pipes where a high resistance to abrasion is required. Cast basalt has a very high <a href="/wiki/Hardness" title="Hardness">hardness</a> of 8 <a href="/wiki/Mohs_scale_of_mineral_hardness" title="Mohs scale of mineral hardness">Mohs</a> (<a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">diamond</a> is 10 Mohs) but is also susceptible to mechanical impact and <a href="/wiki/Thermal_shock" title="Thermal shock">thermal shock</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> which could be a problem on the Moon.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">Glass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glass_fiber" title="Glass fiber">glass fiber</a> are straightforward to process on the Moon and Mars, and it has been argued that the glass is optically superior to that made on the Earth because it can be made <a href="/wiki/Anhydrous" title="Anhydrous">anhydrous</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mining_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-mining-36">[36]</a></sup> Successful tests have been performed on Earth using two <a href="/wiki/Lunar_regolith_simulant" title="Lunar regolith simulant">lunar regolith simulants</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MLS-1" title="MLS-1">MLS-1</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MLS-2" title="MLS-2">MLS-2</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basalt_fibre" title="Basalt fibre">Basalt fibre</a> has also been made from lunar regolith simulators.
</p><p>In August 2005, NASA contracted for the production of 16 tonnes of simulated lunar soil,
or "<a href="/wiki/Lunar_regolith_simulant" title="Lunar regolith simulant">Lunar Regolith Simulant Material</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
This material is now commercially available for research on how lunar soil could be utilized <i>in situ</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Martian_moons.2C_Ceres.2C_asteroids"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Martian_moons,_Ceres,_asteroids">Martian moons, Ceres, asteroids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ_resource_utilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Martian moons, Ceres, asteroids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Other proposals<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> are based on <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">Phobos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deimos_(moon)" title="Deimos (moon)">Deimos</a>. These moons are in reasonably high orbits above Mars, have very low escape velocities, and unlike Mars have return <a href="/wiki/Delta-v_budget" title="Delta-v budget">delta-v</a>'s from their surfaces to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_Earth_Orbit" title="Low Earth Orbit">LEO</a> which are less than the return from the Moon.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ceres_(dwarf_planet)" title="Ceres (dwarf planet)">Ceres</a> is further out than Mars, with a higher delta-v, but launch windows and travel times are better, and the surface gravity is just 0.028 g, with a very low escape velocity of 510 m/s. Researchers have speculated that the interior configuration of Ceres includes a water-ice-rich mantle over a rocky core.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas2005_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas2005-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near_Earth_Asteroids" title="Near Earth Asteroids">Near Earth Asteroids</a> and bodies in the <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroid</a> belt could also be sources of raw materials for ISRU.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Planetary_atmospheres">Planetary atmospheres</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ_resource_utilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Planetary atmospheres">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Proposals have been made for "mining" for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rocket_propulsion" title="Rocket propulsion">rocket propulsion</a>, using what is called a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propulsive_Fluid_Accumulator" title="Propulsive Fluid Accumulator">Propulsive Fluid Accumulator</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_gas" title="Atmospheric gas">Atmospheric gases</a> like oxygen and <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a> could be extracted from the atmosphere of planets like the Earth, Mars, and the outer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_Giants" title="Gas Giants">Gas Giants</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propulsive_Fluid_Accumulator" title="Propulsive Fluid Accumulator">Propulsive Fluid Accumulator</a> satellites in low orbit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones2010_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jones2010-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="ISRU_capability_classification_.28NASA.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="ISRU_capability_classification_(NASA)">ISRU capability classification (NASA)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ_resource_utilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: ISRU capability classification (NASA)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In October 2004, NASA’s Advanced Planning and Integration Office commissioned an ISRU capability roadmap team.
The team's report, along with those of 14 other capability roadmap teams, were published May 22, 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCRES2005_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCRES2005-47">[47]</a></sup>
The report identifies seven ISRU capabilities:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCRES2005_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-NCRES2005-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>278</span></sup>
(i) resource extraction, (ii) material handling and transport, (iii) resource processing, (iv) surface manufacturing with <i>in situ</i> resources, (v) surface construction, (vi) surface ISRU product and consumable storage and distribution, and (vii) ISRU unique development and certification capabilities. 
</p><p>The report focuses on lunar and martian environments. It offers a detailed timeline<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCRES2005_47-2"><a href="#cite_note-NCRES2005-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>274</span></sup> and capability roadmap to 2040<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCRES2005_47-3"><a href="#cite_note-NCRES2005-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>280-281</span></sup> but it assumes lunar landers in 2010 and 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCRES2005_47-4"><a href="#cite_note-NCRES2005-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>280</span></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="ISRU_technology_demonstrators_and_prototypes">ISRU technology demonstrators and prototypes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ_resource_utilization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: ISRU technology demonstrators and prototypes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Mars_Surveyor_2001_Lander" title="Mars Surveyor 2001 Lander">Mars Surveyor 2001 Lander</a> was intended to carry to Mars a test payload, MIP (Mars ISPP Precursor), that was to demonstrate manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> from the atmosphere of Mars,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaplan2001_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kaplan2001-48">[48]</a></sup> but the mission was cancelled.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Mars_Oxygen_ISRU_Experiment" title="Mars Oxygen ISRU Experiment">Mars Oxygen ISRU Experiment</a> (MOXIE) is a 1% scale prototype model aboard the planned <a href="/wiki/Mars_2020" title="Mars 2020">Mars 2020</a> rover that will produce <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> from Martian atmospheric carbon dioxide (<a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a>) in a process called <a href="/wiki/Solid_oxide_electrolyser_cell" title="Solid oxide electrolyser cell">solid oxide electrolysis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-techport_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-techport-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wall_2014_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_2014-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GFSC_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-GFSC-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weinstock_2014_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Weinstock_2014-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p>
