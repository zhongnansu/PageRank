<p><b>Social constructionism</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">theory of knowledge</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Communication_theory" title="Communication theory">communication theory</a> that examines the development of jointly constructed understandings of the world that form the basis for shared assumptions about reality. The theory centers on the notion that meanings are developed in coordination with others rather than separately within each individual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Social constructionism questions what is defined by humans and society to be reality. Therefore, social constructs can be different based on the society and the events surrounding the time period in which they exist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> An example of a social construct is money or the concept of currency, as people in society have agreed to give it importance/ value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:12_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:12-3">[3]</a></sup> Another example of a social construction is the concept of self/ self-identity. <a href="/wiki/Charles_Cooley" title="Charles Cooley">Charles Cooley</a> stated based on his <a href="/wiki/Looking-glass_self" title="Looking-glass self">Looking-Glass-Self</a> theory: "I am not who you think I am; I am not who I think I am; I am who I think you think I am."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> This demonstrates how people in society construct ideas or concepts that may not exist without the existence of people or language to validate those concepts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are weak and strong social constructs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:12_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-:12-3">[3]</a></sup> Weak social constructs rely on <a href="/wiki/Brute_fact" title="Brute fact">brute facts</a> (which are fundamental facts that are difficult to explain or understand, such as <a href="/wiki/Quark" title="Quark">quarks</a>) or institutional facts (which are formed from social conventions).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:12_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-:12-3">[3]</a></sup> Strong social constructs rely on the human perspective and knowledge that doesn't just exist, but is rather constructed by society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_constructionism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A social construct or construction concerns the meaning, notion, or connotation placed on an object or event by a society, and adopted by the inhabitants of that society with respect to how they view or deal with the object or event.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In that respect, a social construct as an idea would be widely accepted as natural by the society.
</p><p>A major focus of social constructionism is to uncover the ways in which individuals and groups participate in the construction of their perceived social reality. It involves looking at the ways <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_phenomena" title="Social phenomena">social phenomena</a> are developed, <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Institutionalization_(disambiguation)" title="Institutionalization (disambiguation)">institutionalized</a>, known, and made into <a href="/wiki/Tradition" title="Tradition">tradition</a> by humans.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_constructionism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In terms of background, social constructionism is rooted in "<a href="/wiki/Symbolic_interactionism" title="Symbolic interactionism">symbolic interactionism</a>" and "phenomenology."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">[6]</a></sup> With Berger and Luckman's <i>The Social Construction of Reality</i> published in 1966, this concept found its hold. More than four decades later, a sizable number of theory and research pledged to the basic tenet that people "make their social and cultural worlds at the same time these worlds make them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">[6]</a></sup> It is a viewpoint that uproots social processes "simultaneously playful and serious, by which reality is both revealed and concealed, created and destroyed by our activities."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">[6]</a></sup> It provides a substitute to the "Western intellectual tradition" where the researcher "earnestly seeks certainty in a representation of reality by means of propositions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>In social constructionist terms, "taken-for-granted realities" are cultivated from "interactions between and among social agents;" furthermore, reality is not some objective truth "waiting to be uncovered through positivist scientific inquiry."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">[6]</a></sup> Rather, there can be "multiple realities that compete for truth and legitimacy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">[6]</a></sup> Social constructionism understands the "fundamental role of language and communication" and this understanding has "contributed to the linguistic turn" and more recently the "turn to <a href="/wiki/Discourse" title="Discourse">discourse</a> theory."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The majority of social constructionists abide by the belief that "language does not mirror reality; rather, it constitutes [creates] it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>A broad definition of social constructionism has its supporters and critics in the organizational sciences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_6-8"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">[6]</a></sup> A constructionist approach to various organizational and managerial phenomena appear to be more commonplace and on the rise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_6-9"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Andy Lock and Tom Strong trace some of the fundamental tenets of social constructionism back to the work of the 18th century Italian political philosopher, rhetorician, historian, and jurist <a href="/wiki/Giambattista_Vico" title="Giambattista Vico">Giambattista Vico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Berger and Luckmann give credit to <a href="/wiki/Max_Scheler" title="Max Scheler">Max Scheler</a> as a large influence as he created the idea of <a href="/wiki/Sociology_of_knowledge" title="Sociology of knowledge">Sociology of knowledge</a> which influenced social construction theory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to Lock and Strong, other influential thinkers whose work has affected the development of social constructionism are: <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Husserl" title="Edmund Husserl">Edmund Husserl</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfred_Schutz" title="Alfred Schutz">Alfred Schutz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Merleau-Ponty" title="Maurice Merleau-Ponty">Maurice Merleau-Ponty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martin_Heidegger" title="Martin Heidegger">Martin Heidegger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hans-Georg_Gadamer" title="Hans-Georg Gadamer">Hans-Georg Gadamer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_Ricoeur" title="Paul Ricoeur">Paul Ricoeur</a>, <a href="/wiki/J%C3%BCrgen_Habermas" title="Jürgen Habermas">Jürgen Habermas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emmanuel_Levinas" title="Emmanuel Levinas">Emmanuel Levinas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Bakhtin" title="Mikhail Bakhtin">Mikhail Bakhtin</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valentin_Volosinov" title="Valentin Volosinov">Valentin Volosinov</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lev_Vygotsky" title="Lev Vygotsky">Lev Vygotsky</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Herbert_Mead" title="George Herbert Mead">George Herbert Mead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Wittgenstein" title="Ludwig Wittgenstein">Ludwig Wittgenstein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gregory_Bateson" title="Gregory Bateson">Gregory Bateson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harold_Garfinkel" title="Harold Garfinkel">Harold Garfinkel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erving_Goffman" title="Erving Goffman">Erving Goffman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Giddens" title="Anthony Giddens">Anthony Giddens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michel_Foucault" title="Michel Foucault">Michel Foucault</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ken_Gergen" title="Ken Gergen">Ken Gergen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mary_Gergen" title="Mary Gergen">Mary Gergen</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rom_Harre" title="Rom Harre">Rom Harre</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=John_Shotter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="John Shotter (page does not exist)">John Shotter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_constructionism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_construct_psychology">Personal construct psychology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_constructionism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal construct psychology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since its appearance in the 1950s, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personal_construct_psychology" title="Personal construct psychology">personal construct psychology</a> (PCP) has mainly developed as a constructivist theory of personality and a system of transforming individual <a href="/wiki/Meaning-making" title="Meaning-making">meaning-making</a> processes, largely in therapeutic contexts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> It was based around the notion of persons as scientists who form and test theories about their worlds. Therefore, it represented one of the first attempts to appreciate the constructive nature of experience and the meaning persons give to their experience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Social constructionism (SC), on the other hand, mainly developed as a form of a critique,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> aimed to transform the oppressing effects of the social meaning-making processes. Over the years, it has grown into a cluster of different approaches,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> with no single SC position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> However, different approaches under the generic term of SC are loosely linked by some shared assumptions about language, knowledge, and reality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>A usual way of thinking about the relationship between PCP and SC is treating them as two separate entities that are similar in some aspects, but also very different in others. This way of conceptualizing this relationship is a logical result of the circumstantial differences of their emergence. In subsequent analyses these differences between PCP and SC were framed around several points of tension, formulated as binary oppositions: personal/social; individualist/relational; agency/structure; constructivist/constructionist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Although some of the most important issues in contemporary psychology are elaborated in these contributions, the polarized positioning also sustained the idea of a separation between PCP and SC, paving the way for only limited opportunities for dialogue between them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Reframing the relationship between PCP and SC may be of use in both the PCP and the SC communities. On one hand, it extends and enriches SC theory and points to benefits of applying the PCP “toolkit” in constructionist therapy and research. On the other hand, the reframing contributes to PCP theory and points to new ways of addressing social construction in therapeutic conversations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Educational_psychology">Educational psychology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_constructionism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Educational psychology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like social constructionism, <a href="/wiki/Social_constructivism" title="Social constructivism">social constructivism</a> states that people work together to construct <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_artifact" title="Social artifact">artifacts</a>. While social constructionism focuses on the artifacts that are created through the social interactions of a group, social constructivism focuses on an individual's learning that takes place because of his or her interactions in a group.
</p><p>Social constructivism has been studied by many educational psychologists, who are concerned with its implications for teaching and learning. For more on the psychological dimensions of social constructivism, see the work of <a href="/wiki/Ernst_von_Glasersfeld" title="Ernst von Glasersfeld">Ernst von Glasersfeld</a> and A. Sullivan Palincsar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Systemic_therapy">Systemic therapy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_constructionism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Systemic therapy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systemic_therapy" title="Systemic therapy">Systemic therapy</a> is a form of psychotherapy which seeks to address people as people in relationship, dealing with the interactions of groups and their interactional patterns and dynamics.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Teleology_of_social_construction">Teleology of social construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_constructionism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Teleology of social construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The concepts of <i>weak</i> and <b>strong</b> as applied to opposing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philosophical_position" title="Philosophical position">philosophical positions</a>, "isms", inform a <a href="/wiki/Teleology" title="Teleology">teleology</a> – the goal-oriented, meaningful or "final end" of an interpretation of reality. "Isms" are not personal opinions, but the extreme, modal, formulations that actual persons, individuals, can then consider, and take a position between. There are opposing philosophical positions concerning the feasibility of co-creating a common, shared, social reality, called <i>weak</i> and <b>strong</b>.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_R._Searle" title="John R. Searle">John R. Searle</a> does not elucidate the terms <b>strong</b> and <i>weak</i> in his book <i>The Construction of Social Reality</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cosr_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-cosr-33">[33]</a></sup> but he clearly uses them in his <a href="/wiki/Chinese_room" title="Chinese room">Chinese room</a> argument, where he debates the feasibility of creating a computing machine with a sharable understanding of reality, and he adds <a href="/wiki/Chinese_room#Computers_vs._machines_vs._brains" title="Chinese room">"We are precisely such machines."</a> <b>Strong</b> artificial intelligence (<a href="/wiki/Artificial_general_intelligence" title="Artificial general intelligence">Strong AI</a>) is the bet that computer programmers will somehow eventually achieve a computing machine with a mind of its own, and that it will eventually be more powerful than a human mind. Weak AI bets they won't.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/David_Deutsch" title="David Deutsch">David Deutsch</a> in his book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fabric_of_Reality#The_Church–Turing_thesis" title="The Fabric of Reality">The Fabric of Reality</a></i> uses a form of <b>strong</b> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turing_principle" title="Turing principle">Turing principle</a> to share <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frank_Tipler" title="Frank Tipler">Frank Tipler</a>'s view of the final state of the universe as an omnipotent (but not <a href="/wiki/Omniscience" title="Omniscience">omniscient</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Omega_point" title="Omega point">Omega point</a>, computer. <i>But this computer is a society of creative thinkers, or people</i> (albeit posthuman transhuman persons), having debates in order to generate information, in the never-ending attempt to attain omniscience of this physics—its evolutionary forms, its computational abilities, and the methods of its epistemology—having an eternity to do so. (p. 356)
</p><p>Because both the Chinese room argument and the construction of social reality deal with Searle and his debates, and because they both use <i>weak</i> and <b>strong</b> to denote a philosophical position, and because both debate the programmability of "the other", it is worth noting the correspondence that "strong AI" is <b>strong</b> social constructionism, and "weak AI" is weak social constructivism.
</p><p>Strong social constructiv<i>ism</i> says "none are able to <i>communicate</i> either a full reality or an accurate ontology, therefore my position must impose, by a sort of <a href="/wiki/Divine_right_of_kings" title="Divine right of kings">divine right</a>, my observer-relative epistemology", whereas weak social constructiv<i>ism</i> says "none are able to <i>know</i> a full reality, therefore <i>we must</i> cooperate, informing and conveying an objective ontology as best we can."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weak_teleology">Weak teleology <span id="Weak"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_constructionism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Weak teleology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Weak social constructionism</i> sees the underlying, objective, "<a href="/wiki/Brute_fact" title="Brute fact">brute fact</a>" elements of the class of languages and functional assignments of human, metaphysical, reality. Brute facts are all facts that are not institutional (metaphysical, social agreement) facts. The skeptic portrays the <i>weak</i> aspect of social constructivism, and wants to spend effort debating the institutional realities.
</p><p>Harvard psychologist <a href="/wiki/Steven_Pinker" title="Steven Pinker">Steven Pinker</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> writes that "some categories really are social constructions: they exist only because people tacitly agree to act as if they exist. Examples include money, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tenure" title="Tenure">tenure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Citizenship" title="Citizenship">citizenship</a>, decorations for bravery, and the presidency of the United States."
</p><p>In a similar vein, <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Fish" title="Stanley Fish">Stanley Fish</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> has suggested that baseball's "balls and strikes" are social constructions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>29–31</span></sup>
</p><p>Both Fish and Pinker agree that the sorts of <a href="/wiki/Object_(philosophy)" title="Object (philosophy)">objects</a> indicated here can be described as part of what <a href="/wiki/John_Searle" title="John Searle">John Searle</a> calls "social reality."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>22</span></sup> In particular, they are, in Searle's terms, <a href="/wiki/Ontology" title="Ontology">ontologically</a> <a href="/wiki/Subjectivity" title="Subjectivity">subjective</a> but <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemologically</a> <a href="/wiki/Objectivity_(philosophy)" title="Objectivity (philosophy)">objective</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cosr_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-cosr-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>63</span></sup> "Social facts" are temporally, ontologically, and logically dependent on "brute facts." For example, "money" in the form of its raw materials (rag, pulp, ink) as constituted socially for barter (for example by a banking system) is a social fact of "money" by virtue of (i) collectively willing and intending (ii) to impose some particular function (purpose for which), (iii) by constitutive rules atop the "brute facts." "Social facts have the remarkable feature of having no analogue among physical brute facts" (34). The existence of language is itself constitutive of the social fact (37), which natural or brute facts do not require. Natural or "brute" facts exist independently of language; thus a "mountain" is a mountain in every language and in no language; it simply is what it is.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cosr_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-cosr-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>29, et seq</span></sup>
</p><p>Searle illustrates the evolution of social facts from brute facts by the constitutive rule: X counts as Y in C. "The Y terms has to assign a new <i>status</i> that the object does not already have just in virtue of satisfying the Y term; and there has to be collective agreement, or at least acceptance, both in the imposition of that status on the stuff referred to by the X term and about the function that goes with that status. Furthermore, because the physical features brute facts specified by the X term are insufficient by themselves to guarantee the fulfillment of the assigned function specified by the Y term, the new status and its attendant functions have to be the sort of things that can be constituted by collective agreement or acceptance."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cosr_33-3"><a href="#cite_note-cosr-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>44</span></sup>
</p><p>It is true that language is not a "brute fact," that it is an institutional fact, a human convention, a metaphysical reality (that happens to be physically uttered), but Searle points out that there are language-independent thoughts "noninstitutional, primitive, biological inclinations and cognitions not requiring any linguistic devices," and that there are many "brute facts" amongst both humans and animals that are truths that should not be altered in the social constructs because language does not truly constitute them, despite the attempt to institute them for any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Group_think" title="Group think">group's gain</a>: money and property are language dependent, but desires (thirst, hunger) and emotions (fear, rage) are not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cosr_33-4"><a href="#cite_note-cosr-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>62</span></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Meditations_on_First_Philosophy" title="Meditations on First Philosophy">Descartes</a> describes the difference between imagination as a sort of vision, or image, and intellect as conceptualizing things by symbolic manipulation.) Therefore, there is doubt that society or a computer can be completely programmed by language and images, (because there is a programmable, emotive effect of images that derives from the language of judgment towards images).
</p><p>Finally, against the strong theory and for the weak theory, Searle insists, "it could not be the case, as some have maintained, that all facts are institutional [i.e., social] facts, that there are no brute facts, because the structure of institutional facts reveals that they are logically dependent on brute facts. To suppose that all facts are institutional [i.e., social] would produce an infinite regress or circularity in the account of institutional facts. In order that some facts are institutional, there must be other facts that are brute [i.e., physical, biological, natural]. This is the consequence of the logical structure of institutional facts.".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cosr_33-5"><a href="#cite_note-cosr-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>56</span></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ian_Hacking" title="Ian Hacking">Ian Hacking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canadians" title="Canadians">Canadian</a> philosopher of science, insists, "the notion that everything is socially constructed has been going the rounds. John Searle [1995] argues vehemently (and in my opinion cogently) against universal constructionism."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hacking1999_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hacking1999-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>24</span></sup> "Universal social constructionism is descended from the doctrine that I once named linguistic idealism and attributed, only half in jest, to Richard Nixon [Hacking, 1975, p. 182]. Linguistic idealism is the doctrine that only what is talked about exists, nothing has reality until it is spoken of, or written about. This extravagant notion is descended from Berkeley's idea-ism, which we call idealism: the doctrine that all that exists is mental."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hacking1999_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hacking1999-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>24</span></sup> "They are a part of what John Searle [1995] calls social reality. His book is titled the <i>Construction of Social Reality,</i> and as I explained elsewhere [Hacking, 1996], that is not a <i>social</i> construction book at all."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hacking1999_39-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hacking1999-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>12</span></sup>
</p><p>Hacking observes, "the label 'social constructionism' is more code than description"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hacking1999_39-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hacking1999-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>15</span></sup> of every Leftist, Marxist, Freudian, and Feminist PostModernist to call into question every moral, sex, gender, power, and deviant claim as just another essentialist claim—including the claim that members of the male and female sex are inherently different, rather than historically and socially constructed. Hacking observes that his 1995 simplistic dismissal of the concept actually revealed to many readers the outrageous implications of the theorists: Is child abuse a real evil, or a social construct, asked Hacking? His dismissive attitude, "gave some readers a way to see that there need be no clash between construction and reality,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hacking1999_39-4"><a href="#cite_note-Hacking1999-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>29</span></sup> inasmuch as "the metaphor of social construction once had excellent shock value, but now it has become tired."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hacking1999_39-5"><a href="#cite_note-Hacking1999-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>35</span></sup>
</p><p>Informally, they require human practices to sustain their existence, but they have an effect that is (basically) universally agreed upon. The disagreement lies in whether this category should be called "socially constructed." Ian Hacking<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> argues that it should not. Furthermore, it is not clear that authors who write "social construction" analyses ever mean "social construction" in Pinker's sense. ".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> If they never do, then Pinker (probably among others) has misunderstood the point of a social constructionist argument.
</p><p>To understand how weak social constructionism can conclude that metaphysics (a human affair) is not the entire "reality," see the arguments <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics#The_value_and_future_of_metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">against the study of metaphysics</a>. This inability to accurately share the <i>full</i> reality, even given time for a rational conversation, is similarly proclaimed by <i>weak artificial intelligence</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_development">History and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_constructionism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: History and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Berger_and_Luckmann">Berger and Luckmann</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_constructionism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Berger and Luckmann">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Constructionism became prominent in the U.S. with <a href="/wiki/Peter_L._Berger" title="Peter L. Berger">Peter L. Berger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Luckmann" title="Thomas Luckmann">Thomas Luckmann</a>'s 1966 book, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Social_Construction_of_Reality" title="The Social Construction of Reality">The Social Construction of Reality</a></i>. Berger and Luckmann argue that all knowledge, including the most basic, taken-for-granted <a href="/wiki/Common_sense" title="Common sense">common sense</a> knowledge of everyday reality, is derived from and maintained by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_interactions" title="Social interactions">social interactions</a>. When people interact, they do so with the understanding that their respective perceptions of reality are related, and as they act upon this understanding their common knowledge of reality becomes reinforced. Since this common sense knowledge is negotiated by people, human <a href="/wiki/Typification" title="Typification">typifications</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sign_(semiotics)" title="Sign (semiotics)">significations</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_institution" title="Social institution">institutions</a> come to be presented as part of an objective reality, particularly for future generations who were not involved in the original process of negotiation. For example, as parents negotiate rules for their children to follow, those rules confront the children as externally produced "givens" that they cannot change. Berger and Luckmann's social constructionism has its roots in <a href="/wiki/Phenomenology_(philosophy)" title="Phenomenology (philosophy)">phenomenology</a>. It links to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heidegger" title="Heidegger">Heidegger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Husserl" title="Edmund Husserl">Edmund Husserl</a> through the teaching of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfred_Schutz" title="Alfred Schutz">Alfred Schutz</a>, who was also Berger's PhD adviser.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Narrative_turn">Narrative turn</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_constructionism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Narrative turn">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the 1970s and 1980s, social constructionist theory underwent a transformation as constructionist sociologists engaged with the work of <a href="/wiki/Michel_Foucault" title="Michel Foucault">Michel Foucault</a> and others as a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Narrative_turn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Narrative turn (page does not exist)">narrative turn</a> in the social sciences was worked out in practice. This particularly affected the emergent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sociology_of_science" title="Sociology of science">sociology of science</a> and the growing field of <a href="/wiki/Science_and_technology_studies" title="Science and technology studies">science and technology studies</a>. In particular, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karin_Knorr-Cetina" title="Karin Knorr-Cetina">Karin Knorr-Cetina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Latour" title="Bruno Latour">Bruno Latour</a>, <a href="/wiki/S._Barry_Barnes" title="S. Barry Barnes">Barry Barnes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steve_Woolgar" title="Steve Woolgar">Steve Woolgar</a>, and others used social constructionism to relate what science has typically characterized as objective facts to the processes of social construction, with the goal of showing that human <a href="/wiki/Subjectivity" title="Subjectivity">subjectivity</a> imposes itself on those facts we take to be objective, not solely the other way around. A particularly provocative title in this line of thought is <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Pickering" title="Andrew Pickering">Andrew Pickering</a>'s <i>Constructing Quarks: A Sociological History of Particle Physics</i>. At the same time, Social Constructionism shaped studies of technology – the Sofield, especially on the <a href="/wiki/Social_construction_of_technology" title="Social construction of technology">Social construction of technology</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SCOT" title="SCOT">SCOT</a>, and authors as <a href="/wiki/Wiebe_Bijker" title="Wiebe Bijker">Wiebe Bijker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trevor_Pinch" title="Trevor Pinch">Trevor Pinch</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Maarten_van_Wesel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Maarten van Wesel (page does not exist)">Maarten van Wesel</a>, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Despite its common perception as objective, mathematics is not immune to social constructionist accounts. Sociologists such as <a href="/wiki/Sal_Restivo" title="Sal Restivo">Sal Restivo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Randall_Collins" title="Randall Collins">Randall Collins</a>, mathematicians including <a href="/wiki/Reuben_Hersh" title="Reuben Hersh">Reuben Hersh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philip_J._Davis" title="Philip J. Davis">Philip J. Davis</a>, and philosophers including <a href="/wiki/Paul_Ernest" title="Paul Ernest">Paul Ernest</a> have published social constructionist treatments of mathematics.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Postmodernism">Postmodernism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_constructionism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Postmodernism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Social constructionism can be seen as a source of the <a href="/wiki/Postmodernism" title="Postmodernism">postmodern</a> movement, and has been influential in the field of <a href="/wiki/Cultural_studies" title="Cultural studies">cultural studies</a>. Some have gone so far as to attribute the rise of cultural studies (the <a href="/wiki/Cultural_turn" title="Cultural turn">cultural turn</a>) to social constructionism. Within the social constructionist strand of postmodernism, the concept of socially constructed reality stresses the ongoing mass-building of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Worldview" title="Worldview">worldviews</a> by <a href="/wiki/Individual" title="Individual">individuals</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dialectic" title="Dialectic">dialectical</a> interaction with society at a time. The numerous <a href="/wiki/Reality" title="Reality">realities</a> so formed comprise, according to this view, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imagined_communities" title="Imagined communities">imagined worlds</a> of human social existence and activity, gradually crystallized by <a href="/wiki/Habituation" title="Habituation">habit</a> into institutions propped up by <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a> conventions, given ongoing legitimacy by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mythology" title="Mythology">mythology</a>, religion and philosophy, maintained by therapies and <a href="/wiki/Socialization" title="Socialization">socialization</a>, and subjectively <a href="/wiki/Internalisation_(sociology)" title="Internalisation (sociology)">internalized</a> by upbringing and education to become part of the <a href="/wiki/Identity_(social_science)" title="Identity (social science)">identity</a> of social citizens.
</p><p>In the book <i>The Reality of Social Construction</i>, the British sociologist Dave Elder-Vass places the development of social constructionism as one outcome of the legacy of postmodernism. He writes "Perhaps the most widespread and influential product of this process [coming to terms with the legacy of postmodernism] is social constructionism, which has been booming [within the domain of social theory] since the 1980s."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_constructionism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Social constructionism falls toward the nurture end of the spectrum of the larger <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nature_and_nurture" title="Nature and nurture">nature and nurture</a> debate. Consequently, critics have argued that it generally ignores biological influences on behaviour or culture, or suggests that they are unimportant to achieve an understanding of human behaviour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> The view of most psychologists and social scientists is that behaviour is a complex outcome of both biological and cultural influences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Other disciplines, such as <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_psychology" title="Evolutionary psychology">evolutionary psychology</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Behaviour_genetics" title="Behaviour genetics">behaviour genetics</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Behavioural_neuroscience" title="Behavioural neuroscience">behavioural neuroscience</a>, <a href="/wiki/Epigenetics" title="Epigenetics">epigenetics</a>, etc., take a nature–nurture interactionism approach to understand behaviour or cultural phenomena.
</p><p>In 1996, to illustrate what he believed to be the intellectual weaknesses of social constructionism and postmodernism, physics professor <a href="/wiki/Alan_Sokal" title="Alan Sokal">Alan Sokal</a> submitted an article to the academic journal <i><a href="/wiki/Social_Text" title="Social Text">Social Text</a></i> deliberately written to be incomprehensible but including phrases and jargon typical of the articles published by the journal. The submission, which was published, was an experiment to see if the journal would "publish an article liberally salted with nonsense if (a) it sounded good and (b) it flattered the editors' ideological preconceptions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Postmodernism_Generator" title="Postmodernism Generator">Postmodernism Generator</a> is a computer program that is designed to produce similarly incomprehensible text.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> In 1999, Sokal, with coauthor Jean Bricmont published the book <i><a href="/wiki/Fashionable_Nonsense" title="Fashionable Nonsense">Fashionable Nonsense</a></i>, which criticized <a href="/wiki/Postmodernism" title="Postmodernism">postmodernism</a> and social constructionism.
</p><p>Philosopher <a href="/wiki/Paul_Boghossian" title="Paul Boghossian">Paul Boghossian</a> has also written against social constructionism. He follows Ian Hacking's argument that many adopt social constructionism because of its potentially liberating stance: if things are the way that they are only because of our social conventions, as opposed to being so naturally, then it should be possible to change them into how we would rather have them be. He then states that social constructionists argue that we should refrain from making absolute judgements about what is true and instead state that something is true in the light of this or that theory. Countering this, he states:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>But it is hard to see how we might coherently follow this advice. Given that the propositions which make up epistemic systems are just very general propositions about what absolutely justifies what, it makes no sense to insist that we abandon making absolute <i>particular</i> judgements about what justifies what while allowing us to <i>accept</i> absolute <i>general</i> judgements about what justifies what. But in effect this is what the epistemic relativist is recommending.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>Later in the same work, Boghossian severely constrains the requirements of relativism. He states that instead of believing that any world view is just as true as any other (<a href="/wiki/Cultural_relativism" title="Cultural relativism">cultural relativism</a>), we should believe that:
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>If we were to encounter an <i>actual</i>, coherent, fundamental, genuine alternative to our epistemic system, C2, whose track record was <i>impressive</i> enough to make us doubt the correctness of our own system, C1, we would not be able to justify C1 over C2 even by our own lights.
</p></blockquote>
<p>Woolgar and Pawluch<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> argue that constructionists tend to 'ontological gerrymander' social conditions in and out of their analysis. Following this point, Thibodeaux<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> argued that constructionism can both separate and combine a subject and their effective environment. To resolve this he argued that objective conditions should be used when analyzing how perspectives are motivated.
</p><p>Social constructionism has been criticized by psychologists such as University of Toronto Professor <a href="/wiki/Jordan_Peterson" title="Jordan Peterson">Jordan Peterson</a> and evolutionary psychologists, including <a href="/wiki/Steven_Pinker" title="Steven Pinker">Steven Pinker</a> in his book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Blank_Slate" title="The Blank Slate">The Blank Slate</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Tooby" title="John Tooby">John Tooby</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leda_Cosmides" title="Leda Cosmides">Leda Cosmides</a> used the term "<a href="/wiki/Standard_social_science_model" title="Standard social science model">standard social science model</a>" to refer to social-science philosophies that they argue fail to take into account the evolved properties of the brain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
