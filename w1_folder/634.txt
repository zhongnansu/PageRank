<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Flatulence</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Flatulence</b> is defined in the medical literature as "flatus expelled through the <a href="/wiki/Anus" title="Anus">anus</a>" or the "quality or state of being flatulent",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> which is defined in turn as "marked by or affected with gases generated in the intestine or stomach; likely to cause digestive flatulence".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The root of these words is from the Latin <i>flatus</i> – "a blowing, a breaking wind".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Flatus is also the medical word for gas generated in the <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomach</a> or bowels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Despite these standard definitions, a proportion of intestinal gas may be swallowed environmental air, and hence flatus is not totally generated in the stomach or bowels. The scientific study of this area of medicine is termed <b>flatology</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tangerman_2009_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tangerman_2009-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is normal for humans to pass flatus through the <a href="/wiki/Rectum" title="Rectum">rectum</a>, although the volume and frequency may vary greatly between individuals. It is also normal for intestinal gas passed through the rectum to have a characteristic <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/feculent" title="wiktionary:feculent">feculent</a> smell, although this too may vary in concentration. Flatus is brought to the rectum by specialised contractions of the muscles in the intestines and colon. The noises commonly associated with flatulence ("<a href="/wiki/Blowing_a_raspberry" title="Blowing a raspberry">blowing a raspberry</a>") are caused by the vibration of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sphincter_ani_internus_muscle" title="Sphincter ani internus muscle">anal sphincters</a>, and occasionally by the closed <a href="/wiki/Buttocks" title="Buttocks">buttocks</a>. Both the noise and smell associated with flatus leaving the anus can be sources of embarrassment or comedy in many cultures.
</p><p>There are five general symptoms related to intestinal gas: pain, bloating and abdominal distension, excessive flatus volume, excessive flatus smell and gas incontinence. Furthermore, <a href="/wiki/Burping" title="Burping">eructation</a> ("an act or instance of belching", colloquially known as "burping") is sometimes included under the topic of flatulence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-K_C_clinical_medicine_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-K_C_clinical_medicine-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><div class="mediaContainer" style="width:220px"><audio class="kskin" controls="" data-durationhint="1.4367346938776" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons" data-mwtitle="Fart.ogg" data-startoffset="0" id="mwe_player_0" preload="none" style="width:220px"><source data-bandwidth="122801" data-height="0" data-shorttitle="Ogg source" data-title="Original Ogg file (123 kbps)" data-width="0" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cf/Fart.ogg" type='audio/ogg; codecs="vorbis"'/><source data-bandwidth="157256" data-height="0" data-shorttitle="MP3" data-title="MP3" data-transcodekey="mp3" data-width="0" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/c/cf/Fart.ogg/Fart.ogg.mp3" type="audio/mpeg"/></audio></div> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fart.ogg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A human flatulence</div></div></div>
<p>Non-medical definitions of the term include "the uncomfortable condition of having gas in the stomach and bowels", or "a state of excessive gas in the alimentary canal". These definitions highlight that many people consider "bloating", <a href="/wiki/Abdominal_distension" title="Abdominal distension">abdominal distension</a> or increased volume of intestinal gas to be synonymous with the term flatulence (although this is technically inaccurate). Colloquially, flatulence may be referred to as "farting", "trumping",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> "tooting", "passing gas", "breaking wind" or simply (in <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a>) "gas" or (<a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a>) "wind". Derived terms include <a href="/wiki/Vaginal_flatulence" title="Vaginal flatulence">vaginal flatulence</a>, otherwise known as a <i>queef</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Signs_and_symptoms">Signs and symptoms</span></h2>
<p>Generally speaking, there are four different types of complaints that relate to intestinal gas, which may present individually or in combination.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bloating_and_pain">Bloating and pain</span></h3>
<p>Patients may complain of bloating as abdominal distension, discomfort and pain from "trapped wind". In the past, functional bowel disorders such as <a href="/wiki/Irritable_bowel_syndrome" title="Irritable bowel syndrome">irritable bowel syndrome</a> (IBS) that produced symptoms of bloating were attributed to increased production of intestinal gas. However, three significant pieces of evidence refute this theory. First, in normal subjects, even very high rates of gas infusion into the small intestine (30 ml/min) are tolerated without complaints of pain or bloating and harmlessly passed as flatus per rectum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Azpiroz_2005_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Azpiroz_2005-8">[8]</a></sup> Secondly, studies aiming to quantify the total volume of gas produced by IBS patients (some including gas emitted from the mouth by eructation) have consistently failed to demonstrate increased volumes compared to healthy subjects. The proportion of hydrogen produced may be increased in a subset of IBS patients, but this does not affect the total volume.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-King_1998_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-King_1998-9">[9]</a></sup> Thirdly, it is known that the total volume of flatus produced by IBS patients who complain of pain and abdominal distension would be tolerated in normal subjects without any complaints of pain.
</p><p>Patients who complain of bloating frequently can be shown to have objective increases in abdominal girth, often increased throughout the day and then resolving during sleep. The increase in girth combined with the fact that the total volume of flatus is not increased led to studies aiming to image the distribution of intestinal gas in patients with bloating. They found that gas was not distributed normally in these patients: there was segmental gas pooling and focal distension.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Azpiroz_2005_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Azpiroz_2005-8">[8]</a></sup> In conclusion, abdominal distension, pain and bloating symptoms are the result of abnormal intestinal gas dynamics rather than increased flatus production.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Excessive_volume">Excessive volume</span></h3>
<p>As mentioned above, the normal range of volumes of flatus in normal individuals varies hugely (476–1,491 ml/24 h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tangerman_2009_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tangerman_2009-5">[5]</a></sup> All intestinal gas is either swallowed environmental air, present intrinsically in foods and beverages or the result of gut fermentation. Swallowing small amounts of air occurs while eating and drinking. This is emitted from the mouth by eructation (burping) and is normal. Excessive swallowing of environmental air is called <a href="/wiki/Aerophagia" title="Aerophagia">aerophagia</a>, and has been shown in a few case reports to be responsible for increased flatus volume. This is however considered a rare cause of increased flatus volume. Gases contained in food and beverages is likewise emitted largely through eructation, e.g., carbonated beverages. Endogenously produced intestinal gases make up 74 percent of flatus in normal subjects. The volume of gas produced is partially dependent upon the composition of the intestinal microbiota, which is normally very resistant to change, but is also very different in different individuals. Some patients are predisposed to increased endogenous gas production by virtue of their gut microbiota composition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Azpiroz_2005_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Azpiroz_2005-8">[8]</a></sup> The greatest concentration of gut bacteria is in the colon, while the small intestine is normally near sterile. Fermentation occurs when unabsorbed food residues may arrive in the colon. Therefore, even more than the composition of the microbiota, diet is the primary factor that dictates the volume of flatus produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Azpiroz_2005_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Azpiroz_2005-8">[8]</a></sup> Diets that aim to reduce the amount of undigested fermentable food residues arriving in the colon have been shown to significantly reduce the volume of flatus produced. Again, it is emphasised that increased volume of intestinal gas will not cause bloating and pain in normal subjects. Abnormal intestinal gas dynamics will create pain, distension, and bloating, regardless of whether there is high or low total flatus volume.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Smell">Smell</span></h3>
<p>Although flatus possesses physiological smell, this may be abnormally increased in some patients and cause social distress to the patient. Increased smell of flatus presents a distinct clinical issue from other complaints related to intestinal gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bailey_2009_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bailey_2009-10">[10]</a></sup> Some patients may exhibit over-sensitivity to bad flatus smell, and in extreme forms, <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_reference_syndrome" title="Olfactory reference syndrome">olfactory reference syndrome</a> may be diagnosed.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Incontinence_of_flatus">Incontinence of flatus</span></h3>
<p>"Gas incontinence" could be defined as loss of voluntary control over the passage of flatus. It is a recognised subtype of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faecal_incontinence" title="Faecal incontinence">faecal incontinence</a>, and is usually related to minor disruptions of the continence mechanisms. Some consider gas incontinence to be the first, sometimes only, symptom of faecal incontinence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCRS_textbook_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ASCRS_textbook-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cause">Cause</span></h2>
<p>Intestinal gas is composed of varying quantities of exogenous sources and endogenous sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH_Gas_in_the_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIH_Gas_in_the-12">[12]</a></sup> The exogenous gases are swallowed (<a href="/wiki/Aerophagia" title="Aerophagia">aerophagia</a>) when eating or drinking or increased swallowing during times of excessive salivation (as might occur when nauseated or as the result of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gastroesophageal_reflux" title="Gastroesophageal reflux">gastroesophageal reflux</a> disease). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endogenous" title="Endogenous">endogenous</a> gases are produced either as a by-product of digesting certain types of food, or of incomplete <a href="/wiki/Digestion" title="Digestion">digestion</a>, as is the case during <a href="/wiki/Steatorrhea" title="Steatorrhea">steatorrhea</a>. Anything that causes food to be incompletely digested by the <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomach</a> or <a href="/wiki/Small_intestine" title="Small intestine">small intestine</a> may cause flatulence when the material arrives in the large intestine, due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">fermentation</a> by <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">yeast</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prokaryotes" title="Prokaryotes">prokaryotes</a> normally or abnormally present in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_gastrointestinal_tract" title="Human gastrointestinal tract">gastrointestinal tract</a>.
</p><p>Flatulence-producing foods are typically high in certain <a href="/wiki/Polysaccharide" title="Polysaccharide">polysaccharides</a>, especially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oligosaccharides" title="Oligosaccharides">oligosaccharides</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Inulin" title="Inulin">inulin</a>. Those foods include <a href="/wiki/Bean" title="Bean">beans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lentil" title="Lentil">lentils</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dairy_product" title="Dairy product">dairy products</a>, <a href="/wiki/Onion" title="Onion">onions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Garlic" title="Garlic">garlic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scallion" title="Scallion">spring onions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leek" title="Leek">leeks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turnip" title="Turnip">turnips</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rutabaga" title="Rutabaga">swedes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radish" title="Radish">radishes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweet_potato" title="Sweet potato">sweet potatoes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Potatoes" title="Potatoes">potatoes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cashew" title="Cashew">cashews</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem_artichoke" title="Jerusalem artichoke">Jerusalem artichokes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oat" title="Oat">oats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">yeast</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bread" title="Bread">breads</a>. <a href="/wiki/Cauliflower" title="Cauliflower">Cauliflower</a>, <a href="/wiki/Broccoli" title="Broccoli">broccoli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cabbage" title="Cabbage">cabbage</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brussels_sprouts" title="Brussels sprouts">Brussels sprouts</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Cruciferous_vegetables" title="Cruciferous vegetables">cruciferous vegetables</a> that belong to the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Brassica" title="Brassica">Brassica</a></i> are commonly reputed to not only increase flatulence, but to increase the pungency of the flatus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>In beans, endogenous gases seem to arise from complex oligosaccharides (<a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a>) that are particularly resistant to digestion by mammals, but are readily digestible by <a href="/wiki/Gut_flora" title="Gut flora">gut flora</a> – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microorganisms" title="Microorganisms">microorganisms</a> (<a href="/wiki/Methanogenesis" title="Methanogenesis">methane-producing</a> archaea; <i><a href="/wiki/Methanobrevibacter_smithii" title="Methanobrevibacter smithii">Methanobrevibacter smithii</a></i>) that inhabit the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digestive_tract" title="Digestive tract">digestive tract</a>. These oligosaccharides pass through the upper intestine largely unchanged, and when they reach the lower intestine, <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> feed on them, producing copious amounts of flatus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McGee_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-McGee-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the case of people who have <a href="/wiki/Lactose_intolerance" title="Lactose intolerance">lactose intolerance</a>, intestinal bacteria feeding on lactose can give rise to excessive gas production when <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">milk</a> or lactose-containing substances have been consumed.
</p><p>Interest in the causes of flatulence was spurred by high-altitude <a href="/wiki/Flight" title="Flight">flight</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manned_spaceflight" title="Manned spaceflight">manned spaceflight</a>; the low <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a>, confined conditions, and stresses peculiar to those endeavours were cause for concern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McGee_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-McGee-15">[15]</a></sup> In the field of mountaineering, the phenomenon of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_altitude_flatus_expulsion" title="High altitude flatus expulsion">high altitude flatus expulsion</a> was first recorded over two hundred years ago.
</p><p>Some infections, such as <a href="/wiki/Giardiasis" title="Giardiasis">giardiasis</a>, are also associated with flatulence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism">Mechanism</span></h2>
<h3><span id="Production.2C_composition.2C_and_smell"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Production,_composition,_and_smell">Production, composition, and smell</span></h3>
<p>Flatus (intestinal gas) is mostly produced as a byproduct of bacterial fermentation in the gastrointestinal (GI) tract, especially the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colon_(anatomy)" title="Colon (anatomy)">colon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tomlin_1991_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tomlin_1991-17">[17]</a></sup> There are reports of <a href="/wiki/Aerophagia" title="Aerophagia">aerophagia</a> (excessive air swallowing) causing excessive intestinal gas, but this is considered rare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hemmink_2009_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hemmink_2009-18">[18]</a></sup> Over 99% of the volume of flatus is composed of non-smelly gases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tangerman_2009_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tangerman_2009-5">[5]</a></sup> These include oxygen, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>. Nitrogen is not produced in the gut, but a component of environmental air. Patients who have excessive intestinal gas that is mostly composed of nitrogen have aerophagia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levitt_1998_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Levitt_1998-19">[19]</a></sup> Hydrogen, carbon dioxide and methane are all produced in the gut and contribute 74% of the volume of flatus in normal subjects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suarez_1997_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Suarez_1997-20">[20]</a></sup> Methane and hydrogen are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flammable" title="Flammable">flammable</a>, and so flatus containing adequate amounts of these can be <a href="/wiki/Fart_lighting" title="Fart lighting">ignited</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mercer2009_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mercer2009-21">[21]</a></sup> Not all humans produce flatus that contains methane. For example, in one study of the <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">faeces</a> of nine adults, only five of the samples contained <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a> capable of producing methane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The prevalence of methane over hydrogen in human farts may correlate with obesity, constipation and irritable bowel syndrome, as archaea that oxidise hydrogen into methane promote the metabolism's ability to absorb fatty acids from food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pimentel2012_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pimentel2012-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>The remaining trace (&lt;1% volume) compounds give flatus its smell. Historically, compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Indole" title="Indole">indole</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skatole" title="Skatole">skatole</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Short_chain_fatty_acids" title="Short chain fatty acids">short chain fatty acids</a> were thought to cause the smell of flatus. More recent evidence proves that the major contribution to the smell of flatus comes from a combination of volatile <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> compounds (VSC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tangerman_2009_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Tangerman_2009-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suarez_1998_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Suarez_1998-24">[24]</a></sup> It is known that <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a> (H<sub>2</sub>S), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methyl_mercaptan" title="Methyl mercaptan">methyl mercaptan</a> (MM, also known as <a href="/wiki/Methanethiol" title="Methanethiol">methanethiol</a>, MT), <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfide" title="Dimethyl sulfide">dimethyl sulfide</a> (DMS), <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_disulfide" title="Dimethyl disulfide">dimethyl disulfide</a> (DMDS) and <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_trisulfide" title="Dimethyl trisulfide">dimethyl trisulfide</a> (DMTS) are present in flatus. The benzopyrrole volatiles <a href="/wiki/Indole" title="Indole">indole</a> and <a href="/wiki/Skatole" title="Skatole">skatole</a> have a mothball smell, and therefore probably do not contribute greatly to the characteristic smell of flatus.
</p><p>In one study, H<sub>2</sub>S concentration was shown to correlate convincingly with perceived bad smell of flatus, followed by MM and DMS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levitt_1998_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Levitt_1998-19">[19]</a></sup> This is supported by the fact that H<sub>2</sub>S may be the most abundant VSC present. These results were generated from subjects who were eating a diet high in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pinto_beans" title="Pinto beans">pinto beans</a> to stimulate flatus production. Others report that MM was the greatest contributor to the smell of flatus in patients not under any specific dietary alterations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tangerman_2009_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-Tangerman_2009-5">[5]</a></sup> It has now been demonstrated that MM, DMS and H<sub>2</sub>S (described as decomposing vegetables, unpleasantly sweet/wild radish and rotten eggs respectively) are all present in human flatus in concentrations above their smell perception thresholds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tangerman_2009_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-Tangerman_2009-5">[5]</a></sup> It is known that increased dietary sulfur-containing amino acids significantly increases the smell of flatus. It is therefore likely that the smell of flatus is created by a combination of VSC, with minimal contribution from non-sulfur volatiles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levitt_1998_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-Levitt_1998-19">[19]</a></sup> Such smell can also be caused by the presence of large numbers of microflora <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> or the presence of faeces in the rectum. Diets high in protein, especially sulfur-containing amino acids, have been demonstrated to significantly increase the smell of flatus.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Volume_and_intestinal_gas_dynamics">Volume and intestinal gas dynamics</span></h3>
<p>Normal flatus volume range is around 476 to 1,491 ml per 24 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tangerman_2009_5-6"><a href="#cite_note-Tangerman_2009-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tomlin_1991_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tomlin_1991-17">[17]</a></sup> This variability between individuals is greatly dependent upon diet. Similarly the number of flatus episodes per day is variable, the normal range is given as 8–20 per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levitt_1998_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-Levitt_1998-19">[19]</a></sup> The volume of flatus associated with each flatulence event again varies (5–375 ml).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tangerman_2009_5-7"><a href="#cite_note-Tangerman_2009-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tomlin_1991_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Tomlin_1991-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suarez_1997_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Suarez_1997-20">[20]</a></sup> The volume of the first flatulence upon waking in the morning is significantly larger than those during the day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tangerman_2009_5-8"><a href="#cite_note-Tangerman_2009-5">[5]</a></sup> This may be due to buildup of intestinal gas in the colon during sleep, the peak in peristaltic activity in the first few hours after waking or the strong prokinetic effect of rectal distension on the rate of transit of intestinal gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Azpiroz_2005_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-Azpiroz_2005-8">[8]</a></sup> It is now known that gas is moved along the gut independently of solids and liquids, and this transit is more efficient in the erect position compared to when supine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Azpiroz_2005_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-Azpiroz_2005-8">[8]</a></sup> It is thought that large volumes of intestinal gas present low resistance, and can be propelled by subtle changes in gut tone, capacitance and proximal contraction and distal relaxation. This process is thought not to affect solid and liquid intra-lumenal contents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Azpiroz_2005_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-Azpiroz_2005-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Researchers investigating the role of sensory nerve endings in the anal canal did not find them to be essential for retaining fluids in the anus, and instead speculate that their role may be to distinguish between flatus and faeces, thereby helping detect a need to defecate or to signal the end of defecation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>The sound varies depending on the tightness of the sphincter <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">muscle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> being propelled, as well as other factors, such as water and body fat. The auditory pitch (sound) of the flatulence outburst can also be affected by the anal <a href="/wiki/Embouchure" title="Embouchure">embouchure</a>. Among humans, flatulence occasionally happens accidentally, such as incidentally to <a href="/wiki/Cough" title="Cough">coughing</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sneezing" title="Sneezing">sneezing</a> or during <a href="/wiki/Orgasm" title="Orgasm">orgasm</a>; on other occasions, flatulence can be voluntarily elicited by tensing the rectum or "bearing down" on stomach or bowel muscles and subsequently relaxing the anal sphincter, resulting in the expulsion of flatus.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Management">Management</span></h2>
<p>Since problems involving intestinal gas present as different (but sometimes combined) complaints, the management is cause related.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pain_and_bloating">Pain and bloating</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Abdominal_distension" title="Abdominal distension">Abdominal distension</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bloating" title="Bloating">Bloating</a></div>
<p>While not affecting the production of the gases themselves, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surfactants" title="Surfactants">surfactants</a> (agents that lower <a href="/wiki/Surface_tension" title="Surface tension">surface tension</a>) can reduce the disagreeable sensations associated with flatulence, by aiding the dissolution of the gases into liquid and solid faecal matter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brecevic_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-brecevic-26">[26]</a></sup> Preparations containing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simethicone" title="Simethicone">simethicone</a> reportedly operate by promoting the coalescence of smaller bubbles into larger ones more easily passed from the body, either by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burp" title="Burp">burping</a> or flatulence. Such preparations do not decrease the total amount of gas generated in or passed from the colon, but make the bubbles larger and thereby allowing them to be passed more easily.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brecevic_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-brecevic-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other drugs including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prokinetics" title="Prokinetics">prokinetics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lubiprostone" title="Lubiprostone">lubiprostone</a>, antibiotics and probiotics are also used to treat bloating in patients with functional bowel disorders such as IBS, and there is some evidence that these measures may reduce symptoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmulson_2011_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schmulson_2011-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>A flexible tube, inserted into the <a href="/wiki/Rectum" title="Rectum">rectum</a>, can be used to collect intestinal gas in a flatus bag. This method is occasionally needed in a hospital setting, when the patient is unable to pass gas normally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Volume">Volume</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Antiflatulent" title="Antiflatulent">Antiflatulent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carminative" title="Carminative">Carminative</a></div>
<p>One method of reducing the volume of flatus produced is dietary modification, reducing the amount of fermentable carbohydrates. This is the theory behind diets such as the low <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FODMAP_diet" title="FODMAP diet">FODMAP diet</a> (low fermentable oligosaccharide, disacharide, monosaccharide and polyols).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Staudacher_2011_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Staudacher_2011-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Certain <a href="/wiki/Spice" title="Spice">spices</a> have been reported to counteract the production of intestinal gas, most notably the closely related <a href="/wiki/Cumin" title="Cumin">cumin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coriander" title="Coriander">coriander</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caraway" title="Caraway">caraway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fennel" title="Fennel">fennel</a> and others such as <a href="/wiki/Ajwain" title="Ajwain">ajwain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turmeric" title="Turmeric">turmeric</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asafoetida" title="Asafoetida">asafoetida</a> (hing), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epazote" title="Epazote">epazote</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kombu" title="Kombu">kombu</a> <a href="/wiki/Kelp" title="Kelp">kelp</a> (a Japanese <a href="/wiki/Seaweed" title="Seaweed">seaweed</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Most starches, including potatoes, corn, noodles, and wheat, produce gas as they are broken down in the large intestine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIH_Gas_in_the_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-NIH_Gas_in_the-12">[12]</a></sup> Intestinal gas can be reduced by fermenting the beans, and making them less gas-inducing, or by cooking them in the liquor from a previous batch. Some <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">legumes</a> also stand up to prolonged cooking, which can help break down the oligosaccharides into simple sugars. On the other hand, fermented bean products such as <a href="/wiki/Miso" title="Miso">miso</a> are less likely to produce as much intestinal gas. Fermentative lactic acid bacteria such as <i><a href="/wiki/Lactobacillus_casei" title="Lactobacillus casei">Lactobacillus casei</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Lactobacillus_plantarum" title="Lactobacillus plantarum">Lactobacillus plantarum</a></i> reduce flatulence in human intestinal tract.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Probiotic" title="Probiotic">Probiotics</a> (<a href="/wiki/Yogurt" title="Yogurt">live yogurt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kefir" title="Kefir">kefir</a>, etc.) are reputed to reduce flatulence when used to restore balance to the normal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intestinal_flora" title="Intestinal flora">intestinal flora</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Live (bioactive) yogurt contains, among other lactic bacteria, <i><a href="/wiki/Lactobacillus_acidophilus" title="Lactobacillus acidophilus">Lactobacillus acidophilus</a></i>, which may be useful in reducing flatulence. <i><a href="/wiki/Lactobacillus_acidophilus" title="Lactobacillus acidophilus">L. acidophilus</a></i> may make the intestinal environment more acidic, supporting a natural balance of the fermentative processes. <i><a href="/wiki/Lactobacillus_acidophilus" title="Lactobacillus acidophilus">L. acidophilus</a></i> is available in supplements. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prebiotics" title="Prebiotics">Prebiotics</a>, which generally are non-digestible oligosaccharides, such as <a href="/wiki/Fructooligosaccharide" title="Fructooligosaccharide">fructooligosaccharide</a>, generally increase flatulence in a similar way as described for lactose intolerance.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Digestive_enzyme" title="Digestive enzyme">Digestive enzyme</a> supplements may significantly reduce the amount of flatulence caused by some components of foods not being digested by the body and thereby promoting the action of microbes in the small and large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intestines" title="Intestines">intestines</a>. It has been suggested that <a href="/wiki/Alpha-galactosidase" title="Alpha-galactosidase">alpha-galactosidase</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enzymes" title="Enzymes">enzymes</a>, which can digest certain complex sugars, are effective in reducing the volume and frequency of flatus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enzymes" title="Enzymes">enzymes</a> <a href="/wiki/Alpha-galactosidase" title="Alpha-galactosidase">alpha-galactosidase</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lactase" title="Lactase">lactase</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amylase" title="Amylase">amylase</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lipase" title="Lipase">lipase</a>, <a href="/wiki/Protease" title="Protease">protease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cellulase" title="Cellulase">cellulase</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amylase#γ-Amylase" title="Amylase">glucoamylase</a>, <a href="/wiki/Invertase" title="Invertase">invertase</a>, malt <a href="/wiki/Diastase" title="Diastase">diastase</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pectinase" title="Pectinase">pectinase</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bromelain" title="Bromelain">bromelain</a> are available, either individually or in combination blends, in commercial products.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Antibiotic" title="Antibiotic">antibiotic</a> <a href="/wiki/Rifaximin" title="Rifaximin">rifaximin</a>, often used to treat <a href="/wiki/Diarrhea" title="Diarrhea">diarrhoea</a> caused by the <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganism</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">E. coli</a></i>, may reduce both the production of intestinal gas and the frequency of flatus events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Smell_2">Smell</span></h3>
<p><b>Bismuth</b>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Odor" title="Odor">Smell</a> from flatulence is commonly treated with <a href="/wiki/Bismuth_subgallate" title="Bismuth subgallate">bismuth subgallate</a>, available over-the-counter in the US as Devrom. Bismuth subgallate is commonly used by individuals who have had <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ostomy" title="Ostomy">ostomy</a> surgery, <a href="/wiki/Bariatric_surgery" title="Bariatric surgery">bariatric surgery</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faecal_incontinence" title="Faecal incontinence">faecal incontinence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irritable_bowel_syndrome" title="Irritable bowel syndrome">irritable bowel syndrome</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bismuth_subsalicylate" title="Bismuth subsalicylate">Bismuth subsalicylate</a> is a compound that binds H<sub>2</sub>S, and one study reported a dose of 524 mg four times a day for 3–7 days bismuth subsalicylate yielded a &gt;95% reduction in faecal H<sub>2</sub>S release in both humans and rats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SuarezFurne1998_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-SuarezFurne1998-36">[36]</a></sup>
Another bismuth compound, bismuth subnitrate was also shown to bind with H<sub>2</sub>S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levitt_2002_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Levitt_2002-37">[37]</a></sup> Another study showed that bismuth acted synergistically with various antibiotics to inhibit sulfate-reducing gut bacteria and sulfide production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ohge_2003_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ohge_2003-38">[38]</a></sup> Some authors proposed a theory that H<sub>2</sub>S was involved in the development of <a href="/wiki/Ulcerative_colitis" title="Ulcerative colitis">ulcerative colitis</a> and that bismuth might be helpful in the management of this condition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Furne_2000_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Furne_2000-39">[39]</a></sup> However, bismuth administration in rats did not prevent them from developing ulcerative colitis despite reduced H<sub>2</sub>S production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Furne_2000_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Furne_2000-39">[39]</a></sup> Also, new evidence suggests that colonic H<sub>2</sub>S is largely present in bound forms, probably sulfides of iron and other metals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tangerman_2009_5-9"><a href="#cite_note-Tangerman_2009-5">[5]</a></sup> Rarely, serious bismuth toxicity may occur with higher doses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gordon_1995_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gordon_1995-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p><b> Activated charcoal</b>
</p><p>Despite being an ancient treatment for various digestive complaints, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Activated_Charcoal" title="Activated Charcoal">activated charcoal</a> did not produce reduction in both the total flatus volume nor the release of sulfur-containing gasses, and there was no reduction in abdominal symptoms (after 0.52 g activated charcoal four times a day for 1 week).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Suarez_1999_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Suarez_1999-41">[41]</a></sup> The authors suggested that saturation of charcoal binding sites during its passage through the gut was the reason for this. A further study concluded that activated charcoal (4 g) does not influence gas formation in vitro or in vivo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Potter_1985_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Potter_1985-42">[42]</a></sup> Other authors reported that activated charcoal was effective. A study in 8 dogs concluded activated charcoal (unknown oral dose) reduced H<sub>2</sub>S by 71%. In combination with yucca schidigera, and zinc acetate, this was increased to an 86% H<sub>2</sub>S reduction, although total flatus volume and number of flatus events was unchanged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Giffard_2001_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Giffard_2001-43">[43]</a></sup> An early study reported activated charcoal (unknown oral dose) prevented a large increase in the number of flatus events and increased breath hydrogen concentrations that normally occur following a gas-producing meal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hall_1981_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hall_1981-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Garments &amp; external devices</b>
</p><p>In 1998, Chester "Buck" Weimer of <a href="/wiki/Pueblo,_Colorado" title="Pueblo, Colorado">Pueblo, Colorado</a>, received a patent for the first <a href="/wiki/Undergarment" title="Undergarment">undergarment</a> that contained a replaceable <a href="/wiki/Activated_carbon" title="Activated carbon">charcoal filter</a>. The undergarments are <a href="/wiki/Hermetic_seal" title="Hermetic seal">air-tight</a> and provide a pocketed <a href="/wiki/Path_of_least_resistance" title="Path of least resistance">escape hole</a> in which a charcoal filter can be inserted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weimer_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-weimer-45">[45]</a></sup> In 2001 Mr Weimer received the <a href="/wiki/Ig_Nobel_Prize" title="Ig Nobel Prize">Ig Nobel Prize</a> for Biology for his invention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>A similar product was released in 2002, but rather than an entire undergarment, consumers are able to purchase an insert similar to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pantiliner" title="Pantiliner">pantiliner</a> that contains activated charcoal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> The inventors, Myra and Brian Conant of <a href="/wiki/Mililani,_Hawaii" title="Mililani, Hawaii">Mililani, Hawaii</a>, still claim on their website to have discovered the undergarment product in 2002 (four years after Chester Weimer filed for a patent for his product), but state that their tests "concluded" that they should release an insert instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Incontinence">Incontinence</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faecal_incontinence" title="Faecal incontinence">Faecal incontinence</a></div>
<p>Flatus incontinence where there is involuntary passage of gas, is a type of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faecal_incontinence" title="Faecal incontinence">faecal incontinence</a>, and is managed similarly.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Society_and_culture">Society and culture</span></h2>
<p>In many <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">cultures</a>, flatulence in public is regarded as embarrassing, but, depending on context, can also be considered humorous. People will often strain to hold in the passing of gas when in polite company, or position themselves to conceal the noise and scent. In other cultures,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:AUDIENCE" title="Wikipedia:AUDIENCE"><span title="Wikipedia:AUDIENCE">example  needed</span></a></i>]</sup> it may be no more embarrassing than <a href="/wiki/Cough" title="Cough">coughing</a>.
</p><p>While the act of passing flatus in said cultures is generally considered to be an unfortunate occurrence in public settings, flatulence may, in casual circumstances and especially among children, be used as either a humorous supplement to a joke ("<a href="/wiki/Pull_my_finger" title="Pull my finger">pull my finger</a>"), or as a comic activity in and of itself. The social acceptability of flatulence-based humour in entertainment and the mass media varies over the course of time and between cultures. Enough entertainers performed with their flatus that the term <a href="/wiki/Flatulist" title="Flatulist">flatulist</a> was coined. The <a href="/wiki/Whoopee_cushion" title="Whoopee cushion">whoopee cushion</a> is a joking device invented in the early 20th century for simulating a fart. In 2008, a farting application for the <a href="/wiki/IPhone" title="IPhone">iPhone</a> earned nearly $10,000 in one day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>A farting game named <i>Touch Wood</i> was documented by <a href="/wiki/John_Gregory_Bourke" title="John Gregory Bourke">John Gregory Bourke</a> in the 1890s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pennlive_essay_on_farting_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-pennlive_essay_on_farting-50">[50]</a></sup> It existed under the name of <i>Safety</i> in the 20th century in the U.S., and has been found being played in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pennlive_essay_on_farting_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-pennlive_essay_on_farting-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>In January 2011, the <a href="/wiki/Malawi" title="Malawi">Malawi</a> Minister of Justice, <a href="/wiki/George_Chaponda" title="George Chaponda">George Chaponda</a>, said that <a href="/wiki/2011_Malawian_Air_Fouling_Legislation" title="2011 Malawian Air Fouling Legislation">Air Fouling Legislation</a> would make public farting illegal in his country. When reporting the story, the media satirised Chaponda's statement with punning headlines. Later, the minister withdrew his statement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MaraviPost_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-MaraviPost-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impact">Environmental impact</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cow_female_black_white.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1680" data-file-width="2190" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Cow_female_black_white.jpg/200px-Cow_female_black_white.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Cow_female_black_white.jpg/300px-Cow_female_black_white.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Cow_female_black_white.jpg/400px-Cow_female_black_white.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cow_female_black_white.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The flatulence of <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cows</a> is only a small portion of cows' methane release. Cows also <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burp" title="Burp">burp</a></i> methane, due to the physiology of their digestive systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Distillations_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Distillations-52">[52]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Flatulence is often blamed as a significant source of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>, owing to the erroneous belief that the methane released by <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a> is in the flatus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> While livestock account for around 20% of global <a href="/wiki/Methane_emissions" title="Methane emissions">methane emissions</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> 90–95% of that is released by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breath" title="Breath">exhaling</a> or <a href="/wiki/Burping" title="Burping">burping</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> In cows, gas and burps are produced by methane-generating microbes called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methanogens" title="Methanogens">methanogens</a>, that live inside the cow's digestive system. Proposals for reducing methane production in cows include the feeding of supplements such as <a href="/wiki/Oregano" title="Oregano">oregano</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seaweed" title="Seaweed">seaweed</a>, and the genetic engineering of gut biome microbes to produce less methane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Distillations_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-Distillations-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> produces large amounts of agricultural products, it is in the unique position of having high methane emissions from livestock compared to other greenhouse gas sources. The New Zealand government is a signatory to the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> and therefore attempts are being made to reduce greenhouse emissions. To achieve this, an <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_emissions_research_levy" title="Agricultural emissions research levy">agricultural emissions research levy</a> was proposed, which promptly became known as a "<a href="/wiki/Agricultural_emissions_research_levy" title="Agricultural emissions research levy">fart tax</a>" or "flatulence tax". It encountered opposition from farmers, farming lobby groups and opposition politicians.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Entertainment">Entertainment</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Flatulist" title="Flatulist">Flatulist</a></div>
<p>Historical comment on the ability to fart at will is observed as early as <a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo" title="Augustine of Hippo">Saint Augustine</a>'s <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_of_God_(book)" title="City of God (book)">The City of God</a></i> (5th century AD). Augustine mentions men who "have such command of their bowels, that they can break wind continuously at will, so as to produce the effect of singing".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Intentional passing of gas and its use as entertainment for others appear to have been somewhat well known in pre-modern Europe, according to mentions of it in medieval and later literature, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rabelais" title="Rabelais">Rabelais</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Le_P%C3%A9tomane" title="Le Pétomane">Le Pétomane</a> ("the Fartomaniac") was a famous French performer in the 19th century who, as well as many <a href="/wiki/Flatulist" title="Flatulist">professional farters</a> before him, did flatulence impressions and held shows. The performer <a href="/wiki/Mr._Methane" title="Mr. Methane">Mr. Methane</a> carries on le Pétomane's tradition today. Also, a 2002 film <i><a href="/wiki/Thunderpants" title="Thunderpants">Thunderpants</a></i> revolves around a boy named Patrick Smash who has  an ongoing flatulence problem from the time of his birth. He eventually overcomes his problems and fulfills his dreams, including one of becoming an <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">astronaut</a>.
</p><p>In literature, farting features prominently in the novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Catcher_in_the_Rye" title="The Catcher in the Rye">The Catcher in the Rye</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Since the 1970s, farting has increasingly been featured in film, especially comedies such as <i><a href="/wiki/Blazing_Saddles" title="Blazing Saddles">Blazing Saddles</a>.</i>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span></h3>
<p>In Islam, flatulence invalidates <i><a href="/wiki/Wudu" title="Wudu">wudu</a></i> (ritual purity).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p>
