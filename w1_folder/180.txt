in modern <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, who speak <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>, especially any of its dialects spoken in historical Flanders, <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flemish_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The sense of "Flemish" identity increased significantly after the <a href="/wiki/Belgian_Revolution" title="Belgian Revolution">Belgian Revolution</a>. Prior to this, the term "Flemings" in the Dutch language was in first place used for the inhabitants of the former County of Flanders. Flemish however had been used since the 14th century to refer to the language and dialects of both the peoples of Flanders and the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Brabant" title="Duchy of Brabant">Duchy of Brabant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup> The modern Belgian province of Limburg was not part of the treaty, and only came to be considered "Flemish" in the 19th century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pieter_Brueghel_de_Jonge_-_De_bruiloft_dans_(1625).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2203" data-file-width="3200" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Pieter_Brueghel_de_Jonge_-_De_bruiloft_dans_%281625%29.jpg/220px-Pieter_Brueghel_de_Jonge_-_De_bruiloft_dans_%281625%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Pieter_Brueghel_de_Jonge_-_De_bruiloft_dans_%281625%29.jpg/330px-Pieter_Brueghel_de_Jonge_-_De_bruiloft_dans_%281625%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Pieter_Brueghel_de_Jonge_-_De_bruiloft_dans_%281625%29.jpg/440px-Pieter_Brueghel_de_Jonge_-_De_bruiloft_dans_%281625%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pieter_Brueghel_de_Jonge_-_De_bruiloft_dans_(1625).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>The Wedding Dance</i> by <a href="/wiki/Pieter_Brueghel_the_Younger" title="Pieter Brueghel the Younger">Pieter Brueghel the Younger</a>, 1625</div></div></div>
<p>In 1830 the southern provinces of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_the_Netherlands" title="United Kingdom of the Netherlands">United Netherlands</a> proclaimed their independence. French-dialect speaking population, as well as the administration and elites, feared the loss of their status and autonomy under Dutch rule while the rapid industrialization in the south highlighted economic differences between the two. Under <a href="/wiki/French_period" title="French period">French rule</a> (1794–1815), French was enforced as the only official language in public life, resulting in a Frenchification of the elites and, to a lesser extent, the middle classes. The Dutch King allowed the use of both Dutch and French dialects as administrative languages in the Flemish provinces. He also enacted laws to reestablish Dutch in schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup> The language policy was not the only cause of the secession; the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">Roman Catholic</a> majority viewed the sovereign, the Protestant <a href="/wiki/William_I_of_the_Netherlands" title="William I of the Netherlands">William I</a>, with suspicion and were heavily stirred by the Roman Catholic Church which suspected William of wanting to enforce <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a>. Lastly, Belgian liberals were dissatisfied with William for his allegedly despotic behaviour.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Following the revolt, the language reforms of 1823 were the first Dutch laws to be abolished and the subsequent years would see a number of laws restricting the use of the Dutch language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> This policy led to the gradual emergence of the <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Movement" title="Flemish Movement">Flemish Movement</a>, that was built on earlier <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-French_sentiment" title="Anti-French sentiment">anti-French</a> feelings of injustice, as expressed in writings (for example by the late 18th-century writer, <a href="/wiki/Jan-Baptist_Verlooy" title="Jan-Baptist Verlooy">Jan Verlooy</a>) which criticized the Southern <a href="/wiki/Francophile" title="Francophile">Francophile</a> elites. The efforts of this movement during the following 150 years, have to no small extent facilitated the creation of the <i><a href="/wiki/De_jure" title="De jure">de jure</a></i> social, political and linguistic equality of Dutch from the end of the 19th century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>After the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hundred_Years_War" title="Hundred Years War">Hundred Years War</a> many Flemings migrated to the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>. By 1490 there were 2,000 Flemings living in the Azores. <a href="/wiki/Willem_van_der_Haegen" title="Willem van der Haegen">Willem van der Haegen</a> was the original sea captain who brought settlers from Flanders to the Azores. Today many Azoreans trace their genealogy from present day Flanders. Many of their customs and traditions are distinctively Flemish in nature such as Windmills used for grain, <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Jorge_cheese" title="São Jorge cheese">São Jorge cheese</a> and several religious events such as the imperios and the feast of the <a href="/wiki/Cult_of_the_Holy_Spirit" title="Cult of the Holy Spirit">Cult of the Holy Spirit</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Identity_and_culture">Identity and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flemish_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Identity and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Quad_Flandria.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2498" data-file-width="3345" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Quad_Flandria.jpg/220px-Quad_Flandria.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Quad_Flandria.jpg/330px-Quad_Flandria.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Quad_Flandria.jpg/440px-Quad_Flandria.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Quad_Flandria.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">medieval</a> <a href="/wiki/County_of_Flanders" title="County of Flanders">County of Flanders</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Within Belgium, Flemings form a clearly distinguishable group set apart by their language and customs. However, when compared to the Netherlands most of these cultural and linguistic differences quickly fade, as the Flemish share the same language, similar or identical customs and (though chiefly with the southern part of today's Netherlands) traditional religion with the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_people" title="Dutch people">Dutch</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> However, the popular perception of being a single polity varies greatly, depending on subject matter, locality and personal background. Generally, Flemings will seldom identify themselves as being Dutch and vice versa, especially on a national level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>This is partly caused by the popular stereotypes in the Netherlands as well as Flanders which are mostly based on the 'cultural extremes' of both Northern and Southern culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> But also in great part because of the history of emancipation of their culture in Belgium, which has left many Flemings with a high degree of national consciousness, which can be very marked among some Dutch-speaking Belgians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup> Alongside this overarching political and social affiliation, there also exists a strong tendency towards <a href="/wiki/Regionalism_(politics)" title="Regionalism (politics)">regionalism</a>, in which individuals greatly identify themselves culturally through their native <a href="/wiki/Province" title="Province">province</a>, city, <a href="/wiki/Region" title="Region">region</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dutch_dialects" title="Dutch dialects">dialect</a> they speak.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flemish_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Flemings speak <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> (specifically its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flemish_language" title="Flemish language">southern variant</a>, which is sometimes colloquially called '<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flemish_language" title="Flemish language">Flemish</a>'). It is the <a href="/wiki/National_language" title="National language">majority language</a> in Belgium, being spoken natively by three-fifths of the population. Its various dialects contain a number of lexical and a few grammatical features which distinguish them from the standard language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> As in the Netherlands, the pronunciation of Standard Dutch is affected by the native dialect of the speaker. At the same time <a href="/wiki/East_Flemish" title="East Flemish">East Flemish</a> forms a continuum with both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brabantian" title="Brabantian">Brabantic</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Flemish" title="West Flemish">West Flemish</a>. Standard Dutch is primarily based on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hollandic" title="Hollandic">Hollandic</a> dialect (spoken in the northwestern Netherlands) and to a lesser extent on Brabantic, which is the most dominant Dutch dialect of the Southern Netherlands and <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flemish_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_Belgium" title="Religion in Belgium">Religion in Belgium</a></div>
<p>Approximately 75% of the Flemish people are by baptism assumed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a>, though a still diminishing minority of less than 8% attends <a href="/wiki/Mass_(liturgy)" title="Mass (liturgy)">Mass</a> on a regular basis and nearly half of the inhabitants of <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agnostic" title="Agnostic">agnostic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atheist" title="Atheist">atheist</a>. A 2006 inquiry in Flanders, showed 55% chose to call themselves religious, 36% believe that God created the universe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_symbols">National symbols</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flemish_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: National symbols">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Flag_of_Flanders.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="627" data-file-width="941" height="100" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Flag_of_Flanders.svg/150px-Flag_of_Flanders.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Flag_of_Flanders.svg/225px-Flag_of_Flanders.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Flag_of_Flanders.svg/300px-Flag_of_Flanders.svg.png 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Flag_of_Flanders.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Flanders" title="Flag of Flanders">Flag of Flanders</a></div></div></div>
<p>The official flag and coat of arms of the <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Community" title="Flemish Community">Flemish Community</a> represents a black lion with red claws and tongue on a yellow field (<i><a href="/wiki/Or_(heraldry)" title="Or (heraldry)">or</a> a lion rampant <a href="/wiki/Sable_(heraldry)" title="Sable (heraldry)">sable</a> armed and langued <a href="/wiki/Gules" title="Gules">gules</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> A flag with a completely black lion had been in wide use before 1991 when the current version was officially adopted by the Flemish Community. That older flag was at times recognized by government sources (alongside the version with red claws and tongue).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> Today, only the flag bearing a lion with red claws and tongue is recognized by Belgian law, while the flag with the all black lion is mostly used by Flemish separatist movements. The Flemish authorities also use two logos of a highly stylized black lion which show the claws and tongue in either red or black.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> The first documented use<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> of the Flemish lion was on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seal_(device)" title="Seal (device)">seal</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philip,_count_of_Flanders" title="Philip, count of Flanders">Philip d'Alsace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Count_of_Flanders" title="Count of Flanders">count of Flanders</a> of 1162. As of that date the use of the Flemish coat of arms (<i>or a lion rampant sable</i>) remained in use throughout the reigns of the d'Alsace, Flanders (2nd) and Dampierre <a href="/wiki/Count_of_Flanders" title="Count of Flanders">dynasties of counts</a>. The motto "Vlaanderen de Leeuw" (Flanders the lion) was allegedly present on the arms of <a href="/wiki/Pieter_de_Coninck" title="Pieter de Coninck">Pieter de Coninck</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Golden_Spurs" title="Battle of the Golden Spurs">Battle of the Golden Spurs</a> on July 11, 1302.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup> After the acquisition of Flanders by the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Burgundy" title="Duke of Burgundy">Burgundian dukes</a> the lion was only used in escutcheons. It was only after the creation of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_the_Netherlands" title="United Kingdom of the Netherlands">United Kingdom of the Netherlands</a> that the coat of arms (surmounted by a chief bearing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coat_of_Arms_of_the_Netherlands" title="Coat of Arms of the Netherlands">Royal Arms of the Netherlands</a>) once again became the official symbol of the new province <a href="/wiki/East_Flanders" title="East Flanders">East Flanders</a>.
</p>
