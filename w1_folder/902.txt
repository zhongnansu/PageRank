<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Absorption (chemistry)</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">Adsorption</a>.</div><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Absorption_(disambiguation)" title="Absorption (disambiguation)">Absorption (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Absorber.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1052" data-file-width="744" height="353" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Absorber.svg/250px-Absorber.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Absorber.svg/375px-Absorber.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Absorber.svg/500px-Absorber.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Absorber.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Laboratory absorber. <b>1a)</b>: CO<sub>2</sub> inlet; <b>1b)</b>: H<sub>2</sub>O inlet; <b>2)</b>: outlet <b>3)</b>: absorption column; <b>4)</b>: packing.</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, <b>absorption</b> is a physical or chemical <a href="/wiki/Phenomenon" title="Phenomenon">phenomenon</a> or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Process_(science)" title="Process (science)">process</a> in which <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecules" title="Molecules">molecules</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> enter some bulk phase – <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a> or <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solid</a> material. This is a different process from <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a>, since molecules undergoing absorption are taken up by the volume, not by the surface (as in the case for adsorption). A more general term is <i><a href="/wiki/Sorption" title="Sorption">sorption</a></i>, which covers absorption, <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ion_exchange" title="Ion exchange">ion exchange</a>. Absorption is a condition in which something takes in another substance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Organic-chemistry_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Organic-chemistry-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In many processes important in technology, the chemical absorption is used in place of the physical process, e.g., absorption of carbon dioxide by sodium hydroxide – such acid-base processes do not follow the Nernst partition law.
</p><p>For some examples of this effect, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid-liquid_extraction" title="Liquid-liquid extraction">liquid-liquid extraction</a>. It is possible to extract from one <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a> phase to another a <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solute</a> without a chemical reaction. Examples of such solutes are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noble_gases" title="Noble gases">noble gases</a> and <a href="/wiki/Osmium_tetroxide" title="Osmium tetroxide">osmium tetroxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Organic-chemistry_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Organic-chemistry-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The process of absorption means that a substance captures and transforms energy. The absorbent distributes the material it captures throughout whole and adsorbent only distributes it through the surface.
</p><p>The process of gas or liquid which penetrate into the body of adsorbent is commonly known as absorption.
</p>

<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style=""><b>Absorption: The process of one material (absorbate) being retained by another (absorbent); this may be the physical solution of a gas, liquid, or solid in a liquid, attachment of molecules of a gas, vapour, liquid, or dissolved substance to a solid surface by physical forces, etc. In spectrophotometry, absorption of light at characteristic wavelengths or bands of wavelengths is used to identify the chemical nature of molecules, atoms or ions and to measure the concentrations of these species.</b></div>

This equation is valid if concentrations are not too large and if the species "x" does not change its form in any of the two phases "1" or "2". If such molecule undergoes association or <a href="/wiki/Dissociation_(chemistry)" title="Dissociation (chemistry)">dissociation</a> then this equation still describes the equilibrium between "x" in both phases, but only for the same form – concentrations of all remaining forms must be calculated by taking into account all the other equilibria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Organic-chemistry_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Organic-chemistry-1">[1]</a></sup>

In alternative fashion, one may use <a href="/wiki/Partial_pressure" title="Partial pressure">partial pressures</a> instead of concentrations.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_absorption">Types of absorption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Absorption_(chemistry)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types of absorption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Absorption is a process that may be chemical (reactive) or physical (non-reactive). 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_absorption">Chemical absorption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Absorption_(chemistry)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical absorption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Chemical absorption or reactive absorption is a chemical reaction between the absorbed and the absorbing substances. Sometimes it combines with physical absorption. This type of absorption depends upon the <a href="/wiki/Stoichiometry" title="Stoichiometry">stoichiometry</a> of the reaction and the concentration of its reactants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_absorption">Physical absorption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Absorption_(chemistry)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Physical absorption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_in_a_solid">Water in a solid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Absorption_(chemistry)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Water in a solid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hydrophile" title="Hydrophile">Hydrophilic</a> solids, which includes many solids of biological origin, can readily absorb water.  <a href="/wiki/Chemical_polarity" title="Chemical polarity">Polar</a> interactions between water and the molecules of the solid favour partition of the water into the solid, which can allow significant absorption of water vapour even in relatively low humidity.  
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Moisture_regain">Moisture regain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Absorption_(chemistry)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Moisture regain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>A plant fibre (or other hydrophilic material) that has been exposed to the atmosphere will usually contain some water even if it feels dry.  The water can be driven off by heating in an oven, leading to a measurable decrease in weight, which will gradually be regained if the fibre is returned to a 'normal' atmosphere.  This effect is important in the textile industry, where the proportion of a material's weight made up by water is called the <i>moisture regain</i>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
