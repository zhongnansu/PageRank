<p><b>Ecological modernization</b> is a school of thought in the social sciences that argues that the <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economy</a> benefits from moves towards <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalism</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It has gained increasing attention among scholars and policymakers in the last several decades internationally. It is an analytical approach as well as a policy strategy and <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmental</a> <a href="/wiki/Discourse" title="Discourse">discourse</a> (Hajer, 1995).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_key_elements">Origins and key elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_modernization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins and key elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ecological modernization emerged in the early 1980s within a group of scholars at Free University and the Social Science Research Centre in Berlin, among them <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Huber_(economist)" title="Joseph Huber (economist)">Joseph Huber</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Martin_J%C3%A4nicke&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Martin Jänicke (page does not exist)">Martin Jänicke</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_J%C3%A4nicke" title="de:Martin Jänicke">de</a>)</span> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Udo_E._Simonis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Udo E. Simonis (page does not exist)">Udo E. Simonis</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Udo_E._Simonis" title="de:Udo E. Simonis">de</a>)</span>. Various authors pursued similar ideas at the time, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Arthur_H._Rosenfeld" title="Arthur H. Rosenfeld">Arthur H. Rosenfeld</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amory_Lovins" title="Amory Lovins">Amory Lovins</a>, Donald Huisingh, René Kemp, or <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Ulrich_von_Weizs%C3%A4cker" title="Ernst Ulrich von Weizsäcker">Ernst Ulrich von Weizsäcker</a>. Further substantial contributions were made by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_P.J._Mol&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Arthur P.J. Mol (page does not exist)">Arthur P.J. Mol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gert_Spaargaren" title="Gert Spaargaren">Gert Spaargaren</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_A_Sonnenfeld" title="David A Sonnenfeld">David A Sonnenfeld</a> (Mol and Sonnenfeld, 2000; Mol, 2001).
</p><p>One basic assumption of ecological modernization relates to environmental readaptation of economic growth and industrial development. On the basis of <a href="/wiki/Enlightened_self-interest" title="Enlightened self-interest">enlightened self-interest</a>, economy and ecology can be favourably combined: Environmental <a href="/wiki/Productivity" title="Productivity">productivity</a>, i.e. productive use of natural resources and environmental media (air, water, soil, ecosystems), can be a source of future growth and development in the same way as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labour_productivity" title="Labour productivity">labour productivity</a> and capital productivity. This includes increases in energy and resource efficiency as well as product and process innovations such as environmental management and sustainable <a href="/wiki/Supply_chain_management" title="Supply chain management">supply chain management</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clean_technology" title="Clean technology">clean technologies</a>, benign substitution of hazardous substances, and product design for environment. Radical innovations in these fields can not only reduce quantities of resource turnover and emissions, but also change the quality or structure of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_metabolism" title="Industrial metabolism">industrial metabolism</a>. In the co-evolution of humans and nature, and in order to upgrade the environment’s <a href="/wiki/Carrying_capacity" title="Carrying capacity">carrying capacity</a>, ecological modernization gives humans an active role to play, which may entail conflicts with nature conservation.
</p><p>There are different understandings of the scope of ecological modernization - whether it is just about techno-industrial progress and related aspects of policy and economy, and to what extent it also includes cultural aspects (ecological modernization of mind, value orientiations, attitudes, behaviour and lifestyles). Similarly, there is some pluralism as to whether ecological modernization would need to rely mainly on government, or markets and entrepreneurship, or civil society, or some sort of <a href="/wiki/Multi-level_governance" title="Multi-level governance">multi-level governance</a> combining the three. Some scholars explicitly refer to general <a href="/wiki/Modernization_theory" title="Modernization theory">modernization theory</a> as well as non-Marxist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World-systems_approach" title="World-systems approach">world-system theory</a>, others don’t.
</p><p>Ultimately, however, there is a common understanding that ecological modernization will have to result in innovative structural change. So research is now still more focused on environmental innovations, or eco-innovations, and the interplay of various societal factors (scientific, economic, institutional, legal, political, cultural) which foster or hamper such innovations (Klemmer et al., 1999; Huber, 2004; Weber and Hemmelskamp, 2005; Olsthoorn and Wieczorek, 2006).
</p><p>Ecological modernization shares a number of features with neighbouring, overlapping approaches. Among the most important are
</p>
<ul><li>the concept of <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a></li>
<li>the approach of <a href="/wiki/Industrial_metabolism" title="Industrial metabolism">industrial metabolism</a> (<a href="/wiki/Robert_Ayres_(scientist)" title="Robert Ayres (scientist)">Ayres</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Udo_Simonis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Udo Simonis (page does not exist)">Simonis</a>, 1994)</li>
<li>the concept of <a href="/wiki/Industrial_ecology" title="Industrial ecology">industrial ecology</a> (<a href="/wiki/Robert_H._Socolow" title="Robert H. Socolow">Socolow</a>, 1994)</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Additional_elements">Additional elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_modernization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Additional elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A special topic of ecological modernization research during recent years was <i>sustainable household</i>, i.e. environment-oriented reshaping of lifestyles, consumption patterns, and demand-pull control of supply chains (Vergragt, 2000; OECD 2002). 
Some scholars of ecological modernization share an interest in <a href="/wiki/Industrial_symbiosis" title="Industrial symbiosis">industrial symbiosis</a>, i.e. inter-site recycling that helps to reduce the consumption of resources via increasing efficiency (i.e. pollution prevention, waste reduction), typically by taking externalities from one economic production process and using them as raw material inputs for another (Christoff, 1996). 
Ecological modernization also relies on product life-cycle assessment and the analysis of materials and energy flows. In this context, ecological modernization promotes 'cradle to cradle' manufacturing (<a href="/wiki/Michael_Braungart" title="Michael Braungart">Braungart</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_McDonough" title="William McDonough">McDonough</a>, 2002), contrasted against the usual 'cradle to grave' forms of manufacturing - where waste is not re-integrated back into the production process. 
Another special interest in the ecological modernization literature has been the role of <a href="/wiki/Social_movement" title="Social movement">social movements</a> and the emergence of civil society as a key agent of change (<a href="/wiki/Dana_R._Fisher" title="Dana R. Fisher">Fisher</a> and Freudenburg, 2001).
</p><p>As a <a href="/wiki/Strategy" title="Strategy">strategy</a> of change, some forms of ecological modernization may be favored by <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">business</a> interests because they seemingly meet the triple bottom line of economics, society, and environment, which, it is held, underpin sustainability, yet do not challenge <a href="/wiki/Free_market" title="Free market">free market</a> principles. This contrasts with many <a href="/wiki/Environmental_movement" title="Environmental movement">environmental movement</a> perspectives, which regard <a href="/wiki/Free_trade" title="Free trade">free trade</a> and its notion of business self-regulation as part of the problem, or even an origin of <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">environmental degradation</a>. Under ecological modernization, the <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a> is seen in a variety of roles and capacities: as the enabler for <a href="/wiki/Market_(economics)" title="Market (economics)">markets</a> that help produce the technological advances via competition; as the regulatory (see <a href="/wiki/Regulation" title="Regulation">regulation</a>) medium through which <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">corporations</a> are forced to 'take back' their various wastes and re-integrate them in some manner into the production of new goods and services (e.g. the way that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">car</a> corporations in Germany are required to accept back cars they manufactured once those vehicles have reached the end of their product lifespan); and in some cases as an institution that is incapable of addressing critical local, national, and global environmental problems. In the latter case, ecological modernization shares with <a href="/wiki/Ulrich_Beck" title="Ulrich Beck">Ulrich Beck</a> (1999, 37-40) and others notions of the necessity of emergence of new forms of <a href="/wiki/Environmental_governance" title="Environmental governance">environmental governance</a>, sometimes referred to as subpolitics or political modernization, where the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_movement" title="Environmental movement">environmental movement</a>, community groups, businesses, and other stakeholders increasingly take on direct and leadership roles in stimulating environmental transformation. Political modernization of this sort requires certain supporting norms and institutions such as a free, independent, or at least critical press, basic <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a> of expression, organization, and assembly, etc.  <a href="/wiki/New_media" title="New media">New media</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> greatly facilitate this.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_modernization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Critics argue that ecological modernization will fail to protect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" title="Environment (biophysical)">the environment</a> and does nothing to alter the  impulses within the capitalist economic mode of production (see <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a>) that inevitably lead to environmental degradation (Foster, 2002). As such, it is just a form of '<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green-washing" title="Green-washing">green-washing</a>'. Critics question whether <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technological</a> advances alone can achieve resource conservation and better <a href="/wiki/Environmental_protection" title="Environmental protection">environmental protection</a>, particularly if left to business <a href="/wiki/Self-regulatory_organization" title="Self-regulatory organization">self-regulation</a> practices (York and Rosa, 2003). For instance, many technological improvements are currently feasible but not widely utilized. The most <a href="/wiki/Environmentally_friendly" title="Environmentally friendly">environmentally friendly</a> product or manufacturing process (which is often also the most economically efficient) is not always the one automatically chosen by self-regulating corporations (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> or <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuel</a> vs. <a href="/wiki/Peak_oil" title="Peak oil">peak oil</a>). In addition, some critics have argued that ecological modernization does not redress gross <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Injustices" title="Injustices">injustices</a> that are produced within the capitalist system, such as <a href="/wiki/Environmental_racism" title="Environmental racism">environmental racism</a> - where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People_of_color" title="People of color">people of color</a> and low income earners bear a disproportionate burden of environmental harm such as pollution, and lack access to environmental benefits such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parks" title="Parks">parks</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">social justice</a> issues such as eliminating <a href="/wiki/Unemployment" title="Unemployment">unemployment</a> (Bullard, 1993; Gleeson and Low, 1999; Harvey, 1996) - <a href="/wiki/Environmental_racism" title="Environmental racism">environmental racism</a> is also referred to as issues of the asymmetric distribution of environmental resources and services (Everett &amp; Neu, 2000). Moreover, the theory seems to have limited global efficacy, applying primarily to its countries of origin - <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, and having little to say about the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_world" title="Developing world">developing world</a> (Fisher and Freudenburg, 2001). Perhaps the harshest criticism though, is that ecological modernization is predicated upon the notion of 'sustainable growth', and in reality this is not possible because growth entails the consumption of natural and human capital at great costs to <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a> and societies.
</p><p>Ecological modernization, its effectiveness and applicability, strengths and limitations, remains a dynamic and contentious area of environmental social science research and policy discourse in the early 21st century.
</p>
