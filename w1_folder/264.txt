<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Calcination</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Calcification" title="Calcification">Calcification</a>.</div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAC" title="IUPAC">IUPAC</a> defines <b>calcination</b> as "heating to high temperatures in air or oxygen".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> However, calcination is also used to mean a <a href="/wiki/Thermal_treatment" title="Thermal treatment">thermal treatment</a> process in the absence or limited supply of air or oxygen applied to <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ores</a> and other solid materials to bring about a <a href="/wiki/Thermal_decomposition" title="Thermal decomposition">thermal decomposition</a>. A calciner is a steel cylinder that rotates inside a heated furnace and performs indirect high-temperature processing (550–1150 °C, or 1000–2100 °F) within a controlled atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_processes">Industrial processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Industrial processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LDLimeShaftKilnBasic.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1323" data-file-width="755" height="386" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/LDLimeShaftKilnBasic.jpg/220px-LDLimeShaftKilnBasic.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/LDLimeShaftKilnBasic.jpg/330px-LDLimeShaftKilnBasic.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/LDLimeShaftKilnBasic.jpg/440px-LDLimeShaftKilnBasic.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LDLimeShaftKilnBasic.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An oven for calcination of limestone</div></div></div>
<p>The process of calcination derives its name from the Latin <i>calcinare</i> (to burn lime)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> due to its most common application, the decomposition of calcium carbonate (<a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>) to <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">calcium oxide</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lime_(mineral)" title="Lime (mineral)">lime</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, in order to create <a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">cement</a>.  The product of calcination is usually referred to in general as "calcine", regardless of the actual minerals undergoing thermal treatment. Calcination is carried out in <a href="/wiki/Furnace" title="Furnace">furnaces</a> or reactors (sometimes referred to as <a href="/wiki/Kiln" title="Kiln">kilns</a> or calciners) of various designs including <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Shaft_furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Shaft furnace (page does not exist)">shaft furnaces</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rotary_kiln" title="Rotary kiln">rotary kilns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Multiple_hearth_furnace" title="Multiple hearth furnace">multiple hearth furnaces</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fluidized_bed_reactor" title="Fluidized bed reactor">fluidized bed reactors</a>.
</p><p>Examples of calcination processes include the following:
</p>
<ul><li>decomposition of carbonate ores, as in the calcination of <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> to drive off <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>;</li>
<li>decomposition of hydrated minerals, as in the calcination of <a href="/wiki/Bauxite" title="Bauxite">bauxite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a>, to remove crystalline <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> as water vapor;</li>
<li>decomposition of volatile matter contained in raw <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_coke" title="Petroleum coke">petroleum coke</a>;</li>
<li>heat treatment to effect phase transformations, as in conversion of <a href="/wiki/Anatase" title="Anatase">anatase</a> to <a href="/wiki/Rutile" title="Rutile">rutile</a> or <a href="/wiki/Devitrification" title="Devitrification">devitrification</a> of <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> materials</li>
<li>removal of <a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">ammonium</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ions" title="Ions">ions</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_synthesis" title="Chemical synthesis">synthesis</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zeolites" title="Zeolites">zeolites</a>.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several reports on Microwave Assisted Calcination methods were made in 2006 and 2011 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Calcination reactions usually take place at or above the thermal decomposition temperature (for decomposition and volatilization reactions) or the transition temperature (for phase transitions).  This temperature is usually defined as the temperature at which the standard <a href="/wiki/Gibbs_free_energy" title="Gibbs free energy">Gibbs free energy</a> for a particular calcination reaction is equal to zero.  For example, in limestone calcination, a decomposition process, the chemical reaction is 
</p>
<dl><dd>CaCO<sub>3</sub> → CaO + CO<sub>2</sub>(g)</dd></dl>
<p>The standard Gibbs free energy of reaction is approximated as <i>ΔG°<sub>r</sub></i> = 177,100 − 158 <i>T</i> (J/mol).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  The standard free energy of reaction is 0 in this case when the temperature, <i>T</i>, is equal to 1121 K, or 848 °C.
</p><p>See also <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate#Calcination_equilibrium" title="Calcium carbonate">calcination equilibrium of calcium carbonate</a>
</p><p>Calcination can be used in carbon negative electricity generation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxidation">Oxidation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Oxidation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In some cases, calcination of a metal results in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidation</a> of the metal. <a href="/wiki/Jean_Rey_(physician)" title="Jean Rey (physician)">Jean Rey</a> noted that <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> when calcinated gained weight, presumably as they were being oxidized.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alchemy">Alchemy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Alchemy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">alchemy</a>, calcination was believed to be one of the <a href="/wiki/Magnum_opus_(alchemy)#Expansion_on_the_four_stages" title="Magnum opus (alchemy)">12 vital processes</a> required for the transformation of a substance.
</p><p>Alchemists distinguished two kinds of calcination, <i>actual</i> and <i>potential</i>. Actual calcination is that brought about by actual fire, from wood, coals, or other fuel, raised to a certain temperature. Potential calcination is that brought about by <i>potential</i> fire, such as corrosive chemicals; for example, gold was calcined in a <a href="/wiki/Reverberatory_furnace" title="Reverberatory furnace">reverberatory furnace</a> with <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sal_ammoniac" title="Sal ammoniac">sal ammoniac</a>; silver with common salt and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkali_salts" title="Alkali salts">alkali salt</a>; <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> with salt and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>; <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> with sal ammoniac and <a href="/wiki/Vinegar" title="Vinegar">vinegar</a>; tin with <a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">antimony</a>; lead with sulfur; and mercury with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aqua_fortis" title="Aqua fortis">aqua fortis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cyclo_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-cyclo-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>There was also <i>philosophical calcination</i>, which was said to occur when horns, hooves, etc., were hung over boiling water, or other liquor, until they had lost their <a href="/wiki/Mucilage" title="Mucilage">mucilage</a>, and were easily reducible into powder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cyclo_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-cyclo-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
