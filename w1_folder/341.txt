<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Locating_the_oil_field">Locating the oil field</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extraction_of_petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Locating the oil field">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Geologists and geophysicists use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seismic" title="Seismic">seismic</a> surveys to search for geological structures that may form oil reservoirs. The "classic" method includes making an underground explosion nearby and observing the seismic response, which provides information about the geological structures underground. However, "passive" methods that extract information from naturally occurring seismic waves are also used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other instruments such as <a href="/wiki/Gravimeter" title="Gravimeter">gravimeters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">magnetometers</a> are also used in the search for petroleum. Extracting crude oil normally starts with drilling wells into an underground reservoir. When an oil well has been tapped, a geologist (known on the rig as the "mudlogger") will note its presence.
</p><p>Historically in the United States, in some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_field" title="Oil field">oil fields</a> the oil rose naturally to the surface, but most of these fields have long since been used up, except in parts of Alaska. Often many wells (called <i>multilateral wells</i>) are drilled into the same reservoir, to an economically viable extraction rate. Some wells (<i>secondary wells</i>) may pump <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a> or various gas mixtures into the reservoir to raise or maintain the reservoir pressure and economical extraction rate.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Drilling">Drilling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extraction_of_petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Drilling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Oil_well" title="Oil well">Oil well</a></div>
<p>The oil well is created by drilling a long hole into the earth with an <a href="/wiki/Drilling_rig" title="Drilling rig">oil rig</a>. A steel pipe (casing) is placed in the hole, to provide <a href="/wiki/Structural_integrity_and_failure" title="Structural integrity and failure">structural integrity</a> to the newly drilled well bore. Holes are then made in the base of the well to enable oil to pass into the bore. Finally a collection of valves called a "<a href="/wiki/Christmas_tree_(oil_well)" title="Christmas tree (oil well)">Christmas Tree</a>" is fitted to the top; the valves regulate pressures and control flow.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oil_extraction_and_recovery">Oil extraction and recovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extraction_of_petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Oil extraction and recovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_recovery">Primary recovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extraction_of_petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Primary recovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the <i>primary recovery stage</i> (also called the 'Kareem Stage'), reservoir drive comes from a number of natural mechanisms. These include: natural water displacing oil downward into the well, expansion of the natural gas at the top of the reservoir, expansion of gas initially dissolved in the crude oil, and gravity drainage resulting from the movement of oil within the reservoir from the upper to the lower parts where the wells are located. Recovery factor during the primary recovery stage is typically 5-15%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EUR_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-EUR-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>While the underground pressure in the oil reservoir is sufficient to force the oil to the surface, all that is necessary is to place a complex arrangement of <a href="/wiki/Valve" title="Valve">valves</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Christmas_tree_(oil_well)" title="Christmas tree (oil well)">Christmas tree</a>) on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Well_head" title="Well head">well head</a> to connect the well to a <a href="/wiki/Pipeline_transport" title="Pipeline transport">pipeline</a> network for storage and processing. Sometimes pumps, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nodding_donkey" title="Nodding donkey">beam pumps</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_submersible_pump" title="Electrical submersible pump">electrical submersible pumps</a> (ESPs), are used to bring the oil to the surface; these are known as artificial lifting mechanisms.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Secondary_recovery">Secondary recovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extraction_of_petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Secondary recovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Over the lifetime of a well, the pressure falls. At some point there is insufficient underground pressure to force the oil to the surface. After natural reservoir drive diminishes, <i>secondary recovery</i> methods are applied. These rely on supplying external energy to the reservoir by injecting fluids to increase reservoir pressure, hence increasing or replacing the natural reservoir drive with an artificial drive.  Secondary recovery techniques increase the reservoir's pressure by <a href="/wiki/Water_injection_(oil_production)" title="Water injection (oil production)">water injection</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gas_reinjection" title="Gas reinjection">natural gas reinjection</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gas_lift" title="Gas lift">gas lift</a>, which injects <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">air</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> or some other gas into the bottom of an active well, reducing the overall density of fluid in the wellbore. The typical recovery factor from water-flood operations is about 30%, depending on the properties of the oil and the characteristics of the reservoir rock. On average, the recovery factor after primary and secondary oil recovery operations is between 35 and 45%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EUR_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-EUR-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enhanced_recovery">Enhanced recovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extraction_of_petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Enhanced recovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_extraction_of_Oil_using_steam.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="704" data-file-width="829" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/The_extraction_of_Oil_using_steam.jpg/220px-The_extraction_of_Oil_using_steam.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/The_extraction_of_Oil_using_steam.jpg/330px-The_extraction_of_Oil_using_steam.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/The_extraction_of_Oil_using_steam.jpg/440px-The_extraction_of_Oil_using_steam.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_extraction_of_Oil_using_steam.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Steam is injected into many oil fields where the oil is thicker and heavier than normal crude oil</div></div></div>
<p>Enhanced, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tertiary_oil_recovery" title="Tertiary oil recovery">Tertiary oil recovery</a> methods, increase the mobility of the oil in order to increase extraction.
</p><p>Thermally enhanced oil recovery methods (TEOR) are tertiary recovery techniques that heat the oil, reducing its viscosity and making it easier to extract. <a href="/wiki/Steam_injection_(oil_industry)" title="Steam injection (oil industry)">Steam injection</a> is the most common form of TEOR, and it is often done with a <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">cogeneration</a> plant. This type of cogeneration plant uses a <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbine</a> to generate <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Waste_heat" title="Waste heat">waste heat</a> is used to produce steam, which is then injected into the reservoir. This form of recovery is used extensively to increase oil extraction in the <a href="/wiki/San_Joaquin_Valley" title="San Joaquin Valley">San Joaquin Valley</a>, which yields a very heavy oil, yet accounts for ten percent of the United States' oil extraction.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Fire flooding (In-situ burning) is another form of TEOR, but instead of steam, some of the oil is burned to heat the surrounding oil.
</p><p>Occasionally, <a href="/wiki/Surfactant" title="Surfactant">surfactants</a> (<a href="/wiki/Detergent" title="Detergent">detergents</a>) are injected to alter the surface tension between the water and the oil in the reservoir, mobilizing oil which would otherwise remain in the reservoir as residual oil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another method to reduce viscosity is <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_flooding" title="Carbon dioxide flooding">carbon dioxide flooding</a>.
</p><p>Tertiary recovery allows another 5% to 15% of the reservoir's oil to be recovered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EUR_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-EUR-2">[2]</a></sup> In some California <a href="/wiki/Heavy_crude_oil" title="Heavy crude oil">heavy oil</a> fields, steam injection has doubled or even tripled the oil reserves and ultimate oil recovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> For example, see <a href="/wiki/Midway-Sunset_Oil_Field#Production_history_and_future_potential" title="Midway-Sunset Oil Field">Midway-Sunset Oil Field</a>, California's largest oilfield.
</p><p>Tertiary recovery begins when secondary oil recovery is not enough to continue adequate extraction, but only when the oil can still be extracted <a href="/wiki/Profit_(economics)" title="Profit (economics)">profitably</a>. This depends on the <a href="/wiki/Cost" title="Cost">cost</a> of the extraction method and the current <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikinews.org/wiki/price_of_crude_oil" title="wikinews:price of crude oil">price of crude oil</a>. When prices are high, previously unprofitable wells are brought back into use, and when they are low, extraction is curtailed.
</p><p>The use of microbial treatments is another tertiary recovery method. Special blends of the microbes are used to treat and break down the hydrocarbon chain in oil, making the oil easy to recover. It is also more economical versus other conventional methods. In some states such as Texas, there are tax incentives for using these microbes in what is called a secondary tertiary recovery. Very few companies supply these,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="This term requires quantification. (March 2017)">quantify</span></a></i>]</sup> however, companies like Bio Tech, Inc. have proven very successful in waterfloods across Texas.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recovery_rates_and_factors">Recovery rates and factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extraction_of_petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Recovery rates and factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The amount of oil that is recoverable is determined by a number of factors, including the <a href="/wiki/Permeability_(earth_sciences)" title="Permeability (earth sciences)">permeability</a> of the rock, the strength of natural drives (the <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> present, pressure from adjacent water or gravity), <a href="/wiki/Porosity" title="Porosity">porosity</a> of the reservoir rock, i.e the rock storage capacity, and the viscosity of the oil. When the reservoir rocks are "tight", as in <a href="/wiki/Shale" title="Shale">shale</a>, oil generally cannot flow through, but when they are permeable, as in <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a>, oil flows freely.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Estimated_ultimate_recovery">Estimated ultimate recovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extraction_of_petroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Estimated ultimate recovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although recovery of a well cannot be known with certainty until the well ceases production, petroleum engineers often determine an <a href="/wiki/Estimated_ultimate_recovery" title="Estimated ultimate recovery">estimated ultimate recovery</a> (EUR) based on decline rate projections years into the future. Various models, mathematical techniques, and approximations are used.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Shale_gas" title="Shale gas">Shale gas</a> EUR is difficult to predict, and it is possible to choose recovery methods that tend to underestimate decline of the well beyond that which is reasonable.
</p>
