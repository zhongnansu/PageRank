<p><i><b>Emiliania huxleyi</b></i>, sometimes known by the abbreviation "Ehux", is a species of <a href="/wiki/Coccolithophore" title="Coccolithophore">coccolithophore</a> found in almost all ocean ecosystems the equator to sub-polar regions, and from nutrient rich upwelling zones to nutrient poor oligotrophic waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">[4]</a></sup> It is one of thousands of different <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthetic</a> plankton that freely drift in the euphotic zone of the ocean, forming the basis of virtually all marine <a href="/wiki/Food_web" title="Food web">food webs</a>. It is studied for the extensive <a href="/wiki/Algal_bloom" title="Algal bloom">blooms</a> it forms in nutrient-depleted waters after the reformation of the summer <a href="/wiki/Thermocline" title="Thermocline">thermocline</a>. Like other coccolithophores, <i>E. huxleyi</i> is a single-celled <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a> covered with uniquely ornamented <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a> disks called <a href="/wiki/Coccolith" title="Coccolith">coccoliths</a> (also informally known as liths or scales). Individual coccoliths are abundant in marine sediments although complete coccospheres are more unusual. In the case of <i>E. huxleyi</i>, not only the shell, but also the soft part of the organism may be recorded in sediments. It produces a group of chemical compounds that are very resistant to decomposition. These chemical compounds, known as <a href="/wiki/Alkenone" title="Alkenone">alkenones</a>, can be found in marine sediments long after other soft parts of the organisms have decomposed. Alkenones are most commonly used by <a href="/wiki/Earth_science" title="Earth science">earth scientists</a> as a means to estimate past <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_facts">Basic facts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emiliania_huxleyi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Basic facts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Emiliania huxleyi</i> was named after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Huxley" title="Thomas Huxley">Thomas Huxley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cesare_Emiliani" title="Cesare Emiliani">Cesare Emiliani</a>, who were the first to examine sea-bottom sediment and discover the coccoliths within it. It is believed to have evolved approximately 270,000 years ago from the older genus <i><a href="/wiki/Gephyrocapsa" title="Gephyrocapsa">Gephyrocapsa</a></i> Kampter<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-6">[6]</a></sup> and became dominant in planktonic assemblages, and thus in the fossil record, approximately 70,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It is the most numerically abundant and widespread coccolithophore species. The species is divided into seven morphological forms called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morphotype" title="Morphotype">morphotypes</a> based on differences in coccolith structure <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>(See <a class="external text" href="http://www.mikrotax.org/Nannotax3/index.php?dir=Coccolithophores/Isochrysidales/Noelaerhabdaceae/Emiliania/Emiliania%20huxleyi" rel="nofollow">Nannotax</a> for more detail on these forms). Its coccoliths are transparent and commonly colourless, but are formed of calcite which refracts light very efficiently in the water column. This, and the high concentrations caused by continual shedding of their coccoliths makes <i>E. huxleyi</i> blooms easily visible from space. <a href="/wiki/Earth_observation_satellite" title="Earth observation satellite">Satellite</a> images show that blooms can cover areas of more than 10,000 km<span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\textstyle ^{2}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Abundance_and_distribution">Abundance and distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emiliania_huxleyi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Abundance and distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Emiliania huxleyi</i>  is considered a ubiquitous species. It exhibits one of the largest temperature ranges (1-30<sup>o</sup>C) of any coccolithophores species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">[3]</a></sup> It has been observed under a range of nutrient levels from <a href="/wiki/Trophic_state_index" title="Trophic state index">oligotrophic</a> (subtropical <a href="/wiki/Ocean_gyre" title="Ocean gyre">gyres</a>) to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eutrophic" title="Eutrophic">eutrophic</a> waters (upwelling zones/ Norwegian fjords).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-:5-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Its presence in plankton communities from the surface to 200m depth indicates a high tolerance for both fluctuating and low light conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-:5-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> This extremely wide tolerance of environmental conditions is believed to be explained by the existence of a range of environmentally adapted <a href="/wiki/Ecotype" title="Ecotype">ecotypes</a> within the species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-6">[6]</a></sup> As a result of these tolerances its distribution ranges from the sub-Arctic to the sub-Antarctic and from coastal to oceanic habitats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Within this range it is present in nearly all euphotic zone water samples and accounts for 20-50% or more of the total coccolithophore community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-:5-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>During massive blooms (which can cover over 100,000 square kilometers), <i>E. huxleyi</i> cell concentrations can outnumber those of all other species in the region combined, accounting for 75% or more of the total number of photosynthetic plankton in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">[11]</a></sup> <i>E. huxleyi</i> blooms regionally act as an important source of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfide" title="Dimethyl sulfide">dimethyl sulfide</a>, the massive production of which can have a significant impact not only on the properties of the surface mixed layer, but also on global climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The blooms can be identified through satellite imagery because of the large amount of light back-scattered from the water column, which provides a method to assess their biogeochemical importance on both basin and global scales. These blooms are prevalent in the <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norwegian</a> <a href="/wiki/Fjord" title="Fjord">fjords</a>, causing satellites to pick up "white waters", which describes the reflectance of the blooms picked up by satellites. This is due to the mass of coccoliths reflecting the incoming sunlight back out of the water, allowing the extent of <i>E. huxleyi</i> blooms to be distinguished in fine detail.
</p><p>Extensive <i>E. huxleyi</i> blooms can have a visible impact on sea <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a>. While multiple scattering can increase light path per unit depth, increasing absorption and solar heating of the water column, <i>E. huxleyi</i> has inspired proposals for <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Geomimesis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Geomimesis (page does not exist)">geomimesis</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> because micron-sized air bubbles are specular reflectors, and so in contrast to <i>E. huxleyi</i>, tend  to lower the temperature of the upper water column. As with self-shading within water-whitening coccolithophore plankton blooms, this may reduce photosynthetic productivity by altering the geometry of the euphotic zone. Both experiments and modeling are needed to quantify the potential biological impact of such effects, and the corollary potential of reflective blooms of other organisms to increase or reduce evaporation and methane evolution by altering fresh water temperatures.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biogeochemical_impacts">Biogeochemical impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emiliania_huxleyi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Biogeochemical impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change">Climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emiliania_huxleyi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As with all phytoplankton, primary production of <i>E. huxleyi</i> through photosynthesis is a sink of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. However, the production of coccoliths through calcification is a source of CO<sub>2</sub>. This means that coccolithophores, including <i>E. huxleyi</i>, have the potential to act as a net source of CO<sub>2</sub> out of the ocean. Whether they are a net source or sink and how they will react to <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a> is not well understood.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ocean_heat_retention">Ocean heat retention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emiliania_huxleyi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ocean heat retention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Scattering stimulated by <i>E. huxleyi</i> blooms not only causes more heat and light to be pushed back up into the atmosphere than usual, but also cause more of the remaining heat to be trapped closer to the ocean surface. This is problematic because it is the surface water that exchanges heat with the atmosphere, and <i>E. huxleyi</i> blooms may tend to make the overall temperature of the water column dramatically cooler over longer time periods. However, the importance of this effect, whether positive or negative, is currently being researched and has not yet been established.
</p>
