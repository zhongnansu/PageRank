<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about water use in plant metabolism. For water use efficiency by humans, see <a href="/wiki/Water_efficiency" title="Water efficiency">Water efficiency</a>.</div>
<p><b>Water-use efficiency (WUE)</b> refers to the <a href="/wiki/Ratio" title="Ratio">ratio</a> of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plant_metabolism" title="Plant metabolism">plant metabolism</a> to water lost by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plant_(botanical)" title="Plant (botanical)">plant</a> through <a href="/wiki/Transpiration" title="Transpiration">transpiration</a>. Two types of water-use efficiency are referred to most frequently:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthetic</a> water-use efficiency (also called intrinsic or instantaneous water-use efficiency), which is defined as the ratio of the rate of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_assimilation" title="Carbon assimilation">carbon assimilation</a> (photosynthesis) to the rate of transpiration, and</li>
<li>water-use efficiency of productivity (also called integrated water-use efficiency), which is typically defined as the ratio of <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> produced to the rate of transpiration.</li></ul>
<p>Increases in water-use efficiency are commonly cited as a response mechanism of plants to moderate to severe <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> water deficits, and has been the focus of many programs that seek to increase <a href="/wiki/Crop" title="Crop">crop</a> tolerance of <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a>. However, there is some question as to the benefit of increased water-use efficiency of plants in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agricultural_systems" title="Agricultural systems">agricultural systems</a>, as the processes of increased yield production and decreased transpirational water loss (that is, the main driver of increases in water-use efficiency) are fundamentally opposed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> If there existed a situation where water deficit induced lower transpirational rates without simultaneously decreasing photosynthetic rates and biomass production, then water-use efficiency would be both greatly improved and a desired trait in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crop_productivity" title="Crop productivity">crop production</a>.
</p>
