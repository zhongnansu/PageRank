<p><b>Foraminifera</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'m' in 'my'">m</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "hole bearers"; informally called "<b>forams</b>") are members of a phylum or class of <a href="/wiki/Amoeba" title="Amoeba">amoeboid</a> <a href="/wiki/Protist" title="Protist">protists</a> characterized by streaming granular ectoplasm for catching food and other uses; and commonly an external shell (called a "<a href="/wiki/Test_(biology)" title="Test (biology)">test</a>") of diverse forms and materials. Tests of <a href="/wiki/Chitin" title="Chitin">chitin</a> (found in some simple genera, and <a href="/wiki/Textularia" title="Textularia">Textularia</a> in particular) are believed to be the most primitive type. Most foraminifera are marine, the majority of which live on or within the seafloor sediment (i.e., are <a href="/wiki/Benthos" title="Benthos">benthic</a>), while a smaller variety float in the water column at various depths (i.e., are <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">planktonic</a>). Fewer are known from freshwater or brackish conditions, and some very few (nonaquatic) soil species have been identified through molecular analysis of small subunit <a href="/wiki/Ribosomal_DNA" title="Ribosomal DNA">ribosomal DNA</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lej_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lej-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Foraminifera typically produce a <a href="/wiki/Test_(biology)" title="Test (biology)">test</a>, or shell, which can have either one or multiple chambers, some becoming quite elaborate in structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kennett_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kennett-4">[4]</a></sup> These shells are commonly made of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">CaCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>) or <a href="/wiki/Agglutination_(biology)" title="Agglutination (biology)">agglutinated</a> sediment particles. Over 50,000 species are recognized, both living (10,000)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adl2007_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-adl2007-5">[5]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> (40,000).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> They are usually less than 1 mm in size, but some are much larger, the <a href="/wiki/Xenophyophore" title="Xenophyophore">largest species</a> reaching up to 20 cm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>In modern Scientific English, the term <i>foraminifera</i> is both singular and plural (irrespective of the word's <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> derivation), and is used to describe one or more specimens or taxa: its usage as singular or plural must be determined from context. Foraminifera is frequently used informally to describe the group, and in these cases is generally lowercase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Foraminifera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The taxonomic position of the Foraminifera has varied since their recognition as protozoa (protists) by Schultze in 1854,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Treatise_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Treatise-10">[10]</a></sup> 
there referred to as an order, Foraminiferida.  <a href="/wiki/Alfred_R._Loeblich_Jr" title="Alfred R. Loeblich Jr">Loeblich</a> and <a href="/wiki/Helen_Ni%C3%B1a_Tappan_Loeblich" title="Helen Niña Tappan Loeblich">Tappan</a> (1992) reranked Foraminifera as a class<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sen_Gupta02_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sen_Gupta02-11">[11]</a></sup> as it is now commonly regarded.
</p><p>The Foraminifera have typically been included in the <a href="/wiki/Protozoa" title="Protozoa">Protozoa</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C-Smith04_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-C-Smith04-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C-Smith03_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-C-Smith03-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> or in the similar Protoctista or <a href="/wiki/Protist" title="Protist">Protist</a> <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_(biology)" title="Kingdom (biology)">kingdom</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Compelling evidence, based primarily on <a href="/wiki/Molecular_phylogenetics" title="Molecular phylogenetics">molecular phylogenetics</a>, exists for their belonging to a major group within the Protozoa known as the <a href="/wiki/Rhizaria" title="Rhizaria">Rhizaria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C-Smith04_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-C-Smith04-12">[12]</a></sup>  Prior to the recognition of evolutionary relationships among the members of the <a href="/wiki/Rhizaria" title="Rhizaria">Rhizaria</a>, the Foraminifera were generally grouped with other <a href="/wiki/Amoeba" title="Amoeba">amoeboids</a> as phylum Rhizopodea (or Sarcodina) in the class Granuloreticulosa.
</p><p>The Rhizaria are problematic, as they are often called a "supergroup", rather than using an established <a href="/wiki/Taxonomic_rank" title="Taxonomic rank">taxonomic rank</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Phylum" title="Phylum">phylum</a>. Cavalier-Smith defines the Rhizaria as an infra-kingdom within the kingdom Protozoa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C-Smith04_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-C-Smith04-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some taxonomies put the Foraminifera in a phylum of their own, putting them on par with the amoeboid Sarcodina in which they had been placed.
</p><p>Although as yet unsupported by morphological correlates, molecular data strongly suggest the Foraminifera are closely related to the <a href="/wiki/Cercozoa" title="Cercozoa">Cercozoa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radiolaria" title="Radiolaria">Radiolaria</a>, both of which also include amoeboids with complex shells; these three groups make up the Rhizaria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C-Smith03_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-C-Smith03-13">[13]</a></sup> However, the exact relationships of the forams to the other groups and to one another are still not entirely clear. Foraminifera are closely related to <a href="/wiki/Testate_amoebae" title="Testate amoebae">testate amoebae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Living_Foraminifera">Living Foraminifera</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Foraminifera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Living Foraminifera">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Modern Foraminifera are primarily marine organisms, but living individuals have been found in brackish, freshwater <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sen_Gupta83_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sen_Gupta83-19">[19]</a></sup> and even terrestrial habitats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lej_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lej-3">[3]</a></sup> The majority of the species are <a href="/wiki/Benthos" title="Benthos">benthic</a>, and a further 40 morphospecies are <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">planktonic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hemleben_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hemleben-20">[20]</a></sup> This count may, however, represent only a fraction of actual diversity, since many genetically distinct species may be morphologically indistinguishable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kucera_and_Darling_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kucera_and_Darling-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>A number of forams have unicellular <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> as <a href="/wiki/Endosymbiont" title="Endosymbiont">endosymbionts</a>, from diverse lineages such as the <a href="/wiki/Green_algae" title="Green algae">green algae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Red_algae" title="Red algae">red algae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Golden_algae" title="Golden algae">golden algae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diatom" title="Diatom">diatoms</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dinoflagellate" title="Dinoflagellate">dinoflagellates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hemleben_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hemleben-20">[20]</a></sup>  Some forams are <a href="/wiki/Kleptoplasty" title="Kleptoplasty">kleptoplastic</a>, retaining <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplasts</a> from ingested algae to conduct <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bernhard_and_Bowser_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bernhard_and_Bowser-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology">Biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Foraminifera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The foraminiferal cell is divided into granular endoplasm and transparent ectoplasm from which a pseudopodial net may emerge through a single opening or through many perforations in the test. Individual pseudopods characteristically have small granules streaming in both directions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sen_Gupta83_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sen_Gupta83-19">[19]</a></sup>
The pseudopods are used for locomotion, anchoring, and in capturing food, which consists of small organisms such as diatoms or bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hemleben_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hemleben-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>The foraminiferal life-cycle involves an alternation between <a href="/wiki/Ploidy#Haploid_and_monoploid" title="Ploidy">haploid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ploidy" title="Ploidy">diploid</a> generations, although they are mostly similar in form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Treatise_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Treatise-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M,L_&amp;F_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-M,L_&amp;F-23">[23]</a></sup> The haploid or gamont initially has a single <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">nucleus</a>, and divides to produce numerous <a href="/wiki/Gamete" title="Gamete">gametes</a>, which typically have two <a href="/wiki/Flagellum" title="Flagellum">flagella</a>.  The diploid or <a href="/wiki/Fission_(biology)#Multiple_fission_of_protists" title="Fission (biology)">schizont</a> is <a href="/wiki/Multinucleate" title="Multinucleate">multinucleate</a>, and after <a href="/wiki/Meiosis" title="Meiosis">meiosis</a> divides to produce new gamonts.  Multiple rounds of <a href="/wiki/Asexual_reproduction" title="Asexual reproduction">asexual reproduction</a> between sexual generations are not uncommon in benthic forms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sen_Gupta83_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sen_Gupta83-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>

Field width 5.22 mm</div></div></div>
<p>The form and composition of their <a href="/wiki/Test_(biology)" title="Test (biology)">tests</a> are the primary means by which forams are identified and classified.  Most have calcareous tests, composed of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sen_Gupta83_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-Sen_Gupta83-19">[19]</a></sup>  In other forams, the tests may be composed of organic material, made from small pieces of sediment cemented together (agglutinated), and in one genus, of <a href="/wiki/Silicon_dioxide" title="Silicon dioxide">silica</a>.  Openings in the test, including those that allow cytoplasm to flow between chambers, are called apertures.   The test contains an organic matrix, which can sometimes be recovered from fossil samples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lana2001_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lana2001-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Tests as fossils are known from as far back as the <a href="/wiki/Cambrian" title="Cambrian">Cambrian</a> period,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and many marine sediments are composed primarily of them.  For instance, the limestone that makes up the pyramids of Egypt is composed almost entirely of nummulitic benthic Foraminifera.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> It is estimated that reef Foraminifera generate about 43 million tons of calcium carbonate per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langer._et_al._1997_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Langer._et_al._1997-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Genetic studies have identified the naked amoeba "Reticulomyxa" and the peculiar <a href="/wiki/Xenophyophore" title="Xenophyophore">xenophyophores</a> as foraminiferans without tests. A few other amoeboids produce reticulose pseudopods, and were formerly classified with the forams as the Granuloreticulosa, but this is no longer considered a natural group, and most are now placed among the Cercozoa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adl._et_al._28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Adl._et_al.-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Deep-sea_species">Deep-sea species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Foraminifera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Deep-sea species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Foraminifera are found in the deepest parts of the ocean such as the <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Trench" title="Mariana Trench">Mariana Trench</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Challenger_Deep" title="Challenger Deep">Challenger Deep</a>, the deepest part known.  At these depths, below the <a href="/wiki/Carbonate_compensation_depth" title="Carbonate compensation depth">carbonate compensation depth</a>, the calcium carbonate of the tests is soluble in water due to the extreme pressure. The Foraminifera found in the Challenger Deep thus have no carbonate test, but instead have one of organic material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-organicWalled_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-organicWalled-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Four species found in the Challenger Deep are unknown from any other place in the oceans, one of which is representative of an endemic genus unique to the region. They are <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Resigella&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Resigella (page does not exist)">Resigella</a> laevis</i> and <i>R. bilocularis</i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nodellum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nodellum (page does not exist)">Nodellum</a> aculeata</i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Conicotheca_nigrans" title="Conicotheca nigrans">Conicotheca nigrans</a></i> (the unique genus).  All have tests that are mainly of transparent organic material which have small (about 100 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanometer" title="Nanometer">nm</a>) plates that appear to be clay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-organicWalled_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-organicWalled-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolutionary_significance">Evolutionary significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Foraminifera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Evolutionary significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dying planktonic Foraminifera continuously rain down on the sea floor in vast numbers, their mineralized tests preserved as fossils in the accumulating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelagic_sediments" title="Pelagic sediments">sediment</a>. Beginning in the 1960s, and largely under the auspices of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deep_Sea_Drilling_Program" title="Deep Sea Drilling Program">Deep Sea Drilling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ocean_Drilling_Program" title="Ocean Drilling Program">Ocean Drilling</a>, and International Ocean Drilling Programmes, as well as for the purposes of oil exploration, advanced deep-sea drilling techniques have been bringing up sediment cores bearing Foraminifera fossils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-30">[30]</a></sup> The effectively unlimited supply of these fossil tests and the relatively high-precision age-control models available for cores has produced an exceptionally high-quality planktonic Foraminifera fossil record dating back to the mid-<a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a>, and presents an unparalleled record for scientists testing and documenting the evolutionary process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-30">[30]</a></sup> The exceptional quality of the fossil record has allowed an impressively detailed picture of species inter-relationships to be developed on the basis of fossils, in many cases subsequently validated independently through molecular genetic studies on extant specimens<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
Larger benthic Foraminifera with complex shell structure react in a highly specific manner to the different benthic environments and, therefore, the composition of the assemblages and the distribution patterns of particular species reflect simultaneously bottom types and the light gradient. In the course of Earth history, larger Foraminifera are replaced frequently. In particular, associations of Foraminifera characterizing particular shallow water facies types are dying out and are replaced after a certain time interval by new associations with the same structure of shell morphology, emerging from a new evolutionary process of adaptation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> These evolutionary processes make the larger Foraminifera useful as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Index_fossil" title="Index fossil">index fossils</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">Permian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cenozoic" title="Cenozoic">Cenozoic</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Foraminifera&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because of their diversity, abundance, and complex morphology, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_prehistoric_foraminiferans" title="List of prehistoric foraminiferans">fossil foraminiferal</a> assemblages are useful for <a href="/wiki/Biostratigraphy" title="Biostratigraphy">biostratigraphy</a>, and can accurately give relative dates to sedimentary rocks, as was discovered by <a href="/wiki/Alva_C._Ellisor" title="Alva C. Ellisor">Alva C. Ellisor</a> in 1920.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_industry" title="Oil industry">oil industry</a> relies heavily on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microfossil" title="Microfossil">microfossils</a> such as forams to find potential hydrocarbon deposits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boardman_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boardman-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Calcareous fossil Foraminifera are formed from elements found in the ancient seas where they lived.  Thus, they are very useful in <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">paleoclimatology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paleoceanography" title="Paleoceanography">paleoceanography</a>.  They can be used, as a climate <a href="/wiki/Proxy_(climate)" title="Proxy (climate)">proxy</a>, to reconstruct past climate by examining the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stable_isotope" title="Stable isotope">stable isotope</a> ratios and trace element content of the shells (tests). Global temperature and ice volume can be revealed by the isotopes of oxygen, and the history of the carbon cycle and oceanic productivity by examining the stable isotope ratios of carbon;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zachos_et_al._35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zachos_et_al.-35">[35]</a></sup> see <a href="/wiki/%CE%9418O" title="Δ18O">δ18O</a> and <a href="/wiki/%CE%9413C" title="Δ13C">δ13C</a>.  The concentration of trace elements, like <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> (Mg),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a> (Li)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Boron" title="Boron">boron</a> (B),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> also hold a wealth of information about global temperature cycles, continental weathering, and the role of the ocean in the global carbon cycle. Geographic patterns seen in the fossil records of planktonic forams are also used to reconstruct ancient <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">ocean currents</a>.  Because certain types of Foraminifera are found only in certain environments, they can be used to figure out the kind of environment under which ancient marine sediments were deposited.
</p><p>For the same reasons they make useful biostratigraphic markers, living foraminiferal assemblages have been used as <a href="/wiki/Bioindicator" title="Bioindicator">bioindicators</a> in coastal environments, including indicators of coral reef health.  Because calcium carbonate is susceptible to dissolution in acidic conditions, Foraminifera may be particularly affected by changing climate and <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a>.
</p><p>Foraminifera have many uses in <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum exploration</a> and are used routinely to interpret the ages and paleoenvironments of sedimentary strata in oil wells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones1996_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jones1996-39">[39]</a></sup>  Agglutinated fossil Foraminifera buried deeply in sedimentary basins can be used to estimate thermal maturity, which is a key factor for petroleum generation. The <a href="/wiki/Foraminiferal_Colouration_Index" title="Foraminiferal Colouration Index">Foraminiferal Colouration Index</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McNeil1996_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-McNeil1996-40">[40]</a></sup> (FCI) is used to quantify colour changes and estimate burial temperature.  FCI data is particularly useful in the early stages of petroleum generation (about 100 °C).
</p><p>Foraminifera can also be used in <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeology</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Provenance" title="Provenance">provenancing</a> of some stone raw material types. Some stone types, such as <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>, are commonly found to contain fossilised Foraminifera.  The types and concentrations of these fossils within a sample of stone can be used to match that sample to a source known to contain the same "fossil signature".
</p>
