<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Lake</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">A body of relatively still water, in a basin surrounded by land</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Lake_(disambiguation)" title="Lake (disambiguation)">Lake (disambiguation)</a>.</div>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lac_Peyto_(4).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="248" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Lac_Peyto_%284%29.jpg/330px-Lac_Peyto_%284%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Lac_Peyto_%284%29.jpg/495px-Lac_Peyto_%284%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Lac_Peyto_%284%29.jpg/660px-Lac_Peyto_%284%29.jpg 2x" width="330"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lac_Peyto_(4).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Peyto_Lake" title="Peyto Lake">Peyto Lake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a></div></div></div>
<p>A <b>lake</b> is an area filled with water, localized in a <a href="/wiki/Depression_(geology)" title="Depression (geology)">basin</a>, that is surrounded by land, apart from any <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a> or other outlet that serves to feed or drain the lake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Lakes lie on land and are not part of the <a href="/wiki/World_Ocean" title="World Ocean">ocean</a>, and therefore are distinct from <a href="/wiki/Lagoon" title="Lagoon">lagoons</a>, and are also larger and deeper than <a href="/wiki/Pond" title="Pond">ponds</a>, though there are no official or scientific definitions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Lakes can be contrasted with <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">streams</a>, which are usually flowing. Most lakes are fed and drained by rivers and streams.
</p><p>Natural lakes are generally found in mountainous areas, <a href="/wiki/Rift_zone" title="Rift zone">rift zones</a>, and areas with ongoing <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciation</a>. Other lakes are found in <a href="/wiki/Endorheic_basin" title="Endorheic basin">endorheic basins</a> or along the courses of mature rivers. In some parts of the world there are many lakes because of chaotic drainage patterns left over from the last <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_Age" title="Ice Age">Ice Age</a>. All lakes are temporary over geologic time scales, as they will slowly fill in with sediments or spill out of the basin containing them.
</p><p>Many lakes are artificial and are constructed for industrial or agricultural use, for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydro-electric_power" title="Hydro-electric power">hydro-electric power</a> generation or domestic water supply, or for aesthetic or recreational purposes or even for other activities.
</p>

<h2><span id="Etymology.2C_meaning.2C_and_usage_of_.22lake.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id='Etymology,_meaning,_and_usage_of_"lake"'>Etymology, meaning, and usage of "lake"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title='Edit section: Etymology, meaning, and usage of "lake"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Oeschinen.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="667" data-file-width="1000" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Oeschinen.jpg/220px-Oeschinen.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Oeschinen.jpg/330px-Oeschinen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Oeschinen.jpg/440px-Oeschinen.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Oeschinen.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Oeschinen_Lake" title="Oeschinen Lake">Oeschinen Lake</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Alps" title="Swiss Alps">Swiss Alps</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Emerald_Bay.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2528" data-file-width="3887" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Emerald_Bay.jpg/220px-Emerald_Bay.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Emerald_Bay.jpg/330px-Emerald_Bay.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Emerald_Bay.jpg/440px-Emerald_Bay.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Emerald_Bay.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Lake_Tahoe" title="Lake Tahoe">Lake Tahoe</a> on the border of <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Caspian_Sea_from_orbit.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5600" data-file-width="4400" height="280" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Caspian_Sea_from_orbit.jpg/220px-Caspian_Sea_from_orbit.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Caspian_Sea_from_orbit.jpg/330px-Caspian_Sea_from_orbit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Caspian_Sea_from_orbit.jpg/440px-Caspian_Sea_from_orbit.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Caspian_Sea_from_orbit.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a> is either the world's largest lake or a full-fledged sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Caspian_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Caspian-3">[3]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The word <i>lake</i> comes from <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> <i>lake</i> ("lake, pond, waterway"), from <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>lacu</i> ("pond, pool, stream"), from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic" title="Proto-Germanic">Proto-Germanic</a> *<i>lakō</i> ("pond, ditch, slow moving stream"), from the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Proto-Indo-European</a> root *<i>leǵ-</i> ("to leak, drain"). Cognates include <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> <i>laak</i> ("lake, pond, ditch"), <a href="/wiki/Middle_Low_German" title="Middle Low German">Middle Low German</a> <i>lāke</i> ("water pooled in a riverbed, puddle") as in: <a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfslake" title="de:Wolfslake">de:Wolfslake</a>, <a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Butterlake" title="de:Butterlake">de:Butterlake</a>, <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> <i>Lache</i> ("pool, puddle"), and <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">Icelandic</a> <i>lækur</i> ("slow flowing stream"). Also related are the English words <i>leak</i> and <i>leach</i>.
</p><p>There is considerable uncertainty about defining the difference between lakes and <a href="/wiki/Pond" title="Pond">ponds</a>, and no current internationally accepted definition of either term across scientific disciplines or political boundaries exists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HeinonenZiglio2008_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-HeinonenZiglio2008-4">[4]</a></sup> For example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Limnologist" title="Limnologist">limnologists</a> have defined lakes as water bodies which are simply a larger version of a pond, which can have wave action on the shoreline or where wind-induced turbulence plays a major role in mixing the water column. None of these definitions completely excludes ponds and all are difficult to measure. For this reason, simple size-based definitions are increasingly used to separate ponds and lakes. One definition of <i>lake</i> is a body of water of 2 hectares (5 acres) or more in area;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BiologicalConservation1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-BiologicalConservation1-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>331</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BiologicalReviews1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-BiologicalReviews1-6">[6]</a></sup> however, others<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (June 2009)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> have defined lakes as waterbodies of 5 hectares (12 acres) and above,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> or 8 hectares (20 acres) and above <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> (see also the <a href="/wiki/Pond#Technical_definitions" title="Pond">definition of "pond"</a>). <a href="/wiki/Charles_Sutherland_Elton" title="Charles Sutherland Elton">Charles Elton</a>, one of the founders of <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, regarded lakes as waterbodies of 40 hectares (99 acres) or more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JournalOfEcology1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-JournalOfEcology1-8">[8]</a></sup> The term <i>lake</i> is also used to describe a feature such as <a href="/wiki/Lake_Eyre" title="Lake Eyre">Lake Eyre</a>, which is a dry basin most of the time but may become filled under seasonal conditions of heavy rainfall. In common usage, many lakes bear names ending with the word <i>pond</i>, and a lesser number of names ending with <i>lake</i> are in quasi-technical fact, ponds. One textbook illustrates this point with the following: "In Newfoundland, for example, almost every lake is called a pond, whereas in Wisconsin, almost every pond is called a lake."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cech2009_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cech2009-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>One <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrology</a> book proposes to define the term "lake" as a body of water with the following five characteristics:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HeinonenZiglio2008_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-HeinonenZiglio2008-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>it partially or totally fills one or several basins connected by straits<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HeinonenZiglio2008_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-HeinonenZiglio2008-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>has essentially the same water level in all parts (except for relatively short-lived variations caused by wind, varying ice cover, large inflows, etc.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HeinonenZiglio2008_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-HeinonenZiglio2008-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>it does not have regular intrusion of <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HeinonenZiglio2008_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-HeinonenZiglio2008-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>a considerable portion of the sediment suspended in the water is captured by the basins (for this to happen they need to have a sufficiently small inflow-to-volume ratio)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HeinonenZiglio2008_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-HeinonenZiglio2008-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>the area measured at the mean water level exceeds an arbitrarily chosen threshold (for instance, one <a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">hectare</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HeinonenZiglio2008_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-HeinonenZiglio2008-4">[4]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>With the exception of the seawater intrusion criterion, the others have been accepted or elaborated upon by other hydrology publications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shahin2002_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shahin2002-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Copernicus2004_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Copernicus2004-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_of_lakes">Distribution of lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution of lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rila_7_lakes_circus_panorama_edit1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1391" data-file-width="4781" height="64" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Rila_7_lakes_circus_panorama_edit1.jpg/220px-Rila_7_lakes_circus_panorama_edit1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Rila_7_lakes_circus_panorama_edit1.jpg/330px-Rila_7_lakes_circus_panorama_edit1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Rila_7_lakes_circus_panorama_edit1.jpg/440px-Rila_7_lakes_circus_panorama_edit1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rila_7_lakes_circus_panorama_edit1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Seven_Rila_Lakes" title="Seven Rila Lakes">Seven Rila Lakes</a> are a group of glacial lakes in the <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgarian</a> <a href="/wiki/Rila" title="Rila">Rila</a> mountains.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wayanad_lake.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2181" data-file-width="9457" height="51" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Wayanad_lake.jpg/220px-Wayanad_lake.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Wayanad_lake.jpg/330px-Wayanad_lake.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Wayanad_lake.jpg/440px-Wayanad_lake.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wayanad_lake.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wayanad_District" title="Wayanad District">Wayanad District</a> <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a> <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a></div></div></div>
<p>The majority of lakes on Earth are <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">fresh water</a>, and most lie in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a> at higher <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitudes</a>. <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, with a <a href="/wiki/Drainage_system_(geomorphology)#Deranged_drainage_pattern" title="Drainage system (geomorphology)">deranged drainage system</a> has an estimated 31,752 lakes larger than 3 square kilometres (1.2 sq mi)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and an unknown total number of lakes, but is estimated to be at least 2 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> has 187,888 lakes 500 square metres (5,400 sq ft) or larger, of which 56,000 are large (10,000 square metres (110,000 sq ft) or larger).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Most lakes have at least one natural outflow in the form of a <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a> or <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">stream</a>, which maintain a lake's average level by allowing the drainage of excess water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fenh_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-fenh-15">[15]</a></sup> Some lakes do not have a natural outflow and lose water solely by evaporation or underground seepage or both. They are termed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endorheic" title="Endorheic">endorheic</a> lakes.
</p><p>Many lakes are artificial and are constructed for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydro-electric" title="Hydro-electric">hydro-electric</a> power generation, aesthetic purposes, <a href="/wiki/Recreation" title="Recreation">recreational</a> purposes, <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industrial</a> use, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agricultural" title="Agricultural">agricultural</a> use or domestic water supply.
</p><p>Evidence of extraterrestrial lakes exists; "definitive evidence of lakes filled with <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>" was announced by NASA<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> as returned by the <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">Cassini Probe</a> observing the moon <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>, which orbited the planet <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a>.
</p><p>Globally, lakes are greatly outnumbered by ponds: of an estimated 304 million standing water bodies worldwide, 91% are 1 hectare (2.5 acres) or less in area (see definition of <a href="/wiki/Pond#Technical_definitions" title="Pond">ponds</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DowningPrairie2006_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-DowningPrairie2006-16">[16]</a></sup> Small lakes are also much more numerous than large lakes: in terms of area, one-third of the world's standing water is represented by lakes and ponds of 10 hectares (25 acres) or less.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, large lakes account for much of the area of standing water with 122 large lakes of 1,000 square kilometres (390 sq mi, 100,000 <a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">ha</a>, 247,000 <a href="/wiki/Acre" title="Acre">acres</a>) or more representing about 29% of the total global area of standing inland water.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_lakes">Origin of lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origin of lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:02162008_Interstate80NWUtah.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3872" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/02162008_Interstate80NWUtah.JPG/220px-02162008_Interstate80NWUtah.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/02162008_Interstate80NWUtah.JPG/330px-02162008_Interstate80NWUtah.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/02162008_Interstate80NWUtah.JPG/440px-02162008_Interstate80NWUtah.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:02162008_Interstate80NWUtah.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A portion of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Salt_Lake" title="Great Salt Lake">Great Salt Lake</a> in <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a>, United States</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A_lake_in_the_Andes_Mountains.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="545" data-file-width="727" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/A_lake_in_the_Andes_Mountains.jpeg/220px-A_lake_in_the_Andes_Mountains.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/A_lake_in_the_Andes_Mountains.jpeg/330px-A_lake_in_the_Andes_Mountains.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/A_lake_in_the_Andes_Mountains.jpeg/440px-A_lake_in_the_Andes_Mountains.jpeg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A_lake_in_the_Andes_Mountains.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A lake in the Andes Mountains</div></div></div><p>Hutchinson<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hutchinson1957a_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hutchinson1957a-17">[17]</a></sup> in 1957 published a monograph that is regarded as a landmark discussion and classification of all major lake types, their origin, morphometric characteristics, and distribution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohen2013a_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen2013a-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HåkansonOthers1983a_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-HåkansonOthers1983a-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Håkanson2012a_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Håkanson2012a-20">[20]</a></sup> As summarized and discussed by these researchers, Hutchinson presented in it a comprehensive analysis of the origin of lakes and proposed what is a widely accepted classification of lakes according to their origin. This classification recognizes 11 major lake types that are divided into 76 subtypes. The 11 major lake types are tectonic lakes, volcanic lakes, landslide lakes, glacial lakes, solution lakes, fluvial lakes, aeolian lakes, shoreline lakes, organic lakes, anthropomorphic lakes, and meteorite (extraterrestrial impact) lakes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HåkansonOthers1983a_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-HåkansonOthers1983a-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohen2013a_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen2013a-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Håkanson2012a_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Håkanson2012a-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tectonic_lakes">Tectonic lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tectonic lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tectonic lakes are lakes formed by the deformation and resulting lateral and vertical movements of the Earth’s crust. These movements include faulting, tilting, folding, and warping. Some of the well-known and largest lakes on Earth are <a href="/wiki/Rift_lake" title="Rift lake">rift lakes</a> occupying rift valleys, e.g. Central African Rift lakes and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baikal" title="Lake Baikal">Lake Baikal</a>. Other well-known tectonic lakes, <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_of_Aral" title="Sea of Aral">Sea of Aral</a>, and other lakes from the Pontocaspian occupy basins that have been separated from the sea by the tectonic uplift of the sea floor above sea level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hutchinson1957a_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hutchinson1957a-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HåkansonOthers1983a_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-HåkansonOthers1983a-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohen2013a_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen2013a-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Håkanson2012a_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Håkanson2012a-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Often, the tectonic action of crustal extension has created an alternating series of parallel <a href="/wiki/Graben" title="Graben">grabens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Horst_(geology)" title="Horst (geology)">horsts</a> that form elongate basins alternating with mountain ranges. Not only does this promote the creation of lakes by the disruption of preexisting drainage networks, it also creates within arid regions <a href="/wiki/Endorheic_basin" title="Endorheic basin">endorheic basins</a> that containing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salt_lake_(geography)" title="Salt lake (geography)">salt lakes</a> (also called <a href="/wiki/Saline_water" title="Saline water">saline</a> lakes). They form where there is no natural outlet, a high evaporation rate and the drainage surface of the <a href="/wiki/Water_table" title="Water table">water table</a> has a higher-than-normal <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a> content. Examples of these salt lakes include <a href="/wiki/Great_Salt_Lake" title="Great Salt Lake">Great Salt Lake</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea" title="Dead Sea">Dead Sea</a>. Another type of tectonic lake caused by faulting is <a href="/wiki/Sag_pond" title="Sag pond">sag ponds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hutchinson1957a_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hutchinson1957a-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HåkansonOthers1983a_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-HåkansonOthers1983a-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohen2013a_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen2013a-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Håkanson2012a_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-Håkanson2012a-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Volcanic_lakes">Volcanic lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Volcanic lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Volcanic lakes are lakes that occupy either local depressions, e.g. craters and <a href="/wiki/Maar" title="Maar">maars</a> or larger basins, e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calderas" title="Calderas">calderas</a>, created by <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">volcanism</a>. <a href="/wiki/Crater_lake" title="Crater lake">Crater lakes</a> are formed in <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_crater" title="Volcanic crater">volcanic craters</a> and calderas, which fill up with precipitation more rapidly than they empty via either evaporation, groundwater discharge, or combination of both. Sometimes the latter are called caldera lakes, although often no distinction is made. An example is <a href="/wiki/Crater_Lake" title="Crater Lake">Crater Lake</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>, in the caldera of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Mazama" title="Mount Mazama">Mount Mazama</a>. The caldera was created in a massive volcanic eruption that led to the <a href="/wiki/Subsidence" title="Subsidence">subsidence</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Mazama" title="Mount Mazama">Mount Mazama</a> around 4860 BC. Other volcanic lakes are created when either rivers or streams are dammed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lava_flow" title="Lava flow">lava flows</a> or volcanic <a href="/wiki/Lahar" title="Lahar">lahars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hutchinson1957a_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hutchinson1957a-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HåkansonOthers1983a_19-4"><a href="#cite_note-HåkansonOthers1983a-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohen2013a_18-4"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen2013a-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Håkanson2012a_20-4"><a href="#cite_note-Håkanson2012a-20">[20]</a></sup> The basin which is now <a href="/wiki/Malheur_Lake" title="Malheur Lake">Malheur Lake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a> was created when a lava flow dammed the <a href="/wiki/Malheur_River" title="Malheur River">Malheur River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1985_lake_atlas_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-1985_lake_atlas-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Glacial_lakes">Glacial lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Glacial lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Glacial lakes are lakes created by the direct action of glaciers and continental ice sheets. A wide variety of glacial processes create enclosed basins. As a result, there are a wide variety of different types of glacial lakes and it is often difficult to define clear-cut distinctions between different types of glacial lakes and lakes influenced by other activities. The general types of glacial lakes that have recognized are lakes in direct contact with ice; glacially carved rock basins and depressions; morainic and outwash lakes; and glacial drift basins. Glacial lakes are the most numerous lakes in the world. Most the lakes in northern Europe and North America have been either influenced or created by the latest, but not last, glaciation, to have covered the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hutchinson1957a_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-Hutchinson1957a-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HåkansonOthers1983a_19-5"><a href="#cite_note-HåkansonOthers1983a-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohen2013a_18-5"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen2013a-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Håkanson2012a_20-5"><a href="#cite_note-Håkanson2012a-20">[20]</a></sup> Glacial lakes include  <a href="/wiki/Proglacial_lake" title="Proglacial lake">proglacial lakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Subglacial_lake" title="Subglacial lake">subglacial lakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finger_lake" title="Finger lake">finger lakes</a>, and epishelf lakes. Epishelf lakes are highly stratified lakes in which a layer of freshwater, derived from ice and snow melt, is dammed behind an <a href="/wiki/Ice_shelf" title="Ice shelf">ice shelf</a> that is attached to the coastline. They are mostly found in Antarctica.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VeilletteOthers2008a_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-VeilletteOthers2008a-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fluvial_lakes">Fluvial lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fluvial lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fluvial (or riverine)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">[23]</a></sup> lakes are lakes produced by running water. These lakes include <a href="/wiki/Plunge_pool" title="Plunge pool">plunge pool lakes</a>, fluviatile dams and meander lakes.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxbow_lakes">Oxbow lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Oxbow lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The most common type of fluvial lake is a crescent-shaped lake called an <i><a href="/wiki/Oxbow_lake" title="Oxbow lake">oxbow lake</a></i> due to the distinctive curved shape. They can form in river valleys as a result of meandering. The slow-moving river forms a sinuous shape as the outer side of bends are eroded away more rapidly than the inner side. Eventually a horseshoe bend is formed and the river cuts through the narrow neck. This new passage then forms the main passage for the river and the ends of the bend become silted up, thus forming a bow-shaped lake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hutchinson1957a_17-5"><a href="#cite_note-Hutchinson1957a-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohen2013a_18-6"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen2013a-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HåkansonOthers1983a_19-6"><a href="#cite_note-HåkansonOthers1983a-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Håkanson2012a_20-6"><a href="#cite_note-Håkanson2012a-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fluviatile_dams">Fluviatile dams</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Fluviatile dams">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>These form where sediment from a tributary blocks the main river.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lateral_lakes">Lateral lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Lateral lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>These form where sediment from the main river blocks a tributary, usually in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Levee#Natural_examples" title="Levee">levee</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solution_lakes">Solution lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Solution lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A solution lake is a lake occupying a basin formed by surface dissolution of bedrock. In areas underlain by soluble bedrock, its solution by precipitation and percolating water commonly produce cavities. These cavities frequently collapse to form <a href="/wiki/Sinkhole" title="Sinkhole">sinkholes</a> that form part of the local <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karst_topography" title="Karst topography">karst topography</a>. Where <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> lies near the grounds surface, a sinkhole will be filled water as a solution lake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hutchinson1957a_17-6"><a href="#cite_note-Hutchinson1957a-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HåkansonOthers1983a_19-7"><a href="#cite_note-HåkansonOthers1983a-19">[19]</a></sup> If such a lake consists of a large area of standing water that occupies an extensive closed depression in limestone, it is also called a <a href="/wiki/Karst_lake" title="Karst lake">karst lake</a>. Smaller solution lakes that consist of a body of standing water in a closed depression within a karst region are known as <i>karst ponds.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackso1983a_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jackso1983a-25">[25]</a></sup> Limestone caves often contain pools of standing water, which are known as <i><a href="/wiki/Underground_lake" title="Underground lake">underground lakes</a>.</i> Classic examples of solution lakes are abundant in the karst regions at the <a href="/wiki/Dalmatia" title="Dalmatia">Dalmatian coast</a> of <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a> and within large parts of <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hutchinson1957a_17-7"><a href="#cite_note-Hutchinson1957a-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Landslide_lakes">Landslide lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Landslide lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Landslide lakes are lakes created by the <a href="/wiki/Landslide_dam" title="Landslide dam">blockage of a valley</a> by either mudflows, rockslides, or screes. Such lakes are common in mountainous regions. Although landslide lakes may be large and quite deep, they are typically short-lived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hutchinson1957a_17-8"><a href="#cite_note-Hutchinson1957a-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HåkansonOthers1983a_19-8"><a href="#cite_note-HåkansonOthers1983a-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohen2013a_18-7"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen2013a-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Håkanson2012a_20-7"><a href="#cite_note-Håkanson2012a-20">[20]</a></sup> An example of a landslide lake is <a href="/wiki/Quake_Lake" title="Quake Lake">Quake Lake</a>, which formed as a result of the <a href="/wiki/1959_Hebgen_Lake_earthquake" title="1959 Hebgen Lake earthquake">1959 Hebgen Lake earthquake</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MyersOthers1964a_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-MyersOthers1964a-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aeolian_lakes">Aeolian lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Aeolian lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Aeolian lakes are lakes produced by wind action. They are found mainly in arid environments although some aeolian lakes are <a href="/wiki/Relict_(geology)" title="Relict (geology)">relict landforms</a> indicative of arid paleoclimates. Aeolian lakes consist of lake basins dammed by wind-blown sand; interdunal lakes that lies between well-oriented sand dunes; and deflation basins formed by wind action under previously arid paleoenvironments. <a href="/wiki/Moses_Lake,_Washington" title="Moses Lake, Washington">Moses Lake, Washington</a>, is an example of a lake basins dammed by wind-blown sand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hutchinson1957a_17-9"><a href="#cite_note-Hutchinson1957a-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HåkansonOthers1983a_19-9"><a href="#cite_note-HåkansonOthers1983a-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohen2013a_18-8"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen2013a-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Håkanson2012a_20-8"><a href="#cite_note-Håkanson2012a-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shoreline_lakes">Shoreline lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Shoreline lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Shoreline lakes are generally lakes created by blockage of estuaries or by the uneven accretion of beach ridges by longshore and other currents. They include maritime coastal lakes, ordinarily in drowned estuaries; lakes enclosed by two tombolos or spits connecting an island to the mainland; lakes cut off from larger lakes by a bar; or lakes divided by the meeting of two spits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hutchinson1957a_17-10"><a href="#cite_note-Hutchinson1957a-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HåkansonOthers1983a_19-10"><a href="#cite_note-HåkansonOthers1983a-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohen2013a_18-9"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen2013a-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Håkanson2012a_20-9"><a href="#cite_note-Håkanson2012a-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organic_lakes">Organic lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Organic lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Organic lakes are lakes created by the actions of plants and animals. On the whole they are relatively rare in occurrence and quite small in size. In addition, they typically ephemeral features relative to the other types of lakes. The basins in which organic lakes occur are associated with beaver dams, coral lakes, or dams formed by vegetation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HåkansonOthers1983a_19-11"><a href="#cite_note-HåkansonOthers1983a-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Håkanson2012a_20-10"><a href="#cite_note-Håkanson2012a-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Peat_lakes">Peat lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Peat lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">Peat</a> lakes are a form of organic lake. They form where a buildup of partly decomposed plant material in a wet environment leaves the vegetated surface below the <a href="/wiki/Water_table" title="Water table">water table</a> for a sustained period of time. They are often low in nutrients and mildly acidic, with bottom waters low in dissolved oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anthropogenic_lakes">Anthropogenic lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Anthropogenic lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Anthropogenic lakes are artificially created lakes formed by human activity. They can be the result of intentional damming of rivers and streams or subsequent filling of abandon excavations by either ground water, precipitation, or a combination of both.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HåkansonOthers1983a_19-12"><a href="#cite_note-HåkansonOthers1983a-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Håkanson2012a_20-11"><a href="#cite_note-Håkanson2012a-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Meteorite_.28extraterrestrial_impact.2F_crater.29_lakes"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Meteorite_(extraterrestrial_impact/_crater)_lakes">Meteorite (extraterrestrial impact/ crater) lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Meteorite (extraterrestrial impact/ crater) lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Meteorite lakes, which are also known as crater lakes, are lakes created by catastrophic <a href="/wiki/Impact_event" title="Impact event">extraterrestrial impacts</a> by either <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">meteorites</a> or <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroids</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hutchinson1957a_17-11"><a href="#cite_note-Hutchinson1957a-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HåkansonOthers1983a_19-13"><a href="#cite_note-HåkansonOthers1983a-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Håkanson2012a_20-12"><a href="#cite_note-Håkanson2012a-20">[20]</a></sup> Examples of meteorite lakes are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lonar_crater_lake" title="Lonar crater lake">Lonar crater lake</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MaloofOther2010a_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-MaloofOther2010a-28">[28]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lake_Elgygytgyn" title="Lake Elgygytgyn">Lake Elgygytgyn</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WennrichOthers2016a_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-WennrichOthers2016a-29">[29]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Pingualuit_crater" title="Pingualuit crater">Pingualuit crater</a> lake, Quebec, Canada,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DesiageOthers2015a_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-DesiageOthers2015a-30">[30]</a></sup> As in case of Lake El'gygytgyn and Pingualuit crater lake, meteorite (extraterrestrial impact/ crater) lakes can contain unique and scientifically valuable sedimentary deposits associated with long records of paleoclimatic changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WennrichOthers2016a_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-WennrichOthers2016a-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DesiageOthers2015a_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-DesiageOthers2015a-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_lakes_according_to_thermal_stratification">Types of lakes according to thermal stratification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Types of lakes according to thermal stratification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition to their origin, there are various other ways of either naming or defining types of lakes. One major way of classification lakes in on the basis of thermal stratification because it is a major control on animal and plant life inhabiting a lake and the fate and distribution
of dissolved and suspended material in a lake. For example, the thermal stratification and the degree and frequency of mixing exerts a strong control on the distribution of oxygen within it. In addition, lake can be classified according important factors such as seasonal variations in lake volume and level, oxygen saturation, and salinity of its water mass. Finally, the names of types of lakes that are used by the lay public and in the scientific for different types of lakes are often informally derived from either from their morphology of other aspects or their physical characteristics.
</p><p>F.A. Forel,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forel1901a_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Forel1901a-31">[31]</a></sup> who is also referred to as the father of limnology, was the first scientist to classify lakes according to their <a href="/wiki/Lake_stratification" title="Lake stratification">thermal stratification</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loffler1957a_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Loffler1957a-32">[32]</a></sup> His system of classification was later modified and improved upon by Hutchinson and Laffler.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HutchinsonOther1956a_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-HutchinsonOther1956a-33">[33]</a></sup> Because the density of water varies with temperature, with a maximum at +4 DC, thermal stratification is an important physical characteristic of lakes that controls the fauna and flora, sedimentation, chemistry, and other aspects of individual lakes. First, the colder, heavier water typically forms a layer near the bottom, which called the <i><a href="/wiki/Hypolimnion" title="Hypolimnion">hypolimnion</a></i>. Second, normally overlying it is a transition zone known as the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metalimnion" title="Metalimnion">metalimnion</a></i>. Finally, overlying the metalimnion is a surface layer of a warmer, lighter water is called the <i><a href="/wiki/Epilimnion" title="Epilimnion">epilimnion</a></i>. However, this <i>typical</i> stratification sequence can vary widely depending either on the specific lake, the time of season, or combination of both.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HåkansonOthers1983a_19-14"><a href="#cite_note-HåkansonOthers1983a-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loffler1957a_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Loffler1957a-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HutchinsonOther1956a_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-HutchinsonOther1956a-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>Based upon thermal stratification, lakes are classified as either <a href="/wiki/Holomictic_lake" title="Holomictic lake">holomictic lakes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Meromictic_lake" title="Meromictic lake">meromictic lakes</a>. A meromictic lake is a lake which has layers of water which do not intermix. The deepest layer of water in such a lake does not contain any dissolved oxygen. In addition, the  layers of sediment at the bottom of a meromictic lake remain relatively undisturbed because there are no living <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerobic_organisms" title="Aerobic organisms">aerobic organisms</a>. The lack of disturbance allows for the development of lacustrine <a href="/wiki/Varve" title="Varve">varves</a>. A Holomictic lake is a lake that has a uniform temperature and density from top to bottom at a specific time during the year. This uniformity temperature and density in allows the lake waters to completely mix. Holomictic lakes are non-meromictic lakes. Based upon thermal stratification and frequency of turnover, holomictic lakes are divided into <a href="/wiki/Amictic_lake" title="Amictic lake">amictic lakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monomictic_lake" title="Monomictic lake">cold monomictic lakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dimictic_lake" title="Dimictic lake">dimictic lakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monomictic_lake" title="Monomictic lake">warm monomictic lakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polymictic_lake" title="Polymictic lake">polymictic lakes</a>, and oligomictic lakes. The classification of lakes by thermal stratification presupposes lakes with sufficient depth to form a hypolimnion. As a results, very shallow lakes are excluded this classification system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HåkansonOthers1983a_19-15"><a href="#cite_note-HåkansonOthers1983a-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HutchinsonOther1956a_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-HutchinsonOther1956a-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>The stratification in a lake is not always the result of variation to density because of thermal gradients. Stratification within a lake can also be the result of differences in density resulting from gradients in <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a>. In case of a difference in salinity, the hypolimnion and epilimnion are separated not by a thermocline but by a <a href="/wiki/Halocline" title="Halocline">halocline</a>, which is sometimes referred to as a <i><a href="/wiki/Chemocline" title="Chemocline">chemocline</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HåkansonOthers1983a_19-16"><a href="#cite_note-HåkansonOthers1983a-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HutchinsonOther1956a_33-3"><a href="#cite_note-HutchinsonOther1956a-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_lake_according_to_seasonal_variation_of_lake_level_and_volume">Types of lake according to seasonal variation of lake level and volume</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Types of lake according to seasonal variation of lake level and volume">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lakes are informally classified and named according to the seasonal variation in their lake level and volume. Some of the names include:
</p>
<ul><li>Ephemeral lake is a short-lived lake or pond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gangstad1979a_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gangstad1979a-34">[34]</a></sup> If it fills with water and dries up (disappears) seasonally it is known as an <i>intermittent lake</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Poehls2009a_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Poehls2009a-35">[35]</a></sup> They often fill <a href="/wiki/Polje" title="Polje">poljes</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dry_lake" title="Dry lake">Dry lake</a> is a popular name for an ephemeral lake that contains water only intermediately at irregular and infrequent intervals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackso1983a_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jackso1983a-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LastOthers2001a_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-LastOthers2001a-37">[37]</a></sup></li>
<li>Perennial lake is a lake that has water in its basin throughout the year and is not subject to extreme fluctuations in level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackso1983a_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jackso1983a-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gangstad1979a_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gangstad1979a-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
<li>Playa lake is a typically shallow, intermittent lake that covers or occupies a playa either in wet seasons or in especially wet years but subsequently drying up in an arid or semiarid region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackso1983a_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-Jackso1983a-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LastOthers2001a_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-LastOthers2001a-37">[37]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vlei" title="Vlei">Vlei</a> is a name used in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> for a shallow lake which varies considerably in level with the seasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theal1877a_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-theal1877a-38">[38]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_lake_according_to_water_chemistry">Types of lake according to water chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Types of lake according to water chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lakes are also informally classified and named according to the general chemistry of their water mass. Some of the types of lakes include:
</p>
<ul><li>An acid lake is a lake that has a pH is below neutral (&lt;6.5). A lake is considered highly acidic when the pH drops below 5.5, below which when biological consequences occur. Such lakes include acid pit lakes occupying abandoned mines and excavations; naturally acid lakes of igneous and metamorphic landscapes; peat bogs in northern regions; acid-saline lakes of arid environments; crater lakes of active and dormant volcanoes; and lakes acidified by acid rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GellerOthers2013a_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-GellerOthers2013a-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PatrickOthers1981a_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-PatrickOthers1981a-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RouwetOthers2015a_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-RouwetOthers2015a-41">[41]</a></sup></li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Salt_lake" title="Salt lake">salt lake</a>, which also known as a <i>brine lake</i>, is an inland body of water situated in an arid or semiarid region, having no outlet to the sea, and containing a high concentration of dissolved salts (principally sodium chloride). Examples include the Great Salt Lake in Utah, and the Dead Sea in the Near East.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackso1983a_25-4"><a href="#cite_note-Jackso1983a-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LastOthers2001a_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-LastOthers2001a-37">[37]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alkali_sink" title="Alkali sink">alkali sink</a>, also known as salt flats, are lakes on the other extreme of the scale from the large and deep saline lakes. They are, shallow saline features that occupy low-lying areas of the arid regions and in groundwater discharge zones. These are typically classifiable as either playas or playa lakes because they are periodically flooded by either rain or flood events and then dry up during drier intervals, leaving accumulations of brines and evaporitic minerals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackso1983a_25-5"><a href="#cite_note-Jackso1983a-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LastOthers2001a_37-3"><a href="#cite_note-LastOthers2001a-37">[37]</a></sup></li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Salt_pan_(geology)" title="Salt pan (geology)">salt pan</a> (saltpan) is either an undrained small shallow natural depression in which water accumulates and evaporates leaving a salt deposit or the shallow lake of brackish water occupying a salt pan. This term is also used for a large pan for recovering salt by evaporation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackso1983a_25-6"><a href="#cite_note-Jackso1983a-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li>A saline pan is a name for an ephemeral saline lake which precipitates a bottom crust that is subsequently modified during subaerial exposure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackso1983a_25-7"><a href="#cite_note-Jackso1983a-25">[25]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lakes_composed_of_other_liquids">Lakes composed of other liquids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Lakes composed of other liquids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Lava_lake" title="Lava lake">Lava lake</a> is a large volume of molten lava, usually basaltic, contained in a volcanic vent, crater, or broad depression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WithamOthers2006a_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-WithamOthers2006a-42">[42]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lakes_of_Titan" title="Lakes of Titan">Hydrocarbon lakes</a> are bodies of liquid <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">ethane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> that occupy depressions on the surface of <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>. They were detected by the Cassini–Huygens space probe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MastrogiuseppeOthers2014a_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-MastrogiuseppeOthers2014a-43">[43]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleolakes">Paleolakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Paleolakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A  <b>paleolake</b>, also spelt <b>palaeolake</b>, is a lake that existed in the past when hydrological conditions were different.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohen2003a_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen2003a-44">[44]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Quaternary" title="Quaternary">Quaternary</a> paleolakes can often be identified on the basis of <a href="/wiki/Relict_(geology)" title="Relict (geology)">relict</a> lacustrine landforms such as relict lake plains and coastal landforms that form recognizable relict shorelines, which are called <i>paleoshorelines.</i> Paleolakes can also be recognized by characteristic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sedimentary" title="Sedimentary">sedimentary</a> deposits that accumulated in them and any <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a> that these <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediments</a> might contain. The paleoshorelines and sedimentary deposits of paleolakes provide evidence for prehistoric hydrological changes during the times that they existed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohen2003a_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen2003a-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goudie2008a_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goudie2008a-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>Types of paleolakes include:
</p>
<ul><li>A <a href="/wiki/Category:Former_lakes" title="Category:Former lakes">former lake</a> is a lake which is no longer in existence. Such lakes include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prehistoric" title="Prehistoric">prehistoric</a> lakes and lakes which have permanently dried up often as the result of either <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a> or human intervention. <a href="/wiki/Owens_Lake" title="Owens Lake">Owens Lake</a> in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, USA, is an example of a former lake. Former lakes are a common feature of the <a href="/wiki/Basin_and_Range_Province" title="Basin and Range Province">Basin and Range</a> area of southwestern North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manivanan2008a_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Manivanan2008a-46">[46]</a></sup></li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Category:Shrunken_lakes" title="Category:Shrunken lakes">shrunken lake</a> is a lake which has drastically decreased in size over geological time. <a href="/wiki/Lake_Agassiz" title="Lake Agassiz">Lake Agassiz</a>, which once covered much of central North America, is a good example of a shrunken lake. Two notable remnants of this lake are <a href="/wiki/Lake_Winnipeg" title="Lake Winnipeg">Lake Winnipeg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Winnipegosis" title="Lake Winnipegosis">Lake Winnipegosis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manivanan2008a_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-Manivanan2008a-46">[46]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Paleolakes are of scientific and economic importance. For example, Quaternary paleolakes in semidesert basins are important for two reasons. First, they played an extremely significant, if transient, role in shaping the floors and <a href="/wiki/Foothills" title="Foothills">piedmonts</a> of many basins. Finally, their sediments contain enormous quantities of geologic and paleontologic information concerning past environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Currey1990a_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Currey1990a-47">[47]</a></sup> In addition, the organic-rich deposits of pre-Quaternary paleolakes are important either for the thick deposits of <a href="/wiki/Oil_shale" title="Oil shale">oil shale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shale_gas" title="Shale gas">shale gas</a> that they contain or as source rocks of <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>. Although of significantly less economic importance, strata deposited along the shore of paleolakes sometimes contain <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> seams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gierlowski-KordeschOthers2000a_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gierlowski-KordeschOthers2000a-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SchnurrenbergerOthers2003a_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-SchnurrenbergerOthers2003a-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SataSarvinen4.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/SataSarvinen4.jpg/220px-SataSarvinen4.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/SataSarvinen4.jpg/330px-SataSarvinen4.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/SataSarvinen4.jpg/440px-SataSarvinen4.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SataSarvinen4.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Lake_P%C3%A4ij%C3%A4nne" title="Lake Päijänne">Lake Päijänne</a> is one of tens of thousands of lakes in <a href="/wiki/Finnish_Lakeland" title="Finnish Lakeland">Finnish Lakeland</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lake_mapourika_NZ.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Lake_mapourika_NZ.jpeg/220px-Lake_mapourika_NZ.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Lake_mapourika_NZ.jpeg/330px-Lake_mapourika_NZ.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Lake_mapourika_NZ.jpeg/440px-Lake_mapourika_NZ.jpeg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lake_mapourika_NZ.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Lake_Mapourika" title="Lake Mapourika">Lake Mapourika</a>, New Zealand</div></div></div>
<p>Lakes have numerous features in addition to lake type, such as <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">drainage basin</a> (also known as catchment area), inflow and outflow, <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrient</a> content, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dissolved_oxygen" title="Dissolved oxygen">dissolved oxygen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">pollutants</a>, <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sedimentation</a>.
</p><p>Changes in the level of a lake are controlled by the difference between the input and output compared to the total volume of the lake. Significant input sources are precipitation onto the lake, runoff carried by streams and channels from the lake's <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">catchment</a> area, <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> channels and aquifers, and artificial sources from outside the catchment area. Output sources are evaporation from the lake, surface and groundwater flows, and any extraction of lake water by humans. As climate conditions and human water requirements vary, these will create fluctuations in the lake level.
</p><p>Lakes can be also <a href="/wiki/Trophic_state_index" title="Trophic state index">categorized</a> on the basis of their richness in nutrients, which typically affect plant growth. Nutrient-poor lakes are said to be <i><a href="/wiki/Oligotroph" title="Oligotroph">oligotrophic</a></i> and are generally clear, having a low concentration of plant life. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesotrophic_lake" title="Mesotrophic lake">Mesotrophic lakes</a></i> have good clarity and an average level of nutrients. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eutrophic" title="Eutrophic">Eutrophic</a></i> lakes are enriched with nutrients, resulting in good plant growth and possible <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Algal_blooms" title="Algal blooms">algal blooms</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">Hypertrophic</a></i> lakes are bodies of water that have been excessively enriched with nutrients. These lakes typically have poor clarity and are subject to devastating algal blooms. Lakes typically reach this condition due to human activities, such as heavy use of fertilizers in the lake catchment area. Such lakes are of little use to humans and have a poor ecosystem due to decreased dissolved oxygen.
</p><p>Due to the unusual relationship between water's <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> and its <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a>, lakes form layers called <a href="/wiki/Thermocline" title="Thermocline">thermoclines</a>, layers of drastically varying temperature relative to depth. Fresh water is most dense at about 4 degrees Celsius (39.2 °F) at sea level. When the temperature of the water at the surface of a lake reaches the same temperature as deeper water, as it does during the cooler months in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate">temperate</a> climates, the water in the lake can mix, bringing oxygen-starved water up from the depths and bringing oxygen down to decomposing sediments. Deep temperate lakes can maintain a reservoir of cold water year-round, which allows some cities to tap that reservoir for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deep_lake_water_cooling" title="Deep lake water cooling">deep lake water cooling</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yaylyu.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Yaylyu.JPG/220px-Yaylyu.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Yaylyu.JPG/330px-Yaylyu.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Yaylyu.JPG/440px-Yaylyu.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yaylyu.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Lake_Teletskoye" title="Lake Teletskoye">Lake Teletskoye</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a></div></div></div>
<p>Since the surface water of deep <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">tropical</a> lakes never reaches the temperature of maximum density, there is no process that makes the water mix. The deeper layer becomes oxygen starved and can become saturated with carbon dioxide, or other gases such as sulfur dioxide if there is even a trace of <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic activity</a>. Exceptional events, such as earthquakes or landslides, can cause mixing which rapidly brings the deep layers up to the surface and release a vast cloud of gas which lay trapped in solution in the colder water at the bottom of the lake. This is called a <a href="/wiki/Limnic_eruption" title="Limnic eruption">limnic eruption</a>. An example is <a href="/wiki/Lake_Nyos#The_1986_disaster" title="Lake Nyos">the disaster at Lake Nyos</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a>. The amount of gas that can be dissolved in water is directly related to pressure. As deep water surfaces, the pressure drops and a vast amount of gas comes out of solution. Under these circumstances carbon dioxide is hazardous because it is heavier than air and displaces it, so it may flow down a river valley to human settlements and cause mass <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asphyxiation" title="Asphyxiation">asphyxiation</a>.
</p><p>The material at the bottom of a lake, or <i>lake bed</i>, may be composed of a wide variety of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inorganic" title="Inorganic">inorganics</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silt</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_material" title="Organic material">organic material</a>, such as decaying plant or animal matter. The composition of the lake bed has a significant impact on the flora and fauna found within the lake's environs by contributing to the amounts and the types of nutrients available.
</p><p>A paired (black and white) layer of the varved lake sediments correspond to a year. During winter, when organisms die, carbon is deposited down, resulting to a black layer. At the same year, during summer, only few organic materials are deposited, resulting to a white layer at the lake bed. These are commonly used to track past paleontological events.
</p><p>Natural lakes provide a <a href="/wiki/Macrocosm_and_microcosm" title="Macrocosm and microcosm">microcosm</a> of living and nonliving elements that are relatively independent of their surrounding environments. Therefore, lake organisms can often be studied in isolation from the lake’s surroundings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Limnology">Limnology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Limnology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Limnology" title="Limnology">Limnology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake_ecosystem" title="Lake ecosystem">Lake ecosystem</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_aeration" title="Lake aeration">Lake aeration</a></div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Limnology" title="Limnology">Limnology</a> is the study of inland bodies of water and related ecosystems. Limnology divides lakes into three zones: the <i><a href="/wiki/Littoral_zone" title="Littoral zone">littoral zone</a></i>, a sloped area close to land; the <i><a href="/wiki/Photic_zone" title="Photic zone">photic</a></i> or <i>open-water zone</i>, where sunlight is abundant; and the deep-water <i><a href="/wiki/Profundal_zone" title="Profundal zone">profundal</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Benthic_zone" title="Benthic zone">benthic zone</a></i>, where little sunlight can reach. The depth to which light can reach in lakes depends on <a href="/wiki/Turbidity" title="Turbidity">turbidity</a>, determined by the density and size of suspended <a href="/wiki/Particle_(ecology)" title="Particle (ecology)">particles</a>. A particle is in <a href="/wiki/Suspension_(chemistry)" title="Suspension (chemistry)">suspension</a> if its weight is less than the random turbidity <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">forces</a> acting upon it. These particles can be sedimentary or <a href="/wiki/Biotic_material" title="Biotic material">biological</a> in origin and are responsible for the color of the water. Decaying plant matter, for instance, may be responsible for a yellow or brown color, while algae may cause greenish water. In very shallow water bodies, iron oxides make water reddish brown. Biological particles include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alga" title="Alga">algae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Detritus" title="Detritus">detritus</a>. Bottom-dwelling detritivorous <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> can be responsible for turbid waters, because they stir the mud in search of food. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piscivorous" title="Piscivorous">Piscivorous</a> fish contribute to turbidity by eating plant-eating (<a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">planktonivorous</a>) fish, thus increasing the amount of algae (see aquatic <a href="/wiki/Trophic_cascade" title="Trophic cascade">trophic cascade</a>). The light depth or transparency is measured by using a <i><a href="/wiki/Secchi_disk" title="Secchi disk">Secchi disk</a></i>, a 20-cm (8 in) disk with alternating white and black <a href="/wiki/Circular_sector" title="Circular sector">quadrants</a>. The depth at which the disk is no longer visible is the <i>Secchi depth</i>, a measure of transparency. The Secchi disk is commonly used to test for eutrophication. For a detailed look at these processes, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lentic_ecosystems" title="Lentic ecosystems">lentic ecosystems</a>.
</p><p>A lake moderates the surrounding region's temperature and <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> because water has a very high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Specific_heat_capacity" title="Specific heat capacity">specific heat capacity</a> (4,186 J·kg<sup>−1</sup>·K<sup>−1</sup>). In the daytime a lake can cool the land beside it with local winds, resulting in a <a href="/wiki/Sea_breeze" title="Sea breeze">sea breeze</a>; in the night it can warm it with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_breeze" title="Land breeze">land breeze</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="How_lakes_disappear">How lakes disappear</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: How lakes disappear">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The lake may be infilled with deposited sediment and gradually become a <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetland</a> such as a <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamp</a> or <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marsh</a>.  Large water plants, typically <a href="/wiki/Phragmites" title="Phragmites">reeds</a>, accelerate this closing process significantly because they partially decompose to form peat soils that fill the shallows. Conversely, peat soils in a marsh can naturally burn and reverse this process to recreate a shallow lake resulting in a dynamic equilibrium between marsh and lake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> This is significant since wildfire has been largely suppressed in the developed world over the past century. This has artificially converted many shallow lakes into emergent marshes. Turbid lakes and lakes with many plant-eating fish tend to disappear more slowly. A "disappearing" lake (barely noticeable on a human timescale) typically has extensive plant mats at the water's edge. These become a new habitat for other plants, like <a href="/wiki/Sphagnum" title="Sphagnum">peat moss</a> when conditions are right, and animals, many of which are very rare. Gradually the lake closes and young <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a> may form, forming a <a href="/wiki/Fen" title="Fen">fen</a>. In lowland river valleys where a river can <a href="/wiki/Meander" title="Meander">meander</a>, the presence of peat is explained by the infilling of historical <a href="/wiki/Oxbow_lake" title="Oxbow lake">oxbow lakes</a>. In the very last stages of <a href="/wiki/Ecological_succession" title="Ecological succession">succession</a>, trees can grow in, eventually turning the wetland into a forest.
</p><p>Some lakes can disappear seasonally. These are called <b>intermittent lakes</b>, <b>ephemeral lakes</b>, or <b>seasonal lakes</b> and can be found in <a href="/wiki/Karst" title="Karst">karstic terrain</a>. A prime example of an intermittent lake is <a href="/wiki/Lake_Cerknica" title="Lake Cerknica">Lake Cerknica</a> in <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenia</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lag_Prau_Pulte" title="Lag Prau Pulte">Lag Prau Pulte</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graub%C3%BCnden" title="Graubünden">Graubünden</a>. Other intermittent lakes are only the result of above-average precipitation in a closed, or <a href="/wiki/Endorheic_basin" title="Endorheic basin">endorheic basin</a>, usually filling dry lake beds. This can occur in some of the driest places on earth, like <a href="/wiki/Death_Valley" title="Death Valley">Death Valley</a>. This occurred in the spring of 2005, after unusually heavy rains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The lake did not last into the summer, and was quickly evaporated (see photos to right). A more commonly filled lake of this type is <a href="/wiki/Sevier_Lake" title="Sevier Lake">Sevier Lake</a> of west-central <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a>.
</p><p>Sometimes a lake will disappear quickly. On 3 June 2005, in <a href="/wiki/Nizhny_Novgorod_Oblast" title="Nizhny Novgorod Oblast">Nizhny Novgorod Oblast</a>, Russia, a lake called <a href="/wiki/Lake_Beloye_(Nizhny_Novgorod_Oblast)" title="Lake Beloye (Nizhny Novgorod Oblast)">Lake Beloye</a> vanished in a matter of minutes. News sources reported that government officials theorized that this strange phenomenon may have been caused by a shift in the soil underneath the lake that allowed its water to drain through channels leading to the <a href="/wiki/Oka_River" title="Oka River">Oka River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>The presence of ground permafrost is important to the persistence of some lakes. According to research published in the journal <i>Science</i> ("Disappearing Arctic Lakes", June 2005), thawing permafrost may explain the shrinking or disappearance of hundreds of large Arctic lakes across western Siberia. The idea here is that rising air and soil temperatures thaw permafrost, allowing the lakes to drain away into the ground.
</p><p>Some lakes disappear because of human development factors. The shrinking <a href="/wiki/Aral_Sea" title="Aral Sea">Aral Sea</a> is described as being "murdered" by the diversion for irrigation of the rivers feeding it.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraterrestrial_lakes">Extraterrestrial lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Extraterrestrial lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PIA10008_Seas_and_Lakes_on_Titan.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1900" data-file-width="2348" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/PIA10008_Seas_and_Lakes_on_Titan.jpg/220px-PIA10008_Seas_and_Lakes_on_Titan.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/PIA10008_Seas_and_Lakes_on_Titan.jpg/330px-PIA10008_Seas_and_Lakes_on_Titan.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/PIA10008_Seas_and_Lakes_on_Titan.jpg/440px-PIA10008_Seas_and_Lakes_on_Titan.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PIA10008_Seas_and_Lakes_on_Titan.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan's</a> north polar hydrocarbon <a href="/wiki/Lakes_of_Titan" title="Lakes of Titan">seas and lakes</a> as seen in a false-color <i><a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">Cassini</a></i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthetic_aperture_radar" title="Synthetic aperture radar">synthetic aperture radar</a> mosaic.</div></div></div>
<p>Only one world other than Earth is known to harbor large lakes, Saturn's largest moon, <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>. Photographs and spectroscopic analysis by the <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">Cassini–Huygens</a> <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> show liquid <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">ethane</a> on the surface, which is thought to be mixed with liquid <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>. The largest Titanean lake, <a href="/wiki/Kraken_Mare" title="Kraken Mare">Kraken Mare</a> at 400,000 km<sup>2</sup>, is three-times<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the size of any lake on Earth, and even the second, <a href="/wiki/Ligeia_Mare" title="Ligeia Mare">Ligeia Mare</a>, is estimated to be slightly larger than Earth's Lake Michigan–Huron.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>'s large moon <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">Io</a> is volcanically active, and as a result <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> deposits have accumulated on the surface. Some photographs taken during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galileo_spacecraft" title="Galileo spacecraft">Galileo mission</a> appear to show lakes of liquid sulfur in volcanic caldera, though these are more analogous to lake of lava than of water on Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>The planet <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> is too cold and has too little <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> to permit the pooling of liquid water. Geologic evidence appears to confirm, however, that ancient lakes once formed on the surface. It is also possible that volcanic activity on Mars will occasionally melt subsurface ice, creating large temporary lakes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This water would quickly freeze and then sublimate, unless insulated in some manner, such as by a coating of volcanic ash.
</p><p>There are dark basaltic plains on the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, similar to <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">lunar maria</a> but smaller, that are called <i>lacus</i> (singular <i>lacus</i>, <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "lake") because they were thought by early astronomers to be lakes of water.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_lakes_on_Earth">Notable lakes on Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Notable lakes on Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Roundtanglelake.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1116" data-file-width="1479" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Roundtanglelake.JPG/220px-Roundtanglelake.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Roundtanglelake.JPG/330px-Roundtanglelake.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Roundtanglelake.JPG/440px-Roundtanglelake.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Roundtanglelake.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Round Tangle Lake, one of the <a href="/wiki/Tangle_Lakes" title="Tangle Lakes">Tangle Lakes</a>, 2,864 feet (873 m) above sea level in <a href="/wiki/Interior_Alaska" title="Interior Alaska">interior Alaska</a></div></div></div>
<ul><li>The <b>largest lake by surface area</b> is <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a>, which is despite its name considered as a lake from the point of view of geography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Its surface area is 143,000 sq. mi./371,000 km<sup>2</sup>.</li>
<li>The <b>second largest lake by surface area</b> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan-Huron" title="Lake Michigan-Huron">Lake Michigan-Huron</a>, which is hydrologically a single lake. Its surface area is 45,300 sq. mi./117,400 km<sup>2</sup>.  For those who consider Lake Michigan-Huron to be separate lakes, and Caspian Sea to be a sea, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Superior" title="Lake Superior">Lake Superior</a> would be the largest lake at 31,700 sq. mi./82,100 km<sup>2</sup>.</li>
<li>The <b>deepest</b> lake is <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baikal" title="Lake Baikal">Lake Baikal</a> in <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, with a bottom at 1,637 metres (5,371 ft). Its <b>mean depth</b> is also the greatest in the world (749 metres (2,457 ft)).<br/> It is also the world's <b>largest lake by volume</b> (23,600 cubic kilometres (5,700 cu mi), though smaller than the Caspian Sea at 78,200 cubic kilometres (18,800 cu mi)), and the second longest (about 630 kilometres (390 mi) from tip to tip).</li>
<li>The <b>longest</b> lake is <a href="/wiki/Lake_Tanganyika" title="Lake Tanganyika">Lake Tanganyika</a>, with a length of about 660 kilometres (410 mi) (measured along the lake's center line).<br/> It is also the second largest by volume and second deepest (1,470 metres (4,820 ft)) in the world, after lake Baikal.</li>
<li>The world's <b>oldest</b> lake is <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baikal" title="Lake Baikal">Lake Baikal</a>, followed by <a href="/wiki/Lake_Tanganyika" title="Lake Tanganyika">Lake Tanganyika</a> (<a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>). <a href="/wiki/Lake_Maracaibo" title="Lake Maracaibo">Lake Maracaibo</a> is considered by some to be the second-oldest lake on Earth, but since it lies at sea level and nowadays is a contiguous body of water with the sea, others consider that it has turned into a small bay.</li>
<li>The world's <b>highest</b> lake, if size is not a criterion, may be the crater lake of <a href="/wiki/Ojos_del_Salado" title="Ojos del Salado">Ojos del Salado</a>, at 6,390 metres (20,965 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></li>
<li>The highest large (greater than 250 square kilometres (97 sq mi)) lake in the world is the 290 square kilometres (110 sq mi) <a href="/wiki/Lake_Puma_Yumco" title="Lake Puma Yumco">Pumoyong Tso</a> (Pumuoyong Tso), in the <a href="/wiki/Tibet_Autonomous_Region" title="Tibet Autonomous Region">Tibet Autonomous Region</a> of China, at 28-34N 90-24E, 5,018 metres (16,463 ft) above sea level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></li>
<li>The world's <b>highest</b> commercially navigable lake is <a href="/wiki/Lake_Titicaca" title="Lake Titicaca">Lake Titicaca</a> in <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a> at 3,812 m (12,507 ft). It is also the largest lake in South America.</li>
<li>The world's <b>lowest</b> lake is the <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea" title="Dead Sea">Dead Sea</a>, bordered by <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a> to the east and <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> and <a href="/wiki/State_of_Palestine" title="State of Palestine">Palestine</a> to the west, at 418 metres (1,371 ft) below sea level. It is also one of the lakes with highest <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a> concentration.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan%E2%80%93Huron" title="Lake Michigan–Huron">Lake Michigan–Huron</a> has the <b>longest lake coastline</b> in the world: about 5,250 kilometres (3,260 mi), excluding the coastline of its many inner islands. Even if it is considered two lakes, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Huron" title="Lake Huron">Lake Huron</a> alone would still have the longest coastline in the world at 2,980 kilometres (1,850 mi).</li>
<li>The largest island in a lake is <a href="/wiki/Manitoulin_Island" title="Manitoulin Island">Manitoulin Island</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lake_Huron" title="Lake Huron">Lake Huron</a>, with a surface area of 2,766 square kilometres (1,068 sq mi). <a href="/wiki/Lake_Manitou" title="Lake Manitou">Lake Manitou</a>, on Manitoulin Island, is the largest lake on an island in a lake.</li>
<li>The largest lake on an island is <a href="/wiki/Nettilling_Lake" title="Nettilling Lake">Nettilling Lake</a> on <a href="/wiki/Baffin_Island" title="Baffin Island">Baffin Island</a>, with an area of 5,542 square kilometres (2,140 sq mi) and a maximum length of 123 kilometres (76 mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></li>
<li>The largest lake in the world that drains naturally in two directions is <a href="/wiki/Wollaston_Lake" title="Wollaston Lake">Wollaston Lake</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lake_Toba" title="Lake Toba">Lake Toba</a> on the island of <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a> is in what is probably the largest resurgent <a href="/wiki/Caldera" title="Caldera">caldera</a> on Earth.</li>
<li>The largest lake completely within the boundaries of a single city is <a href="/wiki/Lake_Wanapitei" title="Lake Wanapitei">Lake Wanapitei</a> in the city of <a href="/wiki/Greater_Sudbury" title="Greater Sudbury">Sudbury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, Canada. Before the current city boundaries came into effect in 2001, this status was held by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Ramsey" title="Lake Ramsey">Lake Ramsey</a>, also in Sudbury.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lake_Enriquillo" title="Lake Enriquillo">Lake Enriquillo</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a> is the only saltwater lake in the world inhabited by <a href="/wiki/Crocodile" title="Crocodile">crocodiles</a>.</li>
<li>Lake Bernard, Ontario, Canada, claims to be the largest lake in the world with no islands.</li>
<li>The largest lake in one country is <a href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan" title="Lake Michigan">Lake Michigan</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.A</a>. However, it is sometimes considered part of Lake Michigan-Huron, making the record go to <a href="/wiki/Great_Bear_Lake" title="Great Bear Lake">Great Bear Lake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Territories" title="Northwest Territories">Northwest Territories</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, the largest lake within one jurisdiction.</li>
<li>The <b>largest lake on an island in a lake on an island</b> is Crater Lake on Vulcano Island in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Taal" title="Lake Taal">Lake Taal</a> on the island of <a href="/wiki/Luzon" title="Luzon">Luzon</a>, The <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>.</li>
<li>The <b>northernmost named lake</b> on Earth is <a href="/wiki/Upper_Dumbell_Lake" title="Upper Dumbell Lake">Upper Dumbell Lake</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Qikiqtaaluk_Region" title="Qikiqtaaluk Region">Qikiqtaaluk Region</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nunavut" title="Nunavut">Nunavut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> at a latitude of 82°28'N. It is 5.2 kilometres (3.2 mi) southwest of <a href="/wiki/Alert,_Nunavut" title="Alert, Nunavut">Alert</a>, the northernmost settlement in the world. There are also several small lakes north of Upper Dumbell Lake, but they are all unnamed and only appear on very detailed maps.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Largest_by_continent">Largest by continent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Largest by continent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The largest lakes (surface area) by <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continent</a> are:
</p>
<ul><li><b>Australia</b> – <a href="/wiki/Lake_Eyre" title="Lake Eyre">Lake Eyre</a> (salt lake)</li>
<li><b>Africa</b> – <a href="/wiki/Lake_Victoria" title="Lake Victoria">Lake Victoria</a>, also the third-largest freshwater lake on Earth. It is one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Lakes_of_Africa" title="Great Lakes of Africa">Great Lakes of Africa</a>.</li>
<li><b>Antarctica</b> – <a href="/wiki/Lake_Vostok" title="Lake Vostok">Lake Vostok</a> (subglacial)</li>
<li><b>Asia</b> – <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baikal" title="Lake Baikal">Lake Baikal</a> (if the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a> is considered a lake, it is the largest in Eurasia, but is divided between the two geographic continents)</li>
<li><b>Oceania</b> – <a href="/wiki/Lake_Eyre" title="Lake Eyre">Lake Eyre</a> when filled; the largest permanent (and freshwater) lake in Oceania is <a href="/wiki/Lake_Taupo" title="Lake Taupo">Lake Taupo</a>.</li>
<li><b>Europe</b> – <a href="/wiki/Lake_Ladoga" title="Lake Ladoga">Lake Ladoga</a>, followed by <a href="/wiki/Lake_Onega" title="Lake Onega">Lake Onega</a>, both in northwestern Russia.</li>
<li><b>North America</b> – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan-Huron" title="Lake Michigan-Huron">Lake Michigan-Huron</a>, which is hydrologically a single lake. However, lakes <a href="/wiki/Lake_Huron" title="Lake Huron">Huron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan" title="Lake Michigan">Michigan</a> are usually considered separate lakes, in which case <a href="/wiki/Lake_Superior" title="Lake Superior">Lake Superior</a> would be the largest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manivanan2008a_46-2"><a href="#cite_note-Manivanan2008a-46">[46]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>South America</b> – <a href="/wiki/Lake_Titicaca" title="Lake Titicaca">Lake Titicaca</a>, which is also the highest navigable body of water on Earth at 3,812 metres (12,507 ft) above sea level. The much larger <a href="/wiki/Lake_Maracaibo" title="Lake Maracaibo">Lake Maracaibo</a> is much older, but perceived by some to no longer be genuinely a lake for multiple reasons.</li></ul>
