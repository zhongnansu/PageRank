<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Methane clathrate</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:235px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Burning_hydrate_inlay_US_Office_Naval_Research.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="338" data-file-width="233" height="338" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/03/Burning_hydrate_inlay_US_Office_Naval_Research.jpg" width="233"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption">"Burning ice". Methane, released by heating, burns; water drips.<br/> Inset: clathrate structure (University of Göttingen, GZG. Abt. Kristallographie).<br/> Source: <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">United States Geological Survey</a>.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Methane clathrate</b> (CH<sub>4</sub>·5.75H<sub>2</sub>O) or (4CH<sub>4</sub>·23H<sub>2</sub>O), also called <b>methane hydrate</b>, <b>hydromethane</b>, <b>methane ice</b>, <b>fire ice</b>, <b>natural gas hydrate</b>, or <b>gas hydrate</b>, is a solid <a href="/wiki/Clathrate_compound" title="Clathrate compound">clathrate compound</a> (more specifically, a <a href="/wiki/Clathrate_hydrate" title="Clathrate hydrate">clathrate hydrate</a>) in which a large amount of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> is trapped within a <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystal</a> structure of water, forming a solid similar to <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Originally thought to occur only in the outer regions of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, where temperatures are low and water ice is common, significant deposits of methane clathrate have been found under <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediments</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a> floors of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Methane clathrates are common constituents of the shallow marine <a href="/wiki/Geosphere" title="Geosphere">geosphere</a> and they occur in deep <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary</a> structures and form <a href="/wiki/Outcrop" title="Outcrop">outcrops</a> on the ocean floor. Methane hydrates are believed to form by migration of gas from deep along <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)" title="Fault (geology)">geological faults</a>, followed by precipitation or crystallization, on contact of the rising gas stream with cold sea water. In 2008, research on Antarctic <a href="/wiki/Vostok_Station" title="Vostok Station">Vostok</a> and <a href="/wiki/European_Project_for_Ice_Coring_in_Antarctica#Concordia_Station_at_Dome_C" title="European Project for Ice Coring in Antarctica">EPICA Dome C</a> ice cores revealed that methane clathrates were also present in deep <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctic</a> <a href="/wiki/Ice_core" title="Ice core">ice cores</a> and record a history of <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_methane" title="Atmospheric methane">atmospheric methane</a> concentrations, dating to 800,000 years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The ice-core methane clathrate record is a primary source of data for <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> research, along with oxygen and carbon dioxide.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General">General</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methane_clathrate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: General">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Methane hydrates were discovered in Russia in the 1960s, and studies for extracting gas from it emerged at the beginning of the 21st century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCMay17_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBCMay17-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_composition">Structure and composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methane_clathrate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure and composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The nominal methane clathrate hydrate composition is (CH<sub>4</sub>)<sub>4</sub>(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>23</sub>, or 1 <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mole</a> of methane for every 5.75 moles of water, corresponding to 13.4% methane by mass, although the actual composition is dependent on how many methane molecules fit into the various cage structures of the water <a href="/wiki/Crystal_structure" title="Crystal structure">lattice</a>. The observed density is around 0.9 g/cm<sup>3</sup>, which means that methane hydrate will float to the surface of the sea or of a lake unless it is bound in place by being formed in or anchored to sediment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Max_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Max-5">[5]</a></sup> One litre of fully saturated methane clathrate solid would therefore contain about 120 grams of methane (or around 169 litres of methane gas at 0 °C and 1 atm),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[nb 1]</a></sup> or one cubic metre of methane clathrate releases about 160 cubic metres of gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCMay17_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBCMay17-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Methane forms a structure I hydrate with two <a href="/wiki/Dodecahedron" title="Dodecahedron">dodecahedral</a> (12 vertices, thus 12 water molecules) and six <a href="/wiki/Tetradecahedron" title="Tetradecahedron">tetradecahedral</a> (14 water molecules) water cages per unit cell. (Because of sharing of water molecules between cages, there are only 46 water molecules per unit cell.) This compares with a <a href="/wiki/Hydration_number" title="Hydration number">hydration number</a> of 20 for methane in aqueous solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dec_2005_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dec_2005-7">[6]</a></sup> A methane clathrate <a href="/wiki/Magic_angle_spinning" title="Magic angle spinning">MAS NMR</a> spectrum recorded at 275 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a> and 3.1 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">MPa</a> shows a peak for each cage type and a separate peak for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_phase" title="Gas phase">gas phase</a> methane.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 2003, a clay-methane hydrate intercalate was synthesized in which a methane hydrate complex was introduced at the interlayer of a sodium-rich <a href="/wiki/Montmorillonite" title="Montmorillonite">montmorillonite</a> clay. The upper temperature stability of this phase is similar to that of structure I hydrate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guggenheim_2003_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guggenheim_2003-8">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

The horizontal axis shows temperature from -15 to 33 Celsius, the vertical axis shows pressure from 0 to 120,000 kilopascals (0 to 1,184 atmospheres). Hydrate forms above the line. For example, at 4 Celsius hydrate forms above a pressure of about 50 atm/5000 kPa, found at about 500m sea depth.</div></div></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_deposits">Natural deposits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methane_clathrate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Natural deposits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methane_hydrate_stability_zone" title="Methane hydrate stability zone">Methane hydrate stability zone</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gas_hydrates_1996.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="477" data-file-width="940" height="112" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Gas_hydrates_1996.svg/220px-Gas_hydrates_1996.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Gas_hydrates_1996.svg/330px-Gas_hydrates_1996.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Gas_hydrates_1996.svg/440px-Gas_hydrates_1996.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gas_hydrates_1996.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Worldwide distribution of confirmed or inferred offshore gas hydrate-bearing sediments, 1996.<br/>Source: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USGS" title="USGS">USGS</a></div></div></div>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gashydrat_mit_Struktur.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="750" data-file-width="745" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Gashydrat_mit_Struktur.jpg/220px-Gashydrat_mit_Struktur.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Gashydrat_mit_Struktur.jpg/330px-Gashydrat_mit_Struktur.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Gashydrat_mit_Struktur.jpg/440px-Gashydrat_mit_Struktur.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gashydrat_mit_Struktur.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Specific structure of a gas hydrate piece, from the subduction zone off Oregon</div></div></div>
<p>Methane clathrates are restricted to the shallow <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a> (i.e. &lt; 2,000 m depth). Furthermore, necessary conditions are found only in either continental <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rocks</a> in polar regions where average surface temperatures are less than 0 °C; or in oceanic <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a> at water depths greater than 300 m where the <a href="/wiki/Water_mass" title="Water mass">bottom water</a> temperature is around 2 °C. In addition, deep fresh water lakes may host gas hydrates as well, e.g. the fresh water <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baikal" title="Lake Baikal">Lake Baikal</a>, Siberia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vanneste_2001_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vanneste_2001-9">[8]</a></sup> Continental deposits have been located in <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siltstone" title="Siltstone">siltstone</a> beds at less than 800 m depth. Oceanic deposits seem to be widespread in the <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a> (see Fig.) and can occur within the sediments at depth or close to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sediment-water_interface" title="Sediment-water interface">sediment-water interface</a>. They may cap even larger deposits of gaseous methane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kvenvolden_1995_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kvenvolden_1995-10">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceanic">Oceanic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methane_clathrate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Oceanic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are two distinct types of oceanic deposit.  The most common is dominated (&gt; 99%) by <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> contained in a structure I <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clathrate" title="Clathrate">clathrate</a> and generally found at depth in the sediment.  Here, the methane is isotopically light (<a href="/wiki/%CE%9413C" title="Δ13C">δ<sup>13</sup>C</a> &lt; −60‰), which indicates that it is derived from the microbial <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduction</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a>.  The clathrates in these deep deposits are thought to have formed in situ from the microbially produced methane, since the δ<sup>13</sup>C values of clathrate and surrounding dissolved methane are similar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kvenvolden_1995_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kvenvolden_1995-10">[9]</a></sup>  However, it is also thought that fresh water used in the pressurization of oil and gas wells in permafrost and along the continental shelves worldwide combines with natural methane to form clathrate at depth and pressure, since methane hydrates are more stable in fresh water than in salt water.  Local variations may be very common, since the act of forming hydrate, which extracts pure water from saline formation waters, can often lead to local, and potentially significant, increases in formation water salinity.  Hydrates normally exclude the salt in the pore fluid from which it forms, thus they exhibit high electric resistivity just like ice, and sediments containing hydrates have a higher resistivity compared to sediments without gas hydrates (Judge [67]).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MH_future_2011_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-MH_future_2011-11">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>9</span></sup>
</p><p>These deposits are located within a mid-depth zone around 300–500 m thick in the sediments (the <a href="/wiki/Gas_hydrate_stability_zone" title="Gas hydrate stability zone">gas hydrate stability zone</a>, or GHSZ) where they coexist with methane dissolved in the fresh, not salt, pore-waters. Above this zone methane is only present in its dissolved form at concentrations that decrease towards the sediment surface. Below it, methane is gaseous. At <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blake_Ridge" title="Blake Ridge">Blake Ridge</a> on the Atlantic <a href="/wiki/Continental_rise" title="Continental rise">continental rise</a>, the GHSZ started at 190 m depth and continued to 450 m, where it reached <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phase_equilibrium" title="Phase equilibrium">equilibrium</a> with the gaseous phase. Measurements indicated that methane occupied 0-9% by volume in the GHSZ, and ~12% in the gaseous zone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dickens_1997_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dickens_1997-12">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sautter2012_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-sautter2012-13">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the less common second type found near the sediment surface some samples have a higher proportion of longer-chain <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a> (&lt; 99% methane) contained in a structure II clathrate. Carbon from this type of clathrate is isotopically heavier (<a href="/wiki/Carbon-13" title="Carbon-13">δ<sup>13</sup>C</a> is −29 to −57 ‰) and is thought to have migrated upwards from deep sediments, where methane was formed by thermal decomposition of <a href="/wiki/Organic_matter" title="Organic matter">organic matter</a>. Examples of this type of deposit have been found in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kvenvolden_1995_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kvenvolden_1995-10">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some deposits have characteristics intermediate between the microbially and thermally sourced types and are considered to be formed from a mixture of the two.
</p><p>The methane in gas hydrates is dominantly generated by microbial consortia degrading organic matter in low oxygen environments, with the methane itself produced by <a href="/wiki/Methanogen" title="Methanogen">methanogenic</a> <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a>. Organic matter in the uppermost few centimetres of sediments is first attacked by aerobic bacteria, generating CO<sub>2</sub>, which escapes from the sediments into the <a href="/wiki/Water_column" title="Water column">water column</a>. Below this region of aerobic activity, anaerobic processes take over, including, successively with depth, the microbial reduction of nitrite/nitrate, metal oxides, and then <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfates</a> are reduced to <a href="/wiki/Sulfide" title="Sulfide">sulfides</a>.  Finally, once sulfate is used up, methanogenesis becomes a dominant pathway for organic carbon <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Remineralization" title="Remineralization">remineralization</a>.
</p><p>If the sedimentation rate is low (about 1 cm/yr), the organic carbon content is low (about 1% ), and oxygen is abundant, aerobic bacteria can use up all the organic matter in the sediments faster than oxygen is depleted, so lower-energy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_acceptors" title="Electron acceptors">electron acceptors</a> are not used. But where sedimentation rates and the organic carbon content are high, which is typically the case on continental shelves and beneath western boundary current upwelling zones, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pore_water" title="Pore water">pore water</a> in the sediments becomes <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(environmental)" title="Hypoxia (environmental)">anoxic</a> at depths of only a few centimeters or less.  In such organic-rich marine sediments, sulfate then becomes the most important terminal electron acceptor due to its high concentration in <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a>, although it too is depleted by a depth of centimeters to meters.  Below this, methane is produced. This production of methane is a rather complicated process, requiring a highly reducing environment (Eh −350 to −450 mV) and a pH between 6 and 8, as well as a complex <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syntrophic" title="Syntrophic">syntrophic</a> consortia of different varieties of archaea and bacteria, although it is only archaea that actually emit methane.
</p><p>In some regions (e.g., Gulf of Mexico) methane in clathrates may be at least partially derived from thermal degradation of organic matter, dominantly in petroleum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The methane in clathrates typically has a biogenic isotopic signature and highly variable δ<sup>13</sup>C (−40 to −100‰), with an approximate average of about −65‰ .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matsumoto_1995_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Matsumoto_1995-17">[16]</a></sup> Below the zone of solid clathrates, large volumes of methane may form bubbles of free gas in the sediments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dickens_1997_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dickens_1997-12">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matsumoto_1996_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Matsumoto_1996-18">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>The presence of clathrates at a given site can often be determined by observation of a "bottom simulating reflector" (BSR), which is a seismic reflection at the sediment to clathrate stability zone interface caused by the unequal densities of normal sediments and those laced with clathrates.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Gas_hydrate_pingo" title="Gas hydrate pingo">Gas hydrate pingos</a>, have been discovered in the Arctic oceans Barents sea. Methane is bubbling from these dome like structures, with some of these gas flares extending close to the sea surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Reservoir_size">Reservoir size</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methane_clathrate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reservoir size">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The size of the oceanic methane clathrate reservoir is poorly known, and estimates of its size decreased by roughly an <a href="/wiki/Order_of_magnitude" title="Order of magnitude">order of magnitude</a> per decade since it was first recognized that clathrates could exist in the oceans during the 1960s and 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Milkov_2004_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Milkov_2004-21">[20]</a></sup>  The highest estimates (e.g. 3<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">18</span></sup> m<sup>3</sup>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trofimuk_1973_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trofimuk_1973-22">[21]</a></sup> were based on the assumption that fully dense clathrates could litter the entire floor of the deep ocean. Improvements in our understanding of clathrate chemistry and sedimentology have revealed that hydrates form in only a narrow range of depths (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_shelves" title="Continental shelves">continental shelves</a>), at only some locations in the range of depths where they could occur (10-30% of the <a href="/wiki/Gas_hydrate_stability_zone" title="Gas hydrate stability zone">Gas hydrate stability zone</a>), and typically are found at low concentrations (0.9–1.5% by volume) at sites where they do occur. Recent estimates constrained by direct sampling suggest the global inventory occupies between 1<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">15</span></sup>and 5<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">15</span></sup> m<sup>3</sup> (0.24 to 1.2 million cubic miles).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Milkov_2004_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Milkov_2004-21">[20]</a></sup>  This estimate, corresponding to 500–2500 gigatonnes carbon (Gt C), is smaller than the 5000 Gt C estimated for all other geo-organic fuel reserves but substantially larger than the ~230 Gt C estimated for other natural gas sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Milkov_2004_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Milkov_2004-21">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_2000_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS_2000-23">[22]</a></sup>  The permafrost reservoir has been estimated at about 400 Gt C in the Arctic,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> but no estimates have been made of possible Antarctic reservoirs. These are large amounts. In comparison, the total carbon in the atmosphere is around 800 gigatons (see <a href="/wiki/Carbon#Occurrence" title="Carbon">Carbon: Occurrence</a>).
</p><p>These modern estimates are notably smaller than the 10,000 to 11,000 Gt C (2<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">16</span></sup> m<sup>3</sup>) proposed<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup> by previous researchers as a reason to consider clathrates to be a geo-organic fuel resource (MacDonald 1990, Kvenvolden 1998). Lower abundances of clathrates do not rule out their economic potential, but a lower total volume and apparently low concentration at most sites<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Milkov_2004_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-Milkov_2004-21">[20]</a></sup> does suggest that only a limited percentage of clathrates deposits may provide an economically viable resource.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Continental">Continental</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methane_clathrate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Continental">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Methane clathrates in continental rocks are trapped in beds of <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> or <a href="/wiki/Siltstone" title="Siltstone">siltstone</a> at depths of less than 800 m. Sampling indicates they are formed from a mix of thermally and microbially derived gas from which the heavier hydrocarbons were later selectively removed. These occur in <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Canada" title="Northern Canada">Northern Canada</a>.
</p><p>In 2008, Canadian and Japanese researchers extracted a constant stream of natural gas from a test project at the <a href="/wiki/Mallik_gas_hydrate_site" title="Mallik gas hydrate site">Mallik gas hydrate site</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Mackenzie_River" title="Mackenzie River">Mackenzie River</a> delta. This was the second such drilling at Mallik: the first took place in 2002 and used heat to release methane. In the 2008  experiment, researchers were able to extract gas by lowering the pressure, without heating, requiring significantly less energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup> The Mallik gas hydrate field was first discovered by <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Oil" title="Imperial Oil">Imperial Oil</a> in 1971-1972.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_use">Commercial use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methane_clathrate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Commercial use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Economic deposits of hydrate are termed Natural Gas Hydrate (NGH) and are unique in that they store 164 m<sup>3</sup> of methane, 0.8 m<sup>3</sup> water in 1 m<sup>3</sup> hydrate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup>  Most NGH is found beneath the seafloor (95%) where it exists in thermodynamic equilibrium.  The sedimentary methane hydrate reservoir probably contains 2–10 times the currently known reserves of conventional <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, as of  2013<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methane_clathrate&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ta201304_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-ta201304-29">[28]</a></sup> This represents a potentially important future source of <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a> <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a>. However, in the majority of sites deposits are thought to be too dispersed for economic extraction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Milkov_2004_21-4"><a href="#cite_note-Milkov_2004-21">[20]</a></sup> Other problems facing commercial exploitation are detection of viable reserves and development of the technology for extracting methane gas from the hydrate deposits.
</p><p>In August 2006, China announced plans to spend 800 million yuan (US$100 million) over the next 10 years to study natural gas hydrates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup> A potentially economic reserve in the Gulf of Mexico may contain approximately 100 billion cubic metres (3.5<span style="margin-left:0.2em">×<span style="margin-left:0.1em">10</span></span><s style="display:none">^</s><sup>12</sup> cu ft) of gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Milkov_2004_21-5"><a href="#cite_note-Milkov_2004-21">[20]</a></sup> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bj%C3%B8rn_Kvamme&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bjørn Kvamme (page does not exist)">Bjørn Kvamme</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Arne_Graue&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Arne Graue (page does not exist)">Arne Graue</a> at the Institute for Physics and technology at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bergen" title="University of Bergen">University of Bergen</a> have developed a method for injecting CO<sub>2</sub> into hydrates and reversing the process; thereby extracting CH<sub>4</sub> by direct exchange.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup> The University of Bergen's method is being field tested by <a href="/wiki/ConocoPhillips" title="ConocoPhillips">ConocoPhillips</a> and state-owned <a href="/wiki/Japan_Oil,_Gas_and_Metals_National_Corporation" title="Japan Oil, Gas and Metals National Corporation">Japan Oil, Gas and Metals National Corporation</a> (JOGMEC), and partially funded by the U.S. Department of Energy. The project has already reached injection phase and was analyzing resulting data by March 12, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>On March 12, 2013, JOGMEC researchers announced that they had successfully extracted natural gas from frozen methane hydrate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extracts_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-extracts-33">[32]</a></sup>  In order to extract the gas, specialized equipment was used to drill into and depressurize the hydrate deposits, causing the methane to separate from the ice.  The gas was then collected and piped to surface where it was ignited to prove its presence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coup_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-coup-34">[33]</a></sup> According to an industry spokesperson, "It [was] the world's first offshore experiment producing gas from methane hydrate".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extracts_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-extracts-33">[32]</a></sup>  Previously, gas had been extracted from onshore deposits, but never from offshore deposits which are much more common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coup_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-coup-34">[33]</a></sup> The hydrate field from which the gas was extracted is located 50 kilometres (31 mi) from central Japan in the <a href="/wiki/Nankai_Trough" title="Nankai Trough">Nankai Trough</a>, 300 metres (980 ft) under the sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extracts_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-extracts-33">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coup_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-coup-34">[33]</a></sup>  A spokesperson for JOGMEC remarked "Japan could finally have an energy source to call its own".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coup_34-3"><a href="#cite_note-coup-34">[33]</a></sup> The experiment will continue for two weeks before it is determined how efficient the gas extraction process has been.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coup_34-4"><a href="#cite_note-coup-34">[33]</a></sup>  Marine geologist Mikio Satoh remarked "Now we know that extraction is possible. The next step is to see how far Japan can get costs down to make the technology economically viable."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coup_34-5"><a href="#cite_note-coup-34">[33]</a></sup>  Japan estimates that there are at least 1.1 trillion cubic meters of methane trapped in the Nankai Trough, enough to meet the country's needs for more than ten years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coup_34-6"><a href="#cite_note-coup-34">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>Both Japan and China announced in May 2017 a breakthrough for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resource_extraction" title="Resource extraction">mining</a> methane clathrates, when they extracted methane from hydrates in the <a href="/wiki/South_China_Sea" title="South China Sea">South China Sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCMay17_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-BBCMay17-4">[4]</a></sup>  However, industry consensus is that commercial-scale production remains years away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_concerns">Environmental concerns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methane_clathrate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Environmental concerns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Experts caution that environmental impacts are still being investigated and that methane − a greenhouse gas with around 25 times as much <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_potential" title="Global warming potential">global warming potential</a> over a 100-year period (GWP100), as carbon dioxide − could potentially escape into the atmosphere if something goes wrong. Furthermore, while cleaner than coal, burning natural gas also creates carbon emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrates_in_natural_gas_processing">Hydrates in natural gas processing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methane_clathrate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Hydrates in natural gas processing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Routine_operations">Routine operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methane_clathrate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Routine operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Methane clathrates (hydrates) are also commonly formed during natural gas production operations, when liquid water is condensed in the presence of methane at high pressure. It is known that larger hydrocarbon molecules like ethane and propane can also form hydrates, although longer molecules (butanes, pentanes) cannot fit into the water cage structure and tend to destabilise the formation of hydrates.
</p><p>Once formed, hydrates can block pipeline and processing equipment. They are generally then removed by reducing the pressure, heating them, or dissolving them by chemical means (methanol is commonly used). Care must be taken to ensure that the removal of the hydrates is carefully controlled, because of the potential for the hydrate to undergo a phase transition from the solid hydrate to release water and gaseous methane at a high rate when the pressure is reduced. The rapid release of methane gas in a closed system can result in a rapid increase in pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Max_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Max-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is generally preferable to prevent hydrates from forming or blocking equipment. This is commonly achieved by removing water, or by the addition of <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">ethylene glycol</a> (MEG) or <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>, which act to depress the temperature at which hydrates will form (i.e. common <a href="/wiki/Antifreeze" title="Antifreeze">antifreeze</a>). In recent years, development of other forms of hydrate inhibitors have been developed, like Kinetic Hydrate Inhibitors (which by far slow the rate of hydrate formation) and anti-agglomerates, which do not prevent hydrates forming, but do prevent them sticking together to block equipment.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_of_hydrate_phase_transition_during_deep_water_drilling">Effect of hydrate phase transition during deep water drilling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methane_clathrate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Effect of hydrate phase transition during deep water drilling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When drilling in oil- and gas-bearing formations submerged in deep water, the reservoir gas may flow into the well bore and form gas hydrates owing to the low temperatures and high pressures found during deep water drilling. The gas hydrates may then flow upward with drilling mud or other discharged fluids. When the hydrates rise, the pressure in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annulus_(oil_well)" title="Annulus (oil well)">annulus</a> decreases and the hydrates dissociate into gas and water. The rapid gas expansion ejects fluid from the well, reducing the pressure further, which leads to more hydrate dissociation and further fluid ejection. The resulting violent expulsion of fluid from the annulus is one potential cause or contributor to the "kick".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wang_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wang-39">[38]</a></sup> (Kicks, which can cause blowouts, typically do not involve hydrates: see <a href="/wiki/Blowout_(well_drilling)#Formation_kick" title="Blowout (well drilling)">Blowout: formation kick</a>).
</p><p>Measures which reduce the risk of hydrate formation include:
</p>
<ul><li>High flow-rates, which limit the time for hydrate formation in a volume of fluid, thereby reducing the kick potential.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wang_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wang-39">[38]</a></sup></li>
<li>Careful measuring of line flow to detect incipient hydrate plugging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wang_39-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wang-39">[38]</a></sup></li>
<li>Additional care in measuring when gas production rates are low and the possibility of hydrate formation is higher than at relatively high gas flow rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wang_39-3"><a href="#cite_note-Wang-39">[38]</a></sup></li>
<li>Monitoring of <a href="/wiki/Casing_(borehole)" title="Casing (borehole)">well casing</a> after it is "<a href="/wiki/Shut-in_(oil_drilling)" title="Shut-in (oil drilling)">shut in</a>" (isolated) may indicate hydrate formation. Following "shut in", the pressure rises while gas diffuses through the reservoir to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bore_hole" title="Bore hole">bore hole</a>; the rate of pressure rise exhibit a reduced rate of increase while hydrates are forming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wang_39-4"><a href="#cite_note-Wang-39">[38]</a></sup></li>
<li>Additions of energy (e.g., the energy released by <a href="/wiki/Casing_(borehole)#Cementing" title="Casing (borehole)">setting cement</a> used in well completion) can raise the temperature and convert hydrates to gas, producing a "kick".</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blowout_recovery">Blowout recovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methane_clathrate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Blowout recovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BP_oil_containment_domes.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="320" data-file-width="490" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/BP_oil_containment_domes.jpg/220px-BP_oil_containment_domes.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/BP_oil_containment_domes.jpg/330px-BP_oil_containment_domes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/BP_oil_containment_domes.jpg/440px-BP_oil_containment_domes.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BP_oil_containment_domes.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Concept diagram of oil containment domes, forming upsidedown funnels in order to pipe oil to surface ships. The sunken oil rig is nearby.</div></div></div>
<p>At sufficient depths, methane complexes directly with water to form methane hydrates, as was observed during the <a href="/wiki/Deepwater_Horizon_oil_spill" title="Deepwater Horizon oil spill">Deepwater Horizon oil spill</a> in 2010. BP engineers developed and deployed a subsea oil recovery system over oil spilling from a deepwater <a href="/wiki/Oil_well" title="Oil well">oil well</a> 5,000 feet (1,500 m) below <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a> to capture escaping oil. This involved placing a 125-tonne (276,000 lb) dome over the largest of the well leaks and piping it to a storage vessel on the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ0503_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ0503-40">[39]</a></sup> This option had the potential to collect some 85% of the leaking oil but was previously untested at such depths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ0503_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ0503-40">[39]</a></sup> BP deployed the system on May 7–8, but it failed due to buildup of methane clathrate inside the dome; with its low density of approximately 0.9 g/cm<sup>3</sup> the methane hydrates accumulated in the dome, adding buoyancy and obstructing flow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methane_clathrates_and_climate_change">Methane clathrates and climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methane_clathrate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Methane clathrates and climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Clathrate_gun_hypothesis" title="Clathrate gun hypothesis">Clathrate gun hypothesis</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">Permafrost</a></div>
<p>Methane is a powerful <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a>. Despite its short atmospheric <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Half_life" title="Half life">half life</a> of 12 years, methane has a <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_potential" title="Global warming potential">global warming potential</a> of 86 over 20 years and 34 over 100 years (IPCC, 2013). The sudden release of large amounts of natural gas from methane clathrate deposits has been hypothesized as a cause of past and possibly future <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> changes. Events possibly linked in this way are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Permian-Triassic_extinction_event" title="Permian-Triassic extinction event">Permian-Triassic extinction event</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleocene-Eocene_Thermal_Maximum" title="Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum">Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum</a>.
</p><p>Climate scientists like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_E._Hansen" title="James E. Hansen">James E. Hansen</a> predict that methane clathrates in the <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">permafrost</a> regions will be released because of global warming, unleashing powerful feedback forces which may cause <a href="/wiki/Runaway_climate_change" title="Runaway climate change">runaway climate change</a> that cannot be halted.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Research carried out in 2008 in the Siberian Arctic found millions of tonnes of methane being released<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup> with concentrations in some regions reaching up to 100 times above normal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>In their Correspondence in the September 2013 <i>Nature Geoscience</i> journal, Vonk and Gustafsson cautioned that the most probable mechanism to strengthen global warming is large-scale thawing of Arctic permafrost which will release methane clathrate into the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vonk_Gustafsson_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vonk_Gustafsson-48">[47]</a></sup> While performing research in July in plumes in the East Siberian Arctic Ocean, Gustafsson and Vonk were surprised by the high concentration of methane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EastSiberia_2014_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-EastSiberia_2014-49">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2014 based on their research on the northern United States Atlantic marine continental margins from <a href="/wiki/Cape_Hatteras" title="Cape Hatteras">Cape Hatteras</a> to <a href="/wiki/Georges_Bank" title="Georges Bank">Georges Bank</a>, a group of scientists from the US Geological Survey, the Department of Geosciences, Mississippi State University, Department of Geological Sciences, Brown University and Earth Resources Technology, claimed there was widespread leakage of methane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2014_US_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-2014_US-50">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Widespread_methane_leakage_2014_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Widespread_methane_leakage_2014-51">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>Scientists from the Center for Arctic Gas Hydrate (CAGE), Environment and Climate at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_University_of_Norway" title="Arctic University of Norway">Arctic University of Norway</a>, published a study in June 2017, describing over a hundred ocean sediment craters, some 3,000 meters wide and up to 300 meters deep, formed due to explosive eruptions, attributed to destabilizing methane hydrates, following ice-sheet retreat during the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last glacial period</a>, around 12,000 years ago, a few centuries after the <a href="/wiki/B%C3%B8lling-Aller%C3%B8d_warming" title="Bølling-Allerød warming">Bølling-Allerød warming</a>. These areas around the <a href="/wiki/Barents_Sea" title="Barents Sea">Barents Sea</a>, still seep methane today, and still existing bulges with methane reservoirs could eventually have the same fate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[51]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_gas_hydrates_versus_liquified_natural_gas_in_transportation">Natural gas hydrates versus liquified natural gas in transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methane_clathrate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Natural gas hydrates versus liquified natural gas in transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since methane clathrates are stable at a higher temperature than <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_natural_gas" title="Liquefied natural gas">liquefied natural gas (LNG)</a> (−20 vs −162 °C), there is some interest in converting natural gas into clathrates rather than liquifying it when transporting it by <a href="/wiki/LNG_carrier" title="LNG carrier">seagoing vessels</a>. A significant advantage would be that the production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_gas_hydrate" title="Natural gas hydrate">natural gas hydrate (NGH)</a> from natural gas at the terminal would require a smaller refrigeration plant and less energy than LNG would. Offsetting this, for 100 tonnes of methane transported, 750 tonnes of methane hydrate would have to be transported; since this would require a ship of 7.5 times greater displacement, or require more ships, it is unlikely to prove economically feasible.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">
<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-6"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-6">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The average methane clathrate hydrate composition is 1 <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mole</a> of methane for every 5.75 moles of water. The observed density is around 0.9 g/cm<sup>3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Max_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Max-5">[5]</a></sup> For one mole of methane, which has a molar mass of about 16.043 g (see <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">Methane</a>), we have 5.75 moles of water, with a molar mass of about 18.015 g (see <a href="/wiki/Properties_of_water" title="Properties of water">Properties of water</a>), so together for each mole of methane the clathrate complex has a mass of <span class="nowrap">16.043 g + 5.75 × 18.015 g</span> ≈ 119.631 g. The fractional contribution of methane to the mass is then equal to <span class="nowrap">16.043 g / 119.631 g</span> ≈ 0.1341. The density is around 0.9 g/cm<sup>3</sup>, so one litre of methane clathrate has a mass of around 0.9 kg, and the mass of the methane contained therein is then about <span class="nowrap">0.1341 × 0.9 kg</span> ≈ 0.1207 kg. At a density as a gas of 0.716 kg/m<sup>3</sup> (at 0 °C; see the info box at <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">Methane</a>), this comes to a volume of <span class="nowrap">0.1207 / 0.716 m<sup>3</sup></span> = 0.1686 m<sup>3</sup> = 168.6 L.</span>
</li>
</ol></div></div>
