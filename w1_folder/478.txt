<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Sabatier reaction</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>Sabatier reaction</b> or <b>Sabatier process</b> was discovered by the <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">chemist</a> <a href="/wiki/Paul_Sabatier_(chemist)" title="Paul Sabatier (chemist)">Paul Sabatier</a> in 1897. It involves the reaction of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> at elevated temperatures (optimally 300–400 °C) and pressures in the presence of a <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> and water. Optionally, <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium" title="Ruthenium">ruthenium</a> on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alumina" title="Alumina">alumina</a> (aluminium oxide) makes a more efficient catalyst. It is described by the following <a href="/wiki/Exothermic_reaction" title="Exothermic reaction">exothermic reaction</a>:

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_storage">Energy storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sabatier_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Energy storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Carbon-neutral_fuel" title="Carbon-neutral fuel">Carbon-neutral fuel</a></div>
<p>It has been seen in a renewable-energy-dominated energy system to use the excess electricity generated by wind, solar photovoltaic, hydro, marine current, etc. to make hydrogen via water electrolysis and the subsequent application of the Sabatier reaction to make methane <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
In contrast to a direct usage of hydrogen for transport or energy storage applications,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-energy_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-energy-3">[3]</a></sup> the methane can be injected into the existing gas network, which in many countries has one to two years of storage capacity <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>. The methane can then be used on demand to generate electricity (and heat—combined heat and power) overcoming low points of renewable energy production. The process is electrolysis of water by electricity to create hydrogen (which can partly be used directly in fuel cells) and the addition of carbon dioxide CO<sub>2</sub> (Sabatier process) to create methane. The CO<sub>2</sub> can be extracted from the air or fossil fuel waste gases by the <a href="/wiki/Amine_gas_treating" title="Amine gas treating">amine process</a>, amongst many others. It is a low-CO<sub>2</sub> system, and has similar efficiencies of today's energy system.
A 6 MW <a href="/wiki/Power-to-gas" title="Power-to-gas">power-to-gas</a> plant went into production in Germany in 2013, and powered a fleet of 1500 Audi A3s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_Space_Station_life_support">International Space Station life support</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sabatier_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: International Space Station life support">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Oxygen generators on board the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> produce oxygen from water using <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water" title="Electrolysis of water">electrolysis</a>; the hydrogen produced was previously discarded into space. As astronauts consume oxygen, carbon dioxide is produced, which must then be removed from the air and discarded as well. This approach required copious amounts of water to be regularly transported to the space station for oxygen generation in addition to that used for human consumption, hygiene, and other uses—a luxury that will not be available to future long-duration missions beyond <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">low Earth orbit</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> is using the Sabatier reaction to recover water from exhaled carbon dioxide and the hydrogen previously discarded from electrolysis on the International Space Station and possibly for future missions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The other resulting chemical, methane, is released into space. As half of the input hydrogen becomes wasted as methane, additional hydrogen is supplied from Earth to make up the difference. However, this creates a nearly-closed cycle between water, oxygen, and carbon dioxide which only requires a relatively modest amount of imported hydrogen to maintain.

<p>The Bosch reaction would present a completely closed hydrogen and oxygen cycle which only produces atomic carbon as waste. However, difficulties maintaining its temperature of up to 600 °C and properly handling carbon deposits mean significantly more research will be required before a Bosch reactor could become a reality. One problem is that the production of elemental carbon tends to foul the catalyst's surface (coking), which is detrimental to the reaction's efficiency.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacturing_propellant_on_Mars">Manufacturing propellant on Mars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sabatier_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Manufacturing propellant on Mars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Sabatier reaction has been proposed as a key step in reducing the cost of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manned" title="Manned">manned</a> exploration of Mars (<a href="/wiki/Mars_Direct" title="Mars Direct">Mars Direct</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interplanetary_Transport_System" title="Interplanetary Transport System">Interplanetary Transport System</a>) through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In-situ_resource_utilization" title="In-situ resource utilization">in-situ resource utilization</a>. Hydrogen is combined with CO<sub>2</sub> from the atmosphere, with methane then stored as fuel and the water side product <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water" title="Electrolysis of water">electrolyzed</a> yielding oxygen to be liquefied and stored as oxidizer and hydrogen to be recycled back into the reactor. The original hydrogen could be transported from Earth or separated from Martian sources of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>A variation of the basic Sabatier methanation reaction may be used via a mixed catalyst bed and a reverse water gas shift in a single reactor to produce methane from the raw materials available on Mars, utilizing carbon dioxide in the Martian atmosphere and water extracted from the Martian subsoil or atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> A 2011 prototype test operation that harvested CO<sub>2</sub> from a simulated Martian atmosphere and reacted it with H<sub>2</sub>, produced methane rocket propellant at a rate of 1 kg/day, operating autonomously for 5 consecutive days, maintaining a nearly 100% conversion rate. An optimized system of this design massing 50 kg "is projected to produce 1 kg/day of O<sub>2</sub>:CH<sub>4</sub> propellant ... with a methane purity of 98+% while consuming 700 Watts of electrical power."  Overall unit conversion rate expected from the optimized system is one <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonne</a> of propellant per 17 MWh energy input.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zubrin20121215_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-zubrin20121215-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
