<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Global hectare</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>global hectare</b> (gha) is a measurement unit for the <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a> of people or activities and the <a href="/wiki/Biocapacity" title="Biocapacity">biocapacity</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">earth</a> or its regions. One global hectare is the world's annual amount of biological production for human use and human waste assimilation, per hectare of biologically productive land and fisheries.
</p><p>It measures production and consumption of different products. It starts with the total biological production and waste assimilation in the world, including crops, forests (both wood production and CO2 absorption), grazing and fishing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-borucke2013_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-borucke2013-1">[1]</a></sup>
The total of these kinds of production, weighted by the richness of the land they use,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-borucke2013_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-borucke2013-1">[1]</a></sup> is divided by the number of hectares used. The calculation does not include <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">deserts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_ocean" title="Open ocean">open ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>"Global hectares per person" refers to the amount of production and waste assimilation per person on the planet. In 2012 there were approximately 12.2 billion global hectares of production and waste assimilation, averaging 1.7 global hectares per person.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lpr2016_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-lpr2016-3">[3]</a></sup>
Consumption totaled 20.1 billion global hectares or 2.8 global hectares per person, meaning about 65% more was consumed than produced. This is possible because there are natural reserves all around the globe that function as backup food, material and energy supplies, although only for a relatively short period of time. Due to rapid population growth, these reserves are being depleted at an ever increasing tempo. See <a href="/wiki/Earth_Overshoot_Day" title="Earth Overshoot Day">Earth Overshoot Day</a>.
</p><p>Opponents and defenders of the concept have discussed its strengths and weaknesses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-question_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-question-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_hectare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The global hectare is a useful measure of biocapacity as it can convert things like <a href="/wiki/Human#Diet" title="Human">human dietary requirements</a> into common units, which can show how many <a href="/wiki/People" title="People">people</a> a certain region on earth can sustain, assuming current <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technologies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agricultural</a> methods. It can be used as a way of determining the relative carrying capacity of the earth.
</p><p>Different hectares of land can provide different amounts of global hectares. For example, a hectare of lush area with high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainfall" title="Rainfall">rainfall</a> would scale higher in global hectares than would a hectare of desert.
</p><p>It can also be used to show that consuming different foods may increase the earth's ability to support larger populations. To illustrate, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meat_production" title="Meat production">producing meat</a> generally requires more land and energy than what producing vegetables requires; sustaining a meat-based diet would require a less populated planet.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hectare_equivalents">Hectare equivalents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_hectare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hectare equivalents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On average, a global hectare can be produced in the area of a standard hectare. A <a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">hectare</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'h' in 'hi'">h</span><span title="/ɛ/: 'e' in 'dress'">ɛ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ɛər/: 'are' in 'bare'">ɛər</span></span>/</a></span></span>; symbol <b>ha</b>) is a unit of area equal to 10,000 square metres (107,639 sq ft) (a square 100 metres on each side or 328 feet on each side), 2.471 acres, 0.01 square kilometers, 0.00386102 square miles, or one square <a href="/wiki/Hectometre" title="Hectometre">hectometre</a> (100 metres squared).
</p>
