<div class="quotebox-title" style=""><a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Pure_and_Applied_Chemistry" title="International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry"> IUPAC</a> definition</div>
<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">Solid in which components are compatible and form a unique phase.
<p><i>Note 1</i>: The definition “crystal containing a second constituent which fits into and<br/>is distributed in the lattice of the host crystal” given in refs.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is not general<br/>and, thus, is not recommended.
</p><p><i>Note 2</i>: The expression is to be used to describe a solid phase containing<br/>more than one substance when, for convenience, one (or more) of the substances,<br/>called the solvent, is treated differently from the other substances, called solutes.
</p><p><i>Note 3</i>: One or several of the components can be <i>macromolecules</i>. Some of<br/>the other components can then act as plasticizers, i.e., as molecularly dispersed<br/>substances that decrease the glass-transition temperature at which the amorphous<br/>phase of a <i>polymer</i> is converted between glassy and rubbery states.
</p><p><i>Note 4</i>: In pharmaceutical preparations, the concept of solid solution is often<br/>applied to the case of mixtures of <i>drug</i> and <i>polymer</i>.
</p>
<i>Note 5</i>: The number of drug molecules that do behave as solvent (plasticizer)<br/>of polymers is small.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></div>
</div>
<p>A <b>solid solution</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solid</a>-<a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">state</a> <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solution</a> of one or more <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">solutes</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvent</a>. Such a multi-component system is considered a solution rather than a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">compound</a> when the <a href="/wiki/Crystal_structure" title="Crystal structure">crystal structure</a> of the solvent remains unchanged by addition of the solutes, and when the chemical components remain in a single homogeneous phase. This often happens when the two elements (generally <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metals</a>) involved are close together on the <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic table</a>; conversely, a chemical compound generally results when two metals involved are not near each other on the periodic table.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The solid solution needs to be distinguished from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanical_mixtures" title="Mechanical mixtures">mechanical mixtures</a> of powdered solids like two salts, sugar and salt, etc. The mechanical mixtures have total or partial <a href="/wiki/Miscibility_gap" title="Miscibility gap">miscibility gap</a> in solid state.
Examples of solid solutions include crystallized salts from their liquid mixture, metal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alloys" title="Alloys">alloys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moisture" title="Moisture">moist</a> solids. In the case of metal alloys <a href="/wiki/Intermetallic" title="Intermetallic">intermetallic</a> compounds occur frequently.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Details">Details</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_solution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Details">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The solute may incorporate into the solvent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystal_lattice" title="Crystal lattice">crystal lattice</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Crystallographic_defect#substit" title="Crystallographic defect">substitutionally</a></i>, by replacing a solvent particle in the lattice, or <i><a href="/wiki/Interstitial_defect" title="Interstitial defect">interstitially</a></i>, by fitting into the space between solvent particles. Both of these types of solid solution affect the properties of the material by distorting the crystal lattice and disrupting the physical and electrical homogeneity of the solvent material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some mixtures will readily form solid solutions over a range of concentrations, while other mixtures will not form solid solutions at all. The propensity for any two substances to form a solid solution is a complicated matter involving the <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemical</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crystallography" title="Crystallography">crystallographic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum</a> properties of the substances in question. Substitutional solid solutions, in accordance with the <a href="/wiki/Hume-Rothery_rules" title="Hume-Rothery rules">Hume-Rothery rules</a>, may form if the solute and solvent have:
</p>
<ul><li>Similar <a href="/wiki/Atomic_radius" title="Atomic radius">atomic radii</a> (15% or less difference)</li>
<li>Same crystal structure</li>
<li>Similar <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">electronegativities</a></li>
<li>Similar <a href="/wiki/Valence_(chemistry)" title="Valence (chemistry)">valency</a> a solid solution mixes with others to form a new solution</li></ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Phase_diagram" title="Phase diagram">phase diagram</a> in <i>Fig. 1</i> displays an <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloy</a> of two metals which forms a solid solution at all relative <a href="/wiki/Concentration" title="Concentration">concentrations</a> of the two species. In this case, the pure phase of each element is of the same crystal structure, and the similar properties of the two elements allow for unbiased substitution through the full range of relative concentrations.
</p><p>Solid solutions have important commercial and industrial applications, as such mixtures often have superior properties to pure materials. Many metal alloys are solid solutions. Even small amounts of solute can affect the electrical and physical properties of the solvent.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Application">Application</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_solution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Application">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the phase diagram, at three different concentrations, the material will be solid until its heated to its <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting point</a>, and then (after adding the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_of_fusion" title="Heat of fusion">heat of fusion</a>) become liquid at that same temperature:
</p>
<ul><li>the unalloyed extreme left</li>
<li>the unalloyed extreme right</li>
<li>the dip in the center (the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eutectic" title="Eutectic">eutectic</a> composition).</li></ul>
<p>At other proportions, the material will enter a mushy or pasty phase until it warms up to being completely melted.
</p><p>The mixture at the dip point of the diagram is called a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eutectic_point" title="Eutectic point">eutectic</a> alloy. Lead-tin mixtures formulated at that point (37/63 mixture) are useful when soldering electronic components, particularly if done manually, since the solid phase is quickly entered as the solder cools. In contrast, when lead-tin mixtures were used to solder seams in automobile bodies a pasty state enabled a shape to be formed with a wooden paddle or tool, so a 70-30 lead to tin ratio was used. (Lead is being removed from such applications owing to its toxicity and consequent difficulty in recycling devices and components that include lead.)
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exsolution">Exsolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solid_solution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Exsolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When a solid solution becomes unstable—due to a lower temperature, for example—exsolution occurs and the two phases separate into distinct microscopic to megascopic <a href="/wiki/Lamella_(materials)" title="Lamella (materials)">lamellae</a>. This is mainly caused by difference in cation size. Cations which have a large difference in radii are not likely to readily substitute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-test_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-test-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Take the alkali <a href="/wiki/Feldspar" title="Feldspar">feldspar</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minerals" title="Minerals">minerals</a> for example, whose <a href="/wiki/Endmember" title="Endmember">end members</a> are <a href="/wiki/Albite" title="Albite">albite</a>, NaAlSi<sub>3</sub>O<sub>8</sub> and <a href="/wiki/Microcline" title="Microcline">microcline</a>, KAlSi<sub>3</sub>O<sub>8</sub>. At high temperatures Na<sup>+</sup> and K<sup>+</sup> readily substitute for each other and so the minerals will form a solid solution, yet at low temperatures albite can only substitute a small amount of K<sup>+</sup> and the same applies for Na<sup>+</sup> in the microcline. This leads to exsolution where they will separate into two separate phases. In the case of the alkali feldspar minerals, thin white albite layers will alternate between typically pink microcline,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-test_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-test-7">[7]</a></sup> resulting in a <a href="/wiki/Perthite" title="Perthite">perthite</a> texture.
</p>
