<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Free-air concentration enrichment</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nevada_Desert_FACE_Facility.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="767" data-file-width="980" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Nevada_Desert_FACE_Facility.jpg/220px-Nevada_Desert_FACE_Facility.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Nevada_Desert_FACE_Facility.jpg/330px-Nevada_Desert_FACE_Facility.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Nevada_Desert_FACE_Facility.jpg/440px-Nevada_Desert_FACE_Facility.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nevada_Desert_FACE_Facility.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>FACE facility in the Nevada desert.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Free-Air Carbon dioxide Enrichment</b> (<b>FACE</b>) is a method used by <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecologists</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plant_biology" title="Plant biology">plant biologists</a> that raises the concentration of CO<sub>2</sub> in a specified area and allows the response of plant growth to be measured. Experiments using FACE are required because most studies looking at the effect of elevated CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations have been conducted in labs and where there are many missing factors including plant competition. Measuring the effect of elevated CO<sub>2</sub> using FACE is a more natural way of estimating how plant growth will change in the future as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_in_the_Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Carbon dioxide in the Earth's atmosphere">CO<sub>2</sub> concentration</a> rises in the atmosphere. FACE also allows the effect of elevated CO<sub>2</sub> on plants that cannot be grown in small spaces (<a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">trees</a> for example) to be measured. However, FACE experiments carry significantly higher costs relative to greenhouse experiments.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Method">Method</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free-air_concentration_enrichment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Method">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Horizontal or vertical pipes are placed in a circle around the experimental plot, which can be between 1m and 30m in <a href="/wiki/Diameter" title="Diameter">diameter</a>, and these emit CO<sub>2</sub> enriched air around the plants. The concentration of CO<sub>2</sub> is maintained at the desired level through placing sensors in the plot which <a href="/wiki/Feedback" title="Feedback">feedback</a> to a computer which then adjusts the flow of CO<sub>2</sub> from the pipes.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free-air_concentration_enrichment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>FACE circles have been used across in parts of the United States in <a href="/wiki/Temperate_forest" title="Temperate forest">temperate forests</a> and also in stands of <a href="/wiki/Aspen" title="Aspen">aspen</a> in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>. The method is also utilized for agricultural research. For example, FACE circles have been used to measure the response of <a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">soybean</a> plants to increased levels of <a href="/wiki/Tropospheric_ozone" title="Tropospheric ozone">ozone</a> and carbon dioxide at research facilities at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Illinois_at_Urbana-Champaign" title="University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign">University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> FACE technologies have yet to be implemented in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_growth_forest" title="Old growth forest">old growth forests</a>, or key biomes for carbon sequestration, such as <a href="/wiki/Tropical_forest" title="Tropical forest">tropical forests</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boreal_forest" title="Boreal forest">boreal forests</a> and identifying future research priorities for these regions is considered an urgent concern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Examples of this method being used globally include TasFACE, which is investigating the effects of elevated CO<sub>2</sub> on a native <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grassland</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. The National Wheat FACE array is presently being established in Horsham, Victoria, Australia as a joint project of the Victorian Department of Primary Industries and the University of Melbourne.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> EucFACE is Australia's only forest FACE experiment, and was established by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Western_Sydney" title="University of Western Sydney">University of Western Sydney</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cumberland_Plain" title="Cumberland Plain">Cumberland Plain</a> Woodland dominated by <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_tereticornis" title="Eucalyptus tereticornis">Eucalyptus tereticornis</a> near <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_New_South_Wales" title="Richmond, New South Wales">Richmond, New South Wales</a> in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>A FACE experiment began at <a href="/wiki/Duke_University" title="Duke University">Duke University</a> in June 1994. The Blackwood Division of the <a href="/wiki/Duke_Forest" title="Duke Forest">Duke Forest</a> contains the Forest-Atmosphere Carbon Transfer and Storage facility. This consists of four free-air CO<sub>2</sub> enrichment plots which provide higher levels of atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentration and four plots that provide ambient CO<sub>2</sub> control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-desc_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-desc-5">[5]</a></sup> There have been 253 <a href="/wiki/Publication" title="Publication">publications</a> reporting on the findings of the experiment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Results">Results</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free-air_concentration_enrichment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Results">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2004, a <a href="/wiki/Meta-analysis" title="Meta-analysis">meta-analysis</a> of 15 years of FACE studies, found the response to elevated CO<sub>2</sub> using FACE only slightly increases yield in crop plants (5-7% in <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a> and 8% in <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>). These responses were lower than was expected from previous studies that measured the effect in labs or enclosures. This has important consequences as previous projections of food production have assumed that decreases in yield as a result of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> would be offset by increases in yield due to elevated CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of 2010, a more complete picture is emerging, with significant difference in response being observed for different plant species, water availabilities and the concentration of ozone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> For example, the 2007-2010 Horsham FACE project (using wheat crops) in Victoria, Australia, found "The effect of eCO2 was to increase crop biomass at maturity by 20% and anthesis root biomass increased by 49%".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> This study also concludes that "a wide gene pool needs to be investigated to see if particular cultivars are able to respond more to eCO2". Increased atmospheric carbon dioxide has been found to reduce plant water use, and consequently, the uptake of nitrogen, so particularly benefiting crop yields in arid regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The carbohydrate content of crops is increased from photosynthesis, but protein content is reduced due to lower nitrogen uptake. Legumes and their symbiotic "nitrogen fixing" bacteria appear to benefit more from increased carbon dioxide levels than most other species.
</p>
