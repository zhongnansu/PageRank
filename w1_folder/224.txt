<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Carboxylate</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:257px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carboxylate-resonance-hybrid.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="742" data-file-width="806" height="235" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Carboxylate-resonance-hybrid.png/255px-Carboxylate-resonance-hybrid.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Carboxylate-resonance-hybrid.png/383px-Carboxylate-resonance-hybrid.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Carboxylate-resonance-hybrid.png/510px-Carboxylate-resonance-hybrid.png 2x" width="255"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Carboxylate-resonance-hybrid.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Carboxylate ion</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Acrylate-ion-3D-balls.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="771" data-file-width="1021" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Acrylate-ion-3D-balls.png/220px-Acrylate-ion-3D-balls.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Acrylate-ion-3D-balls.png/330px-Acrylate-ion-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Acrylate-ion-3D-balls.png/440px-Acrylate-ion-3D-balls.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Acrylate-ion-3D-balls.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Acrylate ion</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>carboxylate</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salt</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">ester</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxylic acid</a>.
<i>Carboxylate salts</i> have the general formula M(RCOO)<sub><i>n</i></sub>, where M is a metal and <i>n</i> is 1, 2,...; <i>carboxylate esters</i> have the general formula RCOOR′. R and R′ are organic groups; R′ ≠ H.
</p><p>A <b>carboxylate ion</b> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conjugate_base" title="Conjugate base">conjugate base</a> of a carboxylic acid, RCOO<sup>−</sup>. It is an <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ion</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negative_charge" title="Negative charge">negative charge</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Resonance_stabilization_of_the_carboxylate_ion">Resonance stabilization of the carboxylate ion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carboxylate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Resonance stabilization of the carboxylate ion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carboxylic acids easily dissociate into a carboxylate anion and a positively charged hydrogen ion (proton), much more readily than alcohols do (into an <a href="/wiki/Alkoxide" title="Alkoxide">alkoxide</a> ion and a proton), because the carboxylate ion is stabilized by <a href="/wiki/Resonance_(chemistry)" title="Resonance (chemistry)">resonance</a>. The negative charge that is left after <a href="/wiki/Deprotonation" title="Deprotonation">deprotonation</a> of the carboxyl group is delocalized between the two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronegative" title="Electronegative">electronegative</a> oxygen atoms in a resonance structure.
</p>
<dl><dd><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carboxylate-resonance-2D.png" title="Equivalence of the resonance forms the delocalised form of a general carboxylate anion"><img alt="Equivalence of the resonance forms the delocalised form of a general carboxylate anion" data-file-height="423" data-file-width="2100" height="81" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Carboxylate-resonance-2D.png/400px-Carboxylate-resonance-2D.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Carboxylate-resonance-2D.png/600px-Carboxylate-resonance-2D.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Carboxylate-resonance-2D.png/800px-Carboxylate-resonance-2D.png 2x" width="400"/></a></dd></dl>
<p>This <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delocalization" title="Delocalization">delocalization</a> of the electron cloud means that both of the oxygen atoms are less strongly negatively charged; the positive proton is therefore less strongly attracted back to the carboxylate group once it has left; hence, the carboxylate ion is more stable. In contrast, an alkoxide ion, once formed, would have a strong negative charge on the oxygen atom, which would make it difficult for the proton to escape. Carboxylic acids thus have a lower <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> than alcohols: the higher the number of protons in solution, the lower the pH.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
