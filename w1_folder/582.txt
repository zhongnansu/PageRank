<p>A <b>corrosive substance</b> is one that will damage or destroy other substances with which it comes into contact by means of a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reaction</a>. 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Corrosive_substance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <i>corrosive</i> is derived from the Latin verb <i>corrodere</i>, which means <i>to gnaw</i>, indicating how these substances seem to "gnaw" their way through flesh or other materials.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_terms">Chemical terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Corrosive_substance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Chemical terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <i><b>corrosive</b></i> refers to any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical" title="Chemical">chemical</a> that will dissolve the structure of an object. They can be <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidizer" title="Oxidizer">oxidizers</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">bases</a>. When they come in contact with a surface, the surface deteriorates. The deterioration can happen in minutes, e.g. concentrated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muriatic_acid" title="Muriatic acid">muriatic acid</a> spilled on skin; or slowly over days or years, e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Rust" title="Rust">rusting of iron</a> in a bridge.
</p><p>Sometimes the word <i><b>caustic</b></i> is used as a synonym for <i>corrosive</i>, but <i>caustic</i> refers only to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strong_base" title="Strong base">strong bases</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Alkali" title="Alkali">alkalis</a>, and not to <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidizer" title="Oxidizer">oxidizers</a>, or other non-alkaline corrosives.
</p><p>At low concentrations, a corrosive substance is called an <i><b>irritant</b></i>, and its effect on living tissue is called <a href="/wiki/Irritation" title="Irritation">irritation</a>. At high concentrations, a corrosive substance causes a <i><b>chemical burn</b></i>, a distinct type of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_burn" title="Chemical burn">tissue damage</a>. Corrosives are different from <i><b>poisons</b></i> in that corrosives are immediately dangerous to the tissues they contact, whereas <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poisons" title="Poisons">poisons</a> may have systemic toxic effects that require time to become evident. Colloquially, corrosives may be called <i>poisons</i> but the concepts are technically distinct. However, there is nothing which precludes a corrosive from being a poison; there are substances that are both corrosives and poisons.
</p><p>Corrosion of non-living surfaces such as metals is a distinct process. For example, a water-air electrochemical cell corrodes <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> to <i><b><a href="/wiki/Rust" title="Rust">rust</a></b></i>, corrodes <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> to <i><b><a href="/wiki/Patina" title="Patina">patina</a></b></i>, and corrodes copper, <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>, and other metals to <i><b><a href="/wiki/Tarnish" title="Tarnish">tarnish</a></b></i>.
</p><p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Globally_Harmonized_System" title="Globally Harmonized System">international system of symbolic chemical labels</a>, both rapid <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> of metals and chemical corrosion of skin qualify for the corrosive symbol.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_living_tissue">Effects on living tissue</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Corrosive_substance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Effects on living tissue">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Common corrosives are either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strong_acid" title="Strong acid">strong acids</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strong_base" title="Strong base">strong bases</a>, or concentrated solutions of certain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weak_acid" title="Weak acid">weak acids</a> or <a href="/wiki/Weak_base" title="Weak base">weak bases</a>. They can exist as any <a href="/wiki/State_of_matter" title="State of matter">state of matter</a>, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquids" title="Liquids">liquids</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solids" title="Solids">solids</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gases" title="Gases">gases</a>, mists or <a href="/wiki/Vapor" title="Vapor">vapors</a>.
</p><p>Their action on <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">living tissue</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Skin" title="Skin">skin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flesh" title="Flesh">flesh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cornea" title="Cornea">cornea</a>) is mainly based on acid-base reactions of amide <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a>, ester hydrolysis and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protein_denaturation" title="Protein denaturation">protein denaturation</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proteins" title="Proteins">Proteins</a> (chemically composed of <a href="/wiki/Amide" title="Amide">amide</a> bonds) are destroyed via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amide_hydrolysis" title="Amide hydrolysis">amide hydrolysis</a> while <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lipids" title="Lipids">lipids</a> (which have <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">ester</a> bonds) are decomposed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ester_hydrolysis" title="Ester hydrolysis">ester hydrolysis</a>. These reactions lead to <a href="/wiki/Chemical_burn" title="Chemical burn">chemical burns</a> and are the mechanism of the destruction posed by corrosives.
</p><p>Some corrosives possess other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_properties" title="Chemical properties">chemical properties</a> which may extend their corrosive effects on living tissue. For example, <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> (H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>) at a high <a href="/wiki/Concentration" title="Concentration">concentration</a> is also a <a href="/wiki/Dehydration_reaction" title="Dehydration reaction">strong dehydrating agent</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OA_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-OA-1">[1]</a></sup> capable of dehydrating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbohydrates" title="Carbohydrates">carbohydrates</a> and liberating extra <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a>. This results in secondary <a href="/wiki/Burn" title="Burn">thermal burns</a> in addition to the chemical burns and may speed up its decomposing reactions on the contact surface. Some corrosives, such as <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> and concentrated sulfuric acid, are strong <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_agent" title="Oxidizing agent">oxidizing agents</a> as well, which significantly contributes to the extra damage caused. <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">Hydrofluoric acid</a> does not necessarily cause noticeable damage upon contact, but produces <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid#Health_and_safety" title="Hydrofluoric acid">tissue damage and toxicity</a> after being painlessly absorbed. <a href="/wiki/Zinc_chloride" title="Zinc chloride">Zinc chloride</a> solutions are capable of destroying <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a> and corroding through paper and <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a> since the <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">cations</a> in the solutions specifically attack <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl_group" title="Hydroxyl group">hydroxyl groups</a>, acting as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_acid" title="Lewis acid">Lewis acid</a>. This effect is not restricted to acids; so strong a base as <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">calcium oxide</a>, which has a strong affinity for water (forming calcium hydroxide, itself a strong and corrosive base), also releases heat capable of contributing thermal burns as well as delivering the corrosive effects of a strong alkali to moist flesh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition, some corrosive chemicals, mostly <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a>, are <a href="/wiki/Volatility_(chemistry)" title="Volatility (chemistry)">volatile</a> and can emit corrosive mists upon contact with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a>. Inhalation can damage the <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_tract" title="Respiratory tract">respiratory tract</a>.
</p><p>Corrosive substances are most hazardous to eyesight. A drop of a corrosive may cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blindness" title="Blindness">blindness</a> within 2–10 seconds through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corneal_opacification" title="Corneal opacification">opacification</a> or direct destruction of the <a href="/wiki/Cornea" title="Cornea">cornea</a>.
</p><p>Ingestion of corrosives can induce severe consequences, including serious damage of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_gastrointestinal_tract" title="Human gastrointestinal tract">gastrointestinal tract</a>, which can lead to <a href="/wiki/Vomiting" title="Vomiting">vomiting</a>, severe <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stomach_aches" title="Stomach aches">stomach aches</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>, and death.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_types">Common types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Corrosive_substance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Common types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Common corrosive chemicals are classified into:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">Acids</a>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strong_acid" title="Strong acid">Strong acids</a> – the most common are <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> (H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>, HNO<sub>3</sub> and HCl, respectively).</li>
<li>Some concentrated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weak_acid" title="Weak acid">weak acids</a>, for example <a href="/wiki/Formic_acid" title="Formic acid">formic acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a></li>
<li>Strong <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_acid" title="Lewis acid">Lewis acids</a> such as anhydrous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum_chloride" title="Aluminum chloride">aluminum chloride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boron_trifluoride" title="Boron trifluoride">boron trifluoride</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_acid" title="Lewis acid">Lewis acids</a> with specific reactivity; e.g., solutions of <a href="/wiki/Zinc_chloride" title="Zinc chloride">zinc chloride</a></li>
<li>Extremely strong acids (<a href="/wiki/Superacid" title="Superacid">superacids</a>)</li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">Bases</a>
<ul><li>Caustics or alkalis, such as <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">potassium hydroxide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Calcium_hydroxide" title="Calcium hydroxide">calcium hydroxide</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkali_metals" title="Alkali metals">Alkali metals</a> in the metallic form (e.g. elemental <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>), and hydrides of alkali and <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_earth_metal" title="Alkaline earth metal">alkaline earth metals</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydride" title="Sodium hydride">sodium hydride</a>, function as strong bases and hydrate to give caustics</li>
<li>Extremely strong bases (<a href="/wiki/Superbase" title="Superbase">superbases</a>) such as <a href="/wiki/Alkoxide" title="Alkoxide">alkoxides</a>, metal amides (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Sodium_amide" title="Sodium amide">sodium amide</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organometallic" title="Organometallic">organometallic</a> bases such as <a href="/wiki/Butyllithium" title="Butyllithium">butyllithium</a></li>
<li>Some concentrated weak bases, such as <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> when anhydrous or in a concentrated solution</li></ul></li>
<li>Dehydrating agents such as concentrated <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus_pentoxide" title="Phosphorus pentoxide">phosphorus pentoxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">calcium oxide</a>, anhydrous <a href="/wiki/Zinc_chloride" title="Zinc chloride">zinc chloride</a>, also elemental alkali metals</li>
<li>Strong oxidizers such as concentrated <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a></li>
<li>Electrophilic <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogens</a>: elemental <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">bromine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a>, and electrophilic salts such as <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hypochlorite" title="Sodium hypochlorite">sodium hypochlorite</a> or N-chloro compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Chloramine-T" title="Chloramine-T">chloramine-T</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Halide" title="Halide">halide</a> ions are not corrosive, except for <a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">fluoride</a></li>
<li>Organic halides and organic acid halides such as <a href="/wiki/Acetyl_chloride" title="Acetyl chloride">acetyl chloride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Benzyl_chloroformate" title="Benzyl chloroformate">benzyl chloroformate</a></li>
<li>Acid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anhydride" title="Anhydride">anhydrides</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkylating_agent" title="Alkylating agent">Alkylating agents</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfate" title="Dimethyl sulfate">dimethyl sulfate</a></li>
<li>Some organic materials such as <a href="/wiki/Phenol" title="Phenol">phenol</a> ("carbolic acid")</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_protective_equipment">Personal protective equipment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Corrosive_substance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal protective equipment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Use of <a href="/wiki/Personal_protective_equipment" title="Personal protective equipment">personal protective equipment</a>, including items such as protective gloves, protective aprons, acid suits, safety goggles, a face shield, or safety shoes, is normally recommended when handling corrosive substances.  Users should consult a <a href="/wiki/Safety_data_sheet" title="Safety data sheet">safety data sheet</a> for the specific recommendation for the corrosive substance of interest.  The material of construction of the personal protective equipment is of critical importance as well.  For example, although rubber gloves and rubber aprons may be made out of a chemically resistant <a href="/wiki/Elastomer" title="Elastomer">elastomer</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Nitrile_rubber" title="Nitrile rubber">nitrile rubber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neoprene" title="Neoprene">neoprene</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Butyl_rubber" title="Butyl rubber">butyl rubber</a>, each of these materials has different resistance to different corrosives and they should not be substituted for each other.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Corrosive_substance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some corrosive chemicals are valued for various uses, the most common of which is in <a href="/wiki/Cleaning_agent" title="Cleaning agent">household cleaning agents</a>. For example, most <a href="/wiki/Drain_cleaner" title="Drain cleaner">drain cleaners</a> contain either acids or alkalis due to their capabilities of dissolving <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">greases</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proteins" title="Proteins">proteins</a> or mineral deposits such as limescale inside water pipes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dc_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-dc-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>In chemical uses, high chemical reactivity is often desirable, as the rates of chemical reactions depend on the <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_activity" title="Thermodynamic activity">activity</a> (effective concentration) of the reactive species. For instance, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalytic" title="Catalytic">catalytic</a> sulfuric acid is used in the <a href="/wiki/Alkylation" title="Alkylation">alkylation</a> process in an oil refinery: the activity of carbocations, the <a href="/wiki/Reactive_intermediate" title="Reactive intermediate">reactive intermediate</a>, is higher with stronger acidity, and thus the reaction proceeds faster. Once used, corrosives are most often recycled or neutralized. However, there have been environmental problems with untreated corrosive effluents or accidental discharges.
</p>
