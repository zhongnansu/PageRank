<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Deficit irrigation</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Deficit irrigation</b> (<b>DI</b>) is a watering strategy that can be applied by different types of <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> application methods. The correct application of DI requires thorough understanding of the yield response to water (crop sensitivity to drought stress) and of the economic impact of reductions in harvest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In regions where <a href="/wiki/Water_resources" title="Water resources">water resources</a> are restrictive it can be more profitable for a farmer to maximize <a href="#Crop_water_productivity">crop water productivity</a> instead of maximizing the harvest per unit land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The saved water can be used for other purposes or to irrigate extra units of land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
DI is sometimes referred to as incomplete supplemental irrigation or regulated DI.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deficit_irrigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Deficit irrigation (DI) has been reviewed and defined as follows:
</p><p><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dx.doi.org_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-dx.doi.org-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote><p><i>"Deficit irrigation is an <a href="/wiki/Process_optimization" title="Process optimization">optimization</a> strategy in which irrigation is applied during drought-sensitive growth stages of a crop. Outside these periods, irrigation is limited or even unnecessary if rainfall provides a minimum supply of water. Water restriction is limited to drought-tolerant <a href="/wiki/Phenology" title="Phenology">phenological</a> stages, often the vegetative stages and the late ripening period. Total irrigation application is therefore not proportional to irrigation requirements throughout the crop cycle. While this inevitably results in plant <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a> stress and consequently in production loss, DI maximizes irrigation water productivity, which is the main limiting factor (English, 1990). In other words, DI aims at stabilizing yields and at obtaining maximum <a href="#Crop_water_productivity">crop water productivity</a> rather than maximum yields (Zhang and Oweis, 1999)."</i></p></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Crop_water_productivity">Crop water productivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deficit_irrigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Crop water productivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Crop water productivity (WP) or water use efficiency (WUE)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> expressed in kg/m³ is an <a href="/wiki/Economic_efficiency" title="Economic efficiency">efficiency</a> term, expressing the amount of marketable product (e.g. kilograms of grain) in relation to the amount of input needed to produce that output (cubic meters of water). The water used for crop production is referred to as crop <a href="/wiki/Evapotranspiration" title="Evapotranspiration">evapotranspiration</a>. This is a combination of water lost by <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a> from the soil surface and <a href="/wiki/Transpiration" title="Transpiration">transpiration</a> by the plant, occurring simultaneously. Except by <a href="#Modeling">modeling</a>, distinguishing between the two processes is difficult. Representative values of WUE for cereals at field level, expressed with evapotranspiration in the denominator, can vary between 0.10 and 4 kg/m3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Experiences_with_deficit_irrigation">Experiences with deficit irrigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deficit_irrigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Experiences with deficit irrigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For certain crops, experiments confirm that DI can increase water use efficiency without severe yield reductions. For example for winter wheat in Turkey, planned DI increased yields by 65% as compared to winter wheat under rainfed cultivation, and had double the water use efficiency as compared to rainfed and fully irrigated winter wheat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Similar positive results have been described for cotton.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Experiments in Turkey and India indicated that the irrigation water use for cotton could be reduced to up to 60 percent of the total crop water requirement with limited yield losses. In this way, high water productivity and a better nutrient-water balance was obtained.
</p><p>Certain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Underutilized_crops" title="Underutilized crops">Underutilized</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horticultural" title="Horticultural">horticultural</a> crops also respond favorably to DI, such as tested at experimental and farmer level for the crop <a href="/wiki/Quinoa" title="Quinoa">quinoa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Yields could be stabilized at around 1.6 tons per hectare by supplementing irrigation water if rainwater was lacking during the plant establishment and reproductive stages. Applying irrigation water throughout the whole season (full irrigation) reduced the water productivity. Also in <a href="/wiki/Viticulture" title="Viticulture">viticulture</a> and fruit tree cultivation, DI is practiced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Scientists affiliated with the <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_Research_Service" title="Agricultural Research Service">Agricultural Research Service</a> (ARS) of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USDA" title="USDA">USDA</a> found that conserving water by forcing drought (or deficit irrigation) on peanut plants early in the growing season has shown to cause early maturation of the plant yet still maintain sufficient yield of the crop. <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.ars.usda.gov/is/pr/2010/100114.htm" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>  Inducing drought through deficit irrigation earlier in the season caused the peanut plants to physiologically "learn" how to adapt to a stressful drought environment, making the plants better able to cope with drought that commonly occurs later in the growing season.  Deficit irrigation is beneficial for the farmers because it reduces the cost of water and prevents a loss of crop yield (for certain crops) later on in the growing season due to drought.  In addition to these findings, ARS scientists suggest that deficit irrigation accompanied with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_tillage" title="Conservation tillage">conservation tillage</a> would greatly reduce the peanut crop water requirement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>For other crops, the application of deficit irrigation will result in a lower water use efficiency and yield. This is the case when crops are sensitive to drought stress throughout the complete season, such as <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">maize</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Apart from university research groups and farmers associations, international organizations such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FAO" title="FAO">FAO</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ICARDA" title="ICARDA">ICARDA</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IWMI" title="IWMI">IWMI</a> and the <a href="/wiki/CGIAR" title="CGIAR">CGIAR</a> Challenge Program on Water and Food are studying DI.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reasons_for_increased_water_productivity_under_deficit_irrigation">Reasons for increased water productivity under deficit irrigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deficit_irrigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reasons for increased water productivity under deficit irrigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>If crops have certain phenological phases in which they are tolerant to water stress, DI can increase the ratio of yield over crop water consumption (<a href="/wiki/Evapotranspiration" title="Evapotranspiration">evapotranspiration</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dx.doi.org_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-dx.doi.org-4">[4]</a></sup> by either reducing the water loss by unproductive <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a>, and/or by
increasing the proportion of marketable yield to the totally produced biomass (harvest index), and/or by increasing the proportion of total <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> production to <a href="/wiki/Transpiration" title="Transpiration">transpiration</a> due to hardening of the crop - although this effect is very limited due to the conservative relation between biomass production and crop transpiration,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> - and/or due to adequate <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizer</a> application<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and/or by avoiding bad agronomic conditions during crop growth, such as water logging in the root zone, <a href="/wiki/Pest_(organism)" title="Pest (organism)">pests</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diseases" title="Diseases">diseases</a>, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages">Advantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deficit_irrigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Advantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The correct application of DI for a certain crop:
</p>
<ul><li>maximizes the productivity of water, generally with adequate harvest quality;</li>
<li>allows economic planning and stable income due to a stabilization of the harvest in comparison with rainfed cultivation;</li>
<li>decreases the risk of certain diseases linked to high humidity (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a>) in comparison with full irrigation;</li>
<li>reduces nutrient loss by <a href="/wiki/Leaching_(agriculture)" title="Leaching (agriculture)">leaching</a> of the root zone, which results in better <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> quality<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and lower <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizer</a> needs as for cultivation under full irrigation;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li>improves control over the sowing date and length of the growing period<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> independent from the onset of the rainy season and therefore improves agricultural planning.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Constraints">Constraints</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deficit_irrigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Constraints">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A number of constraints apply to deficit irrigation:
</p>
<ul><li>Exact knowledge of the crop response to water stress is imperative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li>There should be sufficient flexibility in access to water during periods of high demand (drought sensitive stages of a crop).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li>A minimum quantity of water should be guaranteed for the crop, below which DI has no significant beneficial effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li>An individual farmer should consider the benefit for the total water users community (extra land can be irrigated with the saved water), when he faces a below-maximum yield;</li>
<li>Because irrigation is applied more efficiently, the risk for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soil_salinization" title="Soil salinization">soil salinization</a> is higher under DI as compared to full irrigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modeling">Modeling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deficit_irrigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Modeling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Field experimentation is necessary for correct application of DI for a particular crop in a particular region. In addition, simulation of the soil <a href="/wiki/Water_balance" title="Water balance">water balance</a> and related crop growth (crop water productivity modeling) can be a valuable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decision_support" title="Decision support">decision support</a> tool.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> By conjunctively simulating the effects of different influencing factors (<a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, management, crop characteristics) on crop production, models allow to (1) better understand the mechanism behind improved water use efficiency, to (2) schedule the necessary irrigation applications during the <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a> sensitive crop growth stages, considering the possible variability in climate, to (3) test DI strategies of specific crops in new regions, and to (4) investigate the effects of future climate scenarios or scenarios of altered management practices on crop production.
</p>
