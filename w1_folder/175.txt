<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Dry ice</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Solid CO2</div>
<p><b>Dry ice</b>, sometimes referred to as "<b>cardice</b>" (chiefly by <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British</a> chemists), is the <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solid</a> form of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. It is used primarily as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cooling_agent" title="Cooling agent">cooling agent</a>. Its advantages include lower temperature than that of <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">water ice</a> and not leaving any residue (other than incidental frost from moisture in the atmosphere). It is useful for preserving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frozen_foods" title="Frozen foods">frozen foods</a> where <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">mechanical cooling</a> is unavailable.
</p><p>Dry ice <a href="/wiki/Sublimation_(phase_transition)" title="Sublimation (phase transition)">sublimates</a> at 194.65 K (−78.5 °C; −109.3 °F), at Earth atmospheric pressures. This extreme cold makes the solid dangerous to handle without protection due to burns caused by freezing (<a href="/wiki/Frostbite" title="Frostbite">frostbite</a>). While generally not very toxic, the <a href="/wiki/Outgassing" title="Outgassing">outgassing</a> from it can cause <a href="/wiki/Hypercapnia" title="Hypercapnia">hypercapnia</a>  (abnormally elevated carbon dioxide levels in the blood) due to buildup in confined locations.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dry_ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For supplementary chemical data, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_data" title="Carbon dioxide data">Carbon dioxide data</a>.</div>
<p>Dry ice is the solid form of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>), a molecule consisting of a single <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> atom <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">bonded</a> to two <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a>. Dry ice is colorless, non-flammable, with a sour zesty odor, and can lower the <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> of a solution when dissolved in <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, forming <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a> (H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yaws_2001_p=125_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yaws_2001_p=125-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Comparison_carbon_dioxide_water_phase_diagrams.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="235" data-file-width="512" height="184" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Comparison_carbon_dioxide_water_phase_diagrams.svg/400px-Comparison_carbon_dioxide_water_phase_diagrams.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Comparison_carbon_dioxide_water_phase_diagrams.svg/600px-Comparison_carbon_dioxide_water_phase_diagrams.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Comparison_carbon_dioxide_water_phase_diagrams.svg/800px-Comparison_carbon_dioxide_water_phase_diagrams.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Comparison_carbon_dioxide_water_phase_diagrams.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Comparison of <a href="/wiki/Phase_diagram" title="Phase diagram">phase diagrams</a> of carbon dioxide (red) and water (blue) as a log-lin chart with phase transitions points at 1 atmosphere</div></div></div>
<p>At pressures below 5.13 <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">atm</a> and temperatures below −56.4 °C (−69.5 °F) (the <a href="/wiki/Triple_point" title="Triple point">triple point</a>), CO<sub>2</sub> changes from a solid to a gas with no intervening liquid form, through a process called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sublimation_(chemistry)" title="Sublimation (chemistry)">sublimation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[note 1]</a></sup> The opposite process is called <a href="/wiki/Deposition_(phase_transition)" title="Deposition (phase transition)">deposition</a>, where CO<sub>2</sub> changes from the <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> to <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solid</a> phase (dry ice). At atmospheric pressure, sublimation/deposition occurs at −78.5 °C (−109.3 °F) or 194.65 K.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> of dry ice varies, but usually ranges between about 1.4 and 1.6 g/cm<sup>3</sup> (87 and 100 lb/cu ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[2]</a></sup> The low temperature and direct sublimation to a gas makes dry ice an effective <a href="/wiki/Coolant" title="Coolant">coolant</a>, since it is colder than <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">water ice</a> and leaves no residue as it changes state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup> Its <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_sublimation" title="Enthalpy of sublimation">enthalpy of sublimation</a> is 571 kJ/kg (25.2 kJ/mol).
</p><p>Dry ice is <a href="/wiki/Chemical_polarity#Nonpolar_molecules" title="Chemical polarity">non-polar</a>, with a <a href="/wiki/Dipole#Molecular_dipoles" title="Dipole">dipole moment</a> of zero, so attractive <a href="/wiki/Intermolecular_force" title="Intermolecular force">intermolecular</a> <a href="/wiki/Van_der_Waals_force" title="Van der Waals force">van der Waals forces</a> operate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> The composition results in low <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conduction" title="Thermal conduction">thermal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electrical_resistivity_and_conductivity" title="Electrical resistivity and conductivity">electrical conductivity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dry_ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is generally accepted that dry ice was first observed in 1835 by French inventor <a href="/wiki/Adrien-Jean-Pierre_Thilorier" title="Adrien-Jean-Pierre Thilorier">Adrien-Jean-Pierre Thilorier</a> (1790–1844), who published the first account of the substance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup>  In his experiments, it was noted that when opening the lid of a large cylinder containing liquid carbon dioxide, most of the liquid carbon dioxide quickly evaporated. This left only solid dry ice in the container. In 1924, Thomas B. Slate applied for a US <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a> to sell dry ice commercially. Subsequently, he became the first to make dry ice successful as an <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Industry_Dry_ice_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Industry_Dry_ice-9">[8]</a></sup>
In 1925, this solid form of CO<sub>2</sub> was trademarked by the DryIce Corporation of America as "Dry ice", thus leading to its common name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-About_Dry_ice_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-About_Dry_ice-10">[9]</a></sup>
That same year the DryIce Co. sold the substance commercially for the first time; marketing it for refrigerating purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Industry_Dry_ice_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Industry_Dry_ice-9">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The alternative name "cardice" is a <a href="/wiki/Trademark" title="Trademark">registered trademark</a> of <a href="/wiki/Air_Liquide" title="Air Liquide">Air Liquide UK Ltd</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cardice_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-cardice-11">[10]</a></sup> It is sometimes written as "card ice".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plumbing_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-plumbing-12">[11]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Dry ice is easily manufactured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uigi_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-uigi-14">[13]</a></sup> First, gases with a high concentration of carbon dioxide are produced. Such gases can be a byproduct of another process, such as producing <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> from <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, oil refinery activities or large-scale <a href="/wiki/Industrial_fermentation" title="Industrial fermentation">fermentation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uigi_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-uigi-14">[13]</a></sup> Second, the carbon dioxide-rich gas is pressurized and refrigerated until it liquefies. Next, the pressure is reduced. When this occurs some liquid carbon dioxide vaporizes, causing a rapid lowering of temperature of the remaining liquid. As a result, the extreme cold causes the liquid to solidify into a snow-like consistency. Finally, the snow-like solid carbon dioxide is compressed into small pellets or larger blocks of dry ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hsw_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-hsw-16">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Dry ice is typically produced in three standard forms: large blocks, cylindrical small (<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> or <span class="frac nowrap"><sup>5</sup>⁄<sub>8</sub></span> in [13 or 16 mm] diameter) pellets and cylindrical tiny (<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>8</sub></span> inch [3.2 mm] diameter), high surface to volume pellets that float on oil or water and do not stick to skin because of their high radii of curvature. Tiny dry ice pellets are used primarily for ice blasting, quick freezing, fire fighting, oil solidifying and have been found to be safe for experimentation by middle school students wearing appropriate <a href="/wiki/Personal_protective_equipment" title="Personal protective equipment">personal protective equipment</a> such as gloves and safety glasses. A standard block weighing approximately 30 kg (66 lb)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> covered in a taped paper wrapping is most common. These are commonly used in shipping, because they sublime relatively slowly due to a low ratio of surface area to volume. Pellets are around 1 cm (0.4 in) in diameter and can be bagged easily. This form is suited to small scale use, for example at <a href="/wiki/Grocery_store" title="Grocery store">grocery stores</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laboratory" title="Laboratory">laboratories</a> where it is stored in a thickly insulated chest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dry_ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial">Commercial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dry_ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Commercial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ice_cream_cart_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1485853.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="480" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Ice_cream_cart_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1485853.jpg/220px-Ice_cream_cart_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1485853.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Ice_cream_cart_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1485853.jpg/330px-Ice_cream_cart_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1485853.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Ice_cream_cart_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1485853.jpg/440px-Ice_cream_cart_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1485853.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ice_cream_cart_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1485853.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An ice cream cart</div></div></div>
<p>The most common use of dry ice is to preserve food,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yaws_2001_p=125_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Yaws_2001_p=125-1">[1]</a></sup> using <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration#Non-cyclic_refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">non-cyclic refrigeration</a>.
</p><p>It is frequently used to package items that must remain cold or frozen, such as ice cream or biological samples, without the use of <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">mechanical cooling</a>.
</p><p>Dry ice can be used to <a href="/wiki/Flash_freezing" title="Flash freezing">flash-freeze</a> food<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flashfreeze_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-flashfreeze-18">[17]</a></sup> or laboratory biological samples,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Carbonated_water" title="Carbonated water">carbonate</a> beverages,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flashfreeze_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-flashfreeze-18">[17]</a></sup>  make <a href="/wiki/Ice_cream" title="Ice cream">ice cream</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup> solidify <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_spills" title="Oil spills">oil spills</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> and stop <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_sculptures" title="Ice sculptures">ice sculptures</a> and ice walls from melting.
</p><p>Dry ice can be used to arrest and prevent insect activity in closed containers of grains and grain products, as it displaces oxygen, but does not alter the taste or quality of foods. For the same reason, it can prevent or retard food oils and fats from becoming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rancidity" title="Rancidity">rancid</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dry_ice_in_cup.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1656" data-file-width="2003" height="182" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Dry_ice_in_cup.jpg/220px-Dry_ice_in_cup.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Dry_ice_in_cup.jpg/330px-Dry_ice_in_cup.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Dry_ice_in_cup.jpg/440px-Dry_ice_in_cup.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dry_ice_in_cup.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sublimation</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dry_Ice_in_Water.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1836" data-file-width="3264" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Dry_Ice_in_Water.jpg/220px-Dry_Ice_in_Water.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Dry_Ice_in_Water.jpg/330px-Dry_Ice_in_Water.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Dry_Ice_in_Water.jpg/440px-Dry_Ice_in_Water.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dry_Ice_in_Water.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dry ice in water</div></div></div>
<p>When dry ice is placed in water, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sublimation_(chemistry)" title="Sublimation (chemistry)">sublimation</a> is accelerated, and low-sinking, dense clouds of smoke-like fog are created. This is used in <a href="/wiki/Fog_machine" title="Fog machine">fog machines</a>, at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theater" title="Theater">theaters</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haunted_house_attractions" title="Haunted house attractions">haunted house attractions</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nightclub" title="Nightclub">nightclubs</a> for dramatic effects. Unlike most artificial <a href="/wiki/Fog_machine" title="Fog machine">fog machines</a>, in which fog rises like smoke, fog from dry ice hovers near the ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hsw_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-hsw-16">[15]</a></sup> Dry ice is useful in theater productions that require dense fog effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> The fog originates from the bulk water into which the dry ice is placed, and not from atmospheric water vapor (as is commonly assumed).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is occasionally used to freeze and remove <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warts" title="Warts">warts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Liquid_nitrogen" title="Liquid nitrogen">liquid nitrogen</a> performs better in this role, since it is colder so requires less time to act, and less pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aa1317_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-aa1317-25">[24]</a></sup> Dry ice has fewer problems with storage, since it can be generated from compressed carbon dioxide gas as needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aa1317_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-aa1317-25">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Plumber" title="Plumber">Plumbers</a> use equipment that forces pressurised liquid CO<sub>2</sub> into a jacket around a pipe. The dry ice formed causes the water to freeze, forming an ice plug, allowing them to perform repairs without turning off the water mains. This technique can be used on pipes up to 4 inches (100 mm) in diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plumbing2_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-plumbing2-26">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Dry ice can be used as <a href="/wiki/Bait_(luring_substance)" title="Bait (luring substance)">bait</a> to trap <a href="/wiki/Mosquito" title="Mosquito">mosquitoes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bedbugs" title="Bedbugs">bedbugs</a>, and other insects, due to their attraction to carbon dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>It can be used to exterminate rodents. This is done by dropping pellets into rodent tunnels in the ground and then sealing off the entrance, thus suffocating the animals as the dry ice sublimates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Tiny dry ice pellets can be used to fight fire by both cooling fuel and suffocating the fire by excluding oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>The extreme temperature of dry ice can cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viscoelastic" title="Viscoelastic">viscoelastic</a> materials to change to <a href="/wiki/Glass_transition" title="Glass transition">glass</a> phase. Thus it is useful for removing many types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pressure_sensitive_adhesive" title="Pressure sensitive adhesive">pressure sensitive adhesives</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial">Industrial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dry_ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Industrial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rubbermold.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="221" data-file-width="220" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/df/Rubbermold.jpg" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rubbermold.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dry ice blasting used for cleaning a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubber" title="Rubber">rubber</a> mold</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:%C4%8Ci%C5%A1%C4%87enje_instalacija_suhim_ledom.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3864" data-file-width="5152" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/%C4%8Ci%C5%A1%C4%87enje_instalacija_suhim_ledom.jpg/220px-%C4%8Ci%C5%A1%C4%87enje_instalacija_suhim_ledom.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/%C4%8Ci%C5%A1%C4%87enje_instalacija_suhim_ledom.jpg/330px-%C4%8Ci%C5%A1%C4%87enje_instalacija_suhim_ledom.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/%C4%8Ci%C5%A1%C4%87enje_instalacija_suhim_ledom.jpg/440px-%C4%8Ci%C5%A1%C4%87enje_instalacija_suhim_ledom.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:%C4%8Ci%C5%A1%C4%87enje_instalacija_suhim_ledom.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dry ice blasting used for cleaning electrical installations</div></div></div>
<p>Dry ice can be used for loosening asphalt floor tiles or car sound deadening material making it easy to prise off,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PM169_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-PM169-30">[29]</a></sup> as well as freezing water in valveless pipes to enable repair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PM159_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-PM159-31">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>One of the largest mechanical uses of dry ice is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dry_ice_blasting" title="Dry ice blasting">blast cleaning</a>. Dry ice pellets are shot from a nozzle with <a href="/wiki/Compressed_air" title="Compressed air">compressed air</a>, combining the power of the speed of the pellets with the action of the sublimation. This can remove residues from industrial equipment. Examples of materials removed include ink, glue, oil, paint, mold and rubber. Dry ice blasting can replace sandblasting, steam blasting, water blasting or solvent blasting. The primary environmental residue of dry ice blasting is the sublimed CO<sub>2</sub>, thus making it a useful technique where residues from other blasting techniques are undesirable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup> Recently, blast cleaning has been introduced as a method of removing smoke damage from structures after fires.
</p><p>Dry ice is also useful for the de-gassing of flammable vapours from storage tanks — the sublimation of dry ice pellets inside an emptied and vented tank causes an outrush of CO<sub>2</sub> that carries with it the flammable vapours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>The removal and fitting of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cylinder_liner" title="Cylinder liner">cylinder liners</a> in large engines requires the use of dry ice to chill and thus shrink the liner so that it freely slides into the engine block. When the liner then warms up, it expands, and the resulting <a href="/wiki/Interference_fit" title="Interference fit">interference fit</a> holds it tightly in place.  Similar procedures may be used in fabricating mechanical assemblies with a high resultant strength, replacing the need for pins, keys or welds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Dry-ice_blasting" title="Dry-ice blasting">Dry-ice blasting</a>, a form of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_cleaning" title="Carbon dioxide cleaning">carbon dioxide cleaning</a>, is used in a number of industrial applications.
</p><p>It is also useful as a <a href="/wiki/Cutting_fluid#CO2_Coolant" title="Cutting fluid">cutting fluid</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific">Scientific</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dry_ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Scientific">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laboratories" title="Laboratories">laboratories</a>, a slurry of dry ice in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_solvent" title="Organic solvent">organic solvent</a> is a useful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freezing_mixture" title="Freezing mixture">freezing mixture</a> for cold <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_reactions" title="Chemical reactions">chemical reactions</a> and for condensing solvents in <a href="/wiki/Rotary_evaporator" title="Rotary evaporator">rotary evaporators</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-House_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-House-35">[34]</a></sup> Dry ice and <a href="/wiki/Acetone" title="Acetone">acetone</a> forms a cold bath of −78 °C (−108 °F; 195 K), which can be used for instance to prevent <a href="/wiki/Thermal_runaway" title="Thermal runaway">thermal runaway</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Swern_oxidation" title="Swern oxidation">Swern oxidation</a>.
</p><p>The process of <a href="/wiki/Cloud_seeding" title="Cloud seeding">altering cloud precipitation</a> can be done with the use of dry ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keyescs_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Keyescs-36">[35]</a></sup> It was widely used in experiments in the US in the 1950s and early 1960s before it was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Silver_iodide" title="Silver iodide">silver iodide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keyescs_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Keyescs-36">[35]</a></sup> Dry ice has the advantage of being relatively cheap and completely non-toxic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keyescs_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-Keyescs-36">[35]</a></sup> Its main drawback is the need to be delivered directly into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supercooled" title="Supercooled">supercooled</a> region of clouds being seeded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keyescs_36-3"><a href="#cite_note-Keyescs-36">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dry_ice_bombs">Dry ice bombs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dry_ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Dry ice bombs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dry_ice_bomb" title="Dry ice bomb">Dry ice bomb</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dry_Ice_Bomb.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1272" data-file-width="1908" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Dry_Ice_Bomb.jpg/220px-Dry_Ice_Bomb.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Dry_Ice_Bomb.jpg/330px-Dry_Ice_Bomb.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Dry_Ice_Bomb.jpg/440px-Dry_Ice_Bomb.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dry_Ice_Bomb.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Dry_ice_bomb" title="Dry ice bomb">Dry ice bomb</a></div></div></div>
<p>A "dry ice bomb" is a balloon-like device using dry ice in a sealed container such as a <a href="/wiki/Plastic_bottle" title="Plastic bottle">plastic bottle</a>.  Water is usually added to accelerate the sublimation of the dry ice. As the dry ice <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sublimation_(chemistry)" title="Sublimation (chemistry)">sublimes</a>, pressure increases, causing the bottle to burst causing a loud noise that can be avoided when a #3 rubber stopper replaces the screw-on cap to make a <a href="/wiki/Water_rocket" title="Water rocket">water rocket</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Two-liter_bottle" title="Two-liter bottle">two-liter bottle</a>.
</p><p>The dry ice bomb device was featured on <i><a href="/wiki/MythBusters" title="MythBusters">MythBusters</a></i>, episode 57 <a href="/wiki/MythBusters_(2006_season)#Episode_57_.E2.80.93_.22Diet_Coke_and_Mentos.22" title="MythBusters (2006 season)">Mentos and Soda</a>, which first aired on August 9, 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup> It was also featured in an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Time_Warp_(TV_series)" title="Time Warp (TV series)">Time Warp</a></i>, as well as in an episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Archer_(TV_series)" title="Archer (TV series)">Archer</a></i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraterrestrial_occurrence">Extraterrestrial occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dry_ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Extraterrestrial occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following the Mars flyby of the <a href="/wiki/Mariner_4" title="Mariner 4">Mariner 4</a> spacecraft in 1966, scientists concluded that <a href="/wiki/Mars#Polar_caps" title="Mars">Mars' polar caps</a> consist entirely of dry ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NGMars_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-NGMars-38">[37]</a></sup> However, findings made in 2003 by researchers at the <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">California Institute of Technology</a> have shown that Mars' polar caps are almost completely made of water ice, and that dry ice only forms a thin surface layer that thickens and thins seasonally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NGMars_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-NGMars-38">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ScienceJournal_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-ScienceJournal-39">[38]</a></sup> A phenomenon named dry ice storms was proposed to occur over the polar regions of Mars. They are comparable to Earth's thunderstorms, with crystalline CO<sub>2</sub> taking the place of water in the clouds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NGMars2_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-NGMars2-40">[39]</a></sup> Dry ice is also proposed as a mechanism for <a href="/wiki/Geysers_on_Mars" title="Geysers on Mars">Geysers on Mars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LPS2006_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-LPS2006-41">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2012, the <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">European Space Agency</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Venus_Express" title="Venus Express">Venus Express</a> probe detected a cold layer in the atmosphere of <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> where temperatures are close to the triple point of carbon dioxide and it is possible that flakes of dry ice precipitate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>Observations from the <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a> flyby by <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">Voyager 2</a> indicates that dry ice is present on the surface of its large moons <a href="/wiki/Ariel_(moon)" title="Ariel (moon)">Ariel</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-43">[42]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Umbriel_(moon)" title="Umbriel (moon)">Umbriel</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-43">[42]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Titania_(moon)" title="Titania (moon)">Titania</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_43-2"><a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-43">[42]</a></sup> Scientist speculate that the magnetic field of Uranus contributes to the generation of CO2 ice on the surfaces of its moons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CartwrightEmery2015_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-CartwrightEmery2015-44">[43]</a></sup> Voyager 2 observations of Neptune's moon <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">Triton</a> suggested the presence of dry ice on the surface, though follow up observations indicate that the carbon ices on the surface are carbon monoxide but that the moon's crust is composed of a significant quantity of dry ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lellouchde_Bergh2010_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lellouchde_Bergh2010-45">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dry_ice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dry_ice_pellet_subliming_in_water.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="727" data-file-width="1206" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Dry_ice_pellet_subliming_in_water.jpg/220px-Dry_ice_pellet_subliming_in_water.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Dry_ice_pellet_subliming_in_water.jpg/330px-Dry_ice_pellet_subliming_in_water.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Dry_ice_pellet_subliming_in_water.jpg/440px-Dry_ice_pellet_subliming_in_water.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dry_ice_pellet_subliming_in_water.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dry ice pellet subliming in water, releasing thick white fog.</div></div></div>
<p>Prolonged exposure to dry ice can cause severe skin damage through <a href="/wiki/Frostbite" title="Frostbite">frostbite</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a> produced may also hinder attempts to withdraw from contact in a safe manner. Because it sublimes into large quantities of carbon dioxide gas, which could pose a danger of <a href="/wiki/Hypercapnia" title="Hypercapnia">hypercapnia</a>, dry ice should only be exposed to open air in a well-ventilated environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PM169_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-PM169-30">[29]</a></sup> For this reason, dry ice is assigned the <a href="/wiki/List_of_S-phrases" title="List of S-phrases">S-phrase</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S9:_Keep_container_in_a_well-ventilated_place" title="S9: Keep container in a well-ventilated place"><abbr class="abbr" title="S9: Keep container in a well-ventilated place">S9</abbr></a> in the context of laboratory safety. Industrial dry ice may contain contaminants that make it unsafe for direct contact with foodstuffs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup> Tiny dry ice pellets used in dry ice blast cleaning do not contain oily residues.
</p><p>Although dry ice is not classified as a dangerous substance by the <a href="/wiki/CLP_Regulation" title="CLP Regulation">European Union</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32008R1272_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-32008R1272-47">[46]</a></sup> or as a hazardous material by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Transportation" title="United States Department of Transportation">United States Department of Transportation</a> for ground transportation, when shipped by air or water, it is regulated as a dangerous good and <a href="/wiki/International_Air_Transport_Association" title="International Air Transport Association">IATA</a> packing instruction 954 (IATA PI 954) requires that it be labeled specially, including a diamond-shaped black-and white label, <a href="/wiki/List_of_UN_numbers_1801_to_1900" title="List of UN numbers 1801 to 1900">UN 1845</a>. Also, arrangements must be in place to ensure adequate ventilation so that pressure build-up does not rupture the packaging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">Federal Aviation Administration</a> in the US allows airline passengers to carry up to 2.5 kg (5.5 lb) per person either as <a href="/wiki/Checked_baggage" title="Checked baggage">checked baggage</a> or carry-on baggage, when used to refrigerate perishables.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
