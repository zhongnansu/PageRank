<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Syngas</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Not to be confused with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthetic_gasoline" title="Synthetic gasoline">synthetic gasoline</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Woodgas_Flame.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1721" data-file-width="846" height="448" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Woodgas_Flame.jpg/220px-Woodgas_Flame.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Woodgas_Flame.jpg/330px-Woodgas_Flame.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Woodgas_Flame.jpg/440px-Woodgas_Flame.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Woodgas_Flame.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Wood_gas" title="Wood gas">Wood gas</a>, a type of syngas, burning</div></div></div>
<p><b>Syngas</b>, or <b>synthesis gas</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Fuel_gas" title="Fuel gas">fuel gas</a> mixture consisting primarily of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>, and very often some <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. The name comes from its use as <a href="/wiki/Reaction_intermediate" title="Reaction intermediate">intermediates</a> in creating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthetic_natural_gas" title="Synthetic natural gas">synthetic natural gas</a> (SNG)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beychok_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beychok-1">[1]</a></sup> and for producing <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> or <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>. Syngas is usually a product of  <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">gasification</a> and the main application is <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">electricity generation</a>. Syngas is combustible and often used as a fuel of <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It has less than half the <a href="/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">energy density</a> of <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beychok_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Beychok-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Syngas can be produced from many sources, including natural gas, coal, biomass, or virtually any hydrocarbon feedstock, by reaction with steam (<a href="/wiki/Steam_reforming" title="Steam reforming">steam reforming</a>), carbon dioxide (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dry_reforming" title="Dry reforming">dry reforming</a>) or oxygen (<a href="/wiki/Partial_oxidation" title="Partial oxidation">partial oxidation</a>). Syngas is a crucial intermediate resource for production of hydrogen, ammonia, methanol, and synthetic hydrocarbon fuels. Syngas is also used as an intermediate in producing  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synfuel" title="Synfuel">synthetic petroleum</a> for use as a <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lubricant" title="Lubricant">lubricant</a> via the <a href="/wiki/Fischer%E2%80%93Tropsch_process" title="Fischer–Tropsch process">Fischer–Tropsch process</a> and previously the <a href="/wiki/Mobil" title="Mobil">Mobil</a> <a href="/wiki/Gas_to_liquids#Methanol_to_Gasoline_process_(MTG)" title="Gas to liquids">methanol to gasoline</a> process.
</p><p>Production methods include <a href="/wiki/Steam_reforming" title="Steam reforming">steam reforming</a> of natural gas or liquid hydrocarbons to produce hydrogen, the <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">gasification</a> of coal,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>, and in some types of <a href="/wiki/Waste-to-energy" title="Waste-to-energy">waste-to-energy</a> gasification facilities.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Syngas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The chemical composition of syngas varies based on the raw materials and the processes. Syngas produced by coal gasification generally is a mixture of 30 to 60% carbon monoxide, 25 to 30% hydrogen, 5 to 15% carbon dioxide, and 0 to 5% methane. It also contains lesser amount of other gases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The main reaction that produces syngas, <a href="/wiki/Steam_reforming" title="Steam reforming">steam reforming</a>, is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endothermic_reaction" title="Endothermic reaction">endothermic reaction</a> with 206 kJ/mol methane needed for conversion.
</p><p>The first reaction, between incandescent coke and steam, is strongly endothermic, producing carbon monoxide (CO), and hydrogen <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> (<a href="/wiki/Water_gas" title="Water gas">water gas</a> in older terminology). When the coke bed has cooled to a temperature at which the endothermic reaction can no longer proceed, the steam is then replaced by a blast of air.
</p><p>The second and third reactions then take place, producing an <a href="/wiki/Exothermic_reaction" title="Exothermic reaction">exothermic reaction</a>—forming initially carbon dioxide and raising the temperature of the coke bed—followed by the second endothermic reaction, in which the latter is converted to carbon monoxide, CO. The overall reaction is exothermic, forming "producer gas" (older terminology). Steam can then be re-injected, then air etc., to give an endless series of cycles until the coke is finally consumed.  Producer gas has a much lower energy value, relative to water gas, due primarily to dilution with atmospheric nitrogen.  Pure oxygen can be substituted for air to avoid the dilution effect, producing gas of much higher calorific value.
</p><p>When used as an intermediate in the large-scale, industrial synthesis of hydrogen (principally used in the production of <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>), it is also produced from <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> (via the steam reforming reaction) as follows:

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_technologies">Alternative technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Syngas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Alternative technologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biomass_catalytic_partial_oxidation">Biomass catalytic partial oxidation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Syngas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Biomass catalytic partial oxidation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Conversion of biomass to syngas is typically low-yield. The University of Minnesota developed a metal catalyst that reduces the biomass reaction time by up to a factor of 100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The catalyst can be operated at atmospheric pressure and reduces char. The entire process is autothermic and therefore heating is not required.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_dioxide_and_hydrogen">Carbon dioxide and hydrogen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Syngas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Carbon dioxide and hydrogen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Microwave_energy">Microwave energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Syngas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Microwave energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>CO<sub>2</sub> can be split into CO and then combined with hydrogen to form syngas <a class="external autonumber" href="http://cambridgenanosystems.com/" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>. A method for production of carbon monoxide from carbon dioxide by treating it with microwave radiation is being examined by the solar fuels-project of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dutch_Institute_For_Fundamental_Energy_Research" title="Dutch Institute For Fundamental Energy Research">Dutch Institute For Fundamental Energy Research</a>. This technique was alleged to have been used during the Cold war in Russian nuclear submarines to allow them to get rid of CO<sub>2</sub> gas without leaving a bubble trail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Publicly available journals published during the Cold War indicate that American submarines used conventional chemical <a href="/wiki/Scrubber" title="Scrubber">scrubbers</a> to remove CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Documents released after the sinking of the <a href="/wiki/Kursk_submarine_disaster" title="Kursk submarine disaster">Kursk</a>, a Cold War era <a href="/wiki/Oscar-class_submarine" title="Oscar-class submarine">Oscar-class submarine</a>, indicate that <a href="/wiki/Potassium_superoxide" title="Potassium superoxide">potassium superoxide</a> scrubbers were used to remove carbon dioxide on that vessel.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_energy">Solar energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Syngas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Solar energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Heat generated by <a href="/wiki/Concentrated_solar_power" title="Concentrated solar power">concentrated solar power</a> may be used to drive thermochemical reactions to split carbon dioxide to carbon monoxide or to make hydrogen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sunshine_to_Petrol_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sunshine_to_Petrol-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">Natural gas</a> may be used as a feedstock in a facility that integrates concentrated solar power with a power plant fueled by natural gas augmented by syngas while the sun is shining.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SunShot_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-SunShot-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT41013_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT41013-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PNNL41113_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-PNNL41113-13">[13]</a></sup> The Sunshine-to-Petrol project has developed a device allowing for efficient production using this technique. It is called the Counter-Rotating Ring Receiver Reactor Recuperator, or <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Counter-Rotating_Ring_Receiver_Reactor_Recuperator&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Counter-Rotating Ring Receiver Reactor Recuperator (page does not exist)">CR5</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_energy">Wind energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Syngas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Wind energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>An airborne wind energy system has been proposed to supply heat to the steam reforming reaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> This avoids burning natural gas for the heat and radically simplifies the steam reformer.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Co-electrolysis">Co-electrolysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Syngas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Co-electrolysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>By employing co-electrolysis, i.e. the electrochemical conversion of steam and carbon dioxide with the use of renewably generated electricity, syngas can be produced in the framework of a CO<sub>2</sub>-valorization scenario, allowing for a closed carbon cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electricity">Electricity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Syngas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Electricity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Use of electricity to extract carbon dioxide from water<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and then water gas shift to syngas has been trialled by the US Naval Research Lab. This process becomes cost effective if the price of electricity is below $20/MWh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renewable_sources">Renewable sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Syngas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Renewable sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Electricity generated from <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable sources</a> is also used to process carbon dioxide and water into syngas through the <a href="/wiki/High-temperature_electrolysis" title="High-temperature electrolysis">high-temperature electrolysis</a>. This is an attempt to maintain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_neutral" title="Carbon neutral">carbon neutral</a> in the generation process. <a href="/wiki/Audi" title="Audi">Audi</a>, in partnership with company named Sunfire, opened a pilot plant in November 2014 to generate <a href="/wiki/E-diesel" title="E-diesel">e-diesel</a> using this process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Syngas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_lighting">Gas lighting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Syngas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Gas lighting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Coal_gasification" title="Coal gasification">Coal gasification</a> processes to create syngas were used for many years to manufacture <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_manufactured_gas" title="History of manufactured gas">illuminating gas</a> (<a href="/wiki/Coal_gas" title="Coal gas">coal gas</a>) for <a href="/wiki/Gas_lighting" title="Gas lighting">gas lighting</a>, cooking and to some extent, heating, before <a href="/wiki/Electric_light" title="Electric light">electric lighting</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> infrastructure became widely available.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The syngas produced in waste-to-energy gasification facilities can be used to generate electricity.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_capacity">Energy capacity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Syngas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Energy capacity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Syngas that is not methanized typically has a lower heating value of 120 BTU/<a href="/wiki/Standard_cubic_foot" title="Standard cubic foot">scf</a> .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-synheat_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-synheat-27">[27]</a></sup> Untreated syngas can be run in hybrid turbines that allow for greater efficiency because of their lower operating temperatures, and extended part lifetime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-synheat_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-synheat-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sponge_iron">Sponge iron</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Syngas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Sponge iron">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Syngas is used to directly reduce <a href="/wiki/Iron_ore" title="Iron ore">iron ore</a> to <a href="/wiki/Direct_reduced_iron" title="Direct reduced iron">sponge iron</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diesel">Diesel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Syngas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Diesel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Syngas can be used in the <a href="/wiki/Fischer%E2%80%93Tropsch_process" title="Fischer–Tropsch process">Fischer–Tropsch process</a> to produce diesel, or converted into e.g. <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_ether" title="Dimethyl ether">dimethyl ether</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalytic" title="Catalytic">catalytic</a> processes.
</p><p>If the syngas is post-treated by cryogenic processing, it should be taken into account that this technology has great difficulty in recovering pure <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> if relatively large volumes of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> are present due to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> having very similar boiling points which are –191.5 °C and –195.79 °C respectively. Certain process technology selectively removes <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complexation" title="Complexation">complexation</a>/<a href="/wiki/Decomplexation" title="Decomplexation">decomplexation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> with cuprous aluminum chloride (<span class="chemf nowrap">CuAlCl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>) dissolved in an organic liquid such as <a href="/wiki/Toluene" title="Toluene">toluene</a>. The purified <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> can have a purity greater than 99%, which makes it a good feedstock for the chemical industry. The reject gas from the system can contain <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">ethane</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>.  The reject gas can be further processed on a <a href="/wiki/Pressure_swing_adsorption" title="Pressure swing adsorption">pressure swing adsorption</a> system to remove <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, and the hydrogen and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> can be recombined in the proper ratio for catalytic methanol production, Fischer-Tropsch diesel, etc. Cryogenic purification, being very energy-intensive, is not well suited to simply making fuel, because of the greatly reduced <a href="/wiki/Net_energy_gain" title="Net energy gain">net energy gain</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
