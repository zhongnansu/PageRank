<p><b>Hydrochloric acid</b> is a colorless <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_chemistry" title="Inorganic chemistry">inorganic</a> chemical system with the formula <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O:HCl</span>. Hydrochloric acid has a distinctive <a href="/wiki/Pungency" title="Pungency">pungent</a> smell. It is mainly produced as a precursor to <a href="/wiki/Vinyl_chloride" title="Vinyl chloride">vinyl chloride</a> for <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">PVC</a>. It is classified as <a href="/wiki/Acid_strength" title="Acid strength">strongly acidic</a> and can attack the skin over a wide composition range, since the hydrogen chloride practically dissociates completely in solution.
</p><p>Hydrochloric acid is the simplest chlorine-based acid system containing water. It is a solution of hydrogen chloride and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, and a variety of other chemical species, including hydronium and chloride ions. It is an important chemical reagent and industrial chemical, used primarily in the production of <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">polyvinyl chloride</a> for plastic. In households, diluted hydrochloric acid is often used as a <a href="/wiki/Descaling_agent" title="Descaling agent">descaling agent</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Food_industry" title="Food industry">food industry</a>, hydrochloric acid used as a food additive and in the production of <a href="/wiki/Gelatin" title="Gelatin">gelatin</a>. Hydrochloric acid is also used in <a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leather</a> processing.
</p><p>Hydrochloric acid was discovered by the alchemist <a href="/wiki/Jabir_ibn_Hayyan" title="Jabir ibn Hayyan">Jabir ibn Hayyan</a> around the year 800 AD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
Hydrochloric acid was historically called <b>acidum salis</b>, <b>muriatic acid</b>, and <b>spirits of salt</b> because it was produced from <a href="/wiki/Halite" title="Halite">rock salt</a> and "green vitriol" (<a href="/wiki/Iron(II)_sulfate" title="Iron(II) sulfate">Iron(II) sulfate</a>) (by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basilius_Valentinus" title="Basilius Valentinus">Basilius Valentinus</a> in the 15th century) and later from the chemically similar <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">common salt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> (by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johann_Rudolph_Glauber" title="Johann Rudolph Glauber">Johann Rudolph Glauber</a> in the 17th century). Free hydrochloric acid was first formally described in the 16th century by <a href="/wiki/Andreas_Libavius" title="Andreas Libavius">Libavius</a>. Later, it was used by chemists such as <a href="/wiki/Johann_Rudolf_Glauber" title="Johann Rudolf Glauber">Glauber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">Priestley</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Davy" title="Humphry Davy">Davy</a> in their scientific research. Unless pressurized or cooled, hydrochloric acid will turn into a gas if there is around 60% or less of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>. Hydrochloric <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acid</a> is also known as hydronium chloride.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrochloric_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hydrochloric acid was known to European alchemists as <i>spirits of salt</i> or <i>acidum salis</i> (salt acid). Both names are still used, especially in other languages, such as <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">Salzsäure</i>, <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <i lang="nl">Zoutzuur</i>, <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>: <i lang="sv">Saltsyra</i>, <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>: <i lang="tr">Tuz Ruhu</i>, <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>: <i lang="pl">kwas solny</i>, <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">Bulgarian</a>:  солна киселина, <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: соляная кислота, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh">鹽酸</span>,   <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>: 염산, and Taiwanese: iâm-sng. Gaseous HCl was called <i>marine acid air</i>. 
</p><p>The old (pre-<a href="/wiki/Systematic_name" title="Systematic name">systematic</a>) name <i>muriatic acid</i> has the same origin (<i>muriatic</i> means "pertaining to brine or salt", hence <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muriate" title="Muriate">muriate</a></i> means <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloride" title="Hydrochloride">hydrochloride</a>), and this name is still sometimes used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-muriatic_acid_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-muriatic_acid-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The name <i>hydrochloric acid</i> was coined by the French chemist <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Louis_Gay-Lussac" title="Joseph Louis Gay-Lussac">Joseph Louis Gay-Lussac</a> in 1814.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrochloric_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hydrochloric acid has been an important and frequently used chemical from early history and was discovered by the alchemist <a href="/wiki/Jabir_ibn_Hayyan" title="Jabir ibn Hayyan">Jabir ibn Hayyan</a> around the year 800 AD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Aqua_regia" title="Aqua regia">Aqua regia</a>, a mixture consisting of hydrochloric and <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric</a> acids, prepared by dissolving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sal_ammoniac" title="Sal ammoniac">sal ammoniac</a> in nitric acid, was described in the works of <a href="/wiki/Pseudo-Geber" title="Pseudo-Geber">Pseudo-Geber</a>, a 13th-century European alchemist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karpenko01_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Karpenko01-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ceh_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-ceh-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Other references suggest that the first mention of aqua regia is in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine" title="Byzantine">Byzantine</a> manuscripts dating to the end of the 13th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forbes1970_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes1970-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Free hydrochloric acid was first formally described in the 16th century by <a href="/wiki/Andreas_Libavius" title="Andreas Libavius">Libavius</a>, who prepared it by heating salt in clay <a href="/wiki/Crucible" title="Crucible">crucibles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leicester1971_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leicester1971-21">[21]</a></sup> Other authors claim that pure hydrochloric acid was first discovered by the German <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benedictine_monk" title="Benedictine monk">Benedictine monk</a> <a href="/wiki/Basil_Valentine" title="Basil Valentine">Basil Valentine</a> in the 15th century,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> when he heated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_salt" title="Common salt">common salt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iron(II)_sulfate" title="Iron(II) sulfate">green vitriol</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> whereas others argue that there is no clear reference to the preparation of pure hydrochloric acid until the end of the 16th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forbes1970_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes1970-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 17th century, <a href="/wiki/Johann_Rudolf_Glauber" title="Johann Rudolf Glauber">Johann Rudolf Glauber</a> from <a href="/wiki/Karlstadt_am_Main" title="Karlstadt am Main">Karlstadt am Main, Germany</a> used <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride salt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> for the preparation of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_sulfate" title="Sodium sulfate">sodium sulfate</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Mannheim_process" title="Mannheim process">Mannheim process</a>, releasing <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride" title="Hydrogen chloride">hydrogen chloride</a> gas. <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">Joseph Priestley</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leeds,_England" title="Leeds, England">Leeds, England</a> prepared pure hydrogen chloride in 1772,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and by 1808 <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Davy" title="Humphry Davy">Humphry Davy</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penzance,_England" title="Penzance, England">Penzance, England</a> had proved that the chemical composition included <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> in Europe, demand for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkaline" title="Alkaline">alkaline</a> substances increased. A new industrial process developed by <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Leblanc" title="Nicolas Leblanc">Nicolas Leblanc</a> of <a href="/wiki/Issoudun" title="Issoudun">Issoudun, France</a> enabled cheap large-scale production of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">sodium carbonate</a> (soda ash). In this <a href="/wiki/Leblanc_process" title="Leblanc process">Leblanc process</a>, common salt is converted to soda ash, using sulfuric acid, limestone, and coal, releasing hydrogen chloride as a by-product. Until the British <a href="/wiki/Alkali_Act_1863" title="Alkali Act 1863">Alkali Act 1863</a> and similar legislation in other countries, the excess HCl was vented into the air. After the passage of the act, soda ash producers were obliged to absorb the waste gas in water, producing hydrochloric acid on an industrial scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ceh_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-ceh-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aftalion_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-aftalion-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 20th century, the Leblanc process was effectively replaced by the <a href="/wiki/Solvay_process" title="Solvay process">Solvay process</a> without a hydrochloric acid by-product. Since hydrochloric acid was already fully settled as an important chemical in numerous applications, the commercial interest initiated other production methods, some of which are still used today. After the year 2000, hydrochloric acid is mostly made by absorbing by-product hydrogen chloride from <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid#Production" title="Hydrochloric acid">industrial organic compounds production</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ceh_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-ceh-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aftalion_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-aftalion-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G&amp;E_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-G&amp;E-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since 1988, hydrochloric acid has been listed as a Table II precursor under the 1988 <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_Against_Illicit_Traffic_in_Narcotic_Drugs_and_Psychotropic_Substances" title="United Nations Convention Against Illicit Traffic in Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances">United Nations Convention Against Illicit Traffic in Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances</a> because of its use in the production of <a href="/wiki/Heroin" title="Heroin">heroin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cocaine" title="Cocaine">cocaine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Methamphetamine" title="Methamphetamine">methamphetamine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-incb_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-incb-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_reactions">Structure and reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrochloric_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Structure and reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hydrochloric acid is the salt of <a href="/wiki/Hydronium" title="Hydronium">hydronium</a> ion, H<sub>3</sub>O<sup>+</sup> and chloride.  It is usually prepared by treating HCl with water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crc_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-crc-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-perry_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-perry-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>However, the speciation of hydrochloric acid is more complicated than this simple equation implies.  The structure of bulk water is infamously complex, and likewise, the formula H<sub>3</sub>O<sup>+</sup> is also a gross oversimplification of the true nature of the solvated proton, H<sup>+</sup><sub>(aq)</sub>, present in hydrochloric acid.  A combined IR, Raman, X-ray and neutron diffraction study of concentrated solutions of hydrochloric acid revealed that the primary form of H<sup>+</sup><sub>(aq)</sub> in these solutions is H<sub>5</sub>O<sub>2</sub><sup>+</sup>, which, along with the chloride anion, is hydrogen-bonded to neighboring water molecules in several different ways.  (In H<sub>5</sub>O<sub>2</sub><sup>+</sup>, the proton is sandwiched midway between two water molecules at 180°).  The author suggests that H<sub>3</sub>O<sup>+</sup> may become more important in dilute HCl solutions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>  (<i>See</i> <a href="/wiki/Hydronium" title="Hydronium">hydronium ion</a> <i>for further discussion of this issue.</i>)
</p><p>Hydrochloric acid is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strong_acid" title="Strong acid">strong acid</a>, since it is completely dissociated in water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crc_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-crc-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-perry_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-perry-30">[30]</a></sup> It can therefore be used to prepare salts containing the Cl<sup>–</sup> anion called <i>chlorides</i>.
</p><p>As a strong acid, hydrogen chloride has a large <i>K</i><sub>a</sub>. Theoretical attempts to assign the p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> of hydrogen chloride have been made, with the most recent estimate being −5.9.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> However, it is important to distinguish between hydrogen chloride gas and hydrochloric acid.  Due to the <a href="/wiki/Leveling_effect" title="Leveling effect">leveling effect</a>, except when highly concentrated and behavior deviates from ideality, <i>hydrochloric acid</i> (aqueous HCl) is only as acidic as the strongest proton donor available in water, the aquated proton (popularly known as "hydronium ion").  When chloride salts such as NaCl are added to aqueous HCl, they have only a minor effect on <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a>, indicating that Cl<sup>−</sup> is a very weak <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conjugate_base" title="Conjugate base">conjugate base</a> and that HCl is fully dissociated in aqueous solution.  Dilute solutions of HCl have a pH close to that predicted by assuming full dissociation into hydrated H<sup>+</sup> and Cl<sup>−</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>Of the <a href="/wiki/Acid_strength#Common_strong_acids" title="Acid strength">six common</a> strong <a href="/wiki/Mineral_acid" title="Mineral acid">mineral acids</a> in chemistry, hydrochloric acid is the monoprotic acid least likely to undergo an interfering <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">oxidation-reduction</a> reaction. It is one of the least hazardous strong acids to handle; despite its acidity, it consists of the non-reactive and non-toxic chloride ion. Intermediate-strength hydrochloric acid solutions are quite stable upon storage, maintaining their concentrations over time. These attributes, plus the fact that it is available as a pure <a href="/wiki/Reagent" title="Reagent">reagent</a>, make hydrochloric acid an excellent acidifying reagent.
</p><p>Hydrochloric acid is the preferred acid in <a href="/wiki/Titration" title="Titration">titration</a> for determining the amount of <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">bases</a>. Strong acid titrants give more precise results due to a more distinct endpoint. <a href="/wiki/Azeotrope" title="Azeotrope">Azeotropic</a>, or "constant-boiling", hydrochloric acid (roughly 20.2%) can be used as a <a href="/wiki/Primary_standard" title="Primary standard">primary standard</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_analysis" title="Chemical analysis">quantitative analysis</a>, although its exact concentration depends on the <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> when it is prepared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hydrochloric acid is frequently used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_analysis" title="Chemical analysis">chemical analysis</a> to prepare ("digest") samples for analysis. Concentrated hydrochloric acid dissolves many metals and forms oxidized metal chlorides and hydrogen gas. It also reacts with basic compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> or <a href="/wiki/Copper(II)_oxide" title="Copper(II) oxide">copper(II) oxide</a>, forming the dissolved chlorides that can be analyzed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crc_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-crc-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-perry_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-perry-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Physical_property" title="Physical property">Physical properties</a> of hydrochloric acid, such as <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">boiling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">melting points</a>, <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a>, and <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a>, depend on the <a href="/wiki/Concentration" title="Concentration">concentration</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molarity" title="Molarity">molarity</a> of HCl in the aqueous solution. They range from those of water at very low concentrations approaching 0% HCl to values for fuming hydrochloric acid at over 40% HCl.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crc_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-crc-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-perry_30-3"><a href="#cite_note-perry-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aspen_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-aspen-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hydrochloric acid as the binary (two-component) mixture of HCl and H<sub>2</sub>O has a constant-boiling <a href="/wiki/Azeotrope" title="Azeotrope">azeotrope</a> at 20.2% HCl and 108.6 °C (227 °F). There are four constant-<a href="/wiki/Crystallization" title="Crystallization">crystallization</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eutectic" title="Eutectic">eutectic points</a> for hydrochloric acid, between the <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystal</a> form of HCl·H<sub>2</sub>O (68% HCl), HCl·2H<sub>2</sub>O (51% HCl), HCl·3H<sub>2</sub>O (41% HCl), HCl·6H<sub>2</sub>O (25% HCl), and ice (0% HCl). There is also a metastable eutectic point at 24.8% between ice and the HCl·3H<sub>2</sub>O crystallization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aspen_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-aspen-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrochloric_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hydrochloric acid is prepared by dissolving <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride" title="Hydrogen chloride">hydrogen chloride</a> in water. Hydrogen chloride can be generated in many ways, and thus several precursors to hydrochloric acid exist. The large-scale production of hydrochloric acid is almost always integrated with the industrial scale <a href="/wiki/Chemical_industry" title="Chemical industry">production of other chemicals</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_market">Industrial market</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrochloric_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Industrial market">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hydrochloric acid is produced in solutions up to 38% HCl (concentrated grade). Higher concentrations up to just over 40% are chemically possible, but the <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a> rate is then so high that storage and handling require extra precautions, such as pressurization and cooling. Bulk industrial-grade is therefore 30% to 35%, optimized to balance transport efficiency and product loss through evaporation. In the United States, solutions of between 20% and 32% are sold as muriatic acid. Solutions for household purposes in the US, mostly cleaning, are typically 10% to 12%, with strong recommendations to dilute before use. In the United Kingdom, where it is sold as "Spirits of Salt" for domestic cleaning, the potency is the same as the US industrial grade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ceh_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-ceh-14">[14]</a></sup> In other countries, such as Italy, hydrochloric acid for domestic or industrial cleaning is sold as "Acido Muriatico", and its concentration ranges from 5% to 32%.
</p><p>Major producers worldwide include <a href="/wiki/Dow_Chemical_Company" title="Dow Chemical Company">Dow Chemical</a> at 2 million metric tons annually (2 Mt/year), calculated as HCl gas, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georgia_Gulf" title="Georgia Gulf">Georgia Gulf Corporation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tosoh" title="Tosoh">Tosoh Corporation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Akzo_Nobel" title="Akzo Nobel">Akzo Nobel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tessenderlo" title="Tessenderlo">Tessenderlo</a> at 0.5 to 1.5 Mt/year each. Total world production, for comparison purposes expressed as HCl, is estimated at 20 Mt/year, with 3 Mt/year from direct synthesis, and the rest as secondary product from organic and similar syntheses. By far, most hydrochloric acid is consumed captively by the producer. The open world market size is estimated at 5 Mt/year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ceh_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-ceh-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrochloric_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hydrochloric acid is a strong inorganic acid that is used in many industrial processes such as refining metal. The application often determines the required product quality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ceh_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-ceh-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pickling_of_steel">Pickling of steel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrochloric_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pickling of steel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the most important applications of hydrochloric acid is in the <a href="/wiki/Pickling_(metal)" title="Pickling (metal)">pickling</a> of steel, to remove rust or <a href="/wiki/Iron_oxide" title="Iron oxide">iron oxide</a> scale from iron or steel before subsequent processing, such as <a href="/wiki/Extrusion" title="Extrusion">extrusion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rolling_(metalworking)" title="Rolling (metalworking)">rolling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galvanization" title="Galvanization">galvanizing</a>, and other techniques.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ceh_14-6"><a href="#cite_note-ceh-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G&amp;E_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-G&amp;E-27">[27]</a></sup> Technical quality HCl at typically 18% concentration is the most commonly used pickling agent for the pickling of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_steel" title="Carbon steel">carbon steel</a> grades.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Production_of_organic_compounds">Production of organic compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrochloric_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Production of organic compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another major use of hydrochloric acid is in the production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_compounds" title="Organic compounds">organic compounds</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Vinyl_chloride" title="Vinyl chloride">vinyl chloride</a> and <a href="/wiki/1,2-Dichloroethane" title="1,2-Dichloroethane">dichloroethane</a> for <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">PVC</a>. This is often captive use, consuming locally produced hydrochloric acid that never actually reaches the open market. Other <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic</a> compounds produced with hydrochloric acid include <a href="/wiki/Bisphenol_A" title="Bisphenol A">bisphenol A</a> for <a href="/wiki/Polycarbonate" title="Polycarbonate">polycarbonate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Activated_carbon" title="Activated carbon">activated carbon</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ascorbic_acid" title="Ascorbic acid">ascorbic acid</a>, as well as numerous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical" title="Pharmaceutical">pharmaceutical</a> products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G&amp;E_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-G&amp;E-27">[27]</a></sup>

</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regeneration_of_ion_exchangers">Regeneration of ion exchangers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrochloric_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Regeneration of ion exchangers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>High-quality hydrochloric acid is used in the regeneration of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ion_exchange_resin" title="Ion exchange resin">ion exchange resins</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ion_exchange" title="Ion exchange">Cation exchange</a> is widely used to remove <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> such as Na<sup>+</sup> and Ca<sup>2+</sup> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aqueous" title="Aqueous">aqueous</a> solutions, producing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demineralized_water" title="Demineralized water">demineralized water</a>. The acid is used to rinse the cations from the resins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ceh_14-9"><a href="#cite_note-ceh-14">[14]</a></sup> Na<sup>+</sup> is replaced with H<sup>+</sup> and Ca<sup>2+</sup> with 2 H<sup>+</sup>.
</p><p>Ion exchangers and demineralized water are used in all chemical industries, drinking water production, and many food industries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ceh_14-10"><a href="#cite_note-ceh-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrochloric_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hydrochloric acid is used for a large number of small-scale applications, such as leather processing, purification of common salt, household cleaning,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> and building construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G&amp;E_27-6"><a href="#cite_note-G&amp;E-27">[27]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Petroleum#Extraction" title="Petroleum">Oil production</a> may be stimulated by injecting hydrochloric acid into the rock formation of an <a href="/wiki/Oil_well" title="Oil well">oil well</a>, dissolving a portion of the rock, and creating a large-pore structure. Oil well acidizing is a common process in the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea_oil" title="North Sea oil">North Sea oil</a> production industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ceh_14-11"><a href="#cite_note-ceh-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hydrochloric acid has been used for dissolving calcium carbonate, i.e. such things as de-scaling kettles and for cleaning mortar off brickwork, but it is a hazardous liquid which must be used with care. When used on brickwork the reaction with the mortar only continues until the acid has all been converted, producing <a href="/wiki/Calcium_chloride" title="Calcium chloride">calcium chloride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, and water:

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Presence_in_living_organisms">Presence in living organisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrochloric_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Presence in living organisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stomach_mucosal_layer_labeled.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="634" data-file-width="637" height="219" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Stomach_mucosal_layer_labeled.svg/220px-Stomach_mucosal_layer_labeled.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Stomach_mucosal_layer_labeled.svg/330px-Stomach_mucosal_layer_labeled.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Stomach_mucosal_layer_labeled.svg/440px-Stomach_mucosal_layer_labeled.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Stomach_mucosal_layer_labeled.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diagram of alkaline <a href="/wiki/Mucous_membrane" title="Mucous membrane">mucous layer</a> in stomach with mucosal defense mechanisms</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gastric_acid" title="Gastric acid">Gastric acid</a> is one of the main secretions of the stomach. It consists mainly of hydrochloric acid and acidifies the stomach content to a pH of 1 to 2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maton_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-maton-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>Chloride (Cl<sup>−</sup>) and hydrogen (H<sup>+</sup>) ions are secreted separately in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stomach_fundus" title="Stomach fundus">stomach fundus</a> region at the top of the stomach by <a href="/wiki/Parietal_cell" title="Parietal cell">parietal cells</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Gastric_mucosa" title="Gastric mucosa">gastric mucosa</a> into a secretory network called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canaliculus_(parietal_cell)" title="Canaliculus (parietal cell)">canaliculi</a> before it enters the stomach lumen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arthur_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-arthur-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>Gastric acid acts as a barrier against <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a> to prevent infections and is important for the digestion of food. Its low pH <a href="/wiki/Denaturation_(biochemistry)" title="Denaturation (biochemistry)">denatures</a> <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a> and thereby makes them susceptible to degradation by <a href="/wiki/Digestive_enzyme" title="Digestive enzyme">digestive enzymes</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Pepsin" title="Pepsin">pepsin</a>. The low pH also activates the enzyme precursor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pepsinogen" title="Pepsinogen">pepsinogen</a> into the active enzyme pepsin by self-cleavage. After leaving the stomach, the hydrochloric acid of the <a href="/wiki/Chyme" title="Chyme">chyme</a> is neutralized in the <a href="/wiki/Duodenum" title="Duodenum">duodenum</a> by <a href="/wiki/Sodium_bicarbonate" title="Sodium bicarbonate">sodium bicarbonate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maton_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-maton-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>The stomach itself is protected from the strong acid by the <a href="/wiki/Secretion" title="Secretion">secretion</a> of a thick <a href="/wiki/Mucus" title="Mucus">mucus</a> layer, and by <a href="/wiki/Secretin" title="Secretin">secretin</a> induced buffering with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_bicarbonate" title="Sodium bicarbonate">sodium bicarbonate</a>. <a href="/wiki/Heartburn" title="Heartburn">Heartburn</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peptic_ulcer" title="Peptic ulcer">peptic ulcers</a> can develop when these mechanisms fail. Drugs of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antihistaminic" title="Antihistaminic">antihistaminic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proton_pump_inhibitor" title="Proton pump inhibitor">proton pump inhibitor</a> classes can inhibit the production of acid in the stomach, and <a href="/wiki/Antacid" title="Antacid">antacids</a> are used to neutralize excessive existing acid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maton_39-2"><a href="#cite_note-maton-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Concentrated hydrochloric acid (fuming hydrochloric acid) forms acidic mists. Both the mist and the solution have a corrosive effect on human tissue, with the potential to damage respiratory organs, eyes, skin, and intestines irreversibly. Upon mixing hydrochloric acid with common <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidizing</a> chemicals, such as <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hypochlorite" title="Sodium hypochlorite">sodium hypochlorite</a> (bleach, NaClO) or <a href="/wiki/Potassium_permanganate" title="Potassium permanganate">potassium permanganate</a> (KMnO<sub>4</sub>), the toxic gas <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> is produced.

<p><a href="/wiki/Personal_protective_equipment" title="Personal protective equipment">Personal protective equipment</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Latex" title="Latex">latex</a> gloves, protective eye goggles, and chemical-resistant clothing and shoes will minimize risks when handling hydrochloric acid. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a> rates and regulates hydrochloric acid as a <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxic substance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/UN_number" title="UN number">UN number</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_Transport" title="Department of Transport">DOT</a> number is 1789. This number will be displayed on a placard on the container.
</p>
