<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AlapahaRiver2002.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1507" data-file-width="2010" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/AlapahaRiver2002.jpg/300px-AlapahaRiver2002.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/AlapahaRiver2002.jpg/450px-AlapahaRiver2002.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/AlapahaRiver2002.jpg/600px-AlapahaRiver2002.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AlapahaRiver2002.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Image of the entire surface water flow of the <a href="/wiki/Alapaha_River" title="Alapaha River">Alapaha River</a> near <a href="/wiki/Jennings,_Florida" title="Jennings, Florida">Jennings, Florida</a> going into a <a href="/wiki/Sinkhole" title="Sinkhole">sinkhole</a> leading to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floridan_Aquifer" title="Floridan Aquifer">Floridan Aquifer</a> <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a>.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Surface water</b> is <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> on the surface of the planet such as  in a <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetland</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a>. It can be contrasted with <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">atmospheric water</a>.
</p><p>Non-saline surface water uses  is replenished by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> and by recruitment from ground-water. It is lost through <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seepage" title="Seepage">seepage</a> into the ground where it becomes ground-water, used by plants for <a href="/wiki/Transpiration" title="Transpiration">transpiration</a>, extracted by mankind for agriculture, living, industry etc. or discharged to the sea where it becomes <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">saline</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conjunctive_use_of_ground_and_surface_water">Conjunctive use of ground and surface water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Conjunctive use of ground and surface water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Surface and groundwater are two separate entities, so they must be regarded as such. However, there is an ever-increasing need for management of the two as they are part of an interrelated system that is paramount when the demand for water exceeds the available supply (Fetter 464). Depletion of surface and ground water sources for public consumption (including industrial, commercial, and residential) is caused by over-pumping. <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">Aquifers</a> near river systems that are over-pumped have been known to deplete surface water sources as well. Research supporting this has been found in numerous water budgets for a multitude of cities.
</p><p>Response times for an aquifer are long (Young &amp; Bredehoeft 1972). However, a total ban on ground water usage during water recessions would allow surface water to retain better levels required for sustainable aquatic life. By reducing ground water pumping, the surface water supplies will be able to maintain their levels, as they recharge from direct <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">surface runoff</a>, etc.
</p>
