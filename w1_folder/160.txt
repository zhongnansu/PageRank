<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Fermentation</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(disambiguation)" title="Fermentation (disambiguation)">Fermentation (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fermenting.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="211" data-file-width="300" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Fermenting.jpg/220px-Fermenting.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/77/Fermenting.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fermenting.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fermentation in progress: Bubbles of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> form a froth on top of the fermentation mixture.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Fermentation</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolic</a> process that consumes <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a> in the absence of oxygen. The products are organic acids, gases, or <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a>. It occurs in <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">yeast</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, and also in oxygen-starved muscle cells, as in the case of <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid_fermentation" title="Lactic acid fermentation">lactic acid fermentation</a>. The science of fermentation is known as <a href="/wiki/Zymology" title="Zymology">zymology</a>.
</p><p>In microorganisms, fermentation is the primary means of producing <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a> by the degradation of organic nutrients <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_digestion" title="Anaerobic digestion">anaerobically</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prescott_Microbiology_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prescott_Microbiology-1">[1]</a></sup>  Humans have used fermentation to produce foodstuffs and beverages since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neolithic_age" title="Neolithic age">Neolithic age</a>. For example, fermentation is used for preservation in a process that produces <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactic acid</a> as found in such sour <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">foods</a> as <a href="/wiki/Pickled_cucumber" title="Pickled cucumber">pickled cucumbers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kimchi" title="Kimchi">kimchi</a>, wine and <a href="/wiki/Yogurt" title="Yogurt">yogurt</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Fermentation_in_food_processing" title="Fermentation in food processing">fermentation in food processing</a>), as well as for producing alcoholic beverages such as <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Fermentation_in_winemaking" title="Fermentation in winemaking">fermentation in winemaking</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a>. Fermentation occurs within the gastrointestinal tracts of all animals, including humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermentation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Below are some definitions of fermentation. They range from informal, general usages to more scientific definitions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>Preservation methods for food via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microorganisms" title="Microorganisms">microorganisms</a> (general use).</li>
<li>Any process that produces alcoholic beverages or acidic dairy products (general use).</li>
<li>Any large-scale microbial process occurring with or without air (common definition used in industry).</li>
<li>Any energy-releasing metabolic process that takes place only under anaerobic conditions (becoming more scientific).</li>
<li>Any metabolic process that releases energy from a sugar or other organic molecule, does not require oxygen or an electron transport system, and uses an organic molecule as the final electron acceptor (most scientific).</li></ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_role">Biological role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermentation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Biological role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Along with <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerobic_respiration" title="Aerobic respiration">aerobic respiration</a>, fermentation is a way of extracting energy from molecules, but it is the only one common to all bacteria and <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryotes</a>. It is therefore considered the oldest <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_pathway" title="Metabolic pathway">metabolic pathway</a>, suitable for an environment that did not yet have oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tobin_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tobin-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>389</span></sup> Yeast, a form of <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungus</a>, occurs in almost any environment capable of supporting microbes, from the skins of fruits to the guts of insects and mammals and the deep ocean, and they harvest sugar-rich materials to produce ethanol and carbon dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The basic mechanism for fermentation remains present in all cells of higher organisms. <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">Mammalian</a> <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">muscle</a> carries out the fermentation that occurs during periods of intense exercise where oxygen supply becomes limited, resulting in the creation of <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactic acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voet_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Voet-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>63</span></sup> In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Invertebrates" title="Invertebrates">invertebrates</a>, fermentation also produces <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Succinate" title="Succinate">succinate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alanine" title="Alanine">alanine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>141</span></sup>
</p><p>Fermentative bacteria play an essential role in the production of methane in habitats ranging from the <a href="/wiki/Rumen" title="Rumen">rumens</a> of cattle to sewage digesters and freshwater sediments. They produce hydrogen, carbon dioxide, <a href="/wiki/Formate" title="Formate">formate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Acetate" title="Acetate">acetate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxylic acids</a>; and then consortia of microbes convert the carbon dioxide and acetate to methane. Acetogenic bacteria oxidize the acids, obtaining more acetate and either hydrogen or formate. Finally, <a href="/wiki/Methanogenesis" title="Methanogenesis">methanogens</a> (which are in the domain <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archea" title="Archea">Archea</a></i>) convert acetate to methane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biochemical_overview">Biochemical overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermentation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Biochemical overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cellular_respiration.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="887" data-file-width="880" height="262" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Cellular_respiration.gif/260px-Cellular_respiration.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Cellular_respiration.gif/390px-Cellular_respiration.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Cellular_respiration.gif/520px-Cellular_respiration.gif 2x" width="260"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cellular_respiration.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Comparison of aerobic respiration and most known fermentation types in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eucaryota" title="Eucaryota">eucaryotic</a> cell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Numbers in circles indicate counts of carbon atoms in molecules, C6 is <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>12</sub>O<sub>6</sub>, C1 <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> CO<sub>2</sub>. <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrion" title="Mitochondrion">Mitochondrial</a> outer membrane is omitted.</div></div></div><p>Fermentation reacts <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NADH" title="NADH">NADH</a> with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endogeny" title="Endogeny">endogenous</a>, <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic</a> <a href="/wiki/Electron_acceptor" title="Electron acceptor">electron acceptor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prescott_Microbiology_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Prescott_Microbiology-1">[1]</a></sup> Usually this is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyruvate" title="Pyruvate">pyruvate</a> formed from sugar through <a href="/wiki/Glycolysis" title="Glycolysis">glycolysis</a>. The reaction produces <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NAD%2B" title="NAD+">NAD+</a> and an organic product, typical examples being <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen gas (H<sub>2</sub>)</a>. However, more exotic compounds can be produced by fermentation, such as <a href="/wiki/Butyric_acid" title="Butyric acid">butyric acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Acetone" title="Acetone">acetone</a>. Fermentation products contain chemical energy (they are not fully oxidized), but are considered waste products, since they cannot be metabolized further without the use of oxygen.
</p><p>Fermentation normally occurs in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anaerobic_environment" title="Anaerobic environment">anaerobic environment</a>. In the presence of O<sub>2</sub>, NADH and pyruvate are used to generate ATP in <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">respiration</a>. This is called <a href="/wiki/Oxidative_phosphorylation" title="Oxidative phosphorylation">oxidative phosphorylation</a>, and it generates much more ATP than glycolysis alone. For that reason, fermentation is rarely utilized when oxygen is available. However, even in the presence of abundant oxygen, some strains of <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">yeast</a> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_cerevisiae" title="Saccharomyces cerevisiae">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</a></i> prefer fermentation to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerobic_respiration" title="Aerobic respiration">aerobic respiration</a> as long as there is an adequate supply of <a href="/wiki/Sugar#Chemistry" title="Sugar">sugars</a> (a phenomenon known as the <a href="/wiki/Crabtree_effect" title="Crabtree effect">Crabtree effect</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Some fermentation processes involve <a href="/wiki/Obligate_anaerobe" title="Obligate anaerobe">obligate anaerobes</a>, which cannot tolerate oxygen.
</p><p>Although <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">yeast</a> carries out the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(food)" title="Fermentation (food)">fermentation</a> in the production of <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> in <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wines</a>, and other alcoholic drinks, this is not the only possible agent: <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> carry out the fermentation in the production of <a href="/wiki/Xanthan_gum" title="Xanthan gum">xanthan gum</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Products">Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermentation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethanol">Ethanol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermentation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ethanol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fermentation" title="Ethanol fermentation">Ethanol fermentation</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ethanol_fermentation-1.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="344" data-file-width="512" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Ethanol_fermentation-1.svg/260px-Ethanol_fermentation-1.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Ethanol_fermentation-1.svg/390px-Ethanol_fermentation-1.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Ethanol_fermentation-1.svg/520px-Ethanol_fermentation-1.svg.png 2x" width="260"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ethanol_fermentation-1.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Overview of ethanol fermentation.</div></div></div>
<p>In ethanol fermentation, one glucose molecule is converted into two <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> molecules and two <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> molecules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Life_2004._pp._139-140_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Life_2004._pp._139-140-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stryer_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-stryer-13">[13]</a></sup> It is used to make bread dough rise: the carbon dioxide forms bubbles, expanding the dough into a foam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The ethanol is the intoxicating agent in alcoholic beverages such as wine, beer and liquor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Fermentation of feedstocks including <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corn" title="Corn">corn</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sugar_beets" title="Sugar beets">sugar beets</a> produces ethanol that is added to <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usda1_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-usda1-17">[17]</a></sup> In some species of fish, including <a href="/wiki/Goldfish" title="Goldfish">goldfish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carp" title="Carp">carp</a>, it provides energy when oxygen is scarce (along with lactic acid fermentation).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>The figure illustrates the process. Before fermentation, a glucose molecule breaks down into two pyruvate molecules. The energy from this <a href="/wiki/Exothermic_reaction" title="Exothermic reaction">exothermic reaction</a> is used to bind inorganic <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphates</a> to ATP and convert NAD+ to NADH. The pyruvates break down into two <a href="/wiki/Acetaldehyde" title="Acetaldehyde">acetaldehyde</a> molecules and give off two <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> molecules as a waste product. The acetaldehyde is reduced into ethanol using the energy and hydrogen from NADH and the NADH is oxidized into NAD+ so that the cycle may repeat. The reaction is catalysed by the enzymes pyruvate decarboxylase and alcohol dehydrogenase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Life_2004._pp._139-140_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Life_2004._pp._139-140-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lactic_acid">Lactic acid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermentation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lactic acid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid_fermentation" title="Lactic acid fermentation">Lactic acid fermentation</a></div>
<p><i>Homolactic fermentation</i> (producing only lactic acid) is the simplest type of fermentation. The pyruvate from glycolysis<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Introductory_Botany_2007._p._86_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Introductory_Botany_2007._p._86-19">[19]</a></sup> undergoes a simple <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">redox</a> reaction, forming <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactic acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_Biology._Anestis_2006._P._61_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-AP_Biology._Anestis_2006._P._61-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Volume_3._Thorpe_1922._p.159_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Volume_3._Thorpe_1922._p.159-21">[21]</a></sup> It is unique because it is one of the only respiration processes to not produce a gas as a byproduct.
Overall, one molecule of glucose (or any six-carbon sugar) is converted to two molecules of lactic acid:
</p>
<dl><dd>C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>12</sub>O<sub>6</sub> → 2 CH<sub>3</sub>CHOHCOOH<br/></dd></dl>
<p>It occurs in the muscles of animals when they need energy faster than the <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> can supply oxygen. It also occurs in some kinds of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacterium" title="Bacterium">bacteria</a> (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lactobacilli" title="Lactobacilli">lactobacilli</a>) and some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a>. It is the type of bacteria that converts <a href="/wiki/Lactose" title="Lactose">lactose</a> into lactic acid in <a href="/wiki/Yogurt" title="Yogurt">yogurt</a>, giving it its sour taste.  These lactic acid bacteria can carry out either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homolactic_fermentation" title="Homolactic fermentation">homolactic fermentation</a>, where the end-product is mostly lactic acid, or <i>Heterolactic fermentation</i>, where some lactate is further metabolized and results in ethanol and carbon dioxide<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_Biology._Anestis_2006._P._61_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-AP_Biology._Anestis_2006._P._61-20">[20]</a></sup> (via the <a href="/wiki/Phosphoketolase" title="Phosphoketolase">phosphoketolase</a> pathway), acetate, or other metabolic products, e.g.:
</p>
<dl><dd>C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>12</sub>O<sub>6</sub> → CH<sub>3</sub>CHOHCOOH + C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>5</sub>OH + CO<sub>2</sub><br/></dd></dl>
<p>If lactose is fermented (as in yogurts and cheeses), it is first converted into glucose and galactose (both six-carbon sugars with the same atomic formula):
</p>
<dl><dd>C<sub>12</sub>H<sub>22</sub>O<sub>11</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O → 2 C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>12</sub>O<sub>6</sub><br/></dd></dl>
<p>Heterolactic fermentation is in a sense intermediate between <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid_fermentation" title="Lactic acid fermentation">lactic acid fermentation</a>, and other types, e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_fermentation" title="Alcoholic fermentation">alcoholic fermentation</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Fermentation#Hydrogen_gas_production_in_fermentation" title="Fermentation">below</a>). The reasons to go further and convert lactic acid into anything else are:
</p>
<ul><li>The acidity of lactic acid impedes biological processes; this can be beneficial to the fermenting organism as it drives out competitors that are unadapted to the acidity; as a result, the food will have a longer shelf life (part of the reason foods are purposely fermented in the first place); however, beyond a certain point, the acidity starts affecting the organism that produces it.</li>
<li>The high concentration of lactic acid (the final product of fermentation) drives the equilibrium backwards (<a href="/wiki/Le_Chatelier%27s_principle" title="Le Chatelier's principle">Le Chatelier's principle</a>), decreasing the rate at which fermentation can occur, and slowing down growth.</li>
<li>Ethanol, into which lactic acid can be easily converted, is volatile and will readily escape, allowing the reaction to proceed easily. <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> is also produced, but it is only weakly acidic, and even more volatile than ethanol.</li>
<li>Acetic acid (another conversion product) is acidic, and not as volatile as ethanol; however, in the presence of limited oxygen, its creation from lactic acid releases additional energy. It is a lighter molecule than lactic acid, that forms fewer hydrogen bonds with its surroundings (due to having fewer groups that can form such bonds), thus is  more volatile and will also allow the reaction to move forward more quickly.</li>
<li>If <a href="/wiki/Propionic_acid" title="Propionic acid">propionic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Butyric_acid" title="Butyric acid">butyric acid</a>, and longer monocarboxylic acids are produced (see <a href="/wiki/Mixed_acid_fermentation" title="Mixed acid fermentation">mixed acid fermentation</a>), the amount of acidity produced per glucose consumed will decrease, as with ethanol, allowing faster growth.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrogen_gas">Hydrogen gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermentation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Hydrogen gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fermentative_hydrogen_production" title="Fermentative hydrogen production">Fermentative hydrogen production</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">Hydrogen</a> gas is produced in many types of fermentation (<a href="/wiki/Mixed_acid_fermentation" title="Mixed acid fermentation">mixed acid fermentation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Butyric_acid" title="Butyric acid">butyric acid</a> fermentation, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caproate" title="Caproate">caproate</a> fermentation, <a href="/wiki/Butanol" title="Butanol">butanol</a> fermentation, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glyoxylate" title="Glyoxylate">glyoxylate</a> fermentation), as a way to regenerate NAD<sup>+</sup> from NADH. <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">Electrons</a> are transferred to <a href="/wiki/Ferredoxin" title="Ferredoxin">ferredoxin</a>, which in turn is oxidized by <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenase" title="Hydrogenase">hydrogenase</a>, producing H<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Life_2004._pp._139-140_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Life_2004._pp._139-140-12">[12]</a></sup> Hydrogen gas is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Substrate_(biochemistry)" title="Substrate (biochemistry)">substrate</a> for <a href="/wiki/Methanogen" title="Methanogen">methanogens</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulfate-reducing_bacteria" title="Sulfate-reducing bacteria">sulfate reducers</a>, which keep the concentration of hydrogen low and favor the production of such an energy-rich compound,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
but hydrogen gas at a fairly high concentration can nevertheless be formed, as in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flatus" title="Flatus">flatus</a>.
</p><p>As an example of mixed acid fermentation, bacteria such as <i><a href="/wiki/Clostridium_pasteurianum" title="Clostridium pasteurianum">Clostridium pasteurianum</a></i> ferment glucose producing <a href="/wiki/Butyric_acid" title="Butyric acid">butyrate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and hydrogen gas:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
The reaction leading to acetate is:
</p>
<dl><dd>C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>12</sub>O<sub>6</sub> + 4 H<sub>2</sub>O → 2 CH<sub>3</sub>COO<sup>−</sup> + 2 HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup> + 4 H<sup>+</sup> + 4 H<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Glucose could theoretically be converted into just CO<sub>2</sub> and H<sub>2</sub>, but the global reaction releases little energy.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modes_of_operation">Modes of operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermentation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Modes of operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most industrial fermentation uses batch or fed-batch procedures, although continuous fermentation can be more economical if various challenges, particularly the difficulty of maintaining sterility, can be met.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Li_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Li-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Batch">Batch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermentation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Batch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a batch process, all the ingredients are combined and the reactions proceed without any further input. Batch fermentation has been used for millennia to make bread and alcoholic beverages, and it is still a common method, especially when the process is not well understood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cinar_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cinar-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1</span></sup> However, it can be expensive because the fermentor must be sterilized using high pressure steam between batches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Li_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Li-24">[24]</a></sup> Strictly speaking, there is often addition of small quantities of chemicals to control the pH or suppress foaming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cinar_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cinar-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>25</span></sup>
</p><p>Batch fermentation goes through a series of phases. There is a lag phase in which cells adjust to their environment; then a phase in which exponential growth occurs. Once many of the nutrients have been consumed, the growth slows and becomes non-exponential, but production of <i>secondary metabolites</i> (including commercially important antibiotics and enzymes) accelerates. This continues through a stationary phase after most of the nutrients have been consumed, and then the cells die.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cinar_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cinar-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>25</span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fed-batch">Fed-batch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermentation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Fed-batch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Fed-batch_culture" title="Fed-batch culture">Fed-batch culture</a></div>
<p>Fed-batch fermentation is a variation of batch fermentation where some of the ingredients are added during the fermentation. This allows greater control over the stages of the process. In particular, production of secondary metabolites can be increased by adding a limited quantity of nutrients during the non-exponential growth phase. Fed-batch operations are often sandwiched between batch operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cinar_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-Cinar-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmid_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schmid-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Open">Open</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermentation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Open">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The high cost of sterilizing the fermentor between batches can be avoided using various open fermentation approaches that are able to resist contamination. One is to use a naturally evolved mixed culture. This is particularly favored in wastewater treatment, since mixed populations can adapt to a wide variety of wastes. <a href="/wiki/Thermophile" title="Thermophile">Thermophilic</a> bacteria can produce lactic acid at temperatures of around 50 degrees Celsius, sufficient to discourage microbial contamination; and ethanol has been produced at a temperature of 70°C. This is just below its boiling point (78°C), making it easy to extract. <a href="/wiki/Halophile" title="Halophile">Halophilic</a> bacteria can produce bioplastics in hypersaline conditions. Solid-state fermentation adds a small amount of water to a solid substrate; it is widely used in the food industry to produce flavors, enzymes and organic acids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Li_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Li-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Continuous">Continuous</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermentation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Continuous">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In continuous fermentation, substrates are added and final products removed continuously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Li_24-3"><a href="#cite_note-Li-24">[24]</a></sup> There are three varieties: <a href="/wiki/Chemostat" title="Chemostat">chemostats</a>, which hold nutrient levels constant; <a href="/wiki/Turbidostat" title="Turbidostat">turbidostats</a>, which keep cell mass constant; and <a href="/wiki/Plug_flow_reactor_model" title="Plug flow reactor model">plug flow reactors</a> in which the culture medium flows steadily through a tube while the cells are recycled from the outlet to the inlet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmid_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schmid-26">[26]</a></sup> If the process works well, there is a steady flow of feed and effluent and the costs of repeatedly setting up a batch are avoided. Also, it can prolong the exponential growth phase and avoid byproducts that inhibit the reactions by continuously removing them. However, it is difficult to maintain a steady state and avoid contamination, and the design tends to be complex.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Li_24-4"><a href="#cite_note-Li-24">[24]</a></sup> Typically the fermentor must run for over 500 hours to be more economical than batch processors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmid_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-Schmid-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_human_use">History of human use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermentation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: History of human use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The use of <a href="/wiki/Fermentation_in_food_processing" title="Fermentation in food processing">fermentation</a>, particularly for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">beverages</a>, has existed since the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> and has been documented dating from 7000–6600 BCE in <a href="/wiki/Jiahu" title="Jiahu">Jiahu</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neolithic_China" title="Neolithic China">China</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcgovern_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcgovern-27">[27]</a></sup> 5000 BCE in India, Ayurveda mentions many Medicated Wines, 6000 BCE in Georgia,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcgoverng_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcgoverng-28">[28]</a></sup> 3150 BCE in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egypt</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cavalieri_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cavalieri-29">[29]</a></sup> 3000 BCE in <a href="/wiki/Babylon" title="Babylon">Babylon</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-30">[30]</a></sup> 2000 BCE in pre-Hispanic Mexico,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-30">[30]</a></sup> and 1500 BC in <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dirar_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dirar-31">[31]</a></sup> Fermented foods have a religious significance in <a href="/wiki/Chametz" title="Chametz">Judaism</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christianity_and_alcohol" title="Christianity and alcohol">Christianity</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Baltic_mythology" title="Baltic mythology">Baltic god</a> Rugutis was worshiped as the agent of fermentation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Portrait_of_Louis_Pasteur_in_his_laboratory_Wellcome_M0010355.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2739" data-file-width="3920" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Portrait_of_Louis_Pasteur_in_his_laboratory_Wellcome_M0010355.jpg/220px-Portrait_of_Louis_Pasteur_in_his_laboratory_Wellcome_M0010355.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Portrait_of_Louis_Pasteur_in_his_laboratory_Wellcome_M0010355.jpg/330px-Portrait_of_Louis_Pasteur_in_his_laboratory_Wellcome_M0010355.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Portrait_of_Louis_Pasteur_in_his_laboratory_Wellcome_M0010355.jpg/440px-Portrait_of_Louis_Pasteur_in_his_laboratory_Wellcome_M0010355.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Portrait_of_Louis_Pasteur_in_his_laboratory_Wellcome_M0010355.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Louis Pasteur in his laboratory</div></div></div>
<p>In 1837, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Cagniard_de_la_Tour" title="Charles Cagniard de la Tour">Charles Cagniard de la Tour</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theodor_Schwann" title="Theodor Schwann">Theodor Schwann</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Traugott_K%C3%BCtzing" title="Friedrich Traugott Kützing">Friedrich Traugott Kützing</a> independently published papers concluding, as a result of microscopic investigations, that yeast is a living organism that reproduces by <a href="/wiki/Budding" title="Budding">budding</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shurtleff_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shurtleff-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lengeler_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lengeler-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>6</span></sup> Schwann boiled grape juice to kill the yeast and found that no fermentation would occur until new yeast was added. However, a lot of chemists , including <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Lavoisier" title="Antoine Lavoisier">Antoine Lavoisier</a>, continued to view fermentation as a simple chemical reaction and rejected the notion that living organisms could be involved. This was seen as a reversion to <a href="/wiki/Vitalism" title="Vitalism">vitalism</a>, and was lampooned in an anonymous publication by <a href="/wiki/Justus_von_Liebig" title="Justus von Liebig">Justus von Liebig</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_W%C3%B6hler" title="Friedrich Wöhler">Friedrich Wöhler</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tobin_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tobin-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>108–109</span></sup>
</p><p>The turning point came when <a href="/wiki/Louis_Pasteur" title="Louis Pasteur">Louis Pasteur</a> (1822–1895), during the 1850s and 1860s, repeated Schwann's experiments and showed that fermentation is initiated by living organisms in a series of investigations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Volume_3._Thorpe_1922._p.159_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Volume_3._Thorpe_1922._p.159-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lengeler_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lengeler-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>6</span></sup> In 1857, Pasteur showed that lactic acid fermentation is caused by living organisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In 1860, he demonstrated that bacteria cause <a href="/wiki/Souring" title="Souring">souring</a> in milk, a process formerly thought to be merely a chemical change, and his work in identifying the role of microorganisms in food spoilage led to the process of <a href="/wiki/Pasteurization" title="Pasteurization">pasteurization</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In 1877, working to improve the French <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brewing_industry" title="Brewing industry">brewing industry</a>, Pasteur published his famous paper on fermentation, "<i>Etudes sur la Bière</i>", which was translated into English in 1879 as "Studies on fermentation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> He defined fermentation (incorrectly) as "Life without air",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Modern_History_Sourcebook_1895_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Modern_History_Sourcebook_1895-39">[39]</a></sup> but correctly showed that specific types of microorganisms cause specific types of fermentations and specific end-products.
</p><p>Although showing fermentation to be the result of the action of living microorganisms was a breakthrough, it did not explain the basic nature of the fermentation process, or prove that it is caused by the microorganisms that appear to be always present. Many scientists, including Pasteur, had unsuccessfully attempted to extract the fermentation <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzyme</a> from <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">yeast</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Modern_History_Sourcebook_1895_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Modern_History_Sourcebook_1895-39">[39]</a></sup> Success came in 1897 when the German chemist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eduard_Buechner" title="Eduard Buechner">Eduard Buechner</a> ground up yeast, extracted a juice from them, then found to his amazement that this "dead" liquid would ferment a sugar solution, forming carbon dioxide and alcohol much like living yeasts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Buechner's results are considered to mark the birth of biochemistry.  The "unorganized ferments" behaved just like the organized ones. From that time on, the term enzyme came to be applied to all ferments. It was then understood that fermentation is caused by enzymes that are produced by microorganisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> In 1907, Buechner won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in chemistry">Nobel Prize in chemistry</a> for his work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>Advances in microbiology and fermentation technology have continued steadily up until the present. For example, in the 1930s, it was discovered that microorganisms could be <a href="/wiki/Mutation" title="Mutation">mutated</a> with physical and chemical treatments to be higher-yielding, faster-growing, tolerant of less oxygen, and able to use a more concentrated medium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Strain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Selection_(biology)" title="Selection (biology)">selection</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridization</a> developed as well, affecting most modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(food)" title="Fermentation (food)">food fermentations</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fermentation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word "ferment" is derived from the Latin verb <i>fervere</i>, which means to boil. It is thought to have been first used in the late 14th century in <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">alchemy</a>, but only in a broad sense. It was not used in the modern scientific sense until around 1600.
</p>
