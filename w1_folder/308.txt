<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Must</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mosto.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="672" data-file-width="896" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Mosto.jpg/300px-Mosto.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Mosto.jpg/450px-Mosto.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Mosto.jpg/600px-Mosto.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mosto.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Grapes being pressed to create must.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Must</b> (from the Latin <i>vinum mustum</i>, "young wine") is freshly crushed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fruit_juice" title="Fruit juice">fruit juice</a> (usually <a href="/wiki/Grape_juice" title="Grape juice">grape juice</a>) that contains the skins, seeds, and stems of the fruit. The solid portion of the must is called <a href="/wiki/Pomace" title="Pomace">pomace</a> and typically makes up 7–23% of the total weight of the must. Making must is the first step in winemaking. Because of its high <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> content, typically between 10 and 15%, must is also used as a sweetener in a variety of cuisines. Unlike commercially sold grape juice, which is filtered and pasteurized, must is thick with particulate matter, opaque, and comes in various shades of brown and purple.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Winemaking">Winemaking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Must&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Winemaking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The length of time the pomace stays in the juice is critical for the final character of the wine. When the <a href="/wiki/Winemaker" title="Winemaker">winemaker</a> judges the time to be right, the juice is drained off the pomace, which is then pressed to extract the juice retained by the matrix. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yeast_(wine)" title="Yeast (wine)">Yeast</a> is added to the juice to begin the fermentation, while the pomace is often returned to the <a href="/wiki/Vineyard" title="Vineyard">vineyard</a> or <a href="/wiki/Orchard" title="Orchard">orchard</a> for use as <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizer</a>. A portion of selected unfermented must may be kept as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S%C3%BCssreserve" title="Süssreserve">Süssreserve</a>, to be added as a sweetening component before bottling. Some winemakers create a second batch of wine from the used pomace by adding a quantity of water equivalent to the juice removed, letting the mixture sit for 24 hours, and draining off the liquid. This wine may be used as a drink for the employees of the winemaker or as a basis for some <a href="/wiki/Pomace_brandy" title="Pomace brandy">pomace brandies</a>. <a href="/wiki/Grappa" title="Grappa">Grappa</a>, however, must by law be produced only from the pomace solids, with no water added.
The must can also be used for the production of Balsamic Vinegar, the special vinegar from Modena (Italy). Selected bacterial colonies or the lenta in superficie (slow surface) or lenta a truciolo (slow wood shavings) methods are used for acetification, and then there is a maturation phase. Both the acetification and the maturation take place in precious sessile oak (<a href="/wiki/Quercus_petraea" title="Quercus petraea">Quercus petraea</a>), chestnut, oak, mulberry, and juniper barrels. After a minimum maturation period of 60 days, a group of expert technicians will test the resulting product analytically and organoleptically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="For_mead_production">For mead production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Must&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: For mead production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This term is also used by meadmakers for the unfermented honey-water mixture that becomes <a href="/wiki/Mead" title="Mead">mead</a>. The analogous term in beer brewing is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wort_(brewing)" title="Wort (brewing)">wort</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_cookery">In cookery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Must&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In cookery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In ancient Greece, must condensed by boiling was called siraios (σιραίος) and was used as a sweetener in the kitchen in various recipes (and as a syrup over tiganitai (pan-cakes)). From the Greeks, the Romans in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">ancient Rome</a> also used the condensed must in <a href="/wiki/Cooking" title="Cooking">cooking</a>, as a sweetener. Must was boiled in <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a> kettles into a milder concentrate called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defrutum" title="Defrutum">defrutum</a> or a stronger concentrate called sapa. It was often used as a souring agent and preservative, especially in fruit dishes. 
</p><p>Currently, reduced must is used in Greek, other Balkan countries, French and Middle Eastern cookery as a syrup known as petimezi, <a href="/wiki/Pekmez" title="Pekmez"><i>pekmez</i> or <i>dibis</i></a>. In Greece, petimezi is a basic ingredient for a must-custard known as <a href="/wiki/Moustalevria" title="Moustalevria">moustalevria</a>, and a sweet-meal known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soutzoukos" title="Soutzoukos">soutzoukos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Churchkhela" title="Churchkhela">churchkhela</a>. The <i>Moustokoúloura</i>, or "must cookies" are also popular Greek cookies, which are based on a sweet dough made by kneading flour, olive oil, spice, and must. They are made in various shapes and sizes, and they are dark brown in color because of the must and the spice in them. In the wine making areas of South Africa must is used to make a sweet bun known as <a href="/wiki/Mosbolletjies" title="Mosbolletjies">Mosbolletjies</a>.
</p><p>The term petimezi is a Hellenized word of the Armenian/Trebizond term petmez. Petmez was a type of syrup that was made with berries of the White Mulberry tree; petmez was used in Byzantium (Trebizond was part of the Byzantine Empire), where White Mulberries grew in abundance, for their berries and for the silk worms that feed exclusively on Mulberry leaves.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_lead_poisoning_hypothesis">Roman lead poisoning hypothesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Must&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Roman lead poisoning hypothesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Geochemist Jerome Nriagu published an article in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_England_Journal_of_Medicine" title="New England Journal of Medicine">New England Journal of Medicine</a></i> in 1983 hypothesizing that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defrutum" title="Defrutum">defrutum</a> and <i>sapa</i> may have contained enough <a href="/wiki/Lead(II)_acetate" title="Lead(II) acetate">lead acetate</a> to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plumbism" title="Plumbism">toxic</a> to those who consumed them regularly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grout2011_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grout2011-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Christian_liturgy">In Christian liturgy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Must&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In Christian liturgy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic</a> <a href="/wiki/Mass_(liturgy)" title="Mass (liturgy)">Eucharistic liturgy</a>, must may be substituted for <a href="/wiki/Sacramental_wine" title="Sacramental wine">sacramental wine</a>, on condition that the <a href="/wiki/Ordinary_(officer)" title="Ordinary (officer)">ordinary</a> has granted permission for the benefit of a priest or lay person who should not, usually because of alcoholism, ingest wine; but in normal circumstances it may not be used in place of wine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDF_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDF-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Official Roman Catholic documents define must (<i>mustum</i> in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>) precisely as "grape juice that is either fresh or preserved by methods that suspend its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(wine)" title="Fermentation (wine)">fermentation</a> without altering its nature (for example, freezing)",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDF_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-CDF-3">[3]</a></sup> and it excludes <a href="/wiki/Pasteurization" title="Pasteurization">pasteurized</a> grape juice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>This teaching goes back at least to <a href="/wiki/Pope_Julius_I" title="Pope Julius I">Pope Julius I</a> (337–352), who is quoted in <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas" title="Thomas Aquinas">Thomas Aquinas</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Summa_Theologica" title="Summa Theologica">Summa Theologica</a></i> as having declared that in case of necessity, but only then, juice pressed from a grape could be used. Aquinas himself declared that it is forbidden to offer fresh must in the chalice, because this is unbecoming owing to the impurity of the must; but he added that in case of necessity it may be done.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Aquinas himself declared:
</p>
<blockquote><p>Must has already the species of wine, for its sweetness ["Aut dulcis musti Vulcano decoquit humorem"; <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Georgics" title="Georgics">Georg.</a></i> i, 295] indicates fermentation, which is "the result of its natural heat" (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorologica" title="Meteorologica">Meteor.</a></i> iv); consequently this sacrament can be made from must. ... It is forbidden to offer must in the chalice, as soon as it has been squeezed from the grape, since this is unbecoming owing to the impurity of the must. But in case of necessity it may be done: for it is said by the same Pope Julius, in the passage quoted in the argument: "If necessary, let the grape be pressed into the chalice."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Liturgical_norms">Liturgical norms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Must&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Liturgical norms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The latest document from the <a href="/wiki/Congregation_for_the_Doctrine_of_the_Faith" title="Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith">Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith</a> on the matter, issued on 24 July 2003, gave the following norms, which simplify those previously in force:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDF_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-CDF-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote><p>The <a href="/wiki/Ordinary_(officer)" title="Ordinary (officer)">Ordinary</a> is competent to give permission for an individual priest or layperson to use mustum for the celebration of the Eucharist. Permission can be granted habitually, for as long as the situation that occasioned the granting of permission continues (e.g., the priest is an alcoholic).
</p><p>When the principal celebrant at a concelebration has permission to use mustum, a chalice of normal wine is to be prepared for the concelebrants.
</p><p>Given the centrality of the celebration of the Eucharist in the life of a priest, one must proceed with great caution before admitting to Holy Orders those candidates unable to ingest alcohol without serious harm.
</p><p>
Attention should be paid to medical advances in the area of alcoholism and encouragement given to the production of unaltered mustum.</p></blockquote>
