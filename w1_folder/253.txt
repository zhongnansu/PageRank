<p><b>Silicon dioxide</b>, also known as <b>silica</b>, <b>silicic acid</b> or <b>silicic acid anydride</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> of <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formula</a> <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">Si</a><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a><sub>2</sub>, most commonly found in nature as <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a> and in various living organisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fern_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fern-6">[6]</a></sup> In many parts of the world, silica is the major constituent of <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a>. Silica is one of the most complex and most abundant families of materials, existing as a compound of several minerals and as synthetic product. Notable examples include <a href="/wiki/Fused_quartz" title="Fused quartz">fused quartz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fumed_silica" title="Fumed silica">fumed silica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silica_gel" title="Silica gel">silica gel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aerogel" title="Aerogel">aerogels</a>. It is used in structural materials, microelectronics (as an electrical insulator), and as components in the food and pharmaceutical industries.
</p><p>Inhaling finely divided crystalline silica is toxic  and can lead to severe inflammation of the <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">lung</a> tissue, <a href="/wiki/Silicosis" title="Silicosis">silicosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bronchitis" title="Bronchitis">bronchitis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lung_cancer" title="Lung cancer">lung cancer</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systemic_autoimmune_disease" title="Systemic autoimmune disease">systemic autoimmune diseases</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lupus" title="Lupus">lupus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rheumatoid_arthritis" title="Rheumatoid arthritis">rheumatoid arthritis</a>.
</p><p>Uptake of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amorphous" title="Amorphous">amorphous</a> silicon dioxide, in high doses, leads to non-permanent short-term inflammation, where all effects heal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SiO2repeat.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="371" data-file-width="588" height="95" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/SiO2repeat.png/150px-SiO2repeat.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/SiO2repeat.png/225px-SiO2repeat.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/SiO2repeat.png/300px-SiO2repeat.png 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SiO2repeat.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Structural motif found in α-quartz, but also found in almost all forms of silicon dioxide</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Quartzrn.PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="940" data-file-width="1400" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Quartzrn.PNG/220px-Quartzrn.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Quartzrn.PNG/330px-Quartzrn.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Quartzrn.PNG/440px-Quartzrn.PNG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Quartzrn.PNG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Relationship between refractive index and density for some SiO<sub>2</sub> forms<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mel_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-mel-8">[8]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>In the majority of <a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">silicates</a>, the silicon atom shows <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetrahedral_coordination_geometry" title="Tetrahedral coordination geometry">tetrahedral coordination</a>, with four oxygen atoms surrounding a central Si atom. The most common example is seen in the quartz <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorphs</a>.
</p><p>For example, in the unit cell of α-quartz, the central tetrahedron shares all four of its corner O atoms, the two face-centered tetrahedra share two of their corner O atoms, and the four edge-centered tetrahedra share just one of their O atoms with other SiO<sub>4</sub> tetrahedra. This leaves a net average of 12 out of 24 total vertices for that portion of the seven SiO<sub>4</sub> tetrahedra that are considered to be a part of the unit cell for silica (<a class="external text" href="http://www.mindat.org/min-3337.html" rel="nofollow">see 3-D Unit Cell</a>).
</p><p>SiO<sub>2</sub> has a number of distinct crystalline forms (<a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorphs</a>) in addition to amorphous forms. With the exception of <a href="/wiki/Stishovite" title="Stishovite">stishovite</a> and fibrous silica, all of the crystalline forms involve tetrahedral SiO<sub>4</sub> units linked together by shared vertices in different arrangements. Silicon–oxygen bond lengths vary between the different crystal forms; for example in α-quartz the bond length is 161 pm, whereas in α-tridymite it is in the range 154–171 pm. The Si-O-Si angle also varies between a low value of 140° in α-tridymite, up to 180° in β-tridymite. In α-quartz, the Si-O-Si angle is 144°.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fibrous silica has a structure similar to that of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silicon_sulfide" title="Silicon sulfide">SiS<sub>2</sub></a> with chains of edge-sharing SiO<sub>4</sub> tetrahedra. Stishovite, the higher-pressure form, in contrast, has a <a href="/wiki/Rutile" title="Rutile">rutile</a>-like structure where silicon is 6-coordinate. The density of stishovite is 4.287 g/cm<sup>3</sup>, which compares to α-quartz, the densest of the low-pressure forms, which has a density of 2.648 g/cm<sup>3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-10">[10]</a></sup> The difference in density can be ascribed to the increase in coordination as the six shortest Si-O bond lengths in stishovite (four Si-O bond lengths of 176 pm and two others of 181 pm) are greater than the Si-O bond length (161 pm) in α-quartz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
The change in the coordination increases the ionicity of the Si-O bond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> More importantly, any deviations from these standard parameters constitute microstructural differences or variations, which represent an approach to an <a href="/wiki/Amorphous_solid" title="Amorphous solid">amorphous</a>, vitreous, or glassy solid.
</p><p>The only stable form under normal conditions is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha_quartz" title="Alpha quartz">alpha quartz</a>, in which crystalline silicon dioxide is usually encountered. In nature, impurities in crystalline α-quartz can give rise to colors (see list).  The high-temperature minerals, <a href="/wiki/Cristobalite" title="Cristobalite">cristobalite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tridymite" title="Tridymite">tridymite</a>, have both lower densities and indices of refraction than quartz. Since the composition is identical, the reason for the discrepancies must be in the increased spacing in the high-temperature minerals. As is common with many substances, the higher the temperature, the farther apart the atoms are, due to the increased vibration energy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The transformation from α-quartz to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta-quartz" title="Beta-quartz">beta-quartz</a> takes place abruptly at 573 °C. Since the transformation is accompanied by a significant change in volume, it can easily induce fracturing of ceramics or rocks passing through this temperature limit.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The high-pressure minerals, <a href="/wiki/Seifertite" title="Seifertite">seifertite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stishovite" title="Stishovite">stishovite</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Coesite" title="Coesite">coesite</a>, though, have higher densities and indices of refraction than quartz. This is probably due to the intense compression of the atoms occurring during their formation, resulting in more condensed structure.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Faujasite" title="Faujasite">Faujasite</a> silica is another form of crystalline silica. It is obtained by <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dealumination" title="wikt:dealumination">dealumination</a> of a low-sodium, ultra-stable Y <a href="/wiki/Zeolite" title="Zeolite">zeolite</a> with combined acid and thermal treatment. The resulting product contains over 99% silica, and has high crystallinity and surface area (over 800 m<sup>2</sup>/g). Faujasite-silica has very high thermal and acid stability. For example, it maintains a high degree of long-range molecular order or <a href="/wiki/Crystallinity" title="Crystallinity">crystallinity</a> even after boiling in concentrated hydrochloric acid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fau_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-fau-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molten_silica" title="Molten silica">Molten silica</a> exhibits several peculiar physical characteristics that are similar to those observed in liquid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(properties)" title="Water (properties)">water</a>: negative temperature expansion, density maximum at temperatures ~5000 °C, and a heat capacity minimum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Its density decreases from 2.08 g/cm<sup>3</sup> at 1950 °C to 2.03 g/cm<sup>3</sup> at 2200 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Molecular SiO<sub>2</sub> with a linear structure is produced when molecular <a href="/wiki/Silicon_monoxide" title="Silicon monoxide">silicon monoxide</a>, SiO, is condensed in an argon matrix cooled with helium along with oxygen atoms generated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microwave_discharge" title="Microwave discharge">microwave discharge</a>. 
Dimeric silicon dioxide, (SiO<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub> has been prepared by reacting O<sub>2</sub> with matrix isolated dimeric silicon monoxide, (Si<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>). In dimeric silicon dioxide there are two oxygen atoms bridging between the silicon atoms with an Si-O-Si angle of 94° and bond length of 164.6 pm and the terminal Si-O bond length is 150.2 pm. The Si-O bond length is 148.3 pm, which compares with the length of 161 pm in α-quartz. The bond energy is estimated at 621.7 kJ/mol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jutzi_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jutzi-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_occurrence">Natural occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Natural occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg"><img alt="[icon]" data-file-height="31" data-file-width="44" height="14" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" width="20"/></a></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs expansion</b>. <small>You can help by <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Silicon_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=">adding to it</a>.</small> <small><i>(July 2017)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Silica with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formula</a> <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">Si</a><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a><sub>2</sub> is most commonly found in nature as <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a>, which comprises more than 10% by mass of the earth's crust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ull_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ull-17">[17]</a></sup> Quartz is the only polymorph of silica stable at the Earth's surface. Metastable occurrences of the high-pressure forms <a href="/wiki/Coesite" title="Coesite">coesite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stishovite" title="Stishovite">stishovite</a> have been found around <a href="/wiki/Impact_structure" title="Impact structure">impact structures</a> and associated with <a href="/wiki/Eclogite" title="Eclogite">eclogites</a> formed during <a href="/wiki/Ultra-high-pressure_metamorphism" title="Ultra-high-pressure metamorphism">ultra-high-pressure metamorphism</a>. The high-temperature forms of <a href="/wiki/Tridymite" title="Tridymite">tridymite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cristobalite" title="Cristobalite">cristobalite</a> are known from silica-rich <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_rock" title="Volcanic rock">volcanic rocks</a>. In many parts of the world, silica is the major constituent of <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology">Biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Even though it is poorly soluble, silica occurs in many plants. Plant materials with high silica <a href="/wiki/Phytolith" title="Phytolith">phytolith</a> content appear to be of importance to grazing animals, from chewing insects to <a href="/wiki/Ungulate" title="Ungulate">ungulates</a>. Silica accelerates tooth wear, and high levels of silica in plants frequently eaten by herbivores may have developed as a defense mechanism against predation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Silica is also the primary component of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rice_husk_ash" title="Rice husk ash">rice husk ash</a>, which is used, for example, in filtration and cement manufacturing.
</p><p>For well over a billion years, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silicification" title="Silicification">silicification</a> in and by cells has been common in the biological world. In the modern world it occurs in bacteria, single-celled organisms, plants, and animals (invertebrates and vertebrates).
Prominent examples include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Test_(biology)" title="Test (biology)">Tests</a> or <a href="/wiki/Frustule" title="Frustule">frustules</a> (i.e. shells) of <a href="/wiki/Diatom" title="Diatom">diatoms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radiolaria" title="Radiolaria">Radiolaria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Testate_amoebae" title="Testate amoebae">testate amoebae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fern_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fern-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>Silica <a href="/wiki/Phytolith" title="Phytolith">phytoliths</a> in the cells of many plants, including <a href="/wiki/Equisetaceae" title="Equisetaceae">Equisetaceae</a>, practically all grasses, and a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Dicotyledon" title="Dicotyledon">dicotyledons</a>.</li>
<li>The spicules forming the skeleton of many <a href="/wiki/Sponge" title="Sponge">sponges</a>.</li></ul>
<p>Crystalline minerals formed in the physiological environment often show exceptional physical properties (e.g., strength, hardness, fracture toughness) and tend to form hierarchical structures that exhibit microstructural order over a range of scales. The minerals are crystallized from an environment that is undersaturated with respect to silicon, and under conditions of neutral pH and low temperature (0–40 °C).
</p><p>Formation of the mineral may occur either within the cell wall of an organism (such as with phytoliths), or outside the cell wall, as typically happens with tests.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="what are tests, and where might one find them? (February 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Specific biochemical reactions exist for mineral deposition. Such reactions include those that involve lipids, proteins, and carbohydrates.
</p><p>It is unclear in what ways silica is important in the nutrition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animals" title="Animals">animals</a>. This field of research is challenging because silica is ubiquitous and in most circumstances dissolves in trace quantities only. All the same it certainly does occur in the living body, leaving us with the problem that it is hard to create proper silica-free controls for purposes of research. This makes it difficult to be sure when the silica present has had operative beneficial effects, and when its presence is coincidental, or even harmful. The current consensus is that it certainly seems important in the growth, strength, and management of many connective tissues. This is true not only for hard connective tissues such as bone and tooth but possibly in the biochemistry of the subcellular enzyme-containing structures as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structural_use">Structural use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Structural use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An estimated 95% of silicon dioxide produced is consumed in the construction industry, e.g. for the production of <a href="/wiki/Portland_cement" title="Portland cement">Portland cement</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ull_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ull-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Silica, in the form of sand is used as the main ingredient in <a href="/wiki/Sand_casting" title="Sand casting">sand casting</a> for the manufacture of metallic components in engineering and other applications. The high melting point of silica enables it to be used in such applications.
</p><p>Crystalline silica is used in <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_fracturing" title="Hydraulic fracturing">hydraulic fracturing</a> of formations which contain <a href="/wiki/Tight_oil" title="Tight oil">tight oil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shale_gas" title="Shale gas">shale gas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT82313_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT82313-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precursor_to_glass_and_silicon">Precursor to glass and silicon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Precursor to glass and silicon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Silica is the primary ingredient in the production of most <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Glass_transition" title="Glass transition">glass transition</a> temperature of pure SiO<sub>2</sub> is about 1475 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> When molten silicon dioxide SiO<sub>2</sub> is rapidly cooled, it does not crystallize, but solidifies as a glass.
</p><p>The structural geometry of silicon and oxygen in glass is similar to that in quartz and most other crystalline forms of silicon and oxygen with silicon surrounded by regular tetrahedra of oxygen centers. The difference between the glass and crystalline forms arises from the connectivity of the tetrahedral units: Although there is no long range periodicity in the glassy network ordering remains at length scales well beyond the SiO bond length. One example of this ordering is the preference to form rings of 6-tetrahedra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fumed_silica">Fumed silica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Fumed silica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fumed_silica" title="Fumed silica">Fumed silica</a> also known as pyrogenic silica is a very fine particulate or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colloidal" title="Colloidal">colloidal</a> form of silicon dioxide.  It is prepared by burning <a href="/wiki/Silicon_tetrachloride" title="Silicon tetrachloride">SiCl<sub>4</sub></a> in an oxygen-rich hydrogen flame to produce a "smoke" of SiO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>SiCl<sub>4</sub>  +  2 H<sub>2</sub>  +  O<sub>2</sub>  →   SiO<sub>2</sub>  +  4 HCl.</dd></dl>
<p>The majority of <a href="/wiki/Optical_fiber" title="Optical fiber">optical fibers</a> for <a href="/wiki/Telecommunication" title="Telecommunication">telecommunication</a> are also made from silica. It is a primary raw material for many ceramics such as <a href="/wiki/Earthenware" title="Earthenware">earthenware</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stoneware" title="Stoneware">stoneware</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Porcelain" title="Porcelain">porcelain</a>.
</p><p>Silicon dioxide is used to produce elemental <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a>. The process involves <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbothermic_reduction" title="Carbothermic reduction">carbothermic reduction</a> in an <a href="/wiki/Electric_arc_furnace" title="Electric arc furnace">electric arc furnace</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>SiO<sub>2</sub>  +  2 C   →   Si  +  2 CO</dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_pharmaceutical_applications">Food and pharmaceutical applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Food and pharmaceutical applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Silica is a common additive in food production, where it is used primarily as a flow agent in powdered foods, or to <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorb</a> water in <a href="/wiki/Hygroscopy" title="Hygroscopy">hygroscopic</a> applications. It is used as an anti-caking agent in powdered foods such as spices and non-dairy coffee creamer. It is the primary component of <a href="/wiki/Diatomaceous_earth" title="Diatomaceous earth">diatomaceous earth</a>. <a href="/wiki/Colloid" title="Colloid">Colloidal</a> silica is also used as a wine, beer, and juice <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fining_agent_(wine)" title="Fining agent (wine)">fining agent</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ull_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ull-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (July 2017)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In pharmaceutical products, silica aids powder flow when tablets are formed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_care">Personal care</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Personal care">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In cosmetics, its useful for its light-diffusing properties<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and natural absorbency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Hydrated_silica" title="Hydrated silica">Hydrated silica</a> is used in <a href="/wiki/Toothpaste" title="Toothpaste">toothpaste</a> as a hard abrasive to remove tooth plaque.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hydrophobic_silica" title="Hydrophobic silica">Hydrophobic silica</a> is used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-foaming_agent" title="Anti-foaming agent">defoamer component</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>In its capacity as a refractory, it is useful in fiber form as a high-temperature <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_protection" title="Thermal protection">thermal protection</a> fabric.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>It is used as a thermal enhancement<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="insulator? (July 2017)">further explanation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> compound in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ground_source_heat_pump" title="Ground source heat pump">ground source heat pump</a> industry.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Silica is used in the <a href="/wiki/DNA_separation_by_silica_adsorption" title="DNA separation by silica adsorption">extraction of DNA</a> and <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a> due to its ability to bind to the nucleic acids under the presence of <a href="/wiki/Chaotropic_agent" title="Chaotropic agent">chaotropes</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>A silica-based aerogel was used in the <a href="/wiki/Stardust_(spacecraft)" title="Stardust (spacecraft)">Stardust spacecraft</a> to collect extraterrestrial particles.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Pure silica (silicon dioxide), when cooled as fused quartz into a glass with no true melting point, can be used as a glass fiber for fiberglass.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Silicon dioxide is mostly obtained by mining, including <a href="/wiki/Sand_mining" title="Sand mining">sand mining</a> and purification of <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a>. 
Quartz is suitable for many purposes, while chemical processing is required to make a purer or otherwise more suitable (e.g. more reactive or fine-grained) product.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Silica_fume">Silica fume</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Silica fume">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Silica_fume" title="Silica fume">Silica fume</a> is obtained as byproduct from hot processes like <a href="/wiki/Ferrosilicon" title="Ferrosilicon">ferrosilicon</a> production. It is less pure than fumed silica and should not be confused with that product. The production process, particle characteristics and fields of application of fumed silica are all different from those of silica fume.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precipitated_silica">Precipitated silica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Precipitated silica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Precipitated silica or amorphous silica is produced by the acidification of solutions of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_silicate" title="Sodium silicate">sodium silicate</a>. The gelatinous precipitate or <a href="/wiki/Silica_gel" title="Silica gel">silica gel</a>, is first washed and then dehydrated to produce colorless microporous silica.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-10">[10]</a></sup>  The idealized equation involving a trisilicate and <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> is:
</p>
<dl><dd>Na<sub>2</sub>Si<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>  +  H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>   →   3 SiO<sub>2</sub>  +  Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>  +  H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Approximately one billion kilograms/year (1999) of silica were produced in this manner, mainly for use for polymer composites – tires and shoe soles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ull_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ull-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="On_microchips">On microchips</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: On microchips">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Thin films of silica grow spontaneously on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silicon_wafer" title="Silicon wafer">silicon wafers</a> via <a href="/wiki/Thermal_oxidation" title="Thermal oxidation">thermal oxidation</a>, producing a very shallow layer of about 1 <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">nm</a> or 10 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angstrom" title="Angstrom">Å</a> of so-called native oxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
Higher temperatures and alternative environments are used to grow well-controlled layers of silicon dioxide on silicon, for example at temperatures between 600 and 1200 °C, using so-called dry or wet oxidation with <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O<sub>2</sub></a>
</p>
<dl><dd>Si +  O<sub>2</sub>   →   SiO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>or H<sub>2</sub>O, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sunggyu_Lee_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sunggyu_Lee-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morgan&amp;Board_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morgan&amp;Board-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>Si +  2 H<sub>2</sub>O   →   SiO<sub>2</sub>  + 2 H<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>The native oxide layer is beneficial in <a href="/wiki/Microelectronics" title="Microelectronics">microelectronics</a>, where it acts as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_insulator" title="Electric insulator">electric insulator</a> with high chemical stability. It can protect the silicon, store charge, block current, and even act as a controlled pathway to limit current flow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Laboratory_or_special_methods">Laboratory or special methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Laboratory or special methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="From_silicate_esters">From silicate esters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: From silicate esters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Many routes to silicon dioxide start with silicate esters, the best known being <a href="/wiki/Tetraethyl_orthosilicate" title="Tetraethyl orthosilicate">tetraethyl orthosilicate</a> (TEOS).  Simply heating TEOS at 680–730 °C gives the dioxide:
</p>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>Si(OC<sub>2</sub>H<sub>5</sub>)<sub>4</sub>   →   SiO<sub>2</sub>  +  2 O(C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>5</sub>)<sub>2</sub></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p>Similarly TEOS combusts around 400 °C:
</p>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>Si(OC<sub>2</sub>H<sub>5</sub>)<sub>4</sub> + 12 O<sub>2</sub>   →   SiO<sub>2</sub> + 10 H<sub>2</sub>O  +  8 CO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p>TEOS undergoes <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a> via the so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sol-gel_process" title="Sol-gel process">sol-gel process</a>.  The course of the reaction and nature of the product are affected by catalysts, but the idealized equation is:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>Si(OC<sub>2</sub>H<sub>5</sub>)<sub>4</sub>  +  2 H<sub>2</sub>O   →   SiO<sub>2</sub>  +  4 HOCH<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>3</sub></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_methods">Other methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Other methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Being highly stable, silicon dioxide arises from many methods. Conceptually simple, but of little practical value, combustion of <a href="/wiki/Silane" title="Silane">silane</a> gives silicon dioxide.  This reaction is analogous to the combustion of methane:
</p>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>SiH<sub>4</sub> + 2 O<sub>2</sub> → SiO<sub>2</sub> + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O.</dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p>However the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_vapor_deposition" title="Chemical vapor deposition">chemical vapor deposition</a> of silicon dioxide onto crystal surface from silane had been used using nitrogen as a <b>carrier gas</b> at 200-500<sup>o</sup>C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morgan&amp;Board2_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morgan&amp;Board2-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_reactions">Chemical reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Chemical reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kieselsaeure380m2prog.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1280" data-file-width="960" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Kieselsaeure380m2prog.jpg/220px-Kieselsaeure380m2prog.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Kieselsaeure380m2prog.jpg/330px-Kieselsaeure380m2prog.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Kieselsaeure380m2prog.jpg/440px-Kieselsaeure380m2prog.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kieselsaeure380m2prog.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Manufactured silica fume at maximum surface area of 380 m<sup>2</sup>/g</div></div></div>
<p>Silica is converted to silicon by reduction with carbon.
</p><p>Fluorine reacts with silicon dioxide to form SiF<sub>4</sub> and O<sub>2</sub> whereas the other halogen gases (Cl<sub>2</sub>, Br<sub>2</sub>, I<sub>2</sub>) are essentially unreactive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Silicon dioxide is attacked by <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">hydrofluoric acid</a> (HF) to produce <a href="/wiki/Hexafluorosilicic_acid" title="Hexafluorosilicic acid">hexafluorosilicic acid</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>SiO<sub>2</sub> + 6 HF → H<sub>2</sub>SiF<sub>6</sub> + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O.</dd></dl>
<p>HF is used to remove or pattern silicon dioxide in the semiconductor industry.
</p><p>Silicon dioxide acts as a <a href="/wiki/Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction#Lux–Flood_definition" title="Acid–base reaction">Lux–Flood acid</a>, being able to react with bases under certain conditions. As it does not contain any hydrogen, it cannot act as a <a href="/wiki/Br%C3%B8nsted%E2%80%93Lowry_acid%E2%80%93base_theory" title="Brønsted–Lowry acid–base theory">Brønsted–Lowry acid</a>. While not soluble in water, some strong bases will react with glass and have to be stored in plastic bottles as a result.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rodgers2011_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rodgers2011-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Silicon dioxide dissolves in hot concentrated alkali or fused hydroxide, as described in this idealized equation:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>SiO<sub>2</sub> + 2 NaOH → Na<sub>2</sub>SiO<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O.</dd></dl>
<p>Silicon dioxide will neutralise basic metal oxides (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Sodium_oxide" title="Sodium oxide">sodium oxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium_oxide" title="Potassium oxide">potassium oxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lead(II)_oxide" title="Lead(II) oxide">lead(II) oxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zinc_oxide" title="Zinc oxide">zinc oxide</a>, or mixtures of oxides, forming <a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">silicates</a> and glasses as the Si-O-Si bonds in silica are broken successively).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-9">[9]</a></sup> As an example the reaction of sodium oxide and SiO<sub>2</sub> can produce sodium orthosilicate, sodium silicate, and glasses, dependent on the proportions of reactants:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Na<sub>2</sub>O + SiO<sub>2</sub> → Na<sub>4</sub>SiO<sub>4</sub>;</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>Na<sub>2</sub>O + SiO<sub>2</sub> → Na<sub>2</sub>SiO<sub>3</sub>;</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>(0.25–0.8)Na<sub>2</sub>O + SiO<sub>2</sub> → glass.</dd></dl>
<p>Examples of such glasses have commercial significance, e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soda-lime_glass" title="Soda-lime glass">soda-lime glass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Borosilicate_glass" title="Borosilicate glass">borosilicate glass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lead_glass" title="Lead glass">lead glass</a>. In these glasses, silica is termed the network former or lattice former.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-9">[9]</a></sup> The reaction is also used in <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnaces</a> to remove sand impurities in the ore by neutralisation with calcium oxide, forming <a href="/wiki/Calcium_silicate" title="Calcium silicate">calcium silicate</a> <a href="/wiki/Slag" title="Slag">slag</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fibreoptic.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="529" data-file-width="350" height="333" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Fibreoptic.jpg/220px-Fibreoptic.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Fibreoptic.jpg/330px-Fibreoptic.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/49/Fibreoptic.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fibreoptic.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bundle of <a href="/wiki/Optical_fiber" title="Optical fiber">optical fibers</a> composed of high purity silica.</div></div></div>
<p>Silicon dioxide reacts in heated <a href="/wiki/Reflux" title="Reflux">reflux</a> under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dinitrogen" title="Dinitrogen">dinitrogen</a> with <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">ethylene glycol</a> and an <a href="/wiki/Alkali_metal" title="Alkali metal">alkali metal</a> base to produce highly reactive, <a href="/wiki/Hypervalent_molecule#Pentacoordinated_silicon" title="Hypervalent molecule">pentacoordinate</a> silicates which provide access to a wide variety of new silicon compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laine_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Laine-35">[35]</a></sup> The silicates are essentially insoluble in all polar solvent except methanol.
</p><p>Silicon dioxide reacts with elemental silicon at high temperatures to produce SiO:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>SiO<sub>2</sub> + Si   →   2 SiO</dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_solubility">Water solubility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Water solubility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The solubility of silicon dioxide in water strongly depends on its crystalline form and is three-four times higher for silica than quartz; as a function of temperature, it peaks around 340 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> This property is used to grow single crystals of quartz in a hydrothermal process where natural quartz is dissolved in superheated water in a pressure vessel that is cooler at the top. Crystals of 0.5–1 kg can be grown over a period of 1–2 months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-9">[9]</a></sup> These crystals are a source of very pure quartz for use in electronic applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_effects">Health effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Health effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Piasek_kwarcowy.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="280" data-file-width="300" height="205" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Piasek_kwarcowy.jpg/220px-Piasek_kwarcowy.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/53/Piasek_kwarcowy.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Piasek_kwarcowy.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Quartz sand (silica) as main raw material for commercial glass production</div></div></div>
<p>Silica ingested orally is essentially nontoxic, with an <a href="/wiki/Median_lethal_dose" title="Median lethal dose">LD<sub style="font-size:-1">50</sub></a> of 5000 mg/kg (5 g/kg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ull_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ull-17">[17]</a></sup> A 2008 study following subjects for 15 years found that higher levels of silica in water appeared to decrease the risk of <a href="/wiki/Dementia" title="Dementia">dementia</a>. An increase of 10 mg/day of silica in drinking water was associated with a decreased risk of dementia of 11%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Inhaling finely divided crystalline silica dust can lead to <a href="/wiki/Silicosis" title="Silicosis">silicosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bronchitis" title="Bronchitis">bronchitis</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Lung_cancer" title="Lung cancer">lung cancer</a>, as the dust becomes lodged in the lungs and continuously irritates the tissue, reducing lung capacities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> When fine silica particles are inhaled in large enough quantities (such as through occupational exposure), it increases the risk of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systemic_autoimmune_disease" title="Systemic autoimmune disease">systemic autoimmune diseases</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lupus" title="Lupus">lupus</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Rheumatoid_arthritis" title="Rheumatoid arthritis">rheumatoid arthritis</a> compared to expected rates in the general population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Occupational_hazard">Occupational hazard</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Occupational hazard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Silica is an occupational hazard for people who do <a href="/wiki/Abrasive_blasting" title="Abrasive blasting">sandblasting</a>, or work with products that contain powdered crystalline silica. Amorphous silica, such as fumed silica, may cause irreversible lung damage in some cases, but is not associated with development of silicosis. Children, asthmatics of any age, those with allergies, and the elderly (all of whom have reduced lung capacity) can be affected in less time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>Crystalline silica is an occupational hazard for those working with stone <a href="/wiki/Countertop" title="Countertop">countertops</a>, because the process of cutting and installing the countertops creates large amounts of airborne silica.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hazard_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hazard-42">[42]</a></sup> Crystalline silica used in <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_fracturing" title="Hydraulic fracturing">hydraulic fracturing</a> presents a health hazard to workers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT82313_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT82313-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pathophysiology">Pathophysiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Pathophysiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the body, crystalline silica particles do not dissolve over clinically relevant periods. Silica crystals inside the lungs can activate the NLRP3 <a href="/wiki/Inflammasome" title="Inflammasome">inflammasome</a> inside macrophages and dendritic cells and thereby result in production of <a href="/wiki/Interleukin" title="Interleukin">interleukin</a>, a highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pro-inflammatory_cytokine" title="Pro-inflammatory cytokine">pro-inflammatory cytokine</a> in the immune system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation">Regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silicon_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Regulations restricting silica exposure 'with respect to the silicosis hazard' specify that they are concerned only with silica, which is both crystalline and dust-forming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2013, the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">Occupational Safety and Health Administration</a> reduced the exposure limit to 50 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micrograms" title="Micrograms">µg</a>/m<sup>3</sup> of air. Prior to 2013, it had allowed 100  µg/m<sup>3</sup> and in construction workers even 250 µg/m<sup>3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT82313_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-NYT82313-21">[21]</a></sup>
In 2013, OSHA also required "green completion" of fracked wells to reduce exposure to crystalline silica besides restricting the limit of exposure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT82313_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-NYT82313-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
