<p><b>Oxalic acid</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>. It is a colorless crystalline solid that forms a colorless solution in water. Its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Condensed_formula" title="Condensed formula">condensed formula</a> is HOOCCOOH, reflecting its classification as the simplest <a href="/wiki/Dicarboxylic_acid" title="Dicarboxylic acid">dicarboxylic acid</a>.
</p><p>Its acid strength is much greater than that of <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a>. Oxalic acid is a <a href="/wiki/Reducing_agent" title="Reducing agent">reducing agent</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxalic_Acid:_Uses_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oxalic_Acid:_Uses-7">[7]</a></sup> and its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conjugate_base" title="Conjugate base">conjugate base</a>, known as <a href="/wiki/Oxalate" title="Oxalate">oxalate</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">C<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>), is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chelating_agent" title="Chelating agent">chelating agent</a> for metal cations. Typically, oxalic acid occurs as the <a href="/wiki/Water_of_crystallization" title="Water of crystallization">dihydrate</a> with the formula C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>·2H<sub>2</sub>O.
</p><p>It occurs naturally in many foods, but excessive ingestion of oxalic acid or prolonged skin contact can be dangerous.
</p><p>Its name comes from the fact that early investigators isolated oxalic acid from <a href="/wiki/Oxalis" title="Oxalis">wood-sorrel (<i>Oxalis</i>)</a> flowering plants.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxalic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The preparation of salts of oxalic acid from plants had been known, at the latest, since 1745, when the Dutch botanist and physician <a href="/wiki/Herman_Boerhaave" title="Herman Boerhaave">Herman Boerhaave</a> isolated a salt from <a href="/wiki/Oxalis" title="Oxalis">sorrel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> By 1773, François Pierre Savary of Fribourg, Switzerland had isolated oxalic acid from its salt in <a href="/wiki/Oxalis" title="Oxalis">sorrel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1776, Swedish chemists <a href="/wiki/Carl_Wilhelm_Scheele" title="Carl Wilhelm Scheele">Carl Wilhelm Scheele</a> and <a href="/wiki/Torbern_Bergman" title="Torbern Bergman">Torbern Olof Bergman</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> produced oxalic acid by reacting sugar with concentrated <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a>; Scheele called the acid that resulted <i>socker-syra</i> or <i>såcker-syra</i> (sugar acid). By 1784, Scheele had shown that "sugar acid" and oxalic acid from natural sources were identical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1824, the German chemist <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_W%C3%B6hler" title="Friedrich Wöhler">Friedrich Wöhler</a> obtained oxalic acid by reacting <a href="/wiki/Cyanogen" title="Cyanogen">cyanogen</a> with ammonia in aqueous solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This experiment may represent the first synthesis of a <a href="/wiki/Natural_product" title="Natural product">natural product</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxalic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Oxalic acid is mainly manufactured by the oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a> or <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> using <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> or air in the presence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vanadium_pentoxide" title="Vanadium pentoxide">vanadium pentoxide</a>. A variety of precursors can be used including <a href="/wiki/Glycolic_acid" title="Glycolic acid">glycolic acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">ethylene glycol</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> A newer method entails oxidative <a href="/wiki/Carbonylation" title="Carbonylation">carbonylation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohols</a> to give the diesters of oxalic acid:
</p>
<dl><dd>4 ROH + 4 CO + O<sub>2</sub> → 2 (CO<sub>2</sub>R)<sub>2</sub> + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>These diesters are subsequently hydrolyzed to oxalic acid. Approximately 120,000 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> are produced annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Historically oxalic acid was obtained exclusively by using caustics, such as sodium or <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">potassium hydroxide</a>, on sawdust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Laboratory_methods">Laboratory methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxalic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Laboratory methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although it can be readily purchased, oxalic acid can be prepared in the laboratory by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidizer" title="Oxidizer">oxidizing</a> <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a> using <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> in the presence of a small amount of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vanadium_pentoxide" title="Vanadium pentoxide">vanadium pentoxide</a> as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cohen_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-cohen-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>The hydrated solid can be dehydrated with heat or by <a href="/wiki/Azeotropic_distillation" title="Azeotropic distillation">azeotropic distillation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Developed in the Netherlands, an <a href="/wiki/Electrosynthesis" title="Electrosynthesis">electrocatalysis</a> by a <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> complex helps <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">reduce carbon dioxide</a> to oxalic acid;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bouwman_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bouwman-18">[18]</a></sup> this conversion <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">uses carbon dioxide as a feedstock</a> to generate oxalic acid.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxalic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anhydrous oxalic acid exists as two <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorphs</a>; in one the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen-bonding" title="Hydrogen-bonding">hydrogen-bonding</a> results in a chain-like structure whereas the hydrogen bonding pattern in the other form defines a sheet-like structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Because the anhydrous material is both acidic and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophilic" title="Hydrophilic">hydrophilic</a> (water seeking), it is used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Esterification" title="Esterification">esterifications</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxalic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Oxalic acid is a relatively strong acid, despite being a <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxylic acid</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>H<sub>2</sub> ⇌ C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>H<sup>−</sup> + H<sup>+</sup><span class="nowrap">          </span> p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> = 1.27</dd>
<dd>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>H<sup>−</sup> ⇌ <span class="chemf nowrap">C<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> + H<sup>+</sup><span class="nowrap">          </span> p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> = 4.27</dd></dl>
<p>Oxalic acid undergoes many of the reactions characteristic of other carboxylic acids. It forms esters such as <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_oxalate" title="Dimethyl oxalate">dimethyl oxalate</a> (<a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">m.p.</a> 52.5 to 53.5 °C (126.5 to 128.3 °F)).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> It forms an acid chloride called <a href="/wiki/Oxalyl_chloride" title="Oxalyl chloride">oxalyl chloride</a>.
</p><p>Oxalate, the conjugate base of oxalic acid, is an excellent <a href="/wiki/Ligand" title="Ligand">ligand</a> for metal ions, e.g. the drug <a href="/wiki/Oxaliplatin" title="Oxaliplatin">oxaliplatin</a>.
</p><p>Oxalic acid and oxalates can be oxidized by <a href="/wiki/Permanganate" title="Permanganate">permanganate</a> in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autocatalytic" title="Autocatalytic">autocatalytic</a> reaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxalic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biosynthesis">Biosynthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxalic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Biosynthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At least two pathways exist for the enzyme-mediated formation of oxalate. In one pathway, oxaloacetate, a component of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Krebs_citric_acid_cycle" title="Krebs citric acid cycle">Krebs citric acid cycle</a>, is hydrolyzed to oxalate and acetic acid by the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Oxaloacetase" title="Oxaloacetase">oxaloacetase</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>[O<sub>2</sub>CC(O)CH<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>2</sub>]<sup>2−</sup> + H<sub>2</sub>O → <span class="chemf nowrap">C<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> + H<sup>+</sup></dd></dl>
<p>It also arises from the dehydrogenation of <a href="/wiki/Glycolic_acid" title="Glycolic acid">glycolic acid</a>, which is produced by the metabolism of <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">ethylene glycol</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence_in_foods_and_plants">Occurrence in foods and plants<span id="found_in_foods_plants"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxalic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Occurrence in foods and plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxalate" title="Calcium oxalate">Calcium oxalate</a> is the most common component of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kidney_stone" title="Kidney stone">kidney stones</a>. Early investigators isolated oxalic acid from <a href="/wiki/Oxalis" title="Oxalis">wood-sorrel</a> (<i>Oxalis</i>). Members of the <a href="/wiki/Spinach" title="Spinach">spinach</a> family and the <a href="/wiki/Brassica" title="Brassica">brassicas</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cabbage" title="Cabbage">cabbage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Broccoli" title="Broccoli">broccoli</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brussels_sprouts" title="Brussels sprouts">brussels sprouts</a>) are high in oxalates, as are <a href="/wiki/Sorrel" title="Sorrel">sorrel</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umbellifer" title="Umbellifer">umbellifers</a> like <a href="/wiki/Parsley" title="Parsley">parsley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rhubarb" title="Rhubarb">Rhubarb</a> leaves contain about 0.5% oxalic acid, and jack-in-the-pulpit (<i><a href="/wiki/Arisaema_triphyllum" title="Arisaema triphyllum">Arisaema triphyllum</a></i>) contains <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxalate" title="Calcium oxalate">calcium oxalate</a> crystals. Similarly, the <a href="/wiki/Parthenocissus_quinquefolia" title="Parthenocissus quinquefolia">Virginia creeper</a>, a common decorative vine, produces oxalic acid in its berries as well as oxalate crystals in the sap, in the form of <a href="/wiki/Raphide" title="Raphide">raphides</a>. Bacteria produce oxalates from oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Plants of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Fenestraria" title="Fenestraria">Fenestraria</a></i> produce optical fibers made from crystalline oxalic acid to transmit light to subterranean photosynthetic sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Carambola" title="Carambola">Carambola</a>, also known as starfruit, also contains oxalic acid along with <a href="/wiki/Caramboxin" title="Caramboxin">caramboxin</a>.
</p><p>The formation of naturally occurring calcium oxalate patinas on certain <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a> statues and monuments has been proposed to be caused by the chemical reaction of the carbonate stone with oxalic acid secreted by <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichen</a> or other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microorganisms" title="Microorganisms">microorganisms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxalic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Oxidized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bitumen" title="Bitumen">bitumen</a> or bitumen exposed to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma_rays" title="Gamma rays">gamma rays</a> also contains oxalic acid among its degradation products. Oxalic acid may increase the leaching of <a href="/wiki/Radionuclide" title="Radionuclide">radionuclides</a> conditioned in bitumen for <a href="/wiki/High-level_radioactive_waste_management" title="High-level radioactive waste management">radioactive waste disposal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biochemistry">Biochemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxalic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Biochemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conjugate_base" title="Conjugate base">conjugate base</a> of oxalic acid is the hydrogenoxalate anion, and its conjugate base (<a href="/wiki/Oxalate" title="Oxalate">oxalate</a>) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Competitive_inhibitor" title="Competitive inhibitor">competitive inhibitor</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Lactate_dehydrogenase" title="Lactate dehydrogenase">lactate dehydrogenase</a> (LDH) enzyme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> LDH catalyses the conversion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyruvate" title="Pyruvate">pyruvate</a> to <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactic acid</a> (end product of the fermentation (anaerobic) process) oxidising the coenzyme <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NADH" title="NADH">NADH</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NAD%2B" title="NAD+">NAD<sup>+</sup></a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydron_(chemistry)" title="Hydron (chemistry)">H<sup>+</sup></a> concurrently. Restoring NAD<sup>+</sup> levels is essential to the continuation of anaerobic energy metabolism through <a href="/wiki/Glycolysis" title="Glycolysis">glycolysis</a>. As cancer cells preferentially use anaerobic metabolism (see <a href="/wiki/Warburg_effect_(oncology)" title="Warburg effect (oncology)">Warburg effect</a>) inhibition of LDH has been shown to inhibit tumor formation and growth,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> thus is an interesting potential course of cancer treatment.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxalic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>About 25% of produced oxalic acid will be used as a <a href="/wiki/Mordant" title="Mordant">mordant</a> in dyeing processes. It is used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bleach_(chemical)" title="Bleach (chemical)">bleaches</a>, especially for <a href="/wiki/Pulpwood" title="Pulpwood">pulpwood</a>. It is also used in baking powder<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-13">[13]</a></sup> and as a third reagent in silica analysis instruments.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cleaning">Cleaning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxalic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Cleaning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Oxalic acid's main applications include cleaning or bleaching, especially for the removal of rust (iron complexing agent). <a href="/wiki/Bar_Keepers_Friend" title="Bar Keepers Friend">Bar Keepers Friend</a> is an example of a household cleaner containing oxalic acid. Its utility in rust removal agents is due to its forming a stable, water-soluble salt with ferric iron, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_ferrioxalate" title="Sodium ferrioxalate">ferrioxalate</a> ion.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extractive_metallurgy">Extractive metallurgy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxalic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Extractive metallurgy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Oxalic acid is an important reagent in <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanide</a> chemistry. Hydrated lanthanide oxalates form readily in very strongly acidic solutions in a densely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystalline" title="Crystalline">crystalline</a>, easily filtered form, largely free of contamination by nonlanthanide elements. Thermal decomposition of these oxalate gives the <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxides</a>, which is the most commonly marketed form of these elements.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Niche_uses">Niche uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxalic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Niche uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Beecrystals.PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="668" data-file-width="773" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Beecrystals.PNG/220px-Beecrystals.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Beecrystals.PNG/330px-Beecrystals.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Beecrystals.PNG/440px-Beecrystals.PNG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Beecrystals.PNG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Honeybee coated with oxalate crystals</div></div></div>
<p>Vaporized oxalic acid, or a 3.2% solution of oxalic acid in sugar syrup, is used by some <a href="/wiki/Beekeeper" title="Beekeeper">beekeepers</a> as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miticide" title="Miticide">miticide</a> against the parasitic <a href="/wiki/Varroa_destructor" title="Varroa destructor">varroa mite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Oxalic acid is rubbed onto completed marble sculptures to seal the surface and introduce a shine. Oxalic acid is also used to clean iron and manganese deposits from <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a> crystals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>Oxalic acid is used as a bleach for wood, removing black stains caused by dissolved iron that binds to the wood during water penetration.
</p><p>Oxalic acid is sometimes used in the aluminum anodizing process, with or without sulfuric acid. Compared to sulfuric acid anodizing, the coatings obtained are thinner and exhibit lower surface roughness.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicity_of_concentrated_oxalic_acid">Toxicity of concentrated oxalic acid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oxalic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Toxicity of concentrated oxalic acid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Oxalic acid in concentrated form can have harmful effects through contact and if <a href="/wiki/Ingestion" title="Ingestion">ingested</a>; manufacturers provide details in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Material_Safety_Data_Sheet" title="Material Safety Data Sheet">Material Safety Data Sheets</a> (MSDS). It is not identified as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mutagenic" title="Mutagenic">mutagenic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carcinogenic" title="Carcinogenic">carcinogenic</a>; there is a possible risk of congenital malformation in the fetus; may be harmful if <a href="/wiki/Inhalation" title="Inhalation">inhaled</a>, and is extremely destructive to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_tissue" title="Biological tissue">tissue</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mucous_membrane" title="Mucous membrane">mucous membranes</a> and upper <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_tract" title="Respiratory tract">respiratory tract</a>; harmful if swallowed; harmful to and destructive of tissue and causes burns if absorbed through the skin or is in contact with the eyes. Symptoms and effects include a burning sensation, cough, wheezing, <a href="/wiki/Laryngitis" title="Laryngitis">laryngitis</a>, shortness of breath, <a href="/wiki/Spasm" title="Spasm">spasm</a>, inflammation and <a href="/wiki/Edema" title="Edema">edema</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Larynx" title="Larynx">larynx</a>, inflammation and edema of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bronchi" title="Bronchi">bronchi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pneumonitis" title="Pneumonitis">pneumonitis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_edema" title="Pulmonary edema">pulmonary edema</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>In humans, ingested oxalic acid has an oral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lowest_published_lethal_dose" title="Lowest published lethal dose">LD<sub>Lo</sub></a> (lowest published lethal dose) of 600 mg/kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> It has been reported that the lethal oral dose is 15 to 30 grams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>The toxicity of oxalic acid is due to kidney failure caused by precipitation of solid <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxalate" title="Calcium oxalate">calcium oxalate</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> the main component of calcium kidney stones. Oxalic acid can also cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joint_pain" title="Joint pain">joint pain</a> due to the formation of similar precipitates in the joints. Ingestion of <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">ethylene glycol</a> results in oxalic acid as a metabolite which can also cause acute kidney failure.
</p>
