<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Fig leaf</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about the act of covering up. For the plant, see <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Fig_(disambiguation)" title="Fig (disambiguation)">Fig</a>. For the 1926 silent comedy film, see <a href="/wiki/Fig_Leaves" title="Fig Leaves">Fig Leaves</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Figleafva.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="491" height="269" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Figleafva.jpg/220px-Figleafva.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Figleafva.jpg/330px-Figleafva.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Figleafva.jpg/440px-Figleafva.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Figleafva.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A fig leaf cast in plaster used to cover the genitals of a copy of a statue of David in the <a href="/wiki/Cast_Courts_(Victoria_and_Albert_Museum)" title="Cast Courts (Victoria and Albert Museum)">Cast Courts of the Victoria and Albert Museum</a>. In the reign of <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a>, display of male nudity was contentious and the Queen herself was said to find it shocking. The museum commissioned this fig leaf and kept it in readiness in case of a visit by the Queen or other female dignitaries: the fig leaf was then hung on the figure using a pair of hooks. Today, the fig leaf is no longer used, but it is displayed in a case at the back of the cast's plinth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mercury_fig_leaf.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="951" data-file-width="1273" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Mercury_fig_leaf.jpg/220px-Mercury_fig_leaf.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Mercury_fig_leaf.jpg/330px-Mercury_fig_leaf.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Mercury_fig_leaf.jpg/440px-Mercury_fig_leaf.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mercury_fig_leaf.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A statue of <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(mythology)" title="Mercury (mythology)">Mercury</a> holding the <a href="/wiki/Caduceus" title="Caduceus">caduceus</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_Palace" title="Apostolic Palace">Vatican</a>, with a fig leaf placed over the genitalia. The fig leaf was placed there under the more "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chaste" title="Chaste">chaste</a>" <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Popes</a>; later, most such coverings were removed.</div></div></div>
<p>The expression "<b>fig leaf</b>" is widely used figuratively to convey the covering up of an act or an object that is embarrassing or distasteful with something of innocuous appearance, a <a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">metaphorical</a> reference to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biblical" title="Biblical">Biblical</a> <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">Book of Genesis</a>, in which <a href="/wiki/Adam_and_Eve" title="Adam and Eve">Adam and Eve</a> used <a href="/wiki/Ficus" title="Ficus">fig</a> leaves to cover their <a href="/wiki/Nudity" title="Nudity">nudity</a> after eating the forbidden fruit from the <a href="/wiki/Tree_of_the_knowledge_of_good_and_evil" title="Tree of the knowledge of good and evil">tree of the knowledge of good and evil</a>. Some paintings and statues have the genitals of their subjects covered by a representation of an actual fig leaf or similar object, either as part of the work or added afterwards for perceived modesty.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fig_leaf&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_art" title="Ancient Greek art">Ancient Greek art</a>, male nakedness, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genitals" title="Genitals">genitals</a>, was common, although the female <a href="/wiki/Mons_pubis" title="Mons pubis">vulval area</a>  was generally covered in art for public display.  This tradition continued in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Roman_art" title="Ancient Roman art">Ancient Roman art</a> until the conversion of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> to <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, when <a href="/wiki/Heroic_nudity" title="Heroic nudity">heroic nudity</a> vanished.  During the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, only the unfortunate (most often the <a href="/wiki/Damnation" title="Damnation">damned</a>) were usually shown naked, although the depictions were then often rather explicit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Adam and Eve were often shown wearing fig or other leaves, following the Biblical description.  This was especially a feature of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Renaissance" title="Northern Renaissance">Northern Renaissance</a> art.
</p><p>From about 1530, the developing reaction to <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> freedoms and excesses that led to the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Trent" title="Council of Trent">Council of Trent</a> also led to a number of artworks, especially in churches or public places, being altered to reduce the amount of nudity on display.  Often, as in the famous case of <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Judgment_(Michelangelo)" title="The Last Judgment (Michelangelo)">The Last Judgement</a></i>, drapery or extra branches from any nearby bush was used. This has been dubbed the "fig leaf campaign". For free-standing statues this did not work well, and carved or cast fig leaves were sometimes added, such as with the plaster copy of Michelangelo's <i><a href="/wiki/David_(Michelangelo)" title="David (Michelangelo)">David</a></i> displayed in <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian era</a> <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The Adam and Eve panels on the <i><a href="/wiki/Ghent_Altarpiece" title="Ghent Altarpiece">Ghent Altarpiece</a></i>, already equipped with fig leaves by <a href="/wiki/Jan_van_Eyck" title="Jan van Eyck">Jan van Eyck</a>,  were simply replaced with 19th-century panels copying the figures but clothed.   Many of these alterations have since been reversed, damaging some of the statues.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_day">Modern day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fig_leaf&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Modern day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:277px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Falk,_Benjamin_J._(1853-1925)_-_Eugen_Sandow_(1867-1925)-_1894.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="515" data-file-width="640" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Falk%2C_Benjamin_J._%281853-1925%29_-_Eugen_Sandow_%281867-1925%29-_1894.jpg/275px-Falk%2C_Benjamin_J._%281853-1925%29_-_Eugen_Sandow_%281867-1925%29-_1894.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Falk%2C_Benjamin_J._%281853-1925%29_-_Eugen_Sandow_%281867-1925%29-_1894.jpg/413px-Falk%2C_Benjamin_J._%281853-1925%29_-_Eugen_Sandow_%281867-1925%29-_1894.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Falk%2C_Benjamin_J._%281853-1925%29_-_Eugen_Sandow_%281867-1925%29-_1894.jpg/550px-Falk%2C_Benjamin_J._%281853-1925%29_-_Eugen_Sandow_%281867-1925%29-_1894.jpg 2x" width="275"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Falk,_Benjamin_J._(1853-1925)_-_Eugen_Sandow_(1867-1925)-_1894.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In this photo, Sandow portrays "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Dying_Gaul" title="The Dying Gaul">The Dying Gaul</a>", a pose taken from an ancient Roman sculpture.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Eugen_Sandow" title="Eugen Sandow">Eugen Sandow</a>, often considered the first modern-day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bodybuilder" title="Bodybuilder">bodybuilder</a>, was an admirer of the human physique, and in addition to strongman <a href="/wiki/Sideshow" title="Sideshow">sideshows</a>; he performed "muscle displays" by posing in the nude — save for a fig leaf that he would don in imitation of statues he had seen in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> as a boy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sandow_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-sandow-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Metaphorical_use">Metaphorical use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fig_leaf&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Metaphorical use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Masaccio-TheExpulsionOfAdamAndEveFromEden-Restoration.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1173" data-file-width="913" height="257" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Masaccio-TheExpulsionOfAdamAndEveFromEden-Restoration.jpg/200px-Masaccio-TheExpulsionOfAdamAndEveFromEden-Restoration.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Masaccio-TheExpulsionOfAdamAndEveFromEden-Restoration.jpg/300px-Masaccio-TheExpulsionOfAdamAndEveFromEden-Restoration.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Masaccio-TheExpulsionOfAdamAndEveFromEden-Restoration.jpg/400px-Masaccio-TheExpulsionOfAdamAndEveFromEden-Restoration.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Masaccio-TheExpulsionOfAdamAndEveFromEden-Restoration.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Expulsion_from_the_Garden_of_Eden" title="The Expulsion from the Garden of Eden">The Expulsion from the Garden of Eden</a></i>, by <a href="/wiki/Masaccio" title="Masaccio">Masaccio</a>, before and after restoration. It was painted in 1425, covered up in 1680, and restored in 1980.</div></div></div>
<p>The expression <i>fig leaf</i> has a <a href="/wiki/Pejorative" title="Pejorative">pejorative</a> <a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">metaphorical</a> sense meaning a flimsy or minimal cover for anything or behaviour that might be considered shameful, with the implication that the cover is only a token gesture and the truth is obvious to all who choose to see it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>A metaphorical fig leaf is something visible but innocuous, as against a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coverup" title="Coverup">coverup</a> in which the existence of something may be entirely hidden.
</p><p>In the context of negotiation, an offer might be characterized as a "fig leaf" if that offer is actually a ploy to conceal a sinister plan.
</p>
