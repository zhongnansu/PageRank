<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Atmosphere of Earth</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

Numbers are mainly from 1987, with CO<sub>2</sub> and methane from 2009, and do not represent any single source.</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>atmosphere of Earth</b> is the layer of <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gases</a>, commonly known as <b>air</b>, that surrounds the planet <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> and is retained by <a href="/wiki/Gravity_of_Earth" title="Gravity of Earth">Earth's gravity</a>. The atmosphere of Earth protects <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a> on Earth by creating <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">pressure</a> allowing for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_water" title="Liquid water">liquid water</a> to exist on the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Planetary_surface" title="Planetary surface">surface</a>, absorbing <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a>, warming the surface through heat retention (<a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>), and reducing temperature extremes between <a href="/wiki/Daytime" title="Daytime">day</a> and <a href="/wiki/Night" title="Night">night</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_temperature_variation" title="Diurnal temperature variation">diurnal temperature variation</a>).
</p><p>By volume, dry air contains 78.09% <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, 20.95% <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20131003_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20131003-2">[2]</a></sup> 0.93% <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>, 0.04% <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Carbon dioxide in Earth's atmosphere">carbon dioxide</a>, and small amounts of other gases. Air also contains a variable amount of <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a>, on average around 1% at sea level, and 0.4% over the entire atmosphere. Air content and <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> vary at different layers, and air suitable for use in <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> by <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_plant" title="Terrestrial plant">terrestrial plants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Breathing" title="Breathing">breathing</a> of <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_animal" title="Terrestrial animal">terrestrial animals</a> is found only in Earth's <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Breathing_gas" title="Breathing gas">artificial atmospheres</a>.
</p><p>The atmosphere has a mass of about 5.15<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">18</span></sup> kg,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> three quarters of which is within about 11 km (6.8 mi; 36,000 ft) of the surface. The atmosphere becomes thinner and thinner with increasing altitude, with no definite boundary between the atmosphere and <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a>. The <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line" title="Kármán line">Kármán line</a>, at 100 km (62 mi), or 1.57% of Earth's radius, is often used as the border between the atmosphere and outer space. Atmospheric effects become noticeable during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_reentry" title="Atmospheric reentry">atmospheric reentry</a> of spacecraft at an altitude of around 120 km (75 mi). Several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_stratification" title="Atmospheric stratification">layers</a> can be distinguished in the atmosphere, based on characteristics such as temperature and composition.
</p><p>The study of Earth's atmosphere and its processes is called <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_sciences" title="Atmospheric sciences">atmospheric science</a> (aerology). Early pioneers in the field include <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9on_Teisserenc_de_Bort" title="Léon Teisserenc de Bort">Léon Teisserenc de Bort</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Assmann" title="Richard Assmann">Richard Assmann</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_chemistry" title="Atmospheric chemistry">Atmospheric chemistry</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Atmospheric_Water_Vapor_Mean.2005.030.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1280" data-file-width="2431" height="116" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Atmospheric_Water_Vapor_Mean.2005.030.jpg/220px-Atmospheric_Water_Vapor_Mean.2005.030.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Atmospheric_Water_Vapor_Mean.2005.030.jpg/330px-Atmospheric_Water_Vapor_Mean.2005.030.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Atmospheric_Water_Vapor_Mean.2005.030.jpg/440px-Atmospheric_Water_Vapor_Mean.2005.030.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Atmospheric_Water_Vapor_Mean.2005.030.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mean atmospheric water vapor</div></div></div>
<p>The three major constituents of Earth's atmosphere are <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>. Water vapor accounts for roughly 0.25% of the atmosphere by mass. The concentration of water vapor (a greenhouse gas) varies significantly from around 10 ppm by volume in the coldest portions of the atmosphere to as much as 5% by volume in hot, humid air masses, and concentrations of other atmospheric gases are typically quoted in terms of dry air (without water vapor).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WallaceHobbs_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-WallaceHobbs-5">[5]</a></sup> The remaining gases are often referred to as trace gases,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> among which are the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>, principally carbon dioxide, methane, nitrous oxide, and ozone. Filtered air includes trace amounts of many other <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compounds</a>. Many substances of natural origin may be present in locally and seasonally variable small amounts as <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosols</a> in an unfiltered air sample, including <a href="/wiki/Dust" title="Dust">dust</a> of mineral and organic composition, <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spores" title="Spores">spores</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sea_spray" title="Sea spray">sea spray</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_ash" title="Volcanic ash">volcanic ash</a>. Various industrial <a href="/wiki/Pollutant" title="Pollutant">pollutants</a> also may be present as gases or aerosols, such as <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> (elemental or in compounds), <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a> compounds and elemental <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> vapor. Sulfur compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> (SO<sub>2</sub>) may be derived from natural sources or from industrial air pollution.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_of_the_atmosphere">Structure of the atmosphere</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Earth%27s_atmosphere.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="700" data-file-width="420" height="367" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Earth%27s_atmosphere.svg/220px-Earth%27s_atmosphere.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Earth%27s_atmosphere.svg/330px-Earth%27s_atmosphere.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Earth%27s_atmosphere.svg/440px-Earth%27s_atmosphere.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Earth%27s_atmosphere.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><b>Earth's atmosphere</b> Lower 4 layers of the atmosphere in 3 dimensions as seen diagonally from above the exobase.  Layers drawn to scale, objects within the layers are not to scale. Aurorae shown here at the bottom of the thermosphere can actually form at any altitude in this atmospheric layer.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Principal_layers">Principal layers</span></h3>
<p>In general, air pressure and density decrease with altitude in the atmosphere. However, temperature has a more complicated profile with altitude, and may remain relatively constant or even increase with altitude in some regions (see the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#Temperature_and_speed_of_sound" title="Atmosphere of Earth">temperature</a> section, below). Because the general pattern of the temperature/altitude profile is constant and measurable by means of instrumented <a href="/wiki/Weather_balloon" title="Weather balloon">balloon soundings</a>, the temperature behavior provides a useful metric to distinguish atmospheric layers. In this way, Earth's atmosphere can be divided (called atmospheric stratification) into five main layers. Excluding the exosphere, the atmosphere has four primary layers, which are the troposphere, stratosphere, mesosphere, and thermosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> From highest to lowest, the five main layers are:
</p>
<ul><li>Exosphere: 700 to 10,000 km (440 to 6,200 miles)</li>
<li>Thermosphere: 80 to 700 km (50 to 440 miles)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thermosphere_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-thermosphere-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>Mesosphere: 50 to 80 km (31 to 50 miles)</li>
<li>Stratosphere: 12 to 50 km (7 to 31 miles)</li>
<li>Troposphere: 0 to 12 km (0 to 7 miles)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tropopauseheight_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-tropopauseheight-12">[12]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Exosphere">Exosphere</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Exosphere" title="Exosphere">Exosphere</a></div>
<p>The exosphere is the outermost layer of Earth's atmosphere (i.e. the upper limit of the atmosphere). It extends from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exobase" title="Exobase">exobase</a>, which is located at the top of the thermosphere at an altitude of about 700 km above sea level, to about 10,000 km (6,200 mi; 33,000,000 ft) where it merges into the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>.
</p><p>This layer is mainly composed of extremely low densities of hydrogen, helium and several heavier molecules including nitrogen, oxygen and carbon dioxide closer to the exobase. The atoms and molecules are so far apart that they can travel hundreds of kilometers without colliding with one another. Thus, the exosphere no longer behaves like a gas, and the particles constantly escape into space. These free-moving particles follow <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ballistic_trajectories" title="Ballistic trajectories">ballistic trajectories</a> and may migrate in and out of the <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a> or the solar wind.
</p><p>The exosphere is located too far above Earth for any meteorological phenomena to be possible. However, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_borealis" title="Aurora borealis">aurora borealis</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_australis" title="Aurora australis">aurora australis</a> sometimes occur in the lower part of the exosphere, where they overlap into the thermosphere. The exosphere contains most of the satellites orbiting Earth.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermosphere">Thermosphere</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Thermosphere" title="Thermosphere">Thermosphere</a></div>
<p>The thermosphere is the second-highest layer of Earth's atmosphere. It extends from the mesopause (which separates it from the mesosphere) at an altitude of about 80 km (50 mi; 260,000 ft) up to the <a href="/wiki/Thermopause" title="Thermopause">thermopause</a> at an altitude range of 500–1000 km (310–620 mi; 1,600,000–3,300,000 ft). The height of the thermopause varies considerably due to changes in solar activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thermosphere_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-thermosphere-11">[11]</a></sup> Because the thermopause lies at the lower boundary of the exosphere, it is also referred to as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exobase" title="Exobase">exobase</a>. The lower part of the thermosphere, from 80 to 550 kilometres (50 to 342 mi) above Earth's surface, contains the <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a>.
</p><p>The temperature of the thermosphere gradually increases with height. Unlike the stratosphere beneath it, wherein a temperature <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology)" title="Inversion (meteorology)">inversion</a> is due to the absorption of radiation by ozone, the inversion in the thermosphere occurs due to the extremely low density of its molecules. The temperature of this layer can rise as high as 1500 °C (2700 °F), though the gas molecules are so far apart that its <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases" title="Kinetic theory of gases">temperature in the usual sense</a> is not very meaningful. The air is so rarefied that an individual molecule (of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, for example) travels an average of 1 kilometre (0.62 mi; 3300 ft) between collisions with other molecules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Although the thermosphere has a high proportion of molecules with high energy, it would not feel hot to a human in direct contact, because its density is too low to conduct a significant amount of energy to or from the skin.
</p><p>This layer is completely cloudless and free of water vapor. However, non-hydrometeorological phenomena such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_borealis" title="Aurora borealis">aurora borealis</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_australis" title="Aurora australis">aurora australis</a> are occasionally seen in the thermosphere. The <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> orbits in this layer, between 350 and 420 km (220 and 260 mi).
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mesosphere">Mesosphere</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mesosphere" title="Mesosphere">Mesosphere</a></div>
<p>The mesosphere is the third highest layer of Earth's atmosphere, occupying the region above the stratosphere and below the thermosphere. It extends from the stratopause at an altitude of about 50 km (31 mi; 160,000 ft) to the mesopause at 80–85 km (50–53 mi; 260,000–280,000 ft) above sea level.
</p><p>Temperatures drop with increasing altitude to the <a href="/wiki/Mesopause" title="Mesopause">mesopause</a> that marks the top of this middle layer of the atmosphere. It is the coldest place on Earth and has an average temperature around −85 <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">°C</a> (−120 <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">°F</a>; 190 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Just below the mesopause, the air is so cold that even the very scarce water vapor at this altitude can be sublimated into polar-mesospheric <a href="/wiki/Noctilucent_cloud" title="Noctilucent cloud">noctilucent clouds</a>. These are the highest clouds in the atmosphere and may be visible to the naked eye if sunlight reflects off them about an hour or two after sunset or a similar length of time before sunrise. They are most readily visible when the Sun is around 4 to 16 degrees below the horizon. Lightning-induced discharges known as <a href="/wiki/Upper-atmospheric_lightning" title="Upper-atmospheric lightning">transient luminous events (TLEs)</a> occasionally form in the mesosphere above tropospheric <a href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus_cloud" title="Cumulonimbus cloud">thunderclouds</a>. The mesosphere is also the layer where most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteor" title="Meteor">meteors</a> burn up upon atmospheric entrance. It is too high above Earth to be accessible to jet-powered aircraft and balloons, and too low to permit orbital spacecraft. The mesosphere is mainly accessed by <a href="/wiki/Sounding_rocket" title="Sounding rocket">sounding rockets</a> and rocket-powered aircraft.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Stratosphere">Stratosphere</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">Stratosphere</a></div>
<p>The stratosphere is the second-lowest layer of Earth's atmosphere. It lies above the troposphere and is separated from it by the <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a>. This layer extends from the top of the troposphere at roughly 12 km (7.5 mi; 39,000 ft) above Earth's surface to the <a href="/wiki/Stratopause" title="Stratopause">stratopause</a> at an altitude of about 50 to 55 km (31 to 34 mi; 164,000 to 180,000 ft).
</p><p>The atmospheric pressure at the top of the stratosphere is roughly 1/1000 the pressure at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_level_pressure" title="Sea level pressure">sea level</a>. It contains the ozone layer, which is the part of Earth's atmosphere that contains relatively high concentrations of that gas. The stratosphere defines a layer in which temperatures rise with increasing altitude. This rise in temperature is caused by the absorption of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_radiation" title="Ultraviolet radiation">ultraviolet radiation</a> (UV) radiation from the Sun by the <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a>, which restricts turbulence and mixing. Although the temperature may be −60 °C (−76 °F; 210 K) at the tropopause, the top of the stratosphere is much warmer, and may be near 0 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stratopause_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-stratopause-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>The stratospheric temperature profile creates very stable atmospheric conditions, so the stratosphere lacks the weather-producing air turbulence that is so prevalent in the troposphere. Consequently, the stratosphere is almost completely free of clouds and other forms of weather. However, polar stratospheric or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nacreous_cloud" title="Nacreous cloud">nacreous clouds</a> are occasionally seen in the lower part of this layer of the atmosphere where the air is coldest. The stratosphere is the highest layer that can be accessed by jet-powered aircraft.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Troposphere">Troposphere</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">Troposphere</a></div>
<p>The troposphere is the lowest layer of Earth's atmosphere. It extends from Earth's surface to an average height of about 12 km (7.5 mi; 39,000 ft), although this <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a> varies from about 9 km (5.6 mi; 30,000 ft) at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geographic_pole" title="Geographic pole">geographic poles</a> to 17 km (11 mi; 56,000 ft) at the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">Equator</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tropopauseheight_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-tropopauseheight-12">[12]</a></sup> with some variation due to weather. The troposphere is bounded above by the <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a>, a boundary marked in most places by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperature_inversion" title="Temperature inversion">temperature inversion</a> (i.e. a layer of relatively warm air above a colder one), and in others by a zone which is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotherm_(contour_line)" title="Isotherm (contour line)">isothermal</a> with height.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although variations do occur, the temperature usually declines with increasing altitude in the troposphere because the troposphere is mostly heated through energy transfer from the surface. Thus, the lowest part of the troposphere (i.e. Earth's surface) is typically the warmest section of the troposphere. This promotes vertical mixing (hence, the origin of its name in the Greek word τρόπος, <i>tropos</i>, meaning "turn"). The troposphere contains roughly 80% of the <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> of Earth's atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The troposphere is denser than all its overlying atmospheric layers because a larger atmospheric weight sits on top of the troposphere and causes it to be most severely compressed. Fifty percent of the total mass of the atmosphere is located in the lower 5.6 km (3.5 mi; 18,000 ft) of the troposphere.
</p><p>Nearly all atmospheric water vapor or moisture is found in the troposphere, so it is the layer where most of Earth's weather takes place. It has basically all the weather-associated cloud genus types generated by active wind circulation, although very tall cumulonimbus thunder clouds can penetrate the tropopause from below and rise into the lower part of the stratosphere. Most conventional aviation activity takes place in the troposphere, and it is the only layer that can be accessed by propeller-driven aircraft.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_layers">Other layers</span></h3>
<p>Within the five principal layers that are largely determined by temperature, several secondary layers may be distinguished by other properties:
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a> is contained within the stratosphere. In this layer <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> concentrations are about 2 to 8 parts per million, which is much higher than in the lower atmosphere but still very small compared to the main components of the atmosphere. It is mainly located in the lower portion of the stratosphere from about 15–35 km (9.3–21.7 mi; 49,000–115,000 ft), though the thickness varies seasonally and geographically. About 90% of the ozone in Earth's atmosphere is contained in the stratosphere.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a> is a region of the atmosphere that is ionized by solar radiation. It is responsible for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy)" title="Aurora (astronomy)">auroras</a>. During daytime hours, it stretches from 50 to 1,000 km (31 to 621 mi; 160,000 to 3,280,000 ft) and includes the mesosphere, thermosphere, and parts of the exosphere. However, ionization in the mesosphere largely ceases during the night, so auroras are normally seen only in the thermosphere and lower exosphere. The ionosphere forms the inner edge of the <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a>. It has practical importance because it influences, for example, radio propagation on Earth.</li>
<li>The homosphere and heterosphere are defined by whether the atmospheric gases are well mixed. The surface-based homosphere includes the troposphere, stratosphere, mesosphere, and the lowest part of the thermosphere, where the chemical composition of the atmosphere does not depend on molecular weight because the gases are mixed by turbulence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> This relatively homogeneous layer ends at the <i><a href="/wiki/Turbopause" title="Turbopause">turbopause</a></i> found at about 100 km (62 mi; 330,000 ft), the very <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line" title="Kármán line">edge of space</a> itself as accepted by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federation_Aeronautique_Internationale" title="Federation Aeronautique Internationale">FAI</a>, which places it about 20 km (12 mi; 66,000 ft) above the mesopause.</li></ul>
<dl><dd>Above this altitude lies the heterosphere, which includes the exosphere and most of the thermosphere. Here, the chemical composition varies with altitude. This is because the <a href="/wiki/Mean_free_path" title="Mean free path">distance that particles can move without colliding with one another</a> is large compared with the size of motions that cause mixing. This allows the gases to stratify by molecular weight, with the heavier ones, such as oxygen and nitrogen, present only near the bottom of the heterosphere. The upper part of the heterosphere is composed almost completely of hydrogen, the lightest element.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="so what is the intermediate region of the heterosphere composed of?? (February 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></dd></dl>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Planetary_boundary_layer" title="Planetary boundary layer">planetary boundary layer</a> is the part of the troposphere that is closest to Earth's surface and is directly affected by it, mainly through <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulent diffusion</a>. During the day the planetary boundary layer usually is well-mixed, whereas at night it becomes stably stratified with weak or intermittent mixing. The depth of the planetary boundary layer ranges from as little as about 100 metres (330 ft) on clear, calm nights to 3,000 m (9,800 ft) or more during the afternoon in dry regions.</li></ul>
<p>The average temperature of the atmosphere at Earth's surface is 14 °C (57 °F; 287 K)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> or 15 °C (59 °F; 288 K),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> depending on the reference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pressure_and_thickness">Pressure and thickness</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">Atmospheric pressure</a></div>
<p>The average atmospheric pressure at sea level is defined by the <a href="/wiki/International_Standard_Atmosphere" title="International Standard Atmosphere">International Standard Atmosphere</a> as 101325 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">pascals</a> (760.00 <a href="/wiki/Torr" title="Torr">Torr</a>; 14.6959 <a href="/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch" title="Pounds per square inch">psi</a>; 760.00 <a href="/wiki/Millimeter_of_mercury" title="Millimeter of mercury">mmHg</a>). This is sometimes referred to as a unit of <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">standard atmospheres (atm)</a>. Total atmospheric mass is 5.1480×10<sup>18</sup> kg (1.135×10<sup>19</sup> lb),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> about 2.5% less than would be inferred from the average sea level pressure and Earth's area of 51007.2 megahectares, this portion being displaced by Earth's mountainous terrain. Atmospheric pressure is the total weight of the air above unit area at the point where the pressure is measured. Thus air pressure varies with location and <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">weather</a>.
</p><p>If the entire mass of the atmosphere had a uniform density from sea level, it would terminate abruptly at an altitude of 8.50 km (27,900 ft). It actually decreases exponentially with altitude, dropping by half every 5.6 km (18,000 ft) or by a factor of 1/<a href="/wiki/E_(mathematical_constant)" title="E (mathematical constant)">e</a> every 7.64 km (25,100 ft), the average <a href="/wiki/Scale_height" title="Scale height">scale height</a> of the atmosphere below 70 km (43 mi; 230,000 ft). However, the atmosphere is more accurately modeled with a customized equation for each layer that takes gradients of temperature, molecular composition, solar radiation and gravity into account.
</p><p>In summary, the mass of Earth's atmosphere is distributed approximately as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>50% is below 5.6 km (18,000 ft).</li>
<li>90% is below 16 km (52,000 ft).</li>
<li>99.99997% is below 100 km (62 mi; 330,000 ft), the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line" title="Kármán line">Kármán line</a>. By international convention, this marks the beginning of space where human travelers are considered <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">astronauts</a>.</li></ul>
<p>By comparison, the summit of Mt. Everest is at 8,848 m (29,029 ft);
commercial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airliners" title="Airliners">airliners</a> typically cruise between 10 and 13 km (33,000 and 43,000 ft) where the thinner air improves fuel economy; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_balloons" title="Weather balloons">weather balloons</a> reach 30.4 km (100,000 ft) and above; and the highest <a href="/wiki/North_American_X-15" title="North American X-15">X-15</a> flight in 1963 reached 108.0 km (354,300 ft).
</p><p>Even above the Kármán line, significant atmospheric effects such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy)" title="Aurora (astronomy)">auroras</a> still occur. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteors" title="Meteors">Meteors</a> begin to glow in this region, though the larger ones may not burn up until they penetrate more deeply. The various layers of Earth's <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a>, important to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HF_radio" title="HF radio">HF radio</a> propagation, begin below 100 km and extend beyond 500 km. By comparison, the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> and <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> typically orbit at 350–400 km, within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F-layer" title="F-layer">F-layer</a> of the ionosphere where they encounter enough <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_drag" title="Atmospheric drag">atmospheric drag</a> to require reboosts every few months. Depending on solar activity, satellites can experience noticeable atmospheric drag at altitudes as high as 700–800 km.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperature_and_speed_of_sound">Temperature and speed of sound</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_temperature" title="Atmospheric temperature">Atmospheric temperature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">Speed of sound</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Atmospheric_Temperature_Trend.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="610" height="216" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Atmospheric_Temperature_Trend.jpg/220px-Atmospheric_Temperature_Trend.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Atmospheric_Temperature_Trend.jpg/330px-Atmospheric_Temperature_Trend.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Atmospheric_Temperature_Trend.jpg/440px-Atmospheric_Temperature_Trend.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Atmospheric_Temperature_Trend.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Temperature trends in two thick layers of the atmosphere as measured between January 1979 and December 2005 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microwave_Sounding_Unit" title="Microwave Sounding Unit">Microwave Sounding Units</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advanced_Microwave_Sounding_Unit" title="Advanced Microwave Sounding Unit">Advanced Microwave Sounding Units</a> on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">NOAA</a> weather satellites.  The instruments record microwaves emitted from oxygen molecules in the atmosphere. Source:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The division of the atmosphere into layers mostly by reference to temperature is discussed above. Temperature decreases with altitude starting at sea level, but variations in this trend begin above 11 km, where the temperature stabilizes through a large vertical distance through the rest of the troposphere. In the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>, starting above about 20 km, the temperature increases with height, due to heating within the <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a> caused by capture of significant <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> radiation from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> by the dioxygen and ozone gas in this region. Still another region of increasing temperature with altitude occurs at very high altitudes, in the aptly-named <a href="/wiki/Thermosphere" title="Thermosphere">thermosphere</a> above 90 km.
</p><p>Because in an <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas" title="Ideal gas">ideal gas</a> of constant composition the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_sound" title="Speed of sound">speed of sound</a> depends only on temperature and not on the gas pressure or density, the speed of sound in the atmosphere with altitude takes on the form of the complicated temperature profile (see illustration to the right), and does not mirror altitudinal changes in density or pressure.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Density_and_mass">Density and mass</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Atmosphere_model.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="476" data-file-width="770" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Atmosphere_model.png/220px-Atmosphere_model.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Atmosphere_model.png/330px-Atmosphere_model.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Atmosphere_model.png/440px-Atmosphere_model.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Atmosphere_model.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Temperature and mass density against altitude from the <a href="/wiki/NRLMSISE-00" title="NRLMSISE-00">NRLMSISE-00</a> <a href="/wiki/International_Standard_Atmosphere" title="International Standard Atmosphere">standard atmosphere</a> model (the eight dotted lines in each "decade" are at the eight cubes 8, 27, 64, ..., 729)</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Density_of_air" title="Density of air">Density of air</a></div>
<p>The density of air at sea level is about 1.2 kg/m<sup>3</sup> (1.2 g/L, 0.0012 g/cm<sup>3</sup>). Density is not measured directly but is calculated from measurements of temperature, pressure and humidity using the equation of state for air (a form of the <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas_law" title="Ideal gas law">ideal gas law</a>). Atmospheric density decreases as the altitude increases. This variation can be approximately modeled using the <a href="/wiki/Barometric_formula" title="Barometric formula">barometric formula</a>. More sophisticated models are used to predict orbital decay of satellites.
</p><p>The average mass of the atmosphere is about 5 quadrillion (5<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">15</span></sup>) <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> or 1/1,200,000 the mass of Earth. According to the American <a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Atmospheric_Research" title="National Center for Atmospheric Research">National Center for Atmospheric Research</a>, "The total mean mass of the atmosphere is 5.1480<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">18</span></sup> kg with an annual range due to water vapor of 1.2 or 1.5<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">15</span></sup> kg, depending on whether surface pressure or water vapor data are used; somewhat smaller than the previous estimate. The mean mass of water vapor is estimated as 1.27<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">16</span></sup> kg and the dry air mass as 5.1352 ±0.0003<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">18</span></sup> kg."
</p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">Sunlight</a></div>
<p>Solar <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> (or sunlight) is the energy Earth receives from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>. Earth also emits radiation back into space, but at longer wavelengths that we cannot see. Part of the incoming and emitted radiation is absorbed or reflected by the atmosphere. In May 2017, glints of light, seen as twinkling from an orbiting satellite a million miles away, were found to be <a href="/wiki/Reflection_(physics)" title="Reflection (physics)">reflected light</a> from <a href="/wiki/Ice_crystals" title="Ice crystals">ice crystals</a> in the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20170519_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20170519-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GRL-201760515_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-GRL-201760515-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scattering">Scattering</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Scattering" title="Scattering">Scattering</a></div>
<p>When light passes through Earth's atmosphere, <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a> interact with it through <i>scattering</i>. If the light does not interact with the atmosphere, it is called <i>direct radiation</i> and is what you see if you were to look directly at the Sun. <i>Indirect radiation</i> is light that has been scattered in the atmosphere. For example, on an <a href="/wiki/Overcast" title="Overcast">overcast</a> day when you cannot see your shadow there is no direct radiation reaching you, it has all been scattered. As another example, due to a phenomenon called <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">Rayleigh scattering</a>, shorter (blue) wavelengths scatter more easily than longer (red) wavelengths. This is why the sky looks blue; you are seeing scattered blue light. This is also why sunsets are red. Because the Sun is close to the horizon, the Sun's rays pass through more atmosphere than normal to reach your eye. Much of the blue light has been scattered out, leaving the red light in a sunset.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Absorption">Absorption</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(electromagnetic_radiation)" title="Absorption (electromagnetic radiation)">Absorption (electromagnetic radiation)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Atmospheric_electromagnetic_opacity.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="780" data-file-width="1650" height="104" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Atmospheric_electromagnetic_opacity.svg/220px-Atmospheric_electromagnetic_opacity.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Atmospheric_electromagnetic_opacity.svg/330px-Atmospheric_electromagnetic_opacity.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Atmospheric_electromagnetic_opacity.svg/440px-Atmospheric_electromagnetic_opacity.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Atmospheric_electromagnetic_opacity.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rough plot of Earth's atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Transmittance" title="Transmittance">transmittance</a> (or opacity) to various wavelengths of electromagnetic radiation, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light">visible light</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Different molecules absorb different wavelengths of radiation. For example, O<sub>2</sub> and O<sub>3</sub> absorb almost all wavelengths shorter than 300 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanometer" title="Nanometer">nanometers</a>. Water (H<sub>2</sub>O) absorbs many wavelengths above 700 nm. When a molecule absorbs a photon, it increases the energy of the molecule. This heats the atmosphere, but the atmosphere also cools by emitting radiation, as discussed below.
</p><p>The combined <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absorption_spectra" title="Absorption spectra">absorption spectra</a> of the gases in the atmosphere leave "windows" of low <a href="/wiki/Opacity_(optics)" title="Opacity (optics)">opacity</a>, allowing the transmission of only certain bands of light. The <a href="/wiki/Optical_window" title="Optical window">optical window</a> runs from around 300 nm (<a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a>-C) up into the range humans can see, the <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible spectrum</a> (commonly called light), at roughly 400–700 nm and continues to the <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> to around 1100 nm. There are also <a href="/wiki/Infrared_window" title="Infrared window">infrared</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radio_window" title="Radio window">radio windows</a> that transmit some infrared and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_waves" title="Radio waves">radio waves</a> at longer wavelengths. For example, the radio window runs from about one centimeter to about eleven-meter waves.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emission">Emission</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emission_(electromagnetic_radiation)" title="Emission (electromagnetic radiation)">Emission (electromagnetic radiation)</a></div>
<p><i>Emission</i> is the opposite of absorption, it is when an object emits radiation. Objects tend to emit amounts and wavelengths of radiation depending on their "<a href="/wiki/Black_body" title="Black body">black body</a>" emission curves, therefore hotter objects tend to emit more radiation, with shorter wavelengths. Colder objects emit less radiation, with longer wavelengths. For example, the Sun is approximately 6,000 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a> (5,730 <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">°C</a>; 10,340 <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">°F</a>), its radiation peaks near 500 nm, and is visible to the human eye. Earth is approximately 290 K (17 °C; 62 °F), so its radiation peaks near 10,000 nm, and is much too long to be visible to humans.
</p><p>Because of its temperature, the atmosphere emits infrared radiation. For example, on clear nights Earth's surface cools down faster than on cloudy nights. This is because clouds (H<sub>2</sub>O) are strong absorbers and emitters of infrared radiation. This is also why it becomes colder at night at higher elevations.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a> is directly related to this absorption and emission effect. Some gases in the atmosphere absorb and emit infrared radiation, but do not interact with sunlight in the visible spectrum. Common examples of these are CO<sub>2</sub> and H<sub>2</sub>O.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Refractive_index">Refractive index</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scintillation_(astronomy)" title="Scintillation (astronomy)">Scintillation (astronomy)</a></div><p>The <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive index</a> of air is close to, but just greater than 1. Systematic variations in refractive index can lead to the bending of light rays over long optical paths. One example is that, under some circumstances, observers onboard ships can see other vessels just over the <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">horizon</a> because light is refracted in the same direction as the <a href="/wiki/Curvature" title="Curvature">curvature</a> of Earth's surface.
</p><p>The refractive index of air depends on temperature,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edlén_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Edlén-33">[33]</a></sup> giving rise to refraction effects when the temperature gradient is large. An example of such effects is the <a href="/wiki/Mirage" title="Mirage">mirage</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Circulation">Circulation</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_circulation" title="Atmospheric circulation">Atmospheric circulation</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AtmosphCirc2.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="507" data-file-width="576" height="194" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/AtmosphCirc2.png/220px-AtmosphCirc2.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/AtmosphCirc2.png/330px-AtmosphCirc2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/AtmosphCirc2.png/440px-AtmosphCirc2.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AtmosphCirc2.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An idealised view of three large circulation cells.</div></div></div>
<p><i>Atmospheric circulation</i> is the large-scale movement of air through the troposphere, and the means (with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_circulation" title="Ocean circulation">ocean circulation</a>) by which heat is distributed around Earth. The large-scale structure of the atmospheric circulation varies from year to year, but the basic structure remains fairly constant because it is determined by Earth's rotation rate and the difference in solar radiation between the equator and poles.
</p>
<h2><span id="Evolution_of_Earth.27s_atmosphere"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution_of_Earth's_atmosphere">Evolution of Earth's atmosphere</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Earth" title="History of Earth">History of Earth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">Paleoclimatology</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Earliest_atmosphere">Earliest atmosphere</span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Paleoatmosphere" title="Paleoatmosphere">first atmosphere</a> consisted of gases in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_nebula#Formation_of_planets" title="Solar nebula">solar nebula</a>, primarily <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>. There were probably simple hydrides such as those now found in the <a href="/wiki/Gas_giant" title="Gas giant">gas giants</a> (<a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a>), notably water vapor, <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zahnle_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zahnle-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_atmosphere">Second atmosphere</span></h3>
<p>Outgassing from <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">volcanism</a>, supplemented by gases produced during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_heavy_bombardment" title="Late heavy bombardment">late heavy bombardment</a> of Earth by huge <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asteroids" title="Asteroids">asteroids</a>, produced the next atmosphere, consisting largely of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> plus <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and inert gases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zahnle_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zahnle-34">[34]</a></sup> A major part of carbon-dioxide emissions dissolved in water and reacted with metals such as calcium and magnesium during weathering of crustal rocks to form carbonates that were deposited as sediments. Water-related sediments have been found that date from as early as 3.8 billion years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>About 3.4 billion years ago, nitrogen formed the major part of the then stable "second atmosphere". The influence of life has to be taken into account rather soon in the history of the atmosphere, because hints of early life-forms appear as early as 3.5 billion years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> How Earth at that time maintained a climate warm enough for liquid water and life, if the early Sun put out 30% lower solar radiance than today, is a puzzle known as the "<a href="/wiki/Faint_young_Sun_paradox" title="Faint young Sun paradox">faint young Sun paradox</a>".
</p><p>The geological record however shows a continuous relatively warm surface during the complete early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperature_record" title="Temperature record">temperature record</a> of Earth – with the exception of one cold glacial phase about 2.4 billion years ago. In the late <a href="/wiki/Archean" title="Archean">Archean</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eon_(geology)" title="Eon (geology)">Eon</a> an oxygen-containing atmosphere began to develop, apparently produced by photosynthesizing <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">cyanobacteria</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Great_Oxygenation_Event" title="Great Oxygenation Event">Great Oxygenation Event</a>), which have been found as <a href="/wiki/Stromatolite" title="Stromatolite">stromatolite</a> fossils from 2.7 billion years ago. The early basic carbon isotopy (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotope_ratio" title="Isotope ratio">isotope ratio</a> proportions) strongly suggests conditions similar to the current, and that the fundamental features of the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a> became established as early as 4 billion years ago.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Proxy_(climate)#Lake_and_ocean_sediments" title="Proxy (climate)">Ancient sediments</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Gabon" title="Gabon">Gabon</a> dating from between about 2,150 and 2,080 million years ago provide a record of Earth's dynamic oxygenation evolution. These fluctuations in oxygenation were likely driven by the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lomagundi_carbon_isotope_excursion&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lomagundi carbon isotope excursion (page does not exist)">Lomagundi carbon isotope excursion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Third_atmosphere">Third atmosphere</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sauerstoffgehalt-1000mj2.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1409" data-file-width="2000" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Sauerstoffgehalt-1000mj2.png/220px-Sauerstoffgehalt-1000mj2.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Sauerstoffgehalt-1000mj2.png/330px-Sauerstoffgehalt-1000mj2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Sauerstoffgehalt-1000mj2.png/440px-Sauerstoffgehalt-1000mj2.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sauerstoffgehalt-1000mj2.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Oxygen content of the atmosphere over the last billion years<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The constant re-arrangement of continents by <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a> influences the long-term evolution of the atmosphere by transferring carbon dioxide to and from large continental carbonate stores. Free oxygen did not exist in the atmosphere until about 2.4 billion years ago during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Oxygenation_Event" title="Great Oxygenation Event">Great Oxygenation Event</a> and its appearance is indicated by the end of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Banded_iron_formations" title="Banded iron formations">banded iron formations</a>.
</p><p>Before this time, any oxygen produced by photosynthesis was consumed by oxidation of reduced materials, notably iron. Molecules of free oxygen did not start to accumulate in the atmosphere until the rate of production of oxygen began to exceed the availability of reducing materials that removed oxygen. This point signifies a shift from a <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reducing</a> atmosphere to an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidizing" title="Oxidizing">oxidizing</a> atmosphere. O<sub>2</sub> showed major variations until reaching a steady state of more than 15% by the end of the Precambrian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The following time span from 541 million years ago to the present day is the <a href="/wiki/Phanerozoic" title="Phanerozoic">Phanerozoic</a> Eon, during the earliest period of which, the <a href="/wiki/Cambrian" title="Cambrian">Cambrian</a>, oxygen-requiring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metazoan" title="Metazoan">metazoan</a> life forms began to appear.
</p><p>The amount of oxygen in the atmosphere has fluctuated over the last 600 million years, reaching a peak of about 30% around 280 million years ago, significantly higher than today's 21%. Two main processes govern changes in the atmosphere: Plants <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">use carbon dioxide from the atmosphere</a>, releasing oxygen. Breakdown of <a href="/wiki/Pyrite" title="Pyrite">pyrite</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic_eruption" title="Volcanic eruption">volcanic eruptions</a> release <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> into the atmosphere, which oxidizes and hence reduces the amount of oxygen in the atmosphere. However, volcanic eruptions also release carbon dioxide, which plants can convert to oxygen. The exact cause of the variation of the amount of oxygen in the atmosphere is not known. Periods with much oxygen in the atmosphere are associated with rapid development of animals. Today's atmosphere contains 21% oxygen, which is great enough for this rapid development of animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_pollution">Air pollution</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">Air pollution</a></div>
<p><i>Air pollution</i> is the introduction into the atmosphere of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical" title="Chemical">chemicals</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">particulate matter</a> or <a href="/wiki/Organic_matter" title="Organic matter">biological materials</a> that cause harm or discomfort to organisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">Stratospheric</a> <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">ozone depletion</a> is caused by air pollution, chiefly from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbons" title="Chlorofluorocarbons">chlorofluorocarbons</a> and other ozone-depleting substances.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Scientific_consensus" title="Scientific consensus">scientific consensus</a> is that the <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a> greenhouse gases currently accumulating in the atmosphere are the main cause of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grida7_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-grida7-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
