<p>The <b>Kemper Project</b>, also called the <b>Kemper County energy facility</b> or <b>Plant Ratcliffe</b>, was a natural gas-fired electrical generating station currently under construction in <a href="/wiki/Kemper_County,_Mississippi" title="Kemper County, Mississippi">Kemper County, Mississippi</a>. <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Power" title="Mississippi Power">Mississippi Power</a>, a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Southern_Company" title="Southern Company">Southern Company</a>, began construction of the plant in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The initial, coal-fired project was central to President Obama's Climate Plan, as it was to be based on "clean coal"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kemp2016_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-kemp2016-4">[4]</a></sup> and was being considered for more support from the Congress and the incoming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trump_Administration" title="Trump Administration">Trump Administration</a> in late 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> If it had become operational with coal, the Kemper Project would have been a first-of-its-kind electricity plant to employ gasification and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture</a> technologies at this scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Project management problems had been noted at the Kemper Project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kemp2016_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-kemp2016-4">[4]</a></sup> The plant was supposed to be in service by May 2014, at a cost of $2.4 billion.  As of June 2017, the project was still not in service, and the cost had increased to $7.5 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> According to a <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club" title="Sierra Club">Sierra Club</a> analysis, Kemper is the most expensive power plant ever built, based on its generating capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Drajem_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Drajem-8">[8]</a></sup> In June 2017, Southern Company and Mississippi Power announced that the Kemper project would switch to burning only natural gas in an effort to manage costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ars_nat_gas_2017_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ars_nat_gas_2017-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kemper_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kemper_County" title="Kemper County">Kemper County</a> is a small county in eastern Mississippi, roughly 30 miles north of <a href="/wiki/Meridian,_Mississippi" title="Meridian, Mississippi">Meridian</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Kemper County was chosen as the site for the plant to take advantage of local brown coal (<a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a>), an untapped natural resource, while providing geographic diversity to help balance the electric demand and strengthen electric reliability in Mississippi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Power" title="Mississippi Power">Mississippi Power</a> is a large energy company based in Gulfport, providing energy for <a href="/wiki/Gulfport,_Mississippi" title="Gulfport, Mississippi">Gulfport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biloxi,_Mississippi" title="Biloxi, Mississippi">Biloxi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hattiesburg,_Mississippi" title="Hattiesburg, Mississippi">Hattiesburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meridian,_Mississippi" title="Meridian, Mississippi">Meridian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pascagoula,_Mississippi" title="Pascagoula, Mississippi">Pascagoula</a>, <a href="/wiki/Columbia,_Mississippi" title="Columbia, Mississippi">Columbia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laurel,_Mississippi" title="Laurel, Mississippi">Laurel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waveland,_Mississippi" title="Waveland, Mississippi">Waveland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lucedale,_Mississippi" title="Lucedale, Mississippi">Lucedale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Picayune,_Mississippi" title="Picayune, Mississippi">Picayune</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mississippi Power intended the Kemper Project to produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clean_energy" title="Clean energy">cleaner energy</a> through the use of <a href="/wiki/Integrated_gasification_combined_cycle" title="Integrated gasification combined cycle">integrated gasification combined cycle (IGCC)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture</a> technologies, eliminating the majority of emissions normally emitted by a traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_plant" title="Coal plant">coal plant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB01_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB01-13">[13]</a></sup> A study conducted by Southern Company (parent of Mississippi Power) stated that the Kemper Project would have been "a large undertaking with high visibility and ... help set the stage for future coal-based power generation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-netl.doe.gov_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-netl.doe.gov-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>On June 3, 2010, the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Public_Service_Commission" title="Mississippi Public Service Commission">Mississippi Public Service Commission</a> certified the project and the ground-breaking ceremony took place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-netl.doe.gov_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-netl.doe.gov-14">[14]</a></sup> Governor <a href="/wiki/Haley_Barbour" title="Haley Barbour">Haley Barbour</a> was present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lignite">Lignite</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kemper_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lignite">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">Lignite</a> is a soft, brownish-black coal that has the lowest energy content of any type of coal. It is also very dirty when burned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lignite-coal.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="776" data-file-width="1494" height="114" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Lignite-coal.jpg/220px-Lignite-coal.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Lignite-coal.jpg/330px-Lignite-coal.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Lignite-coal.jpg/440px-Lignite-coal.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lignite-coal.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lignite coal</div></div></div>
<p>According to the Lignite Energy Council about 79 percent of lignite coal is used to generate electricity, 13.5 percent to generate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthetic_natural_gas" title="Synthetic natural gas">synthetic natural gas</a>, and 7.5 percent to produce fertilizer products. Mississippi has an estimated five billion tons of coal reserves, consisting almost entirely of <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">eocene</a> lignite. The typical lignite beds that can be economically mined range from two to nine feet thick. Mississippi’s lignite resources equal about 13 percent of total U.S. lignite reserves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Kemper plant was expected to use about 375,000 tons of locally mined lignite per month or almost 185 million tons over the plant’s expected 40-year life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mspowertimeline_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-mspowertimeline-32">[32]</a></sup> TRIG technology can utilize lignite, which is also a driving factor of the technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology">Technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kemper_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Power" title="Mississippi Power">Mississippi Power's</a> Kemper plant was intended to be an <a href="/wiki/Integrated_gasification_combined_cycle" title="Integrated gasification combined cycle">integrated gasification combined cycle</a> (IGCC) facility, utilizing a technology known as "transport integrated gasification" (TRIG) to convert <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a> coal—mined on the Kemper site—into natural gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sseb.org_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-sseb.org-34">[34]</a></sup>  The natural gas would then have been used to power turbines to generate electricity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mufson_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mufson-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mississippi Power stated that, by adding coal to its sources of power, it wished to add balance to its fuel-source choices, and be less reliant on any one form of energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> There is an estimated four billion tons of lignite available to be used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sseb.org_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-sseb.org-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>If successful, the Kemper Project would have been the second TRIG facility in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sseb.org_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-sseb.org-34">[34]</a></sup> Producing electricity from <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> in this way produces tremendous amounts of carbon dioxide, and <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Power" title="Mississippi Power">Mississippi Power</a> hoped that 65 percent of the carbon dioxide would be captured and utilized in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enhanced_Oil_Recovery" title="Enhanced Oil Recovery">Enhanced Oil Recovery</a> at neighboring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_fields" title="Oil fields">oil fields</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sseb.org_34-3"><a href="#cite_note-sseb.org-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport_integrated_gasification_technology">Transport integrated gasification technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kemper_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Transport integrated gasification technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>TRIG was developed by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">Department of Energy</a>, Southern Company and <a href="/wiki/KBR_(company)" title="KBR (company)">KBR</a> at the Power Systems Development Facility in Wilsonville, Alabama.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brewer_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-brewer-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Southern_Company" title="Southern Company">Southern Company</a> stated that TRIG is a superior coal-gasification method with low impacts to the environment. TRIG technology can utilize lignite, which accounts for more than half of the world's coal reserves and drove global interest in the plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gasificationTRIG_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-gasificationTRIG-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Power Magazine</i> posted an article in April 2013, walking through the technology in technical detail. They say, "Commercial TRIG units can be designed to achieve high environmental standards for <span class="chemf nowrap">SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>, NO<sub>x</sub>, dust emissions, mercury, and CO<sub>2</sub>. Cost analysis based on extensive design has shown that the economic benefits offered by the air-blown transport gasifier relative to other systems are preserved even when CO<sub>2</sub> capture and sequestration are incorporated into the design."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Clean_coal">Clean coal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kemper_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Clean coal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>If the <a href="/wiki/Carbon-neutral_fuel" title="Carbon-neutral fuel">carbon, capture and sequestration</a> technology used at the Kemper Project had been successful, it would have been the United States’ first clean coal plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-6">[6]</a></sup> The need for this type of technology has come from decades of debate among energy leaders on how to minimize carbon dioxide emissions into the earth's atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> In 2013, the United States' coal use was 40%, dominating all other energy sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>  Realizing the demand for coal was not decreasing, Mississippi Power, Southern Company, KBR, and the Department of Energy invested in technology to capture emissions from burning fossil fuels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The investing bodies argued the type of clean coal technology they claim are found at the Kemper Project will be adopted worldwide; bringing profits back to Mississippi customers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>Environmentalists state that clean coal is not a possibility, as some emissions will still be emitted into the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_capture_and_sequestration">Carbon capture and sequestration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kemper_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Carbon capture and sequestration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Carbon capture and sequestration, also referred to as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a> (CCS), is a technology that can capture up to 90% of the carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>) emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>  CCS uses a combination of technologies to capture the CO<sub>2</sub> released in the combustion process, transport it to a suitable storage location and finally store it (typically deep underground) where it cannot enter the atmosphere and thus contribute to climate change. CO<sub>2</sub> sequestration options include saline formations and oil wells, where captured CO<sub>2</sub> can be utilized in enhanced oil recovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>Due to rising global demand for energy, the consumption of fossil fuels is expected to rise until 2035, leading to greater CO<sub>2</sub> emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_dioxide_enhanced_oil_recovery">Carbon dioxide enhanced oil recovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kemper_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Carbon dioxide enhanced oil recovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Carbon dioxide <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">enhanced oil recovery</a> or CO2-EOR increases the amount of oil recovered from an underground <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_reservoir" title="Oil reservoir">oil reservoir</a>. By pumping CO<sub>2</sub> into an oil reservoir, previously unrecoverable oil is pushed up to where the oil can be reached. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Department_of_Energy" title="US Department of Energy">US Department of Energy</a> states that this can produce an additional 30 to 60 percent of the original amount of recoverable oil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Once all of the recoverable oil has been reached, the depleted <a href="/wiki/Reservoir" title="Reservoir">reservoir</a> can act as a storage site for the CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Kemper Plant was planned to have 60 miles of pipeline to carry its captured CO<sub>2</sub> to neighboring oil reserves for <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">enhanced oil recovery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NETL_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-NETL-51">[51]</a></sup> Each year, the plant will capture 3 million tons of CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NETL_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-NETL-51">[51]</a></sup> In March 2014, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> published that the diverted CO<sub>2</sub> will be pumped into two Mississippi companies for use in enhanced oil recovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Research_.26_development"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_&amp;_development">Research &amp; development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kemper_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Research &amp; development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Department of Energy, the Southern Company, and construction management firm KBR (Kellogg, Brown &amp; Root) joined together at the Power Systems Development Facility (PSDF) in <a href="/wiki/Wilsonville,_Alabama" title="Wilsonville, Alabama">Wilsonville, Alabama</a> to develop a process known as Transport Integrated Gasification (TRIG). This development started in 1996, and the gasifier design of <a href="/wiki/Southern_Company" title="Southern Company">Southern Company's</a> Kemper Coal Plant is based on this specific research and development. The technology is most cost-effective when using low-heat content, high moisture, or high-ash content coals, including lignite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Energy" title="U.S. Department of Energy">U.S. Department of Energy</a>, coal <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">gasification</a> offers one of the most versatile and clean ways to convert coal into electricity, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, and other valuable energy products. Rather than burning coal directly, gasification (a <a href="/wiki/Thermochemistry" title="Thermochemistry">thermo-chemical</a> process) breaks down coal into its basic chemical constituents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>The technology of processing coal to gas on a commercial scale has been in development since the 1970s, and it has been in use since the mid-1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>The TRIG technology, derived from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluidized" title="Fluidized">fluidized</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalytic" title="Catalytic">catalytic</a> cracking units used in the <a href="/wiki/Petrochemical" title="Petrochemical">petrochemical</a> industry, uses a pressurized, circulating fluidized bed unit. The transport gasification system features higher efficiencies and is capable of processing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_rank" title="Coal rank">low-rank</a> coals, such as lignite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Additionally, commercial TRIG units can be designed to achieve high environmental standards for <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide" title="Nitrogen dioxide">nitrogen dioxide</a>, dust emissions, <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. Cost analysis based on the Kemper Coal Plant's design has shown that the economic benefits offered by the air-blown transport gasifier, relative to other systems, are preserved even when carbon dioxide capture and sequestration methodologies are incorporated into the design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kemper_County_IGCC_Project_Update_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kemper_County_IGCC_Project_Update-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>The largest transport gasifier built to date commenced operation in 1996 at <a href="/wiki/Southern_Company" title="Southern Company">Southern Company</a>'s PSDF. The gasifier and auxiliary equipment at the site were sized to provide reliable data for confident scale-up to commercial scale.  The demonstration unit proved easy to operate and control, achieving more than 15,600 hours of gasification. The demonstration-scale gasifier successfully gasified high-moisture lignite from the Red Hills Mine in Mississippi in four separate test campaigns for more than 2,300 hours of operations. On lignite, the transport gasifier operated smoothly over a range of conditions, confirming the gasifier design for Kemper County.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kemper_County_IGCC_Project_Update_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kemper_County_IGCC_Project_Update-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_issues">Legal issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kemper_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Legal issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In February 2015, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mississippi_Supreme_Court" title="Mississippi Supreme Court">Mississippi Supreme Court</a> ruled <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Power" title="Mississippi Power">Mississippi Power</a> must refund 186,000 South Mississippi ratepayers for rate increases related to the Kemper Project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> These fees are derived from Mississippi's Baseload Act, allowing Mississippi Power to charge ratepayers for powerplants under construction.
</p><p>In May 2016, Southern Company and its subsidiary Mississippi Power announced they were being investigated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="Securities and Exchange Commission">Securities and Exchange Commission</a> related to overruns at the Kemper Project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> The project had been repeatedly delayed and costs increased from $2.88 billion to $6.7 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SEC_investigation_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-SEC_investigation-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2016, Mississippi Power was sued by Treetop Midstream Services over the cancellation of a contract to receive <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> from the Kemper Project as part of the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a> design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Treetop had contracted to buy carbon dioxide from the Kemper plant and had built a pipeline in preparation to receive the gas. Treetop alleged Mississippi Power had fraudulently and "intentionally misrepresenting and concealing the start date" for the Kemper Project, though Mississippi Power stated the suit was without merit.
</p><p>The company was also found to have unlawfully fired a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whistle-blower" title="Whistle-blower">whistle-blower</a> who had criticized alleged false statements by company management.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_controversies">Environmental controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kemper_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Environmental controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Environmental groups argue that the project is an expensive undertaking that offers only limited benefits. Groups such as the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club" title="Sierra Club">Sierra Club</a> and Bridge the Gulf are encouraging the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Army_Corps_of_Engineers" title="U.S. Army Corps of Engineers">U.S. Army Corps of Engineers</a> to deny the required <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetland</a> permits, which Mississippi Company would have to fill to build the plant's facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Mississippi Chapter of the Sierra Club is arguing that the location where the facilities are planned to be built needs to be left alone. They argue that the position of the facilities on a wetland will pollute the environment with tainted water runoff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>  Also, they believe that the extraction of the lignite will erode the environment and force the relocation of many Mississippians. Mitigation construction activities included the enhancement of 31 acres of wetlands, 105 acres of riparian buffer, and approximately 3,000 linear feet of stream channel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> In an agreement with the city of Meridian, the plant is using city wastewater as its only water source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Additionally, the Kemper Project site is a "zero" liquid discharge facility.  Therefore, no processed water from the plant is discharged into rivers, creeks or streams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_controversies">Political controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kemper_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Political controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mississippi Governor <a href="/wiki/Haley_Barbour" title="Haley Barbour">Haley Barbour</a> has praised the planned project's potential of placing Mississippi in national prominence, mostly because it would be the first U.S. commercial-scale power plant to capture <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCullough_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-McCullough-68">[68]</a></sup> Additionally, former <a href="/wiki/Speaker_of_the_United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="Speaker of the United States House of Representatives">Speaker of the House</a> <a href="/wiki/Newt_Gingrich" title="Newt Gingrich">Newt Gingrich</a> expressed his support for the Kemper Project, stating that in his opinion it had the potential to be the single most important experiment in developing electricity in the world today. Gingrich's closing words of encouragement for the Kemper Project and the state of Mississippi: "You have a chance to be a remarkable leader in the country in the next 10 to 20 years."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Kemper Project received an estimated $270 million in Department of Energy funds after the Southern Company’s plan for the proposed Orlando Gasification Project bunked when <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> decided the state was not interested in more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_plant" title="Coal plant">coal plants</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCullough_68-1"><a href="#cite_note-McCullough-68">[68]</a></sup> These transferred funds were moved from Florida to Mississippi in December 2008, after Haley Barbour's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_D.C." title="Washington D.C.">Washington D.C.</a> lobbying firm, the BGR Group, pushed for the reallocation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCullough_68-2"><a href="#cite_note-McCullough-68">[68]</a></sup> Southern Company has been a BGR client since 1999, having spent a total of $2.6 million with the firm, according to federal lobbying disclosure documents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCullough_68-3"><a href="#cite_note-McCullough-68">[68]</a></sup> Southern Company alleges that Governor Barbour did not help them receive any additional funding at all.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McCullough_68-4"><a href="#cite_note-McCullough-68">[68]</a></sup> The BGR Group website has deleted all connections with Southern Company from its website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mississippi state law was changed to permit charging ratepayers for construction of the facility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT6516_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT6516-71">[71]</a></sup> In 2017 the Mississippi Public Service Commission recommended the facility burn natural gas rather than syngas from coal to avoid the risk of further consumer rate increases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ars_nat_gas_2017_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-ars_nat_gas_2017-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The plant missed all its targets and plans for "clean coal" generation were abandoned in July 2017. The plant is expected to go ahead burning natural gas only.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p>
