<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Permafrost</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, <b>permafrost</b> is ground,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osterkamp_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Osterkamp-1">[1]</a></sup> including <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rock</a> or (cryotic) <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, at or below the freezing point of water 0 °C (32 °F) for two or more years. Most permafrost is located in high <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitudes</a> (in and around the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">Arctic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circle" title="Antarctic Circle">Antarctic regions</a>), but at lower latitudes <b>alpine permafrost</b> occurs at higher <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">elevations</a>. Ground ice is not always present, as may be in the case of non-porous bedrock, but it frequently occurs and it may be in amounts exceeding the potential hydraulic saturation of the ground material. Permafrost accounts for 0.022% of total water on Earth<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and exists in 24% of exposed land in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvey,_2013-02-21_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harvey,_2013-02-21-4">[4]</a></sup> It also occurs subsea on the continental shelves of the continents surrounding the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a>, portions of which were exposed during the last glacial period,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPA_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-IPA-5">[5]</a></sup> with global weather implications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>A global temperature rise of 1.5 °C (2.7 °F) above current levels would be enough to start the thawing of permafrost in Siberia, according to one group of scientists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvey,_2013-02-21_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Harvey,_2013-02-21-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extent">Extent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Permafrost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Extent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vertical_Temperature_Profile_in_Permafrost_(English_Text).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="599" data-file-width="579" height="228" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Vertical_Temperature_Profile_in_Permafrost_%28English_Text%29.jpg/220px-Vertical_Temperature_Profile_in_Permafrost_%28English_Text%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Vertical_Temperature_Profile_in_Permafrost_%28English_Text%29.jpg/330px-Vertical_Temperature_Profile_in_Permafrost_%28English_Text%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Vertical_Temperature_Profile_in_Permafrost_%28English_Text%29.jpg/440px-Vertical_Temperature_Profile_in_Permafrost_%28English_Text%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vertical_Temperature_Profile_in_Permafrost_(English_Text).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Red lines: Seasonal temperature extremes (dotted=average).</div></div></div>
<p>Permafrost is <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rock</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a> that is frozen for more than two consecutive years. In areas not overlain by ice, it exists beneath a layer of soil, rock or sediment, which freezes and thaws annually and is called the "active layer".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Definition_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Definition-8">[8]</a></sup> In practice, this means that permafrost occurs at an mean annual temperature of −2 °C (28.4 °F) or colder. Active layer thickness varies with the season, but is 0.3 to 4 meters thick (shallow along the Arctic coast; deep in southern Siberia and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qinghai-Tibetan_Plateau" title="Qinghai-Tibetan Plateau">Qinghai-Tibetan Plateau</a>). The extent of permafrost varies with the climate: in the Northern Hemisphere today, 24% of the ice-free land area, equivalent to 19 million square kilometers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tarnocai_2009_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tarnocai_2009-9">[9]</a></sup> is more or less influenced by permafrost. Of this area slightly more than half is underlain by continuous permafrost, around 20 percent by discontinuous permafrost, and a little less than 30 percent by sporadic permafrost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Most of this area is found in Siberia, northern Canada, Alaska and Greenland. Beneath the active layer annual temperature swings of permafrost become smaller with depth. The deepest depth of permafrost occurs where geothermal heat maintains a temperature above freezing. Above that bottom limit there may be permafrost, whose temperature doesn't change annually—"isothermal permafrost".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Degradation_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Degradation-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Continuity_of_coverage">Continuity of coverage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Permafrost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Continuity of coverage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Permafrost typically forms in any <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> where the mean annual air temperature is less than the freezing point of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>. Exceptions are found in moist-wintered forest climates, such as in Northern Scandinavia and the North-Eastern part of European Russia west of the <a href="/wiki/Ural_Mountains" title="Ural Mountains">Urals</a>, where snow acts as an insulating blanket. Glaciated areas may be exceptions. Since all glaciers are warmed at their base by geothermal heat, <a href="/wiki/Glacier#Types" title="Glacier">temperate glaciers</a>, which are near the <a href="/wiki/Pressure_melting_point" title="Pressure melting point">pressure-melting</a> point throughout, may have liquid water at the interface with the ground and are therefore free of underlying permafrost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>  "Fossil" cold anomalies in the <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_gradient" title="Geothermal gradient">Geothermal gradient</a> in areas where deep permafrost developed during the Pleistocene persist down to several hundred metres. This is evident from temperature measurements in boreholes in North America and Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Discontinuous_permafrost">Discontinuous permafrost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Permafrost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Discontinuous permafrost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Typically, the below-ground temperature varies less from season to season than the air temperature, with mean annual temperatures tending to increase with depth as a result of the geothermal crustal gradient. Thus, if the mean annual air temperature is only slightly below 0 °C (32 °F), permafrost will form only in spots that are sheltered—usually with a northerly <a href="/wiki/Aspect_(geography)" title="Aspect (geography)">aspect</a>—creating <i>discontinuous permafrost</i>. Usually, permafrost will remain discontinuous in a climate where the mean annual soil surface temperature is between −5 and 0 °C (23 and 32 °F). In the moist-wintered areas mentioned before, there may not be even discontinuous permafrost down to −2 °C (28 °F). Discontinuous permafrost is often further divided into <i>extensive discontinuous permafrost</i>, where permafrost covers between 50 and 90 percent of the landscape and is usually found in areas with mean annual temperatures between −2 and −4 °C (28 and 25 °F), and <i>sporadic permafrost</i>, where permafrost cover is less than 50 percent of the landscape and typically occurs at mean annual temperatures between 0 and −2 °C (32 and 28 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BrownPéwé_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-BrownPéwé-14">[14]</a></sup>
In soil science, the sporadic permafrost zone is abbreviated <b>SPZ</b> and the extensive discontinuous permafrost zone <b>DPZ</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Exceptions occur in <i>un-glaciated</i> <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> where the present depth of permafrost is a relic of climatic conditions during glacial ages where winters were up to 11 °C (20 °F) colder than those of today.
</p>

<p>At mean annual soil surface temperatures below −5 °C (23 °F) the influence of aspect can never be sufficient to thaw permafrost and a zone of <i>continuous permafrost</i> (abbreviated to <b>CPZ</b>) forms. A <i>line of continuous permafrost</i> in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> represents the most southerly border where land is covered by continuous permafrost or glacial ice. The line of continuous permafrost varies around the world northward or southward due to regional climatic changes. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_hemisphere" title="Southern hemisphere">southern hemisphere</a>, most of the equivalent line would fall within the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a> if there were land there. Most of the <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctic continent</a> is overlain by glaciers, under which much of the terrain is subject to basal <a href="/wiki/Pressure_melting_point" title="Pressure melting point">melting</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The exposed land of Antarctica is substantially underlain with permafrost,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> some of which is subject to warming and thawing along the coastline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alpine_permafrost">Alpine permafrost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Permafrost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Alpine permafrost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Estimates of the total area of alpine permafrost vary. Bockheim and Munroe<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BockhMunr_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-BockhMunr-16">[16]</a></sup> combined three sources and made the tabulated estimates by region, totaling 3,560,000 km<sup>2</sup> (1,370,000 sq mi).
</p><p>Alpine permafrost in the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a> has not been mapped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Its extent has been modeled to assess the amount of water bound up in these areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In 2009, a researcher from Alaska found permafrost at the 4,700 m (15,400 ft) level on Africa's highest peak, <a href="/wiki/Mount_Kilimanjaro" title="Mount Kilimanjaro">Mount Kilimanjaro</a>, approximately 3° north of the equator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsea_permafrost">Subsea permafrost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Permafrost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Subsea permafrost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Subsea permafrost occurs beneath the seabed and exists in the continental shelves of the polar regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPA_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-IPA-5">[5]</a></sup> These areas formed during the last ice age, when a larger portion of Earth's water was bound up in ice sheets on land and when sea levels were low. As the ice sheets melted to again become seawater, the permafrost became submerged shelves under relatively warm and salty boundary conditions, compared to surface permafrost. Therefore, subsea permafrost exists in conditions that lead to its diminishment. According to Osterkamp, subsea permafrost is a factor in the "design, construction, and operation of coastal facilities, structures founded on the seabed, artificial islands, sub-sea pipelines, and wells drilled for exploration and production."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osterkamp_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Osterkamp-1">[1]</a></sup> It also contains gas hydrates in places, which are both a "potential abundant source of energy", but also may destabilize, as subsea permafrost warms and thaws, producing large amounts of methane gas, which is a potent green-house gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osterkamp_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Osterkamp-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Base_depth">Base depth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Permafrost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Base depth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Permafrost extends to a base depth where geothermal heat from the Earth and the mean annual temperature at the surface achieve an equilibrium temperature of 0 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ostercamp_Burn_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ostercamp_Burn-26">[26]</a></sup> The base depth of permafrost reaches  1,493 m (4,898 ft) in the northern <a href="/wiki/Lena_River" title="Lena River">Lena</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yana_River" title="Yana River">Yana River</a> basins in <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Desonie_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Desonie-27">[27]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_gradient" title="Geothermal gradient">geothermal gradient</a> is the rate of increasing temperature with respect to increasing depth in the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s interior. Away from tectonic plate boundaries, it is about 25–30 °C/km (72–87 °F/mi) near the surface in most of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC-28">[28]</a></sup> It varies with the thermal conductivity of geologic material and is less for permafrost in soil than in bedrock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ostercamp_Burn_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ostercamp_Burn-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Calculations indicate that the time required to form the deep permafrost underlying <a href="/wiki/Prudhoe_Bay,_Alaska" title="Prudhoe Bay, Alaska">Prudhoe Bay, Alaska</a> was over a half-million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lunardini_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lunardini-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> This extended over several glacial and interglacial cycles of the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> and suggests that the present climate of Prudhoe Bay is probably considerably warmer than it has been on average over that period. Such warming over the past 15,000 years is widely accepted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lunardini_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lunardini-25">[25]</a></sup> The table to the right shows that the first hundred metres of permafrost forms relatively quickly but that deeper levels take progressively longer.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Massive_ground_ice">Massive ground ice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Permafrost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Massive ground ice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Permafrost_-_Massive_buried_ice_(blue).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="589" data-file-width="785" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Permafrost_-_Massive_buried_ice_%28blue%29.png/220px-Permafrost_-_Massive_buried_ice_%28blue%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Permafrost_-_Massive_buried_ice_%28blue%29.png/330px-Permafrost_-_Massive_buried_ice_%28blue%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Permafrost_-_Massive_buried_ice_%28blue%29.png/440px-Permafrost_-_Massive_buried_ice_%28blue%29.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Permafrost_-_Massive_buried_ice_(blue).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Massive blue ground ice exposure on the north shore of Herschel Island, Yukon, Canada.</div></div></div>
<p>When the ice content of a permafrost exceeds 250 percent (ice to dry soil by mass) it is classified as <i>massive</i> ice. Massive ice bodies can range in composition, in every conceivable gradation from icy mud to pure ice. Massive icy beds have a minimum thickness of at least 2 m, a short diameter of at least 10 m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mackay1973_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mackay1973-30">[30]</a></sup> First recorded North American observations were by European scientists at Canning River, Alaska in 1919.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-French2007_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-French2007-31">[31]</a></sup> Russian literature provides an earlier date of 1735 and 1739 during the Great North Expedition by P. Lassinius and Kh. P. Laptev, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shumskiy-Vtyurin1966_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shumskiy-Vtyurin1966-32">[32]</a></sup> Two categories of massive ground ice are <i>buried surface ice</i> and <i>intrasedimental ice</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mackay1992_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mackay1992-33">[33]</a></sup> (also called <i>constitutional ice</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shumskiy-Vtyurin1966_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shumskiy-Vtyurin1966-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>Buried surface ice may derive from snow, frozen lake or sea ice, <a href="/wiki/Aufeis" title="Aufeis">aufeis</a> (stranded river ice) and—probably the most prevalent—buried glacial ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Intrasedimental ice forms by in-place freezing of subterranean waters and is dominated by segregational ice which results from the crystallizational differentiation taking place during the freezing of wet sediments, accompanied by water migrating to the freezing front.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shumskiy-Vtyurin1966_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-Shumskiy-Vtyurin1966-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>Intrasedimental or constitutional ice has been widely observed and studied across Canada and also includes intrusive and injection ice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-French2007_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-French2007-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shumskiy-Vtyurin1966_32-3"><a href="#cite_note-Shumskiy-Vtyurin1966-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>Additionally, ice wedges—a separate type of ground ice—produce recognizable patterned ground or tundra polygons. Ice wedges form in a pre-existing geological substrate and were first described in 1919.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-French2007_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-French2007-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shumskiy-Vtyurin1966_32-4"><a href="#cite_note-Shumskiy-Vtyurin1966-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:1002px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Massive_ice_-_retrogressive_thaw_slump_-_Herschel_Island.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="530" data-file-width="4267" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Massive_ice_-_retrogressive_thaw_slump_-_Herschel_Island.png/1000px-Massive_ice_-_retrogressive_thaw_slump_-_Herschel_Island.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Massive_ice_-_retrogressive_thaw_slump_-_Herschel_Island.png/1500px-Massive_ice_-_retrogressive_thaw_slump_-_Herschel_Island.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Massive_ice_-_retrogressive_thaw_slump_-_Herschel_Island.png/2000px-Massive_ice_-_retrogressive_thaw_slump_-_Herschel_Island.png 2x" width="1000"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Massive_ice_-_retrogressive_thaw_slump_-_Herschel_Island.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Several types of massive ground ice, including ice wedges and intrasedimental ice within the cliff wall of a retrogressive thaw slump located on the southern coast of Herschel Island within an approximately 22-metre (72 ft) by 1,300-metre (4,300 ft) headwall.</div></div></div></div>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_cycle_in_permafrost">Carbon cycle in permafrost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Permafrost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Carbon cycle in permafrost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Permafrost_carbon_cycle" title="Permafrost carbon cycle">permafrost carbon cycle</a> (Arctic Carbon Cycle) deals with the transfer of carbon from permafrost soils to terrestrial vegetation and microbes, to the atmosphere, back to vegetation, and finally back to permafrost soils through burial and sedimentation due to cryogenic processes. Some of this carbon is transferred to the ocean and other portions of the globe through the global carbon cycle. The cycle includes the exchange of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> between terrestrial components and the atmosphere, as well as the transfer of carbon between land and water as methane, <a href="/wiki/Dissolved_organic_carbon" title="Dissolved organic carbon">dissolved organic carbon</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dissolved_inorganic_carbon" title="Dissolved inorganic carbon">dissolved inorganic carbon</a>, particulate inorganic carbon and particulate organic carbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcguire_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcguire-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change_effects">Climate change effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Permafrost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Climate change effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Arctic permafrost has been diminishing for many centuries. The consequence is thawing soil, which may be weaker, and release of methane, which contributes to an increased rate of global warming as part of a feedback loop.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_changes">Historical changes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Permafrost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Historical changes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Beaufort_Permafrost2.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1224" data-file-width="1632" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Beaufort_Permafrost2.JPG/220px-Beaufort_Permafrost2.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Beaufort_Permafrost2.JPG/330px-Beaufort_Permafrost2.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Beaufort_Permafrost2.JPG/440px-Beaufort_Permafrost2.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Beaufort_Permafrost2.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Recently thawed Arctic permafrost and coastal erosion on the Beaufort Sea, Arctic Ocean, near <a href="/wiki/Point_Lonely_Short_Range_Radar_Site" title="Point Lonely Short Range Radar Site">Point Lonely, Alaska</a> in 2013.</div></div></div>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">Last Glacial Maximum</a>, continuous permafrost covered a much greater area than it does today, covering all of ice-free <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> south to about <a href="/wiki/Szeged" title="Szeged">Szeged</a> (southeastern Hungary) and the <a href="/wiki/Sea_of_Azov" title="Sea of Azov">Sea of Azov</a> (then dry land)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a> south to present-day <a href="/wiki/Changchun" title="Changchun">Changchun</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abashiri,_Hokkaid%C5%8D" title="Abashiri, Hokkaidō">Abashiri</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, only an extremely narrow belt of permafrost existed south of the <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheet</a> at about the latitude of <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> through southern <a href="/wiki/Iowa" title="Iowa">Iowa</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a>, but permafrost was more extensive in the drier western regions where it extended to the southern border of <a href="/wiki/Idaho" title="Idaho">Idaho</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> In the southern hemisphere, there is some evidence for former permafrost from this period in central <a href="/wiki/Otago" title="Otago">Otago</a> and <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentine</a> <a href="/wiki/Patagonia" title="Patagonia">Patagonia</a>, but was probably discontinuous, and is related to the tundra. Alpine permafrost also occurred in the <a href="/wiki/Drakensberg" title="Drakensberg">Drakensberg</a> during glacial maxima above about 3,000 metres (9,840 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thaw">Thaw</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Permafrost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Thaw">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Thermokarst" title="Thermokarst">Thermokarst</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Permafrost_thaw_ponds_in_Hudson_Bay_Canada_near_Greenland.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2912" data-file-width="4088" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Permafrost_thaw_ponds_in_Hudson_Bay_Canada_near_Greenland.jpg/220px-Permafrost_thaw_ponds_in_Hudson_Bay_Canada_near_Greenland.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Permafrost_thaw_ponds_in_Hudson_Bay_Canada_near_Greenland.jpg/330px-Permafrost_thaw_ponds_in_Hudson_Bay_Canada_near_Greenland.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Permafrost_thaw_ponds_in_Hudson_Bay_Canada_near_Greenland.jpg/440px-Permafrost_thaw_ponds_in_Hudson_Bay_Canada_near_Greenland.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Permafrost_thaw_ponds_in_Hudson_Bay_Canada_near_Greenland.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Permafrost thaw ponds on peatland in <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Bay" title="Hudson Bay">Hudson Bay</a>, Canada in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The ground can consist of many substrate materials, including bedrock, sediment, organic matter, water or ice. Frozen ground is that which is below the freezing point of water, whether or not water is present in the substrate. Ground ice is not always present, as may be the case with nonporous bedrock, but it frequently occurs and may be present in amounts exceeding the potential <a href="/wiki/Phreatic_zone" title="Phreatic zone">hydraulic saturation</a> of the thawed substrate.
</p><p>By definition, permafrost is ground that remains frozen for two or more years. Since frozen soil, including permafrost, comprises a large percentage of substrate materials other than ice, it thaws rather than melts even as any ice content melts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> An analogy is when a freezer door is left open, although the ice in the freezer may change phase to a liquid, the food solids will not experience a phase change. In aggregate, the food <i>thaws</i> but does not <i>melt</i>. <i>Melting</i> implies the phase change of all solids to liquid. One visible sign of permafrost degradation is the <a href="/wiki/Drunken_trees" title="Drunken trees">random displacement of trees from their vertical orientation</a> in permafrost areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stevens_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stevens-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-618-12744-5_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-618-12744-5-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>As a consequence, precipitation has increased which in turn results in the weakening and eventual collapse of buildings in areas such as Norilsk in northern Russia, which lies upon the permafrost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_on_slope_stability">Effect on slope stability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Permafrost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Effect on slope stability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Over the past century, an increasing number of alpine rock slope failure events in mountain ranges around the world have been recorded. It is expected that the high number of structural failures is due to permafrost thawing, which is thought to be linked to climate change. Permafrost thawing is thought to have contributed to the 1987 <a href="/wiki/Val_Pola_landslide" title="Val Pola landslide">Val Pola landslide</a> that killed 22 people in the Italian Alps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> In mountain ranges, much of the structural stability can be attributed to glaciers and permafrost. As climate warms, permafrost thaws, which results in a less stable mountain structure, and ultimately more slope failures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> 
Increasing temperatures allow deeper active layer depths, resulting in increased water infiltration. Ice within the soil melts, causing loss of soil strength, accelerated movement, and potential debris flows.
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>McSaveney <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> reported massive rock and ice falls (up to 11.8 million m<sup>3</sup>), earthquakes (up to 3.9 Richter), floods (up to 7.8 million m<sup>3</sup> water), and rapid rock-ice flow to long distances (up to 7.5 km at 60 m/s) caused by “instability of slopes” in high mountain permafrost.  Instability of slopes in permafrost at elevated temperatures near freezing point in warming permafrost is related to effective stress and buildup of pore-water pressure in these soils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>  Kia and his co-inventors <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> invented a new filter-less rigid piezometer (FRP) for measuring pore-water pressure in partially frozen soils such as warming permafrost soils. They extended the use of effective stress concept to partially frozen soils for use in slope stability analysis of warming permafrost slopes. The use of effective stress concept has many advantages such as ability to extend the concepts of "Critical State Soil Mechanics" into frozen ground engineering.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_consequences">Ecological consequences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Permafrost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Ecological consequences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the northern circumpolar region, permafrost contains 1700 billion tons of organic material equaling almost half of all organic material in all soils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tarnocai_2009_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tarnocai_2009-9">[9]</a></sup> This pool was built up over thousands of years and is only slowly degraded under the cold conditions in the Arctic. The amount of carbon sequestered in permafrost is four times the carbon that has been released to the atmosphere due to human activities in modern time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schuur_2011_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schuur_2011-54">[54]</a></sup> One manifestation of this is <a href="/wiki/Yedoma" title="Yedoma">yedoma</a>, which is an organic-rich (about 2% carbon by mass) <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a>-age <a href="/wiki/Loess" title="Loess">loess</a> permafrost with ice content of 50–90% by volume.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>Formation of permafrost has significant consequences for ecological systems, primarily due to constraints imposed upon rooting zones, but also due to limitations on den and <a href="/wiki/Burrow" title="Burrow">burrow</a> geometries for <a href="/wiki/Fauna" title="Fauna">fauna</a> requiring subsurface homes. Secondary effects impact <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> dependent on plants and animals whose habitat is constrained by the permafrost. One of the most widespread examples is the dominance of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_Spruce" title="Black Spruce">Black Spruce</a> in extensive permafrost areas, since this species can tolerate rooting pattern constrained to the near surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>One gram of soil from the active layer may include more than one billion bacteria cells. If placed along each other, bacteria from one kilogram of active layer soil will form a 1000 km long chain. The number of bacteria in permafrost soil varies widely, typically from 1 to 1000 million per gram of soil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Most of these bacteria and fungi in permafrost soil can not be cultured in the laboratory, but the identity of the microorganisms can be revealed by DNA-based techniques.
</p><p>The Arctic region is one of the many natural sources of the greenhouse gas methane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Global warming accelerates its release, due to both release of methane from existing stores, and from <a href="/wiki/Methanogenesis" title="Methanogenesis">methanogenesis</a> in rotting <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_methane_release" title="Arctic methane release">Large quantities of methane are stored in the Arctic</a> in natural gas deposits, permafrost, and as submarine <a href="/wiki/Methane_clathrate" title="Methane clathrate">clathrates</a>. Permafrost and clathrates degrade on warming, thus large releases of methane from these sources may arise as a result of global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zimov2006_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-zimov2006-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shakhova2005_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-shakhova2005-61">[61]</a></sup> Other sources of methane include submarine <a href="/wiki/Talik" title="Talik">taliks</a>, river transport, ice complex retreat, submarine permafrost and decaying gas hydrate deposits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SS07_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-SS07-62">[62]</a></sup> Preliminary computer analyses suggest that permafrost could produce carbon equal to 15 percent or so of today’s emissions from human activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p>A hypothesis forwarded by <a href="/wiki/Sergey_Zimov" title="Sergey Zimov">Sergey Zimov</a> is that the reduction of herds of large herbivores has increased the ratio of energy emission and energy absorption tundra (energy balance) in a manner that increases the tendency for net thawing of permafrost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> He is testing this hypothesis in an experiment at <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene_Park" title="Pleistocene Park">Pleistocene Park</a>, a nature reserve in northeastern Siberia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Predicted_rate_of_change_in_the_Arctic">Predicted rate of change in the Arctic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Permafrost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Predicted rate of change in the Arctic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_methane_release" title="Arctic methane release">Arctic methane release</a></div>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fifth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fifth Assessment Report">IPCC Fifth Assessment Report</a> there is high confidence that permafrost temperatures have increased in most regions since the early 1980s. Observed warming was up to 3 °C in parts of Northern Alaska (early 1980s to mid-2000s) and up to 2 °C in parts of the Russian European North (1971–2010).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Yukon" title="Yukon">Yukon</a>, the zone of continuous permafrost might have moved 100 kilometres (62 mi) poleward since 1899, but accurate records only go back 30 years. It is thought that permafrost thawing could exacerbate global warming by releasing <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a>, which are powerful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gases</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> It also could encourage <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> because permafrost lends stability to barren Arctic slopes.
</p><p>Arctic temperatures are expected to increase at roughly twice the global rate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC) will in their <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fifth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fifth Assessment Report">fifth report</a> establish scenarios for the future, where the temperature in the Arctic will rise between 1.5 and 2.5 °C by 2040 and with 2 to 7.5 °C by 2100. Estimates vary on how many tons of greenhouse gases are emitted from thawed permafrost soils. One estimate suggests that 110–231 billion tons of CO<sub>2</sub> equivalents (about half from carbon dioxide and the other half from methane) will be emitted by 2040, and 850–1400 billion tons by 2100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schuur_2011_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schuur_2011-54">[54]</a></sup> This corresponds to an average annual emission rate of 4–8 billion tons of CO<sub>2</sub> equivalents in the period 2011–2040 and annually 10–16 billion tons of CO<sub>2</sub> equivalents in the period 2011–2100 as a result of thawing permafrost. For comparison, the anthropogenic emission of all greenhouse gases in 2010 is approximately 48 billion tons of CO<sub>2</sub> equivalents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> Release of greenhouse gases from thawed permafrost to the atmosphere may increase global warming.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_issues">Other issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Permafrost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Other issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Permafrost_Association" title="International Permafrost Association">International Permafrost Association</a> (IPA) is an integrator of issues regarding permafrost. It convenes International Permafrost Conferences, undertakes special projects such as preparing databases, maps, bibliographies, and glossaries, and coordinates international field programmes and networks. Among other issues addressed by the IPA are: Problems for construction on permafrost owing to the change of soil properties of the ground on which structures are placed and the biological processes in permafrost, e.g. the preservation of organisms frozen <i>in situ</i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction_on_permafrost">Construction on permafrost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Permafrost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Construction on permafrost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Building on permafrost is difficult because the heat of the building (or <a href="/wiki/Pipeline_transport" title="Pipeline transport">pipeline</a>) can thaw the permafrost and destabilize the structure. Three common solutions include: using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foundation_(architecture)" title="Foundation (architecture)">foundations</a> on wood <a href="/wiki/Deep_foundation" title="Deep foundation">piles</a>; building on a thick <a href="/wiki/Gravel" title="Gravel">gravel</a> pad (usually 1–2 metres/3.3–6.6 feet thick); or using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anhydrous_ammonia" title="Anhydrous ammonia">anhydrous ammonia</a> <a href="/wiki/Heat_pipe" title="Heat pipe">heat pipes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASCE_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-ASCE-72">[72]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Trans-Alaska_Pipeline_System" title="Trans-Alaska Pipeline System">Trans-Alaska Pipeline System</a> uses <a href="/wiki/Heat_pipe#Permafrost_cooling" title="Heat pipe">heat pipes built into vertical supports</a> to prevent the pipeline from sinking and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qingzang_railway" title="Qingzang railway">Qingzang railway</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a> employs a variety of methods to keep the ground cool, both in areas with <a href="/wiki/Frost_heaving#Frost-susceptible_soils" title="Frost heaving">frost-susceptible soil</a>. Permafrost may necessitate special enclosures for buried utilities, called "<a href="/wiki/Utility_tunnel#In_Arctic_towns" title="Utility tunnel">utilidors</a>".
</p><p>The <i>Permafrost Research Institute</i> in <a href="/wiki/Yakutsk" title="Yakutsk">Yakutsk</a>, found that the sinking of large buildings into the ground can be prevented by using pile foundations extending down to 15 metres (49 ft) or more. At this depth the temperature does not change with the seasons, remaining at about −5 °C (23 °F).
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Revival_of_organisms_preserved_in_permafrost">Revival of organisms preserved in permafrost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Permafrost&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Revival of organisms preserved in permafrost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2012, Russian researchers proved that permafrost can serve as a natural repository for ancient life forms by reviving of <i><a href="/wiki/Silene_stenophylla" title="Silene stenophylla">Silene stenophylla</a></i> from 30,000 year old tissue found in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_Age" title="Ice Age">Ice Age</a> squirrel burrow in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siberian" title="Siberian">Siberian</a> permafrost. This is the oldest plant tissue ever revived. The plant was fertile, producing white flowers and viable seeds. The study demonstrated that tissue can survive ice preservation for tens of thousands of years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> A 2016 outbreak of <a href="/wiki/Anthrax" title="Anthrax">anthrax</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Yamal_Peninsula" title="Yamal Peninsula">Yamal Peninsula</a> is believed to be due to thawing permafrost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup>
</p>
