<p>An <b>economic indicator</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Statistic" title="Statistic">statistic</a> about an <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economic activity</a>. Economic indicators allow analysis of economic performance and <a href="/wiki/Prediction" title="Prediction">predictions</a> of future performance. One application of economic indicators is the study of <a href="/wiki/Business_cycle" title="Business cycle">business cycles</a>. Economic indicators include various indices, earnings reports, and economic summaries: for example, the <a href="/wiki/Unemployment" title="Unemployment">unemployment</a> rate, quits rate (quit rate in U.S. English), <a href="/wiki/Housing_starts" title="Housing starts">housing starts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Consumer_price_index" title="Consumer price index">consumer price index</a> (a measure for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inflation_(economics)" title="Inflation (economics)">inflation</a>), <a href="/wiki/Consumer_leverage_ratio" title="Consumer leverage ratio">consumer leverage ratio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Industrial_production" title="Industrial production">industrial production</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bankruptcies" title="Bankruptcies">bankruptcies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadband_internet_access" title="Broadband internet access">broadband internet penetration</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retail_sales" title="Retail sales">retail sales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stock_market" title="Stock market">stock market</a> prices, and <a href="/wiki/Money_supply" title="Money supply">money supply</a> changes.
</p><p>The leading business cycle dating committee in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States of America</a> is the private <a href="/wiki/National_Bureau_of_Economic_Research" title="National Bureau of Economic Research">National Bureau of Economic Research</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Labor_Statistics" title="Bureau of Labor Statistics">Bureau of Labor Statistics</a> is the principal fact-finding agency for the U.S. government in the field of labor economics and statistics. Other producers of economic indicators includes the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a> and United States <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Economic_Analysis" title="Bureau of Economic Analysis">Bureau of Economic Analysis</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_by_timing">Classification by timing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economic_indicator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification by timing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CycleSandwich.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="447" data-file-width="657" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/CycleSandwich.png/220px-CycleSandwich.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/CycleSandwich.png/330px-CycleSandwich.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/CycleSandwich.png/440px-CycleSandwich.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CycleSandwich.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Stock" title="Stock">Equities</a> as leading, <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">GDP</a> as coincident, and business <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Credit_(finance)" title="Credit (finance)">credit</a> as lagging indicator.</div></div></div>
<p>Economic indicators can be classified into three categories according to their usual timing in relation to the business cycle: leading indicators, lagging indicators, and coincident indicators.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leading_indicators">Leading indicators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economic_indicator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Leading indicators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Leading indicators are indicators that usually, but not always, change before the economy as a whole changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> They are therefore useful as short-term <a href="/wiki/Dependent_and_independent_variables" title="Dependent and independent variables">predictors</a> of the economy. <a href="/wiki/Stock_market" title="Stock market">Stock market</a> returns are a leading indicator: the stock market usually begins to decline before the economy as a whole declines and usually begins to improve before the general economy begins to recover from a slump. Other leading indicators include the index of consumer expectations, building permits, and the money supply. <a href="/wiki/The_Conference_Board" title="The Conference Board">The Conference Board</a> publishes a composite <a href="/wiki/Conference_Board_Leading_Economic_Index" title="Conference Board Leading Economic Index">Leading Economic Index</a> consisting of ten indicators designed to predict activity in the U. S. economy six to nine months in future.
</p><p>Components of the Conference Board's Leading Economic Indicators Index
</p>
<ol><li><b>Average weekly hours (manufacturing)</b> — Adjustments to the working hours of existing employees are usually made in advance of new hires or layoffs, which is why the measure of average weekly hours is a leading indicator for changes in unemployment.</li>
<li><b>Average weekly jobless claims for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unemployment_insurance" title="Unemployment insurance">unemployment insurance</a></b> — The CB reverses the value of this component from positive to negative because a positive reading indicates a loss in jobs. The initial jobless-claims data is more sensitive to business conditions than other measures of unemployment, and as such leads the monthly unemployment data released by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Labor" title="U.S. Department of Labor">U.S. Department of Labor</a>.</li>
<li><b>Manufacturers' new orders for consumer goods/materials</b> — This component is considered a leading indicator because increases in new orders for consumer goods and materials usually mean positive changes in actual production. The new orders decrease inventory and contribute to unfilled orders, a precursor to future revenue.</li>
<li><b>Vendor performance (slower deliveries diffusion index)</b> — This component measures the time it takes to deliver orders to industrial companies. Vendor performance leads the business cycle because an increase in delivery time can indicate rising demand for manufacturing supplies. Vendor performance is measured by a monthly survey from the National Association of Purchasing Managers (NAPM). This diffusion index measures one-half of the respondents reporting no change and all respondents reporting slower deliveries.</li>
<li><b>Manufacturers' new orders for non-defense <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capital_goods" title="Capital goods">capital goods</a></b> — As stated above, new orders lead the business cycle because increases in orders usually mean positive changes in actual production and perhaps rising demand. This measure is the producer's counterpart of new orders for consumer goods/materials component (#3).</li>
<li><b>Building permits</b> for new private housing units.</li>
<li>The <b>Standard &amp; Poor's 500 stock index</b> — The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S%26P_500" title="S&amp;P 500">S&amp;P 500</a> is considered a leading indicator because changes in stock prices reflect investor's expectations for the future of the economy and interest rates. <div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EquityBDP.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="757" data-file-width="1137" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/EquityBDP.png/220px-EquityBDP.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/EquityBDP.png/330px-EquityBDP.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/EquityBDP.png/440px-EquityBDP.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EquityBDP.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Corporate equities as leading indicator with respect to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GDP" title="GDP">GDP</a>.</div></div></div></li>
<li><b>Money Supply (M2)</b> — The <a href="/wiki/Money_supply" title="Money supply">money supply</a> measures demand deposits, traveler's checks, savings deposits, currency, money market accounts, and small-denomination <a href="/wiki/Time_deposit" title="Time deposit">time deposits</a>. Here, M2 is adjusted for inflation by means of the deflator published by the federal government in the GDP report. Bank lending, a factor contributing to account deposits, usually declines when inflation increases faster than the money supply, which can make economic expansion more difficult. Thus, an increase in demand deposits will indicate expectations that inflation will rise, resulting in a decrease in bank lending and an increase in savings.</li>
<li><b>Interest rate spread</b> (10-year Treasury vs. Federal Funds target) — The <a href="/wiki/Interest_rate" title="Interest rate">interest rate</a> spread is often referred to as the yield curve and implies the expected direction of short-, medium- and long-term interest rates. Changes in the <a href="/wiki/Yield_curve" title="Yield curve">yield curve</a> have been the most accurate predictors of downturns in the economic cycle. This is particularly true when the curve becomes inverted, that is, when the longer-term returns are expected to be less than the short rates.</li>
<li><b>Index of consumer expectations</b> — This is the only component of the leading indicators that is based solely on expectations. This component leads the business cycle because consumer expectations can indicate future consumer spending or tightening. The data for this component comes from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Michigan" title="University of Michigan">University of Michigan</a>'s Survey Research Center, and is released once a month.</li></ol>
<ol><li><b>Equities</b>- This is also a leading indicator.</li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lagging_indicators">Lagging indicators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economic_indicator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lagging indicators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lagging indicators are indicators that usually change after the economy as a whole does. Typically the lag is a few quarters of a year. The unemployment rate is a lagging indicator: employment tends to increase two or three quarters after an upturn in the general economy<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.  In finance, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bollinger_bands" title="Bollinger bands">Bollinger bands</a> are one of various lagging indicators in frequent use. In a performance measuring system, profit earned by a business is a lagging indicator as it reflects a historical performance; similarly, improved customer satisfaction is the result of initiatives taken in the past.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The Index of Lagging Indicators is published monthly by <a href="/wiki/The_Conference_Board" title="The Conference Board">The Conference Board</a>, a non-governmental organization, which determines the value of the index from seven components. 
</p><p>The Index tends to <i>follow</i> changes in the overall economy.
</p><p>The components on the Conference Board's index are:
</p>
<ul><li>The average duration of <a href="/wiki/Unemployment" title="Unemployment">unemployment</a> (inverted)</li>
<li>The value of outstanding commercial and industrial <a href="/wiki/Loan" title="Loan">loans</a></li>
<li>The change in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consumer_Price_Index" title="Consumer Price Index">Consumer Price Index</a> for services</li>
<li>The change in labour cost per unit of output</li>
<li>The ratio of manufacturing and trade inventories to sales</li>
<li>The ratio of consumer credit outstanding to personal income</li>
<li>The average <a href="/wiki/Prime_rate" title="Prime rate">prime rate</a> charged by banks</li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FedfundsCAP.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="801" data-file-width="1204" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/FedfundsCAP.png/250px-FedfundsCAP.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/FedfundsCAP.png/375px-FedfundsCAP.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/FedfundsCAP.png/500px-FedfundsCAP.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FedfundsCAP.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Funds_Rate" title="Federal Funds Rate">Federal Funds Rate</a> in the USA lagging behind <a href="/wiki/Capacity_utilization" title="Capacity utilization">capacity utilization</a> in <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a>.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coincident_indicators">Coincident indicators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economic_indicator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Coincident indicators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Coincident indicators change at approximately the same time as the whole economy, thereby providing information about the current state of the economy.  There are many coincident economic indicators, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gross_Domestic_Product" title="Gross Domestic Product">Gross Domestic Product</a>, industrial production, personal income and retail sales. A coincident index may be used to identify, after the fact, the dates of peaks and troughs in the business cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are four economic statistics comprising the Index of Coincident Economic Indicators:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Number of employees on non-agricultural <a href="/wiki/Payroll" title="Payroll">payrolls</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Personal_income" title="Personal income">Personal income</a> less transfer payments</li>
<li>Industrial production</li>
<li>Manufacturing and <a href="/wiki/Trade_sale" title="Trade sale">trade sale</a></li></ul>
<p>The Philadelphia Federal Reserve produces state-level coincident indexes based on 4 state-level variables:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Nonfarm payroll employment</li>
<li>Average hours worked in manufacturing</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unemployment_rate" title="Unemployment rate">Unemployment rate</a></li>
<li>Wage and salary <a href="/wiki/Disbursement" title="Disbursement">disbursements</a> deflated by the consumer price index (U.S. city average)</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="By_direction">By direction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economic_indicator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: By direction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are also three terms that describe an economic indicator's <i>direction</i> relative to the direction of the general economy:
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Goodwin2_fredgraph.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1780" data-file-width="2680" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Goodwin2_fredgraph.png/250px-Goodwin2_fredgraph.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Goodwin2_fredgraph.png/375px-Goodwin2_fredgraph.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Goodwin2_fredgraph.png/500px-Goodwin2_fredgraph.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Goodwin2_fredgraph.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Wage_share" title="Wage share">wage share</a> (arguably) as countercyclical, but also as a lagging indicator with respect to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Employment_rate" title="Employment rate">employment rate</a> as procyclical indicator in the USA</div></div></div>
<dl><dt><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Procyclical_and_countercyclical" title="Procyclical and countercyclical">Procyclical</a> indicators</dt>
<dd>move in the same direction as the general economy: they increase when the economy is doing well; decrease when it is doing badly. <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">Gross domestic product</a> (GDP) is a procyclic indicator.</dd>
<dt><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Procyclical_and_countercyclical" title="Procyclical and countercyclical">Countercyclical</a> indicators</dt>
<dd>move in the opposite direction to the general economy. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unemployment_rate" title="Unemployment rate">unemployment rate</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Wage_share" title="Wage share">wage share</a> are countercyclic: in the short run they rise when the economy is deteriorating.</dd>
<dt><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/acyclical" title="wikt:acyclical">Acyclical</a> indicators</dt>
<dd>are those with little or no correlation to the business cycle: they may rise or fall when the general economy is doing well, and may rise or fall when it is not doing well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_indicators">Local indicators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economic_indicator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Local indicators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Local governments often need to project future tax revenues.  The city of San Francisco, for example, uses the price of a one-bedroom apartment on <a href="/wiki/Craigslist" title="Craigslist">Craigslist</a>, weekend subway ridership numbers, parking garage usage, and monthly reports on passenger landings at the city's airport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
