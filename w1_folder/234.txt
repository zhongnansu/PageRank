<p><b>Reduction potential</b> (also known as <b>redox potential</b>, <b>oxidation / reduction potential</b>, <b>ORP</b>, <b>pE</b>, <b>ε</b>, or <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\displaystyle E_{h}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
<msub>
<mi>E</mi>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mi>h</mi>
</mrow>
</msub>
</mstyle>
</mrow>
<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle E_{h}}</annotation>
</semantics>
</math></span><img alt="E_{{h}}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa200246fd00460ad123dd7140dc00eb537d8cc0" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:2.894ex; height:2.509ex;"/></span>) is a measure of the tendency of a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_species" title="Chemical species">chemical species</a> to acquire <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> and thereby be <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduced</a>. Reduction potential is measured in <a href="/wiki/Volt" title="Volt">volts</a> (V), or millivolts (mV).  Each species has its own intrinsic reduction potential; the more positive the potential, the greater the species' affinity for electrons and tendency to be reduced.  ORP is a common measurement for water quality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-suslow_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-suslow-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement_and_interpretation">Measurement and interpretation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reduction_potential&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Measurement and interpretation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Aqueous_solution" title="Aqueous solution">aqueous solutions</a>,   reduction potential is a measure of the tendency of the solution to either gain or lose electrons when it is subjected to change by introduction of a new species. A solution with a higher (more positive) reduction potential than the new species will have a tendency to gain electrons from the new species (i.e. to be reduced by oxidizing the new species) and a solution with a lower (more negative) reduction potential will have a tendency to lose electrons to the new species (i.e. to be oxidized by reducing the new species). 
Because the <a href="/wiki/Absolute_electrode_potential" title="Absolute electrode potential">absolute potentials</a> are difficult to accurately measure, reduction potentials are defined relative to a reference electrode.  Reduction potentials of aqueous solutions are determined by measuring the potential difference between an inert sensing electrode in contact with the solution and a stable reference electrode connected to the solution by a <a href="/wiki/Salt_bridge" title="Salt bridge">salt bridge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Environmental_Chemistry_(vanLoon)_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Environmental_Chemistry_(vanLoon)-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The sensing electrode acts as a platform for electron transfer to or from the reference <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Half_cell" title="Half cell">half cell</a>. It is typically <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a>, although <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> and <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a> can be used as well. The reference half cell consists of a redox standard of known potential.  The <a href="/wiki/Standard_hydrogen_electrode" title="Standard hydrogen electrode">standard hydrogen electrode</a> (SHE) is the reference from which all standard redox potentials are determined and has been assigned an arbitrary half cell potential of 0.0 mV. However, it is fragile and impractical for routine laboratory use.  Therefore, other more stable reference electrodes such as <a href="/wiki/Silver_chloride_electrode" title="Silver chloride electrode">silver chloride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saturated_calomel_electrode" title="Saturated calomel electrode">saturated calomel</a> (SCE) are commonly used because of their more reliable performance.
</p><p>Although measurement of the reduction potential in aqueous solutions is relatively straightforward, many factors limit its interpretation, such as effects of solution temperature and pH, <a href="/wiki/Reversible_reaction" title="Reversible reaction">irreversible reactions</a>, slow electrode kinetics, non-equilibrium, presence of multiple redox couples, electrode poisoning, small exchange currents and inert redox couples. Consequently, practical measurements seldom correlate with calculated values. Nevertheless, reduction potential measurement has proven useful as an analytical tool in monitoring changes in a system rather than determining their absolute value (e.g. process control and <a href="/wiki/Titration" title="Titration">titrations</a>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Explanation">Explanation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reduction_potential&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Explanation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Just as the transfer of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> ions between chemical species determines the <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> of an aqueous solution, the transfer of electrons between chemical species determines the reduction potential of an aqueous solution. Like pH, the reduction potential represents how strongly electrons are transferred to or from species in solution. It does not characterize the amount of electrons available for oxidation or reduction, in much the same way that pH does not characterize the buffering capacity.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (February 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In fact, it is possible to define pE, the negative logarithm of electron concentration (-log[e]) in a solution, which will be directly proportional to the redox potential.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Environmental_Chemistry_(vanLoon)_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Environmental_Chemistry_(vanLoon)-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Sometimes pE is used as a unit of reduction potential instead of Eh, for example in environmental chemistry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Environmental_Chemistry_(vanLoon)_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Environmental_Chemistry_(vanLoon)-2">[2]</a></sup> If we normalize pE of hydrogen to zero, we will have the relation pE=16.9 Eh at room temperature. This point of view is useful for understanding redox potential, although the transfer of electrons, rather than the absolute concentration of free electrons in thermal equilibrium, is how one usually thinks of redox potential. Theoretically, however, the two approaches are equivalent.
</p><p>Conversely, one could define a potential corresponding to pH as a potential difference between a solute and pH neutral water, separated by porous membrane (that is permeable to hydrogen ions). Such potential differences actually do occur from differences in acidity on biological membranes. This potential (where pH neutral water is set to 0 V) is analogous with redox potential (where standardized hydrogen solution is set to 0 V), but instead of hydrogen ions, electrons are transferred across in the redox case. Both pH and redox potentials are properties of solutions, not of elements or chemical compounds per se, and depend on concentrations, temperature etc.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Half_cells">Half cells</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reduction_potential&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Half cells">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The relative <a href="/wiki/Reactivity_(chemistry)" title="Reactivity (chemistry)">reactivities</a> of different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Half_cell" title="Half cell">half cells</a> can be compared to predict the direction of electron flow. A higher <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\displaystyle E_{0}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

</p><p>Sometimes when <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> is carried out in an <a href="/wiki/Aqueous_solution" title="Aqueous solution">aqueous solution</a>, water, rather than the solute, is oxidized or reduced. It is the reduction potential of each species present that will determine which species will be oxidized or reduced.
</p><p>Absolute reduction potentials can be determined if we find the actual potential between electrode and electrolyte for any one reaction. Surface polarization interferes with measurements, but various sources give an estimated potential for the standard hydrogen electrode of 4.4 V to 4.6 V (the electrolyte being positive.)
</p><p>Half-cell equations can be combined if one is reversed to an oxidation in a manner that cancels out the electrons to obtain an equation without electrons in it.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_chemistry">Environmental chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reduction_potential&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Environmental chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Pourbaix_diagram" title="Pourbaix diagram">Pourbaix diagram</a></div>
<p>In the field of environmental chemistry, the reduction potential is used to determine if oxidizing or reducing conditions are prevalent in water or soil, and to <a href="/wiki/Pourbaix_diagram" title="Pourbaix diagram">predict the states of different chemical species in the water</a>, such as dissolved metals. pE values in water range from -12 to 25; the levels where the water itself becomes reduced or oxidized, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Environmental_Chemistry_(vanLoon)_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Environmental_Chemistry_(vanLoon)-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The reduction potentials in natural systems often lie comparatively near one of the boundaries of the stability region of water. Aerated surface water, rivers, lakes, oceans, rainwater and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acid_mine_water" title="Acid mine water">acid mine water</a>, usually have oxidizing conditions (positive potentials). In places with limitations in air supply, such as submerged soils, swamps and marine sediments, reducing conditions (negative potentials) are the norm. Intermediate values are rare and usually a temporary condition found in systems moving to higher or lower pE values.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Environmental_Chemistry_(vanLoon)_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Environmental_Chemistry_(vanLoon)-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In environmental situations, it is common to have complex non-equilibrium conditions between a large number of species, meaning that it is often not possible to make accurate and precise measurements of the reduction potential. However, it is usually possible to obtain an approximate value and define the conditions as being in the oxidizing or reducing regime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Environmental_Chemistry_(vanLoon)_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Environmental_Chemistry_(vanLoon)-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the soil there are two main redox constituents: 1) anorganic redox systems (mainly ox/red compounds of Fe and Mn) and measurement in water extracts; 2) natural soil samples with all microbial and root components and measurement by direct method [Husson O. et al.: Practical improvements in soil redox potential (Eh) measurement for characterisation of soil properties. Application for comparison of conventional and conservation agriculture cropping systems. Anal. Chim. Acta 906 (2016): 98-109].
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_quality">Water quality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reduction_potential&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Water quality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Oxidation reduction potential (ORP) can be used for water system monitoring with the benefit of a single-value measure of the disinfection potential, showing the activity of the disinfectant rather than the applied dose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-suslow_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-suslow-1">[1]</a></sup>  For example, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E._coli" title="E. coli">E. coli</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Salmonella" title="Salmonella">Salmonella</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Listeria" title="Listeria">Listeria</a></i> and other pathogens have survival times of under 30 s when the ORP is above 665 mV, compared against &gt;300 s when it is below 485 mV.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-suslow_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-suslow-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>A study was conducted comparing traditional parts per million chlorination reading and ORP in Hennepin County, Minnesota. The results of this study argue for the inclusion of ORP above 650mV in local health codes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reduction_potential&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Pourbaix_diagram" title="Pourbaix diagram">Pourbaix diagram</a></div>
<p>Eh-pH (Pourbaix) diagrams are commonly used in mining and geology for assessment of the stability fields of minerals and dissolved species.  Under conditions where a mineral (solid) phase is the most stable form of an element, these diagrams show that mineral.  As with results from all thermodynamic (equilibrium) evaluations, these diagrams should be used with caution.  Although the formation of a mineral or its dissolution may be predicted to occur under a set of conditions, the process may be negligible because its rate is so slow.  Under those circumstances, kinetic evaluations are necessary.  However, the equilibrium conditions can be used to evaluate the direction of spontaneous changes and the magnitude of the driving force behind them.
</p>
