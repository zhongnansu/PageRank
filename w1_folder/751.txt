<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Netherlands fallacy</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>Netherlands fallacy</b> refers to an error  <a href="/wiki/Paul_R._Ehrlich" title="Paul R. Ehrlich">Paul R. Ehrlich</a> and his co-authors claim others make in assuming that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_impact" title="Environmental impact">environmental impacts</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> and other rich nations are contained within their national borders.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecologist" title="Ecologist">Ecologists</a> since the late 20th century have analyzed the ecological sink status and sink capacities of poor nations. As <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_pollution" title="Industrial pollution">polluting industries</a> migrate from rich to poor nations, the national <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a> of rich nations shrinks, whereas the international ecological footprint may increase or also decrease. The nature of the <a href="/wiki/Fallacy" title="Fallacy">fallacy</a> is to ignore increasing environmental damage in many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_nations" title="Developing nations">developing nations</a> and in <a href="/wiki/International_waters" title="International waters">international waters</a> attributable to the imported goods or changes in the economy of such nations directly due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developed_nations" title="Developed nations">developed nations</a>.
</p><p>Such an approach may lead to incorrect assertions such as the environmental impact of a particular developed country is reducing, when a holistic, international approach suggests the opposite.  This may in turn support over-optimistic predictions toward the improvement of global environmental conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEhrlichEhrlich1990[httpswebarchiveorgweb20060706131457httpwww2thinkorgtpeshtml_39],_269_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEhrlichEhrlich1990[httpswebarchiveorgweb20060706131457httpwww2thinkorgtpeshtml_39],_269-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
