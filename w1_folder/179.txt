<p><b>Carbonic acid </b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formula</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">H</a><sub>2</sub><a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">CO<sub>3</sub></a><sub></sub><sub></sub><sub></sub><sub></sub><sub></sub><sub></sub><sub></sub><sub></sub><sub></sub><sub></sub><sub></sub><sub></sub><sub></sub><sub></sub><sub></sub><sub></sub><sub></sub><sub></sub><sub></sub> (equivalently OC(OH)<sub>2</sub>). It is also a name sometimes given to <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solutions</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" title="Water (molecule)">water</a> (<a href="/wiki/Carbonated_water" title="Carbonated water">carbonated water</a>), because such solutions contain small amounts of H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>. In <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiology</a>, carbonic acid is described as <i>volatile acid</i> or <i>respiratory acid</i>, because it is the only acid excreted as a gas by the lungs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It plays an important role in the <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate_buffer_system" title="Bicarbonate buffer system">bicarbonate buffer system</a> to maintain <a href="/wiki/Acid%E2%80%93base_homeostasis" title="Acid–base homeostasis">acid–base homeostasis</a>.
</p><p>Carbonic acid, which is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weak_acid" title="Weak acid">weak acid</a>, forms two kinds of <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonates</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonates</a>. In geology, carbonic acid causes <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> to dissolve, producing <a href="/wiki/Calcium_bicarbonate" title="Calcium bicarbonate">calcium bicarbonate</a>, which leads to many limestone features such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stalactites" title="Stalactites">stalactites</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stalagmites" title="Stalagmites">stalagmites</a>.
</p><p>It was long believed that carbonic acid could not exist as a pure compound. However, in 1991 it was reported that <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> scientists had succeeded in making solid H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> samples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moore_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moore-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Hydrate" title="Hydrate">hydration</a> <a href="/wiki/Equilibrium_constant" title="Equilibrium constant">equilibrium constant</a> at 25 °C is called K<sub>h</sub>, which in the case of carbonic acid is [H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>]/[CO<sub>2</sub>] ≈ 1.7×10<sup>−3</sup> in pure water<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HS_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-HS-5">[5]</a></sup> and ≈ 1.2×10<sup>−3</sup> in <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SB_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-SB-6">[6]</a></sup> Hence, the majority of the carbon dioxide is not converted into carbonic acid, remaining as CO<sub>2</sub> molecules. In the absence of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a>, the equilibrium is reached quite slowly. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rate_constant" title="Rate constant">rate constants</a> are 0.039 s<sup>−1</sup> for the forward reaction (CO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O → H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>) and 23 s<sup>−1</sup> for the reverse reaction (H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> → CO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O). The addition of two molecules of water to CO<sub>2</sub> would give <i><a href="/wiki/Orthocarbonic_acid" title="Orthocarbonic acid">orthocarbonic acid</a></i>, C(OH)<sub>4</sub>, which exists only in minute amounts in aqueous solution.
</p><p>Addition of base to an excess of carbonic acid gives <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> (hydrogen carbonate).  With excess base, carbonic acid reacts to give <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonate</a> salts.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_of_carbonic_acid_in_blood">Role of carbonic acid in blood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Role of carbonic acid in blood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bicarbonate is an intermediate in the transport of CO<sub>2</sub> out of the body by <a href="/wiki/Gas_exchange" title="Gas exchange">respiratory gas exchange</a>. The hydration reaction of CO<sub>2</sub> is generally very slow in the absence of a catalyst, but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_blood_cells" title="Red blood cells">red blood cells</a> contain <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_anhydrase" title="Carbonic anhydrase">carbonic anhydrase</a>, which increases the reaction rate, producing <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> (HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>) dissolved in the <a href="/wiki/Blood_plasma" title="Blood plasma">blood plasma</a>. This catalysed reaction is reversed in the lungs, where it converts the bicarbonate back into CO<sub>2</sub> and allows it to be expelled.  This equilibration plays an important role as a <a href="/wiki/Buffer_solution" title="Buffer solution">buffer</a> in mammalian blood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> A 2016 theoretical report suggests that carbonic acid may play a pivotal role in protonating various nitrogen bases in blood serum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_of_carbonic_acid_in_ocean_chemistry"><span id="Ocean"></span> Role of carbonic acid in ocean chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Role of carbonic acid in ocean chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The oceans of the world have absorbed almost half of the CO<sub>2</sub> emitted by humans from the burning of fossil fuels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sabine_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-sabine-9">[9]</a></sup>  It has been estimated that the extra dissolved carbon dioxide has caused the ocean's average surface pH to shift by about −0.1 unit from pre-industrial levels. This is known as <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a>, even though the ocean remains <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basic_(chemistry)" title="Basic (chemistry)">basic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acidity_of_carbonic_acid">Acidity of carbonic acid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Acidity of carbonic acid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Care must be taken when quoting and using the first dissociation constant of carbonic acid. In aqueous solution, carbonic acid exists in equilibrium with carbon dioxide, and the concentration of H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> is much lower than the concentration of CO<sub>2</sub>. In many analyses, H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> includes dissolved CO<sub>2</sub> (referred to as CO<sub>2</sub>(aq)), H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>* is used to represent the two species when writing the aqueous chemical equilibrium equation. The equation may be rewritten as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Green_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Green-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>* ⇌ HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup> + H<sup>+</sup></dd>
<dd><i>K</i><sub>a</sub>(app) = 4.47×10<sup>−7</sup>; p<i>K</i>(app) = 6.35 at 25 °C and <a href="/wiki/Ionic_strength" title="Ionic strength">ionic strength</a> = 0.0.</dd></dl>
<p>Whereas this apparent p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> is quoted as the dissociation constant of carbonic acid, it is ambiguous: it might better be referred to as the acidity constant of dissolved carbon dioxide, as it is particularly useful for calculating the <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> of CO<sub>2</sub>-containing solutions.  A similar situation applies to <a href="/wiki/Sulfurous_acid" title="Sulfurous acid">sulfurous acid</a> (H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>3</sub>), which exists in equilibrium with substantial amounts of unhydrated <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a>.
</p><p>The second constant is for the dissociation of the <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> ion into the <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonate</a> ion CO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup>:

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pure_carbonic_acid">Pure carbonic acid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pure carbonic acid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It was long believed that carbonic acid could not exist as a pure compound. However, in 1991 scientists at NASA's <a href="/wiki/Goddard_Space_Flight_Center" title="Goddard Space Flight Center">Goddard Space Flight Center</a> (USA) succeeded in making solid H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> samples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moore_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Moore-3">[3]</a></sup> They did so by exposing a frozen mixture of water and carbon dioxide to high-energy proton radiation, and then warming to remove the excess water. The carbonic acid that remained was characterized by infrared spectroscopy. The fact that the carbonic acid was prepared by irradiating a solid H<sub>2</sub>O + CO<sub>2</sub> mixture, or even by irradiation of <a href="/wiki/Dry_ice" title="Dry ice">dry ice</a> alone, has given rise to suggestions that H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> might be found in outer space or on <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>, where frozen ices of H<sub>2</sub>O and CO<sub>2</sub> are found, as well as cosmic rays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-on_the_surprising_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-on_the_surprising-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-''alpha''-carbonic_acid_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-''alpha''-carbonic_acid-12">[12]</a></sup> It was announced in 1993 that solid carbonic acid had been created by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogenic" title="Cryogenic">cryogenic</a> reaction of <a href="/wiki/Potassium_bicarbonate" title="Potassium bicarbonate">potassium bicarbonate</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HCl" title="HCl">HCl</a> dissolved in <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SynthesisByProtonation_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-SynthesisByProtonation-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Later work showed that in fact the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methyl_ester" title="Methyl ester">methyl ester</a> had been formed, but other methods were successful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-''alpha''-carbonic_acid_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-''alpha''-carbonic_acid-12">[12]</a></sup> Theoretical calculations showed that a single molecule of water can catalyze the decomposition of a gas-phase carbonic acid molecule to carbon dioxide and water. In the absence of water, the dissociation of gaseous carbonic acid has been predicted to be very slow, with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 180,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-on_the_surprising_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-on_the_surprising-11">[11]</a></sup> This only applies if the molecules are few and far between, because it has also been predicted that gas-phase carbonic acid will catalyze its own decomposition by forming dimers, which then break apart into two molecules each of water and carbon dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>For a while it was thought that there were two <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorphs</a> of solid carbonic acid, called α and β. The polymorph denoted β-carbonic acid was prepared by heating alternating layers of glassy aqueous solutions of bicarbonate and acid in vacuum, which causes protonation of the bicarbonate, followed by removal of the solvent. The previously suggested α-carbonic acid, which was prepared by the same technique using methanol rather than water as a solvent, was later shown to be a monomethyl ester CH<sub>3</sub>OCOOH.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-''alpha''-carbonic_acid_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-''alpha''-carbonic_acid-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
