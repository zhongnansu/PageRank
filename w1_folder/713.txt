<p>The <b>World Wide Fund for Nature</b> (<b>WWF</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Internationalism_(politics)" title="Internationalism (politics)">international</a> <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organization" title="Non-governmental organization">non-governmental organization</a> founded in 1961, working in the field of the wilderness preservation, and the reduction of <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">human impact on the environment</a>. It was formerly named the <b>World Wildlife Fund</b>, which remains its official name in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Living_Planet_Report" title="Living Planet Report">Living Planet Report</a> is published every two years by WWF since 1998; it is based on a <a href="/wiki/Living_Planet_Index" title="Living Planet Index">Living Planet Index</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a> calculation. 
</p><p>It is the world's largest <a href="/wiki/Environmental_organization" title="Environmental organization">conservation organization</a> with over five million supporters  worldwide, working in more than 100 countries, supporting around 1,300<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> conservation and environmental projects.  They have invested over $1 billion in more than 12,000 conservation initiatives since 1995.  <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WorldWildLife_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-WorldWildLife-5">[5]</a></sup> WWF is a foundation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  with 55% of funding from individuals and bequests, 19% from government sources (such as the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a>, <a href="/wiki/Department_for_International_Development" title="Department for International Development">DFID</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Agency_for_International_Development" title="United States Agency for International Development">USAID</a>) and 8% from corporations in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWF-INT_Annual_Review_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-WWF-INT_Annual_Review-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The group aims to "stop the degradation of the planet’s natural environment and to build a future in which humans live in harmony with nature."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quickfacts_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-quickfacts-8">[8]</a></sup> Currently, their work is organized around these six areas: food, climate, freshwater, wildlife, forests, and oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WorldWildLife_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-WorldWildLife-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Conservation_Foundation">The Conservation Foundation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The Conservation Foundation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>The Conservation Foundation</b>, a precursor to WWF, was founded in 1948 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_Fairfield_Osborn,_Jr." title="Henry Fairfield Osborn, Jr.">Fairfield Osborn</a> as an affiliate of the New York Zoological Society (today known as the <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_Conservation_Society" title="Wildlife Conservation Society">Wildlife Conservation Society</a>) with an aim of protecting the world's natural resources. The advisory council included leading scientists such as <a href="/wiki/Charles_Sutherland_Elton" title="Charles Sutherland Elton">Charles Sutherland Elton</a>, <a href="/wiki/G._Evelyn_Hutchinson" title="G. Evelyn Hutchinson">G. Evelyn Hutchinson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aldo_Leopold" title="Aldo Leopold">Aldo Leopold</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carl_Sauer" title="Carl Sauer">Carl Sauer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Sears" title="Paul Sears">Paul Sears</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It supported much of the scientific work cited by <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Carson" title="Rachel Carson">Rachel Carson</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Silent_Spring" title="Silent Spring">Silent Spring</a></i>, including that of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=John_L._George_(biologist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="John L. George (biologist) (page does not exist)">John L. George</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_Hale&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Roger Hale (page does not exist)">Roger Hale</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Rudd&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Robert Rudd (page does not exist)">Robert Rudd</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Woodwell" title="George Woodwell">George Woodwell</a>. 
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Prince_Bernhard_of_Lippe-Biesterfeld" title="Prince Bernhard of Lippe-Biesterfeld">Prince Bernhard of Lippe-Biesterfeld</a> helped found the World Wildlife Fund, becoming its first President in 1961, and in 1970 established the WWF's <a href="/wiki/Financial_endowment" title="Financial endowment">financial endowment</a> "<a href="/wiki/The_1001:_A_Nature_Trust" title="The 1001: A Nature Trust">The 1001: A Nature Trust</a>". He resigned his post after being involved in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lockheed_Bribery_Scandal" title="Lockheed Bribery Scandal">Lockheed Bribery Scandal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1963, the Foundation held a conference and published a major report warning of <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a> <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, written by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Noel_Eichhorn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Noel Eichhorn (page does not exist)">Noel Eichhorn</a> based on the work of <a href="/wiki/Frank_Fraser_Darling" title="Frank Fraser Darling">Frank Fraser Darling</a> (then foundation vice president), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edward_Deevey" title="Edward Deevey">Edward Deevey</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Erik_Eriksson_(scientist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Erik Eriksson (scientist) (page does not exist)">Erik Eriksson</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_Keeling" title="Charles Keeling">Charles Keeling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Plass" title="Gilbert Plass">Gilbert Plass</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lionel_Walford&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lionel Walford (page does not exist)">Lionel Walford</a>, and <a href="/wiki/William_Garnett_(photographer)" title="William Garnett (photographer)">William Garnett</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1990, the Conservation Foundation was merged into WWF, after becoming an affiliate of WWF in 1985, when it became a distinct legal entity but with the same staff and board. The organization now known as the Conservation Foundation in the United States is the former <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Conservation_Foundation_(Illinois)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Conservation Foundation (Illinois) (page does not exist)">Forest Foundation of DuPage County</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wwfhistory_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-wwfhistory-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Morges_Manifesto">Morges Manifesto</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Morges Manifesto">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The idea for a fund on behalf of endangered animals was officially proposed by <a href="/wiki/Victor_Stolan" title="Victor Stolan">Victor Stolan</a> to <a href="/wiki/Julian_Huxley" title="Julian Huxley">Sir Julian Huxley</a> in response to articles he published in the British newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">The Observer</a></i>. This proposal led Huxley to put Stolan in contact with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Max_Nicholson" title="Max Nicholson">Max Nicholson</a>, a person who had had thirty years experience of linking <a href="/wiki/Progressivism" title="Progressivism">progressive</a> intellectuals with <a href="/wiki/Big_business" title="Big business">big business</a> interests through the <a href="/wiki/Political_and_Economic_Planning" title="Political and Economic Planning">Political and Economic Planning</a> <a href="/wiki/Think_tank" title="Think tank">think tank</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWF_history_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-WWF_history-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kellaway_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kellaway-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Nicholson thought up the name of the organization. WWF was conceived on 29 April 1961, under the name of <i>World Wildlife Fund</i>, and its first office was opened on 11 September that same year in <a href="/wiki/Morges" title="Morges">Morges</a>, Switzerland. WWF was conceived to act as a funding institution for existing conservation groups such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Union_for_the_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for the Conservation of Nature">International Union for the Conservation of Nature and Natural Resources</a> and The Conservation Foundation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wwfhistory_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-wwfhistory-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Godfrey_A._Rockefeller" title="Godfrey A. Rockefeller">Godfrey A. Rockefeller</a> also played an important role in its creation, assembling the first staff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWF_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-WWF-2">[2]</a></sup> Its establishment was marked with the signing of the "Morges Manifesto", the founding document that sets out the fund's commitment to assisting worthy organizations struggling to save the world's wildlife:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_history">Later history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Later history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>WWF has set up offices and operations around the world. It originally worked by fundraising and providing grants to existing non-governmental organizations, based on the best-available scientific knowledge and with an initial focus on the protection of <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered species</a>. As more resources became available, its operations expanded into other areas such as the preservation of <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">biological diversity</a>, sustainable use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_resources" title="Natural resources">natural resources</a>, the reduction of <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>. The organization also began to run its own conservation projects and campaigns, and by the 1980s started to take a more strategic approach to its conservation activities.
</p><p>In 1986, the organization changed its name to <i>World Wide Fund for Nature</i>, while retaining the WWF initials. However, it continued at that time to operate under the original name in the United States and Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quickfacts_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-quickfacts-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>That year was the 25th anniversary of WWF's foundation, an event marked by a gathering in Assisi, Italy to which the organization's International President HRH Prince Philip, the Duke of Edinburgh, invited religious authorities representing Buddhism, Christianity, Hinduism, Islam and Judaism. These leaders produced The Assisi Declarations, theological statements showing the spiritual relationship between their followers and nature that triggered a growth in the engagement of those religions with conservation around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1990s, WWF revised its mission statement to:
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Stop the degradation of the planet's natural environment and to build a future in which humans live in harmony with nature, by:
</p><ul><li>conserving the world's biological diversity;</li>
<li>ensuring that the use of renewable natural resources is sustainable; [and]</li>
<li>promoting the reduction of pollution and wasteful consumption.</li></ul>
</blockquote>
<p>WWF scientists and many others identified 238 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecoregions" title="Ecoregions">ecoregions</a> that represent the world's most biologically outstanding <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">terrestrial</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceans" title="Oceans">marine</a> habitats, based on a worldwide biodiversity analysis which the organization says was the first of its kind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In the early 2000s (decade), its work was focused on a subset of these ecoregions, in the areas of forest, freshwater and marine <a href="/wiki/Habitat_conservation" title="Habitat conservation">habitat conservation</a>, endangered species conservation, climate change, and the elimination of the most toxic chemicals.
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>"We shan't save all we should like to, but we shall save a great deal more than if we had never tried." – Sir <a href="/wiki/Peter_Scott" title="Peter Scott">Peter Scott</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWF_Finland_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-WWF_Finland-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>In 1996, the organization obtained general <a href="/wiki/Consultative_status" title="Consultative status">consultative status</a> from <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Panda_symbol">Panda symbol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Panda symbol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Grosser_Panda.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4272" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Grosser_Panda.JPG/220px-Grosser_Panda.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Grosser_Panda.JPG/330px-Grosser_Panda.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Grosser_Panda.JPG/440px-Grosser_Panda.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Grosser_Panda.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The giant panda has become the symbol of WWF.</div></div></div>
<p>WWF's <a href="/wiki/Giant_panda" title="Giant panda">giant panda</a> logo originated from a panda named <a href="/wiki/Chi_Chi_(giant_panda)" title="Chi Chi (giant panda)">Chi Chi</a> that had been transferred from <a href="/wiki/Beijing_Zoo" title="Beijing Zoo">Beijing Zoo</a> to <a href="/wiki/London_Zoo" title="London Zoo">London Zoo</a> in 1958, three years before WWF became established.  Being famous as the only panda residing in the Western world at that time, its uniquely recognisable physical features and status as an endangered species were seen as ideal to serve the organization's need for a strong recognisable symbol that would overcome all language barriers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The organization also needed an animal that would have an impact in black and white printing. The logo was then designed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peter_Scott_(conservationist)" title="Peter Scott (conservationist)">Sir Peter Scott</a> from preliminary sketches by Gerald Watterson, a Scottish naturalist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The logo was slightly simplified and made more geometric in 1978, and was revised significantly again in 1986, at the time that the organization changed its name, with the new version featuring solid black shapes for eyes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In 2000 a change was made to the font used for the initials "WWF" in the logo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>WWF's strategy for achieving its mission specifically concentrates on restoring populations of 36 <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> (species or species groups that are important for their <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> or to people, including <a href="/wiki/Elephant" title="Elephant">elephants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tuna" title="Tuna">tunas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cetacea" title="Cetacea">whales, dolphins and porpoises</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a> in 6 areas (carbon emissions, cropland, grazing land, fishing, forestry and water).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The organization also works on a number of <a href="/wiki/Global_issue" title="Global issue">global issues</a> driving biodiversity loss and unsustainable use of natural resources, including finance, business practices, laws, and consumption choices. Local offices also work on national or regional issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>WWF works with a large number of different groups to achieve its goals, including other NGOs, governments, business, investment banks, scientists, fishermen, farmers and local communities. It also undertakes public campaigns to influence decision makers, and seeks to educate people on how to live in a more environmentally friendly manner.It urges people to donate funds to protect the environment. The donors can also choose to receive gifts in return.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publications">Publications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>WWF publishes the <i><a href="/wiki/Living_Planet_Index" title="Living Planet Index">Living Planet Index</a></i> in collaboration with the <a href="/wiki/Zoological_Society_of_London" title="Zoological Society of London">Zoological Society of London</a>. Along with <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a> calculations, the <i>Index</i> is used to produce a bi-yearly <i><a href="/wiki/Living_Planet_Report" title="Living Planet Report">Living Planet Report</a></i> giving an overview of the impact of human activity on the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>The organization also regularly publishes reports, fact sheets and other documents on issues related to its work, to raise awareness and provide information to policy and decision makers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Policy-making">Policy-making</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Policy-making">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Policies of the WWF are made by board members elected for three-year terms. An Executive Team guides and develops WWF's strategy. There is also a National Council which stands as an advisory group to the board and a team of scientists and experts in conservation who research for WWF.
</p><p>National and international law plays an important role in determining how habitats and resources are managed and used. Laws and regulations become one of the organization’s global priorities.
</p><p>The WWF has been opposed to the extraction of oil from the Canadian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tar_sands" title="Tar sands">tar sands</a> and has campaigned on this matter. Between 2008 and 2010 the WWF worked with <a href="/wiki/The_Co-operative_Group" title="The Co-operative Group">The Co-operative Group</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK" title="UK">UK</a>'s largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consumer_co-operative" title="Consumer co-operative">consumer co-operative</a> to publish reports which concluded that: (1) exploiting the Canadian tar sands to their full potential would be sufficient to bring about what they described as 'runaway climate change;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> (2) carbon capture and storage (CCS) technology cannot be used to reduce the release of carbon dioxide into the atmosphere to a level comparable to that of other methods of oil extraction;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> (3) the $379 billion which is expected to be spent extracting oil from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tar_sands" title="Tar sands">tar sands</a> could be better spent on research and development in renewable energy technology;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> and (4) the expansion of tar sands extraction poses a serious threat to the caribou in Alberta .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>The organization convinces and helps governments and other political bodies to adopt, enforce, strengthen and/or change policies, guidelines and laws that affect biodiversity and natural resource use. It also ensures government consent and/or keeps their commitment to international instruments relating to the protection of biodiversity and natural resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2012, David Nussbaum, Chief Executive of WWF-UK, spoke out against the way <a href="/wiki/Shale_gas" title="Shale gas">shale gas</a> is used in the UK, saying: "...the Government must reaffirm its commitment to tackling climate change and prioritise renewables and energy efficiency."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_partnerships">Corporate partnerships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Corporate partnerships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>WWF has been accused by the campaigner <a href="/wiki/Corporate_Watch" title="Corporate Watch">Corporate Watch</a> of being too close to business to campaign objectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> WWF claims partnering with corporations such as <a href="/wiki/Coca-Cola" title="Coca-Cola">Coca-Cola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lafarge_(company)" title="Lafarge (company)">Lafarge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Slim" title="Carlos Slim">Carlos Slim</a>'s and <a href="/wiki/IKEA" title="IKEA">IKEA</a> will reduce their effect on the environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>  WWF received €56 million (US$80 million) from corporations in 2010 (an 8% increase in support from corporations compared to 2009), accounting for 11% of total revenue for the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWF-INT_Annual_Review_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-WWF-INT_Annual_Review-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Project_.22Lock.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id='Project_"Lock"'>Project "Lock"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title='Edit section: Project "Lock"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1988, Prince Bernhard, previously WWF's first President, sold paintings for <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">GBP</a> 700,000 to raise money for the World Wildlife Fund. The money was deposited in a Swiss WWF bank account. In 1989, Charles de Haes, then WWF Director-General, transferred GBP 500,000 back to Bernhard for what he (de Haes) called a "private project". It was then revealed, in 1991, that Prince Bernhard had used the money to hire KAS International, owned by <a href="/wiki/Special_Air_Service" title="Special Air Service">SAS</a> founder <a href="/wiki/David_Stirling" title="David Stirling">David Stirling</a>, for an operation called Project "Lock" during which <a href="/wiki/Mercenary" title="Mercenary">mercenaries</a> (mostly British) fought <a href="/wiki/Poaching" title="Poaching">poachers</a> in nature reserves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mekong_River_dolphins_report">Mekong River dolphins report</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Mekong River dolphins report">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In June 2009, Touch Seang Tana, chairman of <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>'s Commission for Conservation and Development of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mekong_River" title="Mekong River">Mekong River</a> Dolphins Eco-tourism Zone, argued that the WWF had misrepresented the danger of extinction of the <a href="/wiki/Irrawaddy_dolphin" title="Irrawaddy dolphin">Mekong dolphin</a> to boost fundraising.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The report stated that the deaths were caused by a bacterial disease that became fatal due to environmental contaminants suppressing the dolphins' immune systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> He called the report unscientific and harmful to the Cambodian government and threatened the WWF's Cambodian branch with suspension unless they met with him to discuss his claims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Touch Seang Tana later said he would not press charges of supplying false information and would not make any attempt to prevent WWF from continuing its work in Cambodia, but advised WWF to adequately explain its findings and check with the commission before publishing another report. After this, in January 2012, Touch Seang Tana signed the "Kratie Declaration on the Conservation of the Mekong River Irrawaddy Dolphin" along with WWF and the Cambodian Fisheries Administration, an agreement binding the parties to work together on a "roadmap" addressing dolphin conservation in the Mekong River.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pandaleaks">Pandaleaks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Pandaleaks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2012, German investigative journalist Wilfried Huissmann published a book called "The Silence of the Pandas". It became a bestseller in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, but was banned from Britain until 2014, when it was released under the title of "Pandaleaks", after a series of injunctions and court orders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theguardian.com_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-theguardian.com-43">[43]</a></sup> The book criticizes WWF for its supposed involvement with corporations that are responsible for large-scale destruction of the environment, such as <a href="/wiki/Coca-Cola" title="Coca-Cola">Coca-Cola</a>, and gives details into the existence of the secret <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1001_Club" title="1001 Club">1001 Club</a>, whose members, Huismann claims, continue to have an unhealthy influence on WWF's policy making.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theguardian.com_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-theguardian.com-43">[43]</a></sup> However, WWF has sought to deny the allegations made against it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="ARD_documentary">ARD documentary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: ARD documentary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The German public television <a href="/wiki/ARD_(broadcaster)" title="ARD (broadcaster)">ARD</a> aired a documentary on 22 June 2011 that claimed to show how the WWF cooperates with corporations such as <a href="/wiki/Monsanto" title="Monsanto">Monsanto</a>, providing sustainability certification in exchange for donations<span class="nowrap"> </span>– essentially <a href="/wiki/Greenwashing" title="Greenwashing">greenwashing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> WWF has denied the allegations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> By encouraging high-impact eco-tourism, the program alleges that WWF contributes to the destruction of habitat and species it claims to protect. WWF-India is not active at the tiger reserve given as the example,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> but it is active elsewhere seeking to limit adverse tourism effects and better sharing of tourism benefits to local communities. The program also alleges WWF certified a palm oil plantation operated by <a href="/wiki/Wilmar_International" title="Wilmar International">Wilmar International</a>, a Singaporean company, on the Indonesian island of <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">Borneo</a>, even though the establishment of the plantation led to the destruction of over 14,000 hectares of rainforest. Only 80 hectares were ultimately conserved, the ARD documentary claims. According to the programme, two orangutans live on the conserved land, but have very slim chances of survival because no fruit trees remain and the habitat is too small to sustain them. To survive, they steal palm nuts from the neighbouring plantation, thereby risking being shot by plantation workers. WWF notes that the plantation filmed is PT Rimba Harapan Sakti, which has not been certified as a sustainable producer by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Round_Table_on_Sustainable_Palm_Oil" title="Round Table on Sustainable Palm Oil">Round Table on Sustainable Palm Oil</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hunting">Hunting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Hunting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The President of Honor of WWF in Spain used to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_Juan_Carlos_I" title="King Juan Carlos I">King Juan Carlos I</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> who has been a known hunting enthusiast. In 1962, when he was 24, he was invited by the German Baron Werner <a href="/wiki/House_of_Alvensleben" title="House of Alvensleben">von Alvensleben</a> to a hunt in <a href="/wiki/Mozambique" title="Mozambique">Mozambique</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Since then, the king has taken part in hunting forays in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a>. In October 2004, he was a member of a hunt in <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> that killed a <a href="/wiki/Wolf" title="Wolf">wolf</a> and nine <a href="/wiki/Brown_bear" title="Brown bear">brown bears</a>, including one that was pregnant, according to the Romanian newspaper <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romania_Libera" title="Romania Libera">Romania Libera</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> He was also accused by a Russian official of killing a bear called Mitrofan, supposedly after giving vodka to the animal, in an episode that sparked controversy in Spain, although the claim was never proven.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> In the same year, according to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>, the Polish government allowed him to kill a <a href="/wiki/European_bison" title="European bison">European bison</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bia%C5%82owie%C5%BCa_Forest" title="Białowieża Forest">Białowieża Forest</a>, even though it is an <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered species</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Further controversy arose in April 2012 when the king's participation in an elephant hunt in <a href="/wiki/Botswana" title="Botswana">Botswana</a> was discovered only after he returned to Spain on an emergency flight after tripping over a step and fracturing his hip.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Many Spanish environmental groups and <a href="/wiki/Left-wing_politics" title="Left-wing politics">leftist</a> parties criticized the monarch's hobby,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> and the WWF stripped him of the honorary position in July 2012, in an extraordinary assembly by 94% of the votes of the members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Charles" title="Prince Charles">Prince Charles</a>, the UK head of the WWF,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> has stated that he enjoys hunting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> He is believed, however, to adhere to legal British traditional hunting and speaks out against hunting endangered species.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_1001:_A_Nature_Trust">The 1001: A Nature Trust</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: The 1001: A Nature Trust">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_1001:_A_Nature_Trust" title="The 1001: A Nature Trust">The 1001: A Nature Trust</a></div>
<p>In the early 1970s, Prince Bernhard, <a href="/wiki/Prince_Philip,_Duke_of_Edinburgh" title="Prince Philip, Duke of Edinburgh">Prince Philip, Duke of Edinburgh</a> and a few associates set up <a href="/wiki/The_1001:_A_Nature_Trust" title="The 1001: A Nature Trust">The 1001: A Nature Trust</a> to handle the WWF's administration and fund-raising. 1001 members each contributed $10,000 to the trust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WWF_Finland_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-WWF_Finland-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Initialism_dispute">Initialism dispute</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Initialism dispute">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/WWE#Legal_disputes_and_controversies" title="WWE">WWE § Legal disputes and controversies</a></div>
<p>In 2000, the World Wide Fund for Nature sued the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Wrestling_Federation" title="World Wrestling Federation">World Wrestling Federation</a> (now named <a href="/wiki/WWE" title="WWE">WWE</a>) for unfair trade practices. Both parties had shared the initials "WWF" since 1979. The conservation organization claimed that the <a href="/wiki/Professional_wrestling" title="Professional wrestling">professional wrestling</a> company had violated a 1994 agreement regarding international use of the WWF initials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 10 August 2001, a UK court ruled in favour of the World Wide Fund for Nature. The World Wrestling Federation filed an appeal in October 2001. However, on May 10, 2002, the World Wrestling Federation changed its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Web_address" title="Web address">Web address</a> from <i>WWF.com</i> to <i>WWE.com</i>, and replaced every "WWF" reference on the existing site with "WWE", as a prelude to changing the company's name to "World Wrestling Entertainment." Its stock ticker also switched from WWF to WWE.
</p><p>The wrestling organization's abandonment of the "WWF" <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Initialism" title="Initialism">initialism</a> did not end the two organizations' legal conflict. Later in 2002, the World Wide Fund for Nature petitioned the court for $360 million in damages, but was not successful. A subsequent request to overturn by the World Wide Fund for Nature was dismissed by the British <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Court_of_Appeal_of_England_and_Wales" title="Court of Appeal of England and Wales">Court of Appeal</a> on June 28, 2007. In 2003, World Wrestling Entertainment won a limited decision which permitted them to continue marketing certain pre-existing products with the abandoned WWF logo. However, WWE was mandated to issue newly branded merchandise such as apparel, action figures, video games, and <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVDs</a> with the "WWE" initials. Additionally, the court order required the company to remove both auditory and visual references to "WWF" in its library of video footage outside the United Kingdom.
</p><p>Starting with the 1,000th episode of <i>Raw</i> in July 2012, the WWF "scratch" logo is no longer censored in archival footage. In addition, the WWF initials are no longer censored when spoken or when written in plain text in archival footage.  In exchange, WWE is no longer permitted to use the WWF initials or logo in any new, original footage, packaging, or advertising, with any old-school logos for retro-themed programming now using a modification of the original WWF logo without the F.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_music">In music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: In music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/No_One%27s_Gonna_Change_Our_World" title="No One's Gonna Change Our World">No One's Gonna Change Our World</a></i> was a charity album released in 1969, for the benefit of the WWF.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Peter_Rose_and_Anne_Conlon" title="Peter Rose and Anne Conlon">Peter Rose and Anne Conlon</a> are music theatre writers, well known for their environmental musicals for children, who were commissioned by WWF-UK to write several environmental musicals as part of an education plan. Some were narrated by <a href="/wiki/David_Attenborough" title="David Attenborough">David Attenborough</a>, and broadcast on television in numerous countries.
</p><p>The British pop group <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S_Club" title="S Club">S Club 7</a> were ambassadors for WWF-UK during their time together as a band (1999-2003). Each of the members sponsored an endangered animal, and in 2000, traveled to the various locations around the world of their chosen animals for a seven-part <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> documentary series entitled <i><a href="/wiki/S_Club_7_Go_Wild!" title="S Club 7 Go Wild!">S Club 7 Go Wild</a></i>.
</p><p><i>Environmentally Sound: A Select Anthology of Songs Inspired by the Earth</i> is a benefit album released in 2006, for WWF-Philippines, featuring artists that included <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Up_Dharma_Down" title="Up Dharma Down">Up Dharma Down</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_Sago_Project" title="Radioactive Sago Project">Radioactive Sago Project</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kala_(band)" title="Kala (band)">Kala</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cynthia_Alexander" title="Cynthia Alexander">Cynthia Alexander</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Joey_Ayala" title="Joey Ayala">Joey Ayala</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2012, WWF launched an online <a href="/wiki/Music_download" title="Music download">music download</a> store with <a href="/wiki/Fairsharemusic" title="Fairsharemusic">fairsharemusic</a> from which 50% of the profit goes to the charity.
</p><p>In April 2015, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hailey_Gardiner" title="Hailey Gardiner">Hailey Gardiner</a> released her solo EP, titled The Woods. In honor of <a href="/wiki/Earth_Day" title="Earth Day">Earth Day</a>, 15% of the proceeds made towards the purchase of the EP would be donated to the WWF.
</p>
