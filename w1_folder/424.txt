<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">C<sub>3</sub> carbon fixation</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Calvin-cycle4.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="766" data-file-width="836" height="275" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Calvin-cycle4.svg/300px-Calvin-cycle4.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Calvin-cycle4.svg/450px-Calvin-cycle4.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Calvin-cycle4.svg/600px-Calvin-cycle4.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Calvin-cycle4.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Calvin–Benson cycle</div></div></div>
<p><b>C<sub>3</sub> carbon fixation</b> is one of three <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_pathway" title="Metabolic pathway">metabolic pathways</a> for <a href="/wiki/Carbon_fixation" title="Carbon fixation">carbon fixation</a> in <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/C4_carbon_fixation" title="C4 carbon fixation">C<sub>4</sub></a> and <a href="/wiki/Crassulacean_acid_metabolism" title="Crassulacean acid metabolism">CAM</a>. This process converts <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ribulose_bisphosphate" title="Ribulose bisphosphate">ribulose bisphosphate</a> (RuBP, a 5-carbon sugar) into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3-phosphoglycerate" title="3-phosphoglycerate">3-phosphoglycerate</a> through the following reaction:
</p>
<dl><dd><b>CO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O + RuBP → (2) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3-phosphoglycerate" title="3-phosphoglycerate">3-phosphoglycerate</a></b></dd></dl>
<p>This reaction occurs in all plants as the first step of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calvin%E2%80%93Benson_cycle" title="Calvin–Benson cycle">Calvin–Benson cycle</a>. In <a href="/wiki/C4_carbon_fixation" title="C4 carbon fixation">C<sub>4</sub></a> plants, carbon dioxide is drawn out of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malate" title="Malate">malate</a> and into this reaction rather than directly from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:382px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cross_section_of_Arabidopsis_thaliana,_a_C3_plant..jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="946" data-file-width="1423" height="253" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Cross_section_of_Arabidopsis_thaliana%2C_a_C3_plant..jpg/380px-Cross_section_of_Arabidopsis_thaliana%2C_a_C3_plant..jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Cross_section_of_Arabidopsis_thaliana%2C_a_C3_plant..jpg/570px-Cross_section_of_Arabidopsis_thaliana%2C_a_C3_plant..jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Cross_section_of_Arabidopsis_thaliana%2C_a_C3_plant..jpg/760px-Cross_section_of_Arabidopsis_thaliana%2C_a_C3_plant..jpg 2x" width="380"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cross_section_of_Arabidopsis_thaliana,_a_C3_plant..jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cross section of a C3 plant, specifically of an <i>Arabidopsis thaliana</i> leaf. Vascular bundles shown. Drawing based on microscopic images courtesy of Cambridge University Plant Sciences Department.</div></div></div>
<p>Plants that survive solely on C<sub>3</sub> fixation (<b>C<sub>3</sub> plants</b>) tend to thrive in areas where sunlight intensity is moderate, temperatures are moderate, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> concentrations are around 200 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">ppm</a> or higher,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> is plentiful. The C<sub>3</sub> plants, originating during <a href="/wiki/Mesozoic" title="Mesozoic">Mesozoic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paleozoic" title="Paleozoic">Paleozoic</a> eras, predate the <a href="/wiki/C4_carbon_fixation" title="C4 carbon fixation">C<sub>4</sub></a> plants and still represent approximately 95% of Earth's plant biomass. C<sub>3</sub> plants lose 97% of the water taken up through their roots to transpiration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raven2001_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Raven2001-2">[2]</a></sup>  Examples include rice and barley.
</p><p>C<sub>3</sub> plants cannot grow in very hot areas because <a href="/wiki/RuBisCO" title="RuBisCO">RuBisCO</a> incorporates more oxygen into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RuBP" title="RuBP">RuBP</a> as temperatures increase. This leads to <a href="/wiki/Photorespiration" title="Photorespiration">photorespiration</a> (also known as the oxidative photosynthetic carbon cycle, or C2 photosynthesis), which leads to a net loss of carbon and nitrogen from the plant and can therefore limit growth. In dry areas, C<sub>3</sub> plants shut their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stomata" title="Stomata">stomata</a> to reduce water loss, but this stops CO<sub>2</sub> from entering the leaves and therefore reduces the concentration of CO<sub>2</sub> in the leaves. This lowers the CO<sub>2</sub>:O<sub>2</sub> ratio and therefore also increases photorespiration.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> C<sub>4</sub> and <a href="/wiki/Crassulacean_acid_metabolism" title="Crassulacean acid metabolism">CAM</a> plants have adaptations that allow them to survive in hot and dry areas, and they can therefore out-compete C<sub>3</sub> plants in these areas.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Isotopic_signature" title="Isotopic signature">isotopic signature</a> of C<sub>3</sub> plants shows higher degree of <sup>13</sup>C depletion than the C<sub>4</sub> plants, due to variation in <a href="/wiki/Fractionation_of_carbon_isotopes_in_oxygenic_photosynthesis" title="Fractionation of carbon isotopes in oxygenic photosynthesis">fractionation of carbon isotopes in oxygenic photosynthesis</a> across plant types.
</p>
