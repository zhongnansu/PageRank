<p><b>Calcium carbonate</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">Ca</a><a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">CO<sub>3</sub></a>. It is a common substance found in <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rocks</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a> <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">aragonite</a> (most notably as <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>, which is a type of sedimentary rock consisting mainly of calcite) and is the main component of <a href="/wiki/Pearl" title="Pearl">pearls</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Seashell" title="Seashell">shells of marine organisms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snail" title="Snail">snails</a>, and eggs. Calcium carbonate is the active ingredient in <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_lime" title="Agricultural lime">agricultural lime</a> and is created when calcium ions in <a href="/wiki/Hard_water" title="Hard water">hard water</a> react with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonate_ion" title="Carbonate ion">carbonate ions</a> to create <a href="/wiki/Limescale" title="Limescale">limescale</a>. It is medicinally used as a <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> supplement or as an <a href="/wiki/Antacid" title="Antacid">antacid</a>, but excessive consumption can be hazardous.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Calcium carbonate shares the typical properties of other carbonates. Notably,
</p>
<ul><li>it reacts with <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a>, releasing <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>:</li></ul>
<dl><dd>CaCO<sub>3</sub>(s) + 2H<sup>+</sup>(aq) → Ca<sup>2+</sup>(aq) + CO<sub>2</sub>(g) + H<sub>2</sub>O (l)</dd></dl>
<ul><li>it releases carbon dioxide upon heating, called a <a href="/wiki/Thermal_decomposition" title="Thermal decomposition">thermal decomposition</a> reaction, or <a href="/wiki/Calcination" title="Calcination">calcination</a> (to above 840 °C in the case of CaCO<sub>3</sub>), to form <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">calcium oxide</a>, commonly called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quicklime" title="Quicklime">quicklime</a>, with reaction <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy" title="Enthalpy">enthalpy</a> 178 kJ/mole:</li></ul>
<dl><dd>CaCO<sub>3</sub> (s) → CaO (s) + CO<sub>2</sub> (g)</dd></dl>
<p>Calcium carbonate will react with water that is saturated with carbon dioxide to form the soluble <a href="/wiki/Calcium_bicarbonate" title="Calcium bicarbonate">calcium bicarbonate</a>.
</p>
<dl><dd>CaCO<sub>3</sub> + CO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O → Ca(HCO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>This reaction is important in the <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carbonate_rock" title="Carbonate rock">carbonate rock</a>, forming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cavern" title="Cavern">caverns</a>, and leads to <a href="/wiki/Hard_water" title="Hard water">hard water</a> in many regions.
</p><p>An unusual form of calcium carbonate is the hexahydrate, <a href="/wiki/Ikaite" title="Ikaite">ikaite</a>, CaCO<sub>3</sub>·6H<sub>2</sub>O. Ikaite is stable only below 6 °C.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The vast majority of calcium carbonate used in industry is extracted by mining or quarrying. Pure calcium carbonate (e.g. for food or pharmaceutical use), can be produced from a pure quarried source (usually <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a>).
</p><p>Alternatively, calcium carbonate is prepared from <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">calcium oxide</a>. Water is added to give <a href="/wiki/Calcium_hydroxide" title="Calcium hydroxide">calcium hydroxide</a> then <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> is passed through this solution to precipitate the desired calcium carbonate, referred to in the industry as precipitated calcium carbonate (PCC):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PCC_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-PCC-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The thermodynamically stable form of CaCO<sub>3</sub> under normal conditions is hexagonal β-CaCO<sub>3</sub>, (the mineral <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ropp_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ropp-8">[8]</a></sup> Other forms can be prepared, the denser,(2.83 g/cc) orthorhombic λ-CaCO<sub>3</sub> ( the mineral <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">aragonite</a>) and μ-CaCO<sub>3</sub>, occurring as the mineral <a href="/wiki/Vaterite" title="Vaterite">vaterite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ropp_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ropp-8">[8]</a></sup> The aragonite form can be prepared by precipitation at temperatures above 85 °C, the vaterite form can be prepared by precipitation at 60 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ropp_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ropp-8">[8]</a></sup> Calcite contains calcium atoms coordinated by 6 oxygen atoms, in aragonite they are coordinated by 9 oxygen atoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ropp_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ropp-8">[8]</a></sup> The vaterite structure is not fully understood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DemichelisRaiteri2013_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-DemichelisRaiteri2013-9">[9]</a></sup> Magnesium carbonate MgCO<sub>3</sub> has the calcite structure, whereas strontium and barium carbonate (SrCO<sub>3</sub> and BaCO<sub>3</sub>) adopt the aragonite structure, reflecting their larger ionic radii.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ropp_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ropp-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geological_sources">Geological sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Geological sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">Calcite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">aragonite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vaterite" title="Vaterite">vaterite</a> are pure calcium carbonate minerals. Industrially important source rocks which are predominantly calcium carbonate include <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a> and <a href="/wiki/Travertine" title="Travertine">travertine</a>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_sources">Biological sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Biological sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Eggshells, snail shells and most seashells are predominantly calcium carbonate and can be used as industrial sources of that chemical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Oyster shells have enjoyed recent recognition as a source of dietary calcium, but are also a practical industrial source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Dark green vegetables such as broccoli and kale contain dietarily significant amounts of calcium carbonate, however, they are not practical as an industrial source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraterrestrial">Extraterrestrial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Extraterrestrial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Beyond Earth, strong evidence suggests the presence of calcium carbonate on <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>. Signs of calcium carbonate have been detected at more than one location (notably at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gusev_crater" title="Gusev crater">Gusev</a> and <a href="/wiki/Huygens_(crater)" title="Huygens (crater)">Huygens</a> craters). This provides some evidence for the past presence of liquid water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clark2007_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Clark2007-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carbonate is found frequently in geologic settings and constitutes an enormous <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon reservoir</a>. Calcium carbonate occurs as <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">aragonite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dolomite" title="Dolomite">dolomite</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonate_mineral" title="Carbonate mineral">carbonate minerals</a> form the rock types: <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a>, <a href="/wiki/Travertine" title="Travertine">travertine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tufa" title="Tufa">tufa</a>, and others.
</p><p>In warm, clear tropical waters <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a> are more abundant than towards the poles where the waters are cold. Calcium carbonate contributors, including <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Coccolith" title="Coccolith">coccoliths</a> and planktic <a href="/wiki/Foraminifera" title="Foraminifera">foraminifera</a>), <a href="/wiki/Coralline_algae" title="Coralline algae">coralline algae</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_sponge" title="Sea sponge">sponges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brachiopod" title="Brachiopod">brachiopods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Echinoderm" title="Echinoderm">echinoderms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bryozoa" title="Bryozoa">bryozoa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mollusc_shell" title="Mollusc shell">mollusks</a>, are typically found in shallow water environments where sunlight and filterable food are more abundant. Cold-water carbonates do exist at higher latitudes but have a very slow growth rate. The <a href="/wiki/Calcification" title="Calcification">calcification</a> processes are changed by <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a>.
</p><p>Where the <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_crust" title="Oceanic crust">oceanic crust</a> is <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subducted</a> under a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_plate" title="Continental plate">continental plate</a> sediments will be carried down to warmer zones in the <a href="/wiki/Asthenosphere" title="Asthenosphere">asthenosphere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>. Under these conditions calcium carbonate decomposes to produce <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> which, along with other gases, give rise to explosive <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic eruptions</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbonate_compensation_depth">Carbonate compensation depth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Carbonate compensation depth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Carbonate_compensation_depth" title="Carbonate compensation depth">carbonate compensation depth</a> (CCD) is the point in the ocean where the rate of precipitation of calcium carbonate is balanced by the rate of dissolution due to the conditions present. Deep in the ocean, the temperature drops and pressure increases. Calcium carbonate is unusual in that its solubility increases with decreasing temperature. Increasing pressure also increases the solubility of calcium carbonate. The carbonate compensation depth can range from 4–6 km below sea level.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_taphonomy">Role in taphonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Role in taphonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Calcium carbonate can <a href="/wiki/Taphonomy" title="Taphonomy">preserve fossils</a> through <a href="/wiki/Permineralization" title="Permineralization">permineralization</a>. Most of the vertebrate fossils of the <a href="/wiki/Two_Medicine_Formation" title="Two Medicine Formation">Two Medicine Formation</a>—a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geologic_formation" title="Geologic formation">geologic formation</a> known for its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duck-billed_dinosaur" title="Duck-billed dinosaur">duck-billed dinosaur</a> eggs—are preserved by CaCO<sub>3</sub> permineralization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twoturn_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-twoturn-16">[16]</a></sup> This type of preservation conserves high levels of detail, even down to the microscopic level. However, it also leaves specimens vulnerable to <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a> when exposed to the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twoturn_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-twoturn-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Trilobite" title="Trilobite">Trilobite</a> populations were once thought to have composed the majority of aquatic life during the <a href="/wiki/Cambrian" title="Cambrian">Cambrian</a>, due to the fact that their calcium carbonate-rich shells were more easily preserved than those of other species,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> which had purely chitinous shells.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_applications">Industrial applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Industrial applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The main use of calcium carbonate is in the construction industry, either as a building material or limestone aggregate for road building or as an ingredient of cement or as the starting material for the preparation of builder's lime by burning in a kiln. However, because of weathering mainly caused by <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> calcium carbonate (in limestone form) is no longer used for building purposes on its own, but only as a raw/primary substance for building materials.
</p><p>Calcium carbonate is also used in the purification of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> from <a href="/wiki/Iron_ore" title="Iron ore">iron ore</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnace</a>. The carbonate is calcined <i>in situ</i> to give calcium oxide, which forms a slag with various impurities present, and separates from the purified iron.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_industry" title="Oil industry">oil industry</a>, calcium carbonate is added to <a href="/wiki/Drilling_fluid" title="Drilling fluid">drilling fluids</a> as a formation-bridging and filtercake-sealing agent; it is also a weighting material which increases the density of drilling fluids to control the downhole pressure. Calcium carbonate is added to swimming pools, as a <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> corrector for maintaining <a href="/wiki/Alkalinity" title="Alkalinity">alkalinity</a> and offsetting the acidic properties of the disinfectant agent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is also used as a raw material in the refining of sugar from <a href="/wiki/Sugar_beet" title="Sugar beet">sugar beet</a>; It is calcined in a kiln with anthracite to produce calcium oxide and carbon dioxide. This burnt lime is then slaked in sweet water to produce a calcium hydroxide suspension for the precipitation of impurities in raw juice during <a href="/wiki/Carbonatation" title="Carbonatation">carbonatation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Calcium carbonate has traditionally been a major component of blackboard chalk. However, modern manufactured chalk is mostly <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a>, hydrated <a href="/wiki/Calcium_sulfate" title="Calcium sulfate">calcium sulfate</a> CaSO<sub>4</sub>·2H<sub>2</sub>O. Calcium carbonate is a main source for growing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seacrete" title="Seacrete">Seacrete</a>. Precipitated calcium carbonate (PCC), pre-dispersed in slurry form, is a common filler material for latex gloves with the aim of achieving maximum saving in material and production costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-precaco3_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-precaco3-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fine ground calcium carbonate (GCC) is an essential ingredient in the microporous film used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diapers" title="Diapers">diapers</a> and some building films as the pores are nucleated around the calcium carbonate particles during the manufacture of the film by biaxial stretching. GCC or PCC is used as a filler in paper because they are cheaper than wood fiber. In terms of market volume, GCC are the most important types of fillers currently used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Printing and writing paper can contain 10–20% calcium carbonate. In North America, calcium carbonate has begun to replace <a href="/wiki/Kaolinite" title="Kaolinite">kaolin</a> in the production of glossy paper. Europe has been practicing this as alkaline <a href="/wiki/Papermaking" title="Papermaking">papermaking</a> or acid-free papermaking for some decades. PCC used for paper filling and paper coatings is precipitated and prepared in a variety of shapes and sizes having characteristic narrow particle size distributions and equivalent spherical diameters of 0.4 to 3 micrometres.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Calcium carbonate is widely used as an extender in paints,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reade_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-reade-24">[24]</a></sup> in particular matte emulsion paint where typically 30% by weight of the paint is either chalk or marble. It is also a popular filler in plastics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reade_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-reade-24">[24]</a></sup> Some typical examples include around 15 to 20% loading of chalk in <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">unplasticized polyvinyl chloride</a> (uPVC) drain pipe, 5 to 15% loading of stearate coated chalk or marble in uPVC window profile. <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">PVC</a> cables can use calcium carbonate at loadings of up to 70 phr (parts per hundred parts of resin) to improve mechanical properties (tensile strength and elongation) and electrical properties (volume resistivity).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Polypropylene" title="Polypropylene">Polypropylene</a> compounds are often filled with calcium carbonate to increase rigidity, a requirement that becomes important at high use temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Imerys_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Imerys-25">[25]</a></sup> Here the percentage is often 20–40%. It also routinely used as a filler in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermosetting_plastic" title="Thermosetting plastic">thermosetting resins</a> (sheet and bulk molding compounds)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Imerys_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Imerys-25">[25]</a></sup> and has also been mixed with <a href="/wiki/Acrylonitrile_butadiene_styrene" title="Acrylonitrile butadiene styrene">ABS</a>, and other ingredients, to form some types of compression molded "clay" poker chips.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Precipitated calcium carbonate, made by dropping <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">calcium oxide</a> into water, is used by itself or with additives as a white paint, known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whitewashing" title="Whitewashing">whitewashing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JimohEtAl2017_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-JimohEtAl2017-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Calcium carbonate is added to a wide range of trade and <a href="/wiki/Do_it_yourself" title="Do it yourself">do it yourself</a> adhesives, sealants, and decorating fillers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reade_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-reade-24">[24]</a></sup> Ceramic tile adhesives typically contain 70 to 80% limestone. Decorating crack fillers contain similar levels of marble or dolomite. It is also mixed with putty in setting <a href="/wiki/Stained_glass" title="Stained glass">stained glass</a> windows, and as a resist to prevent glass from sticking to kiln shelves when firing glazes and paints at high temperature.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceramics_(art)" title="Ceramics (art)">ceramics</a>/glazing applications, calcium carbonate is known as <i>whiting</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reade_24-3"><a href="#cite_note-reade-24">[24]</a></sup> and is a common ingredient for many glazes in its white powdered form. When a glaze containing this material is fired in a kiln, the whiting acts as a <a href="/wiki/Ceramic_flux" title="Ceramic flux">flux</a> material in the glaze. Ground calcium carbonate is an <a href="/wiki/Abrasive" title="Abrasive">abrasive</a> (both as scouring powder and as an ingredient of household scouring creams), in particular in its calcite form, which has the relatively low hardness level of 3 on the <a href="/wiki/Mohs_scale_of_mineral_hardness" title="Mohs scale of mineral hardness">Mohs scale of mineral hardness</a>, and will therefore not scratch <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> and most other <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vitreous_enamel" title="Vitreous enamel">enamel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>, and have a moderate effect on softer metals like <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>. A paste made from calcium carbonate and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deionized_water" title="Deionized water">deionized water</a> can be used to clean <a href="/wiki/Tarnish" title="Tarnish">tarnish</a> on <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Make_it_Shine_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Make_it_Shine-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_and_dietary_applications">Health and dietary applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Health and dietary applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:500_mg_calcium_supplements_with_vitamin_D.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2122" data-file-width="3185" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/500_mg_calcium_supplements_with_vitamin_D.jpg/220px-500_mg_calcium_supplements_with_vitamin_D.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/500_mg_calcium_supplements_with_vitamin_D.jpg/330px-500_mg_calcium_supplements_with_vitamin_D.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/500_mg_calcium_supplements_with_vitamin_D.jpg/440px-500_mg_calcium_supplements_with_vitamin_D.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:500_mg_calcium_supplements_with_vitamin_D.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>500-milligram calcium supplements made from calcium carbonate</div></div></div>
<p>Calcium carbonate is widely used medicinally as an inexpensive dietary calcium supplement for <a href="/wiki/Antacid" title="Antacid">gastric antacid</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-medline_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-medline-30">[30]</a></sup> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Tums" title="Tums">Tums</a>). It may be used as a <a href="/wiki/Phosphate_binder" title="Phosphate binder">phosphate binder</a> for the treatment of <a href="/wiki/Hyperphosphatemia" title="Hyperphosphatemia">hyperphosphatemia</a> (primarily in patients with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chronic_renal_failure" title="Chronic renal failure">chronic renal failure</a>). It is also used in the pharmaceutical industry as an inert <a href="/wiki/Excipient" title="Excipient">filler</a> for <a href="/wiki/Tablet_(pharmacy)" title="Tablet (pharmacy)">tablets</a> and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceuticals" title="Pharmaceuticals">pharmaceuticals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Calcium carbonate is used in the production of calcium oxide as well as toothpaste and has seen a resurgence as a food preservative and color retainer, when used in or with products such as organic apples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>Excess calcium from supplements, fortified food and high-calcium diets, can cause <a href="/wiki/Milk-alkali_syndrome" title="Milk-alkali syndrome">milk-alkali syndrome</a>, which has serious toxicity and can be fatal. In 1915, Bertram Sippy introduced the "Sippy regimen" of hourly ingestion of milk and cream, and the gradual addition of eggs and cooked cereal, for 10 days, combined with alkaline powders, which provided symptomatic relief for peptic ulcer disease. Over the next several decades, the Sippy regimen resulted in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renal_failure" title="Renal failure">renal failure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alkalosis" title="Alkalosis">alkalosis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hypercalcaemia" title="Hypercalcaemia">hypercalcaemia</a>, mostly in men with peptic ulcer disease. These adverse effects were reversed when the regimen stopped, but it was fatal in some patients with protracted vomiting. Milk-alkali syndrome declined in men after effective treatments for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peptic_ulcer" title="Peptic ulcer">peptic ulcer</a> disease arose. During the past 15 years, it has been reported in women taking calcium supplements above the recommended range of 1.2 to 1.5 g daily, for prevention and treatment of osteoporosis, and is exacerbated by <a href="/wiki/Dehydration" title="Dehydration">dehydration</a>. Calcium has been added to over-the-counter products, which contributes to inadvertent excessive intake. Excessive calcium intake can lead to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypercalcemia" title="Hypercalcemia">hypercalcemia</a>, complications of which include vomiting, abdominal pain and altered mental status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>As a <a href="/wiki/Food_additive" title="Food additive">food additive</a> it is designated E170,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> and it has an INS number of 170. Used as an acidity regulator, anticaking agent, stabiliser or colour it is approved for usage in the EU,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> USA<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> It is used in some <a href="/wiki/Soy_milk" title="Soy milk">soy milk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Almond_milk" title="Almond milk">almond milk</a> products as a source of dietary calcium; one study suggests that calcium carbonate might be as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioavailable" title="Bioavailable">bioavailable</a> as the calcium in cow's milk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Calcium carbonate is also used as a <a href="/wiki/Firming_agent" title="Firming agent">firming agent</a> in many canned or bottled vegetable products.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agricultural_use">Agricultural use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Agricultural use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Agricultural_lime" title="Agricultural lime">Agricultural lime</a>, powdered chalk or limestone, is used as a cheap method for neutralising <a href="/wiki/Soil_pH" title="Soil pH">acidic soil</a>, making it suitable for planting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oates2008_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oates2008-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Household_use">Household use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Household use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Calcium carbonate is a key ingredient in many household cleaning powders like <a href="/wiki/Comet_(cleanser)" title="Comet (cleanser)">Comet (cleanser)</a> and is used as a scrubbing agent.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_applications">Environmental applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Environmental applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1989, a researcher, Ken Simmons, introduced CaCO<sub>3</sub> into the Whetstone Brook in <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> His hope was that the calcium carbonate would counter the acid in the stream from acid rain and save the trout that had ceased to spawn. Although his experiment was a success, it did increase the amount of aluminium ions in the area of the brook that was not treated with the limestone. This shows that CaCO<sub>3</sub> can be added to neutralize the effects of acid rain in <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a> ecosystems. Currently calcium carbonate is used to neutralize acidic conditions in both soil and water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-env_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-env-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Since the 1970s, such <i>liming</i> has been practiced on a large scale in Sweden to mitigate acidification and several thousand lakes and streams are limed repeatedly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>Calcium carbonate is also used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flue_gas_desulfurisation" title="Flue gas desulfurisation">flue gas desulfurisation</a> applications eliminating harmful SO<sub>2</sub> and NO<sub>2</sub> emissions from coal and other fossil fuels burnt in large fossil fuel power stations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-env_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-env-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Calcination_equilibrium">Calcination equilibrium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Calcination equilibrium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Calcination" title="Calcination">Calcination</a> of <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> using <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a> fires to produce <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">quicklime</a> has been practiced since antiquity by cultures all over the world. The temperature at which limestone yields calcium oxide is usually given as 825 °C, but stating an absolute threshold is misleading. Calcium carbonate exists in equilibrium with calcium oxide and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> at any temperature. At each temperature there is a <a href="/wiki/Partial_pressure" title="Partial pressure">partial pressure</a> of carbon dioxide that is in equilibrium with calcium carbonate. At room temperature the equilibrium overwhelmingly favors calcium carbonate, because the equilibrium CO<sub>2</sub> pressure is only a tiny fraction of the partial CO<sub>2</sub> pressure in air, which is about 0.035 kPa.
</p><p>At temperatures above 550 °C the equilibrium CO<sub>2</sub> pressure begins to exceed the CO<sub>2</sub> pressure in air. So above 550 °C, calcium carbonate begins to outgas CO<sub>2</sub> into air. However, in a charcoal fired kiln, the concentration of CO<sub>2</sub> will be much higher than it is in air. Indeed, if all the <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> in the kiln is consumed in the fire, then the partial pressure of CO<sub>2</sub> in the kiln can be as high as 20 kPa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solvaypcc2007_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-solvaypcc2007-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>
The table shows that this partial pressure is not achieved until the temperature is nearly 800 °C. For the outgassing of CO<sub>2</sub> from calcium carbonate to happen at an economically useful rate, the equilibrium pressure must significantly exceed the ambient pressure of CO<sub>2</sub>. And for it to happen rapidly, the equilibrium pressure must exceed total atmospheric pressure of 101 kPa, which happens at 898 °C.</p><div style="clear:right;"></div>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solubility">Solubility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Solubility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The effect of the latter is especially evident in day-to-day life of people who have hard water. Water in aquifers underground can be exposed to levels of CO<sub>2</sub> much higher than atmospheric. As such water percolates through calcium carbonate rock, the CaCO<sub>3</sub> dissolves according to the second trend. When that same water then emerges from the tap, in time it comes into equilibrium with CO<sub>2</sub> levels in the air by outgassing its excess CO<sub>2</sub>. The calcium carbonate becomes less soluble as a result and the excess precipitates as lime scale. This same process is responsible for the formation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stalactites" title="Stalactites">stalactites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stalagmite" title="Stalagmite">stalagmites</a> in limestone caves.

</p>
<h3><span id="With_varying_pH.2C_temperature_and_salinity:_CaCO3_scaling_in_swimming_pools"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="With_varying_pH,_temperature_and_salinity:_CaCO3_scaling_in_swimming_pools">With varying pH, temperature and salinity: CaCO<sub>3</sub> scaling in swimming pools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: With varying pH, temperature and salinity: CaCO3 scaling in swimming pools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CaCO3-pH.gif"><img alt="Effects of salinity and pH on the maximum calcium ion level before scaling is anticipated at 25 C and 1 mM bicarbonate (e.g. in swimming pools)" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="287" data-file-width="460" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/CaCO3-pH.gif/220px-CaCO3-pH.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/CaCO3-pH.gif/330px-CaCO3-pH.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/CaCO3-pH.gif/440px-CaCO3-pH.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CaCO3-pH.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CaCO3-Temp.gif"><img alt="Effects of temperature and bicarbonate concentration on the maximum calcium ion level before scaling is anticipated at pH 7 and 5,000 ppm salinity (e.g. in swimming pools)" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="296" data-file-width="466" height="140" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/CaCO3-Temp.gif/220px-CaCO3-Temp.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/CaCO3-Temp.gif/330px-CaCO3-Temp.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/CaCO3-Temp.gif/440px-CaCO3-Temp.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CaCO3-Temp.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div>
<p>In contrast to the open equilibrium scenario above, many swimming pools are managed by addition of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_bicarbonate" title="Sodium bicarbonate">sodium bicarbonate</a> (NaHCO<sub>3</sub>) to about 2 mM as a buffer, then control of pH through use of HCl, NaHSO<sub>4</sub>, Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>, NaOH or chlorine formulations that are acidic or basic. In this situation, dissolved inorganic carbon (<a href="/wiki/Total_inorganic_carbon" title="Total inorganic carbon">total inorganic carbon</a>) is far from equilibrium with atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub>. Progress towards equilibrium through outgassing of CO<sub>2</sub> is slowed by (i) the slow reaction <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub></a> ⇌ CO<sub>2</sub>(aq) + H<sub>2</sub>O;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> (ii) limited aeration in a deep water column and (iii) periodic replenishment of bicarbonate to maintain buffer capacity (often estimated through measurement of <a href="/wiki/Alkalinity" title="Alkalinity">‘total alkalinity’</a>).
</p><p>In this situation, the dissociation constants for the much faster reactions H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> ⇌ H<sup>+</sup> + HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>‾</sup> ⇌ 2 H<sup>+</sup> + CO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup> allow the prediction of concentrations of each dissolved inorganic carbon species in solution, from the added concentration of HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup> (which constitutes more than 90% of <a href="/wiki/Bjerrum_plot" title="Bjerrum plot">Bjerrum plot</a> species from pH 7 to pH 8 at 25 °C in fresh water).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mook_2000_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mook_2000-49">[49]</a></sup> Addition of HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup> will increase CO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup> concentration at any pH. Rearranging the equations given above, we can see that [Ca<sup>2+</sup>] = Ksp / [CO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup>], and [CO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup>] = K<sub>a2</sub> × [HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>] / [H<sup>+</sup>]. Therefore, when HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup> concentration is known, the maximum concentration of Ca<sup>2+</sup> ions before scaling through CaCO<sub>3</sub> precipitation can be predicted from the formula:
</p>
<dl><dd>Ca<sup>2+</sup><sub>max</sub> = (K<sub>sp</sub> / K<sub>a2</sub>) × ([H<sup>+</sup>] / [HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>])</dd></dl>
<p>The solubility product for CaCO<sub>3</sub> (K<sub>sp</sub>) and the dissociation constants for the dissolved inorganic carbon species (including K<sub>a2</sub>) are all substantially affected by temperature and <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mook_2000_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mook_2000-49">[49]</a></sup> with the overall effect that Ca<sup>2+</sup><sub>max</sub> increases from fresh to salt water, and decreases with rising temperature, pH, or added bicarbonate level, as illustrated in the accompanying graphs.
</p><p>The trends are illustrative for pool management, but whether scaling occurs also depends on other factors including interactions with Mg<sup>2+</sup>, B(OH)<sub>4</sub><sup>−</sup> and other ions in the pool, as well as supersaturation effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Scaling is commonly observed in electrolytic chlorine generators, where there is a high pH near the cathode surface and scale deposition further increases temperature. This is one reason that some pool operators prefer borate over bicarbonate as the primary pH buffer, and avoid the use of pool chemicals containing calcium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>For the same total acid concentration, the initial pH of the weak acid is less acid than the one of the strong acid; however, the maximum amount of CaCO<sub>3</sub> which can be dissolved is approximately the same. This is because in the final state, the pH is larger than the p<i>K</i><sub>A</sub>, so that the weak acid is almost completely dissociated, yielding in the end as many H<sup>+</sup> ions as the strong acid to "dissolve" the calcium carbonate.
</p>
