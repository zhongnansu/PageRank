<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Azolla event</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Azolla_filiculoides_MUN.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Azolla_filiculoides_MUN.jpg/220px-Azolla_filiculoides_MUN.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Azolla_filiculoides_MUN.jpg/330px-Azolla_filiculoides_MUN.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Azolla_filiculoides_MUN.jpg/440px-Azolla_filiculoides_MUN.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Azolla_filiculoides_MUN.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The modern fern <i><a href="/wiki/Azolla_filiculoides" title="Azolla filiculoides">Azolla filiculoides</a></i>. Blooms of a related species may have pulled the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> into the current icehouse world.</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Azolla event</b> occurred in the middle <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a> epoch,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brinkhuis2006_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brinkhuis2006-1">[1]</a></sup> around <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=49">49</a> million years ago</span>, when <a href="/wiki/Algal_bloom" title="Algal bloom">blooms</a> of the freshwater <a href="/wiki/Fern" title="Fern">fern</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Azolla" title="Azolla">Azolla</a></i> are thought to have happened in the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a>.  As they sank to the stagnant sea floor, they were incorporated into the <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a>; the resulting draw-down of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> has been speculated to have helped transform the planet from a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_Earth" title="Greenhouse Earth">greenhouse Earth</a>" state, hot enough for turtles and palm trees to prosper at the poles, to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Icehouse_Earth" title="Icehouse Earth">icehouse Earth</a> it has been since.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geological_evidence_of_the_event">Geological evidence of the event</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Azolla_event&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geological evidence of the event">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:65_Myr_Climate_Change.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="397" data-file-width="650" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/65_Myr_Climate_Change.png/300px-65_Myr_Climate_Change.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/65_Myr_Climate_Change.png/450px-65_Myr_Climate_Change.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/65_Myr_Climate_Change.png/600px-65_Myr_Climate_Change.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:65_Myr_Climate_Change.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delta-O-18" title="Delta-O-18"><span class="texhtml">δ</span><sup>18</sup>O<sub></sub></a> – a proxy for temperature – over the past 65 million years. The Azolla event marks the end of the Eocene optimum and the beginning of a long-term decline in global temperatures<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</div></div></div>
<p>In sedimentary layers throughout the Arctic basin, a unit reaching at least 8 m in thickness (the bottom of the longest core was not recovered, but it may have reached 20 m+<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>) is discernible.  This unit consists of alternating layers; siliceous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clastic" title="Clastic">clastic</a> layers representing the background sedimentation of <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">planktonic</a> organisms, usual to marine sediments, switch with millimetre-thick laminations comprising <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossilised</a> <i>Azolla</i> matter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Waddell2008_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Waddell2008-2">[2]</a></sup> This organic matter can also be detected in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray_logging" title="Gamma ray logging">gamma radiation spike</a>, that has been noted throughout the Arctic basin, making the event a useful aid in lining up cores drilled at different locations.  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palynological" title="Palynological">Palynological</a> controls and calibration with the high-resolution <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_reversal" title="Geomagnetic reversal">geomagnetic reversal</a> record allows the duration of the event to be estimated at 800,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brinkhuis2006_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brinkhuis2006-1">[1]</a></sup>  The event coincides precisely with a catastrophic decline in <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> levels, which fell from 3500 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">ppm</a> in the early <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a> to 650 ppm during this event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearson2000_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pearson2000-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Azolla"><i>Azolla</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Azolla_event&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Azolla">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Azolla</i> has been deemed a "super-plant" as it can draw down as much as a tonne of nitrogen per acre per year<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belnap2002_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Belnap2002-4">[4]</a></sup> (0.25 kg/m²/yr); this is matched by 6 tonnes per acre of carbon drawdown (1.5 kg/m²/yr). Its ability to use atmospheric nitrogen for growth means that the main limit to its growth is usually the availability of phosphorus: carbon, nitrogen and sulphur being three of the key elements of proteins, and phosphorus being required for DNA, RNA and in energy metabolism.  The plant can grow at great speed in favourable conditions – modest warmth and 20 hours of sunlight, both of which were in evidence at the poles during the early Eocene – and can double its biomass over two to three days in such a climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brinkhuis2006_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Brinkhuis2006-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conditions_encouraging_the_event">Conditions encouraging the event</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Azolla_event&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Conditions encouraging the event">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Early_Eocene_Arctic_basin.PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="317" data-file-width="315" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f4/Early_Eocene_Arctic_basin.PNG/220px-Early_Eocene_Arctic_basin.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/f/f4/Early_Eocene_Arctic_basin.PNG 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Early_Eocene_Arctic_basin.PNG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The continental configuration during the Early Eocene resulted in an isolated Arctic basin.</div></div></div>
<p>During the early Eocene, the <a href="/wiki/Continental_drift" title="Continental drift">continental configuration</a> was such that the Arctic sea was almost entirely cut off from the wider oceans.  This meant that mixing — provided today by deep water currents such as the Gulf Stream — did not occur, leading to a stratified water column resembling today's <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stein2006_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stein2006-5">[5]</a></sup>
High temperatures and winds led to high evaporation, increasing the density of the ocean, and — through an increase in rainfall<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> — high discharge from rivers which fed the basin.  This low-density freshwater formed a <a href="/wiki/Nepheloid_layer" title="Nepheloid layer">nepheloid layer</a>, floating on the surface of the dense sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gleason2007_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gleason2007-7">[7]</a></sup>
Even a few centimetres of fresh water would be enough to allow colonization by <i>Azolla</i>; further, this river water would be rich in minerals such as phosphorus, which it would accumulate from mud and rocks it interacted with as it crossed the continents. To further aid the growth of the plant, concentrations of carbon (in the form of carbon dioxide) in the atmosphere are known to have been high at this time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearson2000_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pearson2000-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Blooms alone are not enough to have any geological impact; to permanently draw down CO<sub>2</sub> and cause <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, the carbon must be sequestered by the plants being buried and the remains rendered inaccessible to decomposing organisms.  The anoxic bottom of the Arctic basin, a result of the stratified water column, permitted just this: the anoxic environment inhibits the activity of decomposing organisms and allows the plants to sit unrotted until they are buried by sediment.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_effects">Global effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Azolla_event&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Global effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With 800,000 years of <i>Azolla</i> bloom episodes and a 4,000,000 km² basin to cover, even by very conservative estimates more than enough carbon could be sequestered by plant burial to account for the observed 80% drop in CO<sub>2</sub> by this one phenomenon alone.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It should be stressed that other factors almost certainly played a role. This drop initiated the switch from a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_and_icehouse_Earth" title="Greenhouse and icehouse Earth">greenhouse to the current icehouse Earth</a>; the Arctic cooled from an average sea-surface temperature of 13 °C to today's −9 °C,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brinkhuis2006_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Brinkhuis2006-1">[1]</a></sup> and the rest of the globe underwent a similar change. For perhaps the first time in its history,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> the planet had ice caps at <i>both</i> of its poles.  A geologically rapid decrease in temperature between 49 and <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=47">47</a> million years ago</span>, around the <i>Azolla</i> event, is evident: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dropstones" title="Dropstones">dropstones</a> — which are taken as evidence for the presence of glaciers — are common in Arctic sediments thereafter. This is set against a backdrop of gradual, long-term cooling: it is not until <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=15">15</a> million years ago</span> that evidence for widespread northern polar freezing is common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NatGeo_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-NatGeo-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_explanations">Alternative explanations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Azolla_event&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Alternative explanations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While a verdant Arctic Ocean is a viable working model, skeptical scientists point out that it would be possible for <i>Azolla</i> colonies in <a href="/wiki/River_delta" title="River delta">river deltas</a> or freshwater <a href="/wiki/Lagoon" title="Lagoon">lagoons</a> to be swept into the Arctic Ocean by strong currents, removing the necessity for a freshwater layer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NatGeo_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-NatGeo-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_considerations">Economic considerations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Azolla_event&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Economic considerations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Much of the current interest in oil exploration in the Arctic regions is directed towards the <i>Azolla</i> deposits<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.  The burial of large amounts of organic material provides the <a href="/wiki/Source_rock" title="Source rock">source rock</a> for oil, so given the right thermal history, the preserved <i>Azolla</i> blooms might have been converted to oil or gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-10">[10]</a></sup>  A research team has been set up in the Netherlands devoted to <i>Azolla</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
