<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Fluidization</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Fluidization</b> (or <b>fluidisation</b>) is a process similar to <a href="/wiki/Liquefaction" title="Liquefaction">liquefaction</a> whereby a <a href="/wiki/Granular_material" title="Granular material">granular material</a> is converted from a static <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solid</a>-like state to a dynamic <a href="/wiki/Fluid" title="Fluid">fluid</a>-like state. This process occurs when a fluid (<a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a>) is passed up through the granular material.
</p><p>When a gas flow is introduced through the bottom of a bed of solid particles, it will move upwards through the bed via the empty spaces between the particles.  At low gas velocities, aerodynamic <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">drag</a> on each particle is also low, and thus the bed remains in a fixed state.  Increasing the velocity, the aerodynamic drag forces will begin to counteract the gravitational forces, causing the bed to expand in volume as the particles move away from each other.  Further increasing the velocity, it will reach a critical value at which the upward drag forces will exactly equal the downward gravitational forces, causing the particles to become suspended within the fluid.  At this critical value, the bed is said to be fluidized and will exhibit fluidic behavior.  By further increasing gas velocity, the bulk density of the bed will continue to decrease, and its fluidization becomes more violent, until the particles no longer form a bed and are "conveyed" upwards by the gas flow. 
</p><p>When fluidized, a bed of solid particles will behave as a fluid, like a liquid or gas.  Like <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Bucket" title="Bucket">bucket</a>: the bed will conform to the volume of the chamber, its surface remaining perpendicular to <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a>; objects with a lower density than the bed density will float on its surface, bobbing up and down if pushed downwards, while objects with a higher density sink to the bottom of the bed.  The fluidic behavior allows the particles to be transported like a fluid, channeled through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_pipe" title="Water pipe">pipes</a>, not requiring mechanical transport (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Conveyor_belt" title="Conveyor belt">conveyor belt</a>).
</p><p>A simplified every-day-life example of a gas-solid <a href="/wiki/Fluidized_bed" title="Fluidized bed">fluidized bed</a> would be a hot-air <a href="/wiki/Popcorn_maker" title="Popcorn maker">popcorn popper</a>.  The <a href="/wiki/Popcorn" title="Popcorn">popcorn kernels</a>, all being fairly uniform in size and shape, are suspended in the hot air rising from the bottom chamber. Because of the intense mixing of the particles, akin to that of a boiling liquid, this allows for a uniform temperature of the kernels throughout the chamber, minimizing the amount of burnt popcorn.  After popping, the now larger popcorn particles encounter increased aerodynamic drag which pushes them out of the chamber and into a bowl.
</p><p>The process is also key in the formation of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sand_volcano" title="Sand volcano">sand volcano</a> and fluid escape structures in <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediments</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rocks</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fluidization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information on applications: <a href="/wiki/Fluidized_bed" title="Fluidized bed">Fluidized bed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fluidized_bed_combustion" title="Fluidized bed combustion">Fluidized bed combustion</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fluidized_bed_reactor" title="Fluidized bed reactor">Fluidized bed reactor</a></div>
<p>Most of the fluidization applications use one or more of three important characteristics of fluidized beds:
</p>
<ol><li>Fluidized solids can be easily transferred between reactors.</li>
<li>The intense mixing within a fluidized bed means that its temperature is uniform.</li>
<li>There is excellent heat transfer between a fluidized bed and heat exchangers immersed in the bed.</li></ol>
<p>In the 1920s, the Winkler process was developed to gasify coal in a fluidized bed, using oxygen.  It was not commercially successful.
</p><p>The first large scale commercial implementation, in the early 1940s, was the <a href="/wiki/Fluid_catalytic_cracking" title="Fluid catalytic cracking">fluid catalytic cracking (FCC)</a> process,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> which converted heavier <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> cuts into <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>.  Carbon-rich "<a href="/wiki/Coke_(fuel)" title="Coke (fuel)">coke</a>" deposits on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a> particles and deactivates the catalyst in less than 1 <a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">second</a>.  The fluidized catalyst particles are shuttled between the fluidized bed reactor and a fluidized bed burner where the coke deposits are burned off, generating heat for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endothermic" title="Endothermic">endothermic</a> cracking reaction.
</p><p>By the 1950s, fluidized bed technology was being applied to mineral and metallurgical processes such as drying, <a href="/wiki/Calcination" title="Calcination">calcining</a>, and sulfide <a href="/wiki/Roasting_(metallurgy)" title="Roasting (metallurgy)">roasting</a>.
</p><p>In the 1960s, several fluidized bed processes dramatically reduced the cost of some important <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monomers" title="Monomers">monomers</a>.  Examples are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sohio" title="Sohio">Sohio</a> process for <a href="/wiki/Acrylonitrile" title="Acrylonitrile">acrylonitrile</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and the oxychlorination process for <a href="/wiki/Vinyl_chloride" title="Vinyl chloride">vinyl chloride</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>  These chemical reactions are highly exothermic and fluidization ensures a uniform temperature, minimizing unwanted side reactions, and efficient heat transfer to cooling tubes, ensuring a high productivity.
</p><p>In the late 1970s, a fluidized bed process for the synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">polyethylene</a> dramatically reduced the cost of this important <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a>, making its use economical in many new applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  The polymerization reaction generates heat and the intense mixing associated with fluidization prevents hot spots where the polyethylene particles would melt.  A similar process is used for the synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Polypropylene" title="Polypropylene">polypropylene</a>.
</p><p>Currently, most of the processes that are being developed for the industrial production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_nanotubes" title="Carbon nanotubes">carbon nanotubes</a> use a fluidized bed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>  Arkema uses a fluidized bed to produce 400 tonnes/year of multiwall carbon nanotubes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>A new potential application of fluidization technology is <a href="/wiki/Chemical_looping_combustion" title="Chemical looping combustion">chemical looping combustion</a>, which has not yet been commercialized.   One solution to reducing the potential effect of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> generated by <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">fuel combustion</a> (e.g. in <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">power stations</a>) on <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> is <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">carbon dioxide sequestration</a>.  Regular <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a> produces a gas that is mostly <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> (as it is air's main component at about 80% by volume), which prevents economical sequestration.  Chemical looping uses a <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> as a solid <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> carrier. These metal oxide particles replace air (specifically <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> in the air) in a combustion reaction with a solid, liquid or gaseous fuel in a fluidized bed, producing solid metal particles from the <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduction</a> of the metal oxides and a mixture of carbon dioxide and <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a>, the major products of any combustion reaction.  The <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> vapor is condensed, leaving pure carbon dioxide which can be sequestered.  The solid metal particles are circulated to another fluidized bed where they react with air (and again, specifically oxygen in the air), producing heat and <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">oxidizing</a> the metal particles to metal oxide particles that are recirculated to the fluidized bed combustor.
</p><p>Fluidization has many applications with the use of <a href="/wiki/Ion_exchange" title="Ion exchange">ion exchange</a> particles for the purification and processing of many industrial liquid streams. Industries such as food &amp; beverage, hydrometallurgical, water softening, catalysis, bio-based chemical etc. use ion exchange as a critical step in processing.  Conventionally ion exchange has been used in a packed bed where a pre-clarified liquid passes downward through a column. Much work has been done at the University of Western Ontario in London Ontario, Canada on the use of a continuous fluidized ion exchange system, named "Liquid-solid circulating fluidized bed" (LSCFB), recently being called "Circulating fluidized ion exchange" (CFIX). This system has widespread applications extending the use of traditional ion exchange systems because it can handle feed streams with large amounts of suspended solids due to the use of fluidization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
