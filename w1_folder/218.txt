<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Electrophile</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>In organic 
<a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, an <b>electrophile</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Reagent" title="Reagent">reagent</a> attracted to <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a>. Electrophiles are positively charged or neutral species <b>having vacant orbitals</b> that are attracted to an electron rich centre. It participates in a chemical reaction by accepting an <a href="/wiki/Electron_pair" title="Electron pair">electron pair</a> in order to <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">bond</a> to a <a href="/wiki/Nucleophile" title="Nucleophile">nucleophile</a>. Because electrophiles accept electrons, they are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_acid" title="Lewis acid">Lewis acids</a> (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acid-base_reaction_theories" title="Acid-base reaction theories">acid-base reaction theories</a>). Most electrophiles are positively <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">charged</a>, have an atom that carries a partial positive charge, or have an atom that does not have an octet of electrons.
</p><p>The electrophiles are attacked by the most electron-populated part of one <a href="/wiki/Nucleophile" title="Nucleophile">nucleophile</a>. The electrophiles frequently seen in the <a href="/wiki/Organic_synthesis" title="Organic synthesis">organic syntheses</a> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cations" title="Cations">cations</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_ion" title="Hydrogen ion">H<sup>+</sup></a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrosonium" title="Nitrosonium">NO<sup>+</sup></a>, polarized neutral molecules such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride" title="Hydrogen chloride">HCl</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkyl_halide" title="Alkyl halide">alkyl halides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acyl_halide" title="Acyl halide">acyl halides</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonyl_compound" title="Carbonyl compound">carbonyl compounds</a>, polarizable neutral molecules such as <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">Cl<sub>2</sub></a> and <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">Br<sub>2</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_agent" title="Oxidizing agent">oxidizing agents</a> such as organic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peracid" title="Peracid">peracids</a>, chemical species that do not satisfy the <a href="/wiki/Octet_rule" title="Octet rule">octet rule</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Carbene" title="Carbene">carbenes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radical_(chemistry)" title="Radical (chemistry)">radicals</a>, and some Lewis acids such as <a href="/wiki/Borane" title="Borane">BH<sub>3</sub></a>  and <a href="/wiki/Diisobutylaluminium_hydride" title="Diisobutylaluminium hydride">DIBAL</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organic_chemistry">Organic chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Organic chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Addition_of_halogens">Addition of halogens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Addition of halogens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These occur between alkenes and electrophiles, often halogens as in <a href="/wiki/Halogen_addition_reaction" title="Halogen addition reaction">halogen addition reactions</a>. Common reactions include use of bromine water to <a href="/wiki/Titration" title="Titration">titrate</a> against a sample to deduce the number of double bonds present. For example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethene" title="Ethene">ethene</a> + <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">bromine</a> → <a href="/wiki/1,2-Dibromoethane" title="1,2-Dibromoethane">1,2-dibromoethane</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>4</sub> + Br<sub>2</sub> → BrCH<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>Br</dd></dl>
<p>This takes the form of 3 main steps shown below;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Electrophilic_addition_of_Br2.png"><img alt="Electrophilic addition of Br2.png" data-file-height="325" data-file-width="1241" height="107" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Electrophilic_addition_of_Br2.png/410px-Electrophilic_addition_of_Br2.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Electrophilic_addition_of_Br2.png/615px-Electrophilic_addition_of_Br2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Electrophilic_addition_of_Br2.png/820px-Electrophilic_addition_of_Br2.png 2x" width="410"/></a></dd></dl>
<ol><li><b>Forming of a π-complex</b>
<dl><dd>The electrophilic Br-Br molecule interacts with electron-rich alkene molecule to form a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pi-complex" title="Pi-complex">π-complex</a> <b>1</b>.</dd></dl></li>
<li><b>Forming of a three-membered bromonium ion</b>
<dl><dd>The alkene is working as an electron donor and bromine as an electrophile. The three-membered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bromonium_ion" title="Bromonium ion">bromonium ion</a> <b>2</b> consisted of two carbon atoms and a bromine atom forms with a release of Br<sup>−</sup>.</dd></dl></li>
<li><b>Attacking of bromide ion</b>
<dl><dd>The bromonium ion is opened by the attack of Br<sup>−</sup> from the back side. This yields the <a href="/wiki/Vicinal_(chemistry)" title="Vicinal (chemistry)">vicinal</a> dibromide with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antiperiplanar" title="Antiperiplanar">antiperiplanar</a> configuration. When other nucleophiles such as water or alcohol are existing, these may attack <b>2</b> to give an alcohol or an ether.</dd></dl></li></ol>
<p>This process is called <b>Ad<sub>E</sub>2 mechanism</b>. <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">Iodine</a> (I<sub>2</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> (Cl<sub>2</sub>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulfenyl" title="Sulfenyl">sulfenyl</a> ion (RS<sup>+</sup>), <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> cation (Hg<sup>2+</sup>), and <a href="/wiki/Dichlorocarbene" title="Dichlorocarbene">dichlorocarbene</a> (:CCl<sub>2</sub>) also react through similar pathways. The direct conversion of <b>1</b> to <b>3</b> will appear when the Br<sup>−</sup> is large excess in the reaction medium. A β-bromo <a href="/wiki/Carbenium_ion" title="Carbenium ion">carbenium ion</a> intermediate may be predominant instead of <b>3</b> if the alkene has a cation-stabilizing substituent like phenyl group. There is an example of the isolation of the bromonium ion <b>2</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Addition_of_hydrogen_halides">Addition of hydrogen halides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Addition of hydrogen halides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hydrogen halides such as hydrogen chloride (HCl) adds to alkenes to give alkyl halides in <a href="/wiki/Hydrohalogenation" title="Hydrohalogenation">hydrohalogenation</a>. For example, the reaction of HCl with ethylene furnishes chloroethane. The reaction proceeds with a cation intermediate, being different from the above halogen addition. An example is shown below:
</p>
<dl><dd><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Electrophilic_addition_of_HCl.png"><img alt="Electrophilic addition of HCl.png" data-file-height="265" data-file-width="1186" height="87" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Electrophilic_addition_of_HCl.png/390px-Electrophilic_addition_of_HCl.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Electrophilic_addition_of_HCl.png/585px-Electrophilic_addition_of_HCl.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Electrophilic_addition_of_HCl.png/780px-Electrophilic_addition_of_HCl.png 2x" width="390"/></a></dd></dl>
<ol><li>Proton (H<sup>+</sup>) adds (by working as an electrophile) to one of the carbon atoms on the alkene to form cation <b>1</b>.</li>
<li>Chloride ion (Cl<sup>−</sup>) combines with the cation <b>1</b> to form the adducts <b>2</b> and <b>3</b>.</li></ol>
<p>In this manner, the <a href="/wiki/Stereoselectivity" title="Stereoselectivity">stereoselectivity</a> of the product, that is, from which side Cl<sup>−</sup> will attack relies on the types of alkenes applied and conditions of the reaction. At least, which of the two carbon atoms will be attacked by H<sup>+</sup> is usually decided by <a href="/wiki/Markovnikov%27s_rule" title="Markovnikov's rule">Markovnikov's rule</a>. Thus, H<sup>+</sup> attacks the carbon atom that carries fewer substituents so as the more stabilized carbocation (with the more stabilizing substituents) will form.
</p><p>This process is called <b>A-S<sub>E</sub>2 mechanism</b>. <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">Hydrogen fluoride</a> (HF) and hydrogen iodide (HI) react with alkenes in a similar manner, and Markovnikov-type products will be given. Hydrogen bromide (HBr) also takes this pathway, but sometimes a radical process competes and a mixture of isomers may form.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydration">Hydration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hydration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the more complex <a href="/wiki/Hydration_reaction" title="Hydration reaction">hydration reactions</a> utilises <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a>. This reaction occurs in a similar way to the addition reaction but has an extra step in which the OSO<sub>3</sub>H group is replaced by an OH group, forming an alcohol:
</p>
<dl><dd>C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>4</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O → C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>5</sub>OH</dd></dl>
<p>As can be seen, the H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> does take part in the overall reaction, however it remains unchanged so is classified as a catalyst.
</p><p>This is the reaction in more detail:
</p>
<dl><dd><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Electrophilic_reaction_of_sulfuric_acid_with_ethene.png"><img alt="Electrophilic reaction of sulfuric acid with ethene.png" data-file-height="435" data-file-width="1761" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Electrophilic_reaction_of_sulfuric_acid_with_ethene.png/580px-Electrophilic_reaction_of_sulfuric_acid_with_ethene.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Electrophilic_reaction_of_sulfuric_acid_with_ethene.png/870px-Electrophilic_reaction_of_sulfuric_acid_with_ethene.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Electrophilic_reaction_of_sulfuric_acid_with_ethene.png/1160px-Electrophilic_reaction_of_sulfuric_acid_with_ethene.png 2x" width="580"/></a></dd></dl>
<ol><li>The H–OSO<sub>3</sub>H molecule has a δ+ charge on the initial H atom. This is attracted to and reacts with the double bond in the same way as before.</li>
<li>The remaining (negatively charged) <sup>−</sup>OSO<sub>3</sub>H ion then attaches to the <a href="/wiki/Carbocation" title="Carbocation">carbocation</a>, forming ethyl hydrogensulphate (upper way on the above scheme).</li>
<li>When water (H<sub>2</sub>O) is added and the mixture heated, ethanol (C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>5</sub>OH) is produced. The "spare" hydrogen atom from the water goes into "replacing" the "lost" hydrogen and, thus, reproduces sulfuric acid. Another pathway in which water molecule combines directly to the intermediate carbocation (lower way) is also possible. This pathway become predominant when aqueous sulfuric acid is used.</li></ol>
<p>Overall, this process adds a molecule of water to a molecule of ethene.
</p><p>This is an important reaction in industry, as it produces <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>, whose purposes include fuels and starting material for other chemicals.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chiral_derivatives">Chiral derivatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Chiral derivatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many electrophiles are <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)" title="Chirality (chemistry)">chiral and optically stable</a>.  Typically chiral electrophiles are also optically pure.
</p><p>One such <a href="/wiki/Reagent" title="Reagent">reagent</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Fructose" title="Fructose">fructose</a>-derived organocatalyst used in the <a href="/wiki/Shi_epoxidation" title="Shi epoxidation">Shi epoxidation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The catalyst can accomplish highly enantioselective epoxidations of <a href="/wiki/Cis%E2%80%93trans_isomerism" title="Cis–trans isomerism"><i>trans</i></a>-disubstituted and trisubstituted <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkenes</a>. The Shi catalyst, a <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">ketone</a>, is oxidized by stoichiometric <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxone" title="Oxone">oxone</a> to the active <a href="/wiki/Dioxirane" title="Dioxirane">dioxirane</a> form before proceeding in the catalytic cycle.
</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Oxaziridine" title="Oxaziridine">Oxaziridines</a> such as chiral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Davis_oxaziridine" title="Davis oxaziridine">N-sulfonyloxaziridines</a> effect enantioselective ketone alpha oxidation en route to the AB-ring segments of various <a href="/wiki/Natural_product" title="Natural product">natural products</a>, including γ-rhodomycionone and α-citromycinone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Polymer-bound chiral <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a> electrophiles effect asymmetric selenenylation reactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The reagents are aryl selenenyl bromides, and they were first developed for solution phase chemistry and then modified for solid phase bead attachment via an aryloxy moiety. The solid-phase reagents were applied toward the selenenylation of various alkenes with good enantioselectivities. The products can be cleaved from the solid support using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organotin" title="Organotin">organotin</a> hydride reducing agents. Solid-supported reagents offers advantages over solution phase chemistry due to the ease of workup and purification.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Superelectrophiles">Superelectrophiles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrophile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Superelectrophiles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Superelectrophiles</b> are defined as cationic electrophilic reagents with greatly enhanced reactivities in the presence of <a href="/wiki/Superacid" title="Superacid">superacids</a>. These compounds were first described by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_A._Olah" title="George A. Olah">George A. Olah</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Superelectrophiles form as a doubly electron deficient superelectrophile by protosolvation of a cationic electrophile. As observed by Olah, a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boron_trifluoride" title="Boron trifluoride">boron trifluoride</a> is able to remove a hydride ion from <a href="/wiki/Isobutane" title="Isobutane">isobutane</a> when combined with <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">hydrofluoric acid</a> via the formation of a <a href="/wiki/Superacid" title="Superacid">superacid</a> from BF<sub>3</sub> and HF. The responsible <a href="/wiki/Reactive_intermediate" title="Reactive intermediate">reactive intermediate</a> is the CH<sub>3</sub>CO<sub>2</sub>H<sub>3</sub> dication. Likewise, <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> can be nitrated to <a href="/wiki/Nitromethane" title="Nitromethane">nitromethane</a> with <a href="/wiki/Nitronium_tetrafluoroborate" title="Nitronium tetrafluoroborate">nitronium tetrafluoroborate</a> NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>BF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span> only in presence of a strong acid like <a href="/wiki/Fluorosulfuric_acid" title="Fluorosulfuric acid">fluorosulfuric acid</a>.
</p><p>In <b>gitionic</b> (<b>gitonic</b>) superelectrophiles, charged centers are separated by no more than one atom, for example, the protonitronium ion O=N<sup>+</sup>=O<sup>+</sup>—H (a protonated <a href="/wiki/Nitronium_ion" title="Nitronium ion">nitronium ion</a>).  And, in <b>distonic</b> superelectrophiles, they are separated by 2 or more atoms, for example, in the fluorination reagent <a href="/wiki/Selectfluor" title="Selectfluor">F-TEDA-BF<sub>4</sub></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
