<p><b>Calcification</b> is the accumulation of <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> salts in a <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">body tissue</a>. It normally occurs in the formation of <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bone</a>, but calcium can be deposited abnormally in <a href="/wiki/Soft_tissue" title="Soft tissue">soft tissue</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BertazzoGentleman2013_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-BertazzoGentleman2013-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup> causing it to harden. Calcifications may be classified on whether there is mineral balance or not, and the location of the calcification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Calcification may also refer to the processes of normal mineral deposition in biological systems, such as the formation of <a href="/wiki/Stromatolite" title="Stromatolite">stromatolites</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mollusc_shell" title="Mollusc shell">mollusc shells</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Mineralization_(biology)" title="Mineralization (biology)">Mineralization (biology)</a> or <a href="/wiki/Biomineralization" title="Biomineralization">Biomineralization</a>).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Signs_and_symptoms">Signs and symptoms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Signs and symptoms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Calcification can manifest itself in many ways in the body depending on the location.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes_of_soft_tissue_calcification">Causes of soft tissue calcification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Causes of soft tissue calcification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Calcification of soft tissue (arteries, cartilage, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_valves" title="Heart valves">heart valves</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BertazzoGentleman2013_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-BertazzoGentleman2013-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup> etc.) can be caused by <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_K_deficiency" title="Vitamin K deficiency">vitamin K<sub>2</sub> deficiency</a> or by poor <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> absorption due to a high calcium/vitamin D ratio.  This can occur with or without a mineral imbalance.
</p><p>Intake of excessive <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_D" title="Vitamin D">vitamin D</a> can cause <a href="/wiki/Hypervitaminosis_D" title="Hypervitaminosis D">vitamin D poisoning</a> and excessive intake of calcium from the intestine, when accompanied by a deficiency of vitamin K (perhaps induced by an <a href="/wiki/Anticoagulant" title="Anticoagulant">anticoagulant</a>) can result in calcification of arteries and other soft tissue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  Such metastatic soft tissue calcification is mainly in tissues containing "calcium catchers" such as elastic fibres or sour mucopolysaccharides. These tissues especially include the lungs (pumice lung) and the aorta.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mineral_balance">Mineral balance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mineral balance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Dystrophic_calcification" title="Dystrophic calcification">Dystrophic calcification</a>, without a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systemic_circulation" title="Systemic circulation">systemic</a> mineral imbalance.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Metastatic_calcification" title="Metastatic calcification">Metastatic calcification</a>, a systemic elevation of calcium levels in the blood and all tissues.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forms">Forms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Forms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Calcification can be pathological or a standard part of the aging process. Nearly all adults show calcification of the <a href="/wiki/Pineal_gland" title="Pineal gland">pineal gland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rsna_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-rsna-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Extraskeletal_calcification&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Extraskeletal calcification (page does not exist)">Extraskeletal calcification</a>, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Calciphylaxis" title="Calciphylaxis">calciphylaxis</a></li>
<li>Brain, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Fahr%27s_syndrome" title="Fahr's syndrome">Fahr's syndrome</a></li>
<li>Tumor calcification<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>Arthritic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bone_spurs" title="Bone spurs">bone spurs</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kidney_stones" title="Kidney stones">Kidney stones</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gall_stones" title="Gall stones">Gall stones</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterotopic_bone" title="Heterotopic bone">Heterotopic bone</a></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pattern">Pattern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pattern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Patterns of calcifications may indicate pathological processes. Laminated appearance suggests granulomatous disease while popcorn calcification indicates <a href="/wiki/Hamartoma" title="Hamartoma">hamartoma</a>. Malignant lesions may have stippled or eccentric calcification.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breast_disease">Breast disease</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Breast disease">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a number of <a href="/wiki/Breast_disease" title="Breast disease">breast pathologies</a>, calcium is often deposited at sites of cell death or in association secretions or hyalinized stroma, resulting in pathologic calcification. For example, small, irregular, linear calcifications may be seen, via <a href="/wiki/Mammography" title="Mammography">mammography</a>, in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mammary_ductal_carcinoma" title="Mammary ductal carcinoma">ductal carcinoma-in-situ</a> to produce visible radio-opacities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnosis">Diagnosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Diagnosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In terms of diagnosis, in this case vascular calcification, an ultrasound and radiography of said area is sufficient.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Treatment of high calcium/vitamin D ratio may most easily be accomplished by intake of more <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_D" title="Vitamin D">vitamin D</a> if vitamin K is normal.  Intake of too much vitamin D would be evident by <a href="/wiki/Anorexia_(symptom)" title="Anorexia (symptom)">anorexia</a>, loss of appetite, or soft tissue calcification.
</p>
