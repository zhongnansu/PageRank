<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Police" title="Police">Police</a>.</div>
<p>A <b>policy</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Deliberation" title="Deliberation">deliberate</a> <a href="/wiki/System" title="System">system</a> of <a href="/wiki/Principle" title="Principle">principles</a> to guide decisions and achieve rational outcomes. A policy is a statement of intent, and is implemented as a procedure or protocol. Policies are generally adopted by a <a href="/wiki/Governance" title="Governance">governance</a> body within an organization. Policies can assist in both <i>subjective</i> and <i>objective</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decision_making" title="Decision making">decision making</a>. Policies to assist in subjective decision making usually assist senior management with decisions that must be based on the relative merits of a number of factors, and as a result are often hard to test objectively, e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Work-life_balance" title="Work-life balance">work-life balance</a> policy. In contrast policies to assist in objective decision making are usually operational in nature and can be objectively tested, e.g. password policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The term may apply to government, private sector organizations and groups, as well as individuals. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Executive_order_(United_States)" title="Executive order (United States)">Presidential executive orders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Privacy_policy" title="Privacy policy">corporate privacy policies</a>, and parliamentary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rules_of_order" title="Rules of order">rules of order</a> are all examples of policy. Policy differs from <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_law" title="Rule of law">rules</a> or <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a>. While law can compel or prohibit behaviors (e.g. a law requiring the payment of taxes on income), policy merely guides actions toward those that are most likely to achieve a desired outcome.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Policy or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Policy_study" title="Policy study">policy study</a> may also refer to the process of making important organizational decisions, including the identification of different alternatives such as programs or spending priorities, and choosing among them on the basis of the impact they will have. Policies can be understood as political, <a href="/wiki/Management" title="Management">managerial</a>, financial, and administrative mechanisms arranged to reach explicit goals. In public corporate finance, a <a href="/wiki/Critical_accounting_policy" title="Critical accounting policy">critical accounting policy</a> is a policy for a firm/company or an industry that is considered to have a notably high subjective element, and that has a material impact on the <a href="/wiki/Financial_statement" title="Financial statement">financial statements</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intended_effects">Intended effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Intended effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The intended effects of a policy vary widely according to the organization and the context in which they are made. Broadly, policies are typically instituted to avoid some negative effect that has been noticed in the 
organization, or to seek some positive benefit.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Corporate <a href="/wiki/Purchasing_process" title="Purchasing process">purchasing policies</a> provide an example of how organizations attempt to avoid negative  effects. Many large companies have policies that all purchases above a certain value must be performed through a purchasing process. By requiring this standard purchasing process through policy, the organization can limit waste and standardize the way purchasing is done.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The State of <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> provides an example of benefit-seeking policy. In recent years, the numbers of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hybrid_cars" title="Hybrid cars">hybrid cars</a> in California has increased dramatically, in part because of policy changes in <a href="/wiki/Federal_law" title="Federal law">Federal law</a> that provided USD $1,500 in tax credits (since phased out) as well as the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HOV" title="HOV">high-occupancy vehicle</a> lanes to hybrid owners (no loew hybrid vehicles). In this case, the organization (state and/or federal government) created an effect (increased ownership and use of hybrid vehicles) through policy (tax breaks, highway lanes).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unintended_effects">Unintended effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Unintended effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Policies frequently have side effects or <a href="/wiki/Unintended_consequences" title="Unintended consequences">unintended consequences</a>.  Because the environments that policies seek to influence or manipulate are typically <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complex_adaptive_systems" title="Complex adaptive systems">complex adaptive systems</a> (e.g. governments, societies, large companies), making a policy change can have <a href="/wiki/Counterintuitive" title="Counterintuitive">counterintuitive</a> results.  For example, a government may make a policy decision to raise taxes, in hopes of increasing overall tax revenue.  Depending on the size of the tax increase, this may have the overall effect of reducing tax revenue by causing <a href="/wiki/Capital_flight" title="Capital flight">capital flight</a> or by creating a rate so high that citizens are deterred from earning the money that is taxed. (See the <a href="/wiki/Laffer_curve" title="Laffer curve">Laffer curve</a>.)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The policy formulation process theoretically includes an attempt to assess as many areas of potential policy impact as possible, to lessen the chances that a given policy will have unexpected or unintended consequences.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Policy_cycle">Policy cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Policy cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political science</a>, the <b>policy cycle</b> is a tool used for the analyzing of the development of a policy item. It can also be referred to as a "stagist approach", "stages heuristic" or "stages approach". It is thus a rule of thumb rather than the actual reality of how policy is created, but has been influential in how <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political scientists</a> looked at policy in general.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It was developed as a theory from <a href="/wiki/Harold_Lasswell" title="Harold Lasswell">Harold Lasswell</a>'s work.
</p><p>One version by James E. Anderson, in his <i>Public Policy-Making</i> (1974) has the following stages:
</p>
<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Political_agenda" title="Political agenda">Agenda</a> setting (Problem identification) - The recognition of certain subject as a problem demanding further government attention.</li>
<li>Policy Formulation - Involves exploring a variation of options or alternative courses of action available for addressing the problem. (appraisal, dialogue, formulation, and consolidation)</li>
<li>Decision-making - Government decides on an ultimate course of action, whether to perpetuate the policy status quo or alter it. (Decision could be 'positive', 'negative', or 'no-action')</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Implementation" title="Implementation">Implementation</a> - The ultimate decision made earlier will be put into practice.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Evaluation" title="Evaluation">Evaluation</a> - Assesses the effectiveness of a public policy in terms of its perceived intentions and results. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Actor_(policy_debate)" title="Actor (policy debate)">Policy actors</a> attempt to determine whether the course of action is a success or failure by examining its impact and outcomes.</li></ol>
<p>An eight step policy cycle is developed in detail in <i>The Australian Policy Handbook</i> by Peter Bridgman and <a href="/wiki/Glyn_Davis" title="Glyn Davis">Glyn Davis</a>: (now with Catherine Althaus in its 4th and 5th editions)
</p>
<ol><li>Issue identification</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Policy_analysis" title="Policy analysis">Policy analysis</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Public_consultation" title="Public consultation">Consultation</a> (which permeates the entire process)</li>
<li>Policy instrument development</li>
<li>Building coordination and coalitions</li>
<li>Program Design: <a href="/wiki/Decision_theory" title="Decision theory">Decision</a> making</li>
<li>Policy <a href="/wiki/Implementation" title="Implementation">Implementation</a></li>
<li>Policy <a href="/wiki/Evaluation" title="Evaluation">Evaluation</a></li></ol>
<p>The Althaus, Bridgman &amp; Davis model is <a href="/wiki/Heuristic" title="Heuristic">heuristic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iterative" title="Iterative">iterative</a>. It is <span class="cleanup-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">intentionally <a href="/wiki/Norm_(philosophy)" title="Norm (philosophy)">normative</a></span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (February 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and not meant to be <span class="cleanup-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;"><a href="/wiki/Organizational_diagnostics" title="Organizational diagnostics">diagnostic</a></span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (February 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Predictive" title="Predictive">predictive</a>. Policy cycles are typically characterized as adopting a classical approach, and tend to describe processes from the perspective of policy decision makers. Accordingly, some <a href="/wiki/Postpositivism" title="Postpositivism">postpositivist</a> academics challenge cyclical models as unresponsive and unrealistic, preferring systemic and more complex models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> They consider a broader range of actors involved in the policy space that includes <a href="/wiki/Voluntary_sector" title="Voluntary sector">civil society organisations</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">media</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intellectuals" title="Intellectuals">intellectuals</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Think_tanks" title="Think tanks">think tanks</a> or policy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Research_institutes" title="Research institutes">research institutes</a>, corporations, <a href="/wiki/Advocacy_group" title="Advocacy group">lobbyists</a>, etc.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Content">Content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Policies are typically <a href="/wiki/Promulgation" title="Promulgation">promulgated</a> through official written documents. Policy documents often come with the endorsement or signature of the executive powers within an organization to legitimize the policy and demonstrate that it is considered in force. Such documents often have standard formats that are particular to the organization issuing the policy.  While such formats differ in form, policy documents usually contain certain standard components including <b><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></b> :
</p>
<ul><li>A <b>purpose statement</b>, outlining why the organization is issuing the policy, and what its desired effect or outcome of the policy should be.</li>
<li>An <b>applicability and scope</b> statement, describing who the policy affects and which actions are impacted by the policy. The applicability and scope may expressly exclude certain people, organizations, or actions from the policy requirements.  Applicability and scope is used to focus the policy on only the desired targets, and avoid unintended consequences where possible.</li>
<li>An <b>effective date</b> which indicates when the policy comes into force.  <a href="/wiki/Ex_post_facto_law" title="Ex post facto law">Retroactive policies</a> are rare, but can be found.</li>
<li>A <b>responsibilities</b> section, indicating which parties and organizations are responsible for carrying out individual policy statements.  Many policies may require the establishment of some ongoing function or action.  For example, a purchasing policy might specify that a purchasing office be created to process purchase requests, and that this office would be responsible for ongoing actions.  Responsibilities often include identification of any relevant <a href="/wiki/Regulation" title="Regulation">oversight</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Governance" title="Governance">governance</a> structures.</li>
<li><b>Policy statements</b> indicating the specific regulations, requirements, or modifications to organizational behavior that the policy is creating.  Policy statements are extremely diverse depending on the organization and intent, and may take almost any form.</li></ul>
<p>Some policies may contain additional sections, including:
</p>
<ul><li><b>Background</b>, indicating any reasons, history, ethical background statements, and/or intent that led to the creation of the policy, which may be listed as <b>motivating factors</b>.  This information is often quite valuable when policies must be evaluated or used in ambiguous situations, just as the intent of a law can be useful to a court when deciding a case that involves that law.</li>
<li><b>Definitions</b>, providing clear and unambiguous definitions for terms and concepts found in the policy document.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Typologies">Typologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Typologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The American political scientist <a href="/wiki/Theodore_J._Lowi" title="Theodore J. Lowi">Theodore J. Lowi</a> proposed four types of policy, namely <b>distributive</b>, <b>redistributive</b>, <b>regulatory</b> and <b>constituent</b> in his article 'Four systems of Policy, Politics and Choice' and in 'American Business, Public Policy, Case Studies and Political Theory'.
Policy addresses the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intent" title="Intent">intent</a> of the organization, whether government, business, professional, or voluntary. Policy is intended to affect the 'real' world, by guiding the decisions that are made. Whether they are formally written or not, most organizations have identified policies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Policies may be classified in many different ways. The following is a sample of several different types of policies broken down by their effect on members of the organization.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Distributive_policies">Distributive policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Distributive policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Distributive_tendency" title="Distributive tendency">Distributive policies</a> extend goods and services to members of an organization, as well as distributing the costs of the goods/services amongst the members of the organization. Examples include government policies that impact spending for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Welfare_(financial_aid)" title="Welfare (financial aid)">welfare</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_education" title="Public education">public education</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Highways" title="Highways">highways</a>, and public safety, or a professional organization's benefits plan.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulatory_policies">Regulatory policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Regulatory policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Regulatory policies, or mandates, limit the discretion of individuals and agencies, or otherwise compel certain types of behavior. These policies are generally thought to be best applied when good behavior can be easily defined and bad behavior can be easily regulated and punished through fines or sanctions. An example of a fairly successful public regulatory policy is that of a highway speed limit.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Constituent_policies">Constituent policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Constituent policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Constituent policies create executive power entities, or deal with laws. Constituent policies also deal with Fiscal Policy in some circumstances.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="As well as the governing board creates laws Noman Dhakkn (February 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Redistributive_policies">Redistributive policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Redistributive policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Policies are dynamic; they are not just static lists of goals or laws. Policy blueprints have to be implemented, often with unexpected results. Social policies are what happens 'on the ground' when they are implemented, as well as what happens at the decision making or legislative stage.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>When the term policy is used, it may also refer to:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Official government policy (legislation or guidelines that govern how laws should be put into operation)</li>
<li>Broad ideas and goals in political manifestos and pamphlets</li>
<li>A company or organization's policy on a particular topic.  For example, the equal opportunity policy of a company shows that the company aims to treat all its staff equally.</li></ul>
<p>The actions the organization actually takes may often vary significantly from stated policy. This difference is sometimes caused by <a href="/wiki/Horse_trading" title="Horse trading">political compromise</a> over policy, while in other situations it is caused by lack of policy implementation and enforcement. Implementing policy may have unexpected results, stemming from a policy whose reach extends further than the problem it was originally crafted to address. Additionally, unpredictable results may arise from selective or idiosyncratic enforcement of policy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Types of <a href="/wiki/Policy_analysis" title="Policy analysis">policy analysis</a> include: 
</p>
<ul><li>Causal (resp. non-causal)</li>
<li>Deterministic (resp. stochastic, randomized and sometimes non-deterministic)</li>
<li>Index</li>
<li>Memoryless (<i>e.g.</i>, non-stationary)</li>
<li>Opportunistic (resp. non-opportunistic)</li>
<li>Stationary (resp. non-stationary)</li></ul>
<p>These qualifiers can be combined, so one could, for example, have a stationary-memoryless-index policy.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses_of_the_term">Other uses of the term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Policy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Other uses of the term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>In <a href="/wiki/Enterprise_architecture" title="Enterprise architecture">enterprise architecture</a> for systems design, <a href="/wiki/Policy_appliances" title="Policy appliances">policy appliances</a> are technical control and logging mechanisms to enforce or reconcile policy (systems use) rules and to ensure accountability in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Information_systems" title="Information systems">information systems</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a>, policies are contracts between insurer and insured used to <a href="/wiki/Indemnity" title="Indemnity">indemnify</a> (protect) against potential loss from specified perils. While these documents are referred to as policies, they are in actuality a form of <a href="/wiki/Contract" title="Contract">contract</a> - see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insurance_contract" title="Insurance contract">insurance contract</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Gambling" title="Gambling">gambling</a>, policy is a form of an unsanctioned lottery, where players purport to purchase <i><a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a></i> against a chosen number being picked by a legitimate <a href="/wiki/Lottery" title="Lottery">lottery</a>. Or can refer to an ordinary <a href="/wiki/Numbers_game" title="Numbers game">Numbers game</a></li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a> planning  and <i>reinforcement learning</i>, a policy prescribes a non-empty deliberation (sequence of actions) given a non-empty sequence of states.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Debate_(parliamentary_procedure)" title="Debate (parliamentary procedure)">debate</a>, the term "policy" is slang for <a href="/wiki/Policy_debate" title="Policy debate">policy or cross-examination</a> debate.</li></ul>
