<p><b>August Wilhelm von Hofmann</b> (8 April 1818 – 5 May 1892<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>) was a German <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">chemist</a>. After studying under <a href="/wiki/Justus_von_Liebig" title="Justus von Liebig">Justus von Liebig</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Giessen" title="University of Giessen">University of Giessen</a>, Hofmann became the first director of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_College_of_Chemistry" title="Royal College of Chemistry">Royal College of Chemistry</a> in London, in 1845.  In 1865 he returned to Germany to accept a position at the <a href="/wiki/Humboldt_University_of_Berlin" title="Humboldt University of Berlin">University of Berlin</a> as a teacher and researcher. After his return he co-founded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Chemical_Society" title="German Chemical Society">German Chemical Society</a> (Deutsche Chemische Gesellschaft) (1867).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB2014_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB2014-3">[3]</a></sup>
In both London and Berlin, Hofmann recreated the style of laboratory instruction established by Liebig at Giessen, fostering a school of chemistry focused on experimental organic chemistry and its industrial applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia2008_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia2008-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>He made considerable contributions to organic chemistry. His research on <a href="/wiki/Aniline" title="Aniline">aniline</a> helped lay the basis of the aniline-dye industry.  His earliest research on <a href="/wiki/Coal_tar" title="Coal tar">coal tar</a> laid the groundwork for his student <a href="/wiki/Charles_Blachford_Mansfield" title="Charles Blachford Mansfield">Charles Mansfield's</a> practical methods for extracting <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toluene" title="Toluene">toluene</a> and converting them into nitro compounds and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amines" title="Amines">amines</a>. Hofmann prepared three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethylamines" title="Ethylamines">ethylamines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tetraethylammonium" title="Tetraethylammonium">tetraethylammonium</a> compounds and established their structural relationship to <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>. His discoveries include <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">formaldehyde</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrazobenzene" title="Hydrazobenzene">hydrazobenzene</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isonitriles" title="Isonitriles">isonitriles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Allyl_alcohol" title="Allyl alcohol">allyl alcohol</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB2014_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-EB2014-3">[3]</a></sup> His name is associated with the <a href="/wiki/Hofmann_voltameter" title="Hofmann voltameter">Hofmann voltameter</a> and with a number of processes which he investigated, including the <a href="/wiki/Hofmann_rearrangement" title="Hofmann rearrangement">Hofmann rearrangement</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hofmann%E2%80%93Martius_rearrangement" title="Hofmann–Martius rearrangement">Hofmann–Martius rearrangement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hofmann_elimination" title="Hofmann elimination">Hofmann elimination</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hofmann-L%C3%B6ffler_reaction" title="Hofmann-Löffler reaction">Hofmann-Löffler reaction</a>.
</p><p>Hofmann received several significant awards in the field of chemistry, including the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Medal" title="Royal Medal">Royal Medal</a> (1854), the <a href="/wiki/Copley_Medal" title="Copley Medal">Copley Medal</a> (1875) and the <a href="/wiki/Albert_Medal_(Royal_Society_of_Arts)" title="Albert Medal (Royal Society of Arts)">Albert Medal</a> (1881). He was ennobled on his seventieth birthday.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia2008_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia2008-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=August_Wilhelm_von_Hofmann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A._W._Hofmann_1846.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2387" data-file-width="1920" height="274" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/A._W._Hofmann_1846.jpg/220px-A._W._Hofmann_1846.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/A._W._Hofmann_1846.jpg/330px-A._W._Hofmann_1846.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/A._W._Hofmann_1846.jpg/440px-A._W._Hofmann_1846.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A._W._Hofmann_1846.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hofmann, 1846</div></div></div>
<p>August Wilhelm Hofmann was born at <a href="/wiki/Giessen" title="Giessen">Giessen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Hesse" title="Grand Duchy of Hesse">Grand Duchy of Hesse</a>, on 8 April 1818.  He was the son of Johann Philipp Hofmann, privy councillor and provincial architect to the court at Darmstadt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meinel_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Meinel-5">[5]</a></sup> As a young man, he travelled widely with his father.  August Wilhelm matriculated at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Giessen" title="University of Giessen">University of Giessen</a> in 1836.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia2008_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia2008-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>He originally took up the study of law and <a href="/wiki/Philology" title="Philology">philology</a> at <a href="/wiki/Giessen" title="Giessen">Giessen</a>. He may have become interested in chemistry when his father enlarged Liebig's Giessen laboratories in 1839.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia2008_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia2008-4">[4]</a></sup> August Wilhelm changed his studies to <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, and studied under <a href="/wiki/Justus_von_Liebig" title="Justus von Liebig">Justus von Liebig</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Travis_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Travis-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peppas_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Peppas-7">[7]</a></sup>
He obtained his PhD there in 1841. In 1843, after his father's death, he became one of Liebig's assistants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackson2014_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jackson2014-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>His association with Liebig eventually became personal as well as professional. Both his first wife, Helene Moldenhauer (m. 12 August 1846), and his third wife, Elise Moldenhauer (m. 19 May 1866), were nieces of Liebig's wife, Henriette Moldenhauer.  Hofman reportedly courted Elise after Liebig's daughter Johanna refused him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brock_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brock-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>44, 318</span></sup>  In between, he married Rosamond Wilson (m. 13 December 1856), and later Bertha Tiemann (m. 11 August 1873)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Volhard_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Volhard-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackson2006_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jackson2006-10">[10]</a></sup> He had eleven children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Travis_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Travis-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=August_Wilhelm_von_Hofmann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Royal_College_of_Chemistry_in_London">Royal College of Chemistry in London</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=August_Wilhelm_von_Hofmann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Royal College of Chemistry in London">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Liebig_Company_Trading_Card_Ad_01.12.007_front.tif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="825" data-file-width="1308" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Liebig_Company_Trading_Card_Ad_01.12.007_front.tif/lossy-page1-250px-Liebig_Company_Trading_Card_Ad_01.12.007_front.tif.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Liebig_Company_Trading_Card_Ad_01.12.007_front.tif/lossy-page1-375px-Liebig_Company_Trading_Card_Ad_01.12.007_front.tif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Liebig_Company_Trading_Card_Ad_01.12.007_front.tif/lossy-page1-500px-Liebig_Company_Trading_Card_Ad_01.12.007_front.tif.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Liebig_Company_Trading_Card_Ad_01.12.007_front.tif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hofmann at the Inauguration of the School of Chemistry in London. Chimistes Celebres, <a href="/wiki/Liebig%27s_Extract_of_Meat_Company" title="Liebig's Extract of Meat Company">Liebig's Extract of Meat Company</a> Trading Card, 1929</div></div></div>
<p>As president of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> in London, <a href="/wiki/Albert,_Prince_Consort" title="Albert, Prince Consort">Albert, Prince Consort</a> to <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a>, was determined to foster scientific advancement in Britain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crowther1961_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crowther1961-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1845, he proposed to start a school of practical chemistry in London, under the style of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_College_of_Chemistry" title="Royal College of Chemistry">Royal College of Chemistry</a>.  Liebig was approached for advice, and recommended Hofmann to the directorship of the new institution.  Hofmann and the Prince met when Prince Albert, on a visit to his alma mater at Bonn, found his old rooms now occupied by Hofmann and his chemical apparatus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crowther1961_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Crowther1961-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1845 Hofmann was approached by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_James_Clark" title="Sir James Clark">Sir James Clark</a>, physician to Queen Victoria with the offer of the directorship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beer_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beer-12">[12]</a></sup> With the support of Prince Albert, and funding from a variety of private sources, the institution opened in 1845 with Hofmann as its first director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brock_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brock-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>112</span></sup>
</p><p>The financial position of the new institution was somewhat precarious.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beer_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Beer-12">[12]</a></sup> Hofmann accepted the position on the condition that he be appointed as extraordinary professor at Bonn, with leave of absence for two years, so that he could resume his career in Germany if the English appointment did not go well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911563_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911563-13">[13]</a></sup> The college opened in 1845 with 26 students at 16 Hanover Square, moving to cheaper premises at 299 Oxford Street in 1848.  Hofmann himself relinquished his free personal accommodation in Hanover Square and gave up part of his salary.  Despite this rocky start, the institution became successful for a time, and was an international leader in the development of aniline dyes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beer_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Beer-12">[12]</a></sup> Many of the men who studied there made significant contributions to chemical history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Griffith_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Griffith-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1853, the Royal College of Chemistry became part of the governmental Department of Science and Art, under the new School of Mines, putting it in a position to receive governmental funding on a somewhat more secure basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beer_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Beer-12">[12]</a></sup> However, with the death of Prince Albert in 1861, the institution lost one of its most significant supporters. Hofmann felt the loss deeply, writing in 1863, "[Albert's] early kindness exercised so powerful an influence upon the destinies of my existence. Year by year do I feel more deeply the debt of gratitude which I owe to him... it is to him, I feel, that I owe my opportunities through life."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brock_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Brock-9">[9]</a></sup> Without the Prince's encouragement, British government and industry lost interest in of science and technology. Hofmann's decision to return to Germany can be seen as a symptom of that decline, and with him gone, the Royal College of Chemistry lost its focus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beer_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-Beer-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Berlin_University">Berlin University</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=August_Wilhelm_von_Hofmann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Berlin University">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1864 Hofmann was offered a chair of chemistry at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bonn" title="University of Bonn">University of Bonn</a>, and another at the <a href="/wiki/Humboldt_University_of_Berlin" title="Humboldt University of Berlin">University of Berlin</a>. While taking his time to decide which offer to accept, Hofmann designed laboratory buildings for both universities, which were both subsequently built. In 1865 he succeeded <a href="/wiki/Eilhard_Mitscherlich" title="Eilhard Mitscherlich">Eilhard Mitscherlich</a> at the University of Berlin as professor of chemistry and director of the chemical laboratory. He held the position until his death in 1892. Following his return to Germany Hofmann was the principal founder of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Chemical_Society" title="German Chemical Society">German Chemical Society</a> (Deutsche Chemische Gesellschaft zu Berlin) (1867) and served 14 terms as its president.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB2014_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-EB2014-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contributions">Contributions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=August_Wilhelm_von_Hofmann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Contributions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hofmann's work covered a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organic_synthesis">Organic synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=August_Wilhelm_von_Hofmann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Organic synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hofmann was a major contributor to the development of techniques for organic synthesis, which originated at Liebig's laboratory in Giessen.  Hofmann and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=John_Blyth_(chemist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="John Blyth (chemist) (page does not exist)">John Blyth</a> were the first to use the term "synthesis", in their paper "On Styrole, and Some of the Products of Its Decomposition,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Styrole_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Styrole-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blyth1845_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blyth1845-16">[16]</a></sup> predating Kolbe's use of the term by some months. What Blyth and Hofmann called "synthesis" enabled them to make inferences about the constitution of styrole. A subsequent paper, <a href="/wiki/James_Sheridan_Muspratt" title="James Sheridan Muspratt">Muspratt</a> and Hofmann's "On Toluidine", described some of the first "synthetical experiments" (synthetische Versuche) in the field of organic chemistry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Toluidine_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Toluidine-17">[17]</a></sup> While the ultimate goal of such experiments was to artificially produce naturally occurring substances, such a goal was not practically attainable at the time.  The immediate purpose of the technique was the application of known reactions to a variety of materials to discover what products could be formed. Understanding a substance's method of formation was an important step in placing it within a developing taxonomy of substances. This technique became the basis of Hofmann's research program. He used organic synthesis as a method of investigation, to increase chemical understanding of reaction products and the processes by which they were formed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackson2014_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jackson2014-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coal_tar_and_anilines">Coal tar and anilines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=August_Wilhelm_von_Hofmann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Coal tar and anilines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hofmann's first research investigations, carried out in Liebig's laboratory at Giessen, was an examination of the organic bases of <a href="/wiki/Coal_tar" title="Coal tar">coal tar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alston_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alston-18">[18]</a></sup> Hofmann successfully isolated <i>Kyanol</i> and <i>Leucol</i>, bases previously reported by <a href="/wiki/Friedlieb_Ferdinand_Runge" title="Friedlieb Ferdinand Runge">Friedlieb Ferdinand Runge</a>, and showed that <i>Kyanol</i> was almost entirely aniline, previously shown to be a decomposition product of the plant dye <a href="/wiki/Indigo" title="Indigo">indigo</a>. In his first publication (1843) he demonstrated that a variety of substances which had been identified in contemporary chemical literature as obtainable from coal tar naphtha and its derivatives were all a single nitrogenous base, aniline. These included <i>Kyanol</i>, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Julius_Fritzsche" title="Carl Julius Fritzsche">Carl Julius Fritzsche</a>'s <i>Anilin</i>, <a href="/wiki/Otto_Unverdorben" title="Otto Unverdorben">Otto Unverdorben</a>'s <i>Krystallin</i>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nikolai_Zinin" title="Nikolai Zinin">Nikolai Zinin</a>'s <i>Benzidam</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackson2014_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jackson2014-8">[8]</a></sup> Much of his subsequent work further developed understanding of the natural alkaloids.
</p><p>Hofmann drew an analogy between aniline and <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>. He wanted to convince chemists that organic bases could be described in terms of derivatives of ammonia.  Hofmann successfully converted ammonia into <a href="/wiki/Ethylamine" title="Ethylamine">ethylamine</a> and the compounds <a href="/wiki/Diethylamine" title="Diethylamine">diethylamine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Triethylamine" title="Triethylamine">triethylamine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tetraethylammonium" title="Tetraethylammonium">tetraethylammonium</a>.  He was the first chemist to synthesize the quaternary amines. His method of converting an <a href="/wiki/Amide" title="Amide">amide</a> into an <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amine</a> is known as the <a href="/wiki/Hofmann_rearrangement" title="Hofmann rearrangement">Hofmann rearrangement</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alston_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Alston-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>While primary, secondary, and tertiary amines were stable when distilled at high temperatures under alkaline conditions, the quaternary amine was not. Heating quaternary tetraethylammonium hydroxide yielded tertiary triethylamine vapour. This became the basis of what is now known as the <a href="/wiki/Hofmann_elimination" title="Hofmann elimination">Hofmann elimination</a>, a method for converting quaternary amines into tertiary amines. Hofman successfully applied the method to <a href="/wiki/Coniine" title="Coniine">coniine</a>, the cholinergic poison of <a href="/wiki/Conium_maculatum" title="Conium maculatum">hemlock</a>, to derive the first structure of an alkaloid.  His method became extremely significant as a tool for examining the molecular structures of alkaloids, and was eventually applied to morphine, coca amine, atropine, and tubocurarine, among others. Coniine eventually became the first of the alkaloids to be artificially synthesised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alston_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-Alston-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1848, Hofmann's student <a href="/wiki/Charles_Blachford_Mansfield" title="Charles Blachford Mansfield">Charles Blachford Mansfield</a> developed a method of <a href="/wiki/Fractional_distillation" title="Fractional distillation">fractional distillation</a> of coal tar and separated out <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xylene" title="Xylene">xylene</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Toluene" title="Toluene">toluene</a>, an essential step towards the development of products from coal tar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB2014_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-EB2014-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McGrayne_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-McGrayne-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1856, Hofmann's student <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_William_Henry_Perkin" title="Sir William Henry Perkin">William Henry Perkin</a> was attempting to synthesize <a href="/wiki/Quinine" title="Quinine">quinine</a> at the Royal College of Chemistry in London, when he discovered the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aniline_dye" title="Aniline dye">aniline dye</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mauveine" title="Mauveine">mauveine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Perkin_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Perkin-20">[20]</a></sup> The discovery led to the creation of a wide range of artificially created colourful textile dyes, revolutionising the fashion world.  Hofmann's researches on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rosaniline" title="Rosaniline">rosaniline</a>, which he first prepared in 1858, were the beginning of a series of investigations on colouring matter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911563_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911563-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1863, Hofmann showed that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aniline_blue" title="Aniline blue">aniline blue</a> is a triphenyl derivative of rosaniline and discovered that different alkyl groups could be introduced into the rosaniline molecule to produce dyes of various purple or violet colours, which became known as 'Hofmann's violets'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Travis_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Travis-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1864, Hofmann confirmed that magenta can only be made by oxidation of commercial aniline in which isomeric orthotoluidine and paratoluidine are present as impurities, not from pure aniline.  Other students of Hofmann's who became involved in the British dyestuffs industry include <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Chambers_Nicholson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Edward Chambers Nicholson (page does not exist)">Edward Chambers Nicholson</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=George_Maule&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="George Maule (page does not exist)">George Maule</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=George_Simpson_(chemist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="George Simpson (chemist) (page does not exist)">George Simpson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mauve_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mauve-21">[21]</a></sup> After his return to Germany, Hofmann continued to experiment with dyestuffs, finally creating <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Quinoline_red&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Quinoline red (page does not exist)">quinoline red</a> in 1887.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB2014_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-EB2014-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hofmann studied nitrogen bases, including the development of methods for separating mixtures of amines and the preparation of large numbers of "polyammonias" (diamines and triamines such as ethylenediamine and diethylenediamine). He worked with <a href="/wiki/Auguste_Andr%C3%A9_Thomas_Cahours" title="Auguste André Thomas Cahours">Auguste Cahours</a> on phosphorus bases between 1855 and 1857.  With him, in 1857, Hofmann prepared the first aliphatic <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Unsaturated_alcohol&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Unsaturated alcohol (page does not exist)">unsaturated alcohol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Allyl_alcohol" title="Allyl alcohol">allyl alcohol</a>, C3 H5OH.  He also examined its derivative, <a href="/wiki/Allyl_isothiocyanate" title="Allyl isothiocyanate">allyl isothiocyanate</a> (mustard oil), in 1868, and studied various other isocyanates and isonitriles (isocyanides, or carbylamines).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB2014_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-EB2014-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hofmann also developed a method for determining the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_weights" title="Molecular weights">molecular weights</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquids" title="Liquids">liquids</a> from <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vapour_densities&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vapour densities (page does not exist)">vapour densities</a>.  Hofmann isolated <a href="/wiki/Sorbic_acid" title="Sorbic acid">sorbic acid</a> from rowanberries' oil in 1859, a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> that is widely used as a food <a href="/wiki/Preservative" title="Preservative">preservative</a>.
</p><p>In 1865, inspired by <a href="/wiki/Auguste_Laurent" title="Auguste Laurent">Auguste Laurent</a>, Hofmann suggested a systematic nomenclature for hydrocarbons and their derivatives. It was adopted internationally by the Geneva Congress, with some modifications, in 1892.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB2014_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-EB2014-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Molecular_models">Molecular models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=August_Wilhelm_von_Hofmann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Molecular models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Molecular_Model_of_Methane_Hofmann.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="341" data-file-width="256" height="213" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Molecular_Model_of_Methane_Hofmann.jpg/160px-Molecular_Model_of_Methane_Hofmann.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Molecular_Model_of_Methane_Hofmann.jpg/240px-Molecular_Model_of_Methane_Hofmann.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b3/Molecular_Model_of_Methane_Hofmann.jpg 2x" width="160"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Molecular_Model_of_Methane_Hofmann.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hofmann's methane model</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:127px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hofmann_voltameter-es.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="325" data-file-width="209" height="194" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Hofmann_voltameter-es.svg/125px-Hofmann_voltameter-es.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Hofmann_voltameter-es.svg/188px-Hofmann_voltameter-es.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Hofmann_voltameter-es.svg/250px-Hofmann_voltameter-es.svg.png 2x" width="125"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hofmann_voltameter-es.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hofmann voltameter</div></div></div>
<p>Hofmann was apparently the first to introduce molecular models into organic chemistry, following <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/August_Kekule" title="August Kekule">August Kekule</a>'s introduction of the theory of chemical structure in 1858, and <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Crum_Brown" title="Alexander Crum Brown">Alexander Crum Brown</a>'s introduction of printed structural formulas in 1861.  At a Friday Evening Discourse at London's Royal Institution on April 7, 1865, he displayed molecular models of simple organic substances such as methane, ethane, and methyl chloride, which he had had constructed from differently colored table croquet balls connected together with thin brass tubes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>  Hofmann's original colour scheme (<a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> = black, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> = white, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> = blue, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> = red, <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> = green, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulphur" title="Sulphur">sulphur</a> = yellow) has evolved into the <a href="/wiki/CPK_coloring" title="CPK coloring">CPK colour scheme</a> and is in use even today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>  After 1874, when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jacobus_van_%27t_Hoff" title="Jacobus van 't Hoff">van't Hoff</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Achille_Le_Bel" title="Achille Le Bel">Le Bel</a> independently suggested organic molecules can be three-dimensional, <a href="/wiki/Molecular_model" title="Molecular model">molecular models</a> began to assume their modern appearance.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hofmann_voltameter">Hofmann voltameter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=August_Wilhelm_von_Hofmann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Hofmann voltameter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Hofmann_voltameter" title="Hofmann voltameter">Hofmann voltameter</a> is an apparatus for <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water" title="Electrolysis of water">electrolyzing water</a>, invented by August Wilhelm von Hofmann in 1866.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> It consists of three joined upright cylinders, usually glass. The inner cylinder is open at the top to allow addition of water and an ionic compound to improve conductivity, such as a small amount of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulphuric_acid" title="Sulphuric acid">sulphuric acid</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a> electrode is placed inside the bottom of each of the two side cylinders, connected to the positive and negative terminals of a source of electricity. When <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">current</a> is run through Hofmann's Voltameter, gaseous <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> forms at the <a href="/wiki/Anode" title="Anode">anode</a> and gaseous <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Cathode" title="Cathode">cathode</a>. Each gas displaces water and collects at the top of the two outer tubes.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publications">Publications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=August_Wilhelm_von_Hofmann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Hofmann was multilingual and published extensively, particularly about his work on coal tar and its derivatives. In 1865 Hofmann published <i>An Introduction to Modern Chemistry</i>, summarising type theory and emerging ideas about chemical structure. Type theory modelled four inorganic molecules, hydrogen, hydrogen chloride, water, and ammonia, and used them as a basis for systematising and categorising both organic and inorganic compounds by exploring the substitution of one or more atoms of hydrogen for an equivalent atom or group. Hofmann himself had focused on researching ammonia, but discussed all four models in his book.  In it, he also first introduced the term <a href="/wiki/Valence_(chemistry)" title="Valence (chemistry)">valence</a>, under its longer variant quantivalence, to describe the combining capacity of an atom. His textbook strongly influenced introductory textbooks in both Europe and the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BASCalendar_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-BASCalendar-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition to his scientific works, Hofmann wrote biographical notices and essays on the history of chemistry, including a study of Liebig.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encyclopedia2008_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopedia2008-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_honours">Awards and honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=August_Wilhelm_von_Hofmann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Awards and honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hofmann-Denkmal_im_Hofmann-Haus_1900.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1116" data-file-width="588" height="418" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Hofmann-Denkmal_im_Hofmann-Haus_1900.jpg/220px-Hofmann-Denkmal_im_Hofmann-Haus_1900.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Hofmann-Denkmal_im_Hofmann-Haus_1900.jpg/330px-Hofmann-Denkmal_im_Hofmann-Haus_1900.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Hofmann-Denkmal_im_Hofmann-Haus_1900.jpg/440px-Hofmann-Denkmal_im_Hofmann-Haus_1900.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hofmann-Denkmal_im_Hofmann-Haus_1900.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Monument to Hofmann at Berlin, destroyed in 1944 by British air raid</div></div></div>
<p>He was elected a <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Society">fellow of the Royal Society</a> in 1851. He was awarded the society's <a href="/wiki/Royal_Medal" title="Royal Medal">Royal Medal</a> in 1854 and their <a href="/wiki/Copley_Medal" title="Copley Medal">Copley Medal</a> in 1875.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> On his 70th birthday, in 1888, he was ennobled, enabling him to add the prefix "von" before his last name.
</p><p>In 1900, the German Chemical Society built the "Hofmann-Haus" at Berlin and in 1902 created the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=August_Wilhelm_von_Hofmann_Gold_Medal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="August Wilhelm von Hofmann Gold Medal (page does not exist)">August Wilhelm von Hofmann Gold Medal</a> in his honour, to be awarded for outstanding achievements in chemistry. The first recipients were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_William_Ramsay" title="Sir William Ramsay">Sir William Ramsay</a> of England and Professor <a href="/wiki/Henri_Moissan" title="Henri Moissan">Henri Moissan</a> of Paris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gray1903_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gray1903-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=August_Wilhelm_von_Hofmann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hofmann died in 1892 and was buried in Berlin's Friedhof der Dorotheenstädtischen und Friedrichswerderschen Gemeinden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
