<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Peat</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Peat</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span></span>/</a></span></span>), also known as <b>turf</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ɜːr/: 'ur' in 'fur'">ɜːr</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span></span>/</a></span></span>), is an accumulation of partially <a href="/wiki/Decomposition" title="Decomposition">decayed</a> <a href="/wiki/Vegetation" title="Vegetation">vegetation</a> or organic matter. It is unique to natural areas called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peatland" title="Peatland">peatlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mire" title="Mire">mires</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moorland" title="Moorland">moors,</a> or <a href="/wiki/Muskeg" title="Muskeg">muskegs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joosten_Clarke2002_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Joosten_Clarke2002-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PERG2013_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-PERG2013-2">[2]</a></sup> The peatland ecosystem is the most efficient <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sink" title="Carbon sink">carbon sink</a> on the planet,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PERG2013_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-PERG2013-2">[2]</a></sup> because peatland plants capture <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> naturally released from the peat, maintaining an equilibrium. In natural peatlands, the "annual rate of biomass production is greater than the rate of decomposition", but it takes "thousands of years for peatlands to develop the deposits of 1.5 to 2.3 m [4.9 to 7.5 ft], which is the average depth of the boreal [northern] peatlands".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PERG2013_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-PERG2013-2">[2]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Sphagnum" title="Sphagnum">Sphagnum</a></i> moss, also called peat moss, is one of the most common components in peat, although many other plants can contribute. Soils consisting primarily of peat are known as <a href="/wiki/Histosol" title="Histosol">histosols</a>. Peat forms in <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetland</a> conditions, where flooding obstructs the flow of oxygen from the atmosphere, slowing the rate of decomposition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Peatlands, particularly <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bogs</a>, are the primary source of peat,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gorham,_E._1957_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gorham,_E._1957-4">[4]</a></sup>
although less-common wetlands including <a href="/wiki/Fen" title="Fen">fens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pocosin" title="Pocosin">pocosins</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Peat_swamp_forest" title="Peat swamp forest">peat swamp forests</a> also deposit peat. Landscapes covered in peat are home to specific kinds of plants including <i>Sphagnum</i> moss, <a href="/wiki/Ericaceae" title="Ericaceae">ericaceous</a> shrubs, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sedges" title="Sedges">sedges</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bog</a> for more information on this aspect of peat). Because organic matter accumulates over thousands of years, peat deposits provide records of past vegetation and climate by preserving plant remains, such as pollen. This allows humans to reconstruct past environments and study changes in human land use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Peat is harvested as an important source of <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a> in certain parts of the world. By volume, there are about 4 trillion cubic metres (5.2 trillion cubic yards) of peat in the world, covering a total of around 2% of the global land area (about 3 million square kilometres or 1.2 million square miles), containing about 8 billion <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terajoule" title="Terajoule">terajoules</a> of energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Over time, the formation of peat is often the first step in the geological formation of other <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, particularly low-grade coal such as <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Depending on the agency, peat is not generally regarded as a <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable source of energy</a>, due to its extraction rate in industrialized countries far exceeding its slow regrowth rate of 1mm per year,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keddy,_P.A_2010_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Keddy,_P.A_2010-8">[8]</a></sup> and as it is also reported that peat regrowth takes place only in 30-40% of peatlands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Because of this, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNFCCC" title="UNFCCC">UNFCCC</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and another organization affiliated with the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> classified peat as a <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> However, the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC) has begun to classify peat as a "slow-renewable" fuel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This is also the classification used by many in the peat industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>  At 106 g CO<sub>2</sub>/<a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">MJ</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> the carbon dioxide <a href="/wiki/Emission_intensity" title="Emission intensity">emission intensity</a> of peat is higher than that of coal (at 94.6 g CO<sub>2</sub>/MJ) and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> (at 56.1) (IPCC).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Peat forms when plant material does not fully decay in acidic and anaerobic conditions. It is composed mainly of wetland vegetation: principally bog plants including mosses, sedges, and shrubs. As it accumulates, the peat holds water. This slowly creates wetter conditions that allow the area of wetland to expand. Peatland features can include ponds, ridges, and raised bogs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gorham,_E._1957_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gorham,_E._1957-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Most modern peat bogs formed 12,000 years ago in high latitudes after the glaciers retreated at the end of the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last ice age</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Peat usually accumulates slowly at the rate of about a millimetre per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keddy,_P.A_2010_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Keddy,_P.A_2010-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_peat_material">Types of peat material</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types of peat material">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Peat material is either fibric, hemic, or sapric. Fibric peats are the least decomposed and consist of intact fibre. Hemic peats are partially decomposed and sapric are the most decomposed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Phragmites" title="Phragmites">Phragmites</a></i> peat are composed of reed grass, <i>Phragmites australis</i>, and other grasses. It is denser than many other types of peat.
</p><p>Engineers may describe a soil as peat which has a relatively high percentage of organic material. This soil is problematic because it exhibits poor <a href="/wiki/Consolidation_(soil)" title="Consolidation (soil)">consolidation</a> properties – it cannot be easily compacted to serve as a stable foundation to support loads, such as roads or buildings.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Peatlands_distribution">Peatlands distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Peatlands distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a widely cited article, Joosten and Clarke (2002) defined peatlands or mires (which they claim are the same)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[Notes 1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joosten_Clarke2002_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Joosten_Clarke2002-1">[1]</a></sup> as,
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>...the most widespread of all wetland types in the world, representing 50 to 70% of global wetlands. They cover over 4 million square kilometres [1.5 million square miles] or 3% of the land and freshwater surface of the planet. In these ecosystems are found one third of the world’s soil carbon and 10% of global freshwater resources. These ecosystems are characterized by the unique ability to accumulate and store dead organic matter from Sphagnum and many other non-moss species, as peat, under conditions of almost permanent water saturation. Peatlands are adapted to the extreme conditions of high water and low oxygen content, of toxic elements and low availability of plant nutrients. Their water chemistry varies from alkaline to acidic. Peatlands occur on all continents, from the tropical to boreal and Arctic zones from sea level to high alpine conditions.</p><div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>Joosten and Clarke 2002</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Peatlands are areas of land with naturally formed layers of peat. They can be found in at least 175 countries and cover around 4 million square kilometres (1.5 million square miles); that is 3% of the world’s land area. In Europe, peatlands extend to about 515,000 km<sup>2</sup> (199,000 sq mi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUCNFull_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-IUCNFull-18">[17]</a></sup> About 60% of the world's <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a> are made of peat.
</p><p>Peat deposits are found in many places around the world, including northern Europe and North America. The North American peat deposits are principally found in Canada and the Northern United States. Some of the world's largest peatlands include the <a href="/wiki/West_Siberian_taiga" title="West Siberian taiga">West Siberian Lowland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Bay_Lowlands" title="Hudson Bay Lowlands">Hudson Bay Lowlands</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Mackenzie_River" title="Mackenzie River">Mackenzie River</a> Valley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fraser,_L.H_2005_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fraser,_L.H_2005-19">[18]</a></sup>
There is less peat in the Southern Hemisphere, in part because there is less land. That said, the vast <a href="/wiki/Magellanic_subpolar_forests#Flora" title="Magellanic subpolar forests">Magellanic Moorland</a> in South America (Southern <a href="/wiki/Patagonia" title="Patagonia">Patagonia</a>/<a href="/wiki/Tierra_del_Fuego" title="Tierra del Fuego">Tierra del Fuego</a>) is an extensive peat-dominated landscape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fraser,_L.H_2005_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fraser,_L.H_2005-19">[18]</a></sup> Peat can be found in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kerguelen_Islands" title="Kerguelen Islands">Kerguelen</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Kalimantan" title="Kalimantan">Kalimantan</a> [Sungai Putri, Danau Siawan, Sungai Tolak], Rasau Jaya [West Kalimantan], and Sumatra). Indonesia has more tropical peatlands and mangrove forests than any other nation on earth, but Indonesia is losing wetlands by 100,000 hectares (250,000 acres) per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>About 7% of all peatlands have been exploited for <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Forestry" title="Forestry">forestry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> Under proper conditions, peat will turn into <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a> coal over geologic periods of time.
</p>

<p>Under pressure, water is forced out of peat, which is soft and easily compressed, and once dry can be used as fuel. In many countries, including <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, peat has traditionally been used for cooking and domestic heating, and peat is stacked to dry in rural areas. It remains harvested on an industrial scale for this purpose in countries such as Ireland and Finland. Its insulating properties make it useful in industry.
</p><p>Although humans have many uses for peat, it presents severe problems at times. Wet or dry, it can be a major fire hazard. Peat fires may burn for great lengths of time, or smoulder underground and reignite after winter if an oxygen source is present. Because they are easily compressed under minimal weight, peat deposits pose major difficulties to builders of structures, roads, and railways. When the <a href="/wiki/West_Highland_Line" title="West Highland Line">West Highland railway line</a> was built across <a href="/wiki/Rannoch_Moor" title="Rannoch Moor">Rannoch Moor</a> in western Scotland, its builders had to float the tracks on a multi-thousand-ton mattress of tree roots, brushwood, earth and ash.
</p><p>In the Bronze and Iron Ages, people used peat bogs for rituals to nature gods and spirits.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Bodies of the victims of such sacrifices have been found in various places in Scotland, England, Ireland, and especially northern Germany and Denmark. They are almost perfectly preserved by the <a href="/wiki/Tanning_(leather)" title="Tanning (leather)">tanning</a> properties of the acidic water (see <a href="/wiki/Tollund_Man" title="Tollund Man">Tollund Man</a> for one of the most famous examples of a <a href="/wiki/Bog_body" title="Bog body">bog body</a>). Peat wetlands also used to have a degree of <a href="/wiki/Metallurgy" title="Metallurgy">metallurgical</a> importance, being during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Dark Ages</a> the primary source of <a href="/wiki/Bog_iron" title="Bog iron">bog iron</a> that was used to create swords and armour for <a href="/wiki/Vikings" title="Vikings">Vikings</a>. Many peat swamps along the coast of <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> serve as a natural means of flood mitigation, with any overflow being absorbed by the peat, provided forests are still present to prevent peat fires.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics_and_uses_by_nation">Characteristics and uses by nation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Characteristics and uses by nation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Finland">Finland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Finland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Peat_energy_in_Finland" title="Peat energy in Finland">Peat energy in Finland</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Toppila_power_plant.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2031" data-file-width="2048" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Toppila_power_plant.JPG/220px-Toppila_power_plant.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Toppila_power_plant.JPG/330px-Toppila_power_plant.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Toppila_power_plant.JPG/440px-Toppila_power_plant.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Toppila_power_plant.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Toppila_Power_Station" title="Toppila Power Station">Toppila Power Station</a>, a peat-fired facility in <a href="/wiki/Oulu" title="Oulu">Oulu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a></div></div></div>
<p>The climate, geography, and environment of <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> favours bog and peat bog formation. Thus, peat is available in considerable quantities. This abundant resource (often mixed with wood at an average of 2.6%) is burned to produce <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>. Peat provides around 6.2% of Finland's annual energy production, second only to Ireland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> The contribution of peat to <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions of Finland can exceed 10 million metric tonnes of carbon dioxide per year – equal to the total emissions of all passenger-car traffic in Finland.
</p><p>Finland classifies peat as a slowly renewing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biomass_fuel" title="Biomass fuel">biomass fuel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup> Peat producers in Finland often claim that peat is a special form of <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuel</a> because of the relatively fast retake rate of released <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> if the bog is not forested for the following 100 years. Also, agricultural and forestry-drained peat bogs actively release more CO<sub>2</sub> annually than is released in peat energy production in Finland. The average regrowth rate of a single peat bog, however, is indeed slow, from 1,000 up to 5,000 years. Furthermore, it is a common practice to forest used peat bogs instead of giving them a chance to renew. This leads to lower levels of CO<sub>2</sub> storage than the original peat bog.
</p><p>At 106 g CO<sub>2</sub>/<a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">MJ</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imcg.net_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-imcg.net-24">[23]</a></sup> the carbon dioxide emissions of peat are higher than those of coal (at 94.6 g CO<sub>2</sub>/MJ) and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> (at 56.1). According to one study, increasing the average amount of wood in the fuel mixture from the current 2.6% to 12.5% would take the emissions down to 93 g CO<sub>2</sub>/MJ. That said, little effort is being made to achieve this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Peat extraction is also seen by some conservationists<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (July 2009)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> as the main threat to mire <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> in Finland. The International Mire Conservation Group (IMCG) in 2006 urged the local and national governments of Finland to protect and conserve the remaining pristine peatland ecosystems. This includes the cessation of drainage and peat extraction in intact mire sites and the abandoning of current and planned groundwater extraction that may affect these sites. A proposal for a Finnish peatland management strategy was presented to the government in 2011, after a lengthy consultation phase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ireland">Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BordnaMona_2930.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2167" data-file-width="3872" height="123" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/BordnaMona_2930.jpg/220px-BordnaMona_2930.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/BordnaMona_2930.jpg/330px-BordnaMona_2930.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/BordnaMona_2930.jpg/440px-BordnaMona_2930.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BordnaMona_2930.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Industrial-milled peat production in a section of the <a href="/wiki/Bog_of_Allen" title="Bog of Allen">Bog of Allen</a> in the Irish Midlands: The 'turf' in the foreground is machine-produced for domestic use.</div></div></div>
<p>In Ireland, large-scale domestic and industrial peat usage is widespread. In the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>, a state-owned company called <i lang="ga" title="Irish language text"><a href="/wiki/Bord_na_M%C3%B3na" title="Bord na Móna">Bord na Móna</a></i> is responsible for managing peat extraction. It processes the extracted peat into milled peat which is used in power stations and sells processed peat fuel in the form of peat <a href="/wiki/Briquette" title="Briquette">briquettes</a> which are used for domestic heating. These are oblong bars of densely compressed, dried, and shredded peat. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peat_moss" title="Peat moss">Peat moss</a> is a manufactured product for use in garden cultivation. Turf (dried out peat <a href="/wiki/Sod" title="Sod">sods</a>) is also commonly used in rural areas.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Russia">Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Russia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Shatura_steam_power_plant_(2010).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Shatura_steam_power_plant_%282010%29.jpg/220px-Shatura_steam_power_plant_%282010%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Shatura_steam_power_plant_%282010%29.jpg/330px-Shatura_steam_power_plant_%282010%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Shatura_steam_power_plant_%282010%29.jpg/440px-Shatura_steam_power_plant_%282010%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Shatura_steam_power_plant_(2010).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Shatura_Power_Station" title="Shatura Power Station">Shatura Power Station</a>. <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> has the largest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_peat_power_stations" title="List of peat power stations">peat power</a> capacity in the world</div></div></div>
<p>Use of peat for energy production was prominent in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, especially in 1965. In 1929, over 40% of the Soviet Union's electric energy came from peat, which dropped to 1% by 1980.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Peat_Briquette_Factory.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Peat_Briquette_Factory.jpg/220px-Peat_Briquette_Factory.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Peat_Briquette_Factory.jpg/330px-Peat_Briquette_Factory.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Peat_Briquette_Factory.jpg/440px-Peat_Briquette_Factory.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Peat_Briquette_Factory.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kerzhenets_peat_narrow_gauge_railway" title="Kerzhenets peat narrow gauge railway">Bor Peat Briquette Factory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a></div></div></div>
<p>In the 1960s, larger sections of swamps and bogs in Western Russia were drained for agricultural and mining purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wildfire_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-wildfire-27">[26]</a></sup> Plans are underway to increase peat output and increase peat's contribution to Russian energy generation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-treehugger_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-treehugger-28">[27]</a></sup> There is concern about the environmental impact as peat fields are flammable, drainage degrades <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>, and burning of peat releases carbon dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-treehugger_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-treehugger-28">[27]</a></sup> Due to <a href="/wiki/2010_Russian_wildfires" title="2010 Russian wildfires">2010 forest and peat fires</a>, the Russian government is under heavy pressure to finance re-flooding of the previously drained bogs around Moscow. The initial costs for the programme are estimated to be about 20 to 25 billion rubles; that is close to 500 million euros (540 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USD" title="USD">USD</a>).
</p><p>Currently, Russia is responsible for 17% of the world's peat production and 20% of that peat (1.5 million tons) is used for energy purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Energy_survey_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Energy_survey-29">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RGS_Peat_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-RGS_Peat-30">[29]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Shatura_Power_Station" title="Shatura Power Station">Shatura Power Station</a> in <a href="/wiki/Moscow_Oblast" title="Moscow Oblast">Moscow Oblast</a> and Kirov Power Station in <a href="/wiki/Kirov_Oblast" title="Kirov Oblast">Kirov Oblast</a> are the two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_peat_power_stations" title="List of peat power stations">largest peat power stations</a> in the world.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Netherlands">The Netherlands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The Netherlands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:500vc_ex_leg_copy.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="616" data-file-width="510" height="266" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/500vc_ex_leg_copy.jpg/220px-500vc_ex_leg_copy.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/500vc_ex_leg_copy.jpg/330px-500vc_ex_leg_copy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/500vc_ex_leg_copy.jpg/440px-500vc_ex_leg_copy.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:500vc_ex_leg_copy.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Peat covered area (brown) 2500 years BP in the Netherlands</div></div></div><p>2500 years BP the area now named the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> was largely covered with peat. Drainage, causing compaction and oxidation and excavation have reduced peatlands to about 3,356 km<sup>2</sup> (1,296 sq mi) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup> or 10% of the land area, mostly used as meadows. Drainage and excavation have lowered the surface of the peatlands. In the west of the country dikes and mills were built, creating <a href="/wiki/Polder" title="Polder">polders</a> so that dwelling and economic activities could <a href="/wiki/Flood_control_in_the_Netherlands" title="Flood control in the Netherlands">continue</a> below sea level. The first polder was in the 11th century, and the last in <a href="/wiki/Flevopolder" title="Flevopolder">1968</a>. Harvesting of peat could continue in suitable locations as the lower peat layers below current sea level became exposed. They were deposited before the rise of the sea level in the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a>. As a result approximate 26% of its area <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup> and 21% of its population <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup> of the Netherlands are presently below sea level. The deepest point is in the <a href="/wiki/Zuidplaspolder" title="Zuidplaspolder">Zuidplaspolder</a>, 6.76 m (22.2 ft) <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam_Ordnance_Datum" title="Amsterdam Ordnance Datum">below average sea level</a>. </p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Netherlands_compared_to_sealevel.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1003" data-file-width="962" height="229" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/The_Netherlands_compared_to_sealevel.png/220px-The_Netherlands_compared_to_sealevel.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/The_Netherlands_compared_to_sealevel.png/330px-The_Netherlands_compared_to_sealevel.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/The_Netherlands_compared_to_sealevel.png/440px-The_Netherlands_compared_to_sealevel.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Netherlands_compared_to_sealevel.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Netherlands compared to sealevel</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="England">England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Peat_extraction_on_the_Somerset_Levels" title="Peat extraction on the Somerset Levels">extraction of peat</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Somerset_Levels" title="Somerset Levels">Somerset Levels</a> began during the Roman times and has been carried out since the Levels were first drained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Dartmoor" title="Dartmoor">Dartmoor</a>, there were several commercial distillation plants formed and run by the British Patent Naphtha Company in 1844. These produced <a href="/wiki/Naphtha" title="Naphtha">naphtha</a> on a commercial scale from the high-quality local peat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fenn%27s,_Whixall_and_Bettisfield_Mosses" title="Fenn's, Whixall and Bettisfield Mosses">Fenn's, Whixall and Bettisfield Mosses</a> is an element of a post-<a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">Ice Age</a> peat bog that straddles the England–<a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> border and contains many rare plant and animal species due to the acidic environment created by the peat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup> Only lightly hand-dug, it is now a <a href="/wiki/National_nature_reserve_(United_Kingdom)" title="National nature reserve (United Kingdom)">national nature reserve</a> and is being restored to its natural condition.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_Ireland">Northern Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Northern Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, there is small-scale domestic turf cutting in rural areas, but areas of bogs have been diminished because of changes in agriculture. In response, <a href="/wiki/Afforestation" title="Afforestation">afforestation</a> has seen the establishment of tentative steps towards conservation such as <a href="/wiki/Peatlands_Park" title="Peatlands Park">Peatlands Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Armagh" title="County Armagh">County Armagh</a> which is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Area_of_Special_Scientific_Interest" title="Area of Special Scientific Interest">Area of Special Scientific Interest</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Scotland">Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Some <a href="/wiki/Scotch_whisky" title="Scotch whisky">Scotch whisky</a> distilleries, such as those on <a href="/wiki/Islay" title="Islay">Islay</a>, use peat fires to dry malted <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a>. The drying process takes about 30 hours. This gives the whiskies a distinctive smoky flavour, often called "peatiness".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup>
The peatiness, or degree of peat flavour, of a whisky, is calculated in <a href="/wiki/Parts-per_notation#ppm" title="Parts-per notation">ppm</a> of <a href="/wiki/Phenol" title="Phenol">phenol</a>. Normal Highland whiskies have a peat level of up to 30 ppm, and the whiskies on Islay usually have up to 50 ppm. In rare types like the <a href="/wiki/Bruichladdich_distillery" title="Bruichladdich distillery">Octomore</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup> the whisky can have more than 100 ppm of phenol. Scotch Ales can also use peat roasted malt, imparting a similar smoked flavor.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Generic_characteristics_and_uses">Generic characteristics and uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Generic characteristics and uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">Agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Sweden, farmers use dried peat to absorb excrement from cattle that are wintered indoors. The most important property of peat is retaining moisture in container soil when it is dry while preventing the excess of water from killing roots when it is wet. Peat can store <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrients</a> although it is not fertile itself – it is <a href="/wiki/Polyelectrolyte" title="Polyelectrolyte">polyelectrolytic</a> with a high ion-exchange capacity due to its oxidized lignin. Peat is discouraged as a soil amendment by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Botanic_Gardens,_Kew" title="Royal Botanic Gardens, Kew">Royal Botanic Gardens, Kew</a>, England, since 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup> While bark-based peat-free potting soil mixes are on the rise, particularly in the U.K., peat remains an important raw material for <a href="/wiki/Horticulture" title="Horticulture">horticulture</a> in Canada, as well as parts of the United States. However, it is recommended to treat peat thermally, e.g., through <a href="/wiki/Soil_steam_sterilization" title="Soil steam sterilization">soil steaming</a> in order to kill pests and reactivate nutrients.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Freshwater_aquaria">Freshwater aquaria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Freshwater aquaria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Peat is sometimes used in freshwater <a href="/wiki/Aquarium" title="Aquarium">aquaria</a>. It is seen most commonly in soft water or <a href="/wiki/Blackwater_river" title="Blackwater river">blackwater river</a> systems such as those mimicking the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon River</a> basin. In addition to being soft in texture and therefore suitable for demersal (bottom-dwelling) species such as <i><a href="/wiki/Corydoras" title="Corydoras">Corydoras</a></i> catfish, peat is reported to have a number of other beneficial functions in freshwater aquaria. It softens water by acting as an <a href="/wiki/Ion_exchange" title="Ion exchange">ion exchanger</a>; it also contains substances that are beneficial for plants, and for the reproductive health of fishes. Peat can prevent algae growth and kill microorganisms. Peat often stains the water yellow or brown due to the leaching of <a href="/wiki/Tannin" title="Tannin">tannins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_filtration">Water filtration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Water filtration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Peat is used in water filtration, such as for the treatment of septic tank effluent and as for urban runoff.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Balneotherapy">Balneotherapy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Balneotherapy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Peat is widely used in <a href="/wiki/Balneotherapy" title="Balneotherapy">balneotherapy</a> (the use of bathing to treat disease). Many traditional spa treatments include peat as part of <a href="/wiki/Peloid" title="Peloid">peloids</a>. Such health treatments have an enduring tradition in European countries including Poland, the Czech Republic, Germany, and Austria. Some of these old spas date back to the 18th century and are still active today. The most common types of peat application in balneotherapy are peat <a href="/wiki/Mud_bath" title="Mud bath">muds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poultice" title="Poultice">poultices</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Suspension_bath&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Suspension bath (page does not exist)">suspension baths</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Peat_archives">Peat archives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Peat archives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Authors Rydin and Jeglum in <i>Biology of Habitats</i> described the concept of peat archives, a phrase coined by influential peatland scientist <a href="/wiki/Harry_Godwin" title="Harry Godwin">Harry Godwin</a> in 1981.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rydin_2013_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rydin_2013-44">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keddy_2010_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Keddy_2010-45">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>In a peat profile there is a fossilized record of changes over time in the vegetation, pollen, spores, animals (from microscopic to the giant elk), and archaeological remains that have been deposited in place, as well as pollen, spores and particles brought in by wind and weather. These remains are collectively termed the peat archives.</p><div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>Rydin, 2013</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>In <i>Quaternary Palaeoecology</i>, first published in 1980, Birks and Birks described how <a href="/wiki/Paleoecology" title="Paleoecology">paleoecological</a> studies "of peat can be used to reveal what plant communities were present (locally and regionally), what time period each community occupied, how environmental conditions changed, and how the environment affected the ecosystem in that time and place."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rydin_2013_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rydin_2013-44">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Birks_1980_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Birks_1980-46">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>Scientists continue to compare modern <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> (Hg) accumulation rates in bogs with historical natural-archives records in peat bogs and lake sediments to estimate the potential human impacts on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biogeochemical" title="Biogeochemical">biogeochemical</a> cycle of mercury, for example.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biester_Bindler_2009_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Biester_Bindler_2009-47">[46]</a></sup> Over the years, different dating models and technologies for measuring date sediments and peat profiles accumulated over the last 100–150 years, have been used, including the widely used vertical distribution of 210Pb, the <a href="/wiki/Inductively_coupled_plasma_mass_spectrometry" title="Inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry">inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry</a> (ICP-SMS),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup> and more recently the initial penetration (IP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Peat_haggs_at_start_of_Allt_Lagan_a%27_Bhainne_tributary_on_Eilrig_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1420692.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Peat_haggs_at_start_of_Allt_Lagan_a%27_Bhainne_tributary_on_Eilrig_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1420692.jpg/220px-Peat_haggs_at_start_of_Allt_Lagan_a%27_Bhainne_tributary_on_Eilrig_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1420692.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Peat_haggs_at_start_of_Allt_Lagan_a%27_Bhainne_tributary_on_Eilrig_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1420692.jpg/330px-Peat_haggs_at_start_of_Allt_Lagan_a%27_Bhainne_tributary_on_Eilrig_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1420692.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Peat_haggs_at_start_of_Allt_Lagan_a%27_Bhainne_tributary_on_Eilrig_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1420692.jpg/440px-Peat_haggs_at_start_of_Allt_Lagan_a%27_Bhainne_tributary_on_Eilrig_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1420692.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Peat_haggs_at_start_of_Allt_Lagan_a%27_Bhainne_tributary_on_Eilrig_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1420692.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Peat hags at the start of Allt Lagan a' Bhainne tributary on Eilrig</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Peat_hags">Peat hags</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Peat hags">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Peat "hags" are a form of erosion that occurs at the sides of gullies that cut into the peat or, sometimes, in isolation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YPP_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-YPP-50">[49]</a></sup> Hags may result when flowing water cuts downwards into the peat and when fire or overgrazing exposes the peat surface. Once the peat is exposed in these ways, it is prone to further erosion by wind, water, and livestock. The result is overhanging vegetation and peat. Hags are too steep and unstable for vegetation to establish itself, so they continue to erode unless restorative action is taken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YPP_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-YPP-50">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<div style="clear:right;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_and_ecological_issues">Environmental and ecological issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Environmental and ecological issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Co2.recent.ch.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="447" data-file-width="585" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/48/Co2.recent.ch.png/220px-Co2.recent.ch.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/48/Co2.recent.ch.png/330px-Co2.recent.ch.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/48/Co2.recent.ch.png/440px-Co2.recent.ch.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Co2.recent.ch.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Increase, and change relative to previous year, of the atmospheric concentration of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The distinctive ecological conditions of peat wetlands provide a habitat for distinctive fauna and flora. For example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whooping_cranes" title="Whooping cranes">whooping cranes</a> nest in North American peatlands, while <a href="/wiki/Siberian_crane" title="Siberian crane">Siberian cranes</a> nest in the West Siberian peatland. Such habitats also have many species of wild orchids and carnivorous plants. It takes centuries for a peat bog to recover from disturbance. (For more on biological communities, see <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bog</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fen" title="Fen">fen</a>.)
</p><p>The world's largest peat bog is located in Western Siberia. It is the size of France and Germany combined. Recent studies show that it is thawing for the first time in 11,000 years. As the permafrost melts, it could release billions of tonnes of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> gas into the atmosphere. The world's peatlands are thought to contain 180 to 455 billion <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">metric tonnes</a> of sequestered carbon, and they release into the atmosphere 20 to 45 million metric tons of methane annually. The peatlands' contribution to long-term fluctuations in these atmospheric gases has been a matter of considerable debate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>One of the characteristics for peat is the bioaccumulations of metals often concentrated in the peat. Accumulated mercury is of significant environmental concern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[51]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Peat_drainage">Peat drainage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Peat drainage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Large areas of organic wetland (peat) soils are currently drained for agriculture, forestry, and peat extraction. This process is taking place all over the world. This not only destroys the habitat of many species but also heavily fuels climate change.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> As a result of peat drainage, the organic carbon – which built over thousands of years and is normally underwater – is suddenly exposed to the air. It decomposes and turns into <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>), which is released into the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[52]</a></sup> The global CO<sub>2</sub> emissions from drained peatlands have increased from 1,058 Mton in 1990 to 1,298 Mton in 2008 (a 20% increase). This increase has particularly taken place in developing countries, of which Indonesia, China, Malaysia, and Papua New Guinea are the fastest-growing top emitters. This estimate excludes emissions from peat fires (conservative estimates amount to at least 4,000 Mton/CO<sub>2</sub>-eq./yr for south-east Asia). With 174 Mton/CO<sub>2</sub>-eq./yr the EU is after Indonesia (500 Mton) and before Russia (161 Mton) the world's second-largest emitter of drainage-related peatland CO<sub>2</sub> (excl. extracted peat and fires). Total CO<sub>2</sub> emissions from the worldwide 500,000 km<sup>2</sup> of degraded peatland may exceed 2.0 Gtons (including emissions from peat fires) which is almost 6% of all global carbon emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Peat_fires"><span id="Fires"></span><span id="Peat_Fires"></span> Peat fires</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Peat fires">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slash_and_burn" title="Slash and burn">Slash and burn</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_methane_release" title="Arctic methane release">Arctic methane release</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TOMS_indonesia_smog_lrg.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="801" data-file-width="1234" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/TOMS_indonesia_smog_lrg.jpg/220px-TOMS_indonesia_smog_lrg.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/TOMS_indonesia_smog_lrg.jpg/330px-TOMS_indonesia_smog_lrg.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/TOMS_indonesia_smog_lrg.jpg/440px-TOMS_indonesia_smog_lrg.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TOMS_indonesia_smog_lrg.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Smoke and ozone pollution from <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesian</a> fires, 1997</div></div></div>
<p>Peat has a high carbon content and can burn under low moisture conditions. Once ignited by the presence of a heat source (e.g., a wildfire penetrating the subsurface), it <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smolder" title="Smolder">smoulders</a>. These smouldering fires can burn undetected for very long periods of time (months, years, and even centuries) propagating in a creeping fashion through the underground peat layer.
</p><p>Despite the damage that the burning of raw peat can cause, bogs are naturally subject to wildfires and depend on the wildfires to keep woody competition from lowering the water table and shading out many bog plants. Several families of plants including the carnivorous <i><a href="/wiki/Sarracenia" title="Sarracenia">Sarracenia</a></i> (trumpet pitcher), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dionaea_(plant)" title="Dionaea (plant)"><i>Dionaea</i></a> (Venus flytrap), <i><a href="/wiki/Utricularia" title="Utricularia">Utricularia</a></i> (bladderworts) and non-carnivorous plants such as the <a href="/wiki/Lilium_pyrophilum" title="Lilium pyrophilum">sandhills lily</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ctenium_aromaticum" title="Ctenium aromaticum">toothache grass</a> and many species of orchid are now threatened and in some cases endangered from the combined forces of human drainage, negligence, and absence of fire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>The recent burning of peat bogs in Indonesia, with their large and deep growths containing more than 50 billion tonnes of carbon, has contributed to increases in world <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> levels.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Peat deposits in Southeast Asia could be destroyed by 2040.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is estimated that in 1997, <a href="/wiki/1997_Southeast_Asian_haze" title="1997 Southeast Asian haze">peat and forest fires in Indonesia</a> released between 0.81 and 2.57 Gt of carbon; equivalent to 13–40 percent of the amount released by global fossil fuel burning, and greater than the carbon uptake of the world's biosphere. These fires may be responsible for the acceleration in the increase in carbon dioxide levels since 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[60]</a></sup> More than 100 peat fires in <a href="/wiki/Kalimantan" title="Kalimantan">Kalimantan</a> and East <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a> have continued to burn since 1997; each year, these peat fires ignite new forest fires above the ground.
</p><p>In North America, peat fires can occur during severe droughts throughout their occurrence, from boreal forests in Canada to swamps and fens in the subtropical southern Florida <a href="/wiki/Everglades" title="Everglades">Everglades</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[61]</a></sup> Once a fire has burnt through the area, hollows in the peat are burnt out, and hummocks are desiccated but can contribute to <i>Sphagnum</i> recolonization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[62]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the summer of 2010, an unusually high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2010_Northern_Hemisphere_summer_heat_wave" title="2010 Northern Hemisphere summer heat wave">heat wave</a> of up to 40 °C (104 °F) ignited large deposits of peat in Central Russia, <a href="/wiki/2010_Russian_wildfires" title="2010 Russian wildfires">burning thousands of houses</a> and covering the capital of Moscow with a toxic <a href="/wiki/Smog" title="Smog">smoke blanket</a>. The situation remained critical until the end of August 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[64]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wise_use_and_protection">Wise use and protection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Wise use and protection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In June 2002, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Development_Programme" title="United Nations Development Programme">United Nations Development Programme</a> launched the Wetlands Ecosystem and Tropical Peat Swamp Forest Rehabilitation Project. This project was targeted to last for 5 years, and brings together the efforts of various non-government organisations.
</p><p>In November 2002, the International Peatland (formerly Peat) Society (IPS) and the International Mire Conservation Group (IMCG) published guidelines on the "Wise Use of Mires and Peatlands – Backgrounds and Principles including a framework for decision-making". The aim of this publication is to develop mechanisms that can balance the conflicting demands on the global peatland heritage, to ensure its wise use to meet the needs of humankind.
</p><p>In June 2008, the IPS published the book <i>Peatlands and Climate Change</i>, summarising the currently available knowledge on the topic. In 2010, IPS presented a "Strategy for Responsible Peatland Management", which can be applied worldwide for decision-making.
</p>
