<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Borehole</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Tube_well" title="Tube well">Tube well</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Drilling_boreholes_for_clean_water_(9084603915).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1504" data-file-width="1000" height="331" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Drilling_boreholes_for_clean_water_%289084603915%29.jpg/220px-Drilling_boreholes_for_clean_water_%289084603915%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Drilling_boreholes_for_clean_water_%289084603915%29.jpg/330px-Drilling_boreholes_for_clean_water_%289084603915%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Drilling_boreholes_for_clean_water_%289084603915%29.jpg/440px-Drilling_boreholes_for_clean_water_%289084603915%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Drilling_boreholes_for_clean_water_(9084603915).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A woman in Uganda collects water from a borehole.</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>borehole</b> is a narrow <a href="/wiki/Shaft_mining" title="Shaft mining">shaft</a> <a href="/wiki/Boring_(earth)" title="Boring (earth)">bored</a> in the ground, either vertically or horizontally. A borehole may be constructed for many different purposes, including the extraction of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, other liquids (such as <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a>) or gases (such as <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>), as part of a <a href="/wiki/Geotechnical_investigation" title="Geotechnical investigation">geotechnical investigation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phase_I_Environmental_Site_Assessment#Other_types_of_ESA" title="Phase I Environmental Site Assessment">environmental site assessment</a>,  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mineral_exploration" title="Mineral exploration">mineral exploration</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> measurement, as a pilot hole for installing piers or underground utilities, for geothermal installations, or for underground storage of unwanted substances, e.g. in <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Borehole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AlburyBoreHole.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="3072" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/AlburyBoreHole.jpg/220px-AlburyBoreHole.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/AlburyBoreHole.jpg/330px-AlburyBoreHole.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/AlburyBoreHole.jpg/440px-AlburyBoreHole.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AlburyBoreHole.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A water resources borehole into the <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a> <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifer</a> under the <a href="/wiki/North_Downs" title="North Downs">North Downs</a>, England at <a href="/wiki/Albury,_Surrey" title="Albury, Surrey">Albury</a></div></div></div><p><a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">Engineers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_consulting" title="Environmental consulting">environmental consultants</a> use the term <i>borehole</i> to collectively describe all of the various types of holes drilled as part of a <a href="/wiki/Geotechnical_investigation" title="Geotechnical investigation">geotechnical investigation</a> or environmental site assessment (a so-called Phase II ESA). This includes holes advanced to collect soil samples, water samples or rock cores, to advance <i><a href="/wiki/In_situ" title="In situ">in situ</a></i> sampling equipment, or to install <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monitoring_well" title="Monitoring well">monitoring wells</a> or <a href="/wiki/Piezometer" title="Piezometer">piezometers</a>.  Samples collected from boreholes are often tested in a laboratory to determine their physical properties, or to assess levels of various chemical constituents or contaminants. 
</p><p>Typically, a borehole used as a <a href="/wiki/Water_well" title="Water well">water well</a> is completed by installing a vertical pipe (casing) and well screen to keep the borehole from caving. This also helps prevent surface <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contaminants" title="Contaminants">contaminants</a> from entering the borehole and protects any installed pump from drawing in sand and sediment. <a href="/wiki/Oil_well" title="Oil well">Oil and natural gas wells</a> are completed in a similar, albeit usually more complex, manner.
</p><p>As detailed in <a href="/wiki/Proxy_(climate)#Boreholes" title="Proxy (climate)">proxy (climate)</a>, borehole temperature measurements at a series of different depths can be effectively "<a href="/wiki/Invertible_matrix" title="Invertible matrix">inverted</a>" (a mathematical formula to solve a matrix equation) to help estimate historic surface temperatures.
</p><p>Clusters of small-diameter boreholes equipped with heat exhangers made of plastic PEX pipe can be used to store heat or cold between opposing seasons in a mass of native rock. The technique is called <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_thermal_energy_storage" title="Seasonal thermal energy storage">seasonal thermal energy storage</a>. Media that can be used for this technique range from gravel to bedrock. There can be a few to several hundred boreholes, and in practice, depths have ranged from 150 to 1000 feet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Borehole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Borehole drilling has a long history. By at least the Han Dynasty (202 BC – 220 AD), the Chinese used deep borehole drilling for mining and other projects. The British sinologist and historian <a href="/wiki/Michael_Loewe" title="Michael Loewe">Michael Loewe</a> states that borehole sites could reach as deep as 600 m (2000 ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loewe_1968_194_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-loewe_1968_194-3">[3]</a></sup> K.S. Tom describes the drilling process: "The Chinese method of deep drilling was accomplished by a team of men jumping on and off a beam to impact the drilling bit while the boring tool was rotated by buffalo and oxen."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tom_1989_103_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-tom_1989_103-4">[4]</a></sup> This was the same method used for extracting <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> during the 1860s (i.e. "Kicking Her Down").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tom_1989_103_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-tom_1989_103-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> A Western Han Dynasty bronze foundry discovered in Xinglong, <a href="/wiki/Hebei" title="Hebei">Hebei</a> had nearby <a href="/wiki/Shaft_mining" title="Shaft mining">mining shafts</a> which reached depths of 100 m (328 ft) with spacious mining areas; the shafts and rooms were complete with a timber frame, ladders and iron tools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loewe_1968_191_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-loewe_1968_191-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wang_1982_105_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-wang_1982_105-7">[7]</a></sup> By the first century BC, Chinese craftsmen cast iron drill bits and drillers were able to drill boreholes up to 4800 feet (1500 m) deep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> By the eleventh century AD, the Chinese were able to drill boreholes up to 3000 feet in depth. Drilling for boreholes was time consuming and long. As the depth of the holes varied, the drilling of a single well could last nearly one full decade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tom_1989_103_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-tom_1989_103-4">[4]</a></sup> It wasn't up until the 19th century that Europe and the West would catch up and rival ancient Chinese borehole drilling technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>For many years, the world’s longest borehole was the <a href="/wiki/Kola_Superdeep_Borehole" title="Kola Superdeep Borehole">Kola Superdeep Borehole</a> in Russia. From 2011 until August 2012 the record was held by the 12,345-metre (40,502 ft) long <a href="/wiki/Sakhalin-I" title="Sakhalin-I">Sakhalin-I</a> Odoptu OP-11 Well, offshore the Russian island <a href="/wiki/Sakhalin" title="Sakhalin">Sakhalin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Sakhalin-I#Fields:_Chayvo.2C_Odoptu_and_Arkutun-Dagi" title="Sakhalin-I">Chayvo</a> Z-44 extended-reach well took the title of the world's longest borehole on 27 August 2012. Z-44's total measured depth is 12,376 m (40,604 ft). However, ERD wells are more shallow than Kola Superdeep Borehole, owing to a large horizontal displacement.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methodology">Methodology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Borehole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Methodology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Borewell_digging.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4096" data-file-width="2304" height="391" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Borewell_digging.jpg/220px-Borewell_digging.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Borewell_digging.jpg/330px-Borewell_digging.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Borewell_digging.jpg/440px-Borewell_digging.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Borewell_digging.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Borewell digging</div></div></div>
<p>Drillers may sink a borehole using a <a href="/wiki/Drilling_rig" title="Drilling rig">drilling rig</a> or a hand-operated rig. The machinery and techniques to advance a borehole vary considerably according to manufacturer, geological conditions, and the intended purpose. For <a href="/wiki/Offshore_drilling" title="Offshore drilling">offshore drilling</a> floating units or platforms supported by the seafloor are used for the drilling rig.
