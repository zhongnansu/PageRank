<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Crust (geology)</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">The outermost solid shell of a rocky planet, dwarf planet, or natural satellite</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For any other use, see <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Crust_(disambiguation)" title="Crust (disambiguation)">Crust (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Earth_poster.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1360" data-file-width="2200" height="216" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Earth_poster.svg/350px-Earth_poster.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Earth_poster.svg/525px-Earth_poster.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Earth_poster.svg/700px-Earth_poster.svg.png 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Earth_poster.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The internal structure of Earth</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, the <b>crust</b> is the outermost solid shell of a <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_planet" title="Terrestrial planet">rocky</a> <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">dwarf planet</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">natural satellite</a>. It is usually distinguished from the underlying <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> by its chemical makeup; however, in the case of icy satellites, it may be distinguished based on its phase (solid crust vs. liquid mantle).
</p><p>The crusts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_(planet)" title="Earth (planet)">Earth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">Io</a>, and other planetary bodies formed via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Igneous" title="Igneous">igneous</a> processes, and were later modified by <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">impact cratering</a>, volcanism, and sedimentation.
</p><p>Most terrestrial planets have fairly uniform crusts. Earth, however, has two distinct types: <a href="/wiki/Continental_crust" title="Continental crust">continental crust</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_crust" title="Oceanic crust">oceanic crust</a>. These two types have different chemical compositions and physical properties, and were formed by different geological processes.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_crust">Types of crust</span></h2>
<p>Planetary geologists divide crust into three categories, based on how and when they formed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hargitai_2014_1–8_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hargitai_2014_1–8-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Primary_crust_.2F_primordial_crust"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_crust_/_primordial_crust">Primary crust / primordial crust</span></h3>
<p>This is a planet's "original" crust. It forms from solidification of a magma ocean. Toward the end of <a href="/wiki/Formation_and_evolution_of_the_Solar_System" title="Formation and evolution of the Solar System">planetary accretion</a>, the terrestrial planets likely had surfaces that were magma oceans. As these cooled, they solidified into crust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  This crust was likely destroyed by large impacts and re-formed many times as the <a href="/wiki/Formation_and_evolution_of_the_Solar_System" title="Formation and evolution of the Solar System">Era of Heavy Bombardment</a> drew to a close.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The nature of primary crust is still debated: its chemical, mineralogic, and physical properties are unknown, as are the igneous mechanisms that formed them. This is because it is difficult to study: none of Earth's primary crust has survived to today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Earth's high rates of erosion and crustal recycling from plate tectonics has destroyed <a href="/wiki/Oldest_dated_rocks" title="Oldest dated rocks">all rocks older than about 4 billion years</a>, including whatever primary crust Earth once had. 
</p><p>However, geologists can glean information about primary crust by studying it on other terrestrial planets. Mercury's highlands might represent primary crust, though this is debated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Anorthosite" title="Anorthosite">anorthosite</a> <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_the_Moon" title="Geology of the Moon">highlands</a> of the Moon are primary crust, formed as <a href="/wiki/Plagioclase" title="Plagioclase">plagioclase</a> crystallized out of the Moon's initial magma ocean and floated to the top;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> however, it is unlikely that Earth followed a similar pattern, as the Moon was a water-less system and Earth had water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Martian_meteorite" title="Martian meteorite">Martian meteorite</a> <a href="/wiki/Allan_Hills_84001" title="Allan Hills 84001">ALH84001</a> might represent primary crust of Mars; however, again, this is debated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> Like Earth, Venus lacks primary crust, as the entire planet has been repeatedly resurfaced and modified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Secondary_crust">Secondary crust</span></h3>
<p>Secondary crust is formed by <a href="/wiki/Partial_melting" title="Partial melting">partial melting</a> of silicate materials in the mantle, and so is usually basaltic in composition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hargitai_2014_1–8_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hargitai_2014_1–8-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>This is the most common type of crust in the Solar System. Most of the surfaces of Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars comprise secondary crust, as do the <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">lunar maria</a>. On Earth, we see secondary crust forming primarily at <a href="/wiki/Mid-ocean_ridge" title="Mid-ocean ridge">mid-ocean spreading centers</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_process" title="Adiabatic process">adiabatic</a> rise of mantle causes partial melting. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tertiary_crust">Tertiary crust</span></h3>
<p>Tertiary crust is more chemically-modified than either primary or secondary. It can form in several ways:
</p>
<ul><li>Igneous processes: partial-melting of secondary crust, coupled with differentiation or dehydration<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>Erosion and sedimentation: sediments derived from primary, secondary, or tertiary crust</li></ul>
<p>The only known example of tertiary crust is the continental crust of the Earth. It is unknown whether other terrestrial planets can be said to have tertiary crust, though the evidence so far suggests that they do not. This is likely because plate tectonics is needed to create tertiary crust, and Earth is the only planet in our Solar System with plate tectonics. 
</p>
<h2><span id="Earth.27s_crust"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth's_crust">Earth's crust</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Plates_tect2_en.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2844" data-file-width="4167" height="239" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Plates_tect2_en.svg/350px-Plates_tect2_en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Plates_tect2_en.svg/525px-Plates_tect2_en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Plates_tect2_en.svg/700px-Plates_tect2_en.svg.png 2x" width="350"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Plates_tect2_en.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Plates in the crust of Earth</div></div></div>
<p>The crust is a thin shell on the outside of the Earth, accounting for less than 1% of Earth's volume. It is the top component of <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>: a division of Earth's layers that includes the crust and the upper part of the <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The lithosphere is broken into tectonic plates that move, allowing heat to escape from the interior of the Earth into space. 
</p><p>The crust lies on top of the <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a>, a configuration that is stable because the upper mantle is made of <a href="/wiki/Peridotite" title="Peridotite">peridotite</a> and so is significantly denser than the crust. The boundary between the crust and mantle is conventionally placed at the <a href="/wiki/Mohorovi%C4%8Di%C4%87_discontinuity" title="Mohorovičić discontinuity">Mohorovičić discontinuity</a>, a boundary defined by a contrast in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seismic" title="Seismic">seismic</a> velocity. 
</p>

width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #abc; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;"> </span> 20–65 <a href="/wiki/Year#SI_prefix_multipliers" title="Year">Ma</a></div> <div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #89a; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;"> </span> &gt;65 <a href="/wiki/Year#SI_prefix_multipliers" title="Year">Ma</a></div> </td></tr></tbody></table></div></div></div></div>
<p>The crust of the Earth is of two distinctive types: 
</p>
<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Oceanic_crust" title="Oceanic crust">Oceanic</a>: 5 km (3 mi) to 10 km (6 mi) thick<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and composed primarily of denser, more <a href="/wiki/Mafic" title="Mafic">mafic</a> rocks, such as <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basalt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diabase" title="Diabase">diabase</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gabbro" title="Gabbro">gabbro</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Continental_crust" title="Continental crust">Continental</a>: 30 km (20 mi) to 50 km (30 mi) thick and mostly composed of less dense, more <a href="/wiki/Felsic" title="Felsic">felsic</a> rocks, such as <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a>.</li></ol>
<p>Because both continental and oceanic crust are less dense than the mantle below, both types of crust <a href="/wiki/Isostasy" title="Isostasy">"float" on the mantle</a>. This is isostasy, and it's also one of the reasons continental crust is higher than oceanic: continental is less dense and so "floats" higher. As a result, water pools in above the oceanic crust, forming the oceans. 
</p><p>The temperature of the crust increases with depth,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> reaching values typically in the range from about 200 °C (392 °F) to 400 °C (752 °F) at the boundary with the underlying mantle. The temperature increases by as much as 30 °C (54 °F) for every kilometer locally in the upper part of the crust, but the <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_gradient" title="Geothermal gradient">geothermal gradient</a> is smaller in deeper crust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Abundance_of_elements_in_Earth%27s_crust" title="Abundance of elements in Earth's crust">Abundance of elements in Earth's crust</a> and <a href="/wiki/Goldschmidt_classification" title="Goldschmidt classification">Goldschmidt classification</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Elemental_abundances.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="620" data-file-width="800" height="388" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Elemental_abundances.svg/500px-Elemental_abundances.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Elemental_abundances.svg/750px-Elemental_abundances.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Elemental_abundances.svg/1000px-Elemental_abundances.svg.png 2x" width="500"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Elemental_abundances.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Abundance (atom fraction) of the chemical elements in Earth's upper continental crust as a function of atomic number. The rarest elements in the crust (shown in yellow) are not the heaviest, but are rather the siderophile (iron-loving) elements in the <a href="/wiki/Goldschmidt_classification" title="Goldschmidt classification">Goldschmidt classification</a> of elements. These have been depleted by being relocated deeper into Earth's core. Their abundance in <a href="/wiki/Meteoroid" title="Meteoroid">meteoroid</a> materials is higher. Additionally, tellurium and selenium have been depleted from the crust due to formation of volatile hydrides.</div></div></div>
<p>The continental crust has an average composition similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Andesite" title="Andesite">andesite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The most abundant <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a> in <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Continental_crust" title="Continental crust">continental crust</a> are <a href="/wiki/Feldspar" title="Feldspar">feldspars</a>, which make up about 41% of the crust by weight, followed by <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a> at 12%, and <a href="/wiki/Pyroxene" title="Pyroxene">pyroxenes</a> at 11%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Continental crust is enriched in <a href="/wiki/Incompatible_element" title="Incompatible element">incompatible elements</a> compared to the <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basaltic</a> ocean crust and much enriched compared to the underlying mantle. Although the continental crust comprises only about 0.6 weight percent of the silicate on Earth, it contains 20% to 70% of the incompatible elements.
</p>

Estimates of average density for the upper crust range between 2.69 and 2.74 g/cm<sup>3</sup> and for lower crust between 3.0 and 3.25 g/cm<sup>3</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amonline_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-amonline-15">[15]</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_and_evolution">Formation and evolution</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formation_of_the_Earth" title="Formation of the Earth">Formation of the Earth</a></div>
<p>Earth formed approximately 4.6 billion years ago from a disk of dust and gas orbiting the newly formed Sun. It formed via accretion, where planetesimals and other smaller rocky bodies collided and stuck, gradually growing into a planet. This process generated an enormous amount of heat, which caused early Earth to melt completely. As planetary accretion slowed, Earth began to cool, forming its first crust, called a primary or primordial crust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uopp_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-uopp-16">[16]</a></sup> This crust was likely repeatedly destroyed by large impacts, then reformed from the magma ocean left by the impact. None of Earth's primary crust has survived to today; all was destroyed by erosion, impacts, and plate tectonics over the past several billion years. 
</p><p>Since then, Earth has been forming secondary and tertiary crust. Secondary crust forms at mid-ocean spreading centers, where partial-melting of the underlying mantle yields basaltic magmas and new ocean crust forms. This "ridge push" is one of the driving forces of plate tectonics, and it is constantly creating new ocean crust. That means that old crust must be destroyed somewhere, so, opposite a spreading center, there is usually a subduction zone: a trench where an ocean plate is being shoved back into the mantle. This constant process of creating new ocean crust and destroying old ocean crust means that the oldest ocean crust on Earth today is only about 200 million years old. 
</p><p>In contrast, the bulk of the continental crust is much older. The oldest continental crustal rocks on Earth have ages in the range from about 3.7 to 4.28  billion years <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and have been found in the <a href="/wiki/Narryer_Gneiss_Terrane" title="Narryer Gneiss Terrane">Narryer Gneiss Terrane</a> in <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Acasta_Gneiss" title="Acasta Gneiss">Acasta Gneiss</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Territories" title="Northwest Territories">Northwest Territories</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Shield" title="Canadian Shield">Canadian Shield</a>, and on other cratonic regions such as those on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fennoscandian_Shield" title="Fennoscandian Shield">Fennoscandian Shield</a>. Some <a href="/wiki/Zircon" title="Zircon">zircon</a> with age as great as 4.3 billion years has been found in the <a href="/wiki/Narryer_Gneiss_Terrane" title="Narryer Gneiss Terrane">Narryer Gneiss Terrane</a>.
</p><p>The average age of the current Earth's continental crust has been estimated to be about 2.0 billion years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Most crustal rocks formed before 2.5 billion years ago are located in <a href="/wiki/Craton" title="Craton">cratons</a>. Such old continental crust and the underlying mantle asthenosphere are less dense than elsewhere in Earth and so are not readily destroyed by <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subduction</a>. Formation of new continental crust is linked to periods of intense <a href="/wiki/Orogeny" title="Orogeny">orogeny</a>; these periods coincide with the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Supercontinent" title="Supercontinent">supercontinents</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Rodinia" title="Rodinia">Rodinia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a>. The crust forms in part by aggregation of <a href="/wiki/Island_arc" title="Island arc">island arcs</a> including granite and metamorphic fold belts, and it is preserved in part by depletion of the underlying mantle to form buoyant lithospheric mantle.
</p>
<h2><span id="Moon.27s_crust"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Moon's_crust">Moon's crust</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_the_Moon" title="Geology of the Moon">Geology of the Moon</a></div>
<p>A theoretical <a href="/wiki/Protoplanet" title="Protoplanet">protoplanet</a> named "<a href="/wiki/Theia_(planet)" title="Theia (planet)">Theia</a>" is thought to have collided with the forming Earth, and part of the material ejected into space by the collision accreted to form the Moon. As the Moon formed, the outer part of it is thought to have been molten, a “<a href="/wiki/Lunar_magma_ocean" title="Lunar magma ocean">lunar magma ocean</a>.” <a href="/wiki/Plagioclase" title="Plagioclase">Plagioclase</a> <a href="/wiki/Feldspar" title="Feldspar">feldspar</a> crystallized in large amounts from this <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> ocean and floated toward the surface. The <a href="/wiki/Cumulate_rock" title="Cumulate rock">cumulate rocks</a> form much of the crust. The upper part of the crust probably averages about 88% plagioclase (near the lower limit of 90% defined for <a href="/wiki/Anorthosite" title="Anorthosite">anorthosite</a>): the lower part of the crust may contain a higher percentage of ferromagnesian minerals such as the <a href="/wiki/Pyroxene" title="Pyroxene">pyroxenes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">olivine</a>, but even that lower part probably averages about 78% plagioclase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The underlying mantle is denser and olivine-rich.
</p><p>The thickness of the crust ranges between about 20 and 120 km. Crust on the <a href="/wiki/Far_side_of_the_Moon" title="Far side of the Moon">far side of the Moon</a> averages about 12 km thicker than that on the <a href="/wiki/Near_side_of_the_Moon" title="Near side of the Moon">near side</a>. Estimates of average thickness fall in the range from about 50 to 60 km. Most of this plagioclase-rich crust formed shortly after formation of the moon, between about 4.5 and 4.3 billion years ago. Perhaps 10% or less of the crust consists of igneous rock added after the formation of the initial plagioclase-rich material. The best-characterized and most voluminous of these later additions are the mare <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basalts</a> formed between about 3.9 and 3.2 billion years ago. Minor volcanism continued after 3.2 billion years, perhaps as recently as 1 billion years ago. There is no evidence of <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a>.
</p><p>Study of the Moon has established that a crust can form on a rocky planetary body significantly smaller than Earth. Although the radius of the Moon is only about a quarter that of Earth, the lunar crust has a significantly greater average thickness. This thick crust formed almost immediately after formation of the Moon. Magmatism continued after the period of intense meteorite impacts ended about 3.9 billion years ago, but igneous rocks younger than 3.9 billion years make up only a minor part of the crust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
