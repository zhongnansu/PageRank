<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Power-to-gas</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Power-to-gas</b> (often abbreviated <b>P2G</b>) is a technology that converts <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electrical</a> power to a gas <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>  When using surplus power from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wind_generation" title="Wind generation">wind generation</a>, the concept is sometimes called <b>windgas</b>.  There are currently three methods in use; all use electricity to <a href="/wiki/Water_splitting" title="Water splitting">split water</a> into <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> by means of <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In the first method, the resulting hydrogen is injected into the natural gas grid or is used in transport or industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eberle_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eberle-2">[2]</a></sup> The second method is to combine the hydrogen with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and convert the two gases to <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>) using a <a href="/wiki/Methanation" title="Methanation">methanation</a> reaction such as the <a href="/wiki/Sabatier_reaction" title="Sabatier reaction">Sabatier reaction</a>, or biological methanation resulting in an extra <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_conversion" title="Energy conversion">energy conversion</a> <a href="/wiki/Energy_conversion_efficiency" title="Energy conversion efficiency">loss</a> of 8%. The methane /SNG may then be fed into the natural gas grid or further converted in to <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_petroleum_gas" title="Liquefied petroleum gas">LPG</a> by synthesising <a href="/wiki/Substitute_natural_gas" title="Substitute natural gas">SNG</a>  with partial reverse <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenation</a> at high pressure and low temperature. LPG in turn can be    converted into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkylate" title="Alkylate">alkylate</a> which is a premium <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> blending stock because it has exceptional antiknock properties and gives clean burning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The third method uses the output gas of a <a href="/wiki/Wood_gas_generator" title="Wood gas generator">wood gas generator</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">biogas</a> plant, after the <a href="/wiki/Biogas_upgrader" title="Biogas upgrader">biogas upgrader</a> is mixed with the produced hydrogen from the electrolyzer, to upgrade the quality of the biogas.
</p><p>Impurities, such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particulate" title="Particulate">particulates</a>, must be removed from the biogas if the gas is used for pipeline storage to prevent damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Storage_function">Storage function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power-to-gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Storage function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Power-to-gas systems may be deployed as adjuncts to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wind_park" title="Wind park">wind parks</a> or <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">solar-electric generation</a>. The excess power or off-peak power generated by wind generators or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_array" title="Solar array">solar arrays</a> may then be used at a later time for load balancing in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_grid" title="Energy grid">energy grid</a>. Before switching to <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, the German gas networks were operated using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Towngas" title="Towngas">towngas</a>, which for 50–60 % consisted of hydrogen. The storage capacity of the German natural gas network is more than 200,000 GWh which is enough for several months of energy requirement. By comparison, the capacity of all German pumped storage power plants amounts to only about 40 GWh.  The storage requirement in Germany is estimated at 16GW in 2023, 80GW in 2033 and 130GW in 2050.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The transport of energy through a gas network is done with much less loss (&lt;0.1%) than in a power network (8%). The storage costs per kilowatt hour are estimated at €0.10 for hydrogen and €0.15 for methane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The use of the existing <a href="/wiki/List_of_natural_gas_pipelines" title="List of natural gas pipelines">natural gas pipelines</a> for hydrogen was studied by the EU NaturalHy project<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and US <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">DOE</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The blending technology is also used in <a href="/wiki/HCNG" title="HCNG">HCNG</a>.
</p><p><br/>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Efficiency">Efficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power-to-gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Efficiency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2013 the round-trip efficiency of power-to-gas-storage was well below 50%, with the hydrogen path being able to reach a maximum efficiency of ~ 43% and methane of ~ 39% by using combined-cycle powerplants. If <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">cogeneration</a> plants are used that produce both electricity and heat, efficiency can be above 60%, but is still less than pumped hydro or battery storage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> However, there is potential to increase efficiency of power-to-gas storage. In 2015 a study published in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_and_Environmental_Science" title="Energy and Environmental Science">Energy and Environmental Science</a> found that by using <a href="/wiki/Unitized_regenerative_fuel_cell" title="Unitized regenerative fuel cell">reversible solid oxide electrochemical cells</a> and recycling waste heat in the storage process a round-trip efficiency electricity to electricity of more than 70% can be reached at low cost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_to_hydrogen">Power to hydrogen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power-to-gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Power to hydrogen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In this method, electricity is used to <a href="/wiki/Water_splitting" title="Water splitting">split water</a> into hydrogen and oxygen by means of electrolysis. The resulting hydrogen is injected into the natural gas grid or is used in transport or industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eberle_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eberle-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/ITM_Power" title="ITM Power">ITM Power</a> won a tender in March 2013 for a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Th%C3%BCga_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thüga Group (page does not exist)">Thüga Group</a> project, to supply a 360 kW self-pressurising <a href="/wiki/High-pressure_electrolysis" title="High-pressure electrolysis">high pressure electrolysis</a> rapid response <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proton_exchange_membrane" title="Proton exchange membrane">PEM</a> <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water" title="Electrolysis of water">electrolyser</a> <i>Rapid Response Electrolysis</i> Power-to-Gas energy storage plant. The unit produces 125 kg/day of hydrogen gas and incorporates <a href="/wiki/AEG" title="AEG">AEG</a> power electronics. It will be situated at a <a href="/wiki/Mainova" title="Mainova">Mainova</a> AG site in the Schielestraße, <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a> in the state of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hessen" title="Hessen">Hessen</a>. The operational data will be shared by the whole Thüga group – the largest network of energy companies in Germany with around 100 municipal utility members. The project partners include: badenova AG &amp; Co. kg, Erdgas Mittelsachsen GmbH, Energieversorgung Mittelrhein GmbH, erdgas schwaben GmbH, Gasversorgung Westerwald GmbH, <a href="/wiki/Mainova" title="Mainova">Mainova</a> Aktiengesellschaft, Stadtwerke Ansbach GmbH, Stadtwerke Bad Hersfeld GmbH, Thüga Energienetze GmbH, WEMAG AG, e-rp GmbH, ESWE Versorgungs AG with Thüga Aktiengesellschaft as project coordinator. Scientific partners will participate in the operational phase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> It can produce 60 cubic metres of hydrogen per hour and feed 3,000 cubic metres of natural gas enriched with hydrogen into the grid per hour. An expansion of the pilot plant is planned from 2016, facilitating the full conversion of the hydrogen produced into methane to be directly injected into the natural gas grid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grid_injection_without_compression">Grid injection without compression</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power-to-gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Grid injection without compression">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The core of the system is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proton_exchange_membrane" title="Proton exchange membrane">proton exchange membrane</a> (PEM) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrolyser" title="Electrolyser">electrolyser</a>. The electrolyser converts electrical energy into chemical energy, which in turn facilitates the storage of electricity. A gas mixing plant ensures that the proportion of hydrogen in the natural gas stream does not exceed two per cent by volume, the technically permissible maximum value when a natural gas filling station is situated in the local distribution network. The electrolyser supplies the hydrogen-methane mixture at the same pressure as the gas distribution network, namely 3.5 bar.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_to_methane">Power to methane</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power-to-gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Power to methane">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Methanation_of_CO2.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="800" height="240" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Methanation_of_CO2.png/400px-Methanation_of_CO2.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Methanation_of_CO2.png/600px-Methanation_of_CO2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2a/Methanation_of_CO2.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Methanation_of_CO2.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Methanation of CO<sub>2</sub> by electrolytically obtained hydrogen</div></div></div>
<p>The power to methane method is to combine hydrogen from an electrolyzer with carbon dioxide and convert the two gases to methane<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> (see <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>) using a <a href="/wiki/Methanation" title="Methanation">methanation</a> reaction such as the Sabatier reaction or biological methanation resulting in an extra energy conversion loss of 8%,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the methane may then be fed into the natural gas grid if the purity requirement is reached.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p2m_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-p2m-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>ZSW (Center for Solar Energy and Hydrogen Research) and SolarFuel GmbH (now ETOGAS GmbH) realized a demonstration project with 250 kW electrical input power in Stuttgart, Germany. The plant was put into operation on October 30, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first industry-scale Power-to-Methane plant was realized by ETOGAS for Audi AG in Werlte, Germany. The plant with 6 MW electrical input power is using CO<sub>2</sub> from a waste-<a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">biogas</a> plant and intermittent renewable power to produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthetic_natural_gas" title="Synthetic natural gas">synthetic natural gas</a> (SNG) which is directly fed into the local gas grid (which is operated by EWE).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The plant is part of the Audi e-fuels program. The produced synthetic natural gas, named Audi e-gas, enables CO<sub>2</sub>-neutral mobility with standard CNG vehicles. Currently it is available to customers of Audi's first CNG car, the Audi A3 g-tron.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Helmeth_PtG_Anlage.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Helmeth_PtG_Anlage.jpg/220px-Helmeth_PtG_Anlage.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Helmeth_PtG_Anlage.jpg/330px-Helmeth_PtG_Anlage.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Helmeth_PtG_Anlage.jpg/440px-Helmeth_PtG_Anlage.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Helmeth_PtG_Anlage.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>HELMETH Power-to-Gas Prototype</div></div></div>
<p>In April 2014 the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union’s</a> co-financed and from the <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe_Institute_of_Technology" title="Karlsruhe Institute of Technology">KIT</a> coordinated<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> HELMETH<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> (Integrated <b>H</b>igh-Temperature <b>EL</b>ectrolysis and <b>METH</b>anation for Effective Power to Gas Conversion) research project started.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> The objective of the project is the proof of concept of a highly efficient Power-to-Gas technology by thermally integrating high temperature electrolysis (<a href="/wiki/Solid_oxide_electrolyser_cell" title="Solid oxide electrolyser cell">SOEC</a> technology) with CO<sub>2</sub>-methanation. 
Through the thermal integration of exothermal methanation and steam generation for the high temperature steam electrolysis  conversion efficiency &gt; 85% (<a href="/wiki/Heat_of_combustion" title="Heat of combustion">higher heating value</a> of produced methane per used electrical energy) are theoretically possible. The process consists of a pressurized high-temperature steam <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water" title="Electrolysis of water">electrolysis</a> and a pressurized CO<sub>2</sub>-methanation module. 
The project was completed in 2017 and achieved an efficiency of 76 % for the prototype with an indicated growth potential of 80 % for industrial scale plants<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>. The operating conditions of the CO<sub>2</sub>-methanation are a gas pressure of 10 - 30 bar, a <a href="/wiki/Substitute_natural_gas" title="Substitute natural gas">SNG</a> production of 1 - 5.4 m<sup>3</sup>/h (NTP) and a <a href="/wiki/Conversion_(chemistry)" title="Conversion (chemistry)">reactant conversion</a> that produces SNG with H<sub>2</sub> &lt; 2 vol.-% resp. CH<sub>4</sub> &gt; 97 vol.-%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>. Thus, the generated substitute natural gas can be injected in the entire German natural gas network without limitations<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DIN_EN_16723-2_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-DIN_EN_16723-2-40">[40]</a></sup>. As a cooling medium for the exothermic reaction boiling water is used at up to 300 °C, which corresponds to a <a href="/wiki/Vapour_pressure_of_water" title="Vapour pressure of water">water vapour pressure</a> of about 87 bar. The SOEC works with a pressure of up to 15 bar, steam conversions of up to 90% and generates one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_cubic_meter" title="Standard cubic meter">standard cubic meter</a> of hydrogen from 3.37 <a href="/wiki/Kilowatt_hour" title="Kilowatt hour">kWh</a> of electricity as feed for the methanation.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> demonstration project STORE&amp;GO.<br/>
The technological maturity of Power to Gas is evaluated in the European 27 partner project STORE&amp;GO, which has started in March 2016 with a runtime of four years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Three different technological concepts are demonstrated in three different European countries (<a href="/wiki/Falkenhagen" title="Falkenhagen">Falkenhagen</a>/<a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solothurn" title="Solothurn">Solothurn</a>/<a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Troia,_Apulia" title="Troia, Apulia">Troia</a>/<a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>). The technologies involved include biological and chemical <a href="/wiki/Methanation" title="Methanation">methanation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct_air_capture" title="Direct air capture">direct capture of  CO<sub>2</sub></a> from atmosphere, liquefaction of the synthesized methane to bio-<a href="/wiki/Liquefied_natural_gas" title="Liquefied natural gas">LNG</a>, and direct injection into the gas grid. The overall goal of the project is to assess those technologies and various usage paths under technical,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> economic,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
and legal <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> aspects to identify business cases on the short and on the long term.  The project is co-funded by the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union’s</a> <a href="/wiki/Framework_Programmes_for_Research_and_Technological_Development#Horizon_2020" title="Framework Programmes for Research and Technological Development">Horizon 2020</a> research and innovation programme (18 million euro) and the Swiss government (6 million euro), with another 4 million euro coming from participating industrial partners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> The coordinator of the overall project is the research center of the 
<a href="/wiki/Deutscher_Verein_des_Gas-_und_Wasserfaches" title="Deutscher Verein des Gas- und Wasserfaches">DVGW</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> located at the <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe_Institute_of_Technology" title="Karlsruhe Institute of Technology">KIT</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Microbial_methanation">Microbial methanation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power-to-gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Microbial methanation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The biological methanation combines both processes, the <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> of water to form <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and the subsequent CO<sub>2</sub> reduction to <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> using this <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>. During this process, methane forming microorganisms (methanogenic <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methanogens" title="Methanogens">methanogens</a>) release <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enzymes" title="Enzymes">enzymes</a> that reduce the <a href="/wiki/Overpotential" title="Overpotential">overpotential</a> of a non-catalytic <a href="/wiki/Electrode" title="Electrode">electrode</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Cathode" title="Cathode">cathode</a>) so that it can produce <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> This microbial power-to-gas reaction occurs at ambient conditions, i.e. room temperature and pH 7, at efficiencies that routinely reach 80-100%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> However, methane is formed more slowly than in the Sabatier reaction due to the lower temperatures. A direct conversion of CO<sub>2</sub> to <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> has also been postulated, circumventing the need for <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
Microorganisms involved in the microbial power-to-gas reaction are typically members of the order <i><a href="/wiki/Methanobacteriales" title="Methanobacteriales">Methanobacteriales</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">Genera</a> that were shown to catalyze this reaction are <i><a href="/wiki/Methanobacterium" title="Methanobacterium">Methanobacterium</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Methanobrevibacter" title="Methanobrevibacter">Methanobrevibacter</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Methanothermobacter" title="Methanothermobacter">Methanothermobacter</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Thermophile" title="Thermophile">thermophile</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_to_food">Power to food</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power-to-gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Power to food">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The synthetic methane generated from electricity can also be used for generating protein rich feed for cattle, poultry and fish economically by cultivating <i><a href="/wiki/Methylococcus_capsulatus" title="Methylococcus capsulatus">Methylococcus capsulatus</a></i> bacteria culture with tiny land and water foot print.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> The carbon dioxide gas produced as by product from these plants can be  recycled in the generation of synthetic methane (SNG). Similarly, oxygen gas produced as by product from the electrolysis of water and the <a href="/wiki/Methanation" title="Methanation">methanation</a>  process can be consumed in the cultivation of bacteria culture. With these integrated plants, the abundant renewable solar / wind power potential can be converted in to high value food products with out any water pollution or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_house_gas" title="Green house gas">green house gas</a> (GHG) emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biogas-upgrading_to_biomethane">Biogas-upgrading to biomethane</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power-to-gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Biogas-upgrading to biomethane">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the third method the carbon dioxide in the output of a wood gas generator or a biogas plant after the biogas upgrader is mixed with the produced hydrogen from the electrolyzer to produce methane. The free heat coming from the electrolyzer is used to cut heating costs in the biogas plant. The impurities carbon dioxide, water, hydrogen sulfide, and particulates must be removed from the biogas if the gas is used for pipeline storage to prevent damage.
</p><p>2014-Avedøre wastewater Services in <a href="/wiki/Aved%C3%B8re" title="Avedøre">Avedøre</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kopenhagen" title="Kopenhagen">Kopenhagen</a> (Denmark) is adding a 1 MW electrolyzer plant to upgrade the <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_digestion" title="Anaerobic digestion">anaerobic digestion</a> biogas from sewage sludge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> The produced hydrogen is used with the carbon dioxide from the biogas in a Sabatier reaction to produce methane. Electrochaea<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> is testing another project outside P2G BioCat with biocatalytic methanation. The company uses an adapted strain of the thermophilic methanogen Methanothermobacter thermautotrophicus and has demonstrated its technology at laboratory-scale in an industrial environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> A pre-commercial demonstration project with a 10,000-liter reactor vessel was executed between January and November 2013 in <a href="/wiki/Foulum" title="Foulum">Foulum</a>, Denmark.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2016 <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Torrgas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Torrgas (page does not exist)">Torrgas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siemens" title="Siemens">Siemens</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stedin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stedin (page does not exist)">Stedin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gasunie" title="Gasunie">Gasunie</a>, A.Hak, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hanze_University_of_Applied_Sciences,_Groningen" title="Hanze University of Applied Sciences, Groningen">Hanzehogeschool</a>/EnTranCe and Energy Valley intend to open a 12 MW Power to Gas facility in <a href="/wiki/Delfzijl" title="Delfzijl">Delfzijl</a> (The Netherlands) where biogas from Torrgas (<a href="/wiki/Torrefaction" title="Torrefaction">biocoal</a>) will be upgraded with hydrogen from electrolysis and delivered to nearby industrial consumers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
