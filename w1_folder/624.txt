<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Coalbed methane extraction</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Coalbed methane extraction</b> (CBM extraction) is a method for extracting <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> from a <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> deposit.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_principles">Basic principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalbed_methane_extraction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Basic principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Methane <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorbed</a> into a solid coal matrix (coal macerals) will be released if the coal seam is depressurised.  Methane may be extracted by <a href="/wiki/Well_drilling" title="Well drilling">drilling wells</a> into the coal seam. The goal is to decrease the water pressure by pumping water from the well.  The decrease in pressure allows methane to desorb from the coal and flow as a gas up the well to the surface. Methane is then compressed and piped to market.
</p><p>The objective is to avoid putting methane into the water line, but allow it to flow up the backside of the well (casing) to the compressor station. If the water level is pumped too low during dewatering, methane may travel up the tubing into the water line causing the well to become "gassy". Although methane may be recovered in a water-gas separator at the surface, pumping water and gas is inefficient and can cause pump wear and breakdown.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Areas_with_CBM_extraction">Areas with CBM extraction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalbed_methane_extraction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Areas with CBM extraction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tens of thousands of methane wells have been drilled, and extensive support facilities such as roads, pipelines, and compressors have been installed for CBM extraction in the <a href="/wiki/Powder_River_Basin" title="Powder River Basin">Powder River Basin</a> of northeast <a href="/wiki/Wyoming" title="Wyoming">Wyoming</a> and southeast <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a> and now in India at West Bengal- Ranigunj, Panagarh etc. Seven percent of the <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> (methane) currently produced in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> comes from CBM extraction. Methane from coalbed reservoirs can be recovered economically, but disposal of water is an environmental concern.
</p><p>There are also sites in Central Scotland at Letham Moss.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Most gas in coal is stored on the internal surfaces of organic matter. Because of its large internal surface area, coal stores 6 to 7 times more gas than the equivalent rock volume of a conventional gas reservoir. Gas content generally increases with coal rank, with depth of burial of the coal bed, and with reservoir pressure. Fractures, or cleats, within coal beds are usually filled with water. Deeper coal beds contain less water, but that water is more saline. Removing water from the coal bed reduces pressure and releases methane. Large amounts of water, sometimes saline <a href="/wiki/Brine" title="Brine">brine</a>, are produced from <a href="/wiki/Coalbed_methane" title="Coalbed methane">coalbed methane</a> wells. The greatest water volumes are produced during the early stages of production. Environmentally acceptable disposal of brine is a major cost factor for economic methane production. Fresh water may be discharged on the surface, but brine is usually injected into rock at a depth where the salinity of the injected brine is less than <a href="/wiki/Connate_fluids" title="Connate fluids">connate fluids</a> of the host rock. Evaporation of water for recovery of potentially salable solid residues might be feasible in regions having high evaporation rates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measuring_the_gas_content_of_coal">Measuring the gas content of coal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalbed_methane_extraction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Measuring the gas content of coal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Coal bed gas content measurements are commonly used in mine safety as well as coal bed methane resource assessment and recovery applications. Gas content determination techniques generally fall into two categories: (1) direct methods which actually measure the volume of methane released from a coal sample sealed into a <a href="/wiki/Desorption" title="Desorption">desorption</a> canister and (2) indirect methods based on empirical correlations, or laboratory derived <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sorption_isotherm" title="Sorption isotherm">sorption isotherm</a> methane storage capacity data. Laboratory sorption isotherms provide information about the storage capacity of a coal sampleif these are measured under geological realistic pressure and temperature conditions. Thus, the maximum gas content which can be expected for methane recovery can be assessed from such laboratory isotherm measurements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The total gas content by the indirect methods is based on the <a href="/wiki/Empirical_formula" title="Empirical formula">empirical formula</a> given by Meinser and Kim. The quantity of gas is determined by Meisner and Kim formula with using the moisture content, volatile content, volume of methane adsorbed on wet coal, fixed carbon, thickness of coal and temperature.
<br/>Meinser (1984) observed that the amount of methane gas (VCH4) is related to volatile matter (daf).
</p><p><br/>V<sub>CH4</sub> = −325.6 × log (V.M/37.8)
</p><p><br/>Estimation of <i><a href="/wiki/In_situ" title="In situ">in situ</a></i> gas content of the coal will be evaluated by using Kim's (Kim 1977) equation 
</p><p><br/>V = (100 −M − A) /100 × [ Vw /Vd ]  [K(P)<sup>N</sup> - (b × T)]
<br/>Where,
<br/>V = Volume of methane gas adsorbed (cc/g) 
<br/>M = Moisture content (%) 
<br/>A = Ash content (%).
<br/>Vw/Vd =   1/(0.25 ×M + 1)
<br/>Vw = Volume of gas adsorbed on wet coal (cc/g) 
<br/>Vd = Volume of gas adsorbed on dry coal (cc/g)
<br/>The values of K and N depend on the rank of the coal and can be expressed in terms of ratio of fixed carbon (FC) to Volatile matter(VM)
<br/>K = 0.8 (F.C /V.M) + 5.6
Where
<br/>F.C = Fixed carbon (%)
<br/>VM = Volatile matter (%)
<br/>N = Composition of coal (for most bituminous coals, N = (0.39 - 0.013 × K)
<br/>b =Adsorption constant due to temperature change (cc/g/◦C).
<br/>T = Geothermal Gradient × (h/ 100) + To
<br/>T = Temperature at given depth
<br/>To = Ground temperature
<br/>h = Depth (m)
</p><p><br/><b>Estimation of methane content in coal seams by Karol curve</b>
</p><p><br/>In the absence measured methane content of coal beds, and production data from coal bed methane wells, gas content can be estimated using the Eddy curve. Eddy and others constructed a series of curves estimating maximum producible methane content of coal bed as a function of depth and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_rank" title="Coal rank">rank</a>.
<br/>The estimation of methane content of a coal bed is determined from the Eddy curve by locating the average depth of each coal seam on the depth axis. A normal line is extended upward from the depth axis (feet) to intersect the specific coal rank curves. A line from the point on the curve is extended normal to the lost and desorbed gas axis(cm<sup>3</sup>/gm). The intersection of the line and the axis is the estimated methane content of the coal seam.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretation_of_Ash_analysis">Interpretation of Ash analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalbed_methane_extraction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Interpretation of Ash analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ash_(analytical_chemistry)" title="Ash (analytical chemistry)">Ash</a> is an important indicator of <a href="/wiki/Clastic_rock" title="Clastic rock">clastic</a> input, derived from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_(ocean)" title="Marine (ocean)">marine</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluvial" title="Fluvial">fluvial</a> deposition of <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silt</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a> during <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a> development. Outcrop ash content appears to be less than ash content of subsurface samples. Lower ash contents of outcrop samples may be due to coal deposits being up <a href="/wiki/Strike_and_dip" title="Strike and dip">dip</a> and further away from a marine influence than samples down-dip.
</p>
