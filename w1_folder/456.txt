<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Autoregulation</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Autoregulation</b> is a process within many biological systems, resulting from an internal adaptive mechanism that works to adjust (or mitigate) that system's response to stimuli.  While most systems of the body show some degree of autoregulation, it is most clearly observed in the <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidney</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cvphysiology_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-cvphysiology-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Perfusion" title="Perfusion">Perfusion</a> of these organs is essential for life, and through autoregulation the body can divert blood (and thus, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>) where it is most needed.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cerebral_autoregulation">Cerebral autoregulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Autoregulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Cerebral autoregulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_autoregulation" title="Cerebral autoregulation">Cerebral autoregulation</a></div>
<p>More so than most other organs, the brain is very sensitive to increased or decreased blood flow, and several mechanisms (metabolic, myogenic, and neurogenic) are involved in maintaining an appropriate cerebral blood pressure. Brain blood flow autoregulation is abolished in several disease states such as <a href="/wiki/Traumatic_brain_injury" title="Traumatic brain injury">traumatic brain injury</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> brain tumors, or persistent abnormally high CO2 levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Homeometric_and_heterometric_autoregulation_of_the_heart">Homeometric and heterometric autoregulation of the heart</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Autoregulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Homeometric and heterometric autoregulation of the heart">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Homeometric autoregulation</b>, in the context of the <a href="/wiki/Circulatory_system" title="Circulatory system">circulatory system</a>, is the heart's ability to increase <a href="/wiki/Contractility" title="Contractility">contractility</a> and restore <a href="/wiki/Stroke_volume" title="Stroke volume">stroke volume</a> when <a href="/wiki/Afterload" title="Afterload">afterload</a> increases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  Homeometric autoregulation occurs <b>independently of cardiomyocyte fiber length,</b> via the Bowditch and/or Anrep effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Via the <a href="/wiki/Bowditch_effect" title="Bowditch effect">Bowditch effect</a>, positive <a href="/wiki/Inotrope" title="Inotrope">inotropy</a> occurs secondary to an increased cardiac frequency. The exact mechanism for this remains unknown, but it appears to be the result of an increased exposure of the heart to contractile substances arising from the increased flow caused by an increased cardiac frequency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>Via the <a href="/wiki/Anrep_effect" title="Anrep effect">Anrep effect</a>, positive inotropy occurs secondary to increased ventricular pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>This is in contrast to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frank%E2%80%93Starling_law_of_the_heart" title="Frank–Starling law of the heart"><b>heterometric regulation</b></a>, governed by the Frank-Starling law, which results from a more favorable positioning of actin and myosin filaments in cardiomyocytes <b>as a result of changing fiber lengths</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coronary_circulatory_autoregulation">Coronary circulatory autoregulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Autoregulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Coronary circulatory autoregulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since the heart is a very aerobic organ, needing oxygen for the efficient production of ATP &amp; Creatine Phosphate from fatty acids (and to a smaller extent, glucose &amp; very little lactate), the coronary circulation is auto regulated so that the heart receives the right flow of blood &amp; hence sufficient supply of oxygen. If a sufficient flow of oxygen is met and the resistance in the coronary circulation rises (perhaps due to vasoconstriction), then the coronary perfusion pressure (CPP) increases proportionally, to maintain the same flow. In this way, the same flow through the coronary circulation is maintained over a range of pressures. This part of coronary circulatory regulation is known as auto regulation and it occurs over a plateau, reflecting the constant blood flow at varying CPP &amp; resistance. The slope of a CBF (coronary blood flow) vs. CPP graph gives 1/Resistance.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Renal_autoregulation">Renal autoregulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Autoregulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Renal autoregulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tubuloglomerular_feedback" title="Tubuloglomerular feedback">Tubuloglomerular feedback</a></div>
<p>Regulation of renal blood flow is important to maintaining a stable glomerular filtration rate (GFR) despite changes in systemic blood pressure (within about 80-180 mmHg).  In a mechanism called <a href="/wiki/Tubuloglomerular_feedback" title="Tubuloglomerular feedback">tubuloglomerular feedback</a>, the kidney changes its own blood flow in response to changes in sodium concentration.  The sodium chloride levels in the urinary filtrate are sensed by the <a href="/wiki/Macula_densa" title="Macula densa">macula densa</a> cells at the end of the <a href="/wiki/Ascending_limb_of_loop_of_Henle" title="Ascending limb of loop of Henle">ascending limb</a>.  When sodium levels are moderately increased, the macula densa releases <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and reduces <a href="/wiki/Prostaglandin_E2" title="Prostaglandin E2">prostaglandin E2</a> release<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Komlosi_2004_463–469_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Komlosi_2004_463–469-10">[10]</a></sup> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juxtaglomerular_cells" title="Juxtaglomerular cells">juxtaglomerular cells</a> nearby. The juxtaglomerular cells in the afferent arteriole constrict, and juxtaglomerular cells in both the afferent and efferent arteriole decrease their renin secretion.  These actions function to lower GFR.  Further increase in sodium concentration leads to the release of <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a>, a vasodilating substance, to prevent excessive vasoconstriction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Komlosi_2004_463–469_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Komlosi_2004_463–469-10">[10]</a></sup>  In the opposite case, juxtaglomerular cells are stimulated to release more renin, which stimulates the <a href="/wiki/Renin%E2%80%93angiotensin_system" title="Renin–angiotensin system">renin–angiotensin system</a>, producing angiotensin I which is converted by Angio-Tensin Converting Enzyme  (ACE) to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angiotensin_II" title="Angiotensin II">angiotensin II</a>. Angiotensin II then causes preferential constriction of the efferent arteriole of the glomerulus and increases the GFR.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Autoregulation_of_genes">Autoregulation of genes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Autoregulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Autoregulation of genes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This is so-called "steady-state system". An example is a system in which a protein P that is a product of gene G "positively regulates its own production by binding to a regulatory element of the gene coding for it,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jablonka_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jablonka-11">[11]</a></sup> and the protein gets used or lost at a rate that increases as its concentration increases. This feedback loop creates two possible states "on" and "off". If an outside factor makes the concentration of P increase to some threshold level, the production of protein P is "on", i.e. P will maintain its own concentration at a certain level, until some other stimulus will lower it down below the threshold level, when concentration of P will be insufficient to make gene G express at the rate that would overcome the loss or use of the protein P.
This state ("on" or "off") gets inherited after cell division, since the concentration of protein a usually remains the same after mitosis. However, the state can be easily disrupted by outside factors.
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jablonka_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jablonka-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Similarly, this phenomenon is not only restricted to genes but may also apply to other genetic units, including mRNA transcripts. Regulatory segments of mRNA called a <a href="/wiki/Riboswitch" title="Riboswitch">Riboswitch</a> can autoregulate its transcription by sequestering cis-regulatory elements (particularly the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shine-Dalgarno" title="Shine-Dalgarno">Shine-Dalgarno</a> sequence) located on the same transcript as the Riboswitch. The Riboswitch stem-loop has a region complementary to the Shine-Dalgarno but is sequestered by complimentary base pairing in the loop. With sufficient ligand, the ligand may bind to the stem-loop and disrupt intermolecular bonding, resulting in the complimentary Shine-Dalgarno stem-loop segment binding to the complimentary Riboswitch segment, preventing Ribosome from binding, inhibiting translation.
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arXiv:1210.6998_[q-bio.MN]_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-arXiv:1210.6998_[q-bio.MN]-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
