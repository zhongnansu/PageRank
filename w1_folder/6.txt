<p>The <b>ecological footprint</b> measures human demand on nature, i.e., the quantity of nature it takes to support people or an economy. It tracks this demand through an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecological_accounting" title="Ecological accounting">ecological accounting</a> system. The accounts contrast the biologically productive area people use for their consumption to the biologically productive area available within a region or the world (<a href="/wiki/Biocapacity" title="Biocapacity">biocapacity</a> - the productive area that can regenerate what people demand from nature). In short, it is a measure of <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">human impact on Earth's ecosystem</a> and reveals the dependence of the human economy on <a href="/wiki/Natural_capital" title="Natural capital">natural capital</a>.
</p><p>The ecological footprint is defined as the biologically productive area needed to provide for everything people use: fruits and vegetables, fish, wood, fibers, absorption of carbon dioxide from <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> use, and space for buildings and roads.
</p><p>Footprint and biocapacity can be compared at the individual, regional, national or global scale. Both footprint and biocapacity change every year with number of people, per person consumption, efficiency of production, and productivity of ecosystems. At a global scale, footprint assessments show how big humanity's demand is compared to what planet <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> can renew. Since 2003, <a href="/wiki/Global_Footprint_Network" title="Global Footprint Network">Global Footprint Network</a> has calculated the ecological footprint from UN data sources for the world as a whole and for over 200 nations (known as the National Footprint Accounts). Every year the calculations are updated with the newest data. The time series are recalculated with every update since UN statistics also change historical data sets. As shown in Lin et al (2018)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-data.footprintnetwork.org_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-data.footprintnetwork.org-2">[2]</a></sup> the time trends for countries and the world have stayed consistent despite data updates. Also, a recent study by the Swiss Ministry of Environment independently recalculated the Swiss trends and reproduced them within 1-4% for the time period that they studied (1996-2015)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>. Global Footprint Network estimates that, as of 2014, humanity has been using <a href="/wiki/Natural_capital" title="Natural capital">natural capital</a> 1.7 times as fast as Earth can renew it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Data_Sources_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Data_Sources-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-data.footprintnetwork.org_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-data.footprintnetwork.org-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This means humanity's ecological footprint corresponds to 1.7 planet Earths.
</p><p>Ecological footprint analysis is widely used around the Earth in support of <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> assessments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-London_Ecological_Footprint_2003_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-London_Ecological_Footprint_2003-6">[6]</a></sup> It enables people to measure and manage the use of resources throughout the economy and explore the sustainability of individual <a href="/wiki/Lifestyle_(sociology)" title="Lifestyle (sociology)">lifestyles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goods_and_services" title="Goods and services">goods and services</a>, organizations, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industry_sector" title="Industry sector">industry sectors</a>, neighborhoods, cities, regions and nations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Since 2006, a first set of ecological footprint standards exist that detail both communication and calculation procedures. The latest version are the updated standards from 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Footprint_measurements_and_methodology">Footprint measurements and methodology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Footprint measurements and methodology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Blue_Marble_Western_Hemisphere.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3718" data-file-width="3718" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Blue_Marble_Western_Hemisphere.jpg/220px-Blue_Marble_Western_Hemisphere.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Blue_Marble_Western_Hemisphere.jpg/330px-Blue_Marble_Western_Hemisphere.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Blue_Marble_Western_Hemisphere.jpg/440px-Blue_Marble_Western_Hemisphere.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Blue_Marble_Western_Hemisphere.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a> of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> are <a href="/wiki/Non-renewable_resource" title="Non-renewable resource">finite</a>, and unsustainably strained by current levels of human activity.</div></div></div><p> For 2014, Global Footprint Network estimated humanity's ecological footprint as 1.7 planet Earths. This means that, according to their calculations, humanity's demands were 1.7 times faster than what the planet's ecosystems renewed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-data.footprintnetwork.org_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-data.footprintnetwork.org-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ecological footprints can be calculated at any scale: for an activity, a person, a community, a city, a town, a region, a nation, or humanity as a whole. Cities, due to their <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population concentration</a>, have large ecological footprints and have become ground zero for footprint reduction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The ecological footprint accounting method at the national level is described on the web page of Global Footprint Network <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Methodology_and_Data_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Methodology_and_Data-11">[11]</a></sup> or in greater detail in academic papers, including Borucke et al.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The National Accounts Review Committee has also published a research agenda on how to improve the accounts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first academic publication about ecological footprints was by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Rees_(academic)" title="William Rees (academic)">William Rees</a> in 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The ecological footprint concept and calculation method was developed as the PhD dissertation of <a href="/wiki/Mathis_Wackernagel" title="Mathis Wackernagel">Mathis Wackernagel</a>, under Rees' supervision at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_British_Columbia" title="University of British Columbia">University of British Columbia</a> in Vancouver, Canada, from 1990–1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Originally, Wackernagel and Rees called the concept "appropriated carrying capacity".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> To make the idea more accessible, Rees came up with the term "ecological footprint", inspired by a computer technician who praised his new computer's "small footprint on the desk".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In early 1996, Wackernagel and Rees published the book <i>Our Ecological Footprint: Reducing Human Impact on the Earth</i> with illustrations by Phil Testemale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wackernagel_&amp;_Rees_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wackernagel_&amp;_Rees-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Footprint values at the end of a survey are categorized for Carbon, Food, Housing, and Goods and Services as well as the total footprint number of Earths needed to sustain the world's population at that level of consumption. This approach can also be applied to an activity such as the manufacturing of a product or driving of a car. This resource accounting is similar to <a href="/wiki/Life-cycle_assessment" title="Life-cycle assessment">life-cycle analysis</a> wherein the consumption of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> (<a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fiber" title="Fiber">fiber</a>), <a href="/wiki/Building_material" title="Building material">building material</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">resources</a> are converted into a normalized measure of land area called <a href="/wiki/Global_hectare" title="Global hectare">global hectares</a> (gha).
</p><p>Per capita ecological footprint (EF), or ecological footprint analysis (EFA), is a means of comparing consumption and lifestyles, and checking this against nature's ability to provide for this consumption. The tool can inform policy by examining to what extent a nation uses more (or less) than is available within its territory, or to what extent the nation's lifestyle would be replicable worldwide. The footprint can also be a useful tool to educate people about <a href="/wiki/Carrying_capacity" title="Carrying capacity">carrying capacity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Overconsumption" title="Overconsumption">overconsumption</a>, with the aim of altering personal behavior. Ecological footprints may be used to argue that many current lifestyles are not <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable</a>. Such a global comparison also clearly shows the inequalities of resource use on this planet at the beginning of the twenty-first century.
</p><p>In 2007, the average biologically productive area per person worldwide was approximately 1.8 <a href="/wiki/Global_hectare" title="Global hectare">global hectares</a> (gha) per capita. The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a> footprint per capita was 9.0 gha, and that of <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> was 5.6 gha, while <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>'s was 1.8 gha.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chambers_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chambers-20">[20]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature" title="World Wide Fund for Nature">WWF</a> claims that the human footprint has exceeded the <a href="/wiki/Biocapacity" title="Biocapacity">biocapacity</a> (the available supply of natural resources) of the planet by 20%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Wackernagel and Rees originally estimated that the available biological capacity for the 6 billion people on Earth at that time was about 1.3 hectares per person, which is smaller than the 1.8 global hectares published for 2006, because the initial studies neither used global hectares nor included bioproductive marine areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wackernagel_&amp;_Rees_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wackernagel_&amp;_Rees-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>A number of NGOs offer ecological footprint calculators (<i>see</i> <a href="#External_links">Footprint Calculator</a>, below).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Studies_in_the_United_Kingdom">Studies in the United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Studies in the United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The UK's average ecological footprint is 5.45 <a href="/wiki/Global_hectare" title="Global hectare">global hectares</a> per capita (gha) with variations between regions ranging from 4.80 gha (Wales) to 5.56 gha (East England).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chambers_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chambers-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Two recent studies<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> have examined relatively low-impact small communities. <a href="/wiki/BedZED" title="BedZED">BedZED</a>, a 96-home <a href="/wiki/Mixed-income_housing" title="Mixed-income housing">mixed-income housing</a> development in South <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, was designed by Bill Dunster Architects and sustainability consultants BioRegional for the <a href="/wiki/Peabody_Trust" title="Peabody Trust">Peabody Trust</a>. Despite being populated by relatively "mainstream" home-buyers, BedZED was found to have a footprint of 3.20 gha due to on-site renewable energy production, energy-efficient architecture, and an extensive green lifestyles program that included on-site London's first <a href="/wiki/Carsharing" title="Carsharing">carsharing</a> club. The report did not measure the added footprint of the 15,000 visitors who have toured BedZED since its completion in 2002. <a href="/wiki/Findhorn_Ecovillage" title="Findhorn Ecovillage">Findhorn Ecovillage</a>, a rural <a href="/wiki/Intentional_community" title="Intentional community">intentional community</a> in <a href="/wiki/Moray" title="Moray">Moray</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, had a total footprint of 2.56 gha, including both the many guests and visitors who travel to the community to undertake residential courses there and the nearby campus of <a href="/wiki/Cluny_Hill" title="Cluny Hill">Cluny Hill</a> College. However, the residents alone have a footprint of 2.71 gha, a little over half the UK national average and one of the lowest ecological footprints of any community measured so far in the industrialized world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Keveral Farm, an organic farming community in Cornwall, was found to have a footprint of 2.4 gha, though with substantial differences in footprints among community members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_footprint_at_the_individual_level">Ecological footprint at the individual level</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ecological footprint at the individual level">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a 2012 study of consumers acting "green" vs. "brown" (where green people are «expected to have significantly lower ecological impact than “brown” consumers»), the conclusion was "the research found no significant difference between the carbon footprints of green and brown consumers".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corvinus_University_of_Budapest_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Corvinus_University_of_Budapest-26">[26]</a></sup>  A 2013 study concluded the same.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2017 study published in <i><a href="/wiki/Environmental_Research_Letters" title="Environmental Research Letters">Environmental Research Letters</a></i> posited that the most significant way individuals could reduce their own carbon footprint is to have fewer children, followed by living without a vehicle, forgoing air travel and adopting a plant-based diet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reviews_and_critiques">Reviews and critiques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reviews and critiques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Early criticism was published by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jeroen_C.J.M._van_den_Bergh" title="Jeroen C.J.M. van den Bergh">van den Bergh</a> and Verbruggen in 1999,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-van_den_Bergh_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-van_den_Bergh-30">[30]</a></sup> which was updated in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-van_den_BerghGrazi2014_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-van_den_BerghGrazi2014-31">[31]</a></sup> Another criticism was published in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fiala2008_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fiala2008-32">[32]</a></sup> A more complete review commissioned by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Directorate-General_for_the_Environment_(European_Commission)" title="Directorate-General for the Environment (European Commission)">Directorate-General for the Environment (European Commission)</a> was published in June 2008. The review found Ecological Footprint "a useful indicator for assessing progress on the EU’s Resource Strategy" the authors noted that Ecological Footprint analysis was unique "in its ability to relate resource use to the concept of carrying capacity." The review noted that further improvements in data quality, methodologies and assumptions were needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>A recent critique of the concept is due to Blomqvist et al., 2013a,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blomqvist1_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blomqvist1-34">[34]</a></sup> with a reply from Rees and Wackernagel, 2013,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rees_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rees-35">[35]</a></sup> and a rejoinder by Blomqvist et al., 2013b.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blomqvist2_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blomqvist2-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>An additional strand of critique is due to Giampietro and Saltelli (2014a),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G&amp;S1_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-G&amp;S1-37">[37]</a></sup> with a reply from Goldfinger et al., 2014,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goldfinger_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goldfinger-38">[38]</a></sup> a rejoinder by Giampietro and Saltelli (2014a),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-G&amp;S2_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-G&amp;S2-39">[39]</a></sup> and additional comments from van den Bergh and Grazi (2015).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vdBerg&amp;Grazi_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-vdBerg&amp;Grazi-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>A number of countries have engaged in research collaborations to test the validity of the method. This includes Switzerland, Germany, United Arab Emirates, and Belgium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>Grazi et al. (2007) have performed a systematic comparison of the ecological footprint method with spatial welfare analysis that includes environmental <a href="/wiki/Externality" title="Externality">externalities</a>, agglomeration effects and trade advantages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> They find that the two methods can lead to very distinct, and even opposite, rankings of different spatial patterns of economic activity. However this should not be surprising, since the two methods address different research questions.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Peter_Newman_(environmental_scientist)" title="Peter Newman (environmental scientist)">Newman</a> (2006) has argued that the ecological footprint concept may have an anti-urban bias, as it does not consider the opportunities created by urban growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Calculating the ecological footprint for densely populated areas, such as a city or small country with a comparatively large population — e.g. New York and Singapore respectively — may lead to the perception of these populations as "parasitic". This is because these communities have little intrinsic biocapacity, and instead must rely upon large <i><a href="/wiki/Hinterland" title="Hinterland">hinterlands</a></i>. Critics argue that this is a dubious characterization since mechanized rural farmers in developed nations may easily consume more resources than urban inhabitants, due to transportation requirements and the unavailability of <a href="/wiki/Economies_of_scale" title="Economies of scale">economies of scale</a>. Furthermore, such moral conclusions seem to be an argument for <a href="/wiki/Autarky" title="Autarky">autarky</a>. Some even take this train of thought a step further, claiming that the Footprint denies the benefits of trade.  Therefore, the critics argue that the Footprint can only be applied globally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>The method seems to reward the replacement of original ecosystems with high-productivity agricultural <a href="/wiki/Monoculture" title="Monoculture">monocultures</a> by assigning a higher biocapacity to such regions. For example, replacing ancient woodlands or tropical forests with monoculture forests or plantations may improve the ecological footprint. Similarly, if <a href="/wiki/Organic_farming" title="Organic farming">organic farming</a> yields were lower than those of conventional methods, this could result in the former being "penalized" with a larger ecological footprint.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Of course, this insight, while valid, stems from the idea of using the footprint as one's only metric. If the use of ecological footprints are complemented with other indicators, such as one for <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>, the problem might be solved. Indeed, <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature" title="World Wide Fund for Nature">WWF</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Living_Planet_Index" title="Living Planet Index">Living Planet Report</a> complements the biennial Footprint calculations with the Living Planet Index of biodiversity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Manfred Lenzen and Shauna Murray have created a modified Ecological Footprint that takes biodiversity into account for use in Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the ecological footprint model prior to 2008 treated <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> in the same manner as coal power,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Questions_and_Answers_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Questions_and_Answers-48">[48]</a></sup> the actual real world effects of the two are radically different. A life cycle analysis centered on the Swedish <a href="/wiki/Forsmark_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Forsmark Nuclear Power Plant">Forsmark Nuclear Power Plant</a> estimated carbon dioxide emissions at 3.10 g/kW⋅h<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> and 5.05 g/kW⋅h in 2002 for the <a href="/wiki/Torness_Nuclear_Power_Station" title="Torness Nuclear Power Station">Torness Nuclear Power Station</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> This compares to 11 g/kW⋅h for hydroelectric power, 950 g/kW⋅h for installed coal, 900 g/kW⋅h for oil and 600 g/kW⋅h for natural gas generation in the United States in 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> Figures released by Mark Hertsgaard, however, show that because of the delays in building nuclear plants and the costs involved, investments in energy efficiency and renewable energies have seven times the return on investment of investments in nuclear energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="floatright"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Greenhouse_emissions_by_electricity_source.PNG" title="The Vattenfall study found Nuclear, Hydro, and Wind to have far less greenhouse emissions than other sources represented."><img alt="The Vattenfall study found Nuclear, Hydro, and Wind to have far less greenhouse emissions than other sources represented." data-file-height="537" data-file-width="944" height="284" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Greenhouse_emissions_by_electricity_source.PNG/500px-Greenhouse_emissions_by_electricity_source.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Greenhouse_emissions_by_electricity_source.PNG/750px-Greenhouse_emissions_by_electricity_source.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d3/Greenhouse_emissions_by_electricity_source.PNG 2x" width="500"/></a></div>
<p>The Swedish utility <a href="/wiki/Vattenfall" title="Vattenfall">Vattenfall</a> did a study of full <a href="/wiki/Life-cycle_greenhouse-gas_emissions_of_energy_sources" title="Life-cycle greenhouse-gas emissions of energy sources">life-cycle greenhouse-gas emissions of energy sources</a> the utility uses to produce electricity, namely: Nuclear, Hydro, Coal, Gas, Solar Cell, Peat and Wind. The net result of the study was that nuclear power produced 3.3 grams of carbon dioxide per kW⋅h of produced power. This compares to 400 for <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> and 700 for <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> (according to this study). The study also concluded that nuclear power produced the smallest amount of CO<sub>2</sub> of any of their electricity sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>Claims exist that the problems of nuclear waste do not come anywhere close to approaching the problems of fossil fuel waste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> A 2004 article from the BBC states: "The <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> (WHO) says 3 million people are killed worldwide by outdoor air pollution annually from vehicles and industrial emissions, and 1.6 million indoors through using solid fuel."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> In the U.S. alone, fossil fuel waste kills 20,000 people each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> A coal power plant releases 100 times as much radiation as a nuclear power plant of the same wattage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colmain_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-colmain-58">[58]</a></sup> It is estimated that during 1982, US coal burning released 155 times as much radioactivity into the atmosphere as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three_Mile_Island" title="Three Mile Island">Three Mile Island</a> incident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> In addition, fossil fuel waste causes <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, which leads to increased deaths from hurricanes, flooding, and other weather events. The <a href="/wiki/World_Nuclear_Association" title="World Nuclear Association">World Nuclear Association</a> provides a comparison of deaths due to accidents among different forms of energy production. In their comparison, deaths per TW-yr of electricity produced (in UK and USA) from 1970 to 1992 are quoted as 885 for hydropower, 342 for coal, 85 for natural gas, and 8 for nuclear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wna_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-wna-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Western Australian government State of the Environment Report included an Ecological Footprint measure for the average Western Australian seven times the average footprint per person on the planet in 2007, a total of about 15 hectares.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Footprint_by_country">Footprint by country</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecological_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Footprint by country">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint_sustainability.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="522" data-file-width="850" height="184" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint_sustainability.jpg/300px-Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint_sustainability.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint_sustainability.jpg/450px-Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint_sustainability.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint_sustainability.jpg/600px-Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint_sustainability.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint_sustainability.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ecological footprint for different nations compared to their <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Index" title="Human Development Index">Human Development Index</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_ecological_footprint" title="List of countries by ecological footprint">List of countries by ecological footprint</a></div>
<p>The world-average ecological footprint in 2013 was 2.8 global hectares per person.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-data.footprintnetwork.org_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-data.footprintnetwork.org-2">[2]</a></sup> The average per country ranges from over 10 to under 1 global hectares per person. There is also a high variation within countries, based on individual lifestyle and economic possibilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Humanity_Now_Demanding_1.6_Earths_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Humanity_Now_Demanding_1.6_Earths-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GHG_footprint" title="GHG footprint">GHG footprint</a> or the more narrow <a href="/wiki/Carbon_footprint" title="Carbon footprint">carbon footprint</a> are a component of the ecological footprint. Often, when only the carbon footprint is reported, it is expressed in weight of CO2 (or CO2e representing GHG warming potential (GGWP)), but it can also be expressed in land areas like ecological footprints. Both can be applied to products, people or whole societies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guidance_on_how_to_measure_and_report_your_greenhouse_gas_emissions_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guidance_on_how_to_measure_and_report_your_greenhouse_gas_emissions-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p>
