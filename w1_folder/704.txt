<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Mathis Wackernagel</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Mathis Wackernagel</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Swiss</a>-born <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> advocate.  He is President of <a href="/wiki/Global_Footprint_Network" title="Global Footprint Network">Global Footprint Network</a>, an international sustainability <a href="/wiki/Think_tank" title="Think tank">think tank</a> with offices in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oakland" title="Oakland">Oakland</a>, California; Brussels, Belgium, and Geneva, Switzerland. The think-tank is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-profit" title="Non-profit">non-profit</a> that focuses on developing and promoting <a href="/wiki/Metric_(mathematics)" title="Metric (mathematics)">metrics</a> for sustainability.
</p><p>After earning a degree in <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_engineering" title="Mechanical engineering">mechanical engineering</a> from the <a href="/wiki/ETH_Zurich" title="ETH Zurich">Swiss Federal Institute of Technology</a>, he completed his Ph.D. in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Community_planning" title="Community planning">community</a> and <a href="/wiki/Regional_planning" title="Regional planning">regional planning</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_British_Columbia" title="University of British Columbia">University of British Columbia</a> in Vancouver, Canada in 1994. There, as his doctoral dissertation under Professor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Rees_(academic)" title="William Rees (academic)">William Rees</a>, he created with Professor Rees the <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a> concept and developed the methodology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> 
He has worked on sustainability issues for organizations in Europe, Latin America, North America, Asia and Australia.  Wackernagel previously served as the director of the Sustainability Program at Redefining Progress in Oakland, California (1999 - 2003), and directed the Centre for Sustainability Studies / Centro de Estudios para la Sustentabilidad in Mexico (1995-2001). In 2004, he was also an adjunct faculty at SAGE of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wisconsin%E2%80%93Madison" title="University of Wisconsin–Madison">University of Wisconsin–Madison</a>. In 2010, he was appointed Frank H. T. Rhodes Class of 1956 Visiting Professor at <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a> (1 July 2011 – 30 June 2013).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Wackernagel has said that "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Overshoot_(ecology)" title="Overshoot (ecology)">Overshoot</a> will ultimately liquidate the planet's ecological assets."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_honors">Awards and honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mathis_Wackernagel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Awards and honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wackernagel, along with Susan Burns, received the Skoll Award for Social Entrepreneurship from the Skoll Foundation in 2007. He received an honorary doctorate from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bern" title="University of Bern">University of Bern</a> in 2007, a 2006 <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature" title="World Wide Fund for Nature">World Wide Fund for Nature</a> Award for Conservation Merit, and the 2005 <a href="/wiki/Herman_Daly" title="Herman Daly">Herman Daly</a> Award of the US Society for Ecological Economics. With Global Footprint Network, he received the International Prize <a href="/wiki/Calouste_Gulbenkian" title="Calouste Gulbenkian">Calouste Gulbenkian</a> 2008 (Lisbon, Portugal) “dedicated to the respect for biodiversity and defense of the environment in man’s relationship with nature.”
</p><p>In 2013, Mathis Wackernagel received the <i>Prix Nature Swisscanto</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Prior, he received the <i>2012 Binding-Prize for Nature Conservation</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> the bi-annual Kenneth Boulding Award of the <i><a class="external text" href="http://www.isecoeco.org" rel="nofollow">International Society for Ecological Economics</a></i>, and the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Planet_Prize" title="Blue Planet Prize">Blue Planet Prize</a> of the Asahi Glass Foundation (the latter two with <a href="/wiki/William_E._Rees" title="William E. Rees">William E. Rees</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He also received the <i>2011 Zayed International Prize for Environment</i> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Data_Sources_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Data_Sources-8">[8]</a></sup> in the category "action leading to positive change in society."  The Zayed prize recognized Wackernagel’s contribution to “translate[ing] the complexity of humanity's impact on the environment and natural resources into a more understandable and actionable form. The concept of ‘ecological limits' and relating the demands of human beings to the planet's available ecological resources, has attracted and is catalyzing action among governments, business and civil society."
</p><p>The <a class="external text" href="http://enrichlist.org" rel="nofollow">(En)Rich List</a> ranked Mathis Wackernagel as the 19th of the 100 most inspirational individuals whose contributions enrich paths to sustainable futures.(<a class="external free" href="http://enrichlist.org/the-list/mathis-wackernagel/" rel="nofollow">http://enrichlist.org/the-list/mathis-wackernagel/</a>). John Elkington identified Mathis in 2012 among the “Zeronaut 50” Roll of Honor,i.e., leading pioneers who are driving the world’s most significant problems to zero (<a class="external free" href="http://www.csrwire.com/blog/posts/455-unreasonable-people-introducing-the-zeronauts" rel="nofollow">http://www.csrwire.com/blog/posts/455-unreasonable-people-introducing-the-zeronauts</a>).
</p>
