<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>The <b>National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health</b> (<b>NIOSH</b>) (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="/aɪ/: 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="/ʃ/: 'sh' in 'shy'">ʃ</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_federal_agencies" title="List of United States federal agencies">United States federal agency</a> responsible for conducting research and making recommendations for the prevention of work-related <a href="/wiki/Occupational_injury" title="Occupational injury">injury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Occupational_disease" title="Occupational disease">illness</a>. NIOSH is part of the <a href="/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control_and_Prevention" title="Centers for Disease Control and Prevention">Centers for Disease Control and Prevention</a> (CDC) within the U.S. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_Health_and_Human_Services" title="Department of Health and Human Services">Department of Health and Human Services</a>.
</p><p>NIOSH is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, with research laboratories and offices in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cincinnati,_Ohio" title="Cincinnati, Ohio">Cincinnati, Ohio</a>; <a href="/wiki/Morgantown,_West_Virginia" title="Morgantown, West Virginia">Morgantown, West Virginia</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pittsburgh,_Pennsylvania" title="Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania">Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denver,_Colorado" title="Denver, Colorado">Denver, Colorado</a>; <a href="/wiki/Anchorage,_Alaska" title="Anchorage, Alaska">Anchorage, Alaska</a>; <a href="/wiki/Spokane,_Washington" title="Spokane, Washington">Spokane, Washington</a>; and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlanta,_Georgia" title="Atlanta, Georgia">Atlanta, Georgia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> NIOSH is a professionally diverse organization with a staff of 1,200 people representing a wide range of disciplines including <a href="/wiki/Epidemiology" title="Epidemiology">epidemiology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Occupational_hygiene" title="Occupational hygiene">industrial hygiene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Occupational_safety_and_health" title="Occupational safety and health">safety</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>, engineering, chemistry, and statistics.
</p><p>The director of NIOSH is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Howard_(public_health_administrator)" title="John Howard (public health administrator)">John Howard</a>.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Act" title="Occupational Safety and Health Act">Occupational Safety and Health Act</a>, signed by <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_M._Nixon" title="Richard M. Nixon">Richard M. Nixon</a>, on December 29, 1970, created both NIOSH and the <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">Occupational Safety and Health Administration</a> (OSHA). NIOSH was established to help ensure safe and healthful working conditions by providing research, information, education, and training in the field of occupational safety and health. NIOSH provides national and world leadership to prevent work-related illness, injury, disability, and <a href="/wiki/Occupational_fatality" title="Occupational fatality">death</a> by gathering information, conducting scientific research, and translating the knowledge gained into products and services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-about-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Strategic_goals">Strategic goals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Strategic goals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>NIOSH abides by a strategic plan for meeting institutional goals and allocating resources. The Institute has three overarching goals:
</p>

<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“
</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;"><div><ul><li>Conduct research to reduce work-related illnesses and injuries</li><li>Promote safe and healthy workplaces through interventions, recommendations and capacity building</li><li>Enhance global workplace safety and health through international collaborations</li></ul></div>

<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”

<p>The goals are supported by NIOSH's program portfolio. The portfolio categorizes Institute efforts into 10 groups representing industrial sectors. The program portfolio further subdivides efforts into 24 cross sectors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="NIOSH_authority">NIOSH authority</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: NIOSH authority">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Unlike its counterpart, the Occupational Safety and Health Administration, NIOSH is not a regulatory agency. It does not issue safety and health standards that are enforceable under U.S. law. Rather, NIOSH's authority under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Act" title="Occupational Safety and Health Act">Occupational Safety and Health Act</a> [29 CFR § 671] is to "develop recommendations for health and safety standards", to "develop information on safe levels of exposure to toxic materials and harmful physical agents and substances", and to "conduct research on new safety and health problems". NIOSH may also "conduct on-site investigations (<a href="/wiki/Health_Hazard_Evaluation_Program" title="Health Hazard Evaluation Program">Health Hazard Evaluations</a>) to determine the toxicity of materials used in workplaces" and "fund research by other agencies or private organizations through grants, contracts, and other arrangements".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-About_NIOSH_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-About_NIOSH-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>NIOSH was intended to function as an agency at the same level as, and independent from, the Centers for Disease Control.  NIOSH was initially placed within the Centers for Disease Control in order to obtain administrative support from the Centers until NIOSH was ready to assume those responsibilities for itself; the Centers, however, never relinquished control and the original intent of the Act never came to pass.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Also, pursuant to its authority granted to it by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mine_Safety_and_Health_Act_of_1977" title="Mine Safety and Health Act of 1977">Mine Safety and Health Act of 1977</a>, NIOSH may "develop recommendations for mine health standards for the <a href="/wiki/Mine_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Mine Safety and Health Administration">Mine Safety and Health Administration</a>", "administer a medical surveillance program for miners, including chest X‑rays to detect <a href="/wiki/Pneumoconiosis" title="Pneumoconiosis">pneumoconiosis</a> (<a href="/wiki/Coalworker%27s_pneumoconiosis" title="Coalworker's pneumoconiosis">black lung</a> disease) in coal miners", "conduct on-site investigations in mines similar to those authorized for general industry under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Act" title="Occupational Safety and Health Act">Occupational Safety and Health Act</a>; and "test and certify <a href="/wiki/Personal_protective_equipment" title="Personal protective equipment">personal protective equipment</a> and hazard-measurement instruments".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-About_NIOSH_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-About_NIOSH-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="NIOSH_products_and_publications">NIOSH products and publications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: NIOSH products and publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>NIOSH research covers a wide range of fields. The knowledge obtained through intramural and extramural research programs is used to develop products and publication offering innovative solutions for a wide range of work settings. Some of the publications produced by NIOSH include:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Alerts</i> are put out by the agency to request assistance in preventing, solving, and controlling newly identified occupational hazards. They briefly present what is known about the risk for occupational injury, illness, and death.</li>
<li><i>Criteria Documents</i> contain recommendations for the prevention of occupational diseases and injuries. These documents are submitted to the <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">Occupational Safety and Health Administration</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Mine_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Mine Safety and Health Administration">Mine Safety and Health Administration</a> for consideration in their formulation of legally binding safety and health standards.</li>
<li><i>Current Intelligence Bulletins</i> analyze new information about occupational health and safety hazards.</li>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/National_Agricultural_Safety_Database" title="National Agricultural Safety Database">National Agricultural Safety Database</a></i> contains citations and summaries of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scholarly_journal" title="Scholarly journal">scholarly journal</a> articles and reports about agricultural health and safety.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Fatality_Assessment_and_Control_Evaluation" title="Fatality Assessment and Control Evaluation">Fatality Assessment and Control Evaluation</a> program publishes <a href="/wiki/Occupational_fatality" title="Occupational fatality">occupational fatality</a> data that are used to publish fatality reports by specific sectors of industry and types of fatal incidents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <i><a href="/wiki/NIOSH_Power_Tools_Database" title="NIOSH Power Tools Database">NIOSH Power Tools Database</a></i> contains sound power levels, sound pressure levels, and vibrations data for a variety of common power tools that have been tested by NIOSH researchers.</li>
<li>The <i>NIOSH Hearing Protection Device Compendium</i> contains attenuation information and features for commercially available earplugs, earmuffs and semi-aural insert devices (canal caps).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="external text" href="https://www.cdc.gov/niosh/nmam/default.html" rel="nofollow"><i>NIOSH Manual of Analytical Methods</i></a> contains recommendations for collection, sampling and analysis of contaminants in the workplace and industrial hygiene samples, including air filters, biological fluids, wipes and bulks for occupationally relevant analytes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <i><a class="external text" href="https://www.cdc.gov/niosh/npg/default.html" rel="nofollow">NIOSH Pocket Guide to Chemical Hazards</a></i> informs workers, employers, and occupational health professionals about workplace chemicals and their hazards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="NIOSH_education_and_research_centers">NIOSH education and research centers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: NIOSH education and research centers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/NIOSH_Education_and_Research_Centers" title="NIOSH Education and Research Centers">NIOSH Education and Research Centers</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/NIOSH_Education_and_Research_Centers" title="NIOSH Education and Research Centers">NIOSH Education and Research Centers</a> are multidisciplinary centers supported by the National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health for education and research in the field of occupational health. Through the centers, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NIOSH" title="NIOSH">NIOSH</a> supports academic degree programs and research opportunities, as well as continuing education for OSH professionals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The ERCs, distributed in regions across the United States, establish academic, labor, and industry research partnerships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The research conducted at the centers is related to the <a href="/wiki/National_Occupational_Research_Agenda" title="National Occupational Research Agenda">National Occupational Research Agenda</a> (NORA) established by NIOSH.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Founded in 1977, NIOSH ERCs are responsible for nearly half of post-baccalaureate graduates entering occupational health and safety fields. The ERCs focus on industrial hygiene, occupational health nursing, occupational medicine, occupational safety, and other areas of specialization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> At many ERCs, students in specific disciplines have their tuition paid in full and receive additional stipend money.  ERCs provide a benefit to local businesses by offering reduced price assessments to local businesses.
</p>
