<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Ecosystem</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

</p>
<div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">A community of living organisms together with the nonliving components of their environment</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about natural ecosystems. For the term used in man-made systems, see <a href="/wiki/Digital_ecosystem" title="Digital ecosystem">Digital ecosystem</a>.</div>

<p>An <b>ecosystem</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Community_(ecology)" title="Community (ecology)">community</a> made up of living organisms and <a href="/wiki/Abiotic_component" title="Abiotic component">nonliving components</a> such as air, water, and mineral soil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Ecosystems can be studied in two different ways. They can be thought of as interdependent collections of plants and animals, or as structured systems and communities governed by general rules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sagoff2003_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sagoff2003-4">[4]</a></sup> The living (<a href="/wiki/Biotic_component" title="Biotic component">biotic</a>) and non-living (<a href="/wiki/Abiotic_component" title="Abiotic component">abiotic</a>) components interact through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nutrient_cycles" title="Nutrient cycles">nutrient cycles</a> and energy flows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odum1971_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Odum1971-5">[5]</a></sup> Ecosystems include interactions among organisms, and between organisms and their environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schulze400_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schulze400-6">[6]</a></sup> Ecosystems can be of any size but each ecosystem has a specific, limited space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  Some scientists view the entire planet as one ecosystem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-planet_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-planet-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Energy, water, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> and soil minerals are essential abiotic components of an ecosystem. The energy used by ecosystems comes primarily from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a>, via <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>. Photosynthesis uses energy from the sun and also captures <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> from the atmosphere. <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">Animals</a> also play an important role in the movement of matter and energy through ecosystems. They influence the amount of plant and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microbe" title="Microbe">microbial</a> <a href="/wiki/Biomass_(ecology)" title="Biomass (ecology)">biomass</a> that lives in the system. As organic matter dies, carbon is released back into the atmosphere. This process also facilitates <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nutrient_cycling" title="Nutrient cycling">nutrient cycling</a> by converting nutrients stored in dead biomass back to a form that can be used again by plants and other microbes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin10_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin10-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ecosystems are controlled by both external and internal factors. External factors such as <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Parent_material" title="Parent material">parent material</a> that forms the soil, <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a> and time each affect ecosystems. However, these external factors are not themselves influenced by the ecosystem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin11_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin11-10">[10]</a></sup>  Ecosystems are dynamic: they are subject to periodic disturbances and are often in the process of recovering from past <a href="/wiki/Disturbance_(ecology)" title="Disturbance (ecology)">disturbances</a> and seeking balance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin281_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin281-11">[11]</a></sup> Internal factors are different: They not only control ecosystem processes but are also controlled by them. Another way of saying this is that internal factors are subject to <a href="/wiki/Feedback" title="Feedback">feedback loops</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin11_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin11-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">Humans</a> operate within ecosystems and can influence both internal and external factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin11_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin11-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">Global warming</a> is an example of a cumulative effect of human activities. Ecosystems provide benefits, called "<a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a>", which people depend on for their <a href="/wiki/Livelihood" title="Livelihood">livelihood</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_management" title="Ecosystem management">Ecosystem management</a> is more efficient than trying to manage individual <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>.</p><div class="toclimit-3"><div class="toc" id="toc"><input class="toctogglecheckbox" id="toctogglecheckbox" role="button" style="display:none" type="checkbox"/><div class="toctitle" dir="ltr" lang="en"><h2>Contents</h2><span class="toctogglespan"><label class="toctogglelabel" for="toctogglecheckbox"></label></span></div>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span></h2>
<p>There is no single definition of what constitutes an ecosystem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sagoff2003_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sagoff2003-4">[4]</a></sup> German ecologist Ernst-Detlef Schulze and coauthors defined an ecosystem as an area which is "uniform regarding the biological turnover, and contains all the fluxes above and below the ground area under consideration." They explicitly reject <a href="/wiki/Gene_Likens" title="Gene Likens">Gene Likens</a>' use of entire <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">river catchments</a> as "too wide a demarcation" to be a single ecosystem, given the level of heterogeneity within such an area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schulze399_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schulze399-12">[12]</a></sup> Other authors have suggested that an ecosystem can encompass a much larger area, even the whole planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-planet_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-planet-8">[8]</a></sup> Schulze and coauthors also rejected the idea that a single rotting log could be studied as an ecosystem because the size of the flows between the log and its surroundings are too large, relative to the proportion cycles within the log.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schulze399_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schulze399-12">[12]</a></sup> Philosopher of science Mark Sagoff considers the failure to define "the kind of object it studies" to be an obstacle to the development of theory in <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_ecology" title="Ecosystem ecology">ecosystem ecology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sagoff2003_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sagoff2003-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ecosystems can be studied in a variety of ways. Those include theoretical studies or more practical studies that monitor specific ecosystems over long periods of time or look at differences between ecosystems to better understand how they work. Some studies involve experimenting with direct manipulation of the ecosystem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carpenter1998_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carpenter1998-13">[13]</a></sup> Studies can be carried out at a variety of scales, ranging from whole-ecosystem studies to studying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microcosm:_Model_/_experimental_ecosystem" title="Microcosm: Model / experimental ecosystem">microcosms</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mesocosm" title="Mesocosm">mesocosms</a> (simplified representations of ecosystems).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schindler1998_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schindler1998-14">[14]</a></sup> American ecologist <a href="/wiki/Stephen_R._Carpenter" title="Stephen R. Carpenter">Stephen R. Carpenter</a> has argued that microcosm experiments can be "irrelevant and diversionary" if they are not carried out in conjunction with field studies done at the ecosystem scale. Microcosm experiments often fail to accurately predict ecosystem-level dynamics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carpenter1996_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carpenter1996-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubbard_Brook_Ecosystem_Study" title="Hubbard Brook Ecosystem Study">Hubbard Brook Ecosystem Study</a> started in 1963 to study the <a href="/wiki/White_Mountains_(New_Hampshire)" title="White Mountains (New Hampshire)">White Mountains in New Hampshire</a>. It was the first successful attempt to study an entire <a href="/wiki/Watershed_management" title="Watershed management">watershed</a> as an ecosystem. The study used stream <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> as a means of monitoring ecosystem properties, and developed a detailed <a href="/wiki/Biogeochemistry" title="Biogeochemistry">biogeochemical model</a> of the ecosystem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lindenmayer2010_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lindenmayer2010-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Long_Term_Ecological_Research_Network" title="Long Term Ecological Research Network">Long-term research</a> at the site led to the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a> in North America in 1972. Researchers documented the depletion of soil <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cations" title="Cations">cations</a> (especially calcium) over the next several decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Likens2004_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Likens2004-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_concepts">Related concepts</span></h3>

Aquatic ecosystems are split into <a href="/wiki/Marine_ecosystem" title="Marine ecosystem">marine ecosystems</a> and <a href="/wiki/Freshwater_ecosystem" title="Freshwater ecosystem">freshwater ecosystems</a>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="External_and_internal_factors">External and internal factors</span></h3>
<p>Ecosystems are controlled both by external and internal factors. External factors, also called state factors, control the overall structure of an ecosystem and the way things work within it, but are not themselves influenced by the ecosystem. The most important of these is <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin11_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin11-10">[10]</a></sup> Climate determines the <a href="/wiki/Biome" title="Biome">biome</a> in which the ecosystem is embedded. Rainfall patterns and seasonal temperatures influence photosynthesis and thereby determine the amount of water and energy available to the ecosystem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin11_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin11-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Parent_material" title="Parent material">Parent material</a> determines the nature of the soil in an ecosystem, and influences the supply of mineral nutrients. <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">Topography</a> also controls ecosystem processes by affecting things like <a href="/wiki/Microclimate" title="Microclimate">microclimate</a>, soil development and the movement of water through a system. For example, ecosystems can be quite different if situated in a small depression on the landscape, versus one present on an adjacent steep hillside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin11_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin11-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other external factors that play an important role in ecosystem functioning include time and potential <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biota_(ecology)" title="Biota (ecology)">biota</a>. Similarly, the set of organisms that can potentially be present in an area can also significantly affect ecosystems. Ecosystems in similar environments that are located in different parts of the world can end up doing things very differently simply because they have different pools of species present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin11_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin11-10">[10]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">introduction of non-native species</a> can cause substantial shifts in ecosystem function.
</p><p>Unlike external factors, internal factors in ecosystems not only control ecosystem processes but are also controlled by them. Consequently, they are often subject to <a href="/wiki/Feedback" title="Feedback">feedback loops</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin11_10-7"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin11-10">[10]</a></sup> While the <a href="/wiki/Resource_(biology)" title="Resource (biology)">resource</a> inputs are generally controlled by external processes like climate and parent material, the availability of these resources within the ecosystem is controlled by internal factors like decomposition, root competition or shading.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin11_10-8"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin11-10">[10]</a></sup> Other factors like disturbance, succession or the types of species present are also internal factors. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_production">Primary production</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seawifs_global_biosphere.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="819" data-file-width="1280" height="256" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Seawifs_global_biosphere.jpg/400px-Seawifs_global_biosphere.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Seawifs_global_biosphere.jpg/600px-Seawifs_global_biosphere.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Seawifs_global_biosphere.jpg/800px-Seawifs_global_biosphere.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Seawifs_global_biosphere.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Global oceanic and terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Phototroph" title="Phototroph">phototroph</a> abundance, from September 1997 to August 2000. As an estimate of <a href="/wiki/Autotroph" title="Autotroph">autotroph</a> biomass, it is only a rough indicator of primary production potential and not an actual estimate of it.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Primary_production" title="Primary production">Primary production</a></div>
<p>Primary production is the production of <a href="/wiki/Organic_matter" title="Organic matter">organic matter</a> from inorganic carbon sources. This mainly occurs through <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>. The energy incorporated through this process supports life on earth, while the carbon makes up much of the organic matter in living and dead biomass, <a href="/wiki/Soil_carbon" title="Soil carbon">soil carbon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>. It also drives the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a>, which influences global <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> via the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>.
</p><p>Through the process of photosynthesis, plants capture energy from light and use it to combine <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and water to produce <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>. The photosynthesis carried out by all the plants in an ecosystem is called the gross primary production (GPP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin97_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin97-18">[18]</a></sup> About 48–60% of the GPP is consumed in plant respiration. 
</p><p>The remainder, that portion of GPP that is not used up by respiration, is known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Net_primary_production" title="Net primary production">net primary production</a> (NPP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin123_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin123-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_flow">Energy flow</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Energy_flow_(ecology)" title="Energy flow (ecology)">Energy flow (ecology)</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Food_web" title="Food web">Food web</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trophic_level" title="Trophic level">Trophic level</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">Energy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> enter ecosystems through <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>, are incorporated into living tissue, transferred to other organisms that feed on the living and dead plant matter, and eventually released through respiration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin123_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin123-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>The carbon and energy incorporated into plant tissues (net primary production) is either consumed by animals while the plant is alive, or it remains uneaten when the plant tissue dies and becomes <a href="/wiki/Detritus" title="Detritus">detritus</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_ecosystem" title="Terrestrial ecosystem">terrestrial ecosystems</a>, roughly 90% of the net primary production ends up being broken down by <a href="/wiki/Decomposition" title="Decomposition">decomposers</a>. The remainder is either consumed by animals while still alive and enters the plant-based trophic system, or it is consumed after it has died, and enters the detritus-based trophic system. 
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_ecosystem" title="Aquatic ecosystem">aquatic systems</a>, the proportion of plant biomass that gets consumed by <a href="/wiki/Herbivore" title="Herbivore">herbivores</a> is much higher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin244_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin244-20">[20]</a></sup>
In trophic systems photosynthetic organisms are the primary producers. The organisms that consume their tissues are called primary consumers or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secondary_production" title="Secondary production">secondary producers</a>—<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbivores" title="Herbivores">herbivores</a>. Organisms which feed on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microbe" title="Microbe">microbes</a> (<a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a>) are termed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microbivore" title="Microbivore">microbivores</a>. Animals that feed on primary consumers—<a href="/wiki/Carnivore" title="Carnivore">carnivores</a>—are secondary consumers. Each of these constitutes a <a href="/wiki/Trophic_level" title="Trophic level">trophic level</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin244_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin244-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>The sequence of consumption—from plant to herbivore, to carnivore—forms a <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a>. Real systems are much more complex than this—organisms will generally feed on more than one form of food, and may feed at more than one trophic level. Carnivores may capture some prey which are part of a plant-based trophic system and others that are part of a detritus-based trophic system (a bird that feeds both on herbivorous grasshoppers and earthworms, which consume detritus). Real systems, with all these complexities, form <a href="/wiki/Food_web" title="Food web">food webs</a> rather than food chains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin244_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin244-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecosystem_ecology">Ecosystem ecology</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_ecology" title="Ecosystem ecology">Ecosystem ecology</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_model" title="Ecosystem model">Ecosystem model</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:East_Scotia_Ridge_-_Plos_Biol_04.tif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1447" data-file-width="2031" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/East_Scotia_Ridge_-_Plos_Biol_04.tif/lossy-page1-220px-East_Scotia_Ridge_-_Plos_Biol_04.tif.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/East_Scotia_Ridge_-_Plos_Biol_04.tif/lossy-page1-330px-East_Scotia_Ridge_-_Plos_Biol_04.tif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/East_Scotia_Ridge_-_Plos_Biol_04.tif/lossy-page1-440px-East_Scotia_Ridge_-_Plos_Biol_04.tif.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:East_Scotia_Ridge_-_Plos_Biol_04.tif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent" title="Hydrothermal vent">hydrothermal vent</a> is an ecosystem on the ocean floor. (The scale bar is 1 m.)</div></div></div>
<p>Ecosystem ecology studies "the flow of energy and materials through organisms and the physical environment". It seeks to understand the processes which govern the stocks of material and energy in ecosystems, and the flow of matter and energy through them. The study of ecosystems can cover 10 <a href="/wiki/Order_of_magnitude" title="Order of magnitude">orders of magnitude</a>, from the surface layers of rocks to the surface of the planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decomposition">Decomposition</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Decomposition" title="Decomposition">Decomposition</a></div>
<p>The carbon and nutrients in <a href="/wiki/Soil_organic_matter" title="Soil organic matter">dead organic matter</a> are broken down by a group of processes known as <a href="/wiki/Decomposition" title="Decomposition">decomposition</a>. This releases nutrients that can then be re-used for plant and microbial production and returns carbon dioxide to the atmosphere (or water) where it can be used for <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>. In the absence of decomposition, the dead organic matter would accumulate in an ecosystem, and nutrients and atmospheric carbon dioxide would be depleted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin151_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin151-22">[22]</a></sup> Approximately 90% of terrestrial net primary production goes directly from plant to decomposer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin244_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin244-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Decomposition processes can be separated into three categories—<a href="/wiki/Leaching_(agriculture)" title="Leaching (agriculture)">leaching</a>, fragmentation and chemical alteration of dead material. 
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Leaching">Leaching</span></h4>
<p>As water moves through dead organic matter, it dissolves and carries with it the water-soluble components. These are then taken up by organisms in the soil, react with mineral soil, or are transported beyond the confines of the ecosystem (and are considered lost to it).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin151_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin151-22">[22]</a></sup> Newly shed leaves and newly dead animals have high concentrations of water-soluble components and include <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a> and mineral nutrients. Leaching is more important in wet environments and much less important in dry ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin151_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin151-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fragmentation">Fragmentation</span></h4>
<p>Fragmentation processes break organic material into smaller pieces, exposing new surfaces for colonization by microbes. Freshly shed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leaf_litter" title="Leaf litter">leaf litter</a> may be inaccessible due to an outer layer of <a href="/wiki/Plant_cuticle" title="Plant cuticle">cuticle</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bark_(botany)" title="Bark (botany)">bark</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Protoplasm" title="Protoplasm">cell contents</a> are protected by a <a href="/wiki/Cell_wall" title="Cell wall">cell wall</a>. Newly dead animals may be covered by an <a href="/wiki/Exoskeleton" title="Exoskeleton">exoskeleton</a>. Fragmentation processes, which break through these protective layers, accelerate the rate of microbial decomposition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin151_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin151-22">[22]</a></sup> Animals fragment detritus as they hunt for food, as does passage through the gut. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freeze-thaw_cycle" title="Freeze-thaw cycle">Freeze-thaw cycles</a> and cycles of wetting and drying also fragment dead material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin151_22-4"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin151-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_alteration">Chemical alteration</span></h4>
<p>The chemical alteration of the dead organic matter is primarily achieved through bacterial and fungal action. Fungal <a href="/wiki/Hypha" title="Hypha">hyphae</a> produce enzymes which can break through the tough outer structures surrounding dead plant material. They also produce enzymes which break down <a href="/wiki/Lignin" title="Lignin">lignin</a>, which allows them access to both cell contents and to the nitrogen in the lignin. Fungi can transfer carbon and nitrogen through their hyphal networks and thus, unlike bacteria, are not dependent solely on locally available resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin151_22-5"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin151-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Decomposition_rates">Decomposition rates</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Decomposition#Rate_of_decomposition" title="Decomposition">Decomposition § Rate of decomposition</a></div>
<p>Decomposition rates vary among ecosystems. The rate of decomposition is governed by three sets of factors—the physical environment (temperature, moisture, and soil properties), the quantity and quality of the dead material available to decomposers, and the nature of the microbial community itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin159_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin159-23">[23]</a></sup> Temperature controls the rate of microbial respiration; the higher the temperature, the faster microbial decomposition occurs. It also affects soil moisture, which slows microbial growth and reduces leaching. Freeze-thaw cycles also affect decomposition—freezing temperatures kill soil microorganisms, which allows leaching to play a more important role in moving nutrients around. This can be especially important as the soil thaws in the spring, creating a pulse of nutrients which become available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin159_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin159-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Decomposition rates are low under very wet or very dry conditions. Decomposition rates are highest in wet, moist conditions with adequate levels of oxygen. Wet soils tend to become deficient in oxygen (this is especially true in <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a>), which slows microbial growth. In dry soils, decomposition slows as well, but bacteria continue to grow (albeit at a slower rate) even after soils become too dry to support plant growth.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutrient_cycling">Nutrient cycling</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Nutrient_cycle" title="Nutrient cycle">Nutrient cycle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biogeochemical_cycle" title="Biogeochemical cycle">Biogeochemical cycle</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nitrogen_Cycle.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="370" data-file-width="475" height="312" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Nitrogen_Cycle.jpg/400px-Nitrogen_Cycle.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/de/Nitrogen_Cycle.jpg 1.5x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nitrogen_Cycle.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Biological nitrogen cycling</div></div></div>
<p>Ecosystems continually exchange energy and carbon with the wider <a href="/wiki/Environment_(systems)" title="Environment (systems)">environment</a>. Mineral nutrients, on the other hand, are mostly cycled back and forth between plants, animals, microbes and the soil. Most nitrogen enters ecosystems through biological <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation" title="Nitrogen fixation">nitrogen fixation</a>, is deposited through precipitation, dust, gases or is applied as <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin197_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin197-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Nitrogen_cycle">Nitrogen cycle</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_cycle" title="Nitrogen cycle">Nitrogen cycle</a></div>
<p>Since most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terrestrial_ecosystems" title="Terrestrial ecosystems">terrestrial ecosystems</a> are nitrogen-limited, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_cycle" title="Nitrogen cycle">nitrogen cycling</a> is an important control on ecosystem production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin197_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin197-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Until modern times, nitrogen fixation was the major source of nitrogen for ecosystems. Nitrogen-fixing bacteria either live <a href="/wiki/Symbiosis" title="Symbiosis">symbiotically</a> with plants or live freely in the soil. The energetic cost is high for plants which support nitrogen-fixing symbionts—as much as 25% of gross primary production when measured in controlled conditions. Many members of the <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">legume</a> plant family support nitrogen-fixing symbionts. Some <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">cyanobacteria</a> are also capable of nitrogen fixation. These are <a href="/wiki/Phototroph" title="Phototroph">phototrophs</a>, which carry out photosynthesis. Like other nitrogen-fixing bacteria, they can either be free-living or have symbiotic relationships with plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin197_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin197-24">[24]</a></sup> Other sources of nitrogen include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acid_deposition" title="Acid deposition">acid deposition</a> produced through the combustion of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> gas which evaporates from agricultural fields which have had fertilizers applied to them, and dust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin197_24-3"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin197-24">[24]</a></sup> Anthropogenic nitrogen inputs account for about 80% of all nitrogen fluxes in ecosystems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin197_24-4"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin197-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>When plant tissues are shed or are eaten, the nitrogen in those tissues becomes available to animals and microbes. Microbial decomposition releases nitrogen compounds from dead organic matter in the soil, where plants, fungi, and bacteria compete for it. Some soil bacteria use organic nitrogen-containing compounds as a source of carbon, and release <a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">ammonium</a> ions into the soil. This process is known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ammonification" title="Ammonification">nitrogen mineralization</a>. Others convert ammonium to <a href="/wiki/Nitrite" title="Nitrite">nitrite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrate</a> ions, a process known as <a href="/wiki/Nitrification" title="Nitrification">nitrification</a>. <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">Nitric oxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a> are also produced during nitrification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin197_24-5"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin197-24">[24]</a></sup> Under nitrogen-rich and oxygen-poor conditions, nitrates and nitrites are converted to <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen gas</a>, a process known as <a href="/wiki/Denitrification" title="Denitrification">denitrification</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin197_24-6"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin197-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_nutrients">Other nutrients</span></h4>
<p>Other important nutrients include <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin215_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin215-25">[25]</a></sup> Phosphorus enters ecosystems through <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a>. As ecosystems age this supply diminishes, making phosphorus-limitation more common in older landscapes (especially in the tropics).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin215_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin215-25">[25]</a></sup> Calcium and sulfur are also produced by weathering, but acid deposition is an important source of sulfur in many ecosystems. Although magnesium and manganese are produced by weathering, exchanges between soil organic matter and living cells account for a significant portion of ecosystem fluxes. Potassium is primarily cycled between living cells and soil organic matter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin215_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin215-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Function_and_biodiversity">Function and biodiversity</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">Biodiversity</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:View_of_loch_lomond.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/View_of_loch_lomond.JPG/220px-View_of_loch_lomond.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/View_of_loch_lomond.JPG/330px-View_of_loch_lomond.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/View_of_loch_lomond.JPG/440px-View_of_loch_lomond.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:View_of_loch_lomond.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Loch_Lomond" title="Loch Lomond">Loch Lomond</a> in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> forms a relatively isolated ecosystem. The fish community of this lake has remained stable over a long period until a number of <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">introductions</a> in the 1970s restructured it's <a href="/wiki/Food_web" title="Food web">food web</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Spiny_Forest_Ifaty_Madagascar.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="1200" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Spiny_Forest_Ifaty_Madagascar.jpg/220px-Spiny_Forest_Ifaty_Madagascar.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Spiny_Forest_Ifaty_Madagascar.jpg/330px-Spiny_Forest_Ifaty_Madagascar.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Spiny_Forest_Ifaty_Madagascar.jpg/440px-Spiny_Forest_Ifaty_Madagascar.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Spiny_Forest_Ifaty_Madagascar.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Spiny forest at Ifaty, <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>, featuring various <i><a href="/wiki/Adansonia" title="Adansonia">Adansonia</a></i> (baobab) species, <i><a href="/wiki/Alluaudia_procera" title="Alluaudia procera">Alluaudia procera</a></i> (Madagascar ocotillo) and other vegetation.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">Biodiversity</a> plays an important role in ecosystem functioning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schulze449_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schulze449-27">[27]</a></sup> The reason for this is that ecosystem processes are driven by the number of species in an ecosystem, the exact nature of each individual species, and the relative abundance organisms within these species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin265_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin265-28">[28]</a></sup> Ecosystem processes are broad generalizations that actually take place through the actions of individual organisms. The nature of the organisms—the species, <a href="/wiki/Functional_group_(ecology)" title="Functional group (ecology)">functional groups</a> and trophic levels to which they belong—dictates the sorts of actions these individuals are capable of carrying out and the relative efficiency with which they do so. 
</p><p>Ecological theory suggests that in order to coexist, species must have some level of <a href="/wiki/Limiting_similarity" title="Limiting similarity">limiting similarity</a>—they must be different from one another in some fundamental way, otherwise one species would <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Competitive_exclusion" title="Competitive exclusion">competitively exclude</a> the other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-niche_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-niche-29">[29]</a></sup> Despite this, the cumulative effect of additional species in an ecosystem is not linear—additional species may enhance nitrogen retention, for example, but beyond some level of species richness, additional species may have little additive effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin265_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin265-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>The addition (or loss) of species which are ecologically similar to those already present in an ecosystem tends to only have a small effect on ecosystem function. Ecologically distinct species, on the other hand, have a much larger effect. Similarly, dominant species have a large effect on ecosystem function, while rare species tend to have a small effect. <a href="/wiki/Keystone_species" title="Keystone species">Keystone species</a> tend to have an effect on ecosystem function that is disproportionate to their abundance in an ecosystem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin265_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin265-28">[28]</a></sup> Similarly, an <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_engineer" title="Ecosystem engineer">ecosystem engineer</a> is any <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organism</a> that creates, significantly modifies, maintains or destroys a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitat_(ecology)" title="Habitat (ecology)">habitat</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dynamics">Dynamics</span></h3>
<p>Ecosystems are dynamic entities. They are subject to periodic disturbances and are in the process of recovering from some past disturbance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin281_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin281-11">[11]</a></sup> When a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perturbation_(biology)" title="Perturbation (biology)">perturbation</a> occurs, an ecoystem responds by moving away from its initial state. The tendency of an ecosystem to remain close to its equilibrium state, despite that disturbance, is termed its <a href="/wiki/Resistance_(ecology)" title="Resistance (ecology)">resistance</a>. On the other hand, the speed with which it returns to its initial state after disturbance is called its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resilience_(ecology)" title="Resilience (ecology)">resilience</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin281_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin281-11">[11]</a></sup> Time plays a role in the development of soil from bare rock and the <a href="/wiki/Ecological_succession" title="Ecological succession">recovery of a community from disturbance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin11_10-9"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin11-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>From one year to another, ecosystems experience variation in their biotic and abiotic environments. A drought, an especially cold winter and a pest outbreak all constitute short-term variability in environmental conditions. Animal populations vary from year to year, building up during resource-rich periods and crashing as they overshoot their food supply. These changes play out in changes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Net_primary_production" title="Net primary production">net primary production</a> <a href="/wiki/Decomposition" title="Decomposition">decomposition</a> rates, and other ecosystem processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin281_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin281-11">[11]</a></sup> Longer-term changes also shape ecosystem processes—the forests of eastern North America still show legacies of cultivation which ceased 200 years ago, while <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> production in eastern <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberian</a> lakes is controlled by organic matter which accumulated during the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin281_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin281-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Disturbance_(ecology)" title="Disturbance (ecology)">Disturbance</a> also plays an important role in ecological processes. <a href="/wiki/F._Stuart_Chapin_III" title="F. Stuart Chapin III">F. Stuart Chapin</a> and coauthors define disturbance as "a relatively discrete event in time and space that alters the structure of populations, communities, and ecosystems and causes changes in resources availability or the physical environment".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin_285_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin_285-30">[30]</a></sup> This can range from tree falls and insect outbreaks to hurricanes and wildfires to volcanic eruptions. Such disturbances can cause large changes in plant, animal and microbe populations, as well soil organic matter content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin281_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin281-11">[11]</a></sup> Disturbance is followed by <a href="/wiki/Ecological_succession" title="Ecological succession">succession</a>, a "directional change in ecosystem structure and functioning resulting from biotically driven changes in resources supply."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin_285_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin_285-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>The frequency and severity of disturbance determine the way it affects ecosystem function. A major disturbance like a volcanic eruption or <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glacial</a> advance and retreat leave behind soils that lack plants, animals or organic matter. Ecosystems that experience such disturbances undergo <a href="/wiki/Primary_succession" title="Primary succession">primary succession</a>. A less severe disturbance like forest fires, hurricanes or cultivation result in <a href="/wiki/Secondary_succession" title="Secondary succession">secondary succession</a> and a faster recovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin281_11-6"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin281-11">[11]</a></sup> More severe disturbance and more frequent disturbance result in longer recovery times. 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_methods">Classification methods</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_diversity" title="Ecosystem diversity">Ecosystem diversity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion" title="Ecoregion">Ecoregion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecological_land_classification" title="Ecological land classification">Ecological land classification</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ecotope" title="Ecotope">Ecotope</a></div>
<p>Classifying ecosystems into ecologically homogeneous units is an important step towards effective ecosystem management.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lugo1999_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lugo1999-31">[31]</a></sup> There is no single, agreed-upon way to do this. A variety of systems exist, based on vegetation cover, remote sensing, and <a href="/wiki/Holdridge_life_zones" title="Holdridge life zones">bioclimatic classification</a> systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lugo1999_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lugo1999-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ecological_land_classification" title="Ecological land classification">Ecological land classification</a> is a cartographical delineation or <a href="/wiki/Regionalisation" title="Regionalisation">regionalisation</a> of distinct <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecological</a> areas, identified by their <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soils</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vegetation" title="Vegetation">vegetation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> conditions, living species, <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitats</a>, water resources, and sometimes also <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/anthropic" title="wikt:anthropic">anthropic</a> factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_activities">Human activities</span></h2>
<p>Human activities are important in almost all ecosystems. Although humans exist and operate within ecosystems, their cumulative effects are large enough to influence external factors like climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin11_10-10"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin11-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecosystem_goods_and_services">Ecosystem goods and services</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Avalanche_Lake,_looking_south.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Avalanche_Lake%2C_looking_south.jpg/220px-Avalanche_Lake%2C_looking_south.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Avalanche_Lake%2C_looking_south.jpg/330px-Avalanche_Lake%2C_looking_south.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Avalanche_Lake%2C_looking_south.jpg/440px-Avalanche_Lake%2C_looking_south.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Avalanche_Lake,_looking_south.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/High_Peaks_Wilderness_Area" title="High Peaks Wilderness Area">High Peaks Wilderness Area</a> in the 6,000,000-acre (2,400,000 ha) <a href="/wiki/Adirondack_Park" title="Adirondack Park">Adirondack Park</a> is an example of a diverse ecosystem.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">Ecosystem services</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecological_goods_and_services" title="Ecological goods and services">Ecological goods and services</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_valuation" title="Ecosystem valuation">Ecosystem valuation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecological_yield" title="Ecological yield">Ecological yield</a></div>
<p>Ecosystems provide a variety of goods and services upon which people depend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christensen1998_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Christensen1998-33">[33]</a></sup> Ecosystem goods include the "tangible, material products" of ecosystem processes such as food, construction material, medicinal plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2007_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2007-34">[34]</a></sup> They also include less tangible items like <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourism</a> and recreation, and genes from wild plants and animals that can be used to improve domestic species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christensen1998_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Christensen1998-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">Ecosystem services</a>, on the other hand, are generally "improvements in the condition or location of things of value".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2007_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2007-34">[34]</a></sup> These include things like the maintenance of hydrological cycles, cleaning air and water, the maintenance of oxygen in the atmosphere, crop <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollination</a> and even things like beauty, inspiration and opportunities for research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christensen1998_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-Christensen1998-33">[33]</a></sup> While ecosystem goods have traditionally been recognized as being the basis for things of economic value, ecosystem services tend to be taken for granted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2007_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2007-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecosystem_management">Ecosystem management</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_management" title="Ecosystem management">Ecosystem management</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">Ecological economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">Sustainability</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">Sustainable development</a></div>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource_management" title="Natural resource management">natural resource management</a> is applied to whole ecosystems, rather than single species, it is termed <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_management" title="Ecosystem management">ecosystem management</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grumbine1994_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grumbine1994-35">[35]</a></sup> Although definitions of ecosystem management abound, there is a common set of principles which underlie these definitions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin362_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin362-36">[36]</a></sup> A fundamental principle is the long-term <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> of the production of goods and services by the ecosystem;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin362_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin362-36">[36]</a></sup> "intergenerational sustainability [is] a precondition for management, not an afterthought".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christensen1998_33-3"><a href="#cite_note-Christensen1998-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>While ecosystem management can be used as part of a plan for <a href="/wiki/Wilderness" title="Wilderness">wilderness</a> conservation, it can also be used in intensively managed ecosystems<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christensen1998_33-4"><a href="#cite_note-Christensen1998-33">[33]</a></sup> (see, for example, <a href="/wiki/Agroecosystem" title="Agroecosystem">agroecosystem</a> and <a href="/wiki/Close_to_nature_forestry" title="Close to nature forestry">close to nature forestry</a>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats_caused_by_humans">Threats caused by humans</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Planetary_boundaries" title="Planetary boundaries">Planetary boundaries</a></div>
<p>As human population and per capita consumption grow, so do the resource demands imposed on ecosystems and the effects of the human <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a>. Natural resources are vulnerable and limited. The environmental impacts of <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a> actions are becoming more apparent. Problems for all ecosystems include: <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">environmental pollution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_loss" title="Biodiversity loss">biodiversity loss</a>. For <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_ecosystem" title="Terrestrial ecosystem">terrestrial ecosystems</a> further threats include <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soil_retrogression_and_degradation" title="Soil retrogression and degradation">soil degradation</a>,  and <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>. For <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aquatic_ecosystems" title="Aquatic ecosystems">aquatic ecosystems</a> threats include also unsustainable exploitation of marine resources (for example <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a> of certain species), <a href="/wiki/Marine_pollution" title="Marine pollution">marine pollution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microplastics" title="Microplastics">microplastics</a> pollution, <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">water pollution</a>, and building on coastal areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Society is increasingly becoming aware that <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a> are not only limited but also that they are threatened by human activities. The need to better consider long-term <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_health" title="Ecosystem health">ecosystem health</a> and its role in enabling human habitation and economic activity is urgent. To help inform decision-makers, many ecosystem services are being assigned economic values, often based on the cost of replacement with anthropogenic alternatives. The ongoing challenge of prescribing economic value to nature, for example through <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_banking" title="Biodiversity banking">biodiversity banking</a>, is prompting transdisciplinary shifts in how we recognize and manage the environment, <a href="/wiki/Social_responsibility" title="Social responsibility">social responsibility</a>, business opportunities, and our future as a species.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>The term "ecosystem" was first used in 1935 in a publication by British ecologist <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Tansley" title="Arthur Tansley">Arthur Tansley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[fn 1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Willis1997_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Willis1997-39">[38]</a></sup> Tansley devised the concept to draw attention to the importance of transfers of materials between organisms and their environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin7_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin7-40">[39]</a></sup> He later refined the term, describing it as "The whole system, ... including not only the organism-complex, but also the whole complex of physical factors forming what we call the environment".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tansley1935_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tansley1935-41">[40]</a></sup> Tansley regarded ecosystems not simply as natural units, but as "mental isolates".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tansley1935_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tansley1935-41">[40]</a></sup> Tansley later defined the spatial extent of ecosystems using the term <a href="/wiki/Ecotope" title="Ecotope">ecotope</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tansley1939_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tansley1939-42">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/G._Evelyn_Hutchinson" title="G. Evelyn Hutchinson">G. Evelyn Hutchinson</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Limnologist" title="Limnologist">limnologist</a> who was a contemporary of Tansley's, combined <a href="/wiki/Charles_Sutherland_Elton" title="Charles Sutherland Elton">Charles Elton</a>'s ideas about <a href="/wiki/Trophic_level" title="Trophic level">trophic</a> ecology with those of Russian geochemist <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Vernadsky" title="Vladimir Vernadsky">Vladimir Vernadsky</a>. As a result, he suggested that mineral nutrient availability in a lake limited <a href="/wiki/Algal_bloom" title="Algal bloom">algal production</a>. This would, in turn, limit the abundance of animals that feed on algae. <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Lindeman" title="Raymond Lindeman">Raymond Lindeman</a> took these ideas further to suggest that the flow of energy through a lake was the primary driver of the ecosystem. Hutchinson's students, brothers <a href="/wiki/Howard_T._Odum" title="Howard T. Odum">Howard T. Odum</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eugene_P._Odum" title="Eugene P. Odum">Eugene P. Odum</a>, further developed a "systems approach" to the study of ecosystems. This allowed them to study the flow of energy and material through ecological systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chapin7_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chapin7-40">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
