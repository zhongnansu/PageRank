<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Deposition (phase transition)</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Frost_patterns_4.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2640" data-file-width="3960" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Frost_patterns_4.jpg/300px-Frost_patterns_4.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Frost_patterns_4.jpg/450px-Frost_patterns_4.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Frost_patterns_4.jpg/600px-Frost_patterns_4.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Frost_patterns_4.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">Water vapor</a> from humid winter-air deposits directly into a solid, crystalline frost pattern on a window, without ever being liquid in the process.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Deposition</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_process" title="Thermodynamic process">thermodynamic process</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Phase_transition" title="Phase transition">phase transition</a> in which <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> transforms into <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solid</a> without passing through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_phase" title="Liquid phase">liquid phase</a>. The reverse of deposition is <a href="/wiki/Sublimation_(phase_transition)" title="Sublimation (phase transition)">sublimation</a> and hence sometimes deposition is called <b>desublimation</b>.
</p><p>One example of deposition is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Process_(science)" title="Process (science)">process</a> by which, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sub-freezing" title="Sub-freezing">sub-freezing</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> changes directly to <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a> without first becoming a <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a>. This is how <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a> forms in <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">clouds</a>, as well as how <a href="/wiki/Frost" title="Frost">frost</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hoar_frost" title="Hoar frost">hoar frost</a> form on the ground or other surfaces. Another example is when frost forms on a leaf. For deposition to occur, thermal energy must be removed from a gas. When the air becomes cold enough, water vapor in the air surrounding the leaf loses enough thermal energy to change into a solid. Even though the air temperature may be below the dew point, the water vapor may not be able to condense spontaneously if there is no way to remove the latent heat.  When the leaf is introduced, the supercooled water vapor immediately begins to condense, but by this point is already past the freezing point.  This causes the water vapor to change directly into a solid.
</p><p>Another example is the <a href="/wiki/Soot" title="Soot">soot</a> that is deposited on the walls of chimneys. Soot molecules rise from the fire in a hot and gaseous state. When they come into contact with the walls they cool, and change to the solid state, without formation of the liquid state. The process is made use of industrially in <a href="/wiki/Combustion_chemical_vapor_deposition" title="Combustion chemical vapor deposition">combustion chemical vapor deposition</a>.
</p><p>There is an industrial coatings process, known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evaporative_deposition" title="Evaporative deposition">evaporative deposition</a>, whereby a solid material is heated to the gaseous state in a low-pressure chamber, the gas molecules travel across the chamber space and then condense to the solid state on a target surface, forming a smooth and thin layer on the target surface. Again, the molecules do not go through an intermediate liquid state when going from the gas to the solid. See also <a href="/wiki/Physical_vapor_deposition" title="Physical vapor deposition">physical vapor deposition</a>, which is a class of processes used to deposit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thin_films" title="Thin films">thin films</a> of various materials onto various surfaces.
</p><p>Deposition releases energy and is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermic</a> phase change.
</p>
