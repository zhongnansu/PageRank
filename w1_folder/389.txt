<p><b>Crustaceans</b> (<b>Crustacea</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/ʌ/: 'u' in 'cut'">ʌ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/eɪ/: 'a' in 'face'">eɪ</span><span title="/ʃ/: 'sh' in 'shy'">ʃ</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>) form a large, diverse <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropod</a> <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxon</a> which includes such familiar animals as <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lobster" title="Lobster">lobsters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crayfish" title="Crayfish">crayfish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shrimp" title="Shrimp">shrimp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Krill" title="Krill">krill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Woodlouse" title="Woodlouse">woodlice</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Barnacle" title="Barnacle">barnacles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
The crustacean group is usually treated as a <a href="/wiki/Subphylum" title="Subphylum">subphylum</a>, and thanks to recent molecular studies it is now well accepted that the crustacean group is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paraphyletic" title="Paraphyletic">paraphyletic</a>, and comprises all animals in the <a href="/wiki/Pancrustacea" title="Pancrustacea">Pancrustacea</a> clade other than <a href="/wiki/Hexapoda" title="Hexapoda">hexapods</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rota-Stabelli2010_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rota-Stabelli2010-2">[2]</a></sup> Some crustaceans are more closely related to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insects" title="Insects">insects</a> and other hexapods than they are to certain other crustaceans.
</p><p>The 67,000 described species range in size from <i><a href="/wiki/Stygotantulus" title="Stygotantulus">Stygotantulus stocki</a></i> at 0.1 mm (0.004 in), to the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_spider_crab" title="Japanese spider crab">Japanese spider crab</a> with a leg span of up to 3.8 m (12.5 ft) and a mass of 20 kg (44 lb).  Like other <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a>, crustaceans have an <a href="/wiki/Exoskeleton" title="Exoskeleton">exoskeleton</a>, which they <a href="/wiki/Ecdysis" title="Ecdysis">moult</a> to grow. They are distinguished from other groups of arthropods, such as <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myriapoda" title="Myriapoda">myriapods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chelicerata" title="Chelicerata">chelicerates</a>, by the possession of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biramous" title="Biramous">biramous</a> (two-parted) limbs, and by their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crustacean_larvae" title="Crustacean larvae">larval forms</a>, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nauplius_(larva)" title="Nauplius (larva)">nauplius</a> stage of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Branchiopod" title="Branchiopod">branchiopods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Copepod" title="Copepod">copepods</a>.
</p><p>Most crustaceans are free-living <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_animal" title="Aquatic animal">aquatic animals</a>, but some are <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_animal" title="Terrestrial animal">terrestrial</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Woodlouse" title="Woodlouse">woodlice</a>), some are <a href="/wiki/Parasitism" title="Parasitism">parasitic</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Rhizocephala" title="Rhizocephala">Rhizocephala</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argulidae" title="Argulidae">fish lice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pentastomida" title="Pentastomida">tongue worms</a>) and some are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sessility_(zoology)" title="Sessility (zoology)">sessile</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Barnacle" title="Barnacle">barnacles</a>). The group has an extensive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_record" title="Fossil record">fossil record</a>, reaching back to the <a href="/wiki/Cambrian" title="Cambrian">Cambrian</a>, and includes <a href="/wiki/Living_fossil" title="Living fossil">living fossils</a> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Triops_cancriformis" title="Triops cancriformis">Triops cancriformis</a></i>, which has existed apparently unchanged since the <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Triassic</a> period. More than 10 million tons of crustaceans are produced by fishery or farming for human consumption, the majority of it being <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shrimp_and_prawn" title="Shrimp and prawn">shrimp and prawns</a>. <a href="/wiki/Krill" title="Krill">Krill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Copepod" title="Copepod">copepods</a> are not as widely fished, but may be the animals with the greatest <a href="/wiki/Biomass_(ecology)" title="Biomass (ecology)">biomass</a> on the planet, and form a vital part of the food chain. The scientific study of crustaceans is known as <a href="/wiki/Carcinology" title="Carcinology">carcinology</a> (alternatively, <i>malacostracology</i>, <i>crustaceology</i> or <i>crustalogy</i>), and a <a href="/wiki/Scientist" title="Scientist">scientist</a> who works in carcinology is a <a href="/wiki/List_of_carcinologists" title="List of carcinologists">carcinologist</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crustacean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Crab_from_Long_Island.jpg"><img alt="A convex oval-shaped piece of shell, covered with fine orange-pink markings: the front edge is lined with 13 coarse serrations, while the rear edge is smooth." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1068" data-file-width="1424" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Crab_from_Long_Island.jpg/220px-Crab_from_Long_Island.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Crab_from_Long_Island.jpg/330px-Crab_from_Long_Island.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Crab_from_Long_Island.jpg/440px-Crab_from_Long_Island.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Crab_from_Long_Island.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A shed <a href="/wiki/Carapace" title="Carapace">carapace</a> of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lady_crab" title="Lady crab">lady crab</a>, part of the hard <a href="/wiki/Exoskeleton" title="Exoskeleton">exoskeleton</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Krillanatomykils.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="380" data-file-width="592" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Krillanatomykils.jpg/220px-Krillanatomykils.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Krillanatomykils.jpg/330px-Krillanatomykils.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Krillanatomykils.jpg/440px-Krillanatomykils.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Krillanatomykils.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Body structure of a typical crustacean – krill</div></div></div>
<p>The body of a crustacean is composed of segments, which are grouped into three regions: the <i>cephalon</i> or <a href="/wiki/Head" title="Head">head</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cephalon_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-cephalon-3">[3]</a></sup> the <i>pereon</i> or <a href="/wiki/Thorax" title="Thorax">thorax</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thorax_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-thorax-4">[4]</a></sup> and the <i>pleon</i> or <a href="/wiki/Abdomen" title="Abdomen">abdomen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abdomen_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-abdomen-5">[5]</a></sup> The head and thorax may be fused together to form a <a href="/wiki/Cephalothorax" title="Cephalothorax">cephalothorax</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> which may be covered by a single large <a href="/wiki/Carapace" title="Carapace">carapace</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The crustacean body is protected by the hard <a href="/wiki/Exoskeleton" title="Exoskeleton">exoskeleton</a>, which must be <a href="/wiki/Ecdysis" title="Ecdysis">moulted</a> for the animal to grow. The shell around each somite can be divided into a dorsal <a href="/wiki/Tergum" title="Tergum">tergum</a>, ventral <a href="/wiki/Sternum_(arthropod_anatomy)" title="Sternum (arthropod anatomy)">sternum</a> and a lateral pleuron. Various parts of the exoskeleton may be fused together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HR_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-HR-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>289</span></sup>
</p><p>Each <a href="/wiki/Somite" title="Somite">somite</a>, or body segment can bear a pair of <a href="/wiki/Appendage" title="Appendage">appendages</a>: on the segments of the head, these include two pairs of <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(biology)" title="Antenna (biology)">antennae</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mandible_(arthropod_mouthpart)" title="Mandible (arthropod mouthpart)">mandibles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maxilla_(arthropod_mouthpart)" title="Maxilla (arthropod mouthpart)">maxillae</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cephalon_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-cephalon-3">[3]</a></sup> the thoracic segments bear <a href="/wiki/Arthropod_leg" title="Arthropod leg">legs</a>, which may be specialised as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pereiopod" title="Pereiopod">pereiopods</a> (walking legs) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maxilliped" title="Maxilliped">maxillipeds</a> (feeding legs).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thorax_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-thorax-4">[4]</a></sup> The abdomen bears <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pleopod" title="Pleopod">pleopods</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abdomen_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-abdomen-5">[5]</a></sup> and ends in a <a href="/wiki/Telson" title="Telson">telson</a>, which bears the <a href="/wiki/Anus" title="Anus">anus</a>, and is often flanked by uropods to form a <a href="/wiki/Decapod_anatomy" title="Decapod anatomy">tail fan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telson_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-telson-9">[9]</a></sup> The number and variety of <a href="/wiki/Appendage" title="Appendage">appendages</a> in different crustaceans may be partly responsible for the group's success.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Crustacean <a href="/wiki/Appendage" title="Appendage">appendages</a> are typically <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biramous" title="Biramous">biramous</a>, meaning they are divided into two parts; this includes the second pair of antennae, but not the first, which is usually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uniramous" title="Uniramous">uniramous</a>, the exception being in the Class Malacostraca where the antennules may be generally biramous or even triramous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-antennule_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-antennule-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berkely_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Berkely-12">[12]</a></sup> It is unclear whether the biramous condition is a derived state which evolved in crustaceans, or whether the second branch of the limb has been lost in all other groups. <a href="/wiki/Trilobite" title="Trilobite">Trilobites</a>, for instance, also possessed biramous appendages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Hemolymph" title="Hemolymph">Hemolymph</a></div>
<p>The main body cavity is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_circulatory_system" title="Open circulatory system">open circulatory system</a>, where blood is pumped into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hemocoel" title="Hemocoel">haemocoel</a> by a <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a> located near the dorsum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Malacostraca have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haemocyanin" title="Haemocyanin">haemocyanin</a> as the oxygen-carrying pigment, while copepods, ostracods, barnacles and branchiopods have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haemoglobin" title="Haemoglobin">haemoglobins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The alimentary canal consists of a straight tube that often has a gizzard-like "gastric mill" for grinding food and a pair of digestive glands that absorb food; this structure goes in a spiral format.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Structures that function as kidneys are located near the antennae. A brain exists in the form of ganglia close to the antennae, and a collection of major ganglia is found below the gut.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encarta_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Encarta-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>In many <a href="/wiki/Decapoda" title="Decapoda">decapods</a>, the first (and sometimes the second) pair of pleopods are specialised in the male for sperm transfer. Many terrestrial crustaceans (such as the <a href="/wiki/Christmas_Island_red_crab" title="Christmas Island red crab">Christmas Island red crab</a>) mate seasonally and return to the sea to release the eggs. Others, such as <a href="/wiki/Woodlouse" title="Woodlouse">woodlice</a>, lay their eggs on land, albeit in damp conditions. In most decapods, the females retain the eggs until they hatch into free-swimming larvae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crustacean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The majority of crustaceans are aquatic, living in either marine or <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">freshwater</a> environments, but a few groups have <a href="/wiki/Adaptation" title="Adaptation">adapted</a> to life on land, such as <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_crab" title="Terrestrial crab">terrestrial crabs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coenobita" title="Coenobita">terrestrial hermit crabs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Woodlouse" title="Woodlouse">woodlice</a>. Marine crustaceans are as ubiquitous in the oceans as insects are on land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The majority of crustaceans are also <a href="/wiki/Motility" title="Motility">motile</a>, moving about independently, although a few taxonomic units are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parasite" title="Parasite">parasitic</a> and live attached to their hosts (including <a href="/wiki/Sea_louse" title="Sea louse">sea lice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Branchiura" title="Branchiura">fish lice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Whale_louse" title="Whale louse">whale lice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pentastomida" title="Pentastomida">tongue worms</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Cymothoa_exigua" title="Cymothoa exigua">Cymothoa exigua</a></i>, all of which may be referred to as "crustacean lice"), and adult barnacles live a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sessility_(zoology)" title="Sessility (zoology)">sessile</a> life – they are attached headfirst to the substrate and cannot move independently. Some branchiurans are able to withstand rapid changes of <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a> and will also switch hosts from marine to non-marine species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NorAm_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-NorAm-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>672</span></sup> <a href="/wiki/Krill" title="Krill">Krill</a> are the bottom layer and the most important part of the food chain in <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">Antarctic</a> animal communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>64</span></sup> Some crustaceans are significant invasive species, such as the Chinese mitten crab, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eriocheir_sinensis" title="Eriocheir sinensis">Eriocheir sinensis</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and the Asian shore crab, <i><a href="/wiki/Hemigrapsus_sanguineus" title="Hemigrapsus sanguineus">Hemigrapsus sanguineus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_cycle">Life cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crustacean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Life cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mating_system">Mating system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crustacean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mating system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The majority of crustaceans have <a href="/wiki/Gonochorism" title="Gonochorism">separate sexes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sexual_reproduction" title="Sexual reproduction">reproduce sexually</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-25">[25]</a></sup> A small number are <a href="/wiki/Hermaphrodite" title="Hermaphrodite">hermaphrodites</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Barnacle" title="Barnacle">barnacles</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Remipede" title="Remipede">remipedes</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Cephalocarida" title="Cephalocarida">Cephalocarida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canada_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Canada-27">[27]</a></sup> Some may even change sex during the course of their life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canada_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Canada-27">[27]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Parthenogenesis" title="Parthenogenesis">Parthenogenesis</a> is also widespread among crustaceans, where viable eggs are produced by a female without needing fertilisation by a male.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-25">[25]</a></sup> This occurs in many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Branchiopod" title="Branchiopod">branchiopods</a>, some <a href="/wiki/Ostracod" title="Ostracod">ostracods</a>, some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isopod" title="Isopod">isopods</a>, and certain "higher" crustaceans, such as the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marmorkrebs" title="Marmorkrebs">Marmorkrebs</a></i> crayfish.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eggs">Eggs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crustacean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Eggs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In many groups of crustaceans, the fertilised eggs are simply released into the <a href="/wiki/Water_column" title="Water column">water column</a>, while others have developed a number of mechanisms for holding on to the eggs until they are ready to hatch. Most <a href="/wiki/Decapoda" title="Decapoda">decapods</a> carry the eggs attached to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pleopod" title="Pleopod">pleopods</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Peracarida" title="Peracarida">peracarids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Notostraca" title="Notostraca">notostracans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anostraca" title="Anostraca">anostracans</a>, and many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isopod" title="Isopod">isopods</a> form a <a href="/wiki/Brood_pouch_(Peracarida)" title="Brood pouch (Peracarida)">brood pouch</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Carapace" title="Carapace">carapace</a> and thoracic limbs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-25">[25]</a></sup> Female Branchiura do not carry eggs in external ovisacs but attach them in rows to rocks and other objects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NorAmFWI_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-NorAmFWI-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>788</span></sup> Most <a href="/wiki/Leptostraca" title="Leptostraca">leptostracans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Krill" title="Krill">krill</a> carry the eggs between their thoracic limbs; some <a href="/wiki/Copepod" title="Copepod">copepods</a> carry their eggs in special thin-walled sacs, while others have them attached together in long, tangled strings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Larvae">Larvae</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crustacean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Larvae">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crustacean_larvae" title="Crustacean larvae">Crustacean larvae</a></div>
<p>Crustaceans exhibit a number of larval forms, of which the earliest and most characteristic is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nauplius_(larva)" title="Nauplius (larva)">nauplius</a>. This has three pairs of <a href="/wiki/Appendage" title="Appendage">appendages</a>, all emerging from the young animal's head, and a single naupliar eye. In most groups, there are further larval stages, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zoea" title="Zoea">zoea</a> (pl. zoeæ or zoeas<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>). This name was given to it when naturalists believed it to be a separate species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> It follows the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nauplius_(larva)" title="Nauplius (larva)">nauplius</a> stage and precedes the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Post-larva" title="Post-larva">post-larva</a>. Zoea larvae swim with their thoracic <a href="/wiki/Appendage" title="Appendage">appendages</a>, as opposed to nauplii, which use cephalic appendages, and megalopa, which use abdominal appendages for swimming. It often has spikes on its <a href="/wiki/Carapace" title="Carapace">carapace</a>, which may assist these small organisms in maintaining directional swimming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In many <a href="/wiki/Decapoda" title="Decapoda">decapods</a>, due to their accelerated development, the zoea is the first larval stage. In some cases, the zoea stage is followed by the mysis stage, and in others, by the megalopa stage, depending on the crustacean group involved.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crustacean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name "crustacean" dates from the earliest works to describe the animals, including those of <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Belon" title="Pierre Belon">Pierre Belon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guillaume_Rondelet" title="Guillaume Rondelet">Guillaume Rondelet</a>, but the name was not used by some later authors, including <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Carl Linnaeus</a>, who included crustaceans among the "<a href="/wiki/Aptera_in_the_10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae" title="Aptera in the 10th edition of Systema Naturae">Aptera</a>" in his <i><i lang="la" title="Latin language text"><a href="/wiki/Systema_Naturae" title="Systema Naturae">Systema Naturae</a></i></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MLW_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-MLW-32">[32]</a></sup> The earliest nomenclaturally valid work to use the name "Crustacea" was <a href="/wiki/Morten_Thrane_Br%C3%BCnnich" title="Morten Thrane Brünnich">Morten Thrane Brünnich</a>'s <i><i lang="la" title="Latin language text">Zoologiæ Fundamenta</i></i> in 1772,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> although he also included <a href="/wiki/Chelicerata" title="Chelicerata">chelicerates</a> in the group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MLW_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-MLW-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>The subphylum Crustacea comprises almost 67,000 described <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> which is thought to be just ​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>10</sub></span> to ​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>100</sub></span> of the total number as the majority of species remain as yet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Undescribed_species" title="Undescribed species">undiscovered</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Although most crustaceans are small, their morphology varies greatly and includes both the largest arthropod in the world – the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_spider_crab" title="Japanese spider crab">Japanese spider crab</a> with a leg span of 3.7 metres (12 ft)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> – and the smallest, the 100-<a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">micrometre</a>-long (0.00004 in)  <i><a href="/wiki/Stygotantulus" title="Stygotantulus">Stygotantulus stocki</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Despite their diversity of form, crustaceans are united by the special <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larval</a> form known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nauplius_(larva)" title="Nauplius (larva)">nauplius</a>.
</p><p>The exact relationships of the Crustacea to other taxa are not completely settled as of  April 2012<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Crustacean&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>. Studies based on morphology led to the <a href="/wiki/Pancrustacea" title="Pancrustacea">Pancrustacea</a> hypothesis,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> in which Crustacea and <a href="/wiki/Hexapoda" title="Hexapoda">Hexapoda</a> (<a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> and allies) are <a href="/wiki/Sister_group" title="Sister group">sister groups</a>. More recent studies using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" title="DNA sequence">DNA sequences</a> suggest that Crustacea is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paraphyletic" title="Paraphyletic">paraphyletic</a>, with the hexapods nested within a larger Pancrustacea <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clade</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Regier_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Regier-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vonJennWillDell11_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-vonJennWillDell11-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the classification of crustaceans has been quite variable, the system used by Martin and Davis<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M&amp;D_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-M&amp;D-41">[41]</a></sup> largely supersedes earlier works. <a href="/wiki/Mystacocarida" title="Mystacocarida">Mystacocarida</a> and <a href="/wiki/Branchiura" title="Branchiura">Branchiura</a>, here treated as part of <a href="/wiki/Maxillopoda" title="Maxillopoda">Maxillopoda</a>, are sometimes treated as their own classes. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_record">Fossil record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crustacean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Fossil record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Crustaceans have a rich and extensive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_record" title="Fossil record">fossil record</a>, which begins with animals such as <i><a href="/wiki/Canadaspis" title="Canadaspis">Canadaspis</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Perspicaris" title="Perspicaris">Perspicaris</a></i> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle_Cambrian" title="Middle Cambrian">Middle Cambrian</a> age <a href="/wiki/Burgess_Shale" title="Burgess Shale">Burgess Shale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bris_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-bris-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Briggs1978_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Briggs1978-43">[43]</a></sup> Most of the major groups of crustaceans appear in the fossil record before the end of the Cambrian, namely the <a href="/wiki/Branchiopoda" title="Branchiopoda">Branchiopoda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maxillopoda" title="Maxillopoda">Maxillopoda</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Barnacle" title="Barnacle">barnacles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pentastomida" title="Pentastomida">tongue worms</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Malacostraca" title="Malacostraca">Malacostraca</a>; there is some debate as to whether or not Cambrian animals assigned to Ostracoda are truly <a href="/wiki/Ostracod" title="Ostracod">ostracods</a>, which would otherwise start in the <a href="/wiki/Ordovician" title="Ordovician">Ordovician</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The only classes to appear later are the <a href="/wiki/Cephalocarida" title="Cephalocarida">Cephalocarida</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> which have no fossil record, and the <a href="/wiki/Remipedia" title="Remipedia">Remipedia</a>, which were first described from the fossil <i><a href="/wiki/Tesnusocaris" title="Tesnusocaris">Tesnusocaris goldichi</a></i>, but do not appear until the <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Most of the early crustaceans are rare, but fossil crustaceans become abundant from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carboniferous_period" title="Carboniferous period">Carboniferous period</a> onwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bris_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-bris-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Within the Malacostraca, no fossils are known for <a href="/wiki/Krill" title="Krill">krill</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> while both <a href="/wiki/Hoplocarida" title="Hoplocarida">Hoplocarida</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phyllopoda" title="Phyllopoda">Phyllopoda</a> contain important groups that are now extinct as well as extant members (Hoplocarida: <a href="/wiki/Mantis_shrimp" title="Mantis shrimp">mantis shrimp</a> are extant, while <a href="/wiki/Aeschronectida" title="Aeschronectida">Aeschronectida</a> are extinct;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Phyllopoda: <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Canadaspidida&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Canadaspidida (page does not exist)">Canadaspidida</a> are extinct, while <a href="/wiki/Leptostraca" title="Leptostraca">Leptostraca</a> are extant<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Briggs1978_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Briggs1978-43">[43]</a></sup>). <a href="/wiki/Cumacea" title="Cumacea">Cumacea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isopoda" title="Isopoda">Isopoda</a> are both known from the <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> as are the first true mantis shrimp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Decapoda" title="Decapoda">Decapoda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dendrobranchiata" title="Dendrobranchiata">prawns</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polychelida" title="Polychelida">polychelids</a> appear in the Triassic,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Caridea" title="Caridea">shrimp</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a> appear in the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wägele_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wägele-55">[55]</a></sup> however, the great radiation of crustaceans occurred in the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a>, particularly in crabs, and may have been driven by the <a href="/wiki/Adaptive_radiation" title="Adaptive radiation">adaptive radiation</a> of their main predators, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bony_fish" title="Bony fish">bony fish</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wägele_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wägele-55">[55]</a></sup> The first true <a href="/wiki/Lobster" title="Lobster">lobsters</a> also appear in the Cretaceous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consumption_by_humans">Consumption by humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crustacean&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Consumption by humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many crustaceans are consumed by humans, and nearly 10,700,000 <a href="/wiki/Ton" title="Ton">tons</a> were produced in 2007; the vast majority of this output is of <a href="/wiki/Decapoda" title="Decapoda">decapod crustaceans</a>: <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lobster" title="Lobster">lobsters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shrimp" title="Shrimp">shrimp</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crawfish" title="Crawfish">crawfish</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dendrobranchiata" title="Dendrobranchiata">prawns</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-57">[57]</a></sup> Over 60% by weight of all crustaceans caught for consumption are shrimp and prawns, and nearly 80% is produced in Asia, with China alone producing nearly half the world's total.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-57">[57]</a></sup> Non-decapod crustaceans are not widely consumed, with only 118,000 tons of <a href="/wiki/Krill" title="Krill">krill</a> being caught,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAO_57-2"><a href="#cite_note-FAO-57">[57]</a></sup> despite krill having one of the greatest <a href="/wiki/Biomass_(ecology)" title="Biomass (ecology)">biomasses</a> on the planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p>
