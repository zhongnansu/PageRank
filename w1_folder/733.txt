<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Living Planet Index</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Living_Planet_(disambiguation)" title="Living Planet (disambiguation)">Living Planet (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p><p>The <b>Living Planet Index</b> (<b>LPI</b>) is an indicator of the state of global <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_diversity" title="Biological diversity">biological diversity</a>, based on trends in <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrate</a> populations of species from around the world.
</p><p>The LPI provides the general public, scientists and policy-makers with information on trends in the <a href="/wiki/Abundance_(ecology)" title="Abundance (ecology)">abundance</a> of the world’s vertebrates and offers insights into which habitats or ecosystems are declining most rapidly. This information can be used to define the impact humans are having on the planet and for guiding actions to address <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_loss" title="Biodiversity loss">biodiversity loss</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Fund_for_Nature" title="World Wide Fund for Nature">World Wide Fund for Nature</a> (WWF) is working in partnership with the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Zoology" title="Institute of Zoology">Institute of Zoology</a> (IoZ), the research division of the <a href="/wiki/Zoological_Society_of_London" title="Zoological Society of London">Zoological Society of London</a> (ZSL), to further develop the project, which began in 1997.
</p><p>The Living Planet Index was originally developed by WWF in collaboration with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNEP-WCMC" title="UNEP-WCMC">UNEP-WCMC</a>, the biodiversity assessment and policy implementation arm of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Environment_Programme" title="United Nations Environment Programme">United Nations Environment Programme</a>. UNEP-WCMC collected data for the index in the first few years of the project.
</p><p>Results are presented biennially in the <b>WWF Living Planet Report</b>, on the World Wide Web, and in publications such as the <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Ecosystem_Assessment" title="Millennium Ecosystem Assessment">Millennium Ecosystem Assessment</a> and the UN Global Biodiversity Outlook. National and regional reports are now being produced to focus on relevant issues at a smaller scale. The latest edition of the Living Planet Report was released in October 2016 (LPR 2016 - Risk and resilience in a new era).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Between 1970 and 2012 the index fell by 58%. This global trend suggests that natural <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">ecosystems are degrading</a> at a rate unprecedented in human history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Calculation">Calculation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Living_Planet_Index&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Calculation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The current <b>Living Planet Database</b> (<b>LPD</b>), maintained by ZSL, contains almost 18,000 population trends for more than 3,600 species of fish, amphibians, reptiles, birds and mammals.
</p><p>The global LPI is calculated using over 14,000 of these population time-series which are gathered from a variety of sources such as journals, online databases and government reports.
</p><p>A generalized additive modelling framework is used to determine the underlying trend in each population time-series. Average rates of change are calculated and aggregated to the species level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Each species trend is aggregated to produce an index for the terrestrial, marine and freshwater systems. This process uses a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weighted_average" title="Weighted average">weighted average</a> method which places most weight on the largest (most species-rich) groups within a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biogeographic" title="Biogeographic">biogeographic</a> realm. This is done to counteract the uneven spatial and taxonomic distribution of data in the LPD. The three system indices are then averaged to produce the global LPI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Convention_on_Biological_Diversity">Convention on Biological Diversity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Living_Planet_Index&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Convention on Biological Diversity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In April 2002, at the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_Biological_Diversity" title="Convention on Biological Diversity">Convention on Biological Diversity</a> (CBD), 188 Nations committed themselves to actions to:  <i>“… achieve, by 2010, a significant reduction of the current rate of biodiversity loss at the global, regional and national levels…”</i>
</p><p>The LPI played a pivotal role in measuring progress towards the CBD's 2010 target.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It has also been adopted by the CBD as an indicator of progress towards its 2011-2020 targets 5, 6, and 12.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The work by the Indicators and Assessments Unit at ZSL is concerned with ensuring the most rigorous and robust methods are implemented for the measurement of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Population_trend" title="Population trend">population trends</a>, expanding the coverage of the LPI to more broadly represent biodiversity, and disaggregating the index in meaningful ways (such as assessing the changes in <a href="/wiki/Exploitation_of_natural_resources" title="Exploitation of natural resources">exploited</a> or <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Building_the_LPD_network">Building the LPD network</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Living_Planet_Index&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Building the LPD network">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Population data invariably comes from the better monitored regions of the world;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> however a network of organisations collecting species population data is currently being developed. With the regular addition of high quality data to the LPD the LPI will aim to have greater coverage of species from poorly known taxonomic groups and regions. In addition, ZSL and WWF have recently begun forming partnerships to develop LPIs for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Invertebrates" title="Invertebrates">invertebrates</a> and plants.
</p>
