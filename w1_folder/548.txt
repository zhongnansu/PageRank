<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Catalysis</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Catalyst" redirects here. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Catalyst_(disambiguation)" title="Catalyst (disambiguation)">Catalyst (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Low_Temperature_Oxidation_Catalyst.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2433" data-file-width="2974" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Low_Temperature_Oxidation_Catalyst.jpeg/220px-Low_Temperature_Oxidation_Catalyst.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Low_Temperature_Oxidation_Catalyst.jpeg/330px-Low_Temperature_Oxidation_Catalyst.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Low_Temperature_Oxidation_Catalyst.jpeg/440px-Low_Temperature_Oxidation_Catalyst.jpeg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Low_Temperature_Oxidation_Catalyst.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An <a href="/wiki/Air_filter" title="Air filter">air filter</a> that utilizes a low-temperature oxidation catalyst to convert <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> to less toxic <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> at room temperature. It can also remove <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">formaldehyde</a> from the air.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Catalysis</b> is the process of increasing the <a href="/wiki/Reaction_rate" title="Reaction rate">rate</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reaction</a> by adding a substance known as a <b>catalyst</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUPACc_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-IUPACc-1">[1]</a></sup> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/əl/: 'le' in 'bottle'">əl</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span></span>/</a></span></span>), which is not consumed in the catalyzed reaction and can continue to act repeatedly. Because of this, only very small amounts of catalyst are required to alter the reaction rate in principle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In general, chemical reactions occur faster in the presence of a catalyst because the catalyst provides an alternative reaction mechanism with a lower <a href="/wiki/Activation_energy" title="Activation energy">activation energy</a> than the non-catalyzed mechanism. In catalyzed mechanisms, the catalyst usually reacts to form a temporary <a href="/wiki/Reaction_intermediate" title="Reaction intermediate">intermediate</a>, which then regenerates the original catalyst in a cyclic process.
</p><p>A substance which provides a mechanism with a higher activation energy does not reduce the rate because the reaction can still occur by the non-catalyzed route.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LM82_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-LM82-3">[3]</a></sup> An added substance which does reduce the reaction rate is not considered a catalyst<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUPACc_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-IUPACc-1">[1]</a></sup> but a <a href="/wiki/Reaction_inhibitor" title="Reaction inhibitor">reaction inhibitor</a> (<a href="#Inhibitors,_poisons,_and_promoters">see below</a>).
</p><p>Catalysts may be classified as either homogeneous or heterogeneous. A homogeneous catalyst is one whose <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> are dispersed in the same phase (usually gaseous or liquid) as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reactant" title="Reactant">reactant</a>'s molecules. A heterogeneous catalyst is one whose molecules are not in the same phase as the reactant's, which are typically gases or liquids that are <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorbed</a> onto the surface of the solid catalyst. <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">Enzymes</a> and other biocatalysts are often considered as a third category.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_perspective">Technical perspective</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catalysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Technical perspective">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the presence of a catalyst, less <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_free_energy" title="Thermodynamic free energy">free energy</a> is required to reach the <a href="/wiki/Transition_state" title="Transition state">transition state</a>, but the total free energy from reactants to products does not change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUPACc_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-IUPACc-1">[1]</a></sup> A catalyst may participate in multiple chemical transformations. The effect of a catalyst may vary due to the presence of other substances known as inhibitors or <a href="/wiki/Catalyst_poisoning" title="Catalyst poisoning">poisons</a> (which reduce the catalytic activity) or promoters (which increase the activity and also affect the temperature of the reaction).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUPACc_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-IUPACc-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Catalyzed reactions have a lower <a href="/wiki/Activation_energy" title="Activation energy">activation energy</a> (rate-limiting free energy of activation) than the corresponding uncatalyzed reaction, resulting in a higher reaction rate at the same temperature and for the same reactant <a href="/wiki/Concentration" title="Concentration">concentrations</a>. However, the detailed mechanics of catalysis is complex. Catalysts may bind to the reagents to polarize bonds, e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acid_catalyst" title="Acid catalyst">acid catalysts</a> for reactions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonyl" title="Carbonyl">carbonyl</a> compounds, or form specific intermediates that are not produced naturally, such as osmate <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">esters</a> in <a href="/wiki/Osmium_tetroxide" title="Osmium tetroxide">osmium tetroxide</a>-catalyzed <a href="/wiki/Dihydroxylation" title="Dihydroxylation">dihydroxylation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkenes</a>, or cause <a href="/wiki/Dissociation_(chemistry)" title="Dissociation (chemistry)">dissociation</a> of reagents to reactive forms, such as <a href="/wiki/Chemisorption" title="Chemisorption">chemisorbed hydrogen</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalytic_hydrogenation" title="Catalytic hydrogenation">catalytic hydrogenation</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Chemical_kinetics" title="Chemical kinetics">Kinetically</a>, catalytic reactions are typical <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reactions</a>; i.e. the reaction rate depends on the frequency of contact of the reactants in the rate-determining step.  Usually, the catalyst participates in this slowest step, and rates are limited by amount of catalyst and its "activity".  In <a href="/wiki/Heterogeneous_catalysis" title="Heterogeneous catalysis">heterogeneous catalysis</a>, the diffusion of reagents to the surface and diffusion of products from the surface can be rate determining. A <a href="/wiki/Nanomaterial-based_catalyst" title="Nanomaterial-based catalyst">nanomaterial-based catalyst</a> is an example of a heterogeneous catalyst. Analogous events associated with <a href="/wiki/Substrate_(chemistry)" title="Substrate (chemistry)">substrate</a> binding and product dissociation apply to homogeneous catalysts.
</p><p>Although catalysts are not consumed by the reaction itself, they may be inhibited, deactivated, or destroyed by secondary processes.  In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterogeneous" title="Heterogeneous">heterogeneous</a> catalysis, typical secondary processes include <a href="/wiki/Coking" title="Coking">coking</a> where the catalyst becomes covered by <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymeric</a> side products.  Additionally, heterogeneous catalysts can dissolve into the solution in a solid–liquid system or sublimate in a solid–gas system.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catalysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The production of most industrially important chemicals involves catalysis. Similarly, most biochemically significant processes are catalysed.  Research into catalysis is a major field in <a href="/wiki/Applied_science" title="Applied science">applied science</a> and involves many areas of chemistry, notably <a href="/wiki/Organometallic_chemistry" title="Organometallic chemistry">organometallic chemistry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">materials science</a>.  Catalysis is relevant to many aspects of <a href="/wiki/Environmental_science" title="Environmental science">environmental science</a>, e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_converter" title="Catalytic converter">catalytic converter</a> in automobiles and the dynamics of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ozone_hole" title="Ozone hole">ozone hole</a>. Catalytic reactions are preferred in environmentally friendly <a href="/wiki/Green_chemistry" title="Green chemistry">green chemistry</a> due to the reduced amount of waste generated,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> as opposed to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stoichiometric" title="Stoichiometric">stoichiometric</a> reactions in which all reactants are consumed and more side products are formed. Many <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metals</a> and transition metal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complex_(chemistry)" title="Complex (chemistry)">complexes</a> are used in catalysis as well. Catalysts called <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> are important in <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>.
</p><p>A catalyst works by providing an alternative reaction pathway to the reaction product. The rate of the reaction is increased as this alternative route has a lower <a href="/wiki/Activation_energy" title="Activation energy">activation energy</a> than the reaction route not mediated by the catalyst. The <a href="/wiki/Disproportionation" title="Disproportionation">disproportionation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a> creates water and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, as shown below.
</p>
<dl><dd>2 H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>  →   2 H<sub>2</sub>O  +  O<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>This reaction is preferable in the sense that the reaction products are more stable than the starting material, though the uncatalysed reaction is slow. In fact, the decomposition of hydrogen peroxide is so slow that hydrogen peroxide solutions are commercially available. This reaction is strongly affected by catalysts such as <a href="/wiki/Manganese_dioxide" title="Manganese dioxide">manganese dioxide</a>, or the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Peroxidase" title="Peroxidase">peroxidase</a> in organisms. Upon the addition of a small amount of <a href="/wiki/Manganese_dioxide" title="Manganese dioxide">manganese dioxide</a>, the hydrogen peroxide reacts rapidly. This effect is readily seen by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effervescence_(chemistry)" title="Effervescence (chemistry)">effervescence</a> of oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The manganese dioxide is not consumed in the reaction, and thus may be recovered unchanged, and re-used indefinitely. Accordingly, manganese dioxide <i>catalyses</i> this reaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General_principles">General principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catalysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: General principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Units">Units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catalysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Catalytic activity is usually denoted by the symbol <i>z</i> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and measured in <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mol</a>/s, a unit which was called <a href="/wiki/Katal" title="Katal">katal</a> and defined the SI unit for catalytic activity since 1999. Catalytic activity is not a kind of reaction rate, but a property of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a> under certain conditions, in relation to a specific <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reaction</a>. Catalytic activity of one katal (Symbol 1 kat = 1 mol/s) of a catalyst means one mole of that catalyst (substance, in <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">Mol</a>) will catalyse 1 mole of the reactant to product in one second. A catalyst may and usually will have different catalytic activity for distinct reactions. See <a href="/wiki/Katal" title="Katal">katal</a> for an example.
</p><p>There are further derived SI units related to catalytic activity, see the above reference for details.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Typical_mechanism">Typical mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catalysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Typical mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Although the catalyst is consumed by reaction <b><a href="#math_1">1</a></b>, it is subsequently produced by reaction <b><a href="#math_4">4</a></b>, so it does not occur in the overall reaction equation:
</p>
<dl><dd>X + Y → Z</dd></dl>
<p>As a catalyst is regenerated in a reaction, often only small amounts are needed to increase the rate of the reaction. In practice, however, catalysts are sometimes consumed in secondary processes.
</p><p>The catalyst does usually appear in the <a href="/wiki/Rate_equation" title="Rate equation">rate equation</a>. For example, if the <a href="/wiki/Rate-determining_step" title="Rate-determining step">rate-determining step</a> in the above reaction scheme is the first step <br/>  X + C → XC, the catalyzed reaction will be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Order_of_reaction" title="Order of reaction">second order</a> with rate equation v = k<sub>cat</sub>[X][C], which is proportional to the catalyst concentration [C]. However [C] remains constant during the reaction so that the catalyzed reaction is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Order_of_reaction#Pseudo-first_order" title="Order of reaction">pseudo-first order</a>:
v = k<sub>obs</sub>[X],   where k<sub>obs</sub> = k<sub>cat</sub>[C].
</p><p>As an example of a detailed mechanism at the microscopic level, in 2008 Danish researchers first revealed the sequence of events when <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> combine on the surface of <a href="/wiki/Titanium_dioxide" title="Titanium dioxide">titanium dioxide</a> (TiO<sub>2</sub>, or <i>titania</i>) to produce water.  With a time-lapse series of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scanning_tunneling_microscopy" title="Scanning tunneling microscopy">scanning tunneling microscopy</a> images, they determined the molecules undergo <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dissociation_(chemistry)" title="Dissociation (chemistry)">dissociation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">diffusion</a> before reacting.  The intermediate reaction states were: HO<sub>2</sub>, H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, then H<sub>3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and the final reaction product (<a href="/wiki/Water_dimer" title="Water dimer">water molecule dimers</a>), after which the water molecule <a href="/wiki/Desorption" title="Desorption">desorbs</a> from the catalyst surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction_energetics">Reaction energetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catalysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reaction energetics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:294px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CatalysisScheme.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="681" data-file-width="1159" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/CatalysisScheme.png/292px-CatalysisScheme.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/CatalysisScheme.png/438px-CatalysisScheme.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/CatalysisScheme.png/584px-CatalysisScheme.png 2x" width="292"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CatalysisScheme.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Generic potential energy diagram showing the effect of a catalyst in a hypothetical exothermic chemical reaction X + Y to give Z. The presence of the catalyst opens a different reaction pathway (shown in red) with a lower activation energy. The final result and the overall thermodynamics are the same.</div></div></div>
<p>Catalysts work by providing an (alternative) mechanism involving a different <a href="/wiki/Transition_state" title="Transition state">transition state</a> and lower <a href="/wiki/Activation_energy" title="Activation energy">activation energy</a>. Consequently,  more molecular collisions have the energy needed to reach the transition state. Hence, catalysts can enable reactions that would otherwise be blocked or slowed by a kinetic barrier.  The catalyst may increase reaction rate or selectivity, or enable the reaction at lower temperatures. This effect can be illustrated with an <a href="/wiki/Energy_profile_(chemistry)" title="Energy profile (chemistry)">energy profile</a> diagram.
</p><p>In the catalyzed <a href="/wiki/Elementary_reaction" title="Elementary reaction">elementary reaction</a>, catalysts do <b>not</b> change the extent of a reaction: they have <b>no</b> effect on the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_equilibrium" title="Chemical equilibrium">chemical equilibrium</a> of a reaction because the rate of both the forward and the reverse reaction are both affected (see also <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>).  The <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">second law of thermodynamics</a> describes why a catalyst does not change the chemical equilibrium of a reaction.  Suppose there was such a catalyst that shifted an equilibrium. Introducing the catalyst to the system would result in a reaction to move to the new equilibrium, producing energy. Production of energy is a necessary result since reactions are spontaneous only if <a href="/wiki/Gibbs_free_energy" title="Gibbs free energy">Gibbs free energy</a> is produced, and if there is no energy barrier, there is no need for a catalyst. Then, removing the catalyst would also result in reaction, producing energy; i.e. the addition and its reverse process, removal, would both produce energy. Thus, a catalyst that could change the equilibrium would be a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perpetual_motion_machine" title="Perpetual motion machine">perpetual motion machine</a>, a contradiction to the laws of thermodynamics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Thus, catalyst <b>does not</b> alter the equilibrium constant. (A catalyst can however change the equilibrium concentrations by reacting in a subsequent step. It is then consumed as the reaction proceeds, and thus it is also a reactant. Illustrative is the base-catalysed <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">esters</a>, where the produced <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxylic acid</a> immediately reacts with the base catalyst and thus the reaction equilibrium is shifted towards hydrolysis.)
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/SI_derived_unit" title="SI derived unit">SI derived unit</a> for measuring the <b>catalytic activity</b> of a catalyst is the <a href="/wiki/Katal" title="Katal">katal</a>, which is moles per second. The productivity of a catalyst can be described by the <a href="/wiki/Turnover_number" title="Turnover number">turnover number</a> (or TON) and the catalytic activity by the <i>turn over frequency</i> (TOF), which is the TON per time unit. The biochemical equivalent is the <a href="/wiki/Enzyme_unit" title="Enzyme unit">enzyme unit</a>.  For more information on the efficiency of enzymatic catalysis, see the article on <i><a href="/wiki/Enzyme#Kinetics" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a></i>.
</p><p>The catalyst stabilizes the transition state more than it stabilizes the starting material.  It decreases the kinetic barrier by decreasing the <i>difference</i> in energy between starting material and transition state. It <b>does not</b> change the energy difference between starting materials and products (thermodynamic barrier), or the available energy (this is provided by the environment as heat or light).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Materials">Materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catalysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The chemical nature of catalysts is as diverse as catalysis itself, although some generalizations can be made.  Proton <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a> are probably the most widely used catalysts, especially for the many reactions involving water, including hydrolysis and its reverse.  Multifunctional solids often are catalytically active, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Zeolite" title="Zeolite">zeolites</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alumina" title="Alumina">alumina</a>, higher-order oxides, graphitic carbon, nanoparticles, nanodots, and facets of bulk materials.  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transition_metals" title="Transition metals">Transition metals</a> are often used to catalyze <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">redox</a> reactions (oxidation, hydrogenation). Examples are <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Raney_nickel" title="Raney nickel">Raney nickel</a> for hydrogenation, and <a href="/wiki/Vanadium(V)_oxide" title="Vanadium(V) oxide">vanadium(V) oxide</a> for oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> into <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_trioxide" title="Sulfur trioxide">sulfur trioxide</a> by the so-called <a href="/wiki/Contact_process" title="Contact process">contact process</a>.  Many catalytic processes, especially those used in organic synthesis, require "late transition metals", such as <a href="/wiki/Palladium" title="Palladium">palladium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium" title="Ruthenium">ruthenium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhodium" title="Rhodium">rhodium</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Iridium" title="Iridium">iridium</a>.
</p><p>Some so-called catalysts are really <b>precatalysts</b>.  Precatalysts convert to catalysts in the reaction. For example, <a href="/wiki/Wilkinson%27s_catalyst" title="Wilkinson's catalyst">Wilkinson's catalyst</a> RhCl(PPh<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub> loses one triphenylphosphine ligand before entering the true catalytic cycle. Precatalysts are easier to store but are easily activated <a href="/wiki/In_situ#Chemistry_and_chemical_engineering" title="In situ">in situ</a>. Because of this preactivation step, many catalytic reactions involve an <a href="/wiki/Induction_period" title="Induction period">induction period</a>.
</p><p>Chemical species that improve catalytic activity are called <b>co-catalysts</b> (<b>cocatalysts</b>) or <b>promotors</b> in <b>cooperative catalysis</b>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catalysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Catalysts can be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterogeneous" title="Heterogeneous">heterogeneous</a> or <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Homogeneous" title="wiktionary:Homogeneous">homogeneous</a>, depending on whether a catalyst exists in the same <a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">phase</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Substrate_(chemistry)" title="Substrate (chemistry)">substrate</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biocatalysts" title="Biocatalysts">Biocatalysts</a> (enzymes) are often seen as a separate group.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heterogeneous_catalysts">Heterogeneous catalysts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catalysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Heterogeneous catalysts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Heterogeneous_catalysis" title="Heterogeneous catalysis">Heterogeneous catalysis</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Zeolite-ZSM-5-vdW.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1420" data-file-width="1876" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Zeolite-ZSM-5-vdW.png/220px-Zeolite-ZSM-5-vdW.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Zeolite-ZSM-5-vdW.png/330px-Zeolite-ZSM-5-vdW.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Zeolite-ZSM-5-vdW.png/440px-Zeolite-ZSM-5-vdW.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Zeolite-ZSM-5-vdW.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The microporous molecular structure of the <a href="/wiki/Zeolite" title="Zeolite">zeolite</a> ZSM-5 is exploited in catalysts used in refineries</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ceolite_nax.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="316" data-file-width="400" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Ceolite_nax.JPG/220px-Ceolite_nax.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Ceolite_nax.JPG/330px-Ceolite_nax.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d9/Ceolite_nax.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ceolite_nax.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Zeolites are extruded as pellets for easy handling in catalytic reactors.</div></div></div>
<p>Heterogeneous catalysts act in a different <a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">phase</a> than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reactants" title="Reactants">reactants</a>. Most heterogeneous catalysts are <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solids</a> that act on substrates in a <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a> or gaseous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reaction_mixture" title="Reaction mixture">reaction mixture</a>. Diverse mechanisms for <a href="/wiki/Reactions_on_surfaces" title="Reactions on surfaces">reactions on surfaces</a> are known, depending on how the adsorption takes place (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Langmuir-Hinshelwood-Hougen-Watson" title="Langmuir-Hinshelwood-Hougen-Watson">Langmuir-Hinshelwood</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eley%E2%80%93Rideal_mechanism" title="Eley–Rideal mechanism">Eley-Rideal</a>, and Mars-van Krevelen).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The total surface area of solid has an important effect on the reaction rate. The smaller the catalyst particle size, the larger the surface area for a given mass of particles.
</p><p>A heterogeneous catalyst has <b>active sites</b>, which are the atoms or crystal faces where the reaction actually occurs. Depending on the mechanism, the active site may be either a planar exposed metal surface, a crystal edge with imperfect metal valence or a complicated combination of the two. Thus, not only most of the volume, but also most of the surface of a heterogeneous catalyst may be catalytically inactive. Finding out the nature of the active site requires technically challenging research. Thus, empirical research for finding out new metal combinations for catalysis continues.
</p><p>For example, in the <a href="/wiki/Haber_process" title="Haber process">Haber process</a>, finely divided <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> serves as a catalyst for the synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> from <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>. The reacting <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gases</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adsorb" title="Adsorb">adsorb</a> onto active sites on the iron particles. Once physically adsorbed, the reagents undergo <a href="/wiki/Chemisorption" title="Chemisorption">chemisorption</a> that results in dissociation into adsorbed atomic species, and new bonds between the resulting fragments form in part due to their close proximity. In this way the particularly strong <a href="/wiki/Triple_bond" title="Triple bond">triple bond</a> in nitrogen is broken, which would be extremely uncommon in the gas phase due to its high activation energy. Thus, the activation energy of the overall reaction is lowered, and the rate of reaction increases.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Another place where a heterogeneous catalyst is applied is in the oxidation of sulfur dioxide on <a href="/wiki/Vanadium(V)_oxide" title="Vanadium(V) oxide">vanadium(V) oxide</a> for the production of <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>.
</p><p>Heterogeneous catalysts are typically "<a href="/wiki/Catalyst_support" title="Catalyst support">supported</a>," which means that the catalyst is dispersed on a second material that enhances the effectiveness or minimizes their cost. Supports prevent or reduce agglomeration and sintering of the small catalyst particles, exposing more surface area, thus catalysts have a higher specific activity (per gram) on a support. Sometimes the support is merely a surface on which the catalyst is spread to increase the surface area. More often, the support and the catalyst interact, affecting the catalytic reaction. Supports are porous materials with a high surface area, most commonly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alumina" title="Alumina">alumina</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zeolites" title="Zeolites">zeolites</a> or various kinds of <a href="/wiki/Activated_carbon" title="Activated carbon">activated carbon</a>. Specialized supports include <a href="/wiki/Silicon_dioxide" title="Silicon dioxide">silicon dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Titanium_dioxide" title="Titanium dioxide">titanium dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Barium_sulfate" title="Barium sulfate">barium sulfate</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrocatalysts">Electrocatalysts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catalysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Electrocatalysts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Electrocatalyst" title="Electrocatalyst">Electrocatalyst</a></div>
<p>In the context of <a href="/wiki/Electrochemistry" title="Electrochemistry">electrochemistry</a>, specifically in <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">fuel cell</a> engineering, various metal-containing catalysts are used to enhance the rates of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Half_reaction" title="Half reaction">half reactions</a> that comprise the fuel cell. One common type of fuel cell electrocatalyst is based upon <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanoparticles" title="Nanoparticles">nanoparticles</a> of <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a> that are supported on slightly larger <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> particles. When in contact with one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrodes" title="Electrodes">electrodes</a> in a fuel cell, this platinum increases the rate of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> reduction either to water, or to <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxide</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Homogeneous_catalysts">Homogeneous catalysts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catalysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Homogeneous catalysts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Homogeneous_catalysis" title="Homogeneous catalysis">Homogeneous catalysis</a></div>
<p>Homogeneous catalysts function in the same phase as the reactants, but the mechanistic principles involved in heterogeneous catalysis are generally applicable. Typically homogeneous catalysts are dissolved in a solvent with the substrates.  One example of homogeneous catalysis involves the influence of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">H</a><sup>+</sup> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Esterification" title="Esterification">esterification</a> of carboxylic acids, such as the formation of <a href="/wiki/Methyl_acetate" title="Methyl acetate">methyl acetate</a> from <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> One high-volume process requiring a homogeneous catalyst is <a href="/wiki/Hydroformylation" title="Hydroformylation">hydroformylation</a>, which adds <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> to an <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkene</a> to produce an alkyl <a href="/wiki/Aldehyde" title="Aldehyde">aldehyde</a>. The aldehyde can be converted to various products such as alcohols or acids (for e.g. detergents) or <a href="/wiki/Polyol" title="Polyol">polyols</a> (for plastics such as <a href="/wiki/Polycarbonate" title="Polycarbonate">polycarbonate</a> or <a href="/wiki/Polyurethane" title="Polyurethane">polyurethane</a>). For inorganic chemists, homogeneous catalysis is often synonymous with <a href="/wiki/Organometallic_chemistry" title="Organometallic chemistry">organometallic catalysts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Organocatalysis">Organocatalysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catalysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Organocatalysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Organocatalysis" title="Organocatalysis">Organocatalysis</a></div>
<p>Whereas transition metals sometimes attract most of the attention in the study of catalysis, small organic molecules without metals can also exhibit catalytic properties, as is apparent from the fact that many <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> lack transition metals.  Typically, organic catalysts require a higher loading (amount of catalyst per unit amount of reactant, expressed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mol%25" title="Mol%">mol%</a> <a href="/wiki/Amount_of_substance" title="Amount of substance">amount of substance</a>) than transition metal(-ion)-based catalysts, but these catalysts are usually commercially available in bulk, helping to reduce costs.  In the early 2000s, these organocatalysts were considered "new generation" and are competitive to traditional <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a>(-ion)-containing catalysts.  Organocatalysts are supposed to operate akin to metal-free enzymes utilizing, e.g., non-covalent interactions such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bonding" title="Hydrogen bonding">hydrogen bonding</a>. The discipline organocatalysis is divided in the application of covalent (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Proline" title="Proline">proline</a>, <a href="/wiki/4-Dimethylaminopyridine" title="4-Dimethylaminopyridine">DMAP</a>) and non-covalent (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Thiourea_organocatalysis" title="Thiourea organocatalysis">thiourea organocatalysis</a>) organocatalysts referring to the preferred catalyst-<a href="/wiki/Substrate_(chemistry)" title="Substrate (chemistry)">substrate</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Binding_(molecular)" title="Binding (molecular)">binding</a> and interaction, respectively.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Photocatalysts">Photocatalysts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catalysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Photocatalysts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Photocatalysis" title="Photocatalysis">Photocatalysis</a> is the phenomenon where the catalyst can receive light (such as visible light), be promoted to an excited state, and then undergo intersystem crossing with the starting material, returning to ground state without being consumed. The excited state of the starting material will then undergo reactions it ordinarily could not if directly illuminated. For example, <a href="/wiki/Singlet_oxygen" title="Singlet oxygen">singlet oxygen</a> is usually produced by photocatalysis. Photocatalysts are also the main ingredient in <a href="/wiki/Dye-sensitized_solar_cell" title="Dye-sensitized solar cell">dye-sensitized solar cells</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enzymes_and_biocatalysts">Enzymes and biocatalysts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catalysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Enzymes and biocatalysts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Enzyme_catalysis" title="Enzyme catalysis">enzyme catalysis</a></div>
<p>In biology, <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> are protein-based catalysts in <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Catabolism" title="Catabolism">catabolism</a>. Most biocatalysts are enzymes, but other non-protein-based classes of biomolecules also exhibit catalytic properties including <a href="/wiki/Ribozyme" title="Ribozyme">ribozymes</a>, and synthetic <a href="/wiki/Deoxyribozyme" title="Deoxyribozyme">deoxyribozymes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Biocatalysts can be thought of as intermediate between homogeneous and heterogeneous catalysts, although strictly speaking soluble enzymes are homogeneous catalysts and <a href="/wiki/Biological_membrane" title="Biological membrane">membrane</a>-bound enzymes are heterogeneous. Several factors affect the activity of enzymes (and other catalysts) including temperature, pH, concentration of enzyme, substrate, and products.  A particularly important reagent in enzymatic reactions is water, which is the product of many bond-forming reactions and a reactant in many bond-breaking processes.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Biocatalysis" title="Biocatalysis">biocatalysis</a>, enzymes are employed to prepare many commodity chemicals including <a href="/wiki/High-fructose_corn_syrup" title="High-fructose corn syrup">high-fructose corn syrup</a> and <a href="/wiki/Acrylamide" title="Acrylamide">acrylamide</a>.
</p><p>Some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monoclonal_antibodies" title="Monoclonal antibodies">monoclonal antibodies</a> whose binding target is a stable molecule which resembles the transition state of a chemical reaction can function as weak catalysts for that chemical reaction by lowering its activation energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>  Such catalytic antibodies are sometimes called "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abzymes" title="Abzymes">abzymes</a>".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nanocatalysts">Nanocatalysts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catalysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Nanocatalysts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nanomaterial-based_catalyst" title="Nanomaterial-based catalyst">Nanomaterial-based catalyst</a></div>
<p>Nanocatalysts are nanomaterials with catalytic activities. They have been extensively explored for wide range of applications. Among them, the nanocatalysts with enzyme mimicking activities are collectively called as <a href="/wiki/Artificial_enzyme#Nanozymes" title="Artificial enzyme">nanozymes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tandem_catalysis">Tandem catalysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catalysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Tandem catalysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tandem_catalysis" title="Tandem catalysis">tandem catalysis</a> two or more different catalysts are coupled in a one-pot reaction.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Autocatalysis">Autocatalysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catalysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Autocatalysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Autocatalysis" title="Autocatalysis">Autocatalysis</a></div>
<p>In autocatalysis, the catalyst <i>is</i> a product of the overall reaction, in contrast to all other types of catalysis considered in this article. The simplest example of autocatalysis is a reaction of type A + B → 2 B, in one or in several steps. The overall reaction is just A → B, so that B is a product. But since B is also a reactant, it may be present in the rate equation and affect the reaction rate. As the reaction proceeds, the concentration of B increases and can accelerate the reaction as a catalyst. In effect, the reaction accelerates itself or is autocatalyzed.
</p><p>A real example is the hydrolysis of an <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">ester</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Aspirin" title="Aspirin">aspirin</a> to a <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxylic acid</a> and an <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a>. In the absence of added acid catalysts, the carboxylic acid product catalyzes the hydrolysis.
</p>

<p>Estimates are that 90% of all commercially produced chemical products involve catalysts at some stage in the process of their manufacture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In 2005, catalytic processes generated about $900 billion in products worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>  Catalysis is so pervasive that subareas are not readily classified.  Some areas of particular concentration are surveyed below.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_processing">Energy processing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catalysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Energy processing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">Petroleum</a> refining makes intensive use of catalysis for <a href="/wiki/Alkylation" title="Alkylation">alkylation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalytic_cracking" title="Catalytic cracking">catalytic cracking</a> (breaking long-chain hydrocarbons into smaller pieces), <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_naphtha" title="Petroleum naphtha">naphtha</a> reforming and <a href="/wiki/Steam_reforming" title="Steam reforming">steam reforming</a> (conversion of <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a> into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthesis_gas" title="Synthesis gas">synthesis gas</a>).  Even the exhaust from the burning of fossil fuels is treated via catalysis: <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_converter" title="Catalytic converter">Catalytic converters</a>, typically composed of <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rhodium" title="Rhodium">rhodium</a>, break down some of the more harmful byproducts of automobile exhaust.
</p>
<dl><dd>2 CO  +  2 NO  →   2 CO<sub>2</sub>  +  N<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>With regard to synthetic fuels, an old but still important process is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fischer-Tropsch_synthesis" title="Fischer-Tropsch synthesis">Fischer-Tropsch synthesis</a> of hydrocarbons from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthesis_gas" title="Synthesis gas">synthesis gas</a>, which itself is processed via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_gas_shift_reaction" title="Water gas shift reaction">water-gas shift reactions</a>, catalysed by iron.  <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">Biodiesel</a> and related biofuels require processing via both inorganic and biocatalysts.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">Fuel cells</a> rely on catalysts for both the anodic and cathodic reactions.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Catalytic_heater" title="Catalytic heater">Catalytic heaters</a> generate flameless heat from a supply of combustible fuel.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bulk_chemicals">Bulk chemicals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catalysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Bulk chemicals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some of the largest-scale chemicals are produced via catalytic oxidation, often using <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>.  Examples include <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> (from ammonia), <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> (from <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> to <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_trioxide" title="Sulfur trioxide">sulfur trioxide</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Contact_process" title="Contact process">contact process</a>), <a href="/wiki/Terephthalic_acid" title="Terephthalic acid">terephthalic acid</a> from p-xylene, and <a href="/wiki/Acrylonitrile" title="Acrylonitrile">acrylonitrile</a> from propane and ammonia.
</p><p>Many other chemical products are generated by large-scale reduction, often via <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenation</a>.  The largest-scale example is <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>, which is prepared via the <a href="/wiki/Haber_process" title="Haber process">Haber process</a> from <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">Methanol</a> is prepared from <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>.
</p><p>Bulk polymers derived from <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">ethylene</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propylene" title="Propylene">propylene</a> are often prepared via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ziegler-Natta_catalysis" title="Ziegler-Natta catalysis">Ziegler-Natta catalysis</a>.  Polyesters, polyamides, and <a href="/wiki/Isocyanate" title="Isocyanate">isocyanates</a> are derived via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acid-base_catalysis" title="Acid-base catalysis">acid-base catalysis</a>.
</p><p>Most <a href="/wiki/Carbonylation" title="Carbonylation">carbonylation</a> processes require metal catalysts, examples include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monsanto_acetic_acid_process" title="Monsanto acetic acid process">Monsanto acetic acid process</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydroformylation" title="Hydroformylation">hydroformylation</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fine_chemicals">Fine chemicals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catalysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Fine chemicals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fine_chemicals" title="Fine chemicals">fine chemicals</a> are prepared via catalysis; methods include those of heavy industry as well as more specialized processes that would be prohibitively expensive on a large scale.  Examples include the <a href="/wiki/Heck_reaction" title="Heck reaction">Heck reaction</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Friedel%E2%80%93Crafts_reaction" title="Friedel–Crafts reaction">Friedel–Crafts reactions</a>.
</p><p>Because most bioactive compounds are <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)" title="Chirality (chemistry)">chiral</a>, many pharmaceuticals are produced by enantioselective catalysis (catalytic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asymmetric_synthesis" title="Asymmetric synthesis">asymmetric synthesis</a>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_processing">Food processing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catalysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Food processing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the most obvious applications of catalysis is the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenation</a> (reaction with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> gas) of fats using <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> catalyst to produce <a href="/wiki/Margarine" title="Margarine">margarine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>  Many other foodstuffs are prepared via biocatalysis (see below).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environment">Environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catalysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Catalysis impacts the environment by increasing the efficiency of industrial processes, but catalysis also plays a direct role in the environment. A notable example is the catalytic role of <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_radical" title="Free radical">free radicals</a> in the breakdown of <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>. These radicals are formed by the action of <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> on <a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon" title="Chlorofluorocarbon">chlorofluorocarbons</a> (CFCs).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catalysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Generally speaking,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> anything that increases the rate of a process is a "catalyst", a term derived from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BA%CE%B1%CF%84%CE%B1%CE%BB%CF%8D%CF%89" title="wikt:καταλύω">καταλύειν</a>, meaning "to annul," or "to untie," or "to pick up."  The concept of catalysis was invented by chemist <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Fulhame" title="Elizabeth Fulhame">Elizabeth Fulhame</a> and described in a 1794 book, based on her novel work in oxidation-reduction experiments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The first chemical reaction in organic chemistry that utilized a catalyst was studied in 1811 by <a href="/wiki/Gottlieb_Kirchhoff" title="Gottlieb Kirchhoff">Gottlieb Kirchhoff</a> who discovered the acid-catalyzed conversion of starch to glucose. The term <i>catalysis</i> was later used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J%C3%B6ns_Jakob_Berzelius" title="Jöns Jakob Berzelius">Jöns Jakob Berzelius</a> in 1835<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> to describe reactions that are accelerated by substances that remain unchanged after the reaction. <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Fulhame" title="Elizabeth Fulhame">Fulhame</a>, who predated Berzelius, did work with water as opposed to metals in her reduction experiments. Other 18th century chemists who worked in catalysis were <a href="/wiki/Eilhard_Mitscherlich" title="Eilhard Mitscherlich">Eilhard Mitscherlich</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> who referred to it as <i>contact</i> processes, and <a href="/wiki/Johann_Wolfgang_D%C3%B6bereiner" title="Johann Wolfgang Döbereiner">Johann Wolfgang Döbereiner</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> who spoke of <i>contact action. </i>He developed <a href="/wiki/D%C3%B6bereiner%27s_lamp" title="Döbereiner's lamp">Döbereiner's lamp</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lighter_(fire_starter)" title="Lighter (fire starter)">lighter</a> based on <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">platinum</a> sponge, which became a commercial success in the 1820s that lives on today. <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Davy" title="Humphry Davy">Humphry Davy</a> discovered the use of platinum in catalysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>  In the 1880s, <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Ostwald" title="Wilhelm Ostwald">Wilhelm Ostwald</a> at <a href="/wiki/Leipzig_University" title="Leipzig University">Leipzig University</a> started a systematic investigation into reactions that were catalyzed by the presence of <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a> and bases, and found that chemical reactions occur at finite rates and that these rates can be used to determine the strengths of acids and bases. For this work, Ostwald was awarded the 1909 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">Nobel Prize in Chemistry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Ipatieff" title="Vladimir Ipatieff">Vladimir Ipatieff</a> performed some of the earliest industrial scale reactions, including the discovery and commercialization of oligomerization and the development of catalysts for hydrogenation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Inhibitors.2C_poisons.2C_and_promoters"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Inhibitors,_poisons,_and_promoters">Inhibitors, poisons, and promoters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catalysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Inhibitors, poisons, and promoters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Substances that reduce the action of catalysts are called <a href="/wiki/Reaction_inhibitor" title="Reaction inhibitor">catalyst inhibitors</a> if reversible, and <a href="/wiki/Catalyst_poisoning" title="Catalyst poisoning">catalyst poisons</a> if irreversible. Promoters are substances that increase the catalytic activity, even though they are not catalysts by themselves.
</p><p>Inhibitors are sometimes referred to as "negative catalysts" since they decrease the reaction rate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laidler78_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Laidler78-30">[30]</a></sup> However the term inhibitor is preferred since they do not work by introducing a reaction path with higher activation energy; this would not reduce the rate since the reaction would continue to occur by the non-catalyzed path. Instead they act either by deactivating catalysts, or by removing reaction intermediates such as free radicals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laidler78_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Laidler78-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LM82_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-LM82-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The inhibitor may modify selectivity in addition to rate. For instance, in the reduction of <a href="/wiki/Alkyne" title="Alkyne">alkynes</a> to <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkenes</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Palladium" title="Palladium">palladium</a> (Pd) catalyst partly "poisoned" with <a href="/wiki/Lead(II)_acetate" title="Lead(II) acetate">lead(II) acetate</a> (Pb(CH<sub>3</sub>CO<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub>) can be used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lindlar_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lindlar-31">[31]</a></sup> Without the deactivation of the catalyst, the alkene produced would be further reduced to <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">alkane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>The inhibitor can produce this effect by, e.g., selectively poisoning only certain types of active sites. Another mechanism is the modification of surface geometry. For instance, in hydrogenation operations, large planes of metal surface function as sites of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenolysis" title="Hydrogenolysis">hydrogenolysis</a> catalysis while sites catalyzing <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenation</a> of unsaturates are smaller. Thus, a poison that covers surface randomly will tend to reduce the number of uncontaminated large planes but leave proportionally more smaller sites free, thus changing the hydrogenation vs. hydrogenolysis selectivity. Many other mechanisms are also possible.
</p><p>Promoters can cover up surface to prevent production of a mat of coke, or even actively remove such material (e.g., rhenium on platinum in <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_reforming" title="Catalytic reforming">platforming</a>). They can aid the dispersion of the catalytic material or bind to reagents .
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_market">Current market</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catalysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Current market">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The global demand for catalysts in 2010 was estimated at approximately 29.5 billion USD. With the rapid recovery in the automotive and chemical industries overall, the global catalyst market is expected to experience fast growth in the coming years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
