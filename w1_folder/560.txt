<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Radical initiator</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>In <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, <b>radical initiators</b> are substances that can produce <a href="/wiki/Radical_(chemistry)" title="Radical (chemistry)">radical species</a> under mild conditions and promote <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radical_reaction" title="Radical reaction">radical reactions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> These substances generally possess weak bonds—bonds that have small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bond_dissociation_energy" title="Bond dissociation energy">bond dissociation energies</a>. Radical initiators are utilized in industrial processes such as <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a> synthesis. Typical examples are <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogen</a> <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a>, <a href="/wiki/Azo_compound" title="Azo compound">azo compounds</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Organic_peroxide" title="Organic peroxide">organic and inorganic peroxides</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_types_of_initiation_reaction">Major types of initiation reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radical_initiator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Major types of initiation reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">Halogens</a> undergo the <a href="/wiki/Homolysis_(chemistry)" title="Homolysis (chemistry)">homolytic fission</a> relatively easily. <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">Chlorine</a>, for example, gives two chlorine radicals (Cl•) by irradiation with <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet light</a>. This process is used for <a href="/wiki/Free-radical_halogenation" title="Free-radical halogenation">chlorination</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">alkanes</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Azo_compound" title="Azo compound">Azo compounds</a> (R-<a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">N</a>=<a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">N</a>-R') can be the precursor of two <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>-centered radicals (R• and R'•) and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> gas upon heating and/or by irradiation.  For example, <a href="/wiki/Azobisisobutyronitrile" title="Azobisisobutyronitrile">AIBN</a> and <a href="/wiki/ABCN" title="ABCN">ABCN</a> yield isobutyronitrile and cyclohexanecarbonitrile radicals, respectively.</li></ul>
<dl><dd><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AIBN-radical-2D.png" title="AIBN initiator"><img alt="AIBN initiator" data-file-height="262" data-file-width="1100" height="95" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/AIBN-radical-2D.png/400px-AIBN-radical-2D.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/AIBN-radical-2D.png/600px-AIBN-radical-2D.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/AIBN-radical-2D.png/800px-AIBN-radical-2D.png 2x" width="400"/></a></dd></dl>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Organic_peroxide" title="Organic peroxide">Organic peroxides</a> each have a peroxide bond (-<a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a>-<a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a>-), which is readily cleaved to give two <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>-centered radicals. The oxyl radicals are unstable and believed to be transformed into relatively stable <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>-centered radicals. For example, <a href="/wiki/Di-tert-butyl_peroxide" title="Di-tert-butyl peroxide">di-<i>tert</i>-butyl peroxide</a> (<sup><i>t</i></sup><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Butyl" title="Butyl">Bu</a><a href="/wiki/Peroxide" title="Peroxide">OO</a><sup><i>t</i></sup><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Butyl" title="Butyl">Bu</a>) gives two <i>t</i>-butoxy radicals (<sup><i>t</i></sup><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Butyl" title="Butyl">Bu</a><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a>•) and the radicals become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methyl" title="Methyl">methyl</a> radicals (<a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">C</a><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">H</a><sub>3</sub>•) with the loss of <a href="/wiki/Acetone" title="Acetone">acetone</a>. <a href="/wiki/Benzoyl_peroxide" title="Benzoyl peroxide">Benzoyl peroxide</a> ((<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phenyl" title="Phenyl">Ph</a><a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">C</a><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">OO</a>)<sub>2</sub>) generates benzoyloxyl radicals (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phenyl" title="Phenyl">Ph</a><a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">C</a><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">OO</a>•), each of which loses <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> to be converted into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phenyl" title="Phenyl">phenyl</a> radical (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phenyl" title="Phenyl">Ph</a>•). <a href="/wiki/Methyl_ethyl_ketone_peroxide" title="Methyl ethyl ketone peroxide">Methyl ethyl ketone peroxide</a> is also common, and <a href="/wiki/Acetone_peroxide" title="Acetone peroxide">acetone peroxide</a> is on rare occasions used as a radical initiator, too.</li>
<li>Inorganic peroxides function analogously to organic peroxides.  Many polymers are often produced from the alkenes upon initiation with <a href="/wiki/Peroxydisulfate" title="Peroxydisulfate">peroxydisulfate</a> salts.  In solution, peroxydisulfate dissociates to give sulfate radicals:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-3">[3]</a></sup></li></ul>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>[O<sub>3</sub>SO-OSO<sub>3</sub>]<sup>2−</sup>   ⇌   2 [SO<sub>4</sub>]<sup>−</sup></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p>The sulfate radical adds to an alkene forming radical sulfate esters, e.g. <sup>.</sup>CHPhCH<sub>2</sub>OSO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>, that add further alkenes via formation of C-C bonds.  Many styrene and fluoroalkene polymers are produced in this way.
</p>
<ul><li>In atom transfer radical polymerization (ATRP) carbon-halides reversibly generate organic radicals in the presence of <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metal</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a>.</li></ul>
<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:602px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ATRP_general.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="493" data-file-width="1697" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/ATRP_general.png/600px-ATRP_general.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/ATRP_general.png/900px-ATRP_general.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/ATRP_general.png/1200px-ATRP_general.png 2x" width="600"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ATRP_general.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>General ATRP Reaction. <span style="color: blue">A.</span> Initiation. <span style="color: blue">B.</span> Equilibrium with dormant species. <span style="color: blue">C.</span>Propagation</div></div></div></div>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radical_initiator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some radical initiators such as <a href="/wiki/Azo_compound" title="Azo compound">azo compounds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peroxide" title="Peroxide">peroxides</a> are often unstable. They are often stored cold.
</p>
