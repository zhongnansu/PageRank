<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Effervescence</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Soda_bubbles_macro.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="750" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Soda_bubbles_macro.jpg/200px-Soda_bubbles_macro.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Soda_bubbles_macro.jpg/300px-Soda_bubbles_macro.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Soda_bubbles_macro.jpg/400px-Soda_bubbles_macro.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Soda_bubbles_macro.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bubbles of carbon dioxide float to the surface of a <a href="/wiki/Soft_drink" title="Soft drink">carbonated soft drink</a>.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Effervescence</b> is the escape of <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> from an <a href="/wiki/Aqueous_solution" title="Aqueous solution">aqueous solution</a> and the foaming or fizzing that results from that release.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The word effervescence is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> verb <i>fervere</i> (to boil), preceded by the adverb <i>ex</i>. It has the same linguistic root as the word <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">fermentation</a>.
</p><p>Effervescence can also be observed when opening a bottle of champagne, beer or <a href="/wiki/Carbonation" title="Carbonation">carbonated beverages</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Soft_drink" title="Soft drink">soft drinks</a>. The visible bubbles are produced by the escape from solution of the dissolved gas (which itself is not visible while dissolved in the liquid).
</p><p>Although CO<sub>2</sub> is most common for beverages, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen#Nitrogen_gas" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen gas</a> is sometimes deliberately added to certain beers. The smaller bubble size creates a smoother <a href="/wiki/Beer_head" title="Beer head">beer head</a>. Due to the poor solubility of nitrogen in beer, <a href="/wiki/Keg" title="Keg">kegs</a> or <a href="/wiki/Widget_(beer)" title="Widget (beer)">widgets</a> are used for this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the laboratory, a common example of effervescence is seen if <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> is added to a block of <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>. If a few pieces of <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a> or an <a href="/wiki/Antacid" title="Antacid">antacid</a> tablet are put in hydrochloric acid in a <a href="/wiki/Test_tube" title="Test tube">test tube</a> fitted with a <a href="/wiki/Bung" title="Bung">bung</a>, effervescence of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> can be witnessed.
</p>
