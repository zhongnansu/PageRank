<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Carbon dioxide scrubber</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Device which absorbs carbon dioxide from circulated gas </div>
<p>A <b>carbon dioxide scrubber</b> is a piece of equipment that absorbs <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>). It is used to treat <a href="/wiki/Exhaust_gas" title="Exhaust gas">exhaust gases</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_plant" title="Industrial plant">industrial plants</a> or from exhaled air in <a href="/wiki/Life_support_system" title="Life support system">life support systems</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Rebreather" title="Rebreather">rebreathers</a> or in <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Submersible" title="Submersible">submersible</a> craft or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airtight_chamber" title="Airtight chamber">airtight chambers</a>. Carbon dioxide scrubbers are also used in <a href="/wiki/Controlled_atmosphere" title="Controlled atmosphere">controlled atmosphere</a> (CA) storage. They have also been researched for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_capture" title="Carbon capture">carbon capture</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technologies">Technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_scrubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Technologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Amine_scrubbing">Amine scrubbing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_scrubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Amine scrubbing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Amine_gas_treating" title="Amine gas treating">Amine gas treating</a></div>
<p>The dominant application for CO<sub>2</sub> scrubbing is for removal of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> from the exhaust of coal- and gas-fired <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_plant" title="Power plant">power plants</a>.  Virtually the only technology being seriously evaluated involves the use of various <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amines</a>, e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monoethanolamine" title="Monoethanolamine">monoethanolamine</a>.  Cold solutions of these organic compounds bind CO<sub>2</sub>, but the binding is reversed at higher temperatures:
</p>
<dl><dd>CO<sub>2</sub>  +  2 HOCH<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>NH<sub>2</sub> ↔ HOCH<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>NH<sub>3</sub><sup>+ </sup> + HOCH<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>NHCO<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<p>As of  2009<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_scrubber&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, this technology has only been lightly implemented because of capital costs of installing the facility and the operating costs of utilizing it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Minerals_and_zeolites">Minerals and zeolites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_scrubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Minerals and zeolites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several minerals and mineral-like materials reversibly bind CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  Most often, these minerals are oxides or hydroxides, and often the CO<sub>2</sub> is bound as carbonate. Carbon dioxide reacts with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quicklime" title="Quicklime">quicklime</a> (calcium oxide) to form <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> (<a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> in a process called carbonate looping. Other minerals include <a href="/wiki/Serpentinite" title="Serpentinite">serpentinite</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> <a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">silicate</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">olivine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Molecular_sieve" title="Molecular sieve">Molecular sieves</a> also function in this capacity.
</p><p>Various scrubbing processes have been proposed to remove CO<sub>2</sub> from the air, or from flue gases.  These usually involve using a variant of the <a href="/wiki/Kraft_process" title="Kraft process">Kraft process</a>. Scrubbing processes may be based on <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChangNYT2008_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ChangNYT2008-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  The CO<sub>2</sub> is absorbed into solution, transferred to lime via a process called causticization and released in a <a href="/wiki/Kiln" title="Kiln">kiln</a>. With some modifications to the existing processes, mainly an oxygen-fired kiln, the end result is a concentrated stream of CO<sub>2</sub> ready for storage or use in fuels. An alternative to this thermo-chemical process is an electrical one in which a nominal voltage is applied across the carbonate solution to release the CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> While simpler, this electrical process consumes more energy as it splits water at the same time. Since it depends on electricity, the electricity needs to be renewable, like PV. Otherwise the CO<sub>2</sub> produced during electricity production has to be taken into account.  Early incarnations of air capture used electricity as the energy source; hence, were dependent on a carbon-free source. Thermal air capture systems use heat generated on-site, which reduces the inefficiencies associated with off-site electricity production, but of course it still needs a source of (carbon-free) heat. <a href="/wiki/Concentrated_solar_power" title="Concentrated solar power">Concentrated solar power</a> is an example of such a source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newscientist1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-newscientist1-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sodium_hydroxide">Sodium hydroxide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_scrubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sodium hydroxide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Zeman and Lackner outlined a specific method of air capture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naoh-zeman_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-naoh-zeman-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>First, CO<sub>2</sub> is absorbed by an alkaline NaOH solution to produce dissolved <a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">sodium carbonate</a>. The absorption reaction is a gas liquid reaction, strongly exothermic, here:
</p>
<dl><dd>2NaOH(aq) + CO<sub>2</sub>(g) → Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>(aq) + H<sub>2</sub>O(l)</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>(aq) + Ca(OH)<sub>2</sub>(s) → 2NaOH(aq) + CaCO<sub>3</sub>(s)</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>ΔH° = -5.3 kJ/mol</dd></dl>
<p>Causticization is performed ubiquitously in the <a href="/wiki/Pulp_and_paper_industry" title="Pulp and paper industry">pulp and paper industry</a> and readily transfers 94% of the carbonate ions from the sodium to the calcium cation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naoh-zeman_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-naoh-zeman-9">[9]</a></sup> Subsequently, the calcium carbonate precipitate is filtered from solution and thermally decomposed to produce gaseous CO<sub>2</sub>. The calcination reaction is the only endothermic reaction in the process and is shown here:
</p>
<dl><dd>CaCO<sub>3</sub>(s) → CaO(s) + CO<sub>2</sub>(g)</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>ΔH° = + 179.2 kJ/mol</dd></dl>
<p>The thermal decomposition of calcite is performed in a lime kiln fired with oxygen in order to avoid an additional gas separation step. Hydration of the lime (CaO) completes the cycle. Lime hydration is an exothermic reaction that can be performed with water or steam. Using water, it is a liquid/solid reaction as shown here:
</p>
<dl><dd>CaO(s) + H<sub>2</sub>O(l) → Ca(OH)<sub>2</sub>(s)</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>ΔH° = -64.5 kJ/mol</dd></dl>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lithium_hydroxide">Lithium hydroxide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_scrubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lithium hydroxide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Other strong <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">bases</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Soda_lime" title="Soda lime">soda lime</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">potassium hydroxide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lithium_hydroxide" title="Lithium hydroxide">lithium hydroxide</a> are able to remove carbon dioxide by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemically_reacting" title="Chemically reacting">chemically reacting</a> with it. In particular, lithium hydroxide was used aboard <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a>, such as in the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo program</a>, to remove carbon dioxide from the atmosphere. It reacts with carbon dioxide to form <a href="/wiki/Lithium_carbonate" title="Lithium carbonate">lithium carbonate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">[10]</a></sup>  Recently <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Www.spiralith.com&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Www.spiralith.com (page does not exist)">lithium hydroxide absorbent technology</a> has been adapted for use in anesthesia machines.  Anesthesia machines which provide life support and inhaled agents during surgery typically employ a closed circuit necessitating the removal of carbon dioxide exhaled by the patient.  Lithium hydroxide may offer some safety and convenience benefits over the older calcium based products.
</p>
<dl><dd>2 LiOH(s) + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O(g) → 2 LiOH·H<sub>2</sub>O(s)</dd>
<dd>2 LiOH·H<sub>2</sub>O(s) + CO<sub>2</sub>(g) → Li<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>(s) + 3 H<sub>2</sub>O(g)</dd></dl>
<p>The net reaction being:
</p>
<dl><dd>2LiOH(s) + CO<sub>2</sub>(g) → Li<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>(s) + H<sub>2</sub>O(g)</dd></dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lithium_peroxide" title="Lithium peroxide">Lithium peroxide</a> can also be used as it absorbs more CO<sub>2</sub> per unit weight with the added advantage of releasing oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regenerative_carbon_dioxide_removal_system">Regenerative carbon dioxide removal system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_scrubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Regenerative carbon dioxide removal system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The regenerative <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_removal_system&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Carbon dioxide removal system (page does not exist)">carbon dioxide removal system</a> (RCRS) on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_shuttle" title="Space shuttle">space shuttle</a> orbiter used a two-bed system that provided continuous removal of carbon dioxide without expendable products. Regenerable systems allowed a shuttle mission a longer stay in space without having to replenish its <a href="/wiki/Sorbent" title="Sorbent">sorbent</a> canisters. Older <a href="/wiki/Lithium_hydroxide" title="Lithium hydroxide">lithium hydroxide</a> (LiOH)-based systems, which are non-regenerable, were replaced by regenerable <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a>-<a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a>-based systems. A system based on metal oxide primarily consisted of a metal oxide sorbent canister and a regenerator assembly. It worked by removing carbon dioxide using a sorbent material and then regenerating the sorbent material. The metal-oxide sorbent canister was regenerated by pumping air at approximately 400 °F (204 °C) through it at a standard flow rate of 7.5 cu ft/min (0.0035 m<sup>3</sup>/s) for 10 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Activated_carbon">Activated carbon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_scrubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Activated carbon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Activated_carbon" title="Activated carbon">Activated carbon</a> can be used as a carbon dioxide scrubber. Air with high carbon dioxide content, such as air from fruit storage locations, can be blown through beds of activated carbon and the carbon dioxide will adsorb onto the activated carbon. Once the bed is <a href="/wiki/Saturation_(chemistry)" title="Saturation (chemistry)">saturated</a> it must then be "regenerated" by blowing low carbon dioxide air, such as ambient air, through the bed. This will release the carbon dioxide from the bed, and it can then be used to scrub again, leaving the net amount of carbon dioxide in the air the same as when the process was started.
</p>
<h3><span id="Metal-organic_frameworks_.28MOFs.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Metal-organic_frameworks_(MOFs)">Metal-organic frameworks (MOFs)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_dioxide_scrubber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Metal-organic frameworks (MOFs)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal-organic_framework" title="Metal-organic framework">Metal-organic frameworks</a> are one of the most promising new technologies for carbon dioxide capture and sequestration via <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a>. Although no large-scale commercial technology exists nowadays, several research studies have indicated the great potential that MOFs have as a CO<sub>2</sub> adsorbent. Its characteristics, such as pore structure and surface functions can be easily tuned to improve CO<sub>2</sub> selectivity over other gases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>A MOF could be specifically designed to act like a CO<sub>2</sub> removal agent in post-combustion power plants. In this scenario, the flue gas would pass through a bed packed with a MOF material, where CO<sub>2</sub> would be stripped. After saturation is reached, CO<sub>2</sub> could be desorbed by doing a <a href="/wiki/Pressure_swing_adsorption" title="Pressure swing adsorption">pressure</a> or temperature swing. Carbon dioxide could then be compressed to supercritical conditions in order to be stored underground or utilized in <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">enhanced oil recovery</a> processes. However, this is not possible in large scale yet due to several difficulties, one of those being the production of MOFs in great quantities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another problem is the availability of metals necessary to synthesize MOFs. In a hypothetical scenario where these materials are used to capture all CO<sub>2</sub> needed to avoid global warming issues, such as maintaining a global temperature rise less than 2<sup>o</sup>C above the pre-industrial average temperature, we would need more metals than are available on Earth. For example, to synthesize all MOFs that utilize <a href="/wiki/Vanadium" title="Vanadium">vanadium</a>, we would need 1620% of 2010 global reserves. Even if using magnesium-based MOFs, which have demonstrated a great capacity to adsorb CO<sub>2</sub>, we would need 14% of  2010 global reserves, which is a considerable amount. Also, extensive mining would be necessary, leading to more potential environmental problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>In a project sponsored by the DOE and operated by <a href="/wiki/UOP_LLC" title="UOP LLC">UOP</a> <a href="/wiki/UOP_LLC" title="UOP LLC">LLC</a> in collaboration with faculty from four different universities, MOFs were tested as possible carbon dioxide removal agents in post-combustion flue gas. They were able to separate 90% of the CO<sub>2</sub> from the flue gas stream using a vacuum pressure swing process. Through extensive investigation, researchers found out that the best MOF to be used was Mg/DOBDC, which has a 21.7 wt% CO<sub>2</sub> loading capacity. Estimations showed that, if a similar system were to be applied to a large scale power plant, the cost of energy would increase by 65%, while a <a href="/wiki/National_Energy_Technology_Laboratory" title="National Energy Technology Laboratory">NETL</a> baseline amine based system would cause an increase of 81% (the DOE goal is 35%). Also, each ton of CO<sub>2</sub> avoided would cost $57, while for the amine system this cost is estimated to be $72. The project ended in 2010,estimating that the total capital required to implement such a project in a 580 MW power plant was 354 million dollars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
