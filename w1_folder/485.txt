<p><b>Norman Neill Greenwood</b> <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Society">FRS</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CChem" title="CChem">CChem</a> <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society_of_Chemistry" title="Fellow of the Royal Society of Chemistry">FRSC</a> (19 January 1925 – 14 November 2012<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>) was an Australian-British <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">chemist</a> and Emeritus Professor at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Leeds" title="University of Leeds">University of Leeds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webofstories_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-webofstories-4">[4]</a></sup> He is probably best known for the innovative textbook <i>Chemistry of the Elements</i>, co-authored with Alan Earnshaw, first published in 1984.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_Greenwood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After attending <a href="/wiki/University_High_School,_Melbourne" title="University High School, Melbourne">University High School</a> (1939–42), Greenwood read <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">Chemistry</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Melbourne" title="University of Melbourne">University of Melbourne</a> and graduated with a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">BSc</a> in 1945 and an <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Science" title="Master of Science">MSc</a> in 1948. In 1948 he was awarded the Exhibition of 1851 Scholarship to enable him to read for a PhD at <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Sussex_College,_Cambridge" title="Sidney Sussex College, Cambridge">Sidney Sussex College, Cambridge</a> under the supervision of <a href="/wiki/Harry_Julius_Emel%C3%A9us" title="Harry Julius Emeléus">Harry Julius Emeléus</a>. He received the PhD in 1951.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Greenwood was a senior research fellow at the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_Energy_Research_Establishment" title="Atomic Energy Research Establishment">Atomic Energy Research Establishment</a> from 1951 until 1953 when he was appointed a lecturer at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Nottingham" title="University of Nottingham">University of Nottingham</a>.  His first PhD student at <a href="/wiki/University_of_Nottingham" title="University of Nottingham">Nottingham</a> was <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Wade" title="Kenneth Wade">Kenneth Wade</a> (1954–1957).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Professor <a href="/wiki/William_Wynne-Jones,_Baron_Wynne-Jones" title="William Wynne-Jones, Baron Wynne-Jones">William Wynne-Jones</a>, who was the Chairman of the School of Chemistry at Kings College, Durham (which was to become the <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_University" title="Newcastle University">University of Newcastle upon Tyne</a> in 1963), recruited Greenwood to the first established chair of inorganic chemistry in the country in 1961.
</p><p>Greenwood was appointed professor and head of the Department of Inorganic and Structural Chemistry at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Leeds" title="University of Leeds">University of Leeds</a> in 1971, a post which he held until his retirement in 1990 when he was given the title <a href="/wiki/Emeritus" title="Emeritus">emeritus</a> professor.
</p><p>Greenwood was elected a fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> (FRS) in 1987.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>His wide-ranging researches in inorganic and structural chemistry have made major advances in the chemistry of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boron_hydrides" title="Boron hydrides">boron hydrides</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Main-group_element" title="Main-group element">main-group element</a> compounds.  He also pioneered the application of <a href="/wiki/M%C3%B6ssbauer_spectroscopy" title="Mössbauer spectroscopy">Mössbauer spectroscopy</a> to problems in chemistry.  He was a prolific writer and inspirational lecturer on chemical and educational themes, and has held numerous visiting professorships throughout the world. He was appointed by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> as principal investigator in the study of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_rocks" title="Lunar rocks">lunar rocks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-webofstories_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-webofstories-4">[4]</a></sup> He served as chairman of the IUPAC <a href="/wiki/Commission_on_Isotopic_Abundances_and_Atomic_Weights" title="Commission on Isotopic Abundances and Atomic Weights">Commission on Atomic Weights</a> from 1970 to 1975 and also as president of the <a href="/wiki/IUPAC_Inorganic_Chemistry_Division" title="IUPAC Inorganic Chemistry Division">IUPAC Inorganic Chemistry Division</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
