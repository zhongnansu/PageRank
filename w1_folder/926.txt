<p>A <b>check valve</b>, <b>clack valve</b>, <b>non-return valve</b>, <b>reflux valve</b>, <b>retention valve</b> or <b>one-way valve</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Valve" title="Valve">valve</a> that normally allows <a href="/wiki/Fluid" title="Fluid">fluid</a> (<a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a>) to flow through it in only one direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Check valves are two-port valves, meaning they have two openings in the body, one for fluid to enter and the other for <a href="/wiki/Fluid" title="Fluid">fluid</a> to leave. There are various types of check valves used in a wide variety of applications. Check valves are often part of common household items. Although they are available in a wide range of sizes and costs, check valves generally are very small, simple, or inexpensive. Check valves work automatically and most are not controlled by a person or any external control; accordingly, most do not have any valve handle or stem. The bodies (external shells) of most check valves are made of plastic or metal.
</p><p>An important concept in check valves is the cracking <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> which is the minimum differential upstream pressure between inlet and outlet at which the valve will operate. Typically the check valve is designed for and can therefore be specified for a specific cracking pressure.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Heart_valve" title="Heart valve">Heart valves</a> are essentially inlet and outlet check valves for the <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a> <a href="/wiki/Ventricle_(heart)" title="Ventricle (heart)">ventricles</a>, since the ventricles act as <a href="/wiki/Pump" title="Pump">pumps</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_terminology">Technical terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Check_valve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Technical terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Cracking pressure</b> — Refers to the minimum pressure differential needed between the inlet and outlet of the valve at which the first indication of flow occurs (steady stream of bubbles).  Cracking pressure is also known as unseating head (pressure) or opening pressure <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>.
</p><p><b>Reseal pressure</b> — Refers to the pressure differential between the inlet and outlet of the valve during the closing process of the CV, 
at which there shall be no visible leak rate. Reseal pressure is also known as sealing pressure<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>, seating head<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> (pressure) or closing pressure <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>.
</p><p><b>Back pressure</b> — a pressure higher at the outlet of a fitting than that at the inlet or a point upstream
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_check_valves">Types of check valves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Check_valve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types of check valves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303569">@media all and (max-width:720px){.mw-parser-output .tmulti>.thumbinner{width:100%!important;max-width:none!important}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .tsingle{float:none!important;max-width:none!important;width:100%!important;text-align:center}}</style><div class="thumb tmulti tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:208px;max-width:208px"><div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:102px;max-width:102px"><div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Checkvalveopen.svg"><img alt="Forward through a hole pushes away a ball that was blocking the far side of the hole, allowing flow to occur. <p><span id="ball_check_valve"></span>A <b>ball check valve</b> is a check valve in which the closing member, the movable part to block the flow, is a ball. In some ball check valves, the ball is <a href="/wiki/Spring_(device)" title="Spring (device)">spring</a>-loaded to help keep it shut. For those designs without a spring, reverse flow is required to move the ball toward the seat and create a seal. The interior surface of the main seats of ball check valves are more or less conically-tapered to guide the ball into the seat and form a positive seal when stopping reverse flow.
</p><p>Ball check valves are often very small, simple, and cheap. They are commonly used in liquid or gel minipump dispenser spigots, spray devices, some rubber bulbs for pumping air, etc., manual air pumps and some other <a href="/wiki/Pump" title="Pump">pumps</a>, and refillable dispensing syringes. Although the balls are most often made of metal, they can be made of other materials; in some specialized cases out of highly durable or inert materials, such as <a href="/wiki/Sapphire" title="Sapphire">sapphire</a>. High pressure <a href="/wiki/High-performance_liquid_chromatography" title="High-performance liquid chromatography">HPLC</a> <a href="/wiki/Pump" title="Pump">pumps</a> and similar applications commonly use small inlet and outlet ball check valves with balls of (artificial) ruby and seats made of sapphire<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> or both ball and seat of ruby<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>, for both hardness and chemical resistance. After prolonged use, such check valves can eventually wear out or the seat can develop a crack, requiring replacement. Therefore, such valves are made to be replaceable, sometimes placed in a small plastic body tightly-fitted inside a metal <a href="/wiki/Compression_fitting" title="Compression fitting">fitting</a> which can withstand high pressure and which is screwed into the pump head.
</p><p>There are similar check valves where the disc is not a ball, but some other shape, such as a <a href="/wiki/Poppet_valve" title="Poppet valve">poppet</a> energized by a spring.  Ball check valves should not be confused with <a href="/wiki/Ball_valve" title="Ball valve">ball valves</a>, which is a different type of valve in which a ball acts as a controllable rotor to stop or direct flow.
</p><p>A <b>diaphragm check valve</b> uses a flexing rubber diaphragm positioned to create a normally-closed valve. Pressure on the upstream side must be greater than the pressure on the downstream side by a certain amount, known as the pressure differential, for the check valve to open allowing flow. Once positive pressure stops, the diaphragm automatically flexes back to its original closed position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>A <b>swing check valve</b> or <b>tilting disc check valve</b> is a check valve in which the disc, the movable part to block the flow, swings on a <a href="/wiki/Hinge" title="Hinge">hinge</a> or <a href="/wiki/Trunnion" title="Trunnion">trunnion</a>, either onto the seat to block reverse flow or off the seat to allow forward flow.  The seat opening cross-section may be perpendicular to the centerline between the two ports or at an angle.  Although swing check valves can come in various sizes, large check valves are often swing check valves. A common issue caused by swing check valves is known as water hammer. This can occur when the swing check closes and the flow abruptly stops, causing a surge of pressure resulting in high velocity shock waves that act against the piping and valves, placing large stress on the metals and vibrations in the system. Undetected, water hammer can rupture pumps, valves, and pipes within the system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Flapper_valve" title="Flapper valve">flapper valve</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Flush_toilet#Tank_style_with_flapper-flush-valve" title="Flush toilet">flush-toilet mechanism</a> is an example of this type of valve. Tank pressure holding it closed is overcome by manual lift of the flapper. It then remains open until the tank drains and the flapper falls due to gravity. Another variation of this mechanism is the <b>clapper valve</b>, used in applications such <a href="/wiki/Firefighting" title="Firefighting">firefighting</a> and fire life safety systems. A hinged gate only remains open in the inflowing direction. The clapper valve often also has a spring that keeps the gate shut when there is no forward pressure. Another example is the <a href="/wiki/Backwater_valve" title="Backwater valve">backwater valve</a> (for sanitary drainage system) that protects  against flooding caused by return flow of sewage waters. Such risk occurs most often in sanitary drainage systems connected to combined sewerage systems and in rainwater drainage systems. It may be caused by intense rainfall, thaw or flood.
</p><p><span id="stop-check_valve"></span>
A <b>stop-check valve</b> is a check valve with override control to stop flow regardless of flow direction or pressure. In addition to closing in response to backflow or insufficient forward pressure (normal check-valve behavior), it can also be deliberately shut by an external mechanism, thereby preventing any flow regardless of forward pressure.
</p><p>A <b>lift-check valve</b> is a check valve in which the disc, sometimes called a <i>lift</i>, can be lifted up off its seat by higher pressure of inlet or upstream fluid to allow flow to the outlet or downstream side. A guide keeps motion of the disc on a vertical line, so the valve can later reseat properly. When the pressure is no longer higher, gravity or higher downstream pressure will cause the disc to lower onto its seat, shutting the valve to stop reverse flow.
</p><p>An <b>in-line check valve</b> is a check valve similar to the lift check valve. However, this valve generally has a spring that will 'lift' when there is pressure on the upstream side of the valve. The pressure needed on the upstream side of the valve to overcome the spring tension is called the 'cracking pressure'. When the pressure going through the valve goes below the cracking pressure, the spring will close the valve to prevent back-flow in the process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ValveMan_Brand_VM6800_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ValveMan_Brand_VM6800-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>A <b><a href="/wiki/Duckbill_valve" title="Duckbill valve">duckbill valve</a></b> is a check valve in which flow proceeds through a soft tube that protrudes into the downstream side. Back-pressure collapses this tube, cutting off flow.
</p><p>A <b><a href="/wiki/Pneumatic_non-return_valve" title="Pneumatic non-return valve">pneumatic non-return valve</a></b>.
</p><p>Multiple check valves can be connected in series. For example, a <a href="/wiki/Double_check_valve" title="Double check valve">double check valve</a> is often used as a <a href="/wiki/Backflow_prevention_device" title="Backflow prevention device">backflow prevention device</a> to keep potentially contaminated water from <a href="/wiki/Siphon" title="Siphon">siphoning</a> back into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Municipal" title="Municipal">municipal</a> water <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supply_line" title="Supply line">supply lines</a>. There are also <i>double ball check valves</i> in which there are two ball/seat combinations sequentially in the same body to ensure positive leak-tight shutoff when blocking reverse flow; and piston check valves, wafer check valves, and ball-and-cone check valves.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Check_valve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pumps">Pumps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Check_valve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pumps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cricklewood_2_railway_statio_geograph-2206898-by-Ben-Brooksbank.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="619" data-file-width="1024" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Cricklewood_2_railway_statio_geograph-2206898-by-Ben-Brooksbank.jpg/250px-Cricklewood_2_railway_statio_geograph-2206898-by-Ben-Brooksbank.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Cricklewood_2_railway_statio_geograph-2206898-by-Ben-Brooksbank.jpg/375px-Cricklewood_2_railway_statio_geograph-2206898-by-Ben-Brooksbank.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Cricklewood_2_railway_statio_geograph-2206898-by-Ben-Brooksbank.jpg/500px-Cricklewood_2_railway_statio_geograph-2206898-by-Ben-Brooksbank.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cricklewood_2_railway_statio_geograph-2206898-by-Ben-Brooksbank.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The check valves on this steam locomotive are located under the small cover between the chimney and the main dome</div></div></div>
<p>Check valves are often used with some types of pumps. Piston-driven and <a href="/wiki/Diaphragm_pump" title="Diaphragm pump">diaphragm pumps</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Metering_pump" title="Metering pump">metering pumps</a> and pumps for <a href="/wiki/Chromatography" title="Chromatography">chromatography</a> commonly use inlet and outlet ball check valves. These valves often look like small cylinders attached to the pump head on the inlet and outlet lines.  Many similar pump-like mechanisms for moving volumes of fluids around use check valves such as ball check valves.  The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feed_pump" title="Feed pump">feed pumps</a> or <a href="/wiki/Injector" title="Injector">injectors</a> which supply water to steam <a href="/wiki/Boiler" title="Boiler">boilers</a> are fitted with check valves to prevent back-flow.
</p><p>Check valves are also used in the pumps that supply water to <a href="/wiki/Water_slide" title="Water slide">water slides</a>. The water to the slide flows through a pipe which doubles as the tower holding the steps to the slide. When the facility with the slide closes for the night, the check valve stops the flow of water through the pipe; when the facility reopens for the next day, the valve is opened and the flow restarts, making the slide ready for use again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_processes">Industrial processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Check_valve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Industrial processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Check valves are used in many fluid systems such as those in <a href="/wiki/Chemical_plant" title="Chemical plant">chemical</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_plant" title="Power plant">power plants</a>, and in many other industrial processes.
</p><p>Typical applications in the nuclear industry are feed water control systems, dump lines, make-up water, miscellaneous process systems, N2 systems, and monitoring and sampling systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In aircraft and aerospace, check valves are used where high vibration, large temperature extremes and corrosive fluids are present. For example, spacecraft and launch vehicle propulsion propellant control for reaction control systems (RCS) and Attitude Control Systems (ACS) and aircraft hydraulic systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Check valves are also often used when multiple gases are mixed into one gas stream. A check valve is installed on each of the individual gas streams to prevent mixing of the gases in the original source. For example, if a fuel and an oxidizer are to be mixed, then check valves will normally be used on both the fuel and oxidizer sources to ensure that the original gas cylinders remain pure and therefore nonflammable.
</p><p>In 2010, NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory slightly modified a simple check valve design with the intention to store liquid samples indicative to life on Mars in separate reservoirs of the device without fear of cross contamination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Domestic_use">Domestic use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Check_valve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Domestic use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some types of <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> <a href="/wiki/Irrigation_sprinkler" title="Irrigation sprinkler">sprinklers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drip_irrigation" title="Drip irrigation">drip irrigation</a> emitters have small check valves built into them to keep the lines from draining when the system is shut off.
</p><p>Check valves used in domestic heating systems to prevent vertical convection, especially in combination with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_thermal" title="Solar thermal">solar thermal</a> installations, also are called gravity brakes.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting" title="Rainwater harvesting">Rainwater harvesting</a> systems that are plumbed into the main water supply of a utility provider may be required to have one or more check valves fitted to prevent contamination of the primary supply by rainwater.
</p><p>Hydraulic jacks use ball check valves to build pressure on the lifting side of the jack.
</p><p>Check valves are commonly used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inflatables" title="Inflatables">inflatables</a>, such as toys, mattresses and boats. This allows the object to be inflated without continuous or uninterrupted air pressure.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Check_valve&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Frank_P._Cotter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Frank P. Cotter (page does not exist)">Frank P. Cotter</a> developed a "simple self sealing check valve, adapted to be connected in the pipe connections without requiring special fittings and which may be readily opened for inspection or repair" 1907 (<a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a> <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a> #865,631).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Nikola_Tesla" title="Nikola Tesla">Nikola Tesla</a> invented a deceptively simple one-way valve for fluids in 1916, called a <a href="/wiki/Tesla_valve" title="Tesla valve">Tesla valve</a>. It was patented in 1920 (<a class="external text" href="http://www.google.com/patents?id=Lt1PAAAAEBAJ&amp;printsec=abstract&amp;zoom=4&amp;source=gbs_overview_r&amp;cad=0#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">U.S. patent 1,329,559</a>).
</p>
