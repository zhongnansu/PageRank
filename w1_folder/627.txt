<p>The <b>Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation</b> (<b>CSIRO</b>) is an independent <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Australia" title="Government of Australia">Australian federal government</a> agency responsible for scientific research. Its chief role is to improve the economic and social performance of industry for the benefit of the community. 
</p><p>CSIRO works with leading organisations around the world. From its headquarters in Canberra, CSIRO maintains more than 50 sites across Australia and in France, Chile and the United States, employing about 5500 people.
</p><p>Federally funded scientific research began in Australia <span class="currentage"></span>102 years ago. The Advisory Council of Science and Industry was established in 1916 but was hampered by insufficient available finance.  In 1926 the research effort was reinvigorated by establishment of the Council for Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR), which strengthened national science leadership and increased research funding. CSIR grew rapidly and achieved significant early successes. In 1949 further legislated changes included re-naming the organisation as CSIRO.
</p><p>Notable developments by CSIRO have included the invention of <a href="/wiki/Atomic_absorption_spectroscopy" title="Atomic absorption spectroscopy">atomic absorption spectroscopy</a>, essential components of <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi" title="Wi-Fi">Wi-Fi</a> technology, development of the first commercially successful <a href="/wiki/Polymer_banknote" title="Polymer banknote">polymer banknote</a>, the invention of the insect repellent in <a href="/wiki/Aerogard" title="Aerogard">Aerogard</a> and the introduction of a series of biological controls into Australia, such as the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Myxomatosis" title="Myxomatosis">myxomatosis</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rabbit_calicivirus" title="Rabbit calicivirus">rabbit calicivirus</a> for the control of <a href="/wiki/European_rabbit" title="European rabbit">rabbit</a> populations.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CSIRO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution_of_the_organisation">Evolution of the organisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CSIRO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Evolution of the organisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A precursor to CSIRO, the Advisory Council of Science and Industry, was established in 1916 on the initiative of prime minister <a href="/wiki/Billy_Hughes" title="Billy Hughes">Billy Hughes</a>. However, the Advisory Council struggled with insufficient funding during the First World War. In 1920 the Council was renamed the Commonwealth Institute of Science and Industry, and was led by <a href="/wiki/George_Handley_Knibbs" title="George Handley Knibbs">George Handley Knibbs</a> (1921–26), but continued to struggle financially.
</p><p>In 1926 the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Australia" title="Parliament of Australia">Australian Parliament</a> modified the principal Act for national scientific research (the <i>Institute of Science and Industry Act 1920</i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> by passing <i>The Science and Industry Research Act 1926</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The new Act replaced the Institute with the Council for Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR). With encouragement from prime minister <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Bruce" title="Stanley Bruce">Stanley Bruce</a>, strengthened national science leadership and increased research funding, CSIR grew rapidly and achieved significant early successes. The council was structured to represent the federal structure of government in Australia, and had state-level committees and a central council. In addition to an improved structure, CSIR benefited from strong bureaucratic management under <a href="/wiki/George_Julius" title="George Julius">George Julius</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Rivett" title="David Rivett">David Rivett</a>, and Arnold Richardson. Research focused on primary and secondary industries. Early in its existence, CSIR established divisions studying animal health and animal nutrition. After the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, research was extended into manufacturing and other secondary industries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ourhistory_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ourhistory-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1949 the Act was changed again<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and the entity name amended to the Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation. The amendment enlarged and reconstituted the organisation and its administrative structure. Under <a href="/wiki/Ian_Clunies_Ross" title="Ian Clunies Ross">Ian Clunies Ross</a> as chairman, CSIRO pursued new areas such as radio astronomy and industrial chemistry. CSIRO still operates under the provisions of the 1949 Act in a wide range of scientific inquiry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ourhistory_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ourhistory-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since 1949 CSIRO has expanded its activities to almost every field of primary, secondary and tertiary industry, including the environment, human nutrition, conservation, urban and rural planning, and water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ourhistory_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-ourhistory-4">[4]</a></sup> It works with leading organisations around the world and maintains more than 50 sites across Australia and in France, Chile and the United States of America, employing about 5500 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historic_research">Historic research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CSIRO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Historic research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CSIRO had a pioneering role in the scientific discovery of the universe through radio "eyes". A team led by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Wild_(Australian_scientist)" title="Paul Wild (Australian scientist)">Paul Wild</a> built and operated (from 1948) the world’s first solar radiospectrograph, and from 1967 the 3-kilometre-diameter (1.9 mi) radioheliograph at Culgoora in New South Wales. For three decades, the Division of Radiophysics had a world-leading role in solar research, attracting prominent solar physicists from around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>CSIRO owned the first computer in Australia, <a href="/wiki/CSIRAC" title="CSIRAC">CSIRAC</a>, built as part of a project began in the Sydney Radiophysics Laboratory in 1947. The CSIR Mk 1 ran its first program in 1949, the fifth electronic computer in the world. It was over 1,000 times faster than the mechanical calculators available at the time. It was decommissioned in 1955 and recommissioned in Melbourne as CSIRAC in 1956 as a general purpose computing machine used by over 700 projects until 1964.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The CSIRAC is the only surviving first-generation computer in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Between 1965 and 1985, <a href="/wiki/George_Bornemissza" title="George Bornemissza">George Bornemissza</a> of CSIRO's Division of Entomology founded and led the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Dung_Beetle_Project" title="Australian Dung Beetle Project">Australian Dung Beetle Project</a>. Bornemissza, upon settling in Australia from Hungary in 1951, noticed that the pastureland was covered in dry cattle dung pads which did not seem to be recycled into the soil and caused areas of rank pasture which were unpalatable to the cattle. He proposed that the reason for this was that native Australian dung beetles, which had co-evolved alongside the marsupials (which produce dung very different in its composition from cattle), were not adapted to utilise cattle dung for their nutrition and breeding since cattle had only relatively recently been introduced to the continent in the 1880s. The Australian Dung Beetle Project sought, therefore, to introduce species of dung beetle from South Africa and Europe (which had co-evolved alongside bovids) in order to improve the fertility and quality of cattle pastures. Twenty-three species were successfully introduced throughout the duration of the project and also had the effect of reducing the pestilent bush fly population by 90%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adbp_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-adbp-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Domain_name">Domain name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CSIRO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Domain name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CSIRO was the first Australian organisation to start using the Internet<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and was able to register the <a href="/wiki/Second-level_domain" title="Second-level domain">second-level domain</a> csiro.au (as opposed to csiro.org.au or csiro.com.au).  Guidelines were introduced in 1996 to regulate the use of the <a href="/wiki/.au" title=".au">.au</a> domain.
</p>

<p>When CSIR was formed in 1926, it was led initially by an Executive Committee of three people, two of whom were designated as the Chairman and the Chief Executive. Since then the roles and responsibilities of the Chair and Chief Executive have changed many times. From 1927 to 1986 the head of CSIR (and from 1949, CSIRO)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ourhistory_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-ourhistory-4">[4]</a></sup> was the Chairman, who was responsible for the management of the organisation, supported by the Chief Executive. From 1 July 1959 to 4 December 1986 CSIRO had no Chief Executive; the Chairman undertook both functions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heads_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-heads-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1986, when the <a href="/wiki/Hawke_Government" title="Hawke Government">Australian Government</a> changed the structure of CSIRO to include a board of non-executive members plus the Chief Executive to lead CSIRO, the roles changed. The Chief Executive is now responsible for management of the organisation in accordance with the strategy, plans and policies approved by the CSIRO Board which, led by the Chair of the Board, is responsible to the Australian Government for the overall strategy, governance and performance of CSIRO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heads_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-heads-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>As with its governance structure, the priorities and structure of CSIRO, and the teams and facilities that implement its research, have changed as Australia's scientific challenges have evolved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CSIRO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Total_Wellbeing_Diet">Total Wellbeing Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CSIRO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Total Wellbeing Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2005 the CSIRO gained worldwide attention, including some criticism, for promoting a high-<a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a>, low-<a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrate</a> diet of their own creation called <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Total_Wellbeing_Diet&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Total Wellbeing Diet (page does not exist)">Total Wellbeing Diet</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup> The CSIRO published the diet in a book which sold over half a million copies in Australia and over 100,000 overseas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup> The diet was criticised in an editorial by <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i> for giving scientific credence to a "fashionable" diet sponsored by meat and dairy industries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="802.11_patent">802.11 patent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CSIRO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: 802.11 patent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the early 1990s, CSIRO radio astronomy scientists John O'Sullivan, Graham Daniels, Terence Percival, Diethelm Ostry and John Deane undertook research directed to finding a way to make wireless networks work as fast as wired networks within confined spaces such as office buildings. The technique they developed, involving a particular combination of <a href="/wiki/Forward_error_correction" title="Forward error correction">forward error correction</a>, frequency-domain interleaving, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Multi-carrier_modulation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Multi-carrier modulation (page does not exist)">multi-carrier modulation</a>, became the subject of <span><a class="external text" href="//www.google.com/patents/US5487069" rel="nofollow">U.S. Patent 5,487,069</a></span>, which was granted on 23 January 1996.
</p><p>In 1997 Macquarie University professor David Skellern and his colleague Neil Weste established the company Radiata, Inc., which took a nonexclusive licence to the CSIRO patent for the purpose of developing commercially viable integrated circuit devices implementing the patented technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Radiata_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Radiata-32">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>During this period, the <a href="/wiki/IEEE_802.11" title="IEEE 802.11">IEEE 802.11</a> Working Group was developing the 802.11a wireless LAN standard. CSIRO did not participate directly in the standards process, however David Skellern was an active participant as secretary of the Working Group, and representative of Radiata.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup> In 1998 it became apparent that the CSIRO patent would be pertinent to the standard. In response to a request from Victor Hayes of Lucent Technologies, who was Chair of the 802.11 Working Group, CSIRO confirmed its commitment to make non-exclusive licenses available to implementers of the standard on reasonable and non-discriminatory terms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1999, <a href="/wiki/Cisco_Systems" title="Cisco Systems">Cisco Systems</a>, Inc. and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadcom" title="Broadcom">Broadcom</a> Corporation each invested A$4 million in Radiata, representing an 11% stake for each investor and valuing the company at around A$36 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Radiata_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Radiata-32">[31]</a></sup> In September 2000, Radiata demonstrated a chip set complying with the recently finalised IEEE 802.11a Wi-Fi standard, and capable of handling transmission rates of up to 54 Mbit/s, at a major international exhibition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Radiata_32-2"><a href="#cite_note-Radiata-32">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 2000, Cisco acquired Radiata in exchange for US$295 million in Cisco common stock with the intention of incorporating the Radiata Baseband Processor and Radio chips into its Aironet family of wireless LAN products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup> Cisco subsequently took a large write-down on the Radiata acquisition, following the 2001 <a href="/wiki/Telecoms_crash" title="Telecoms crash">telecoms crash</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup> and in 2004 it shut down its internal development of wireless chipsets based on the Radiata technology in order to focus on software development and emerging new technologies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>Controversy over the CSIRO patent arose in 2006 after the organisation won an injunction against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buffalo_Technology" title="Buffalo Technology">Buffalo Technology</a> in an infringement suit filed in Federal Court in the Eastern District of Texas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup> The injunction was subsequently suspended on appeal, with the Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit finding that the judge in Texas should have allowed a trial to proceed on Buffalo’s challenge to the validity of the CSIRO patent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup> In 2007, CSIRO declined to provide an assurance to the IEEE that it would not sue companies which refused to take a license for use in 802.11n-compliant devices, while at the same time continuing to defend legal challenges to the validity of the patent brought by <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dell" title="Dell">Dell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hewlett-Packard" title="Hewlett-Packard">Hewlett-Packard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Netgear" title="Netgear">Netgear</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>In April 2009, Hewlett-Packard broke ranks with the rest of the industry becoming the first to reach a settlement of its dispute with CSIRO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup> This agreement was followed quickly by settlements with Microsoft, <a href="/wiki/Fujitsu" title="Fujitsu">Fujitsu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asus" title="Asus">Asus</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup> and then Dell, Intel, <a href="/wiki/Nintendo" title="Nintendo">Nintendo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toshiba" title="Toshiba">Toshiba</a>, Netgear, Buffalo, <a href="/wiki/D-Link" title="D-Link">D-Link</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belkin" title="Belkin">Belkin</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=SMC_Networks&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="SMC Networks (page does not exist)">SMC</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accton" title="Accton">Accton</a>, and <a href="/wiki/3Com" title="3Com">3Com</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>The controversy grew after CSIRO sued US carriers <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AT%26T_Inc." title="AT&amp;T Inc.">AT&amp;T</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verizon" title="Verizon">Verizon</a> and <a href="/wiki/T-Mobile" title="T-Mobile">T-Mobile</a> in 2010, with the organisation being accused of being "Australia's biggest patent troll", a wrathful "patent bully", and of imposing a "WiFi tax" on American innovation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>Further fuel was added to the controversy after a settlement with the carriers, worth around $229 million, was announced in March 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup> Encouraged in part by an announcement by the Australian Minister for Tertiary Education, Skills Science and Research, Senator Chris Evans,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[49]</a></sup> an article in <a href="/wiki/Ars_Technica" title="Ars Technica">Ars Technica</a> portrayed CSIRO as a shadowy organisation responsible for US consumers being compelled to make "a multimillion dollar donation" on the basis of a questionable patent claiming "decades old" technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup> The resulting debate became so heated that the author was compelled to follow up with a defence of the original article.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[51]</a></sup> An alternative view was also published on <a href="/wiki/The_Register" title="The Register">The Register</a>, challenging a number of the assertions made in the Ars Technica piece.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>Total income to CSIRO from the patent is currently estimated at nearly $430 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[53]</a></sup>  On 14 June 2012, the CSIRO inventors received the <a href="/wiki/European_Patent_Office" title="European Patent Office">European Patent Office</a> (EPO) <a href="/wiki/European_Inventor_Award" title="European Inventor Award">European Inventor Award</a> (EIA), in the category of "Non-European Countries".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetically_modified_wheat_trials">Genetically modified wheat trials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CSIRO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Genetically modified wheat trials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 14 July 2011, <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a> activists vandalised a crop of GM wheat, circumventing the scientific trials being undertaken. Greenpeace was forced to pay reparations to CSIRO of $280,000 for the criminal damage, and were accused by the sentencing judge, Justice Hilary Penfold, of cynically using junior members of the organisation with good standing to avoid custodial sentences, while the offenders were given 9-month suspended sentences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>Following the attack Greenpeace criticised CSIRO for a close relationship with industry that had led to an increase in <a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_crops" title="Genetically modified crops">genetically modified crops</a>, even though a core aim of CSIRO is Cooperative Research "working hand in hand with industry [to] build partnerships and engage with industry to generate impact".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flynn_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Flynn-59">[58]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change_censorship:_Clive_Spash">Climate change censorship: Clive Spash</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CSIRO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Climate change censorship: Clive Spash">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 25 November 2009, a debate was held in the Australian Senate concerning the alleged involvement of the CSIRO and the Labor government in censorship. The debate was called for by opposition parties after evidence came to light that a paper critical of carbon emissions trading was being suppressed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[59]</a></sup>  At the time, the Labor government was trying to get such a scheme through the Senate. After the debate, the Science Minister, Kim Carr, was forced to release the paper, but when doing so in the Senate he also delivered a letter from the CEO of the CSIRO, <a href="/wiki/Megan_Clark" title="Megan Clark">Megan Clark</a>, which attacked the report's author and threatened him with unspecified punishment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[60]</a></sup>  The author of the paper, Clive Spash, was cited in the press as having been bullied and harassed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[61]</a></sup> and later gave a radio interview about this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[62]</a></sup>  In the midst of the affair, CSIRO management had considered releasing the paper with edits that Nature reported would be "tiny".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[63]</a></sup> Spash claimed the changes actually demanded amounted to censorship and resigned. He later posted on his website a document detailing the text that CSIRO management demanded be deleted;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[64]</a></sup> by itself, this document forms a coherent set of statements criticising emissions trading without any additional wording needed. In subsequent Senate Estimates hearings during 2010, Senator Carr and Clark went on record claiming the paper was originally stopped from publication solely due to its low quality not meeting CSIRO standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[65]</a></sup>  At the time of its attempted suppression, the paper had been accepted for publication in an academic journal, <a href="/wiki/New_Political_Economy_(journal)" title="New Political Economy (journal)">New Political Economy</a>, which in 2010 had been ranked by the Australian Research Council as an 'A class' publication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[66]</a></sup> In an ABC radio interview, Spash called for a Senate enquiry into the affair and the role played by senior management and the Science Minister.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[67]</a></sup>  After these events, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sydney_Morning_Herald" title="Sydney Morning Herald">Sydney Morning Herald</a></i>  reported that "Questions are being raised about the closeness of BHP Billiton and the CSIRO under its chief executive, Megan Clark".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[68]</a></sup>  After his resignation, an unedited version of the paper was released by Spash as a discussion paper,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[69]</a></sup> and later published as an academic journal article.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[70]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="CSIRO.E2.80.93Novartis.E2.80.93DataTrace_scandal"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="CSIRO–Novartis–DataTrace_scandal">CSIRO–Novartis–DataTrace scandal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CSIRO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: CSIRO–Novartis–DataTrace scandal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 11 April 2013, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sydney_Morning_Herald" title="Sydney Morning Herald">Sydney Morning Herald</a></i> ran a story on how CSIRO had "duped" the Swiss-based pharmaceutical giant <a href="/wiki/Novartis" title="Novartis">Novartis</a> into purchasing an anti-counterfeit technology for its vials of injectable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voltaren" title="Voltaren">Voltaren</a>. The invention was marketed by a small Australian company called DataTrace DNA as a method of identifying fake vials, on the basis that a unique tracer code developed by CSIRO was embedded in the product. However, the code sold to Novartis for more than A$2M was apparently not unique, and was based on a "cheap tracer ... bought in bulk from a Chinese distributor". Novartis was contractually bound not to reverse-engineer the tracer to verify its uniqueness. The <i>Sydney Morning Herald</i> report alleges that this was done with the knowledge of key CSIRO personnel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[71]</a></sup>
</p><p>CSIRO has since conducted a full review of the allegations and found no evidence to support them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[72]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Alleged_bullying.2C_harassment_and_victimisation"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Alleged_bullying,_harassment_and_victimisation">Alleged bullying, harassment and victimisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CSIRO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Alleged bullying, harassment and victimisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In recent years<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (February 2017)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> the CSIRO has fallen under the spotlight for allegedly exhibiting a culture of workplace bullying and harassment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[73]</a></sup>  Former CSIRO employees started to surface with experiences of workplace bullying and other unreasonable behaviour by current and former CSIRO staff members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[75]</a></sup> CSIRO took the allegations seriously and responded to the articles on a number of occasions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[77]</a></sup>
</p><p>The shadow minister for innovation, industry, science and research, <a href="/wiki/Sophie_Mirabella" title="Sophie Mirabella">Sophie Mirabella</a>, wrote to the government requesting it establish an inquiry. Mirabella said she is aware of as many as 100 cases of alleged workplace harassment. On 20 July 2012 <a href="/wiki/Comcare" title="Comcare">Comcare</a> issued CSIRO with an Improvement Notice with regard to handling and management of workplace misconduct/code of conduct type investigations and allegations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[78]</a></sup> On 24 June 2013 Mirabella advised the <a href="/wiki/Australian_House_of_Representatives" title="Australian House of Representatives">Australian House of Representatives</a> that in relation to the worker's compensation claim for psychological injuries of ex-CSIRO employee, Martin Williams, which was vigorously defended by <a href="/wiki/Comcare" title="Comcare">Comcare</a> on the advice of the CSIRO, that CSIRO officers had provided false testimony on no less than 128 occasions under oath when the matter went before the <a href="/wiki/Administrative_Appeals_Tribunal" title="Administrative Appeals Tribunal">Administrative Appeals Tribunal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[79]</a></sup> Mirabella stated, "even in establishing the framework for this inquiry it is obvious there's an inappropriate 'hands on' approach by CSIRO."
</p><p>In response to the allegations Clark commissioned Dennis Pearce,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[80]</a></sup> who is assisted by an investigation team from HWL Ebsworth Lawyers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[81]</a></sup> to conduct an independent investigation into allegations of workplace bullying and other unreasonable behaviour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[82]</a></sup>  Mirabella continued to question the independence of the investigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[83]</a></sup> The first stage of the investigation is scheduled to publish its findings at the end of July 2013, and the final stage is scheduled to be complete by February 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[84]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CSIRO_and_the_Liberal_Government">CSIRO and the Liberal Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CSIRO&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: CSIRO and the Liberal Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In August 2015 the CSIRO discontinued its annual July and August survey, conducted over the previous five years, polling to create a long-term view of how Australians viewed global warming and their support for action.  In the previous 2013 poll, 86 per cent agreed with the statement that climate change was occurring and only 7.6 per cent disagreed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[85]</a></sup>
</p><p>On the 11th February 2016, Dr Larry Marshall – a former venture capitalist with Southern Cross Venture Holdings, who had been appointed CEO of the CSIRO on the 1st January 2015,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[86]</a></sup> caused an international outcry after describing Australia’s national climate change discussion as “more like religion than science,” a week after announcing hundreds of job cuts to the organisation that will reduce the effectiveness of its climate research team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[87]</a></sup>
</p><p>In “an open letter to the Australian Government and CSIRO”, 2,800 of the leading climate scientists from 60 countries say the announcement of cuts to the CSIRO’s Oceans and Atmosphere research program has alarmed the global climate research community.  They say the decision shows a lack of insight and a misunderstanding of the importance of the depth and significance of Australian contributions to global and regional climate research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[88]</a></sup>
</p>
