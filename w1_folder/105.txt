<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">PubMed Central</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>PubMed Central</b> (<b>PMC</b>) is a free <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_repository" title="Digital repository">digital repository</a> that archives <a href="/wiki/Open_access" title="Open access">publicly accessible</a> full-text scholarly articles that have been published within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biomedical" title="Biomedical">biomedical</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_sciences" title="Life sciences">life sciences</a> journal literature. As one of the major research databases within the suite of resources that have been developed by the <a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Biotechnology_Information" title="National Center for Biotechnology Information">National Center for Biotechnology Information</a> (NCBI), PubMed Central is much more than just a document repository. Submissions into PMC undergo an indexing and formatting procedure which results in enhanced metadata, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_ontologies&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Medical ontologies (page does not exist)">medical ontology</a>, and unique identifiers which all enrich the XML structured data for each article on deposit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Content within PMC can easily be interlinked to many other NCBI databases and accessed via <a href="/wiki/Entrez" title="Entrez">Entrez</a> search and retrieval systems, further enhancing the public's ability to freely discover, read and build upon this portfolio of biomedical knowledge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>PubMed Central should not be confused with <a href="/wiki/PubMed" title="PubMed">PubMed</a>. These are two very different services at their core.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> While PubMed is a searchable database of biomedical citations and abstracts, the full-text article referenced in the PubMed record will physically reside elsewhere. (Sometimes in print, sometimes online, sometimes free, sometimes behind a <a href="/wiki/Paywall" title="Paywall">paywall</a> accessible only to paying subscribers.) PubMed Central is a free digital archive of articles, accessible to anyone from anywhere via a basic web browser. The full text of all PubMed Central articles is free to read, with varying provisions for reuse.
</p><p>As of  December 2016<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=PubMed_Central&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the PMC archive contained over 4.1 million articles,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> with contributions coming directly from publishers or authors depositing their own manuscripts into the repository per the <a href="/wiki/NIH_Public_Access_Policy" title="NIH Public Access Policy">NIH Public Access Policy</a>. Older data shows that from Jan 2013 to Jan 2014 author-initiated deposits exceeded 103,000 papers during this 12-month period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> PMC also identifies about 4,000 journals which now participate in some capacity to automatically deposit their published content into the PMC repository.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Some participating publishers will delay the release of their articles on PubMed Central for a set time after publication, this is often referred to as an "embargo period", and can range from a few months to a few years depending on the journal.  (Embargoes of six to twelve months are the most common.) However, PubMed Central is a key example of "systematic external distribution by a third party"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> which is still prohibited by the contributor agreements of many publishers.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adoption">Adoption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PubMed_Central&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Adoption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/NIH_Public_Access_Policy" title="NIH Public Access Policy">NIH Public Access Policy</a></div>
<p>Launched in February 2000, the repository has grown rapidly as the <a href="/wiki/NIH_Public_Access_Policy" title="NIH Public Access Policy">NIH Public Access Policy</a> is designed to make all research funded by the <a href="/wiki/National_Institutes_of_Health" title="National Institutes of Health">National Institutes of Health</a> (NIH) freely accessible to anyone, and, in addition, many publishers are working cooperatively with the NIH to provide free access to their works. In late 2007, the Consolidated Appropriations Act of 2008 (H.R. 2764) was signed into law and included a provision requiring the NIH to modify its policies and require inclusion into PubMed Central complete electronic copies of their peer-reviewed research and findings from NIH-funded research. These articles are required to be included within 12 months of publication. This is the first time the US government has required an agency to provide <a href="/wiki/Open_access" title="Open access">open access</a> to research and is an evolution from the 2005 policy, in which the NIH asked researchers to voluntarily add their research to PubMed Central.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-science_codex_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-science_codex-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>A UK version of the PubMed Central system, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK_PubMed_Central_(UKPMC)" title="UK PubMed Central (UKPMC)">UK PubMed Central (UKPMC)</a>, has been developed by the <a href="/wiki/Wellcome_Trust" title="Wellcome Trust">Wellcome Trust</a> and the <a href="/wiki/British_Library" title="British Library">British Library</a> as part of a nine-strong group of UK research funders. This system went live in January 2007. On 1 November 2012, it became <a href="/wiki/Europe_PubMed_Central" title="Europe PubMed Central">Europe PubMed Central</a>. The Canadian member of the PubMed Central International network, <a href="/wiki/PubMed_Central_Canada" title="PubMed Central Canada">PubMed Central Canada</a>, was launched in October 2009.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Library_of_Medicine" title="National Library of Medicine">National Library of Medicine</a> "NLM Journal Publishing Tag Set" journal article <a href="/wiki/Markup_language" title="Markup language">markup language</a> is freely available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Learned_and_Professional_Society_Publishers" title="Association of Learned and Professional Society Publishers">Association of Learned and Professional Society Publishers</a> comments that "it is likely to become the standard for preparing scholarly content for both books and journals".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> A related <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Document_Type_Definition" title="Document Type Definition">DTD</a> is available for books.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">Library of Congress</a> and the British Library have announced support for the NLM DTD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> It has also been popular with journal service providers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>With the release of public access plans for many agencies beyond NIH, PMC is in the process of becoming the repository for a wider variety of articles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>  This includes NASA content, with the interface branded as "PubSpace".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology">Technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PubMed_Central&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Articles are sent to PubMed Central by publishers in <a href="/wiki/XML" title="XML">XML</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SGML" title="SGML">SGML</a>, using a variety of article <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Document_Type_Definition" title="Document Type Definition">DTDs</a>. Older and larger publishers may have their own established in-house DTDs, but many publishers use the NLM Journal Publishing DTD (see above).
</p><p>Received articles are converted via <a href="/wiki/XSLT" title="XSLT">XSLT</a> to the very similar NLM Archiving and Interchange DTD.  This process may reveal errors that are reported back to the publisher for correction. Graphics are also converted to standard formats and sizes. The original and converted forms are archived. The converted form is moved into a relational database, along with associated files for graphics, multimedia, or other associated data. Many publishers also provide <a href="/wiki/PDF" title="PDF">PDF</a> of their articles, and these are made available without change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Bibliographic citations are parsed and automatically linked to the relevant abstracts in PubMed, articles in PubMed Central, and resources on publishers' Web sites. PubMed links also lead to PubMed Central. Unresolvable references, such as to journals or particular articles not yet available at one of these sources, are tracked in the database and automatically come "live" when the resources become available.
</p><p>An in-house indexing system provides search capability, and is aware of biological and <a href="/wiki/Medical_terminology" title="Medical terminology">medical terminology</a>, such as generic vs. proprietary drug names, and alternate names for organisms, diseases and anatomical parts.
</p><p>When a user accesses a journal issue, a table of contents is automatically generated by retrieving all articles, letters, editorials, etc. for that issue. When an actual item such as an article is reached, PubMed Central converts the NLM markup to <a href="/wiki/HTML" title="HTML">HTML</a> for delivery, and provides links to related data objects. This is feasible because the variety of incoming data has first been converted to standard DTDs and graphic formats.
</p><p>In a separate submission stream, NIH-funded authors may deposit articles into PubMed Central using the NIH Manuscript Submission (NIHMS). Articles thus submitted typically go through XML markup in order to be converted to NLM DTD.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PubMed_Central&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Reactions to PubMed Central among the scholarly publishing community range between a genuine enthusiasm by some,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> to cautious concern by others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> While PMC is a welcome partner to open access publishers in its ability to augment the discovery and dissemination of biomedical knowledge, that same truth causes others to worry about traffic being diverted from the published version-of-record, the economic consequences of less readership, as well as the effect on maintaining a community of scholars within learned societies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Libraries, universities, open access supporters, consumer health advocacy groups, and patient rights organizations have applauded PubMed Central, and hope to see similar public access repositories developed by other federal funding agencies so to freely share any research publications that were the result of taxpayer support.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Antelman study of open access publishing found that in philosophy, political science, electrical and electronic engineering and mathematics, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_access_(publishing)" title="Open access (publishing)">open access</a> papers had a greater research impact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> A randomised trial found an increase in content downloads of open access papers, with no citation advantage over subscription access one year after publication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>The change in procedure has received criticism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The American Physiological Society has expressed reservations about the implementation of the policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="PMCID">PMCID</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PubMed_Central&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: PMCID">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The PMCID (PubMed Central identifier), also known as the PMC reference number, is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bibliographic" title="Bibliographic">bibliographic</a> <a href="/wiki/Identifier" title="Identifier">identifier</a> for the PubMed Central database, much like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PMID" title="PMID">PMID</a> is the bibliographic identifier for the <a href="/wiki/PubMed" title="PubMed">PubMed</a> database. The two identifiers are distinct however. It consists of "PMC" followed by a <a href="/wiki/String_(computer_science)" title="String (computer science)">string</a> of seven numbers. The format is:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>PMCID: PMC1852221</li></ul>
<p>Authors applying for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NIH" title="NIH">NIH</a> awards must include the PMCID in their application.
</p>
