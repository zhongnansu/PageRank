<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Electrolysis</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a>, <b>electrolysis</b> is a technique that uses a <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">direct</a> <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric current</a> (DC) to drive an otherwise non-spontaneous chemical reaction. Electrolysis is commercially important as a stage in the <a href="#Industrial_uses">separation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">elements</a> from naturally occurring sources such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ores" title="Ores">ores</a> using an <a href="/wiki/Electrolytic_cell" title="Electrolytic cell">electrolytic cell</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Voltage" title="Voltage">voltage</a> that is needed for electrolysis to occur is called the <a href="/wiki/Decomposition_potential" title="Decomposition potential">decomposition potential</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word "electrolysis" was introduced by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">Michael Faraday</a> in the 19th century, on the suggestion of the Rev. <a href="/wiki/William_Whewell" title="William Whewell">William Whewell</a>, using the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> words <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">ἤλεκτρον</span> <small></small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Greek" title="Help:IPA/Greek">[ɛ̌ːlektron]</a></span> "amber", which since the 17th century was associated with <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electric phenomena</a>, and <i><span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">λύσις</span></i> <small></small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Greek" title="Help:IPA/Greek">[lýsis]</a></span> meaning "dissolution". Nevertheless, electrolysis, as a tool to study chemical reactions and obtain pure <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">elements</a>, precedes the coinage of the term and formal description by Faraday.
</p>
<ul><li>1785 – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martinus_van_Marum" title="Martinus van Marum">Martinus van Marum</a>'s electrostatic generator was used to reduce <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">antimony</a> from their salts using electrolysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>1800 – <a href="/wiki/William_Nicholson_(chemist)" title="William Nicholson (chemist)">William Nicholson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Carlisle" title="Anthony Carlisle">Anthony Carlisle</a> (view also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johann_Ritter" title="Johann Ritter">Johann Ritter</a>), decomposed <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> into <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>.</li>
<li>1808 – <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">Potassium</a> (1807), <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> (1807), <a href="/wiki/Barium" title="Barium">barium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> were discovered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Humphry_Davy#Electrolysis" title="Sir Humphry Davy">Sir Humphry Davy</a> using electrolysis.</li>
<li>1821 – <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">Lithium</a> was discovered by the English chemist <a href="/wiki/William_Thomas_Brande" title="William Thomas Brande">William Thomas Brande</a>, who obtained it by electrolysis of lithium oxide.</li>
<li>1833 – <a href="/wiki/Michael_Faraday" title="Michael Faraday">Michael Faraday</a> develops his <a href="/wiki/Faraday%27s_laws_of_electrolysis" title="Faraday's laws of electrolysis">two laws of electrolysis</a>, and provides a mathematical explanation of his laws.</li>
<li>1875 – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_%C3%89mile_Lecoq_de_Boisbaudran" title="Paul Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran">Paul Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran</a> discovered <a href="/wiki/Gallium" title="Gallium">gallium</a> using electrolysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li>1886 – <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">Fluorine</a> was discovered by <a href="/wiki/Henri_Moissan" title="Henri Moissan">Henri Moissan</a> using electrolysis.</li>
<li>1886 – <a href="/wiki/Hall%E2%80%93H%C3%A9roult_process" title="Hall–Héroult process">Hall–Héroult process</a> developed for making <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a></li>
<li>1890 – <a href="/wiki/Castner%E2%80%93Kellner_process" title="Castner–Kellner process">Castner–Kellner process</a> developed for making <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Electrolysis is the passing of a <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">direct</a> <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric current</a> through an <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ionic</a> substance that is either molten or dissolved in a suitable solvent, producing chemical reactions at the electrodes and a separation of the materials.
</p><p>The main components required to achieve electrolysis are:
</p>
<ul><li>An <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolyte</a>: a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_substance" title="Chemical substance">substance</a>, frequently an ion-conducting <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a> that contains free <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a>, which carry <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric current</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolyte</a>.  If the <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> are not mobile, as in most solid <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a>, then electrolysis cannot occur.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">direct current</a> (DC) electrical supply: provides the <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> necessary to create or discharge the <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolyte</a>. Electric current is carried by <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> in the external circuit.</li>
<li>Two <a href="/wiki/Electrode" title="Electrode">electrodes</a>: <a href="/wiki/Electrical_conductor" title="Electrical conductor">electrical conductors</a> that provide the physical interface between the <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolyte</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_circuit" title="Electrical circuit">electrical circuit</a> that provides the <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a>.</li></ul>
<p>Electrodes of <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> material are widely used. Choice of suitable electrode depends on chemical reactivity between the electrode and electrolyte and manufacturing cost.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Process_of_electrolysis">Process of electrolysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Process of electrolysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The key process of electrolysis is the interchange of atoms and ions by the removal or addition of electrons from the external circuit. The desired products of electrolysis are often in a different physical state from the electrolyte and can be removed by some physical processes. For example, in the electrolysis of <a href="/wiki/Brine" title="Brine">brine</a> to produce hydrogen and chlorine, the products are gaseous. These gaseous products bubble from the electrolyte and are collected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tilley2004_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tilley2004-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 NaCl + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O → 2 NaOH + H<sub>2</sub> + Cl<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>A liquid containing electrolyte is produced by:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Solvation" title="Solvation">Solvation</a> or reaction of an <a href="/wiki/Ionic_compound" title="Ionic compound">ionic compound</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvent</a> (such as water) to produce mobile ions</li>
<li>An ionic compound is melted by heating</li></ul>
<p>An electrical potential is applied across a pair of <a href="/wiki/Electrode" title="Electrode">electrodes</a> immersed in the electrolyte. 
</p><p>Each electrode attracts ions that are of the opposite <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">charge</a>. Positively charged ions (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">cations</a>) move towards the electron-providing (negative) cathode. Negatively charged ions (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">anions</a>) move towards the electron-extracting (positive) anode.
</p><p>In this process <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> are either absorbed or released. Neutral atoms gain or lose electrons and become charged ions that then pass into the electrolyte. The formation of uncharged atoms from ions is called discharging. When an ion gains or loses enough electrons to become uncharged (neutral) atoms, the newly formed atoms separate from the electrolyte. Positive metal ions like Cu<sup>++  </sup>deposit onto the cathode in a layer. The terms for this are <a href="/wiki/Electroplating" title="Electroplating">electroplating</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrowinning" title="Electrowinning">electrowinning</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrorefining" title="Electrorefining">electrorefining</a>.  When an ion gains or loses electrons without becoming neutral, its electronic charge is altered in the process. In chemistry, the loss of electrons is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">Oxidation</a>, while electron gain is called reduction.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxidation_and_reduction_at_the_electrodes">Oxidation and reduction at the electrodes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Oxidation and reduction at the electrodes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">Oxidation</a> of ions or neutral molecules occurs at the <a href="/wiki/Anode" title="Anode">anode</a>. For example, it is possible to oxidize ferrous ions to ferric ions at the anode:
</p>
<dl><dd>Fe<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>(aq) → Fe<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>(aq) + e<sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">Reduction</a> of ions or neutral molecules occurs at the <a href="/wiki/Cathode" title="Cathode">cathode</a>.
</p><p>It is possible to reduce <a href="/wiki/Ferricyanide" title="Ferricyanide">ferricyanide</a> ions to <a href="/wiki/Ferrocyanide" title="Ferrocyanide">ferrocyanide</a> ions at the cathode:
</p>
<dl><dd>Fe(CN)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3-</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span> + e<sup>−</sup> → Fe(CN)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4-</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></dd></dl>
<p>Neutral molecules can also react at either of the electrodes. For example: p-Benzoquinone can be reduced to hydroquinone at the cathode:
</p><p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:P-Benzochinon.svg"><img alt="P-Benzochinon.svg" data-file-height="176" data-file-width="70" height="88" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/P-Benzochinon.svg/35px-P-Benzochinon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/P-Benzochinon.svg/53px-P-Benzochinon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/P-Benzochinon.svg/70px-P-Benzochinon.svg.png 2x" width="35"/></a> + 2 e<sup>−</sup> + 2 H<sup>+</sup> → <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hydrochinon2.svg"><img alt="Hydrochinon2.svg" data-file-height="80" data-file-width="218" height="80" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Hydrochinon2.svg/218px-Hydrochinon2.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Hydrochinon2.svg/327px-Hydrochinon2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Hydrochinon2.svg/436px-Hydrochinon2.svg.png 2x" width="218"/></a>
</p><p>In the last example, H<sup>+</sup> ions (hydrogen ions) also take part in the reaction, and are provided by an acid in the solution, or by the solvent itself (water, methanol etc.). Electrolysis reactions involving H<sup>+</sup> ions are fairly common in acidic solutions. In aqueous alkaline  solutions, reactions involving OH<sup>−</sup> (hydroxide ions) are common.
</p><p>Sometimes the solvents themselves (usually water) are oxidized or reduced at the electrodes. It is even possible to have electrolysis involving gases. Such as when using a <a href="/wiki/Gas_diffusion_electrode" title="Gas diffusion electrode">Gas diffusion electrode</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_changes_during_electrolysis">Energy changes during electrolysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Energy changes during electrolysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The amount of electrical energy that must be added equals the change in <a href="/wiki/Gibbs_free_energy" title="Gibbs free energy">Gibbs free energy</a> of the reaction plus the losses in the system. The losses can (in theory) be arbitrarily close to zero, so the maximum <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamic</a> efficiency equals the <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy" title="Enthalpy">enthalpy</a> change divided by the free energy change of the reaction. In most cases, the electric input is larger than the enthalpy change of the reaction, so some energy is released in the form of heat. In some cases, for instance, in the electrolysis of <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a> into hydrogen and oxygen at high temperature, the opposite is true and heat energy is absorbed. This heat is absorbed from the surroundings, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heating_value" title="Heating value">heating value</a> of the produced hydrogen is higher than the electric input.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_techniques">Related techniques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Related techniques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The following techniques are related to electrolysis:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Electrochemical_cell" title="Electrochemical cell">Electrochemical cells</a>, including the hydrogen <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">fuel cell</a>, use differences in <a href="/wiki/Standard_electrode_potential" title="Standard electrode potential">Standard electrode potential</a> to generate an electrical potential that provides useful power. Though related via the interaction of ions and electrodes, electrolysis and the operation of electrochemical cells are quite distinct. However, a chemical cell should <i>not</i> be seen as performing <i>electrolysis in reverse</i>.</li></ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_law_of_electrolysis">Second law of electrolysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Second law of electrolysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Faraday discovered that when the same amount of current is passed through different electrolytes/elements connected in series, the mass of substance liberated/deposited at the electrodes  is directly proportional to their <a href="/wiki/Equivalent_weight" title="Equivalent weight">equivalent weight</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_uses">Industrial uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Industrial uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hall-heroult-kk-2008-12-31.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="585" height="193" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Hall-heroult-kk-2008-12-31.png/220px-Hall-heroult-kk-2008-12-31.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Hall-heroult-kk-2008-12-31.png/330px-Hall-heroult-kk-2008-12-31.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Hall-heroult-kk-2008-12-31.png/440px-Hall-heroult-kk-2008-12-31.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hall-heroult-kk-2008-12-31.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hall-Heroult_process" title="Hall-Heroult process">Hall-Heroult process</a> for producing <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a></div></div></div>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Electrometallurgy" title="Electrometallurgy">Electrometallurgy</a> is the process of reduction of metals from metallic compounds to obtain the pure form of metal using electrolysis. <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">Aluminium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>, and in some cases <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, are produced in this way.</li>
<li>Production of <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a></li>
<li>Production of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chlorate" title="Sodium chlorate">sodium chlorate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Potassium_chlorate" title="Potassium chlorate">potassium chlorate</a></li>
<li>Production of perfluorinated organic compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Trifluoroacetic_acid" title="Trifluoroacetic acid">trifluoroacetic acid</a> by the process of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrofluorination" title="Electrofluorination">electrofluorination</a></li>
<li>Production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copper_extraction_techniques#Electrorefining" title="Copper extraction techniques">electrolytic copper</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Cathode" title="Cathode">cathode</a>, from refined <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> of lower purity as an <a href="/wiki/Anode" title="Anode">anode</a>.</li></ul>
<p>Electrolysis has many other uses:
</p>
<ul><li>Production of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> for <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_submarines" title="Nuclear submarines">nuclear submarines</a>.</li>
<li>Production of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> for fuel, using a cheap source of electrical energy.</li></ul>
<p>Electrolysis is also used in the cleaning and preservation of old artifacts. Because the process separates the non-metallic particles from the metallic ones, it is very useful for cleaning a wide variety of metallic objects, from old coins to even larger objects including <a href="/wiki/Rust" title="Rust">rusted</a> <a href="/wiki/Cast_iron" title="Cast iron">cast iron</a> <a href="/wiki/Cylinder_block" title="Cylinder block">cylinder blocks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cylinder_head" title="Cylinder head">heads</a> when rebuilding automobile engines. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rust_removal" title="Rust removal">Rust removal</a> from small iron or steel objects by electrolysis can be done in a home workshop using simple materials such as a plastic bucket, <a href="/wiki/Tap_water" title="Tap water">tap water</a>, lengths of <a href="/wiki/Rebar" title="Rebar">rebar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">washing soda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baling_wire" title="Baling wire">baling wire</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Battery_charger" title="Battery charger">battery charger</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-antique-engines_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-antique-engines-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacturing_processes">Manufacturing processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Manufacturing processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In manufacturing, electrolysis can be used for:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Electroplating" title="Electroplating">Electroplating</a>, where a thin film of metal is deposited over a substrate material. Electroplating is used in many industries for either functional or decorative purposes, as in vehicle bodies and nickel coins.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Electrochemical_machining" title="Electrochemical machining">Electrochemical machining</a> (ECM), where an electrolytic cathode is used as a shaped tool for removing material by anodic oxidation from a workpiece. ECM is often used as technique for <a href="/wiki/Burr_(edge)" title="Burr (edge)">deburring</a> or for etching metal surfaces like tools or knives with a permanent mark or logo.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Competing_half-reactions_in_solution_electrolysis">Competing half-reactions in solution electrolysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Competing half-reactions in solution electrolysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Using a cell containing inert platinum electrodes, electrolysis of aqueous solutions of some salts leads to reduction of the cations (e.g., metal deposition with, e.g., zinc salts) and oxidation of the anions (e.g. evolution of bromine with bromides). However, with salts of some metals (e.g. sodium) hydrogen is evolved at the cathode, and for salts containing some anions (e.g. sulfate SO<sub>4</sub><sup>2−</sup>) oxygen is evolved at the anode. In both cases this is due to water being reduced to form hydrogen or oxidized to form oxygen.
In principle the voltage required to electrolyze a salt solution can be derived from the <a href="/wiki/Standard_electrode_potential" title="Standard electrode potential">standard electrode potential</a> for the reactions at the anode and cathode. The <a href="/wiki/Standard_electrode_potential" title="Standard electrode potential">standard electrode potential</a> is directly related to the <a href="/wiki/Gibbs_free_energy" title="Gibbs free energy">Gibbs free energy</a>, ΔG, for the reactions at each electrode and refers to an electrode with no current flowing. An extract from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Table_of_standard_electrode_potentials" title="Table of standard electrode potentials">table of standard electrode potentials</a> is shown below.

<p>In terms of electrolysis, this table should be interpreted as follows:
</p>
<ul><li>Oxidized species (often a cation) with a more negative cell potential are more difficult to reduce than oxidized species with a more positive cell potential. For example, it is more difficult to reduce a sodium ion to a sodium metal than it is to reduce a zinc ion to a zinc metal.</li>
<li>Reduced species (often an anion) with a more positive cell potential are more difficult to oxidize than reduced species with a more negative cell potential. For example, it is more difficult to oxidize sulfate anions than it is to oxidize bromide anions.</li></ul>
<p>Using the <a href="/wiki/Nernst_equation" title="Nernst equation">Nernst equation</a> the <a href="/wiki/Electrode_potential" title="Electrode potential">electrode potential</a> can be calculated for a specific concentration of ions, temperature and the number of electrons involved. For pure water (<a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> 7):
</p>
<ul><li>the electrode potential for the reduction producing hydrogen is −0.41 V</li>
<li>the electrode potential for the oxidation producing oxygen is +0.82 V.</li></ul>
<p>Comparable figures calculated in a similar way, for 1M <a href="/wiki/Zinc_bromide" title="Zinc bromide">zinc bromide</a>, ZnBr<sub>2</sub>, are −0.76 V for the reduction to Zn metal and +1.10 V for the oxidation producing bromine.
The conclusion from these figures is that hydrogen should be produced at the cathode and oxygen at the anode from the electrolysis of water—which is at variance with the experimental observation that zinc metal is deposited and bromine is produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
The explanation is that these calculated potentials only indicate the thermodynamically preferred reaction. In practice many other factors have to be taken into account such as the kinetics of some of the reaction steps involved. These factors together mean that a higher potential is required for the reduction and oxidation of water than predicted, and these are termed <a href="/wiki/Overpotential" title="Overpotential">overpotentials</a>. Experimentally it is known that <a href="/wiki/Overpotential" title="Overpotential">overpotentials</a> depend on the design of the cell and the nature of the electrodes.
</p><p>For the electrolysis of a neutral (pH 7) sodium chloride solution, the reduction of sodium ion is thermodynamically very difficult and water is reduced evolving hydrogen leaving hydroxide ions in solution. At the anode the oxidation of chlorine is observed rather than the oxidation of water since the overpotential for the oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chloride</a> to <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> is lower than the overpotential for the oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> to <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxide_ion" title="Hydroxide ion">hydroxide ions</a> and dissolved <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> gas react further to form <a href="/wiki/Hypochlorous_acid" title="Hypochlorous acid">hypochlorous acid</a>. The aqueous solutions resulting from this process is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrolyzed_water" title="Electrolyzed water">electrolyzed water</a> and is used as a disinfectant and cleaning agent.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrolysis_of_carbon_dioxide">Electrolysis of carbon dioxide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Electrolysis of carbon dioxide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Electrochemical_reduction_of_carbon_dioxide" title="Electrochemical reduction of carbon dioxide">Electrochemical reduction of carbon dioxide</a></div>
<p>The electrochemical reduction or electrocatalytic conversion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CO2" title="CO2">CO<sub>2</sub></a> can produce value-added chemicals such <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">ethylene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">ethane</a>, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The electrolysis of carbon dioxide gives formate or carbon monoxide, but sometimes more elaborate organic compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">ethylene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>  This technology is under research as a carbon-neutral route to organic compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrolysis_of_acidified_water">Electrolysis of acidified  water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Electrolysis of acidified water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water" title="Electrolysis of water">Electrolysis of water</a></div>
<p>Electrolysis of water produces <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>.
</p>
<dl><dd>2 H<sub>2</sub>O(l) → 2 H<sub>2</sub>(g) + O<sub>2</sub>(g); E<sub>0</sub> = +1.229 V</dd></dl>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Energy_conversion_efficiency" title="Energy conversion efficiency">energy efficiency</a> of water electrolysis varies widely. The efficiency of an electrolyser is a measure of the enthalpy contained in the hydrogen (to undergo combustion with oxygen, or some other later reaction), compared with the input electrical energy. Heat/enthalpy values for hydrogen are well published in science and engineering texts, as 144 MJ/kg.  Note that fuel cells (not electrolysers) cannot use this full amount of heat/enthalpy, which has led to some confusion when calculating efficiency values for both types of technology.  In the reaction, some energy is lost as heat. Some reports quote efficiencies between 50% and 70% for alkaline electrolysers; however, much higher practical efficiencies are available with the use of PEM (<a href="/wiki/Polymer_electrolyte_membrane_electrolysis" title="Polymer electrolyte membrane electrolysis">Polymer Electrolyte Membrane electrolysis</a>) and catalytic technology, such as 95% efficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carmo2013a_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-carmo2013a-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NREL" title="NREL">NREL</a> estimated that 1 kg of hydrogen (roughly equivalent to 3 kg, or 4 L, of petroleum in energy terms) could be produced by wind powered electrolysis for between $5.55 in the near term and $2.27 in the long term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NRELElectrolysis_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-NRELElectrolysis-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>About 4% of hydrogen gas produced worldwide is generated by electrolysis, and normally used onsite. Hydrogen is used for the creation of ammonia for fertilizer via the <a href="/wiki/Haber_process" title="Haber process">Haber process</a>, and converting heavy petroleum sources to lighter fractions via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrocracking" title="Hydrocracking">hydrocracking</a>.
</p>
<h3><span id="Carbon.2Fhydrocarbon_assisted_water_electrolysis"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon/hydrocarbon_assisted_water_electrolysis">Carbon/hydrocarbon assisted water electrolysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Carbon/hydrocarbon assisted water electrolysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_production" title="Hydrogen production">Hydrogen production</a></div>
<p>Recently, to reduce the energy input, the utilization of carbon (<a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>), <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohols</a> (hydrocarbon solution), and organic solution (<a href="/wiki/Glycerol" title="Glycerol">glycerol</a>, formic acid, <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">ethylene glycol</a>, etc.) with co-electrolysis of water has been proposed as a viable option.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The carbon/hydrocarbon assisted water electrolysis (so-called CAWE) process for hydrogen generation would perform this operation in a single <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrochemical" title="Electrochemical">electrochemical</a> reactor. This system energy balance can be required only around 40% electric input with 60% coming from the chemical energy of carbon or hydrocarbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> This process utilizes solid coal/carbon particles or powder as fuels dispersed in acid/alkaline electrolyte in the form of slurry and the carbon contained source co-assist in the electrolysis process as following theoretical overall reactions <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>:
</p><p>Carbon/Coal slurry (C + 2H<sub>2</sub>O) -&gt; CO<sub>2</sub> + 2H<sub>2</sub>      E' = 0.21 V (reversible voltage) / E' = 0.46 V (thermo-neutral voltage)
</p><p>or
</p><p>Carbon/Coal slurry (C + H<sub>2</sub>O) -&gt; CO + H<sub>2</sub>         E' = 0.52 V reversible voltage) / E' = 0.91 V (thermo-neutral voltage)
</p><p>Thus, this CAWE approach is that the actual cell overpotential can be significantly reduced to below 1 V as compared to 1.5 V for conventional water electrolysis.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrocrystallization">Electrocrystallization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Electrocrystallization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A specialized application of electrolysis involves the growth of conductive crystals on one of the electrodes from oxidized or reduced species that are generated in situ.  The technique has been used to obtain single crystals of low-dimensional electrical conductors, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charge-transfer_salt" title="Charge-transfer salt">charge-transfer salts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
