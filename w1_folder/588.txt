<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Cryogenics</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <b>cryogenics</b> is the production and behaviour of materials at very low <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperatures" title="Temperatures">temperatures</a>. A person who studies elements that have been subjected to extremely cold temperatures is called a <i>cryogenicist</i>.
</p><p>It is not well-defined at what point on the temperature scale <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">refrigeration</a> ends and cryogenics begins, but scientists assume a gas to be cryogenic if it can be liquefied at or below −150 <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">°C</a> (123 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a>; −238 <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">°F</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-saturn_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-saturn-1">[1]</a></sup> The U.S. <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">National Institute of Standards and Technology</a> has chosen to consider the field of cryogenics as that involving temperatures below −180 °C (93 K; −292 °F). This is a logical dividing line, since the normal <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">boiling points</a> of the so-called permanent <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gases</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neon" title="Neon">neon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, and normal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">air</a>) lie below −180 °C while the <a href="/wiki/Freon" title="Freon">Freon</a> refrigerants, <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a>, and other common refrigerants have boiling points above −180 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Discovery of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superconducting" title="Superconducting">superconducting</a> materials with critical temperatures significantly above the boiling point of liquid nitrogen has provided new interest in reliable, low cost methods of producing high temperature cryogenic refrigeration. The term "high temperature cryogenic" describes temperatures ranging from above the boiling point of liquid nitrogen, −195.79 °C (77.36 K; −320.42 °F), up to −50 °C (223 K; −58 °F), the generally defined upper limit of study referred to as cryogenics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cryogenicists use the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kelvin_scale" title="Kelvin scale">Kelvin</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rankine_scale" title="Rankine scale">Rankine</a> temperature scale, both of which measure from <a href="/wiki/Absolute_zero" title="Absolute zero">absolute zero</a>, rather than more usual scales such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celsius_scale" title="Celsius scale">Celsius</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fahrenheit_scale" title="Fahrenheit scale">Fahrenheit</a>, with their zeroes at arbitrary temperatures.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions_and_distinctions">Definitions and distinctions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cryogenics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions and distinctions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl><dt>Cryogenics</dt>
<dd>The branches of  engineering that involve the study of very low temperatures, how to produce them, and how materials behave at those temperatures.</dd></dl>
<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/Cryobiology" title="Cryobiology">Cryobiology</a></dt>
<dd>The branch of <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a> involving the study of the effects of low temperatures on <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a> (most often for the purpose of achieving <a href="/wiki/Cryopreservation" title="Cryopreservation">cryopreservation</a>).</dd></dl>
<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/Cryoconservation_of_animal_genetic_resources" title="Cryoconservation of animal genetic resources">Cryoconservation of animal genetic resources</a> </dt>
<dd>The conservation of genetic material with the intention of conserving a breed.</dd></dl>
<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/Cryosurgery" title="Cryosurgery">Cryosurgery</a></dt>
<dd>The branch of surgery applying cryogenic temperatures to destroy malignant tissue, e.g. cancer cells.</dd></dl>
<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/Cryoelectronics" title="Cryoelectronics">Cryoelectronics</a></dt>
<dd>The study of electronic phenomena at cryogenic temperatures. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Superconductivity" title="Superconductivity">superconductivity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Variable-range_hopping" title="Variable-range hopping">variable-range hopping</a>.</dd></dl>
<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/Cryotronics" title="Cryotronics">Cryotronics</a></dt>
<dd>The practical application of cryoelectronics.</dd></dl>
<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/Cryonics" title="Cryonics">Cryonics</a></dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Cryopreservation" title="Cryopreservation">Cryopreserving</a> humans and animals with the intention of future revival. "Cryogenics" is sometimes erroneously used to mean "Cryonics" in <a href="/wiki/Popular_culture" title="Popular culture">popular culture</a> and the press.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cryogenics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <i>cryogenics</i> stems from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>κρύο (cryo)</i> – "cold" + <i>γονική (genic)</i> – "having to do with production".
</p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_low-temperature_technology" title="Timeline of low-temperature technology">Timeline of low-temperature technology</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Liquefied_gas" title="Liquefied gas">Liquefied gases</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Liquid_nitrogen" title="Liquid nitrogen">liquid nitrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liquid_helium" title="Liquid helium">liquid helium</a>, are used in many cryogenic applications. Liquid nitrogen is the most commonly used element in cryogenics and is legally purchasable around the world. Liquid helium is also commonly used and allows for the lowest attainable temperatures to be reached.
</p><p>These liquids may be stored in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dewar_flask" title="Dewar flask">Dewar flasks</a>, which are double-walled containers with a high vacuum between the walls to reduce heat transfer into the liquid.  Typical laboratory Dewar flasks are spherical, made of glass and protected in a metal outer container.  Dewar flasks for extremely cold liquids such as liquid helium have another double-walled container filled with liquid nitrogen.  Dewar flasks are named after their inventor, <a href="/wiki/James_Dewar" title="James Dewar">James Dewar</a>, the man who first liquefied <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>.  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermos" title="Thermos">Thermos</a> bottles are smaller <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_flask" title="Vacuum flask">vacuum flasks</a> fitted in a protective casing.
</p><p>Cryogenic barcode labels are used to mark dewar flasks containing these liquids, and will not frost over down to −195 degrees Celsius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cryogenic transfer pumps are the pumps used on <a href="/wiki/LNG_pier" title="LNG pier">LNG piers</a> to transfer <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_natural_gas" title="Liquefied natural gas">liquefied natural gas</a> from <a href="/wiki/LNG_carrier" title="LNG carrier">LNG carriers</a> to <a href="/wiki/Storage_tank" title="Storage tank">LNG storage tanks</a>, as are cryogenic valves.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cryogenic_processing">Cryogenic processing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cryogenics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cryogenic processing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The field of cryogenics advanced during World War II when scientists found that metals frozen to low temperatures showed more resistance to wear.  Based on this theory of <a href="/wiki/Cryogenic_hardening" title="Cryogenic hardening">cryogenic hardening</a>, the commercial <a href="/wiki/Cryogenic_processor" title="Cryogenic processor">cryogenic processing</a> industry was founded in 1966 by Ed Busch. With a background in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_treatment" title="Heat treatment">heat treating</a> industry, Busch founded a company in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Detroit,_Michigan" title="Detroit, Michigan">Detroit</a> called CryoTech in 1966 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> which merged with <a class="external text" href="http://www.300below.com/" rel="nofollow">300 Below</a> in 1999 to become the world's largest and oldest commercial cryogenic processing company.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Busch originally experimented with the possibility of increasing the life of metal tools to anywhere between 200%-400% of the original life expectancy using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogenic_tempering" title="Cryogenic tempering">cryogenic tempering</a> instead of heat treating.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Materials would re-age at room temperature. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This evolved in the late 1990s into the treatment of other parts.
</p><p>Cryogens, such as liquid <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, are further used for specialty chilling and freezing applications.  Some chemical reactions, like those used to produce the active ingredients for the popular <a href="/wiki/Statin" title="Statin">statin</a> drugs, must occur at low temperatures of approximately −100 °C (−148 °F). Special cryogenic <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reactor" title="Chemical reactor">chemical reactors</a> are used to remove reaction heat and provide a low temperature environment.  The freezing of foods and biotechnology products, like <a href="/wiki/Vaccine" title="Vaccine">vaccines</a>, requires nitrogen in blast freezing or immersion freezing systems. Certain soft or elastic materials become hard and <a href="/wiki/Brittleness" title="Brittleness">brittle</a> at very low temperatures, which makes cryogenic <a href="/wiki/Mill_(grinding)" title="Mill (grinding)">milling</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryomilling" title="Cryomilling">cryomilling</a>) an option for some materials that cannot easily be milled at higher temperatures.
</p><p>Cryogenic processing is not a substitute for heat treatment, but rather an extension of the heating - quenching - tempering cycle. Normally, when an item is quenched, the final temperature is ambient. The only reason for this is that most heat treaters do not have cooling equipment. There is nothing metallurgically significant about ambient temperature. The cryogenic process continues this action from ambient temperature down to −320 °F (140 °R; 78 K; −196 °C).
In most instances the cryogenic cycle is followed by a heat tempering procedure. As all alloys do not have the same chemical constituents, the tempering procedure varies according to the material's chemical composition, thermal history and/or a tool's particular service application.
</p><p>The entire process takes 3–4 days.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fuels">Fuels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cryogenics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fuels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another use of cryogenics is <a href="/wiki/Cryogenic_fuel" title="Cryogenic fuel">cryogenic fuels</a> for rockets with <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">liquid hydrogen</a> as the most widely used example. <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">Liquid oxygen</a> (LOX) is even more widely used but as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidizer" title="Oxidizer">oxidizer</a>, not a fuel. <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>'s workhorse <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_shuttle" title="Space shuttle">space shuttle</a> used cryogenic hydrogen/oxygen propellant as its primary means of getting into <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a>. LOX is also widely used with <a href="/wiki/RP-1" title="RP-1">RP-1</a> kerosene, a non-cryogenic hydrocarbon, such as in the rockets built for the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_space_program" title="Soviet space program">Soviet space program</a> by <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Korolev" title="Sergei Korolev">Sergei Korolev</a>.
</p><p>Russian aircraft manufacturer <a href="/wiki/Tupolev" title="Tupolev">Tupolev</a> developed a version of its popular design <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tu-154" title="Tu-154">Tu-154</a> with a cryogenic fuel system, known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tu-155" title="Tu-155">Tu-155</a>. The plane uses a fuel referred to as  <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_natural_gas" title="Liquefied natural gas">liquefied natural gas</a> or LNG, and made its first flight in 1989.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_applications">Other applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cryogenics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_MUSE_instrument_on_the_VLT.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="869" data-file-width="1280" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/The_MUSE_instrument_on_the_VLT.jpg/220px-The_MUSE_instrument_on_the_VLT.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/The_MUSE_instrument_on_the_VLT.jpg/330px-The_MUSE_instrument_on_the_VLT.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/The_MUSE_instrument_on_the_VLT.jpg/440px-The_MUSE_instrument_on_the_VLT.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_MUSE_instrument_on_the_VLT.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Astronomical instruments on the <a href="/wiki/Very_Large_Telescope" title="Very Large Telescope">Very Large Telescope</a> are equipped with continuous flow cooling systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Some applications of cryogenics:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance" title="Nuclear magnetic resonance">Nuclear magnetic resonance</a> (NMR) is one of the most common methods to determine the physical and chemical properties of atoms by detecting the radio frequency absorbed and subsequent relaxation of nuclei in a magnetic field.  This is one of the most commonly used characterization techniques and has applications in numerous fields.  Primarily, the strong magnetic fields are generated by supercooling electromagnets, although there are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benchtop_NMR_spectrometer" title="Benchtop NMR spectrometer">spectrometers</a> that do not require cryogens.  In traditional superconducting solenoids, liquid helium is used to cool the inner coils because it has a boiling point of around 4 K at ambient pressure.  Cheap metallic superconductors can be used for the coil wiring.  So-called high-temperature superconducting compounds can be made to super conduct with the use of liquid nitrogen which boils at around 77 K.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">Magnetic resonance imaging</a> (MRI) is a complex application of NMR where the geometry of the resonances is deconvoluted and used to image objects by detecting the relaxation of protons that have been perturbed by a radio-frequency pulse in the strong magnetic field. This is mostly commonly used in health applications.</li>
<li>In large cities, <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">electric power transmission</a> is difficult to transmit power by overhead cables in big cities, so underground cables are used. But underground cables get heated and the resistance of the wire increases leading to waste of power.  Superconductors could be used to increase power throughput, although they would require cryogenic liquids such as nitrogen or helium to cool special alloy-containing cables to increase power transmission.  Several feasibility studies have been performed and the field is the subject of an agreement within the <a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">International Energy Agency</a>.</li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cryogenic_Gases_Delivery_Truck_Ypsilanti_Michigan.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1547" data-file-width="4747" height="81" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Cryogenic_Gases_Delivery_Truck_Ypsilanti_Michigan.JPG/250px-Cryogenic_Gases_Delivery_Truck_Ypsilanti_Michigan.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Cryogenic_Gases_Delivery_Truck_Ypsilanti_Michigan.JPG/375px-Cryogenic_Gases_Delivery_Truck_Ypsilanti_Michigan.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Cryogenic_Gases_Delivery_Truck_Ypsilanti_Michigan.JPG/500px-Cryogenic_Gases_Delivery_Truck_Ypsilanti_Michigan.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cryogenic_Gases_Delivery_Truck_Ypsilanti_Michigan.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cryogenic gases delivery truck at a supermarket, <a href="/wiki/Ypsilanti,_Michigan" title="Ypsilanti, Michigan">Ypsilanti, Michigan</a></div></div></div>
<ul><li>Cryogenic gases are used in transportation and storage of large masses of <a href="/wiki/Frozen_food" title="Frozen food">frozen food</a>. When very large quantities of food must be transported to regions like war zones, earthquake hit regions, etc., they must be stored for a long time, so cryogenic food freezing is used. Cryogenic food freezing is also helpful for large scale food processing industries.</li>
<li>Many infra-red (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forward_looking_infrared" title="Forward looking infrared">forward looking infrared</a>) cameras require their detectors to be cryogenically cooled.</li>
<li>Certain rare blood groups are stored at low temperatures, such as −165 °C at blood banks.</li>
<li>Cryogenics technology using <a href="/wiki/Liquid_nitrogen" title="Liquid nitrogen">liquid nitrogen</a> and CO<sub>2</sub> has been built into <a href="/wiki/Nightclub" title="Nightclub">nightclub</a> effect systems to create a chilling effect and white fog that can be illuminated with colored lights.</li>
<li>Cryogenic cooling is used to cool the tool tip at the time of machining in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manufacturing_process" title="Manufacturing process">manufacturing process</a>. It increases the tool life. Oxygen is used to perform several important functions in the steel manufacturing process.</li>
<li>Many <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rockets</a> use cryogenic gases as propellants. These include liquid oxygen, liquid hydrogen, and liquid methane.</li>
<li>By freezing the automobile or truck tire in liquid nitrogen, the rubber is made brittle and can be crushed into small particles. These particles can be used again for other items.</li>
<li>Experimental research on certain physics phenomena, such as spintronics and magnetotransport properties, requires cryogenic temperatures for the effects to be observed.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cryogenics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cryogenic cooling of devices and material is usually achieved via the use of <a href="/wiki/Liquid_nitrogen" title="Liquid nitrogen">liquid nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liquid_helium" title="Liquid helium">liquid helium</a>, or a <a href="/wiki/Cryocooler" title="Cryocooler">mechanical cryocooler</a> (which uses high pressure helium lines). Gifford-McMahon cryocoolers, <a href="/wiki/Pulse_tube_refrigerator" title="Pulse tube refrigerator">pulse tube cryocoolers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stirling_engine#Stirling_cryocoolers" title="Stirling engine">Stirling cryocoolers</a> are in wide use with selection based on required base temperature and cooling capacity. The most recent development in cryogenics is the use of magnets as regenerators as well as refrigerators. These devices work on the principle known as the <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_refrigeration" title="Magnetic refrigeration">magnetocaloric</a> effect.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Detectors">Detectors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cryogenics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Detectors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogenic_detectors" title="Cryogenic detectors">cryogenic detectors</a> which are used to detect cryogenic particles.
</p><p>For cryogenic temperature measurement down to 30K, Pt100 sensors, a <a href="/wiki/Resistance_thermometer" title="Resistance thermometer">resistance temperature detector (RTD)</a>, are used. For temperatures lower than 30K it is necessary to use a <a href="/wiki/Silicon_bandgap_temperature_sensor" title="Silicon bandgap temperature sensor">silicon diode</a> for accuracy.
</p>
