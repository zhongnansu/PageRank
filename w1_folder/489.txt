<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Mole fraction</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>In <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, the <b>mole fraction</b> or <b>molar fraction</b> (<i><b>x<sub>i</sub></b></i>) is defined as the <a href="/wiki/Amount_of_substance" title="Amount of substance">amount</a> of a constituent (expressed in <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">moles</a>), <i>n<sub>i</sub></i>, divided by the total amount of all constituents in a mixture (also expressed in moles), <i>n</i><sub>tot</sub>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldbook_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-goldbook-1">[1]</a></sup>

<p>The same concept expressed with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denominator" title="Denominator">denominator</a> of 100 is the <b>mole percent</b> or <b>molar percentage</b> or <b>molar proportion</b> (<b>mol%</b>).
</p><p>The mole fraction is also called the <b>amount fraction</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldbook_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-goldbook-1">[1]</a></sup> It is identical to the <b>number fraction</b>, which is defined as the number of <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> of a constituent <i>N<sub>i</sub></i> divided by the total number of all molecules <i>N</i><sub>tot</sub>. The mole fraction is sometimes denoted by the lowercase <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">Greek</a> letter <i>χ</i> (<a href="/wiki/Chi_(letter)" title="Chi (letter)">chi</a>) instead of a <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Roman</a> <i>x</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> For mixtures of gases, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAC" title="IUPAC">IUPAC</a> recommends the letter <i>y</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldbook_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-goldbook-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">National Institute of Standards and Technology</a> of the United States prefers the term <b>amount-of-substance fraction</b> over mole fraction because it does not contain the name of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unit_(measurement)" title="Unit (measurement)">unit</a> mole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Whereas mole fraction is a ratio of moles to moles, <a href="/wiki/Molar_concentration" title="Molar concentration">molar concentration</a> is a quotient of moles to volume.
</p><p>The mole fraction is one way of expressing the composition of a mixture with a <a href="/wiki/Dimensionless_quantity" title="Dimensionless quantity">dimensionless quantity</a>; <a href="/wiki/Mass_fraction_(chemistry)" title="Mass fraction (chemistry)">mass fraction</a> (percentage by weight, wt%) and <a href="/wiki/Volume_fraction" title="Volume fraction">volume fraction</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volume_percent" title="Volume percent">percentage by volume</a>, vol%) are others.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mole_fraction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mole fraction is used very frequently in the construction of <a href="/wiki/Phase_diagram" title="Phase diagram">phase diagrams</a>. It has a number of advantages:
</p>
<ul><li>it is not temperature dependent (such as <a href="/wiki/Molar_concentration" title="Molar concentration">molar concentration</a>) and does not require knowledge of the densities of the phase(s) involved</li>
<li>a mixture of known mole fraction can be prepared by weighing off the appropriate masses of the constituents</li>
<li>the measure is <i>symmetric</i>: in the mole fractions <i>x</i> = 0.1 and <i>x</i> = 0.9, the roles of 'solvent' and 'solute' are reversed.</li>
<li>In a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas" title="Ideal gas">ideal gases</a>, the mole fraction can be expressed as the ratio of <a href="/wiki/Partial_pressure" title="Partial pressure">partial pressure</a> to total <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> of the mixture</li>
<li>In a ternary mixture one can express mole fractions of a component as functions of other components mole fraction and binary mole ratios:</li></ul>
