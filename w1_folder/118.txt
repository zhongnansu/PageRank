<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Radiative forcing</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Earth%E2%80%99s_Energy_Budget_Incoming_Solar_Radiation_NASA.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="413" data-file-width="720" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Earth%E2%80%99s_Energy_Budget_Incoming_Solar_Radiation_NASA.jpg/300px-Earth%E2%80%99s_Energy_Budget_Incoming_Solar_Radiation_NASA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Earth%E2%80%99s_Energy_Budget_Incoming_Solar_Radiation_NASA.jpg/450px-Earth%E2%80%99s_Energy_Budget_Incoming_Solar_Radiation_NASA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Earth%E2%80%99s_Energy_Budget_Incoming_Solar_Radiation_NASA.jpg/600px-Earth%E2%80%99s_Energy_Budget_Incoming_Solar_Radiation_NASA.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Earth%E2%80%99s_Energy_Budget_Incoming_Solar_Radiation_NASA.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Incoming solar radiation</div></div></div>
<p><b>Radiative forcing</b> or <b>climate forcing</b> is the difference between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation">insolation</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a>) absorbed by the Earth and energy radiated back to space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The influences that cause changes to the Earth’s climate system altering Earth’s radiative equilibrium, forcing temperatures to rise or fall, are called climate forcings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Positive radiative forcing means Earth receives more incoming energy from sunlight than it radiates to space. This net gain of energy will cause warming. Conversely, negative radiative forcing means that Earth loses more energy to space than it receives from the sun, which produces cooling.
</p><p>Typically, radiative forcing is quantified at the <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a> or at the top of the atmosphere (often accounting for rapid adjustments in temperature) in units of <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">watts</a> per square <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meter" title="Meter">meter</a> of the Earth's surface. Positive forcing (incoming energy exceeding outgoing energy) warms the system, while negative forcing (outgoing energy exceeding incoming energy) cools it. Causes of radiative forcing include changes in <a href="/wiki/Solar_irradiance" title="Solar irradiance">insolation</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concentrations" title="Concentrations">concentrations</a> of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">radiatively active gases</a>, commonly known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gases</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosols</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Radiation_balance">Radiation balance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiative_forcing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Radiation balance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CO2_H2O_absorption_atmospheric_gases_unique_pattern_energy_wavelengths_of_energy_transparent_to_others.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="107" data-file-width="720" height="74" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/CO2_H2O_absorption_atmospheric_gases_unique_pattern_energy_wavelengths_of_energy_transparent_to_others.png/500px-CO2_H2O_absorption_atmospheric_gases_unique_pattern_energy_wavelengths_of_energy_transparent_to_others.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/57/CO2_H2O_absorption_atmospheric_gases_unique_pattern_energy_wavelengths_of_energy_transparent_to_others.png 1.5x" width="500"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CO2_H2O_absorption_atmospheric_gases_unique_pattern_energy_wavelengths_of_energy_transparent_to_others.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Atmospheric gases only absorb some wavelengths of energy but are transparent to others. The absorption patterns of water vapor (blue peaks) and carbon dioxide (pink peaks) overlap in some wavelengths. Carbon dioxide is not as strong a greenhouse gas as water vapor, but it absorbs energy in wavelengths (12-15 micrometers) that water vapor does not, partially closing the “window” through which heat radiated by the surface would normally escape to space. (Illustration NASA, Robert Rohde)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_climate_forcings_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_climate_forcings-3">[3]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiative_balance" title="Radiative balance">Radiative balance</a></div>
<p>Almost all of the energy that affects <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_climate" title="Earth's climate">Earth's climate</a> is received as radiant energy from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>. The planet and its atmosphere absorb and reflect some of the energy, while <a href="/wiki/Outgoing_longwave_radiation" title="Outgoing longwave radiation">long-wave energy is radiated back</a> into space. The balance between absorbed and radiated energy determines the average global temperature. Because the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> absorbs some of the re-radiated long-wave energy, the <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a> is warmer than it would be in the absence of the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>: see <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>.
</p><p>The radiation balance is altered by such factors as the intensity of <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar energy</a>, reflectivity of clouds or gases, absorption by various <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> or surfaces and heat emission by various materials. Any such alteration is a radiative forcing, and changes the balance. This happens continuously as sunlight hits the surface, clouds and aerosols form, the concentrations of atmospheric gases vary and seasons alter the <a href="/wiki/Groundcover" title="Groundcover">groundcover</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="IPCC_usage">IPCC usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiative_forcing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: IPCC usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Radiative-forcings.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="600" height="240" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Radiative-forcings.svg/300px-Radiative-forcings.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Radiative-forcings.svg/450px-Radiative-forcings.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Radiative-forcings.svg/600px-Radiative-forcings.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Radiative-forcings.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Radiative forcings, IPCC 2007.</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPCC_AR4" title="IPCC AR4">AR4</a> report defines radiative forcings as:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"Radiative forcing is a measure of the influence a factor has in altering the balance of incoming and outgoing energy in the Earth-atmosphere system and is an index of the importance of the factor as a potential climate change mechanism. In this report radiative forcing values are for changes relative to preindustrial conditions defined at 1750 and are expressed in Watts per square meter (W/m<sup>2</sup>)."
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>In simple terms, radiative forcing is "...the rate of energy change per unit area of the globe as measured at the top of the atmosphere."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rockstrom09_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rockstrom09-5">[5]</a></sup> In the context of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, the term "forcing" is restricted to changes in the radiation balance of the surface-troposphere system imposed by external factors, with no changes in stratospheric dynamics, no surface and tropospheric feedbacks in operation (<i>i.e.</i>, no secondary effects induced because of changes in tropospheric motions or its <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_state" title="Thermodynamic state">thermodynamic state</a>), and no dynamically induced changes in the amount and distribution of atmospheric water (vapour, liquid, and solid forms).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_sensitivity">Climate sensitivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiative_forcing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Climate sensitivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Climate_sensitivity" title="Climate sensitivity">Climate sensitivity</a></div>

<p>where λ is commonly denoted the climate sensitivity parameter, usually with units K/(W/m<sup>2</sup>), and Δ<i>F</i> is the radiative forcing in W/m<sup>2</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> A typical value of λ, 0.8 K/(W/m<sup>2</sup>), gives an increase in global temperature of about 1.6 K above the 1750 reference temperature due to the increase in CO<sub>2</sub> over that time (278 to 405 ppm, for a forcing of 2.0 W/m<sup>2</sup>), and predicts a further warming of 1.4 K above present temperatures if the CO<sub>2</sub> mixing ratio in the atmosphere were to become double its pre-industrial value; both of these calculations assume no other forcings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_forcing">Solar forcing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiative_forcing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Solar forcing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Radiative forcing (measured in watts per square meter) can be estimated in different ways for different components. For solar irradiance (<i>i.e.,</i> "solar forcing"), the radiative forcing is simply the change in the average amount of solar energy absorbed per square meter of the Earth's area. Since the Earth's cross-sectional area exposed to the Sun (πr<sup>2</sup>) is equal to 1/4 of the surface area of the Earth (4πr<sup>2</sup>), the solar input per unit area is one quarter the change in solar intensity. This must be multiplied by the fraction of incident sunlight that is absorbed, F=(1-R), where R is the reflectivity (<a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a>), of the Earth. The albedo is approximately 0.3, so F is approximately equal to 0.7. Thus, the solar forcing is the change in the solar intensity divided by 4 and multiplied by 0.7.
</p><p>Likewise, a change in albedo will produce a solar forcing equal to the change in albedo divided by 4 multiplied by the <a href="/wiki/Solar_constant" title="Solar constant">solar constant</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forcing_due_to_atmospheric_gas">Forcing due to atmospheric gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiative_forcing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Forcing due to atmospheric gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

These calculations might be simplified into an algebraic formulation that is specific to that gas.

While recently a <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> suggests a significant revision of methane IPCC formula.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_measures">Related measures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiative_forcing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Related measures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Radiative forcing is a useful way to compare different causes of perturbations in a climate system. Other possible tools can be constructed for the same purpose: for example Shine <i>et al.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> say "...recent experiments indicate that for changes in absorbing aerosols and ozone, the predictive ability of radiative forcing is much worse... we propose an alternative, the 'adjusted troposphere and stratosphere forcing'. We present <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_circulation_model" title="Global circulation model">GCM</a> calculations showing that it is a significantly more reliable predictor of this GCM's surface temperature change than radiative forcing. It is a candidate to supplement radiative forcing as a metric for comparing different mechanisms...". In this quote, GCM stands for "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_circulation_model" title="Global circulation model">global circulation model</a>", and the word "predictive" does not refer to the ability of GCMs to forecast climate change. Instead, it refers to the ability of the alternative tool proposed by the authors to help explain the system response.
</p><p>Therefore the concept of radiative forcing has been evolving from the initial proposal, named nowadays <i>instantaneous radiative forcing</i> (IRF), to other proposals that aims to relate better the radiative imbalance with global warming (global surface mean temperature). In this sense the adjusted radiative forcing, in its different calculation methodologies, estimates the imbalance once the stratosphere temperatures has been modified to achieve a radiative equilibrium in the stratosphere (in the sense of zero radiative heating rates). This new methodology is not estimating any <i>adjustment</i> or <i>feedback</i> that could be produced on the troposphere (in addition to stratospheric temperature adjustments), for that goal another definition, named <i>effective radiative forcing</i> has been introduced <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>. In general the ERF is the recommendation of the CMIP6 radiative forcing analysis <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> althougth the stratospherically adjusted methodologies are still being applied in those cases where the adjustments and feedbacks on the troposphere are considered not critical, like in the well mixed greenhouse gases and ozone <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>. Recently a new methodology named <i>radiative kernel approach</i> allows to estimate the climate feedbacks within an offline calculation based on a linear approximation <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The table shows that CO<sub>2</sub> dominates the total forcing, with <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbons" title="Chlorofluorocarbons">chlorofluorocarbons</a> (CFC) becoming relatively smaller contributors to the total forcing over time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_aggi_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-noaa_aggi-19">[19]</a></sup> The five major greenhouse gases account for about 96% of the direct radiative forcing by long-lived greenhouse gas increases since 1750. The remaining 4% is contributed by the 15 minor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halogenated" title="Halogenated">halogenated</a> gases.
</p><p>It might be observed that the total forcing for year 2016, 3.027 W m<sup>−2</sup>, together with the commonly accepted value of climate sensitivity parameter λ, 0.8 K /(W m<sup>−2</sup>), results in an increase in global temperature of 2.4 K, much greater than the observed increase, about 1.2 K.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Part of this difference is due to lag in the global temperature achieving steady state with the forcing. The remainder of the difference is due to negative aerosol forcing<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint noexcerpt Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="per WP:CIRCULAR (February 2018)">better source needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and/or climate sensitivity being less than the commonly accepted value, or some combination thereof.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>The table also includes an "Annual Greenhouse Gas Index" (AGGI), which is defined as the ratio of the total direct radiative forcing due to long-lived greenhouse gases for any year for which adequate global measurements exist to that which was present in 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa_aggi_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-noaa_aggi-19">[19]</a></sup> 1990 was chosen because it is the baseline year for the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a>. This index is a measure of the inter-annual changes in conditions that affect carbon dioxide emission and uptake, methane and nitrous oxide sources and sinks, the decline in the atmospheric abundance of <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">ozone-depleting</a> chemicals related to the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a>. and the increase in their substitutes (hydrogenated CFCs (HCFCs) and hydrofluorocarbons (HFC). Most of this increase is related to CO<sub>2</sub>. For 2013, the AGGI was 1.34 (representing an increase in total direct radiative forcing of 34% since 1990). The increase in CO<sub>2</sub> forcing alone since 1990 was about 46%. The decline in CFCs considerably tempered the increase in net radiative forcing.
</p><p>An alternative table prepared for use in climate model intercomparisons conducted under the auspices of IPCC and including all forcings, not just those of greenhouse gases, is available at <a class="external free" href="http://www.climatechange2013.org/images/report/WG1AR5_AIISM_Datafiles.xlsx" rel="nofollow">http://www.climatechange2013.org/images/report/WG1AR5_AIISM_Datafiles.xlsx</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
