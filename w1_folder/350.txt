<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Pounds per square inch</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>pound per square inch</b> or, more accurately, <b>pound-force per square inch</b> (symbol: <b>lbf/in<sup>2</sup></b>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> abbreviation: <b>psi</b>) is a unit of <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> or of <a href="/wiki/Stress_(mechanics)" title="Stress (mechanics)">stress</a> based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avoirdupois" title="Avoirdupois">avoirdupois</a> units. It is the pressure resulting from a force of one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pound-force" title="Pound-force">pound-force</a> applied to an area of one <a href="/wiki/Square_inch" title="Square inch">square inch</a>. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_units" title="SI units">SI units</a>, 1 psi is approximately equal to 6895 <a href="/wiki/Newton_(unit)" title="Newton (unit)">N</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Square_meter" title="Square meter">m<sup>2</sup></a>.
</p><p><b>Pounds per square inch absolute</b> (<b>psia</b>) is used to make it clear that the pressure is relative to a vacuum rather than the ambient atmospheric pressure. Since atmospheric pressure at sea level is around 14.7 psi, this will be added to any pressure reading made in air at sea level. The converse is <b>pounds per square inch gauge</b> (<b>psig</b>), indicating that the pressure is relative to atmospheric pressure. For example, a bicycle tire pumped up to 65 psig in a local atmospheric pressure at sea level (14.7 psia) will have a pressure of 79.7 psia (14.7 psi + 65 psi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> When gauge pressure is referenced to something other than ambient atmospheric pressure, then the units would be <b>pounds per square inch differential (psid)</b>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Multiples">Multiples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pounds_per_square_inch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Multiples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <b>kilopound per square inch</b> (ksi) is a scaled unit derived from psi, equivalent to a thousand psi (1000 lbf/in<sup>2</sup>).
</p><p>ksi are not widely used for gas pressures. They are mostly used in <a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">materials science</a>, where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tensile_strength" title="Tensile strength">tensile strength</a> of a material is measured as a large number of psi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The conversion in SI Units is 1 ksi = 6.895 MPa, or 1 MPa = 0.145 ksi.
</p><p>The <b>megapound per square inch</b> (Mpsi) is another multiple equal to a million psi. It is used in <a href="/wiki/Mechanics" title="Mechanics">mechanics</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Elastic_modulus" title="Elastic modulus">elastic modulus</a> of materials, especially for metals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The conversion in SI Units is 1 Mpsi = 6.895 GPa, or 1 GPa = 0.145 Mpsi.
</p>
