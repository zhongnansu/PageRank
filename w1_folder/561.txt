<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Chain reaction</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about chain reactions in chemistry and physics. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Chain_reaction_(disambiguation)" title="Chain reaction (disambiguation)">Chain reaction (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>A <b>chain reaction</b> is a sequence of reactions where a reactive product or by-product causes additional reactions to take place. In a chain reaction, <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedback</a> leads to a self-amplifying <a href="/wiki/Chain_of_events" title="Chain of events">chain of events</a>.
</p><p>Chain reactions are one way that systems which are not in <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium" title="Thermodynamic equilibrium">thermodynamic equilibrium</a> can release energy or increase entropy in order to reach a state of higher entropy. For example, a system may not be able to reach a lower energy state by releasing energy into the environment, because it is hindered or prevented in some way from taking the path that will result in the energy release. If a reaction results in a small energy release making way for more energy releases in an expanding chain, then the system will typically collapse explosively until much or all of the stored energy has been released. 
</p><p>A macroscopic metaphor for chain reactions is thus a snowball causing a larger snowball until finally an avalanche results ("<a href="/wiki/Snowball_effect" title="Snowball effect">snowball effect</a>"). This is a result of stored gravitational potential energy seeking a path of release over friction. Chemically, the equivalent to a snow avalanche is a spark causing a forest fire. In nuclear physics, a single stray neutron can result in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prompt_critical" title="Prompt critical">prompt critical</a> event, which may finally be energetic enough for a nuclear reactor meltdown or (in a bomb) a nuclear explosion.
</p><p>Numerous chain reactions can be represented by a <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_model" title="Mathematical model">mathematical model</a> based on <a href="/wiki/Markov_chain" title="Markov chain">Markov chains</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_chain_reactions">Chemical chain reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chain_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Chemical chain reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chain_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1913, the German chemist <a href="/wiki/Max_Bodenstein" title="Max Bodenstein">Max Bodenstein</a> first put forth the idea of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical" title="Chemical">chemical</a> chain reactions. If two molecules react, not only molecules of the final reaction products are formed, but also some unstable molecules which can further react with the parent molecules with a far larger probability than the initial reactants (In the new reaction, further unstable molecules are formed besides the stable products, and so on ..).
</p><p>In 1918, <a href="/wiki/Walther_Nernst" title="Walther Nernst">Walther Nernst</a> proposed that the <a href="/wiki/Photochemistry" title="Photochemistry">photochemical</a> reaction between <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> is a chain reaction in order to explain what's known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Quantum_yield" title="Quantum yield">quantum yield</a></i> phenomena. This means that one <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a> of light is responsible for the formation of as many as 10<sup>6</sup> molecules of the product <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HCl" title="HCl">HCl</a>. Nernst suggested that the photon dissociates a Cl<sub>2</sub> molecule into two Cl atoms which each initiate a long chain of reaction steps forming HCl.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laidler288_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Laidler288-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1923, Danish and Dutch scientists <a href="/wiki/Christian_Christiansen" title="Christian Christiansen">Christian Christiansen</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hendrik_Anthony_Kramers" title="Hendrik Anthony Kramers">Hendrik Anthony Kramers</a>, in an analysis of the formation of polymers, pointed out that such a chain reaction need not start with a molecule excited by light, but could also start with two molecules colliding violently due to thermal energy as previously proposed for initiation of chemical reactions by <a href="/wiki/Jacobus_Henricus_van_%27t_Hoff" title="Jacobus Henricus van 't Hoff">van' t Hoff</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nobel1956_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nobel1956-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Christiansen and Kramers also noted that if, in one link of the reaction chain, two or more unstable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecules" title="Molecules">molecules</a> are produced, the reaction chain would branch and grow. The result is in fact an exponential growth, thus giving rise to explosive increases in reaction rates, and indeed to chemical explosions themselves. This was the first proposal for the mechanism of chemical explosions.
</p><p>A quantitative chain chemical reaction theory was created later on by Soviet physicist <a href="/wiki/Nikolay_Semyonov" title="Nikolay Semyonov">Nikolay Semyonov</a> in 1934. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Semyonov shared the Nobel Prize in 1956 with Sir <a href="/wiki/Cyril_Norman_Hinshelwood" title="Cyril Norman Hinshelwood">Cyril Norman Hinshelwood</a>, who independently developed many of the same quantitative concepts. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nobel1956_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nobel1956-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Typical_steps">Typical steps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chain_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Typical steps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The main types of steps in chain reaction are of the following types.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laidler288_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Laidler288-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chain_initiation" title="Chain initiation">Initiation</a> (formation of <a href="/wiki/Active_center_(polymer_science)" title="Active center (polymer science)">active particles</a> or chain carriers, often <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_radical" title="Free radical">free radicals</a>, in either a thermal or a photochemical step)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chain_propagation" title="Chain propagation">Propagation</a> (may comprise several elementary steps in a cycle, where the active particle through reaction forms another active particle which continues the reaction chain by entering the next elementary step). In effect the active particle serves as a catalyst for the overall reaction of the propagation cycle. Particular cases are:</li></ul>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>* chain branching (a propagation step which forms more new active particles than enter the step);</dd>
<dd>* <a href="/wiki/Chain_transfer" title="Chain transfer">chain transfer</a> (a propagation step in which the active particle is a growing <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a> chain which reacts to form an inactive polymer whose growth is terminated and an active small particle (such as a radical), which may then react to form a new polymer chain).</dd></dl></dd></dl>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Chain_termination" title="Chain termination">Termination</a> (elementary step in which the active particle loses its activity; e. g. by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recombination_(chemistry)" title="Recombination (chemistry)">recombination</a> of two free radicals).</li></ul>
<p>The <i>chain length</i> is defined as the average number of times the propagation cycle is repeated, and equals the overall reaction rate divided by the initiation rate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laidler288_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Laidler288-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some chain reactions have complex <a href="/wiki/Rate_equation" title="Rate equation">rate equations</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Order_of_reaction#Fractional_order" title="Order of reaction">fractional order</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Order_of_reaction#Mixed_order" title="Order of reaction">mixed order</a> kinetics.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Detailed_example:_the_hydrogen-bromine_reaction">Detailed example: the hydrogen-bromine reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chain_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Detailed example: the hydrogen-bromine reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The reaction H<sub>2</sub> + Br<sub>2</sub> → 2 HBr proceeds by the following mechanism:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laidler291_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Laidler291-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atkins_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Atkins-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Initiation</li></ul>
<dl><dd>Br<sub>2</sub> → 2 Br• (thermal) or Br<sub>2</sub> + hν → 2 Br• (photochemical)</dd>
<dd>each Br atom is a free radical, indicated by the symbol « • » representing an unpaired electron.</dd></dl>
<ul><li>Propagation (here a cycle of two steps)</li></ul>
<dl><dd>Br• + H<sub>2</sub> → HBr + H•</dd>
<dd>H• + Br<sub>2</sub> → HBr + Br•</dd>
<dd>the sum of these two steps corresponds to the overall reaction H<sub>2</sub> + Br<sub>2</sub> → 2 HBr, with <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalysis</a> by Br• which participates in the first step and is regenerated in the second step.</dd></dl>
<ul><li>Retardation (inhibition)</li></ul>
<dl><dd>H• + HBr → H<sub>2</sub> + Br•</dd>
<dd>this step is specific to this example, and corresponds to the first propagation step in reverse.</dd></dl>
<ul><li>Termination 2 Br• → Br<sub>2</sub></li></ul>
<dl><dd>recombination of two radicals, corresponding in this example to initiation in reverse.</dd></dl>
<p>As can be explained using the <a href="/wiki/Steady_state_(chemistry)" title="Steady state (chemistry)">steady-state approximation</a>, the thermal  reaction has an initial rate of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Order_of_reaction#Fractional_order" title="Order of reaction">fractional order</a> (3/2), and a complete rate equation with a two-term denominator (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Order_of_reaction#Mixed-order" title="Order of reaction">mixed-order kinetics</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laidler291_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Laidler291-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atkins_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Atkins-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_chemical_examples">Further chemical examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chain_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Further chemical examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>The reaction 2 H<sub>2</sub> + O<sub>2</sub> → 2 H<sub>2</sub>O provides an example of chain branching. The propagation is a sequence of two steps whose net effect is to replace an H atom by another H atom plus two OH radicals. This leads to an explosion under certain conditions of temperature and pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
<ul><li>H + O<sub>2</sub> → OH + O</li>
<li>O + H<sub>2</sub> → OH + H</li></ul></li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Chain-growth_polymerization" title="Chain-growth polymerization">chain-growth polymerization</a>, the propagation step corresponds to the elongation of the growing <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a> chain. Chain transfer corresponds to transfer of the activity from this growing chain, whose growth is terminated, to another molecule which may be a second growing polymer chain. For polymerization, the <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_chain_length" title="Kinetic chain length">kinetic chain length</a> defined above may differ from the <a href="/wiki/Degree_of_polymerization" title="Degree of polymerization">degree of polymerization</a> of the product macromolecule.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Polymerase_chain_reaction" title="Polymerase chain reaction">Polymerase chain reaction</a>, a technique used in <a href="/wiki/Molecular_biology" title="Molecular biology">molecular biology</a> to amplify (make many copies of) a piece of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> by <i><a href="/wiki/In_vitro" title="In vitro">in vitro</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymatic</a> <a href="/wiki/DNA_replication" title="DNA replication">replication</a> using a <a href="/wiki/DNA_polymerase" title="DNA polymerase">DNA polymerase</a>.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_chain_reactions">Nuclear chain reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chain_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nuclear chain reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_chain_reaction" title="Nuclear chain reaction">Nuclear chain reaction</a></div>
<p>A <i>nuclear</i> chain reaction was proposed by <a href="/wiki/Leo_Szilard" title="Leo Szilard">Leo Szilard</a> in 1933, shortly after the neutron was discovered, yet more than five years before <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">nuclear fission</a> was first discovered. Szilárd knew of <i>chemical</i> chain reactions, and he had been reading about an energy-producing <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reaction" title="Nuclear reaction">nuclear reaction</a> involving high-energy protons bombarding lithium, demonstrated by <a href="/wiki/John_Cockcroft" title="John Cockcroft">John Cockcroft</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Walton" title="Ernest Walton">Ernest Walton</a>, in 1932. Now, Szilárd proposed to use neutrons theoretically produced from certain nuclear reactions in lighter isotopes, to induce further reactions in light isotopes that produced more neutrons. This would in theory produce a chain reaction at the level of the nucleus. He did not envision fission as one of these neutron-producing reactions, since this reaction was not known at the time. Experiments he proposed using <a href="/wiki/Beryllium" title="Beryllium">beryllium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indium" title="Indium">indium</a> failed.
</p><p>Later, after fission was discovered in 1938, Szilárd immediately realized the possibility of using neutron-induced fission as the particular nuclear reaction necessary to create a chain-reaction, so long as fission also produced neutrons. In 1939, with Enrico Fermi, Szilárd proved this neutron-multiplying reaction in uranium. In this reaction, a <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutron</a> plus a fissionable <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a> causes a fission resulting in a larger number of neutrons than the single one that was consumed in the initial reaction. Thus was born the practical <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_chain_reaction" title="Nuclear chain reaction">nuclear chain reaction</a> by the mechanism of neutron-induced nuclear fission.
</p><p>Specifically, if one or more of the produced neutrons themselves interact with other fissionable nuclei, and these also undergo fission, then there is a possibility that the macroscopic overall fission reaction will not stop, but continue throughout the reaction material. This is then a self-propagating and thus self-sustaining chain reaction. This is the principle for <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactors</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_bomb" title="Atomic bomb">atomic bombs</a>.
</p><p>Demonstration of a self-sustaining nuclear chain reaction was accomplished by <a href="/wiki/Enrico_Fermi" title="Enrico Fermi">Enrico Fermi</a> and others, in the successful operation of <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Pile-1" title="Chicago Pile-1">Chicago Pile-1</a>, the first artificial nuclear reactor, in late 1942.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electron_avalanche_in_gases">Electron avalanche in gases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chain_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Electron avalanche in gases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Electron_avalanche" title="Electron avalanche">electron avalanche</a> happens between two unconnected electrodes in a gas when an electric field exceeds a certain threshold. Random thermal collisions of gas atoms may result in a few free electrons and positively charged gas ions, in a process called <a href="/wiki/Impact_ionization" title="Impact ionization">impact ionization</a>. Acceleration of these free electrons in a strong <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">electric field</a> causes them to gain energy, and when they impact other atoms, the energy causes release of new free electrons and ions (ionization), which fuels the same process. If this process happens faster than it is naturally quenched by ions recombining, the new ions multiply in successive cycles until the gas breaks down into a plasma and current flows freely in a discharge.
</p><p>Electron avalanches are essential to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dielectric_breakdown" title="Dielectric breakdown">dielectric breakdown</a> process within gases. The process can culminate in <a href="/wiki/Corona_discharge" title="Corona discharge">corona discharges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Streamer_discharge" title="Streamer discharge">streamers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leader_(spark)" title="Leader (spark)">leaders</a>, or in a <a href="/wiki/Electric_spark" title="Electric spark">spark</a> or continuous <a href="/wiki/Electric_arc" title="Electric arc">electric arc</a> that completely bridges the gap. The process may extend huge sparks — streamers in <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a> discharges propagate by formation of electron avalanches created in the high potential gradient ahead of the streamers' advancing tips. Once begun, avalanches are often intensified by the creation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photoelectron" title="Photoelectron">photoelectrons</a> as a result of ultraviolet radiation emitted by the excited medium's atoms in the aft-tip region. The extremely high temperature of the resulting plasma cracks the surrounding gas molecules and the free ions recombine to create new chemical compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The process can also be used to detect radiation that initiates the process, as the passage of a single particles can amplified to large discharges. This is the mechanism of a <a href="/wiki/Geiger_counter" title="Geiger counter">Geiger counter</a> and also the visualization possible with a <a href="/wiki/Spark_chamber" title="Spark chamber">spark chamber</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Wire_chamber" title="Wire chamber">wire chambers</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Avalanche_breakdown_in_semiconductors">Avalanche breakdown in semiconductors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chain_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Avalanche breakdown in semiconductors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Avalanche_breakdown" title="Avalanche breakdown">avalanche breakdown</a> process can happen in semiconductors, which in some ways conduct electricity analogously to a mildly ionized gas. Semiconductors rely on free electrons knocked out of the crystal by thermal vibration for conduction. Thus, unlike metals, semiconductors become better conductors the higher the temperature. This sets up conditions for the same type of positive feedback—heat from current flow causes temperature to rise, which increases charge carriers, lowering resistance, and causing more current to flow. This can continue to the point of complete breakdown of normal resistance at a semiconductor junction, and failure of the device (this may be temporary or permanent depending on whether there is physical damage to the crystal). Certain devices, such as <a href="/wiki/Avalanche_diode" title="Avalanche diode">avalanche diodes</a>, deliberately make use of the effect.
</p>
