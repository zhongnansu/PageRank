<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Nuclear power in the European Union</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Update" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="Ambox current red.svg" data-file-height="290" data-file-width="360" height="34" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/42px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/63px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/84px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png 2x" width="42"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This article needs to be <b>updated</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please update this article to reflect recent events or newly available information.</span> <small><i>(October 2018)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about nuclear power in the whole European Union. For nuclear energy policy in individual member states, see <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_energy_policy_by_country" title="Nuclear energy policy by country">Nuclear energy policy by country</a>.</div>
<p>
<b>Nuclear power in the European Union</b> accounted for approximately 15% of total energy consumption in 2005. The energy policies of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> (EU) member countries vary significantly. As of January 2010, 14 out of 27 countries have <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactors</a>. The countries with reactors are: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_Belgium" title="Nuclear power in Belgium">Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_Bulgaria" title="Nuclear power in Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_the_Czech_Republic" title="Nuclear power in the Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_Finland" title="Nuclear power in Finland">Finland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_France" title="Nuclear power in France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_Germany" title="Nuclear power in Germany">Germany</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_Hungary" title="Nuclear power in Hungary">Hungary</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_the_Netherlands" title="Nuclear power in the Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_Romania" title="Nuclear power in Romania">Romania</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_Slovakia" title="Nuclear power in Slovakia">Slovakia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_Slovenia" title="Nuclear power in Slovenia">Slovenia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_Spain" title="Nuclear power in Spain">Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_Sweden" title="Nuclear power in Sweden">Sweden</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Nuclear power in the United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uic1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-uic1-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stress_tests">Stress tests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_power_in_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Stress tests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Stress tests were developed within the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">EU</a> in the aftermath of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fukushima_nuclear_disaster" title="Fukushima nuclear disaster">Fukushima nuclear disaster</a>, with the goal of making all 132 operating European reactors follow the same safety standards and have the same safety level for a list of possible catastrophical events (e.g. earthquake, flooding or plane crash). Most reactors proved well during the tests, with just 4 reactors in 2 countries having less than one hour for reactivating safety systems; however, most reactors will also have to undergo a program of safety upgrades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In 2012, the costs of additional safety improvements were estimated to be in the range of €30 million to €200 million per reactor unit. Thus, the total costs for the 132 reactors operating in the EU could be in the order of €10–25 billion for all NPP units in the EU over the coming years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_mix">Energy mix</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_power_in_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Energy mix">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The EU-27 in 2005 satisfied its primary energy consumption with 36.7% oil, 24.6% gas, 17.7% coal, 14.2% nuclear, 6.7% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renewables" title="Renewables">renewables</a> and 0.1% industrial waste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Esteban_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Esteban-5">[5]</a></sup> In 2006, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear energy</a> provided the largest source (29.5%) of electricity with a production of 990 TWh, and an installed capacity of 134 <a href="/wiki/Watt#Gigawatt" title="Watt">GWe</a> (17.6% of all installed capacity). It was the leading electric power source in Belgium, France, Hungary, Lithuania and Slovakia. France, where nuclear is also the largest <i>primary</i> energy source, produced 450 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TWh" title="TWh">TWh</a> in 2006 – 45% of the EU's total. Denmark, Estonia, Ireland, Greece, Italy, Cyprus, Latvia, Luxembourg, Malta, Austria, Poland and Portugal did not produce any nuclear energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eurostat_new_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eurostat_new-6">[6]</a></sup> As of June 2013 there are 131 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_nuclear_power_plants_of_Europe_and_CIS" title="List of nuclear power plants of Europe and CIS">nuclear reactors in the European Union</a>. 112 units of these are located in eight of the western EU countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Total nuclear energy generation from EU power plants increased by 25% from 1995 to 2005. The majority of this growth occurred in the 1990s. Installed capacities decreased by 2.6% since 1990 and relative contribution to the overall electricity mix decreased from 30.8% in 1990 to 30.2% in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eurostat_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eurostat-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uranium_resources">Uranium resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_power_in_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Uranium resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2009_EU_uranium_sources.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="371" data-file-width="656" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/2009_EU_uranium_sources.png/300px-2009_EU_uranium_sources.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/2009_EU_uranium_sources.png/450px-2009_EU_uranium_sources.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/2009_EU_uranium_sources.png/600px-2009_EU_uranium_sources.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2009_EU_uranium_sources.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sources of uranium delivered to EU utilities in 2009, from the <a class="external text" href="http://ec.europa.eu/euratom/ar/last.pdf" rel="nofollow">2009 Annual report</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euratom" title="Euratom">Euratom</a> Supply Agency</div></div></div>
<p>In 2009, only 3% of the <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> used in European nuclear reactors was <a href="/wiki/Uranium_mining" title="Uranium mining">mined</a> in Europe. Australia, Russia, Canada and Niger were the largest suppliers of nuclear materials to the EU, supplying more than 70% of the total needs in 2009<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="EU-level_policy">EU-level policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_power_in_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: EU-level policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_power_in_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <p>European nuclear policy is governed by the <a href="/wiki/Euratom_Treaty" title="Euratom Treaty">Euratom Treaty</a>. Therefore, regular EU policy on for example <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Directorate-General_for_the_Environment_(European_Commission)" title="Directorate-General for the Environment (European Commission)">environment</a> or the market does not apply to issues in the nuclear field. Nuclear policy is mainly in the competence of the member states. In the EU level, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Directorate-General_for_Energy_(European_Commission)" title="Directorate-General for Energy (European Commission)">DG ENER</a> is the main authority for EU nuclear issues.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/European_Council" title="European Council">European Council</a> is the locus for intergovernmental decisions. The <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> does not have authority in the field of nuclear policy other than the right to ask questions to the European Commission.
</p><p>In case of a radiological emergency, the EU will trigger its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ECURIE" title="ECURIE">ECURIE</a> alert system, which immediately notifies all national authorities of an impending nuclear hazard. This system was installed after the experience with the <a href="/wiki/Chernobyl_disaster" title="Chernobyl disaster">Chernobyl disaster</a>.
</p><p>The Commission's <a href="/wiki/Energy_policy_of_the_European_Union#SET_Plan" title="Energy policy of the European Union">SET plan</a> mentions the "sustainable nuclear fission initiative" to develop <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generation_IV" title="Generation IV">Generation IV</a> reactors as one of the research priorities of the European Union.
</p><p>The European Commission is proposing a stress test for all nuclear power plants in Europe, to prove the nuclear fleet can withstand incidents like those in Fukushima.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The European Commission is also proposing tests for countries near the EU that make use of nuclear power.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_waste">Nuclear waste</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_power_in_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nuclear waste">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On average, the EU creates about 40,000 cubic meters of <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_waste" title="Radioactive waste">radioactive waste</a> per year. Eighty percent of that is short-lived <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low-level_radioactive_waste" title="Low-level radioactive waste">low-level radioactive waste</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Europa_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Europa-11">[11]</a></sup> France and the United Kingdom are currently the only EU countries that <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reprocessing" title="Nuclear reprocessing">reprocess waste</a>. However, the reprocessing of spent fuel in the UK is being phased out but is expected to continue in France. The countries that currently use this reprocessed fuel (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MOX" title="MOX">MOX</a>) include <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_Germany" title="Nuclear power in Germany">Germany</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_Belgium" title="Nuclear power in Belgium">Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_France" title="Nuclear power in France">France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_Switzerland" title="Nuclear power in Switzerland">Switzerland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cea_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-cea-12">[12]</a></sup> Reprocessing spent fuel significantly decreases its volume and extracts <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a> from it. Although plutonium is commonly associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons" title="Nuclear weapons">nuclear weapons</a>, the plutonium extracted with reprocessing is not suitable for 'classic' nuclear weapons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msnbc_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-msnbc-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/European_Bank_for_Reconstruction_and_Development" title="European Bank for Reconstruction and Development">EBRD</a> is financing the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_decommissioning" title="Nuclear decommissioning">decommissioning</a> of old nuclear plants in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_Bulgaria" title="Nuclear power in Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_Lithuania" title="Nuclear power in Lithuania">Lithuania</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_Slovakia" title="Nuclear power in Slovakia">Slovakia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>EU memberstates Austria, Ireland, Netherlands, Poland, Slovakia, Bulgaria, Italy, Lithuania, Romania, and Slovenia are working since January 2009 together in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=European_Repository_Development_Organisation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="European Repository Development Organisation (page does not exist)">European Repository Development Organisation</a> (ERDO) to address common issues on nuclear waste storage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>ERDO was working early 2010 on a plan to store European nuclear waste somewhere in eastern Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>"Some 7,000 cubic meters of high-level nuclear waste are produced across the EU each year. Most Member States store spent fuel and other highly radioactive wastes in above-ground storage facilities that need continuous maintenance and oversight and are at risk of accidents, such as airplane crashes, fires or earthquakes. Hungary and Bulgaria currently ship nuclear waste to Russia."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 19 July 2011, the European Commission adopted a Directive for regulating and handling nuclear waste in the EU.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-europa.eu_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-europa.eu-18">[18]</a></sup>
"Exports to countries outside the EU is allowed under very strict and binding conditions: The third country needs to have a final repository in operation, when the waste is being shipped. Such a repository for highly radioactive waste is internationally defined to be a deep geological repository. At present, such deep geological repositories do not exist anywhere in the world nor is a repository in construction outside of the EU. It takes currently a minimum of 40 years to develop and build one."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-europa.eu_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-europa.eu-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>The MAX project (2011 to August 2014), funded partly with an almost €3 million European Commission contribution,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> embodied working on transmuting the waste into less toxic shorter-lived elements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The final report from August 2014 is available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> It concludes that one milestone was not achieved.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_decommissioning">Nuclear decommissioning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_power_in_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nuclear decommissioning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By 2025, it is estimated that over a third of the EU's currently operational reactors will be at the end of their lifecycle and in need of shutdown. At the time of the accession to the EU, Bulgaria, Lithuania and Slovakia agreed to shut down reactors at the sites of Kozloduy, Ignalina and Bohunice respectively: these programs are currently under way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Other decommissioning activities are under way for older reactors, phased out for political reasons (e.g. Italy, Germany) or simply because they reached their end-of-life (e.g. United Kingdom).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2016, <a href="/wiki/Reuters" title="Reuters">Reuters</a> reported that the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> estimated that the EU's <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_decommissioning" title="Nuclear decommissioning">nuclear decommissioning</a> liabilities were seriously underfunded by about 118 billion euros, with only 150 billion euros of earmarked assets to cover 268 billion euros of expected decommissioning costs covering both dismantling of nuclear plants and storage of radioactive parts and waste. Among EU member states still operating nuclear plants, only Britain's operators have enough dedicated assets to cover the expected costs, 63 billion euros, the Commission's draft working paper finds. <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_France" title="Nuclear power in France">France</a> had the largest shortfall with only 23 billion euros of earmarked assets to cover 74 billion euros of expected costs, while a stress test carried out by Germany’s Economy Ministry late last year showed the provisions made by the country’s utilities – E.ON, RWE, EnBW and Vattenfall [VATN.UL] – were adequate. Decommissioning costs vary according to reactor type and size, location, the proximity and availability of disposal facilities, the intended future use of the site and the condition of the reactor at the time of decommissioning. Although decommissioning might gradually become cheaper, the cost of final waste depositories is largely unknown and costs could also grow, rather than shrink, over the many decades in question. The European Commission declined to comment on an unpublished document and has not confirmed when the report will be officially published.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters-20160216_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-reuters-20160216-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="European_nuclear_industry">European nuclear industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_power_in_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: European nuclear industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w.svg"><img alt="Wiki letter w.svg" data-file-height="44" data-file-width="44" height="44" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6c/Wiki_letter_w.svg/44px-Wiki_letter_w.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6c/Wiki_letter_w.svg/66px-Wiki_letter_w.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/6c/Wiki_letter_w.svg/88px-Wiki_letter_w.svg.png 2x" width="44"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This article <b>is missing information about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Areva" title="Areva">Areva</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siemens" title="Siemens">Siemens</a> struggling to make a profit in the nuclear industry</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please expand the article to include this information. Further details may exist on the <a href="/wiki/Talk:Nuclear_power_in_the_European_Union" title="Talk:Nuclear power in the European Union">talk page</a>.</span> <small><i>(January 2016)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Flamanville-3_2010-07-15.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="709" data-file-width="1222" height="128" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Flamanville-3_2010-07-15.jpg/220px-Flamanville-3_2010-07-15.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Flamanville-3_2010-07-15.jpg/330px-Flamanville-3_2010-07-15.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Flamanville-3_2010-07-15.jpg/440px-Flamanville-3_2010-07-15.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Flamanville-3_2010-07-15.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>EDF has said its third-generation EPR <a href="/wiki/Flamanville_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Flamanville Nuclear Power Plant">Flamanville</a> 3 project (seen here in 2010) will be delayed until 2019, due to "both structural and economic reasons," and the project's total cost has climbed to EUR 11 billion in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Advanced new reactors under construction in Finland and France, which were meant to lead a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_renaissance" title="Nuclear renaissance">nuclear renaissance</a>, have been delayed and are running over-budget.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jk_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-jk-26">[26]</a></sup> There has been a 15-year gap in building reactors, which has meant that there are difficulties in manufacturing the high quality parts required for a reactor. The new reactor also represents an advance over existing technologies, with better reliability and safety over <a href="/wiki/Generation_II_reactor" title="Generation II reactor">Generation II reactors</a>. Finally, they are "first-of-a-kind" industrial plants, having then all the kinds of problems and delays that should be avoided in following projects.
</p><p>The European nuclear industry is working to develop <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generation_IV" title="Generation IV">Generation IV</a> nuclear reactors. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foratom" title="Foratom">Foratom</a> is a Brussels-based trade organisation that bills itself as the "voice of the nuclear industry".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Along with companies and trade organisations like Foratom, <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hitachi" title="Hitachi">Hitachi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Toshiba" title="Toshiba">Toshiba</a> are all partners in the European Nuclear Industry. Other partners may include <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Electric_Power_Company" title="Tokyo Electric Power Company">TEPCO</a> from Japan and <a href="/wiki/Korea_Electric_Power_Corporation" title="Korea Electric Power Corporation">KEPCO</a> from South Korea. The nuclear industry is regulated by governments and financing is often provided to private contractors who do the work.
</p><p>Nuclear Safety is an ongoing discussion in the EU. The Western European Nuclear Regulators Association has members from 17 states or European countries. Nuclear safety faces many challenges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>  WENR addresses these challenges and commits itself to objective reporting.  An example of a report is the publication, "Stress Test" specifications, Proposal by the WENRA Task Force, 21 April 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_plans">Future plans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_power_in_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Future plans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Currently, eight European countries are building new reactors, or seriously planning to build new ones:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nuclear_Power_in_the_World_Today_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nuclear_Power_in_the_World_Today-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>France</li>
<li>Finland</li>
<li>Slovakia</li>
<li>United Kingdom</li>
<li>Poland</li>
<li>Hungary</li>
<li>Romania</li>
<li>Czech Republic</li></ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenian</a> plans to expand <a href="/wiki/Kr%C5%A1ko_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Krško Nuclear Power Plant">Krško plant</a> seem to have been dropped, instead a 20 years life extension is under evaluation. <a href="/wiki/EPR_(nuclear_reactor)" title="EPR (nuclear reactor)">EPR</a> new reactors under construction in Finland and France have been delayed and are running over-budget.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jk_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-jk-26">[26]</a></sup> Similar problems are for new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LWR" title="LWR">VVR</a> reactors under construction in <a href="/wiki/Mochovce_Nuclear_Power_Plant" title="Mochovce Nuclear Power Plant">Mochovce</a>, Slovakia, which are anyway closing to completion.
</p><p>Power companies are building nuclear reactors in Finland and France and the French state continues to fund nuclear power, with a €1 billion added to help research for fourth-generation technology and nuclear safety.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Several countries, among the ones owning nuclear power plants, have anyway expanded their nuclear power generation capacity by just upgrading existing reactors. Such upgrades granted from 10% to 29% more power per unit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plans_For_New_Reactors_Worldwide_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Plans_For_New_Reactors_Worldwide-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>Following the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fukushima_nuclear_disaster" title="Fukushima nuclear disaster">Fukushima nuclear disaster</a>, Germany has permanently shut down eight of its reactors and pledged to close the rest by 2022;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Annika_Breidthardt_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Annika_Breidthardt-33">[33]</a></sup> but difficulties, costs and subsequent critics of planned <a href="/wiki/Energy_transition" title="Energy transition">energy transition</a> could potentially harm this policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spiegel.de_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-spiegel.de-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paristechreview.com_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-paristechreview.com-35">[35]</a></sup> Italy voted twice, in <a href="/wiki/Italian_referendums,_1987" title="Italian referendums, 1987">1987 to make more difficult to build new plants</a> (the vote was extensively interpreted by following governments as a total repeal of nuclear power plants, leading to the sudden shut down of all Italian operating reactors within few years), and in 2011 to keep their country non-nuclear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Italy_Nuclear_Referendum_Results_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Italy_Nuclear_Referendum_Results-36">[36]</a></sup> Switzerland and Spain have banned the construction of new reactors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nweek11_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-nweek11-37">[37]</a></sup> Belgium is considering phasing out its nuclear plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nweek11_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-nweek11-37">[37]</a></sup> France, frequently heralded as a nuclear commercial model for the world, was as of 2011 locked in a national debate over a partial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_phase-out" title="Nuclear phase-out">nuclear phase-out</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nweek11_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-nweek11-37">[37]</a></sup> In the same time, however, Sweden embraced a nuclear phase-out policy as early as 1980, so preceding all these countries; but only the two oldest reactors, of twelve, were shut down at their end of life; while in 2010 Swedish Parliament repealed this policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nuclear_Power_in_Sweden_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nuclear_Power_in_Sweden-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Atom-Moratorium.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="750" data-file-width="555" height="297" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Atom-Moratorium.svg/220px-Atom-Moratorium.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Atom-Moratorium.svg/330px-Atom-Moratorium.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Atom-Moratorium.svg/440px-Atom-Moratorium.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Atom-Moratorium.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eight German nuclear power reactors (Biblis A and B, Brunsbuettel, Isar 1, Kruemmel, Neckarwestheim 1, Philippsburg 1 and Unterweser) were permanently shutdown on 6 August 2011, following the Japanese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fukushima_nuclear_disaster" title="Fukushima nuclear disaster">Fukushima nuclear disaster</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>As said, the 2011 Japanese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fukushima_nuclear_disaster" title="Fukushima nuclear disaster">Fukushima nuclear disaster</a> has led some European energy officials to re-think about nuclear power generation, above all in Germany and Switzerland. <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> has abandoned plans to replace its old nuclear reactors and will take the last one offline in 2034. <a href="/wiki/Anti-nuclear_movement_in_Germany" title="Anti-nuclear movement in Germany">Anti-nuclear opposition</a> intensified in Germany. In the following months <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cabinet_Merkel_II" title="Cabinet Merkel II">the government</a> decided to shut down eight reactors immediately (6 August 2011) and to have the other nine off the grid by the end of 2022. <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_in_Germany" title="Renewable energy in Germany">Renewable energy in Germany</a> is believed to be able to compensate for much of the loss. In September 2011 Siemens, which had been responsible for constructing all 17 of Germany's existing nuclear power plants, announced that it would exit the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_sector" title="Nuclear sector">nuclear sector</a> following the Fukushima disaster and the subsequent changes to German energy policy. Chief executive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peter_Loescher" title="Peter Loescher">Peter Loescher</a> has supported the German government's planned <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energiewende" title="Energiewende">Energiewende</a></i>, its transition to renewable energy technologies, calling it a "project of the century" and saying Berlin's target of reaching 35% renewable energy sources by 2020 was feasible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Despite this, the phasing out of nuclear energy seems to be much more difficult and costly than expected. The transition is tending more towards polluting fossil fuel plants than clean renewable energies, sparking several critics. Actually, transition plans did not meet their short-term goals and will hardly meet their medium-term goals; fossil fuel prices and technology are still more efficient, cheaper and easier to implement than heavily state-subsidised solar and wind power generation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spiegel.de_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-spiegel.de-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paristechreview.com_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-paristechreview.com-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
