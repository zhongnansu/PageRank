<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Rotating unbalance</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Rotating unbalance</b> is the uneven distribution of mass around an axis of rotation. A rotating mass, or <b>rotor</b>, is said to be out of balance when its <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">center of mass</a> (inertia axis) is out of alignment with the center of rotation (geometric axis). Unbalance causes a moment which gives the rotor a wobbling movement characteristic of <a href="/wiki/Vibration_of_rotating_structures" title="Vibration of rotating structures">vibration of rotating structures</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes_of_unbalance">Causes of unbalance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotating_unbalance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Causes of unbalance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl><dt>Distortion from stress</dt>
<dd>Routine manufacturing processes can cause stress on metal components. Without stress relief, the rotor will distort itself to adjust.</dd>
<dt>Thermal distortion</dt>
<dd>Thermal distortion often occurs with parts exposed to increased temperatures. Metals are able to expand when in contact with heat, so exposure to warmer temperatures can cause either the entire piece of machinery to expand, or just certain parts, causing distortion.</dd>
<dt>Buildups and deposits</dt>
<dd>Rotating parts involved in material handling almost always accumulate buildup. Moreover, when exposed to oil, these parts can be very easily distorted. Without adhering to a maintenance routine or implementing an inspection process, oil can seep into the parts, causing unbalance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></dd></dl>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_balance">Types of balance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotating_unbalance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Types of balance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Static_balance">Static balance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotating_unbalance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Static balance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <b>static balance</b> (sometimes called a <b>force balance</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Centrifugal_Pump_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Centrifugal_Pump-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TAC_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-TAC-3">[3]</a></sup>) occurs when the inertial axis of a rotating mass is displaced from and parallel to the axis of rotation. Static unbalances can occur more frequently in disk-shaped rotors because the thin geometric profile of the disk allows for an uneven distribution of mass with an inertial axis that is nearly parallel to the axis of rotation. Only one plane receives balance correction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Couple_balance">Couple balance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotating_unbalance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Couple balance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <b>couple balance</b> occurs when a rotating mass has two equal unbalance forces that are situated 180° opposite each other.  A system that is statically balanced may still have a couple unbalance.
Couple unbalance occurs frequently in elongated cylindrical rotors.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dynamic_balance">Dynamic balance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotating_unbalance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Dynamic balance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In rotation an unbalance when the mass/inertia axis does not intersect with shaft axis then it is called <b>dynamic balance</b>. Combination of static and couple balance is dynamic unbalance. It occurs virtually in all rotors and is the most common kind of unbalance. It can be fixed by correcting the weight on at least two planes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grades">Grades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotating_unbalance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Grades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">ISO</a> 21940<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> classifies vibration in terms of G codes. Unfortunately, it is the theoretical value assuming the rotor was spinning in free space so it does not relate to actual operating conditions. Rotors of the same type having permissible residual specific unbalance value e<sub>per</sub>, varies inversely with  the speed of the rotor.
</p>
<dl><dd>e<sub>per</sub> × ω = Constant,</dd></dl>
<p>where ω = angular velocity. e<sub>per</sub> = permissible residual specific unbalance
</p><p>This constant is quality grade G. Balance Grades are used to specify the allowable residual imbalance for rotating machinery. The ISO 1940 standard defines balance grades for different classes of machinery. A rotor balanced to G2.5 will vibrate at 2.5 mm/s at operating speed if rotating in a suspended state with no external influences.
</p>
<dl><dd>U<sub>per</sub> = (9.54 × G number × mass)/rpm</dd></dl>
<p>Where  U<sub>per</sub> = balance tolerance (or) residual imbalance
</p>
