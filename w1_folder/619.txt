<p><b>Energy conversion efficiency</b> (<i><b>η</b></i>) is the <a href="/wiki/Ratio" title="Ratio">ratio</a> between the useful output of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_conversion_machine" title="Energy conversion machine">energy conversion machine</a> and the input, in <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> terms. The input, as well as the useful output may be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical" title="Chemical">chemical</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">electric power</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanical_work" title="Mechanical work">mechanical work</a>, <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> (radiation), or <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_conversion_efficiency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Energy conversion efficiency depends on the usefulness of the output. All or part of the heat produced from burning a fuel may become rejected <a href="/wiki/Waste_heat" title="Waste heat">waste heat</a> if, for example, work is the desired output from a <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_cycle" title="Thermodynamic cycle">thermodynamic cycle</a>. Energy converter is an example of an energy transformation. For example a light bulb falls into the categories energy converter.

Even though the definition includes the notion of usefulness, <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/efficiency" title="wikt:efficiency">efficiency</a> is considered a <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technical</a> or <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physical</a> term. Goal or mission oriented terms include <a href="/wiki/Effectiveness" title="Effectiveness">effectiveness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Efficacy" title="Efficacy">efficacy</a>.
</p><p>Generally, energy conversion efficiency is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dimensionless" title="Dimensionless">dimensionless</a> number between 0 and 1.0, or 0% to 100%.  Efficiencies may not exceed 100%, e.g., for a <a href="/wiki/Perpetual_motion" title="Perpetual motion">perpetual motion</a> machine.  However, other <a href="/wiki/Thermal_efficiency" title="Thermal efficiency">effectiveness measures</a> that can exceed 1.0 are used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_pumps" title="Heat pumps">heat pumps</a> and other devices that move heat rather than convert it.
</p><p>When talking about the efficiency of heat engines and power stations the convention should be stated, i.e., HHV (<abbr title="also known as">a.k.a.</abbr> Gross Heating Value, etc.) or LCV (a.k.a. Net Heating value), and  whether gross output (at the generator terminals) or net output (at the power station fence) are being considered.  The two are separate but both must be stated. Failure to do so causes endless confusion.
</p><p>Related, more specific terms include
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Electrical_efficiency" title="Electrical efficiency">Electrical efficiency</a>, useful power output per electrical power consumed;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mechanical_efficiency" title="Mechanical efficiency">Mechanical efficiency</a>, where one form of mechanical energy (e.g. potential energy of water) is converted to mechanical energy (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanical_work" title="Mechanical work">work</a>);</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Thermal_efficiency" title="Thermal efficiency">Thermal efficiency</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fuel_efficiency" title="Fuel efficiency">Fuel efficiency</a>, useful <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Work_(thermodynamics)" title="Work (thermodynamics)">work</a> <a href="/wiki/Work_output" title="Work output">output</a> per input energy such as the <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a> consumed;</li>
<li>'Total efficiency', e.g., for <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">cogeneration</a>, useful <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">electric power</a> and heat output per fuel energy consumed.  Same as the <a href="/wiki/Thermal_efficiency" title="Thermal efficiency">thermal efficiency</a>.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luminous_efficiency" title="Luminous efficiency">Luminous efficiency</a>, that portion of the emitted electromagnetic radiation is usable for human vision.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fuel_heating_values_and_efficiency">Fuel heating values and efficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_conversion_efficiency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fuel heating values and efficiency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Europe the usable energy content of fuel is typically calculated using the <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lower_heating_value" title="Lower heating value">lower heating value</a></b> (LHV) of that fuel, the definition of which assumes that the <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> produced during fuel <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> (oxidation), remains gaseous, and is not <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensed</a> to liquid water so the latent heat of vaporization of that water is not  usable. Using the LHV, a <a href="/wiki/Condensing_boiler" title="Condensing boiler">condensing boiler</a> can achieve a "heating efficiency" in excess of 100% (this does not violate the <a href="/wiki/First_law_of_thermodynamics" title="First law of thermodynamics">first law of thermodynamics</a> as long as the LHV convention is understood, but does cause confusion). This is because the apparatus recovers part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_of_vaporization" title="Heat of vaporization">heat of vaporization</a>, which is not included in the definition of the lower heating value of fuel.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the U.S. and elsewhere, the <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Higher_heating_value" title="Higher heating value">higher heating value</a></b> (HHV) is used, which includes the latent heat for condensing the water vapor, and thus the thermodynamic maximum of 100% efficiency cannot be exceeded with HHV's use.
</p>

<p>In optical systems such as lighting and <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">lasers</a>, the energy conversion efficiency is often referred to as <a href="/wiki/Wall-plug_efficiency" title="Wall-plug efficiency">wall-plug efficiency</a>. The wall-plug efficiency is the measure of output radiative-energy, in <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">watts</a> (<a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">joules</a> per second), per the total of the input electrical-energy in watts. The output-energy is usually measured in terms of <a href="/wiki/Absolute_scale" title="Absolute scale">absolute</a> <a href="/wiki/Irradiance" title="Irradiance">irradiance</a> and the wall-plug efficiency is given as a percentage of the total input-energy, with the inverse percentage representing the losses.
</p><p>The wall-plug efficiency differs from the <i>luminous efficiency</i> in that wall-plug efficiency describes the direct output/input conversion of energy (the amount of <a href="/wiki/Work_(physics)" title="Work (physics)">work</a> that can be performed) whereas luminous efficiency takes into account the human eye's varying sensitivity to different wavelengths (how well it can illuminate a space). Instead of using watts, the power of a light source to produce wavelengths proportional to human perception is measured in <a href="/wiki/Lumen_(unit)" title="Lumen (unit)">lumens</a>. The human eye is most sensitive to wavelengths of 555 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanometer" title="Nanometer">nanometers</a> (greenish-yellow) but the sensitivity decreases dramatically to either side of this wavelength, following a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaussian_profile" title="Gaussian profile">Gaussian</a> power-curve and dropping to zero sensitivity at the red and violet ends of the spectrum. Due to this the eye does not usually see all of the wavelengths emitted by a particular light-source, nor does it see all of the wavelengths within the visual spectrum equally. Yellow and green, for example, make up more than 50% of what the eye perceives as being white, even though in terms of radiant energy white-light is made from equal portions of all colors (i.e.: a 5 mw green laser appears brighter than a 5 mw red laser, yet the red laser stands-out better against a white background). Therefore, the <a href="/wiki/Radiant_intensity" title="Radiant intensity">radiant intensity</a> of a light source may be much greater than its <a href="/wiki/Luminous_intensity" title="Luminous intensity">luminous intensity</a>, meaning that the source emits more energy than the eye can use. Likewise, the lamp's wall-plug efficiency is usually greater than its luminous efficiency. The effectiveness of a light source to convert electrical energy into wavelengths of visible light, in proportion to the sensitivity of the human eye, is referred to as <a href="/wiki/Luminous_efficacy" title="Luminous efficacy">luminous efficacy</a>, which is measured in units of lumens per watt (lm/w) of electrical input-energy. 
</p><p>Unlike efficacy (effectiveness), which is a <a href="/wiki/Unit_of_measurement" title="Unit of measurement">unit of measurement</a>, efficiency is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unitless" title="Unitless">unitless</a> number expressed as a <a href="/wiki/Percentage" title="Percentage">percentage</a>, requiring only that the input and output units be of the same type. Therefore, the luminous efficiency of a light source is the percentage of luminous efficacy per the theoretical-maximum efficacy at a specific wavelength. The amount of energy carried by a photon of light is determined by its wavelength. In lumens, this energy is offset by the eye's sensitivity to the selected wavelengths. For example, a green <a href="/wiki/Laser_pointer" title="Laser pointer">laser pointer</a> can have greater than 30 times the apparent brightness of a red pointer of the same power output. At 555 nm in wavelength, 1 watt of radiant energy is equivalent to 685 lumens, thus a monochromatic light source at this wavelength, with a luminous efficacy of 685 lm/w, has a luminous efficiency of 100%. The theoretical-maximum efficacy lowers for wavelengths at either side of 555 nm. For example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low-pressure_sodium_lamp" title="Low-pressure sodium lamp">low-pressure sodium lamps</a> produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monochromatic_light" title="Monochromatic light">monochromatic light</a> at 589 nm with a luminous efficacy of 200 lm/w, which is the highest of any lamp. The theoretical-maximum efficacy at that wavelength is 525 lm/w, so the lamp has a luminous efficiency of 38.1%. Because the lamp is monochromatic, the luminous efficiency nearly matches the wall-plug efficiency of &lt; 40%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Calculations for luminous efficiency become more complex for lamps that produce white light or a mixture of spectral lines. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluorescent_lamps" title="Fluorescent lamps">Fluorescent lamps</a> have higher wall-plug efficiencies than low-pressure sodium lamps, but only have half the luminous efficacy of ~ 100 lm/w, thus the luminous efficiency of fluorescents is lower than sodium lamps. A xenon <a href="/wiki/Flashtube" title="Flashtube">flashtube</a> has a typical wall-plug efficiency of 50--70%, exceeding that of most other forms of lighting. Because the flashtube emits large amounts of infrared and ultraviolet radiation, only a portion of the output energy is used by the eye. The luminous efficacy is therefore typically around 50 lm/w. However, not all applications for lighting involve the human eye nor are restricted to visible wavelengths. For <a href="/wiki/Laser_pumping" title="Laser pumping">laser pumping</a>, the efficacy is not related to the human eye so it is not called "luminous" efficacy, but rather simply "efficacy" as it relates to the absorption lines of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laser_medium" title="Laser medium">laser medium</a>. Krypton flashtubes are often chosen for pumping <a href="/wiki/Nd:YAG_laser" title="Nd:YAG laser">Nd:YAG lasers</a>, even though their wall-plug efficiency is typically only ~ 40%. <a href="/wiki/Krypton" title="Krypton">Krypton</a>'s spectral lines better match the absorption lines of the <a href="/wiki/Neodymium" title="Neodymium">neodymium</a>-<a href="/wiki/Dopant" title="Dopant">doped</a> crystal, thus the efficacy of krypton for this purpose is much higher than <a href="/wiki/Xenon" title="Xenon">xenon</a>; able to produce up to twice the laser output for the same electrical input.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> All of these terms refer to the amount of energy and lumens as they exit the light source, disregarding any losses that might occur within the lighting fixture or subsequent output optics. <i>Luminaire efficiency</i> refers to the total lumen-output from the fixture per the lamp output.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>With the exception of a few light sources, such as <a href="/wiki/Incandescent_light_bulb" title="Incandescent light bulb">incandescent light bulbs</a>, most light sources have multiple stages of energy conversion between the "wall plug" (electrical input point, which may include batteries, direct wiring, or other sources) and the final light-output, with each stage producing a loss. Low-pressure sodium lamps initially convert the electrical energy using an <a href="/wiki/Electrical_ballast" title="Electrical ballast">electrical ballast</a>, to maintain the proper current and voltage, but some energy is lost in the ballast. Similarly, fluorescent lamps also convert the electricity using a ballast (electronic efficiency). The electricity is then converted into light energy by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_arc" title="Electrical arc">electrical arc</a> (electrode efficiency and discharge efficiency). The light is then transferred to a fluorescent coating that only absorbs suitable wavelengths, with some losses of those wavelengths due to reflection off and transmission through the coating (transfer efficiency). The number of photons absorbed by the coating will not match the number then reemitted as <a href="/wiki/Fluorescence" title="Fluorescence">fluorescence</a> (<a href="/wiki/Quantum_yield" title="Quantum yield">quantum efficiency</a>). Finally, due to the phenomenon of the <a href="/wiki/Stokes_shift" title="Stokes shift">Stokes shift</a>, the reemitted photons will have a shorter wavelength (thus lower energy) than the absorbed photons (fluorescence efficiency). In very similar fashion, lasers also experience many stages of conversion between the wall plug and the output <a href="/wiki/Aperture" title="Aperture">aperture</a>. The terms "wall-plug efficiency" or "energy conversion efficiency" are therefore used to denote the overall efficiency of the energy-conversion device, deducting the losses from each stage, although this may exclude external components needed to operate some devices, such as coolant pumps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
