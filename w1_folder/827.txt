<p><b>Land</b>, sometimes referred to as <b>dry land</b>, is the solid surface of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> that is not permanently covered by <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The vast majority of human activity throughout history has occurred in land areas that support <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a>, and various <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a>. Some life forms (including <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_plant" title="Terrestrial plant">terrestrial plants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_animal" title="Terrestrial animal">terrestrial animals</a>) have developed from predecessor species that lived in <a href="/wiki/Body_of_water" title="Body of water">bodies of water</a>.
</p><p>Areas where land meets large bodies of water are called <a href="/wiki/Coast" title="Coast">coastal zones</a>. The division between land and water is a fundamental concept to humans. The demarcation between land and water can vary by local jurisdiction and other factors. A <a href="/wiki/Maritime_boundary" title="Maritime boundary">maritime boundary</a> is one example of a political demarcation. A variety of natural boundaries exist to help clearly define where water meets land. Solid rock <a href="/wiki/Landform" title="Landform">landforms</a> are easier to demarcate than marshy or swampy boundaries, where there is no clear point at which the land ends and a body of water has begun. Demarcation can further vary due to tides and weather.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_terminology">Etymology and terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The word 'land' is derived from Middle English <i>land</i>, <i>lond</i> and <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>land</i>, <i>lond</i> (“earth, land, soil, ground; defined piece of land, territory, realm, province, district; landed property; country (not town); ridge in a ploughed field”), from Proto-Germanic <i>*landą</i> (“land”), and from Proto-Indo-European <i>*lendʰ-</i> (“land, heath”). Cognate with Scots <i>land</i> (“land”), West Frisian <i>lân</i> (“land”), Dutch <i>land</i> (“land”), German <i>Land</i> (“land, country, state”), Swedish <i>land</i> (“land, country, shore, territory”), Icelandic <i>land</i> (“land”). Non-Germanic cognates include Old Irish <i>lann</i> (“heath”), Welsh <i>llan</i> (“enclosure”), Breton <i>lann</i> (“heath”), Old Church Slavonic <i>lędо</i> from Proto-Slavic <i>*lenda</i> (“heath, wasteland”) and Albanian <i>lëndinë</i> (“heath, grassland”) from <i>lëndë</i> (“matter, substance”).
</p><p>A continuous area of land surrounded by ocean is called a "landmass". Although it may be most often written as one word to distinguish it from the usage "<a href="/wiki/Land#Land_mass" title="Land">land mass</a>"—the measure of land area—it is also used as two words. Landmasses include <a href="/wiki/Supercontinent" title="Supercontinent">supercontinents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continents</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Island" title="Island">islands</a>. There are four major continuous landmasses on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>: <a href="/wiki/Afro-Eurasia" title="Afro-Eurasia">Afro-Eurasia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australia_(continent)" title="Australia (continent)">Australia</a>. Land, capable of being <a href="/wiki/Plough" title="Plough">ploughed</a> and used to grow <a href="/wiki/Crop" title="Crop">crops</a>, is called <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable land</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A country or region may be referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Homeland#Motherland" title="Homeland">motherland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fatherland" title="Fatherland">fatherland</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Homeland" title="Homeland">homeland</a> of its people. Many countries and other places have names incorporating <a href="/wiki/-land" title="-land">-land</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_land_on_Earth">History of land on Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History of land on Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Earth" title="History of Earth">History of Earth</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Earth_formation.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="1920" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Earth_formation.jpg/200px-Earth_formation.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Earth_formation.jpg/300px-Earth_formation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Earth_formation.jpg/400px-Earth_formation.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Earth_formation.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Artist's conception of <a href="/wiki/Hadean" title="Hadean">Hadean Eon</a> Earth.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pangea_animation_03.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="160" data-file-width="200" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8e/Pangea_animation_03.gif" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pangea_animation_03.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An animation showing the movement of the continents from the separation of <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a> until the present day.</div></div></div>
<p>The earliest material found in the Solar System is dated to <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7017144129870720000♠</span>4.5672<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>0.0006 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Billion_years_ago" title="Billion years ago">bya</a></span> (billion years ago);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bowring_housch1995_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-bowring_housch1995-3">[3]</a></sup> therefore, the Earth itself must have been formed by <a href="/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics)" title="Accretion (astrophysics)">accretion</a> around this time. By <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7017143271504000000♠</span>4.54<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>0.04 bya</span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-age_earth1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-age_earth1-4">[4]</a></sup> the primordial Earth had formed. The <a href="/wiki/Formation_and_evolution_of_the_Solar_System" title="Formation and evolution of the Solar System">formation and evolution of the Solar System</a> bodies occurred in tandem with the Sun. In theory, a <a href="/wiki/Formation_and_evolution_of_the_Solar_System#Formation_of_the_planets" title="Formation and evolution of the Solar System">solar nebula</a> partitions a volume out of a <a href="/wiki/Molecular_cloud" title="Molecular cloud">molecular cloud</a> by gravitational collapse, which begins to spin and flatten into a <a href="/wiki/Circumstellar_disc" title="Circumstellar disc">circumstellar disc</a>, which the planets then grow out of in tandem with the star. A nebula contains gas, ice grains and <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_dust" title="Cosmic dust">dust</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Primordial_nuclide" title="Primordial nuclide">primordial nuclides</a>). In <a href="/wiki/Nebular_hypothesis" title="Nebular hypothesis">nebular theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Planetesimal" title="Planetesimal">planetesimals</a> commence forming as <a href="/wiki/Granular_material#Granular_gases" title="Granular material">particulate</a> matter accrues by <a href="/wiki/Cohesion_(geology)" title="Cohesion (geology)">cohesive clumping</a> and then by gravity. The assembly of the primordial Earth proceeded for 10–<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7014631152000000000♠</span>20 <a href="/wiki/Myr" title="Myr">myr</a></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature418_6901_949_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-nature418_6901_949-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Earth's atmosphere and oceans were formed by <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic</a> activity and <a href="/wiki/Outgassing" title="Outgassing">outgassing</a> that included <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Origin_of_the_world%27s_oceans" title="Origin of the world's oceans">origin of the world's oceans</a> was condensation augmented by water and ice delivered by <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Protoplanet" title="Protoplanet">proto-planets</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comets</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-watersource_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-watersource-6">[6]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Faint_young_Sun_paradox" title="Faint young Sun paradox">this model</a>, atmospheric "<a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>" kept the oceans from freezing while the newly forming Sun was only at 70% <a href="/wiki/Solar_luminosity" title="Solar luminosity">luminosity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asp2002_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-asp2002-7">[7]</a></sup> By <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7017110451600000000♠</span>3.5 bya</span>, the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth's magnetic field">Earth's magnetic field</a> was established, which helped prevent the atmosphere from being stripped away by the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-physorg20100304_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-physorg20100304-8">[8]</a></sup> The atmosphere and oceans of the Earth continuously shape the land by eroding and transporting solids on the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a>, which currently forms the Earth's land, was created when the molten outer layer of the planet Earth cooled <a href="/wiki/Phase_transition" title="Phase transition">to form</a> a solid mass as the accumulated water vapor began to act in the atmosphere. Once land became capable of supporting life, biodiversity evolved over hundreds of million years, expanding continually except when punctuated by mass extinctions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SahneyBentonFerry2010_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-SahneyBentonFerry2010-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The two models<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-williams_santosh2004_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-williams_santosh2004-11">[11]</a></sup> that explain land mass propose either a steady growth to the present-day forms<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-science164_1229_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-science164_1229-12">[12]</a></sup> or, more likely, a rapid growth<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tp322_19_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-tp322_19-13">[13]</a></sup> early in Earth history<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rg6_175_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-rg6_175-14">[14]</a></sup> followed by a long-term steady continental area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-science310_5754_1671_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-science310_5754_1671-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jaes23_799_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-jaes23_799-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ajes38_613_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ajes38_613-17">[17]</a></sup> Continents formed by <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a>, a process ultimately driven by the continuous loss of heat from the Earth's interior. On <a href="/wiki/Geologic_time_scale" title="Geologic time scale">time scales</a> lasting hundreds of millions of years, the <a href="/wiki/Supercontinent" title="Supercontinent">supercontinents</a> have formed and broken apart three times. Roughly <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7016236682000000000♠</span>750 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mya_(unit)" title="Mya (unit)">mya</a></span> (million years ago), one of the earliest known supercontinents, <a href="/wiki/Rodinia" title="Rodinia">Rodinia</a>, began to break apart. The continents later recombined to form <a href="/wiki/Pannotia" title="Pannotia">Pannotia</a>, 600–<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7016170411040000000♠</span>540 mya</span>, then finally <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a>, which also broke apart <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7015568036800000000♠</span>180 mya</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-as92_324_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-as92_324-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_mass">Land mass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Land mass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Landmass" redirects here. For the ambient album by Steve Roach, see <a href="/wiki/Landmass_(album)" title="Landmass (album)">Landmass (album)</a>.</div>
<p>"Land mass" refers to the total <a href="/wiki/Surface_area" title="Surface area">surface</a> <a href="/wiki/Area" title="Area">area</a> of the land of a geographical <a href="/wiki/Region" title="Region">region</a> or <a href="/wiki/Country" title="Country">country</a> (which may include discontinuous pieces of land such as <a href="/wiki/Island" title="Island">islands</a>). It is written as two words to distinguish it from the usage "landmass", the contiguous area of land surrounded by <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a>. <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s total land mass is approximately 148,939,063.133 km<sup>2</sup> (57,505,693.767 sq mi) which is about 29.2% of its total surface. Water covers approximately 70.8% of Earth's surface, mainly in the form of <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceans</a> and ice formations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_perspectives">Cultural perspectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cultural perspectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Earth_in_culture" title="Earth in culture">Earth in culture</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Creation_myth" title="Creation myth">Creation myths</a> in many <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religions</a> recall a story involving the creation of the world by a supernatural <a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">deity</a> or deities, including accounts wherein the land is separated from the oceans and the air. The Earth itself has often been personified as a <a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">deity</a>, in particular a <a href="/wiki/Goddess" title="Goddess">goddess</a>. In many cultures, the <a href="/wiki/Mother_goddess" title="Mother goddess">mother goddess</a> is also portrayed as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertility_god" title="Fertility god">fertility deity</a>. To the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aztec" title="Aztec">Aztecs</a>, Earth was called <i><a href="/wiki/Tonantzin" title="Tonantzin">Tonantzin</a></i>—"our mother"; to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incas" title="Incas">Incas</a>, Earth was called <i><a href="/wiki/Pachamama" title="Pachamama">Pachamama</a></i>—"mother earth". The Chinese Earth goddess <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hou_Tu" title="Hou Tu">Hou Tu</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> is similar to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaia_(mythology)" title="Gaia (mythology)">Gaia</a>, the Greek goddess personifying the Earth. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bhumi_Devi" title="Bhumi Devi">Bhuma Devi</a> is the goddess of Earth in <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>, influenced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graha" title="Graha">Graha</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">Norse mythology</a>, the Earth giantess <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6r%C3%B0" title="Jörð">Jörð</a> was the mother of <a href="/wiki/Thor" title="Thor">Thor</a> and the daughter of <a href="/wiki/Annar" title="Annar">Annar</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_religion" title="Ancient Egyptian religion">Ancient Egyptian mythology</a> is different from that of other cultures because Earth (<a href="/wiki/Geb" title="Geb">Geb</a>) is male and sky (<a href="/wiki/Nut_(goddess)" title="Nut (goddess)">Nut</a>) is female.
</p><p>
In the past, there were varying levels of belief in a <a href="/wiki/Flat_Earth" title="Flat Earth">flat Earth</a>. The Jewish conception of a flat earth is found in both biblical and post-biblical times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[note 1]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Neutral_point_of_view" title="Wikipedia:Neutral point of view"><span title="This statement is possibly biased. (May 2017)">neutrality</span></a> is <a href="/wiki/Talk:Land" title="Talk:Land">disputed</a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[note 2]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Neutral_point_of_view" title="Wikipedia:Neutral point of view"><span title="This statement is possibly biased. (May 2017)">neutrality</span></a> is <a href="/wiki/Talk:Land" title="Talk:Land">disputed</a></i>]</sup></p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Baylonianmaps.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="368" height="299" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Baylonianmaps.JPG/220px-Baylonianmaps.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Baylonianmaps.JPG/330px-Baylonianmaps.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a5/Baylonianmaps.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Baylonianmaps.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Babylonian_Map_of_the_World" title="Babylonian Map of the World">Imago Mundi</a></i> Babylonian map, the oldest known world map, 6th century BC <a href="/wiki/Babylonia" title="Babylonia">Babylonia</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>In early Egyptian<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[24]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesopotamian_mythology" title="Mesopotamian mythology">Mesopotamian thought</a>, the world was portrayed as a flat disk floating in the ocean. The Egyptian universe was pictured as a rectangular box with a north-south orientation and with a slightly concave surface, with Egypt in the center. A similar model is found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homeric" title="Homeric">Homeric</a> account of the 8th century BC in which "Okeanos, the personified body of water surrounding the circular surface of the Earth, is the begetter of all life and possibly of all gods."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[25]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Biblical_cosmology#Earth" title="Biblical cosmology">biblical earth</a> is a flat disc floating on water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berlin_2011_189_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Berlin_2011_189-28">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Pyramid_Texts" title="Pyramid Texts">Pyramid Texts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coffin_Texts" title="Coffin Texts">Coffin Texts</a> reveal that the ancient Egyptians believed <a href="/wiki/Nu_(mythology)" title="Nu (mythology)">Nun</a> (the ocean) was a circular body surrounding <i>nbwt</i> (a term meaning "dry lands" or "islands"), and therefore believed in a similar Ancient Near Eastern circular Earth cosmography surrounded by water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>The spherical form of the Earth was suggested by early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_philosophers" title="Greek philosophers">Greek philosophers</a>, a belief espoused by <a href="/wiki/Pythagoras" title="Pythagoras">Pythagoras</a>. Contrary to popular belief, most people in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> did not believe the Earth was flat: this misconception is often called the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myth_of_the_Flat_Earth" title="Myth of the Flat Earth">Myth of the Flat Earth</a>". As evidenced by thinkers such as <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas" title="Thomas Aquinas">Thomas Aquinas</a>, the European belief in a <a href="/wiki/Spherical_Earth" title="Spherical Earth">spherical Earth</a> was widespread by this point in time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[30]</a></sup> Prior to circumnavigation of the planet and the introduction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_flight" title="Space flight">space flight</a>, belief in a spherical Earth was based on observations of the secondary effects of the Earth's shape and parallels drawn with the shape of other planets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraterrestrial_land">Extraterrestrial land</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Extraterrestrial land">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most planets known to humans are either gaseous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jovian_planets" title="Jovian planets">Jovian planets</a> or solid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terrestrial_planets" title="Terrestrial planets">terrestrial planets</a>. Terrestrial planets include <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>. These inner planets have a rocky surface with metal interiors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[32]</a></sup> The Jovian planets consist of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a>. While these planets are larger, their only land surface is a small rocky core surrounded by a large, thick atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[33]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Gas_giant" title="Gas giant">gas giants</a>, Jupiter and Saturn, are thought to have surface layers composed of <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">liquid hydrogen</a> rather than solid land; however, their <a href="/wiki/Planetary_geology" title="Planetary geology">planetary geology</a> is not well understood. The possibility of Uranus and Neptune (the <a href="/wiki/Ice_giant" title="Ice giant">ice giants</a>) possessing hot, highly compressed, supercritical water under their thick atmospheres has been hypothesised.  While their composition is still not fully understood, a 2006 study by Wiktorowicz et al. ruled out the possibility of such a water "ocean" existing on Neptune,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WiktorowiczIngersoll2007_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-WiktorowiczIngersoll2007-36">[34]</a></sup> though some studies have suggested that exotic oceans of liquid diamond are possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silvera2010_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Silvera2010-37">[35]</a></sup> The entire surface of a rocky planet or moon is considered land, even with a lack of seas or oceans for contrast. Planetary bodies that have a thin atmosphere often have land that is marked by impact craters since atmospheric conditions would normally break-down incoming objects and erode rough impact sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[36]</a></sup> Land on planetary bodies other than Earth can also be bought and sold although ownership of <a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_real_estate" title="Extraterrestrial real estate">extraterrestrial real estate</a> is not recognized by any authority.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_and_climate">Land and climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Land and climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The land of the Earth interacts with and influences <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> heavily since the surface of the land heats up and cools down faster than air or water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PBS_Learning_Media_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-PBS_Learning_Media-40">[38]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">Latitude</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">elevation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reflectivity" title="Reflectivity">reflectivity</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land use</a> all have varying effects. The latitude of the land will influence how much <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a> reaches the surface. High latitudes receive less solar radiation than low latitudes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PBS_Learning_Media_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-PBS_Learning_Media-40">[38]</a></sup> The height of the land is important in creating and transforming airflow and <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">precipitation</a> on Earth. Large landforms, such as mountain ranges, divert wind energy and make the air parcel less dense and able to hold less heat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PBS_Learning_Media_40-2"><a href="#cite_note-PBS_Learning_Media-40">[38]</a></sup> As air rises, this cooling effect causes <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">precipitation</a>. Reflectivity of the earth is called planetary <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> and the type of land cover that receives energy from the sun affects the amount of energy that is reflected or transferred to Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alan_Betts:_Atmospheric_Research_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alan_Betts:_Atmospheric_Research-41">[39]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Vegetation" title="Vegetation">Vegetation</a> has a relatively low albedo meaning that vegetated surfaces are good absorbers of the sun’s energy. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forests" title="Forests">Forests</a> have an albedo of 10–15% while <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grasslands" title="Grasslands">grasslands</a> have an albedo of 15–20%. In comparison, sandy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deserts" title="Deserts">deserts</a> have an albedo of 25–40%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alan_Betts:_Atmospheric_Research_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-Alan_Betts:_Atmospheric_Research-41">[39]</a></sup> Land use by humans also plays a role in the regional and global climate. Densely populated cities are warmer and create <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urban_heat_islands" title="Urban heat islands">urban heat islands</a> that have effects on the precipitation, cloud cover, and temperature of the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PBS_Learning_Media_40-3"><a href="#cite_note-PBS_Learning_Media-40">[38]</a></sup>
</p>

<li id="cite_note-24"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-24">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The picture of the universe in Talmudic texts has the Earth in the center of creation with heaven as a hemisphere spread over it. Biblical writings, such as the Genesis creation story and the various Psalms that extol the firmament, the stars, the sun, and the earth, give similar explanations. The Hebrews saw the earth as an almost flat surface consisting of a solid and a liquid part and the sky as the realm of light in which heavenly bodies move. The earth rested on cornerstones and could not be moved except by Jehovah (as in an earthquake). According to the Hebrews, the sun and the moon were only a short distance from one another. "Cosmology." Encyclopedia Americana. Grolier Online, 2012. Author: Giorgio Abetti, Astrophysical Observatory of Arcetri-Firenze.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-25"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-25">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The Earth is usually described as a disk encircled by water. Cosmological and metaphysical speculations were not to be cultivated in public nor were they to be committed to writing. Rather, they were considered to be "secrets of the Torah not to be passed on to all and sundry" (Ketubot 112a). While study of God's creation was not prohibited, speculations about "what is above, what is beneath, what is before, and what is after" (Mishnah Hagigah: 2) were restricted to the intellectual elite. (Topic Overview: Judaism, Encyclopedia of Science and Religion, Ed. J. Wentzel Vrede van Huyssteen. Vol. 2. New York: Macmillan Reference, 2003. pp. 477–483. Hava Tirosh-Samuelson).</span>
