<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Sedimentary rock</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Sedimentary rocks</b> are types of <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rock</a> that are formed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deposition_(sediment)" title="Deposition (sediment)">deposition</a> and subsequent cementation of <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> or <a href="/wiki/Organic_matter" title="Organic matter">organic</a> particles on the floor of oceans or other bodies of water at the <a href="/wiki/Earth#Surface" title="Earth">Earth's surface</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sedimentation" title="Sedimentation">Sedimentation</a> is the collective name for processes that cause these particles to settle in place. The particles that form a sedimentary rock are called <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a>, and may be composed of <a href="/wiki/Detritus_(geology)" title="Detritus (geology)">geological detritus</a> (minerals) or <a href="/wiki/Detritus" title="Detritus">biological detritus</a> (organic matter). Before being deposited, the geological detritus was formed by <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> from the source area, and then transported to the place of deposition by <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mass_wasting" title="Mass wasting">mass movement</a> or <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a>, which are called agents of <a href="/wiki/Denudation" title="Denudation">denudation</a>. Biological detritus was formed by bodies and parts (mainly shells) of dead aquatic organisms, as well as their fecal mass, suspended in water and slowly piling up on the floor of water bodies (<a href="/wiki/Marine_snow" title="Marine snow">marine snow</a>). Sedimentation may also occur as dissolved minerals precipitate from water solution.
</p><p>The sedimentary rock cover of the continents of the <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">Earth's crust</a> is extensive (73% of the Earth's current land surface<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkinson2008_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wilkinson2008-1">[1]</a></sup>), but the total contribution of sedimentary rocks is estimated to be only 8% of the total volume of the crust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Sedimentary rocks are only a thin veneer over a crust consisting mainly of <a href="/wiki/Igneous_rock" title="Igneous rock">igneous</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphic rocks</a>. Sedimentary rocks are deposited in layers as <a href="/wiki/Stratum" title="Stratum">strata</a>, forming a structure called <a href="/wiki/Bed_(geology)" title="Bed (geology)">bedding</a>. The study of sedimentary rocks and rock strata provides information about the subsurface that is useful for <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineering" title="Civil engineering">civil engineering</a>, for example in the construction of <a href="/wiki/Road" title="Road">roads</a>, <a href="/wiki/House" title="House">houses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tunnel" title="Tunnel">tunnels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canal" title="Canal">canals</a> or other structures. Sedimentary rocks are also important sources of <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a> like <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ores</a>.
</p><p>The study of the sequence of sedimentary rock strata is the main source for an understanding of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_the_Earth" title="History of the Earth">Earth's history</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Palaeogeography" title="Palaeogeography">palaeogeography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">paleoclimatology</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_history_of_life" title="Evolutionary history of life">history of life</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_academic_disciplines" title="List of academic disciplines">scientific discipline</a> that studies the properties and origin of sedimentary rocks is called <a href="/wiki/Sedimentology" title="Sedimentology">sedimentology</a>. Sedimentology is part of both <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physical_geography" title="Physical geography">physical geography</a> and overlaps partly with other disciplines in the <a href="/wiki/Earth_science" title="Earth science">Earth sciences</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pedology_(soil_study)" title="Pedology (soil study)">pedology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">geomorphology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geochemistry" title="Geochemistry">geochemistry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Structural_geology" title="Structural geology">structural geology</a>. Sedimentary rocks have also been found on <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_based_on_origin">Classification based on origin</span></h2>
<p>Sedimentary rocks can be subdivided into four groups based on the processes responsible for their formation: clastic sedimentary rocks, biochemical (biogenic) sedimentary rocks, chemical sedimentary rocks, and a fourth category for "other" sedimentary rocks formed by impacts, <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">volcanism</a>, and other minor processes.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Clastic_sedimentary_rocks">Clastic sedimentary rocks</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Clastic_rock" title="Clastic rock">Clastic rock</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GLMsed.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/GLMsed.jpg/220px-GLMsed.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/GLMsed.jpg/330px-GLMsed.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/GLMsed.jpg/440px-GLMsed.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GLMsed.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claystone" title="Claystone">Claystone</a> deposited in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glacial_Lake_Missoula" title="Glacial Lake Missoula">Glacial Lake Missoula</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. Note the very fine and flat bedding, common for distal <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lacustrine</a> deposition.</div></div></div>
<p>Clastic sedimentary rocks are composed of other rock fragments that were cemented by silicate minerals. Clastic rocks are composed largely of <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Feldspar" title="Feldspar">feldspar</a>, rock (lithic) fragments, <a href="/wiki/Clay_minerals" title="Clay minerals">clay minerals</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mica" title="Mica">mica</a>; any type of mineral may be present, but they in general represent the minerals that exist locally.
</p><p>Clastic sedimentary rocks, are subdivided according to the dominant particle size. Most geologists use the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particle_size_(grain_size)#Wentworth" title="Particle size (grain size)">Udden-Wentworth</a> grain size scale and divide unconsolidated sediment into three fractions: <a href="/wiki/Gravel" title="Gravel">gravel</a> (&gt;2 mm diameter), <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a> (1/16 to 2 mm diameter), and <a href="/wiki/Mud" title="Mud">mud</a> (<a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> is &lt;1/256 mm and <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silt</a> is between 1/16 and 1/256 mm). The classification of clastic sedimentary rocks parallels this scheme; <a href="/wiki/Conglomerate_(geology)" title="Conglomerate (geology)">conglomerates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Breccia" title="Breccia">breccias</a> are made mostly of <a href="/wiki/Gravel" title="Gravel">gravel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstones</a> are made mostly of <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mudrock" title="Mudrock">mudrocks</a> are made mostly of the finest material. This tripartite subdivision is mirrored by the broad categories of <a href="/wiki/Rudite" title="Rudite">rudites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arenite" title="Arenite">arenites</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lutite" title="Lutite">lutites</a>, respectively, in older literature.
</p><p>The subdivision of these three broad categories is based on differences in clast shape (<a href="/wiki/Conglomerate_(geology)" title="Conglomerate (geology)">conglomerates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Breccia" title="Breccia">breccias</a>), composition (<a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstones</a>), grain size or texture (<a href="/wiki/Mudrock" title="Mudrock">mudrocks</a>).
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Conglomerates_and_breccias">Conglomerates and breccias</span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Conglomerate_(geology)" title="Conglomerate (geology)">Conglomerates</a> are dominantly composed of rounded <a href="/wiki/Gravel" title="Gravel">gravel</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Breccia" title="Breccia">breccias</a> are composed of dominantly angular <a href="/wiki/Gravel" title="Gravel">gravel</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sandstones">Sandstones</span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sedimentgesteine_auf_Malta.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3888" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Sedimentgesteine_auf_Malta.JPG/220px-Sedimentgesteine_auf_Malta.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Sedimentgesteine_auf_Malta.JPG/330px-Sedimentgesteine_auf_Malta.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Sedimentgesteine_auf_Malta.JPG/440px-Sedimentgesteine_auf_Malta.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sedimentgesteine_auf_Malta.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sedimentary rock with sandstone in <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">Sandstone</a> classification schemes vary widely, but most geologists have adopted the Dott scheme,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dott_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dott-3">[3]</a></sup> which uses the relative abundance of quartz, feldspar, and lithic framework grains and the abundance of a muddy matrix between the larger grains.
</p>
<dl><dd><i>Composition of framework grains</i></dd>
<dd>The relative abundance of sand-sized framework grains determines the first word in a sandstone name. Naming depends on the dominance of the three most abundant components <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Feldspar" title="Feldspar">feldspar</a>, or the lithic fragments that originated from other rocks. All other minerals are considered accessories and not used in the naming of the rock, regardless of abundance.</dd></dl>
<dl><dd><dl><dd><ul><li>Quartz sandstones have &gt;90% quartz grains</li>
<li>Feldspathic sandstones have &lt;90% quartz grains and more feldspar grains than lithic grains</li>
<li>Lithic sandstones have &lt;90% quartz grains and more lithic grains than feldspar grains</li></ul></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><i>Abundance of muddy matrix material between sand grains</i>
<dl><dd>When sand-sized particles are deposited, the space between the grains either remains open or is filled with mud (silt and/or clay sized particle).
<ul><li>"Clean" sandstones with open pore space (that may later be filled with matrix material) are called arenites.</li>
<li>Muddy sandstones with abundant (&gt;10%) muddy matrix are called wackes.</li></ul></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p>Six <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> names are possible using the descriptors for grain composition (quartz-, feldspathic-, and lithic-) and the amount of matrix (wacke or arenite). For example, a quartz arenite would be composed of mostly (&gt;90%) quartz grains and have little or no clayey matrix between the grains, a lithic wacke would have abundant lithic grains and abundant muddy matrix, etc.
</p><p>Although the Dott classification scheme<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dott_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dott-3">[3]</a></sup> is widely used by sedimentologists, common names like <a href="/wiki/Greywacke" title="Greywacke">greywacke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arkose" title="Arkose">arkose</a>, and quartz sandstone are still widely used by non-specialists and in popular literature.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mudrocks">Mudrocks</span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lower_antelope_3_md.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="821" data-file-width="1231" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Lower_antelope_3_md.jpg/220px-Lower_antelope_3_md.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Lower_antelope_3_md.jpg/330px-Lower_antelope_3_md.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Lower_antelope_3_md.jpg/440px-Lower_antelope_3_md.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lower_antelope_3_md.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Antelope_Canyon" title="Antelope Canyon">Lower Antelope Canyon</a> was carved out of the surrounding <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> by both mechanical weathering and chemical weathering. Wind, sand, and water from <a href="/wiki/Flash_flood" title="Flash flood">flash flooding</a> are the primary weathering agents.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mudrock" title="Mudrock">Mudrocks</a> are sedimentary rocks composed of at least 50% <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silt</a>- and <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a>-sized particles. These relatively fine-grained particles are commonly transported by <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulent flow</a> in water or air, and deposited as the flow calms and the particles settle out of <a href="/wiki/Suspension_(chemistry)" title="Suspension (chemistry)">suspension</a>.
</p><p>Most authors presently use the term "mudrock" to refer to all rocks composed dominantly of mud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blatt1980_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blatt1980-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prothero_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs2006_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs2006-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stow_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stow-7">[7]</a></sup> Mudrocks can be divided into siltstones, composed dominantly of silt-sized particles; mudstones with subequal mixture of silt- and clay-sized particles; and claystones, composed mostly of clay-sized particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blatt1980_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blatt1980-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prothero_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero-5">[5]</a></sup> Most authors use "<a href="/wiki/Shale" title="Shale">shale</a>" as a term for a <a href="/wiki/Fissility_(geology)" title="Fissility (geology)">fissile</a> mudrock (regardless of grain size) although some older literature uses the term "shale" as a synonym for mudrock.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biochemical_sedimentary_rocks">Biochemical sedimentary rocks</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:OilShaleEstonia.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="525" data-file-width="700" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/OilShaleEstonia.jpg/220px-OilShaleEstonia.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/OilShaleEstonia.jpg/330px-OilShaleEstonia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/OilShaleEstonia.jpg/440px-OilShaleEstonia.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:OilShaleEstonia.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Outcrop of <a href="/wiki/Ordovician" title="Ordovician">Ordovician</a> oil shale (<a href="/wiki/Kukersite" title="Kukersite">kukersite</a>), northern Estonia</div></div></div>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Biochemical_sedimentary_rocks&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Biochemical sedimentary rocks (page does not exist)">Biochemical sedimentary rocks</a> are created when organisms use materials dissolved in air or water to build their tissue. Examples include:
</p>
<ul><li>Most types of <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> are formed from the calcareous skeletons of organisms such as <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mollusk" title="Mollusk">mollusks</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Foraminifera" title="Foraminifera">foraminifera</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">Coal</a>, formed from plants that have removed <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> from the atmosphere and combined it with other elements to build their tissue.</li>
<li>Deposits of <a href="/wiki/Chert" title="Chert">chert</a> formed from the accumulation of siliceous skeletons of microscopic organisms such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiolarian" title="Radiolarian">radiolaria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diatom" title="Diatom">diatoms</a>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_sedimentary_rocks">Chemical sedimentary rocks</span></h3>
<p>Chemical sedimentary rock forms when mineral constituents in <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solution</a> become <a href="/wiki/Supersaturation" title="Supersaturation">supersaturated</a> and inorganically <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitate" title="Precipitate">precipitate</a>. Common chemical sedimentary rocks include <a href="/wiki/Oolite" title="Oolite">oolitic limestone</a> and rocks composed of <a href="/wiki/Evaporite" title="Evaporite">evaporite</a> minerals, such as <a href="/wiki/Halite" title="Halite">halite</a> (rock salt), <a href="/wiki/Sylvite" title="Sylvite">sylvite</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barite" title="Barite">barite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a>.
</p>
<h3><span id=".22Other.22_sedimentary_rocks"></span><span class="mw-headline" id='"Other"_sedimentary_rocks'>"Other" sedimentary rocks</span></h3>
<p>This fourth miscellaneous category includes rocks formed by <a href="/wiki/Pyroclastic_flow" title="Pyroclastic flow">Pyroclastic flows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Breccia#Impact" title="Breccia">impact breccias</a>, <a href="/wiki/Breccia#Volcanic" title="Breccia">volcanic breccias</a>, and other relatively uncommon processes.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compositional_classification_schemes">Compositional classification schemes</span></h2>
<p>Alternatively, sedimentary rocks can be subdivided into compositional groups based on their mineralogy:
</p>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Clastic_rock" title="Clastic rock">Siliciclastic sedimentary rocks</a></b>, are dominantly composed of silicate minerals. The sediment that makes up these rocks was transported as <a href="/wiki/Sediment_transport#Bed_Load" title="Sediment transport">bed load</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sediment_transport#Suspended_load" title="Sediment transport">suspended load</a>, or by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sediment_gravity_flows" title="Sediment gravity flows">sediment gravity flows</a>. Siliciclastic sedimentary rocks are subdivided into <a href="/wiki/Conglomerate_(geology)" title="Conglomerate (geology)">conglomerates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Breccia" title="Breccia">breccias</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mudrock" title="Mudrock">mudrocks</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Carbonate_rock" title="Carbonate rock">Carbonate sedimentary rocks</a></b> are composed of calcite (rhombohedral <span class="chemf nowrap">CaCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>), aragonite (orthorhombic <span class="chemf nowrap">CaCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>), dolomite (<span class="chemf nowrap">CaMg(CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>), and other carbonate minerals based on the <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> ion. Common examples include <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dolostone" title="Dolostone">dolostone</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Evaporite" title="Evaporite">Evaporite sedimentary rocks</a></b> are composed of minerals formed from the evaporation of water. The most common evaporite minerals are <a href="/wiki/Carbonate_minerals" title="Carbonate minerals">carbonates</a> (calcite and others based on <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>), <a href="/wiki/Chlorine#Occurrence" title="Chlorine">chlorides</a> (<a href="/wiki/Halite" title="Halite">halite</a> and others built on <span class="chemf nowrap">Cl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulfate_mineral" title="Sulfate mineral">sulfates</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a> and others built on <span class="chemf nowrap">SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>). Evaporite rocks commonly include abundant <a href="/wiki/Halite" title="Halite">halite</a> (rock salt), <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anhydrite" title="Anhydrite">anhydrite</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Organic-rich_sedimentary_rocks" title="Organic-rich sedimentary rocks">Organic-rich sedimentary rocks</a></b> have significant amounts of organic material, generally in excess of 3% total organic carbon. Common examples include <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oil_shale" title="Oil shale">oil shale</a> as well as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Source_rocks&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Source rocks (page does not exist)">source rocks</a> for oil and natural gas.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Siliceous_rock" title="Siliceous rock">Siliceous sedimentary rocks</a></b> are almost entirely composed of silica (<span class="chemf nowrap">SiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>), typically as <a href="/wiki/Chert" title="Chert">chert</a>, <a href="/wiki/Opal" title="Opal">opal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chalcedony" title="Chalcedony">chalcedony</a> or other microcrystalline forms.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Iron-rich_sedimentary_rocks" title="Iron-rich sedimentary rocks">Iron-rich sedimentary rocks</a></b> are composed of &gt;15% iron; the most common forms are <a href="/wiki/Banded_iron_formation" title="Banded iron formation">banded iron formations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ironstone" title="Ironstone">ironstones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prothero_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Phosphorite" title="Phosphorite">Phosphatic sedimentary rocks</a></b> are composed of phosphate minerals and contain more than 6.5% <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>; examples include deposits of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphate_nodule" title="Phosphate nodule">phosphate nodules</a>, bone beds, and phosphatic mudrocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs2006_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs2006-6">[6]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Deposition_and_transformation">Deposition and transformation</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sediment_transport_and_deposition">Sediment transport and deposition</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Logan_Formation_Cross_Bedding_Scour.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2140" data-file-width="2716" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Logan_Formation_Cross_Bedding_Scour.jpg/220px-Logan_Formation_Cross_Bedding_Scour.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Logan_Formation_Cross_Bedding_Scour.jpg/330px-Logan_Formation_Cross_Bedding_Scour.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Logan_Formation_Cross_Bedding_Scour.jpg/440px-Logan_Formation_Cross_Bedding_Scour.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Logan_Formation_Cross_Bedding_Scour.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cross-bedding and scour in a fine <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Logan_Formation" title="Logan Formation">Logan Formation</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mississippian_age" title="Mississippian age">Mississippian</a>) of Jackson County, <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a></div></div></div>
<p>Sedimentary <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rocks</a> are formed when <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a> is <a href="/wiki/Deposition_(geology)" title="Deposition (geology)">deposited</a> out of air, ice, wind, gravity, or water flows carrying the particles in <a href="/wiki/Suspension_(chemistry)" title="Suspension (chemistry)">suspension</a>. This sediment is often formed when <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> break down a rock into loose material in a source area. The material is then <a href="/wiki/Sediment_transport" title="Sediment transport">transported</a> from the source area to the deposition area. The type of sediment transported depends on the geology of the <a href="/wiki/Hinterland" title="Hinterland">hinterland</a> (the source area of the sediment). However, some sedimentary rocks, such as <a href="/wiki/Evaporite" title="Evaporite">evaporites</a>, are composed of material that form at the place of deposition. The nature of a sedimentary rock, therefore, not only depends on the sediment supply, but also on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sedimentary_depositional_environment" title="Sedimentary depositional environment">sedimentary depositional environment</a> in which it formed.
</p>
<h3><span id="Transformation_.28Diagenesis.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Transformation_(Diagenesis)">Transformation (Diagenesis)</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pressure_solution_sandstone.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="560" data-file-width="1100" height="112" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Pressure_solution_sandstone.svg/220px-Pressure_solution_sandstone.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Pressure_solution_sandstone.svg/330px-Pressure_solution_sandstone.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Pressure_solution_sandstone.svg/440px-Pressure_solution_sandstone.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pressure_solution_sandstone.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Pressure_solution" title="Pressure solution">Pressure solution</a> at work in a <a href="/wiki/Clastic_rock" title="Clastic rock">clastic rock</a>. While material dissolves at places where grains are in contact, that material may recrystallize from the solution and act as cement in open pore spaces. As a result, there is a net flow of material from areas under high stress to those under low stress, producing a sedimentary rock that is more compact and harder. Loose sand can become sandstone in this way.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Diagenesis" title="Diagenesis">diagenesis</a></div>
<p>The term <a href="/wiki/Diagenesis" title="Diagenesis">diagenesis</a> is used to describe all the chemical, physical, and biological changes, exclusive of surface weathering, undergone by a sediment after its initial deposition. Some of those processes cause the sediment to <a href="/wiki/Consolidation_(soil)" title="Consolidation (soil)">consolidate</a> into a compact, solid substance from the originally loose material. Young sedimentary rocks, especially those of <a href="/wiki/Quaternary" title="Quaternary">Quaternary</a> age (the most recent period of the <a href="/wiki/Geologic_time_scale" title="Geologic time scale">geologic time scale</a>) are often still unconsolidated. As sediment deposition builds up, the <a href="/wiki/Overburden_pressure" title="Overburden pressure">overburden (lithostatic) pressure</a> rises, and a process known as <a href="/wiki/Lithification" title="Lithification">lithification</a> takes place.
</p><p>Sedimentary rocks are often saturated with seawater or <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a>, in which minerals can dissolve, or from which minerals can <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(chemistry)" title="Precipitation (chemistry)">precipitate</a>. Precipitating minerals reduce the <a href="/wiki/Porosity" title="Porosity">pore space</a> in a rock, a process called <a href="/wiki/Cementation_(geology)" title="Cementation (geology)">cementation</a>. Due to the decrease in pore space, the original <a href="/wiki/Connate_fluids" title="Connate fluids">connate fluids</a> are expelled. The precipitated minerals form a cement and make the rock more compact and <a href="/wiki/Competence_(geology)" title="Competence (geology)">competent</a>. In this way, loose clasts in a sedimentary rock can become "glued" together.
</p><p>When sedimentation continues, an older rock layer becomes buried deeper as a result. The lithostatic pressure in the rock increases due to the weight of the overlying sediment. This causes compaction, a process in which grains mechanically reorganize. Compaction is, for example, an important diagenetic process in clay, which can initially consist of 60% water. During compaction, this interstitial water is pressed out of pore spaces. Compaction can also be the result of dissolution of grains by <a href="/wiki/Pressure_solution" title="Pressure solution">pressure solution</a>. The dissolved material precipitates again in open pore spaces, which means there is a net flow of material into the pores. However, in some cases, a certain mineral dissolves and does not precipitate again. This process, called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leaching_(chemical_science)" title="Leaching (chemical science)">leaching</a>, increases pore space in the rock.
</p><p>Some <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemical</a> processes, like the activity of <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, can affect minerals in a rock and are therefore seen as part of diagenesis. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">Fungi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> (by their <a href="/wiki/Root" title="Root">roots</a>) and various other organisms that live beneath the surface can also influence diagenesis.
</p><p>Burial of rocks due to ongoing sedimentation leads to increased pressure and temperature, which stimulates certain chemical reactions. An example is the reactions by which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_material" title="Organic material">organic material</a> becomes <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a> or <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>. When temperature and pressure increase still further, the realm of diagenesis makes way for <a href="/wiki/Metamorphism" title="Metamorphism">metamorphism</a>, the process that forms <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphic rock</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Banded_Iron_Formation_Barberton.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1608" data-file-width="2732" height="129" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Banded_Iron_Formation_Barberton.jpg/220px-Banded_Iron_Formation_Barberton.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Banded_Iron_Formation_Barberton.jpg/330px-Banded_Iron_Formation_Barberton.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Banded_Iron_Formation_Barberton.jpg/440px-Banded_Iron_Formation_Barberton.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Banded_Iron_Formation_Barberton.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A piece of a <a href="/wiki/Banded_iron_formation" title="Banded iron formation">banded iron formation</a>, a type of rock that consists of alternating layers with <a href="/wiki/Iron(III)_oxide" title="Iron(III) oxide">iron(III) oxide</a> (red) and <a href="/wiki/Iron(II)_oxide" title="Iron(II) oxide">iron(II) oxide</a> (grey). BIFs were mostly formed during the <a href="/wiki/Precambrian" title="Precambrian">Precambrian</a>, when the atmosphere was not yet rich in oxygen. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Moories_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Moories Group (page does not exist)">Moories Group</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barberton_Greenstone_Belt" title="Barberton Greenstone Belt">Barberton Greenstone Belt</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a></div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Color">Color</span></h3>
<p>The color of a sedimentary rock is often mostly determined by <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, an element with two major oxides: <a href="/wiki/Iron(II)_oxide" title="Iron(II) oxide">iron(II) oxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iron(III)_oxide" title="Iron(III) oxide">iron(III) oxide</a>. Iron(II) oxide (FeO) only forms under low oxygen (<a href="/wiki/Anoxic_waters" title="Anoxic waters">anoxic</a>) circumstances and gives the rock a grey or greenish colour. Iron(III) oxide (Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) in a richer oxygen environment is often found in the form of the mineral <a href="/wiki/Hematite" title="Hematite">hematite</a> and gives the rock a reddish to brownish colour. In arid continental climates rocks are in direct contact with the atmosphere, and oxidation is an important process, giving the rock a red or orange colour. Thick sequences of red sedimentary rocks formed in arid climates are called <a href="/wiki/Red_beds" title="Red beds">red beds</a>. However, a red colour does not necessarily mean the rock formed in a continental environment or arid climate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levin_p57_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Levin_p57-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The presence of organic material can colour a rock black or grey. Organic material is formed from dead organisms, mostly plants. Normally, such material eventually <a href="/wiki/Decomposition" title="Decomposition">decays</a> by oxidation or bacterial activity. Under anoxic circumstances, however, organic material cannot decay and leaves a dark sediment, rich in organic material. This can, for example, occur at the bottom of deep seas and lakes. There is little water mixing in such environments; as a result, oxygen from surface water is not brought down, and the deposited sediment is normally a fine dark clay. Dark rocks, rich in organic material, are therefore often <a href="/wiki/Shale" title="Shale">shales</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levin_p57_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Levin_p57-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Texture">Texture</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sorting_in_sediment.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="315" data-file-width="670" height="103" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Sorting_in_sediment.svg/220px-Sorting_in_sediment.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Sorting_in_sediment.svg/330px-Sorting_in_sediment.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Sorting_in_sediment.svg/440px-Sorting_in_sediment.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sorting_in_sediment.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diagram showing <a href="/wiki/Sorting_(sediment)" title="Sorting (sediment)">well-sorted</a> (left) and poorly sorted (right) grains</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Grain_size" title="Grain size">size</a>, form and orientation of clasts (the original pieces of rock) in a sediment is called its <a href="/wiki/Texture_(geology)" title="Texture (geology)">texture</a>. The texture is a small-scale property of a rock, but determines many of its large-scale properties, such as the <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a>, <a href="/wiki/Porosity" title="Porosity">porosity</a> or <a href="/wiki/Permeability_(earth_sciences)" title="Permeability (earth sciences)">permeability</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The 3D orientation of the clasts is called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fabric" title="Fabric">fabric</a> of the rock. Between the clasts, the rock can be composed of a <a href="/wiki/Matrix_(geology)" title="Matrix (geology)">matrix</a> (a cement) that consists of crystals of one or more precipitated minerals. The size and form of clasts can be used to determine the velocity and direction of <a href="/wiki/Current_(stream)" title="Current (stream)">current</a> in the sedimentary environment that moved the clasts from their origin; fine, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Calcareous_mud&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Calcareous mud (page does not exist)">calcareous mud</a> only settles in quiet water while gravel and larger clasts are moved only by rapidly moving water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The grain size of a rock is usually expressed with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wentworth_scale" title="Wentworth scale">Wentworth scale</a>, though alternative scales are sometimes used. The grain size can be expressed as a diameter or a volume, and is always an average value – a rock is composed of clasts with different sizes. The <a href="/wiki/Distribution_(mathematics)" title="Distribution (mathematics)">statistical distribution</a> of grain sizes is different for different rock types and is described in a property called the <a href="/wiki/Sorting_(sediment)" title="Sorting (sediment)">sorting</a> of the rock. When all clasts are more or less of the same size, the rock is called 'well-sorted', and when there is a large spread in grain size, the rock is called 'poorly sorted'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rounding_%26_sphericity_EN.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="310" data-file-width="743" height="92" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Rounding_%26_sphericity_EN.svg/220px-Rounding_%26_sphericity_EN.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Rounding_%26_sphericity_EN.svg/330px-Rounding_%26_sphericity_EN.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Rounding_%26_sphericity_EN.svg/440px-Rounding_%26_sphericity_EN.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rounding_%26_sphericity_EN.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diagram showing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rounding_(sediment)" title="Rounding (sediment)">rounding</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sphericity" title="Sphericity">sphericity</a> of grains</div></div></div>
<p>The form of the clasts can reflect the origin of the rock.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Coquina" title="Coquina">Coquina</a>, a rock composed of clasts of broken shells, can only form in energetic water. The form of a clast can be described by using four parameters:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>Surface texture</i> describes the amount of small-scale relief of the surface of a grain that is too small to influence the general shape.</li>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rounding_(sediment)" title="Rounding (sediment)">rounding</a></i> describes the general smoothness of the shape of a grain.</li>
<li>'<a href="/wiki/Sphericity" title="Sphericity">Sphericity</a>' describes the degree to which the grain approaches a <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">sphere</a>.</li>
<li>'<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Grain_form&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Grain form (page does not exist)">Grain form</a>' describes the three dimensional shape of the grain.</li></ul>
<p>Chemical sedimentary rocks have a non-clastic texture, consisting entirely of crystals. To describe such a texture, only the average size of the crystals and the fabric are necessary.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mineralogy">Mineralogy</span></h3>
<p>Most sedimentary rocks contain either <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a> (especially <a href="/wiki/Siliciclastic" title="Siliciclastic">siliciclastic</a> rocks) or <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a> (especially <a href="/wiki/Carbonate_rock" title="Carbonate rock">carbonate rocks</a>). In contrast to igneous and metamorphic rocks, a sedimentary rock usually contains very few different major minerals. However, the origin of the minerals in a sedimentary rock is often more complex than in an igneous rock. Minerals in a sedimentary rock can have formed by precipitation during sedimentation or by diagenesis. In the second case, the mineral precipitate can have grown over an older generation of cement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> A complex diagenetic history can be studied by <a href="/wiki/Optical_mineralogy" title="Optical mineralogy">optical mineralogy</a>, using a <a href="/wiki/Petrographic_microscope" title="Petrographic microscope">petrographic microscope</a>.
</p><p>Carbonate rocks dominantly consist of <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonate</a> minerals such as <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">aragonite</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dolomite" title="Dolomite">dolomite</a>. Both the cement and the clasts (including fossils and <a href="/wiki/Ooid" title="Ooid">ooids</a>) of a carbonate sedimentary rock can consist of carbonate minerals. The mineralogy of a clastic rock is determined by the material supplied by the source area, the manner of its transport to the place of deposition and the stability of that particular mineral. The resistance of rock-forming minerals to weathering is expressed by <a href="/wiki/Bowen%27s_reaction_series" title="Bowen's reaction series">Bowen's reaction series</a>. In this series, quartz is the most stable, followed by <a href="/wiki/Feldspar" title="Feldspar">feldspar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mica" title="Mica">micas</a>, and finally other less stable minerals that are only present when little weathering has occurred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The amount of weathering depends mainly on the distance to the source area, the local climate and the time it took for the sediment to be transported to the point where it is deposited. In most sedimentary rocks, mica, feldspar and less stable minerals have been reduced to <a href="/wiki/Clay_minerals" title="Clay minerals">clay minerals</a> like <a href="/wiki/Kaolinite" title="Kaolinite">kaolinite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illite" title="Illite">illite</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smectite" title="Smectite">smectite</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossils">Fossils</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fossils_in_a_beach_wall.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3888" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Fossils_in_a_beach_wall.JPG/220px-Fossils_in_a_beach_wall.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Fossils_in_a_beach_wall.JPG/330px-Fossils_in_a_beach_wall.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Fossils_in_a_beach_wall.JPG/440px-Fossils_in_a_beach_wall.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fossils_in_a_beach_wall.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fossil-rich layers in a sedimentary rock, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A%C3%B1o_Nuevo_State_Reserve" title="Año Nuevo State Reserve">Año Nuevo State Reserve</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a></div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossilisation" title="Fossilisation">fossilisation</a></div>
<p>Among the three major types of rock, fossils are most commonly found in sedimentary rock. Unlike most igneous and metamorphic rocks, sedimentary rocks form at temperatures and pressures that do not destroy fossil remnants. Often these fossils may only be visible under <a href="/wiki/Microscope" title="Microscope">magnification</a>.
</p><p>Dead organisms in nature are usually quickly removed by <a href="/wiki/Scavenger" title="Scavenger">scavengers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Decomposition" title="Decomposition">rotting</a> and erosion, but sedimentation can contribute to exceptional circumstances where these natural processes are unable to work, causing fossilisation. The chance of fossilisation is higher when the sedimentation rate is high (so that a carcass is quickly buried), in <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(environmental)" title="Hypoxia (environmental)">anoxic</a> environments (where little bacterial activity occurs) or when the organism had a particularly hard skeleton. Larger, well-preserved fossils are relatively rare.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Graafgang.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2304" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Graafgang.jpg/220px-Graafgang.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Graafgang.jpg/330px-Graafgang.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Graafgang.jpg/440px-Graafgang.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Graafgang.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Burrow" title="Burrow">Burrows</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Turbidite" title="Turbidite">turbidite</a>, made by <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=San_Vincente_Formation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="San Vincente Formation (page does not exist)">San Vincente Formation</a> (early <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a>) of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ainsa_Basin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ainsa Basin (page does not exist)">Ainsa Basin</a>, southern <a href="/wiki/Foreland_basin" title="Foreland basin">foreland</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a></div></div></div>
<p>Fossils can be both the direct remains or imprints of organisms and their skeletons. Most commonly preserved are the harder parts of organisms such as bones, shells, and the woody <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">tissue</a> of plants. Soft tissue has a much smaller chance of being fossilized, and the preservation of soft tissue of animals older than 40 million years is very rare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Imprints of organisms made while they were still alive are called <a href="/wiki/Trace_fossil" title="Trace fossil">trace fossils</a>, examples of which are <a href="/wiki/Burrow" title="Burrow">burrows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Footprint" title="Footprint">footprints</a>, etc.
</p><p>As a part of a sedimentary or metamorphic rock, fossils undergo the same <a href="/wiki/Diagenesis" title="Diagenesis">diagenetic processes</a> as does the containing rock. A shell consisting of calcite can, for example, dissolve while a cement of silica then fills the cavity. In the same way, precipitating minerals can fill cavities formerly occupied by <a href="/wiki/Blood_vessel" title="Blood vessel">blood vessels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vascular_tissue" title="Vascular tissue">vascular tissue</a> or other soft tissues. This preserves the form of the organism but changes the chemical composition, a process called <a href="/wiki/Permineralization" title="Permineralization">permineralization</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The most common minerals involved in permineralization are cements of <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonates</a> (especially calcite), forms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amorphous_silica" title="Amorphous silica">amorphous silica</a> (<a href="/wiki/Chalcedony" title="Chalcedony">chalcedony</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flint" title="Flint">flint</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chert" title="Chert">chert</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Pyrite" title="Pyrite">pyrite</a>. In the case of silica cements, the process is called <a href="/wiki/Lithification" title="Lithification">lithification</a>.
</p><p>At high pressure and temperature, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_material" title="Organic material">organic material</a> of a dead organism undergoes chemical reactions in which <a href="/wiki/Volatiles#Igneous_petrology" title="Volatiles">volatiles</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> are expulsed. The fossil, in the end, consists of a thin layer of pure carbon or its mineralized form, <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a>. This form of fossilisation is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonisation" title="Carbonisation">carbonisation</a>. It is particularly important for plant fossils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The same process is responsible for the formation of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a> like <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a> or <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>.
</p>

<p>Structures in sedimentary rocks can be divided into 'primary' structures (formed during deposition) and 'secondary' structures (formed after deposition). Unlike textures, structures are always large-scale features that can easily be studied in the field. <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_structures" title="Sedimentary structures">Sedimentary structures</a> can indicate something about the sedimentary environment or can serve to tell <a href="/wiki/Way_up_structure" title="Way up structure">which side originally faced up</a> where tectonics have tilted or overturned sedimentary layers.
</p><p>Sedimentary rocks are laid down in layers called <a href="/wiki/Bed_(geology)" title="Bed (geology)">beds</a> or <a href="/wiki/Stratum" title="Stratum">strata</a>. A bed is defined as a layer of rock that has a uniform <a href="/wiki/Lithology" title="Lithology">lithology</a> and texture. Beds form by the deposition of layers of sediment on top of each other. The sequence of beds that characterizes sedimentary rocks is called <a href="/wiki/Bed_(geology)" title="Bed (geology)">bedding</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Single beds can be a couple of centimetres to several meters thick. Finer, less pronounced layers are called laminae, and the structure a lamina forms in a rock is called <a href="/wiki/Lamination_(geology)" title="Lamination (geology)">lamination</a>. Laminae are usually less than a few centimetres thick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Though bedding and lamination are often originally horizontal in nature, this is not always the case. In some environments, beds are deposited at a (usually small) angle. Sometimes multiple sets of layers with different orientations exist in the same rock, a structure called <a href="/wiki/Cross-bedding" title="Cross-bedding">cross-bedding</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Cross-bedding forms when small-scale erosion occurs during deposition, cutting off part of the beds. Newer beds then form at an angle to older ones.
</p><p>The opposite of cross-bedding is parallel lamination, where all sedimentary layering is parallel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Differences in laminations are generally caused by cyclic changes in the sediment supply, caused, for example, by seasonal changes in rainfall, temperature or biochemical activity. Laminae that represent seasonal changes (similar to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tree_ring" title="Tree ring">tree rings</a>) are called <a href="/wiki/Varve" title="Varve">varves</a>. Any sedimentary rock composed of millimeter or finer scale layers can be named with the general term <i>laminite</i>. When sedimentary rocks have no lamination at all, their structural character is called massive bedding.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Graded_bedding" title="Graded bedding">Graded bedding</a> is a structure where beds with a smaller grain size occur on top of beds with larger grains. This structure forms when fast flowing water stops flowing. Larger, heavier clasts in suspension settle first, then smaller clasts. Although graded bedding can form in many different environments, it is a characteristic of <a href="/wiki/Turbidity_current" title="Turbidity current">turbidity currents</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>The surface of a particular bed, called the <a href="/wiki/Bedform" title="Bedform">bedform</a>, can be indicative of a particular sedimentary environment, too. Examples of bed forms include <a href="/wiki/Dune" title="Dune">dunes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ripple_marks" title="Ripple marks">ripple marks</a>. Sole markings, such as tool marks and flute casts, are groves dug into a sedimentary layer that are preserved. These are often elongated structures and can be used to establish the direction of the flow during deposition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ripple marks also form in flowing water. There are two types of ripples: symmetric and asymmetric. Environments where the current is in one direction, such as rivers, produce asymmetric ripples. The longer flank of such ripples is on the upstream side of the current.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Symmetric wave ripples occur in environments where currents reverse directions, such as tidal flats.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mudcrack" title="Mudcrack">Mudcracks</a> are a bed form caused by the dehydration of sediment that occasionally comes above the water surface. Such structures are commonly found at tidal flats or <a href="/wiki/Point_bar" title="Point bar">point bars</a> along rivers.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Secondary_sedimentary_structures">Secondary sedimentary structures</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HaliteCrystalMold.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="347" data-file-width="466" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/HaliteCrystalMold.jpg/220px-HaliteCrystalMold.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/HaliteCrystalMold.jpg/330px-HaliteCrystalMold.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/HaliteCrystalMold.jpg/440px-HaliteCrystalMold.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HaliteCrystalMold.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Halite" title="Halite">Halite</a> crystal mold in dolomite, Paadla Formation (<a href="/wiki/Silurian" title="Silurian">Silurian</a>), <a href="/wiki/Saaremaa" title="Saaremaa">Saaremaa</a>, Estonia</div></div></div>
<p>Secondary sedimentary structures are those which formed after deposition. Such structures form by chemical, physical and biological processes within the sediment. They can be indicators of circumstances after deposition. Some can be used as <a href="/wiki/Way_up_structure" title="Way up structure">way up criteria</a>.
</p><p>Organic materials in a sediment can leave more traces than just fossils. Preserved tracks and <a href="/wiki/Burrow" title="Burrow">burrows</a> are examples of <a href="/wiki/Trace_fossil" title="Trace fossil">trace fossils</a> (also called ichnofossils).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Such traces are relatively rare. Most trace fossils are burrows of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molluscs" title="Molluscs">molluscs</a> or <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a>. This burrowing is called <a href="/wiki/Bioturbation" title="Bioturbation">bioturbation</a> by sedimentologists. It can be a valuable indicator of the biological and ecological environment that existed after the sediment was deposited. On the other hand, the burrowing activity of organisms can destroy other (primary) structures in the sediment, making a reconstruction more difficult.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vuursteenknollen_in_krijtgesteente.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2304" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Vuursteenknollen_in_krijtgesteente.jpg/220px-Vuursteenknollen_in_krijtgesteente.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Vuursteenknollen_in_krijtgesteente.jpg/330px-Vuursteenknollen_in_krijtgesteente.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Vuursteenknollen_in_krijtgesteente.jpg/440px-Vuursteenknollen_in_krijtgesteente.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vuursteenknollen_in_krijtgesteente.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Chert" title="Chert">Chert</a> concretions in <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Middle_Lefkara_Formation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Middle Lefkara Formation (page does not exist)">Middle Lefkara Formation</a> (upper <a href="/wiki/Paleocene" title="Paleocene">Paleocene</a> to middle <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a>), <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a></div></div></div>
<p>Secondary structures can also form by <a href="/wiki/Diagenesis" title="Diagenesis">diagenesis</a> or the formation of a <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pedogenesis" title="Pedogenesis">pedogenesis</a>) when a sediment is exposed above the water level. An example of a diagenetic structure common in carbonate rocks is a <a href="/wiki/Stylolite" title="Stylolite">stylolite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Stylolites are irregular planes where material was dissolved into the pore fluids in the rock. This can result in the precipitation of a certain chemical species producing colouring and staining of the rock, or the formation of <a href="/wiki/Concretion" title="Concretion">concretions</a>. Concretions are roughly concentric bodies with a different composition from the host rock. Their formation can be the result of localized precipitation due to small differences in composition or porosity of the host rock, such as around fossils, inside burrows or around plant roots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In carbonate based rocks such as <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chert" title="Chert">chert</a> or <a href="/wiki/Flint" title="Flint">flint</a> concretions are common, while terrestrial sandstones can have iron concretions. Calcite concretions in clay are called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Septarian_nodule" title="Septarian nodule">septarian concretions</a>.
</p><p>After deposition, physical processes can <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deformation_(science)" title="Deformation (science)">deform</a> the sediment, producing a third class of secondary structures. Density contrasts between different sedimentary layers, such as between sand and clay, can result in <a href="/wiki/Flame_structure" title="Flame structure">flame structures</a> or <a href="/wiki/Load_cast" title="Load cast">load casts</a>, formed by inverted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diapirism" title="Diapirism">diapirism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> While the clastic bed is still fluid, diapirism can cause a denser upper layer to sink into a lower layer. Sometimes, density contrasts can result or grow when one of the lithologies dehydrates. Clay can be easily compressed as a result of dehydration, while sand retains the same volume and becomes relatively less dense. On the other hand, when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pore_fluid_pressure" title="Pore fluid pressure">pore fluid pressure</a> in a sand layer surpasses a critical point, the sand can break through overlying clay layers and flow through, forming <a href="/wiki/Discordant" title="Discordant">discordant</a> bodies of sedimentary rock called <a href="/wiki/Clastic_dike" title="Clastic dike">sedimentary dykes</a>. The same process can form <a href="/wiki/Mud_volcano" title="Mud volcano">mud volcanoes</a> on the surface where they broke through upper layers.
</p><p>Sedimentary dykes can also be formed in a cold climate where the soil is permanently frozen during a large part of the year. Frost weathering can form cracks in the soil that fill with rubble from above. Such structures can be used as climate indicators as well as way up structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>Density contrasts can also cause small-scale <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)" title="Fault (geology)">faulting</a>, even while sedimentation progresses (synchronous-sedimentary faulting).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Such faulting can also occur when large masses of non-lithified sediment are deposited on a slope, such as at the front side of a <a href="/wiki/River_delta" title="River delta">delta</a> or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_slope" title="Continental slope">continental slope</a>. Instabilities in such sediments can result in the deposited material to <a href="/wiki/Slump_(geology)" title="Slump (geology)">slump</a>, producing fissures and folding. The resulting structures in the rock are syn-sedimentary <a href="/wiki/Fold_(geology)" title="Fold (geology)">folds</a> and faults, which can be difficult to distinguish from folds and faults formed by <a href="/wiki/Tectonics" title="Tectonics">tectonic</a> forces acting on lithified rocks.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sedimentary_environments">Sedimentary environments</span></h2>
<p>The setting in which a sedimentary rock forms is called the sedimentary environment. Every environment has a characteristic combination of geologic processes and circumstances. The type of sediment that is deposited is not only dependent on the sediment that is transported to a place, but also on the environment itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">marine</a> environment means that the rock was formed in a <a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">sea</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a>. Often, a distinction is made between deep and shallow marine environments. Deep marine usually refers to environments more than 200 m below the water surface. Shallow marine environments exist adjacent to coastlines and can extend to the boundaries of the <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a>. The water movements in such environments have a generally higher energy than that in deep environments, as <a href="/wiki/Wind_wave" title="Wind wave">wave activity</a> diminishes with depth. This means that coarser sediment particles can be transported and the deposited sediment can be coarser than in deeper environments. When the sediment is transported from the continent, an alternation of <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silt</a> is deposited. When the continent is far away, the amount of such sediment deposited may be small, and biochemical processes dominate the type of rock that forms. Especially in warm climates, shallow marine environments far offshore mainly see deposition of carbonate rocks. The shallow, warm water is an ideal habitat for many small organisms that build carbonate skeletons. When these organisms die, their skeletons sink to the bottom, forming a thick layer of calcareous mud that may lithify into <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>. Warm shallow marine environments also are ideal environments for <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reefs</a>, where the sediment consists mainly of the calcareous skeletons of larger organisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>In deep marine environments, the water current working the sea bottom is small. Only fine particles can be transported to such places. Typically sediments depositing on the ocean floor are fine clay or small skeletons of micro-organisms. At 4 km depth, the solubility of carbonates increases dramatically (the depth zone where this happens is called the <a href="/wiki/Lysocline" title="Lysocline">lysocline</a>). Calcareous sediment that sinks below the lysocline dissolves; as a result, no limestone can be formed below this depth. Skeletons of micro-organisms formed of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">silica</a> (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiolarian" title="Radiolarian">radiolarians</a>) are not as soluble and still deposit. An example of a rock formed of silica skeletons is <a href="/wiki/Radiolarite" title="Radiolarite">radiolarite</a>. When the bottom of the sea has a small inclination, for example at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_slope" title="Continental slope">continental slopes</a>, the sedimentary cover can become unstable, causing <a href="/wiki/Turbidity_current" title="Turbidity current">turbidity currents</a>. Turbidity currents are sudden disturbances of the normally quite deep marine environment and can cause the geologically speaking instantaneous deposition of large amounts of sediment, such as sand and silt. The rock sequence formed by a turbidity current is called a <a href="/wiki/Turbidite" title="Turbidite">turbidite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>The coast is an environment dominated by wave action. At a <a href="/wiki/Beach" title="Beach">beach</a>, dominantly denser sediment such as sand or <a href="/wiki/Gravel" title="Gravel">gravel</a>, often mingled with shell fragments, is deposited, while the silt and clay sized material is kept in mechanical suspension. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidal_flat" title="Tidal flat">Tidal flats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shoal" title="Shoal">shoals</a> are places that sometimes dry because of the <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tide</a>. They are often cross-cut by <a href="/wiki/Gully" title="Gully">gullies</a>, where the current is strong and the grain size of the deposited sediment is larger. Where rivers enter the body of water, either on a sea or lake coast, <a href="/wiki/River_delta" title="River delta">deltas</a> can form. These are large accumulations of sediment transported from the continent to places in front of the mouth of the river. Deltas are dominantly composed of clastic sediment (in contrast to chemical).
</p><p>A sedimentary rock formed on land has a continental sedimentary environment. Examples of continental environments are <a href="/wiki/Lagoon" title="Lagoon">lagoons</a>, lakes, <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Floodplain" title="Floodplain">floodplains</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alluvial_fan" title="Alluvial fan">alluvial fans</a>. In the quiet water of swamps, lakes and lagoons, fine sediment is deposited, mingled with organic material from dead plants and animals. In rivers, the energy of the water is much greater and can transport heavier clastic material. Besides transport by water, sediment can in continental environments also be transported by wind or glaciers. Sediment transported by wind is called <a href="/wiki/Aeolian_processes" title="Aeolian processes">aeolian</a> and is always <a href="/wiki/Sorting_(sediment)" title="Sorting (sediment)">very well sorted</a>, while sediment transported by a glacier is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glacial_till" title="Glacial till">glacial till</a> and is characterized by very poor sorting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>Aeolian deposits can be quite striking. The depositional environment of the <a href="/wiki/Touchet_Formation" title="Touchet Formation">Touchet Formation</a>, located in the <a href="/wiki/Northwestern_United_States" title="Northwestern United States">Northwestern United States</a>, had intervening periods of aridity which resulted in a series of <a href="/wiki/Rhythmite" title="Rhythmite">rhythmite</a> layers. Erosional cracks were later infilled with layers of soil material, especially from <a href="/wiki/Aeolian_processes" title="Aeolian processes">aeolian processes</a>. The infilled sections formed vertical inclusions in the horizontally deposited layers of the Touchet Formation, and thus provided evidence of the events that intervened over time among the forty-one layers that were deposited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sedimentary_facies">Sedimentary facies</span></h3>
<p>Sedimentary environments usually exist alongside each other in certain natural successions. A beach, where sand and gravel is deposited, is usually bounded by a deeper marine environment a little offshore, where finer sediments are deposited at the same time. Behind the beach, there can be <a href="/wiki/Dune" title="Dune">dunes</a> (where the dominant deposition is well sorted sand) or a <a href="/wiki/Lagoon" title="Lagoon">lagoon</a> (where fine clay and organic material is deposited). Every sedimentary environment has its own characteristic deposits. The typical rock formed in a certain environment is called its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sedimentary_facies" title="Sedimentary facies">sedimentary facies</a>. When sedimentary strata accumulate through time, the environment can shift, forming a change in facies in the subsurface at one location. On the other hand, when a rock layer with a certain age is followed laterally, the <a href="/wiki/Lithology" title="Lithology">lithology</a> (the type of rock) and facies eventually change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Offlap_%26_onlap_EN.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="680" height="192" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Offlap_%26_onlap_EN.svg/290px-Offlap_%26_onlap_EN.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Offlap_%26_onlap_EN.svg/435px-Offlap_%26_onlap_EN.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Offlap_%26_onlap_EN.svg/580px-Offlap_%26_onlap_EN.svg.png 2x" width="290"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Offlap_%26_onlap_EN.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Shifting sedimentary facies in the case of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transgression_(geology)" title="Transgression (geology)">transgression</a> (above) and <a href="/wiki/Marine_regression" title="Marine regression">regression</a> of the sea (below)</div></div></div>
<p>Facies can be distinguished in a number of ways: the most common are by the lithology (for example: limestone, siltstone or sandstone) or by <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> content. <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">Coral</a>, for example, only lives in warm and shallow marine environments and fossils of coral are thus typical for shallow marine facies. Facies determined by lithology are called <a href="/wiki/Facies" title="Facies">lithofacies</a>; facies determined by fossils are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biofacies" title="Biofacies">biofacies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sedimentary environments can shift their geographical positions through time. Coastlines can shift in the direction of the sea when the <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a> drops, when the surface rises due to tectonic forces in the Earth's crust or when a river forms a large delta. In the subsurface, such geographic shifts of sedimentary environments of the past are recorded in shifts in sedimentary facies. This means that sedimentary facies can change either parallel or perpendicular to an imaginary layer of rock with a fixed age, a phenomenon described by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walther%27s_Law" title="Walther's Law">Walther's Law</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>The situation in which coastlines move in the direction of the continent is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transgression_(geology)" title="Transgression (geology)">transgression</a>. In the case of transgression, deeper marine facies are deposited over shallower facies, a succession called <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Onlap_(geology)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Onlap (geology) (page does not exist)">onlap</a>. <a href="/wiki/Marine_regression" title="Marine regression">Regression</a> is the situation in which a coastline moves in the direction of the sea. With regression, shallower facies are deposited on top of deeper facies, a situation called <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Offlap&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Offlap (page does not exist)">offlap</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>The facies of all rocks of a certain age can be plotted on a map to give an overview of the <a href="/wiki/Palaeogeography" title="Palaeogeography">palaeogeography</a>. A sequence of maps for different ages can give an insight in the development of the regional geography.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sedimentary_basins">Sedimentary basins</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_basin" title="Sedimentary basin">sedimentary basin</a></div>
<p>Places where large-scale sedimentation takes place are called <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_basin" title="Sedimentary basin">sedimentary basins</a>. The amount of sediment that can be deposited in a basin depends on the depth of the basin, the so-called <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Accommodation_space&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Accommodation space (page does not exist)">accommodation space</a>. The depth, shape and size of a basin depend on <a href="/wiki/Tectonics" title="Tectonics">tectonics</a>, movements within the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>. Where the lithosphere moves upward (<a href="/wiki/Tectonic_uplift" title="Tectonic uplift">tectonic uplift</a>), land eventually rises above sea level, so that and <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> removes material, and the area becomes a source for new sediment. Where the lithosphere moves downward (<a href="/wiki/Tectonic_subsidence" title="Tectonic subsidence">tectonic subsidence</a>), a basin forms and sedimentation can take place. When the lithosphere keeps subsiding, new accommodation space keeps being created.
</p><p>A type of basin formed by the moving apart of two pieces of a continent is called a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rift_basin" title="Rift basin">rift basin</a>. Rift basins are elongated, narrow and deep basins. Due to divergent movement, the lithosphere is <a href="/wiki/Deformation_(mechanics)" title="Deformation (mechanics)">stretched</a> and thinned, so that the hot <a href="/wiki/Asthenosphere" title="Asthenosphere">asthenosphere</a> rises and heats the overlying rift basin. Apart from continental sediments, rift basins normally also have part of their infill consisting of <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_rock" title="Volcanic rock">volcanic deposits</a>. When the basin grows due to continued stretching of the lithosphere, the <a href="/wiki/Rift" title="Rift">rift</a> grows and the sea can enter, forming marine deposits.
</p><p>When a piece of lithosphere that was heated and stretched cools again, its <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> rises, causing <a href="/wiki/Isostasy" title="Isostasy">isostatic</a> subsidence. If this subsidence continues long enough, the basin is called a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sag_basin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sag basin (page does not exist)">sag basin</a>. Examples of sag basins are the regions along <a href="/wiki/Passive_margin" title="Passive margin">passive</a> <a href="/wiki/Continental_margin" title="Continental margin">continental margins</a>, but sag basins can also be found in the interior of continents. In sag basins, the extra weight of the newly deposited sediments is enough to keep the subsidence going in a <a href="/wiki/Virtuous_circle_and_vicious_circle" title="Virtuous circle and vicious circle">vicious circle</a>. The total thickness of the sedimentary infill in a sag basins can thus exceed 10 km.
</p><p>A third type of basin exists along <a href="/wiki/Convergent_boundary" title="Convergent boundary">convergent plate boundaries</a> – places where one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic_plate" title="Tectonic plate">tectonic plate</a> moves under another into the asthenosphere. The <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subducting</a> plate bends and forms a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fore-arc_basin" title="Fore-arc basin">fore-arc basin</a> in front of the overriding plate—an elongated, deep asymmetric basin. Fore-arc basins are filled with deep marine deposits and thick sequences of turbidites. Such infill is called <a href="/wiki/Flysch" title="Flysch">flysch</a>. When the convergent movement of the two plates results in <a href="/wiki/Continental_collision" title="Continental collision">continental collision</a>, the basin becomes shallower and develops into a <a href="/wiki/Foreland_basin" title="Foreland basin">foreland basin</a>. At the same time, tectonic uplift forms a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mountain_belt" title="Mountain belt">mountain belt</a> in the overriding plate, from which large amounts of material are eroded and transported to the basin. Such erosional material of a growing mountain chain is called <a href="/wiki/Molasse" title="Molasse">molasse</a> and has either a shallow marine or a continental facies.
</p><p>At the same time, the growing weight of the mountain belt can cause isostatic subsidence in the area of the overriding plate on the other side to the mountain belt. The basin type resulting from this subsidence is called a <a href="/wiki/Back-arc_basin" title="Back-arc basin">back-arc basin</a> and is usually filled by shallow marine deposits and molasse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Blue_lias_cliffs_at_Lyme_Regis.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1972" data-file-width="3195" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Blue_lias_cliffs_at_Lyme_Regis.jpg/220px-Blue_lias_cliffs_at_Lyme_Regis.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Blue_lias_cliffs_at_Lyme_Regis.jpg/330px-Blue_lias_cliffs_at_Lyme_Regis.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Blue_lias_cliffs_at_Lyme_Regis.jpg/440px-Blue_lias_cliffs_at_Lyme_Regis.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Blue_lias_cliffs_at_Lyme_Regis.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cyclic alternation of <a href="/wiki/Competence_(geology)" title="Competence (geology)">competent</a> and less competent beds in the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Lias" title="Blue Lias">Blue Lias</a> at <a href="/wiki/Lyme_Regis" title="Lyme Regis">Lyme Regis</a>, southern England</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_of_astronomical_cycles">Influence of astronomical cycles</span></h3>
<p>In many cases facies changes and other lithological features in sequences of sedimentary rock have a cyclic nature. This cyclic nature was caused by cyclic changes in sediment supply and the sedimentary environment. Most of these cyclic changes are caused by <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomic</a> cycles. Short astronomic cycles can be the difference between the <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tides</a> or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spring_tide" title="Spring tide">spring tide</a> every two weeks. On a larger time-scale, cyclic changes in climate and sea level are caused by <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">Milankovitch cycles</a>: cyclic changes in the orientation and/or position of the Earth's rotational axis and orbit around the Sun. There are a number of Milankovitch cycles known, lasting between 10,000 and 200,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>Relatively small changes in the orientation of the Earth's axis or length of the seasons can be a major influence on the Earth's climate. An example are the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">ice ages of the past 2.6 million years</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary" title="Quaternary">Quaternary</a> <a href="/wiki/Geologic_time_scale" title="Geologic time scale">period</a>), which are assumed to have been caused by astronomic cycles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Climate change can influence the global sea level (and thus the amount of accommodation space in sedimentary basins) and sediment supply from a certain region. Eventually, small changes in astronomic parameters can cause large changes in sedimentary environment and sedimentation.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sedimentation_rates">Sedimentation rates</span></h2>
<p>The rate at which sediment is deposited differs depending on the location. A channel in a tidal flat can see the deposition of a few metres of sediment in one day, while on the deep ocean floor each year only a few millimetres of sediment accumulate. A distinction can be made between normal sedimentation and sedimentation caused by catastrophic processes. The latter category includes all kinds of sudden exceptional processes like <a href="/wiki/Mass_wasting" title="Mass wasting">mass movements</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rock_slide" title="Rock slide">rock slides</a> or <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flooding</a>. Catastrophic processes can see the sudden deposition of a large amount of sediment at once. In some sedimentary environments, most of the total column of sedimentary rock was formed by catastrophic processes, even though the environment is usually a quiet place. Other sedimentary environments are dominated by normal, ongoing sedimentation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reading17_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reading17-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>In many cases, sedimentation occurs slowly. In a <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a>, for example, the wind deposits siliciclastic material (sand or silt) in some spots, or catastrophic flooding of a <a href="/wiki/Wadi" title="Wadi">wadi</a> may cause sudden deposits of large quantities of detrital material, but in most places eolian erosion dominates. The amount of sedimentary rock that forms is not only dependent on the amount of supplied material, but also on how well the material consolidates. Erosion removes most deposited sediment shortly after deposition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reading17_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reading17-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stratigraphy">Stratigraphy</span></h2>

From top to bottom: Rounded tan domes of the <a href="/wiki/Navajo_Sandstone" title="Navajo Sandstone">Navajo Sandstone</a>, layered red <a href="/wiki/Kayenta_Formation" title="Kayenta Formation">Kayenta Formation</a>, cliff-forming, vertically jointed, red <a href="/wiki/Wingate_Sandstone" title="Wingate Sandstone">Wingate Sandstone</a>, slope-forming, purplish <a href="/wiki/Chinle_Formation" title="Chinle Formation">Chinle Formation</a>, layered, lighter-red <a href="/wiki/Moenkopi_Formation" title="Moenkopi Formation">Moenkopi Formation</a>, and white, layered <a href="/wiki/Cutler_Formation" title="Cutler Formation">Cutler Formation</a> sandstone. Picture from <a href="/wiki/Glen_Canyon_National_Recreation_Area" title="Glen Canyon National Recreation Area">Glen Canyon National Recreation Area</a>, Utah.</div></div></div>
<p>That new rock layers are above older rock layers is stated in the <a href="/wiki/Law_of_superposition" title="Law of superposition">principle of superposition</a>. There are usually some gaps in the sequence called <a href="/wiki/Unconformity" title="Unconformity">unconformities</a>. These represent periods where no new sediments were laid down, or when earlier sedimentary layers were raised above sea level and eroded away.
</p><p>Sedimentary rocks contain important information about the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_the_Earth" title="History of the Earth">history of the Earth</a>. They contain <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a>, the preserved remains of ancient <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>. Coal is considered a type of sedimentary rock. The composition of sediments provides us with clues as to the original rock. Differences between successive layers indicate changes to the environment over time. Sedimentary rocks can contain fossils because, unlike most igneous and metamorphic rocks, they form at temperatures and pressures that do not destroy fossil remains.
</p>
