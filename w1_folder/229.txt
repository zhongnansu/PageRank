<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Aryl</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about the aryl organic functional group. For the fleshy covering of certain seeds, see <a href="/wiki/Aril" title="Aril">aril</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Phenyl-group.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="688" data-file-width="1126" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Phenyl-group.png/250px-Phenyl-group.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Phenyl-group.png/375px-Phenyl-group.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Phenyl-group.png/500px-Phenyl-group.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Phenyl-group.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Phenyl_group" title="Phenyl group">phenyl group</a> is the simplest <i>aryl group</i>, here bonded to an "R" group.</div></div></div>
<p>In the context of organic molecules, <b>aryl</b> is any <a href="/wiki/Functional_group" title="Functional group">functional group</a> or <a href="/wiki/Substituent" title="Substituent">substituent</a> derived from an <a href="/wiki/Aromaticity" title="Aromaticity">aromatic ring</a>, usually an <a href="/wiki/Aromatic_hydrocarbon" title="Aromatic hydrocarbon">aromatic hydrocarbon</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phenyl" title="Phenyl">phenyl</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naphthyl" title="Naphthyl">naphthyl</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> "Aryl" is used for the sake of abbreviation or generalization, and "Ar" is used as a placeholder for the aryl group in chemical structure diagrams.
</p><p>A simple aryl group is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phenyl" title="Phenyl">phenyl</a> (with the chemical formula C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>), a group derived from <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a>. Examples of other aryl groups consist of:
</p>
<ul><li>The tolyl group, CH<sub>3</sub>C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>4</sub>, which is derived from <a href="/wiki/Toluene" title="Toluene">toluene</a> (methylbenzene)</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xylyl" title="Xylyl">xylyl</a> group, (CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>3</sub>, which is derived from <a href="/wiki/Xylene" title="Xylene">xylene</a> (dimethylbenzene)</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naphthyl" title="Naphthyl">naphthyl</a> group, C<sub>10</sub>H<sub>8</sub>, which is derived from <a href="/wiki/Naphthalene" title="Naphthalene">naphthalene</a></li></ul>
<p><b>Arylation</b> is a chemical process in which an aryl group is attached to a substituent.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature">Nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aryl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The most basic aryl group is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phenyl" title="Phenyl">phenyl</a>, which is made up of a benzene ring with one hydrogen atom substituted for some substituent, and has the molecular formula C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>−. Note that phenyl groups are not the same as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benzyl" title="Benzyl">benzyl groups</a>, which consists of a phenyl group attached to a methyl group, and has the molecular formula C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>−.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Phenol-2D-skeletal.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1100" data-file-width="646" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Phenol-2D-skeletal.png/100px-Phenol-2D-skeletal.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Phenol-2D-skeletal.png/150px-Phenol-2D-skeletal.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Phenol-2D-skeletal.png/200px-Phenol-2D-skeletal.png 2x" width="100"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Phenol-2D-skeletal.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Phenol (or hydroxybenzene)</div></div></div>
<p>To name compounds containing phenyl groups, the phenyl group can be taken to be the parent hydrocarbon and being represented by the suffix "-benzene". Alternatively, the phenyl group could be treated as the substituent, being described within the name as "phenyl". This is usually done when the group attached to the phenyl group consists of six or more carbon atoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>As an example, consider a hydroxyl group connected to a phenyl group. In this case, if the phenyl group was taken as the parent hydrocarbon, the compound would be named hydroxybenzene. Alternatively, and more commonly, the hydroxyl group could be taken as the parent group (and the phenyl group treated as the substituent), resulting in the more familiar name <a href="/wiki/Phenol" title="Phenol">phenol</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aryl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrophilic_aromatic_substitution">Electrophilic aromatic substitution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aryl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Electrophilic aromatic substitution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Electrophilic_aromatic_substitution" title="Electrophilic aromatic substitution">Electrophilic aromatic substitution</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benzene_ring" title="Benzene ring">Benzene rings</a> have a delocalised <a href="/wiki/Pi_bond" title="Pi bond">pi-system</a> of electrons, which creates areas of high negative charge. This makes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aromatic_compound" title="Aromatic compound">aromatic compounds</a> more prone to attacks by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrophilic" title="Electrophilic">electrophilic</a> reagents. However, due to the high stability of benzene rings, they will only react with highly reactive electrophiles, and will only undergo <a href="/wiki/Substitution_reaction" title="Substitution reaction">substitution reactions</a> (but not <a href="/wiki/Addition_reaction" title="Addition reaction">addition reactions</a>). Benzene's unusual stability is explained by its ability to delocalise charges by resonance. Electrophilic aromatic substitution of benzene takes place in two main steps: electrophilic attack and proton loss. The image below summarizes the general mechanism of an electrophilic aromatic substitution reaction.
</p><p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mechanism_of_Electrophilic_Aromatic_Substitution.png" title="Mechanism of Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution"><img alt="Mechanism of Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution" data-file-height="786" data-file-width="1318" height="304" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Mechanism_of_Electrophilic_Aromatic_Substitution.png/510px-Mechanism_of_Electrophilic_Aromatic_Substitution.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Mechanism_of_Electrophilic_Aromatic_Substitution.png/765px-Mechanism_of_Electrophilic_Aromatic_Substitution.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Mechanism_of_Electrophilic_Aromatic_Substitution.png/1020px-Mechanism_of_Electrophilic_Aromatic_Substitution.png 2x" width="510"/></a>
</p><p>An example of such reaction is between bromine and benzene. In this reaction, a bromine atom will substitute a hydrogen atom in the benzene ring, giving <a href="/wiki/Bromobenzene" title="Bromobenzene">bromobenzene</a>, an aryl halide. However, due to the unreactive nature of benzene, catalyst such as aluminium chloride is needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clayden_et_al_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-clayden_et_al-4">[4]</a></sup> The formula for this reaction is:
</p>
<dl><dd>C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>6</sub> + 0.5 Br<sub>2</sub> → C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>Br</dd></dl>
<p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Electrophilic_Substitution_of_Bromine_on_Benzene.png" title="Electrophilic Substitution of Bromine on Benzene"><img alt="Electrophilic Substitution of Bromine on Benzene" data-file-height="197" data-file-width="727" height="135" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Electrophilic_Substitution_of_Bromine_on_Benzene.png/500px-Electrophilic_Substitution_of_Bromine_on_Benzene.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/78/Electrophilic_Substitution_of_Bromine_on_Benzene.png 1.5x" width="500"/></a>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Halogen-metal_exchange_of_aryl_halides">Halogen-metal exchange of aryl halides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aryl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Halogen-metal exchange of aryl halides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Organometallic_chemistry#Organometallic_compounds" title="Organometallic chemistry">Organometallic compounds</a> are compounds that have a bond between a carbon and a metal atom.
</p><p>The halogen atom of an <a href="/wiki/Aryl_halide" title="Aryl halide">aryl halide</a> atom could be exchanged for a metal atom using an organometallic reagent. An example of such reaction is the reaction between bromobenzene and an organolithium reagent, where there is a nucleophilic attack of the lithium cation on bromine. The formula to such reaction is: 
</p>
<dl><dd>C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>Br + C<sub>4</sub>H<sub>10</sub>Li → C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>Li + C<sub>4</sub>H<sub>10</sub>Br</dd></dl>
<p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Reaction_of_Organolithium_and_Aryl_Halide.png" title="Reaction of Organolithium and Aryl Halide"><img alt="Reaction of Organolithium and Aryl Halide" data-file-height="206" data-file-width="864" height="119" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Reaction_of_Organolithium_and_Aryl_Halide.png/500px-Reaction_of_Organolithium_and_Aryl_Halide.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Reaction_of_Organolithium_and_Aryl_Halide.png/750px-Reaction_of_Organolithium_and_Aryl_Halide.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1d/Reaction_of_Organolithium_and_Aryl_Halide.png 2x" width="500"/></a>
</p><p>The reaction is able to occur since benzene has a higher acidity compared to butane, which can be explained by the stability of the carbanion after the molecule loses a proton. The lithium-phenyl complex (C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>Li) can better delocalize the negative charge by resonating it around the benzene ring; whereas in a lithium-butyl complex (C<sub>4</sub>H<sub>10</sub>Li), the molecule is not capable of such resonance and is further destabilized by the fact that the negative charge is placed on a primary carbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clayden_et_al_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-clayden_et_al-4">[4]</a></sup> Hence, formation of the lithium-phenyl complex can be observed.
</p>
