<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Peter Newman (environmental scientist)</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Peter William Geoffrey Newman</b> (born 1945) is an environmental scientist, author and educator based in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perth,_Western_Australia" title="Perth, Western Australia">Perth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>. He is currently Professor of Sustainability at <a href="/wiki/Curtin_University" title="Curtin University">Curtin University</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and was awarded an Order of Australia for <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_transport" title="Sustainable transport">sustainable transport</a> and urban design in 2014. 
He has a <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">PhD</a> degree in Chemistry (1972, University of Western Australia) and completed post doctoral studies in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_Science" title="Environmental Science">Environmental Science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delft_University_of_Technology" title="Delft University of Technology">Delft University</a>, Dip EST, Environmental Science, 1972.
</p><p>Newman has written 20 books and over 330 papers on sustainable cities and is most known for creating the term ‘<a href="/wiki/Automobile_dependency" title="Automobile dependency">automobile dependence</a>’ in the second half of the 1980s. He was closely associated with the redevelopment of Perth's rail system from 1979 to the present, which is now seen as a model for how car dependent cities can change towards more sustainable transport. He is a Lead Author for Transport on the IPCC - <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career_and_influence">Career and influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Newman_(environmental_scientist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career and influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Newman is best known internationally for popularizing the term ‘<a href="/wiki/Automobile_dependency" title="Automobile dependency">automobile dependence</a>’ in the second half of the 1980s to explain how the cities of the time based on sprawling suburbs were inevitably leading to the growth in automobile use.  He led an international research project with colleague Jeff Kenworthy on transport practices and structures (original data collected on 33 global cities). The results were published in <i>Cities and Automobile Dependence: An International Sourcebook</i>,  which introduced the concept of <a href="/wiki/Automobile_dependency" title="Automobile dependency">car dependence</a> -  now a feature of planning literature and policy.  The two researchers later collaborated on the book <i>Sustainability and Cities: Overcoming Automobile Dependence</i> which was launched in the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> in 1999, as the President’s <a href="/wiki/Commission_on_Sustainable_Development" title="Commission on Sustainable Development">Council on Sustainable Development</a> was moving toward a more urban focus, and more recently The End of Automobile Dependence: How Cities are Moving Beyond Car-based Planning.
</p><p>In <i>Cities as Sustainable Ecosystems: Principles and Practices</i> written with Isabella Jennings, Newman shows how city residents can begin to reintegrate into their bioregional environment, and how cities themselves can be planned with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecological_sustainability" title="Ecological sustainability">ecological sustainability</a> in mind. Drawing on examples from many parts of the world, the authors show how urban redevelopment in some cities has involved harvesting rainwater, greening roofs, and producing <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a>. Other cities have biodiversity parks for endangered species, community gardens that support a connection to their foodshed, and pedestrian-friendly spaces that encourage walking and cycling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sust_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-sust-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Local.2C_State_and_Federal_Government"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Local,_State_and_Federal_Government">Local, State and Federal Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Newman_(environmental_scientist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Local, State and Federal Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Between 1976-80 Newman has served as a local government councillor in the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Fremantle" title="City of Fremantle">City of Fremantle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Curtin_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Curtin-3">[3]</a></sup> Newman has been a government advisor through three secondments to the Western Australian State Government. In the last secondment (2001–03), he was the Director of Sustainability Policy in the Department of Premier and Cabinet where he managed and wrote the State Sustainability Strategy: the first in the world at the state/province level.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 2004-2005 he was the New South Wales Sustainability Commissioner.
</p><p>Between 2008 and 2014 he was a member of <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure_Australia" title="Infrastructure Australia">Infrastructure Australia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Academia">Academia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Newman_(environmental_scientist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Academia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 1989 to 2007 Newman was Director of the Institute for Sustainability and Technology Policy, <a href="/wiki/Murdoch_University" title="Murdoch University">Murdoch University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Murdoch,_Western_Australia" title="Murdoch, Western Australia">Murdoch, Western Australia</a>. In 2007, Newman left Murdoch University to join Curtin University.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-test_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-test-5">[5]</a></sup> In 2006-07 he was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fulbright_Scholar" title="Fulbright Scholar">Fulbright Scholar</a> at the University of Virginia, Charlottesville.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_advisor">International advisor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Newman_(environmental_scientist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: International advisor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Peter Newman is member of the Global Research Network on Human Settlements Advisory Board and the Scientific Advisory Committee of the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <i>SCOPE Ecopolis Project</i>. He is Senior Consultant at <a href="/wiki/Jan_Gehl" title="Jan Gehl">Gehl Architects</a>, Copenhagen, Denmark.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
