<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Ammonia production</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">Ammonia</a> is one of the most highly produced inorganic chemicals. There are numerous large-scale <b>ammonia production</b> plants worldwide, producing a total of 131 million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tonnes" title="Tonnes">tonnes</a> of nitrogen (equivalent to 159 million tonnes of ammonia) in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> China produced 32.1% of the worldwide production, followed by India with 8.9%, Russia with 7.9%, and the United States with 6.3%. 80% or more of the ammonia produced is used for fertilizing agricultural crops. Ammonia is also used for the production of plastics, fibers, explosives,  nitric acid (via the <a href="/wiki/Ostwald_process" title="Ostwald process">Ostwald process</a>) and intermediates for dyes and pharmaceuticals.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ammonia_production&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before the start of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, most ammonia was obtained by the dry <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distillation</a> of nitrogenous vegetable and animal products; by the reduction of <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_acid" title="Nitrous acid">nitrous acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrite" title="Nitrite">nitrites</a> with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>; and also by the decomposition of ammonium salts by alkaline hydroxides or by <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">quicklime</a>, the salt most generally used being the chloride (<a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">sal-ammoniac</a>).
</p><p>Today, most ammonia is produced on a large scale by the <a href="/wiki/Haber_process" title="Haber process">Haber process</a> with capacities of up to 3,300 tonnes per day. In this process, N<sub>2</sub> and H<sub>2</sub> gases are allowed to react at pressures of 200 bar.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_ammonia-producing_plants">Modern ammonia-producing plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ammonia_production&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Modern ammonia-producing plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:311px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AmmoniaSynthesisDiagram.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="233" data-file-width="309" height="233" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/87/AmmoniaSynthesisDiagram.png" width="309"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AmmoniaSynthesisDiagram.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Block flow diagram of the ammonia synthesis process</div></div></div>
<p>A typical modern ammonia-producing plant first converts <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> (i.e., <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquified_petroleum_gas" title="Liquified petroleum gas">LPG</a> (liquefied petroleum gases such as <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Butane" title="Butane">butane</a>) or petroleum <a href="/wiki/Naphtha" title="Naphtha">naphtha</a> into gaseous <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>. The method for producing hydrogen from <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a> is known as <a href="/wiki/Steam_reforming" title="Steam reforming">steam reforming</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The hydrogen is then combined with nitrogen to produce <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> via the Haber-Bosch process.
</p><p>Starting with a <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> feedstock, the processes used in producing the hydrogen are:
</p>
<ul><li>The first step in the process is to remove <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> compounds from the feedstock because sulfur deactivates the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalysts</a> used in subsequent steps. Sulfur removal requires catalytic <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenation</a> to convert sulfur compounds in the feedstocks to gaseous <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>:</li></ul>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>H<sub>2</sub> + RSH →  RH + H<sub>2</sub>S(gas)</dd></dl></dd></dl>
<ul><li>The gaseous hydrogen sulfide is then adsorbed and removed by passing it through beds of <a href="/wiki/Zinc_oxide" title="Zinc oxide">zinc oxide</a> where it is converted to solid <a href="/wiki/Zinc_sulfide" title="Zinc sulfide">zinc sulfide</a>:</li></ul>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>H<sub>2</sub>S + ZnO → ZnS + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl></dd></dl>
<ul><li>Catalytic <a href="/wiki/Steam_reforming" title="Steam reforming">steam reforming</a> of the sulfur-free feedstock is then used to form hydrogen plus <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>:</li></ul>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>CH<sub>4</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O → CO + 3H<sub>2</sub></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<ul><li>The next step then uses catalytic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_gas_shift_reaction" title="Water gas shift reaction">shift conversion</a> to convert the carbon monoxide to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and more hydrogen:</li></ul>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>CO + H<sub>2</sub>O → CO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<ul><li>The carbon dioxide is then removed either by absorption in aqueous <a href="/wiki/Ethanolamine" title="Ethanolamine">ethanolamine</a> solutions or by adsorption in <a href="/wiki/Pressure_swing_adsorption" title="Pressure swing adsorption">pressure swing adsorbers</a> (PSA) using proprietary solid adsorption media.</li>
<li>The final step in producing the hydrogen is to use catalytic methanation to remove any small residual amounts of carbon monoxide or carbon dioxide from the hydrogen:</li></ul>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>CO + 3H<sub>2</sub> → CH<sub>4</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd>
<dd>CO<sub>2</sub> + 4H<sub>2</sub> → CH<sub>4</sub> +2H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p>To produce the desired end-product ammonia, the hydrogen is then catalytically reacted with nitrogen (derived from process air) to form anhydrous liquid ammonia. This step is known as the ammonia synthesis loop (also referred to as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haber-Bosch" title="Haber-Bosch">Haber-Bosch</a> process):
</p>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>3H<sub>2</sub> + N<sub>2</sub> → 2NH<sub>3</sub></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p>Due to the nature of the (typically multi-promoted magnetite) catalyst used in the ammonia synthesis reaction, only very low levels of oxygen-containing (especially CO, CO<sub>2</sub> and H<sub>2</sub>O) compounds can be tolerated in the synthesis (hydrogen and nitrogen mixture) gas. Relatively pure nitrogen can be obtained by <a href="/wiki/Air_separation" title="Air separation">Air separation</a>, but additional oxygen removal may be required.
</p><p>Because of relatively low single pass conversion rates (typically less than 20%), a large recycle stream is required. This can lead to the accumulation of inerts in the loop gas.
</p><p>The steam reforming, shift conversion, carbon dioxide removal and methanation steps each operate at absolute pressures of about 25 to 35 bar, and the ammonia synthesis loop operates at absolute pressures ranging from 60 to 180 bar depending upon which proprietary design is used. There are many engineering and construction companies that offer proprietary designs for ammonia synthesis plants.  <a href="/wiki/Haldor_Tops%C3%B8e_(company)" title="Haldor Topsøe (company)">Haldor Topsoe</a> of Denmark, Thyssenkrupp Industrial Solutions GmbH of Germany, Ammonia Casale of Switzerland and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kellogg_Brown_%26_Root" title="Kellogg Brown &amp; Root">Kellogg Brown &amp; Root</a> of the United States are among the most experienced companies in that field.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_ammonia_production">Sustainable ammonia production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ammonia_production&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sustainable ammonia production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Haber_process#Economic_and_environmental_aspects" title="Haber process">Haber process § Economic and environmental aspects</a></div>
<p>Ammonia production depends on plentiful supplies of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a>, predominantly <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>. Due to ammonia's critical role in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intensive_agriculture" title="Intensive agriculture">intensive agriculture</a> and other processes, sustainable production is desirable. This is possible by using <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> to generate hydrogen by <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> of water. This would be straightforward in a <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_economy" title="Hydrogen economy">hydrogen economy</a> by diverting some hydrogen production from fuel to feedstock use. For example, in 2002, <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_in_Iceland#Hydrogen" title="Renewable energy in Iceland">Iceland produced 2,000 tons of hydrogen gas by electrolysis</a>, using excess electricity production from its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroelectric" title="Hydroelectric">hydroelectric</a> plants, primarily for the production of ammonia for fertilizer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iceland_ammonia_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-iceland_ammonia-3">[3]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Vemork" title="Vemork">Vemork</a> hydroelectric plant in Norway used its surplus electricity output to <a href="/wiki/Birkeland%E2%80%93Eyde_process" title="Birkeland–Eyde process">generate renewable ammonia</a> from 1911 to 1971,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bradley_2004_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-bradley_2004-4">[4]</a></sup> requiring 15 MWh/Ton of nitric acid. The same reaction is carried out by lightning, providing a natural source for converting atmospheric nitrogen to soluble nitrates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In practice, natural gas will remain the major source of hydrogen for ammonia production as long as it is <a href="/wiki/Efficient-market_hypothesis" title="Efficient-market hypothesis">cheapest</a>.
</p><p>As an alternative to the relatively inefficient <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> can be generated from organic wastes (such as biomass or food-industry waste), using catalytic (thermal) reforming.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This releases hydrogen from carbonaceous substances at only 10-20% of energy used by electrolysis and may lead to hydrogen being produced from municipal wastes at below zero cost (allowing for the tipping fees and efficient <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_reforming" title="Catalytic reforming">catalytic reforming</a>, such as cold-plasma).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Catalytic reforming is possible in small, distributed (even mobile) plants, to take advantage of stranded biomass/biowastes or natural gas deposits. Conversion of such wastes into ammonia solves the problem of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_storage" title="Hydrogen storage">hydrogen storage</a>, as hydrogen can be released economically from ammonia on-demand, without the need for high-pressure or cryogenic storage. In 2018, a test was successfully performed by <a href="/wiki/CSIRO" title="CSIRO">CSIRO</a>, filling two different manufacturers' fuel cell vehicles,  with hydrogen extracted directly from ammonia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is also easier to store ammonia on-board of vehicles than to store hydrogen, as ammonia is less flammable than gasoline or LPG.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">Waste water</a> is often high in ammonia. Because discharging ammonia laden water into the environment, even in wastewater treatment plants, can cause problems, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simultaneous_nitrification-denitrification" title="Simultaneous nitrification-denitrification">nitrification</a> is often necessary to remove the ammonia<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>. This may be a potentially sustainable source of ammonia in the future because of its abundance and the need to <a href="/wiki/Denitrification" title="Denitrification">remove it from the water anyway</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Alternatively, ammonia from waste water is sent into an ammonia electrolyzer (ammonia electrolysis) operating with renewable energy sources (Solar PV and Wind turbine) to produce hydrogen and clean treated water <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>. Ammonia electrolysis may require much less thermodynamic energy than water electrolysis (only 0.06 V in alkaline media)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Byproducts">Byproducts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ammonia_production&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Byproducts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of the main industrial byproducts of ammonia production is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CO2" title="CO2">CO2</a>. In 2018, high oil prices resulted in an extended summer shutdown of European ammonia factories causing in a commercial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CO2" title="CO2">CO2</a> shortage, thus limiting production of carbonated drinks such as beer and fizzy soft drinks<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>.
</p>
