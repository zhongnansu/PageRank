<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Asphyxiant gas</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>An <b>asphyxiant gas</b> is a nontoxic or minimally toxic <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> which reduces or displaces the normal <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> concentration in breathing <a href="/wiki/Gas_composition" title="Gas composition">air</a>. Breathing of oxygen-depleted air can lead to death by <a href="/wiki/Asphyxia" title="Asphyxia">asphyxiation</a> (suffocation). Because asphyxiant gases are relatively <a href="/wiki/Inert_gas" title="Inert gas">inert</a> and odorless, their presence in high concentration may not be noticed, except in the case of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hypercapnia" title="Hypercapnia">hypercapnia</a>). 
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">Toxic</a> gases, by contrast, cause death by other mechanisms, such as competing with oxygen on the cellular level (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>) or directly damaging the <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_system" title="Respiratory system">respiratory system</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Phosgene" title="Phosgene">phosgene</a>). Far smaller quantities of these are deadly.
</p><p>Notable examples of asphyxiant gases are <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>, butane and propane. Along with trace gases such as carbon dioxide and <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>, these compose 79% of <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">Earth's atmosphere</a>.  The atmosphere is mostly harmless because the remaining 21% is <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O<sub>2</sub></a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Asphyxia_hazard">Asphyxia hazard</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asphyxiant_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Asphyxia hazard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Asphyxia" title="Asphyxia">Asphyxia</a></div>
<p>Asphyxiant gases in the breathing air are normally not hazardous. Only where elevated concentrations of asphyxiant gases displace the normal oxygen concentration does a hazard exist.  Examples are:
</p>
<ul><li>Environmental gas displacement
<ul><li>Confined spaces, combined with accidental gas leaks, such as <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mines</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Submarines" title="Submarines">submarines</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Refrigerator" title="Refrigerator">refrigerators</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gill_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-gill-4">[4]</a></sup> or other confined spaces<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fire_extinguisher" title="Fire extinguisher">Fire extinguisher</a> systems that flood spaces with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inert_gases" title="Inert gases">inert gases</a>, such as computer <a href="/wiki/Data_center" title="Data center">data centers</a> and sealed vaults<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gill_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-gill-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>Large-scale natural release of gas, such as during the <a href="/wiki/Lake_Nyos" title="Lake Nyos">Lake Nyos</a> disaster in which volcanically-released carbon dioxide killed 1,800 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>Release of <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> boiled off by the energy released in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnet_quench" title="Magnet quench">magnet quench</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider" title="Large Hadron Collider">Large Hadron Collider</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">magnetic resonance imaging</a> machine.</li>
<li>Climbing inside an inflatable balloon filled with helium<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li></ul></li>
<li>Direct administration of gas
<ul><li>Exclusive administration, such as inhaling the contents of a balloon filled with <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>Inadvertent administration of asphyxiant gas in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Respirators" title="Respirators">respirators</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>Use in <a href="/wiki/Suicide" title="Suicide">suicide</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Erotic_asphyxiation" title="Erotic asphyxiation">erotic asphyxiation</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li></ul></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Risk_management">Risk management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asphyxiant_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Risk management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The risk of breathing asphyxiant gases is frequently underestimated leading to fatalities, typically from breathing helium in domestic circumstances and nitrogen in industrial environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The term asphyxiation is often mistakenly associated with the strong desire to breathe that occurs if breathing is prevented. This desire is stimulated from increasing levels of carbon dioxide. However, asphyxiant gases may displace carbon dioxide along with oxygen, preventing the victim from feeling short of breath. In addition the gases may also displace oxygen from cells, leading to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loss_of_consciousness" title="Loss of consciousness">loss of consciousness</a> and death rapidly.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asphyxiant_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The handling of compressed asphyxiant gases and the determination of appropriate environment for their use is regulated in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">Occupational Safety and Health Administration</a> (OSHA).  The <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health" title="National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health">National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health</a> (NIOSH) has an advisory role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> OSHA requires employers who send workers into areas where the oxygen concentration is known or expected to be less than 19.5% to follow the provision of the Respiratory Protection Standard [29 CFR 1910.134]. Generally, work in an oxygen depleted environment requires an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SCBA" title="SCBA">SCBA</a> or airline respirator.  The regulation also requires an evaluation of the worker's ability to perform the work while wearing a respirator, the regular training of personnel, respirator <a href="/wiki/Respirator_fit_test" title="Respirator fit test">fit testing</a>, periodic workplace monitoring, and regular respirator maintenance, inspection, and cleaning."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>  Containers should be labeled according to OSHA's Hazard Communication Standard [29 CFR 1910.1200]. These regulations were developed in accordance with the official recommendations of the <a href="/wiki/Compressed_Gas_Association" title="Compressed Gas Association">Compressed Gas Association</a> (CGA) pamphlet P-1. The specific guidelines for prevention of asphyxiation due to displacement of oxygen by asphyxiant gases is covered under CGA's pamphlet SB-2, <i>Oxygen-Deficient Atmospheres.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Specific guidelines for use of gases other than air in back-up respirators is covered in pamphlet SB-28, <i>Safety of Instrument Air Systems Backed Up by Gases Other Than Air.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Odorized_gas">Odorized gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asphyxiant_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Odorized gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>To decrease the risk of asphyxiation, there have been proposals to add warning odors to some commonly used gases such as nitrogen and argon. However, CGA has  argued against this practice. They are concerned that odorizing may decrease worker vigilance, not everyone can smell the odorants, and assigning a different smell to each gas may be impractical.  Another difficulty is that most odorants (e.g., the <a href="/wiki/Thiol" title="Thiol">thiols</a>) are chemically reactive. This is not a problem with natural gas intended to be burned as fuel, which is routinely <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas#Added_odor" title="Natural gas">odorized</a>, but a major use of asphyxiants such as nitrogen, helium, argon and krypton is to protect reactive materials from the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_mining">In mining</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Asphyxiant_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In mining">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The dangers of excess concentrations of nontoxic gases has been recognized for centuries within the mining industry. The concept of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_damp" title="Black damp">black damp</a> (or "stythe") reflects an understanding that certain gaseous mixtures could lead to death with prolonged exposure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Early mining deaths due to mining fires and explosions were often a result of encroaching asphyxiant gases as the fires consumed available oxygen. Early self-contained <a href="/wiki/Respirator" title="Respirator">respirators</a> were designed by mining engineers such as <a href="/wiki/Henry_Fleuss" title="Henry Fleuss">Henry Fleuss</a> to help in rescue efforts after fires and floods. While <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_Canary" title="Atlantic Canary">canaries</a> were typically used to detect carbon monoxide, tools such as the <a href="/wiki/Davy_lamp" title="Davy lamp">Davy lamp</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Geordie_lamp" title="Geordie lamp">Geordie lamp</a> were useful for detecting <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, two asphyxiant gases. When methane was present, the lamp would burn higher; when carbon dioxide was present, the lamp would gutter or extinguish. Modern methods to detect asphyxiant gases in mines led to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mine_Safety_and_Health_Act_of_1977" title="Mine Safety and Health Act of 1977">Federal Mine Safety and Health Act of 1977</a> in the United States which established ventilation standards in which mines should be "...ventilated by a current of air containing not less than 19.5 volume per centum of oxygen, not more than 0.5 volume per centum of carbon dioxide..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
