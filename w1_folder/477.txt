<p><b>Gilbert Norman Plass</b> (22 March 1920 – 1 March 2004) was a <a href="/wiki/Canadians" title="Canadians">Canadian</a> physicist who in the 1950s made predictions about the increase in global atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) levels in the 20th century and its effect on the average temperature of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">the planet</a> that closely match measurements reported half a century later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SA_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-SA-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Plass worked most of his life as a <a href="/wiki/Physicist" title="Physicist">physicist</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. He graduated from <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> in 1941 and earned a PhD in physics from <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a> in 1947. He worked as an associate physicist at the Metallurgical Laboratory (Manhattan District) of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a> from 1942 to 1945. He became an instructor of physics at <a href="/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_University" title="Johns Hopkins University">Johns Hopkins University</a> in 1946, and eventually became an associate professor there. In 1955, leaving academia, he held a job for a year as a staff scientist with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lockheed_Aircraft_Corporation" title="Lockheed Aircraft Corporation">Lockheed Aircraft Corporation</a>. He then joined the advanced research staff at the <a href="/wiki/Aeronutronic" title="Aeronutronic">Aeronutronic</a> division of the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford Motor Company</a>. In 1960, he became manager of the research lab at Ford’s <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_physics" title="Theoretical physics">theoretical physics</a> department and a consulting editor of the journal <i>Infrared Physics</i> (now called <a href="/wiki/Infrared_Physics_and_Technology" title="Infrared Physics and Technology">Infrared Physics and Technology</a>). In 1963, he accepted a position as the first professor of atmospheric and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_science" title="Space science">space science</a> at the Southwest Center for Advanced Studies (now the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Texas_at_Arlington" title="University of Texas at Arlington">University of Texas at Arlington</a>), where he remained for five years. In 1968, he arrived at <a href="/wiki/Texas_A%26M_University" title="Texas A&amp;M University">Texas A&amp;M University</a>, where he served as professor of physics and head of the department.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_dioxide_research">Carbon dioxide research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gilbert_Plass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Carbon dioxide research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1953 Plass told <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time Magazine</a></i> of his work on the effects of CO<sub>2</sub> from industrial sources as a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a>, and the potential implications of an increased concentration of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere for <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. He said "At its present rate of increase, the CO<sub>2</sub> in the atmosphere will raise the earth's average temperature 1.5° <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">Fahrenheit</a> every 100 years. ... for centuries to come, if man's industrial growth continues, the earth's climate will continue to grow warmer."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TIME_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-TIME-3">[3]</a></sup> From 1956 onwards he published a series of papers on the topic, partly based on advanced calculations of the absorption of <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> radiation, and he made use of early electronic computers. Plass predicted that a doubling of CO<sub>2</sub> would warm the planet by 3.6 °C, that CO<sub>2</sub> levels in 2000 would be 30% higher than in 1900 and that the planet would be about 1 °C warmer in 2000 than in 1900. In 2007 the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">IPCC Fourth Assessment Report</a> estimated a climate sensitivity of 2 to 4.5 °C for CO<sub>2</sub> doubling, a CO<sub>2</sub> rise of 37% since <a href="/wiki/Pre-industrial_society" title="Pre-industrial society">pre-industrial times</a> and a 1900-2000 warm-up of around 0.7 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SA_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-SA-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_work">Other work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gilbert_Plass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Other work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Plass was an avid <a href="/wiki/Philately" title="Philately">philatelist</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NNDB_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NNDB-1">[1]</a></sup> and founded the United States Possessions Philatelic Society in 1978. He then served as the editor of their journal, <i>Possessions,</i> for 14 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He also hosted a classical-music oriented radio program called <i>Collector's Choice</i> on <a href="/wiki/KAMU-FM" title="KAMU-FM">KAMU-FM</a> for 20 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
The award-winning <i>Collector's Choice</i> was created by <a class="external text" href="http://www.sunnynash.com/" rel="nofollow">Sunny Nash</a>, KAMU-FM's first program director after the National Public Radio, (NPR)-affiliated station, signed on the air in 1977, also the year Nash became the first African American journalism graduate from Texas A&amp;M University.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Sunny Nash is author of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bigmama_Didn%27t_Shop_At_Woolworth%27s" title="Bigmama Didn't Shop At Woolworth's">Bigmama Didn't Shop At Woolworth's</a>.
</p>
