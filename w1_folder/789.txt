<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Water pollution</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">contamination of water bodies</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nrborderborderentrythreecolorsmay05-1-.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Nrborderborderentrythreecolorsmay05-1-.JPG/300px-Nrborderborderentrythreecolorsmay05-1-.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Nrborderborderentrythreecolorsmay05-1-.JPG/450px-Nrborderborderentrythreecolorsmay05-1-.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Nrborderborderentrythreecolorsmay05-1-.JPG/600px-Nrborderborderentrythreecolorsmay05-1-.JPG 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nrborderborderentrythreecolorsmay05-1-.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raw_sewage" title="Raw sewage">Raw sewage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Industrial_waste" title="Industrial waste">industrial waste</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_River_(California)" title="New River (California)">New River</a> as it passes from <a href="/wiki/Mexicali" title="Mexicali">Mexicali</a> to <a href="/wiki/Calexico,_California" title="Calexico, California">Calexico, California</a></div></div></div>
<p><b>Water pollution</b> is the contamination of <a href="/wiki/Body_of_water" title="Body of water">water bodies</a>, usually as a result of human activities. Water bodies include for example <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">rivers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marine_pollution" title="Marine pollution">oceans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a>. Water pollution results when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contaminant" title="Contaminant">contaminants</a> are introduced into the natural environment. For example, releasing inadequately treated <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a> into natural water bodies can lead to <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">degradation</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aquatic_ecosystems" title="Aquatic ecosystems">aquatic ecosystems</a>. In turn, this can lead to <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a> problems for people living downstream. They may use the same polluted river water for drinking or bathing or <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>. Water pollution is the leading worldwide cause of death and disease, e.g. due to <a href="/wiki/Waterborne_diseases" title="Waterborne diseases">water-borne diseases</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-death2_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-death2-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-death_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-death-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Water pollution can be grouped into <a href="/wiki/Surface_water" title="Surface water">surface water</a> pollution. <a href="/wiki/Marine_pollution" title="Marine pollution">Marine pollution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nutrient_pollution" title="Nutrient pollution">nutrient pollution</a> are subsets of water pollution. Sources of water pollution are either point sources and non-point sources. Point sources have one identifiable cause of the pollution, such as a <a href="/wiki/Storm_drain" title="Storm drain">storm drain</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wastewater_treatment_plant" title="Wastewater treatment plant">wastewater treatment plant</a> or stream. Non-point sources are more diffuse, such as <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">agricultural runoff</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup> Pollution is the result of the cumulative effect over time. All plants and organisms living in or being exposed to polluted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bodies_of_water" title="Bodies of water">water bodies</a> can be impacted. The effects can damage individual <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> and impact the natural <a href="/wiki/Biocoenosis" title="Biocoenosis">biological communities</a> they are part of.   
</p><p>The causes of water pollution include a wide range of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical" title="Chemical">chemicals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a> as well as physical parameters. Contaminants may include <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inorganic" title="Inorganic">inorganic</a> substances. Elevated temperatures can also lead to polluted water. A common cause of <a href="/wiki/Thermal_pollution" title="Thermal pollution">thermal pollution</a> is the use of water as a <a href="/wiki/Coolant" title="Coolant">coolant</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_plants" title="Power plants">power plants</a> and industrial manufacturers. Elevated water temperatures decrease oxygen levels, which can kill fish and alter <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a> composition, reduce species <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>, and foster invasion by new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermophilic" title="Thermophilic">thermophilic</a> species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goel_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goel-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laws-aquatic_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Laws-aquatic-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>375</span></sup>
</p><p>Water pollution is measured by analysing water samples. Physical, chemical and biological tests can be done. Control of water pollution requires appropriate <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a> and management plans. The infrastructure may include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wastewater_treatment_plants" title="Wastewater treatment plants">wastewater treatment plants</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">Sewage treatment plants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Industrial_wastewater_treatment" title="Industrial wastewater treatment">industrial wastewater</a> treatment plants are usually required to protect water bodies from untreated wastewater. <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_wastewater_treatment" title="Agricultural wastewater treatment">Agricultural wastewater treatment</a> for farms, and <a href="/wiki/Erosion_control" title="Erosion control">erosion control</a> from construction sites can also help prevent water pollution. <a href="/wiki/Nature-based_solutions" title="Nature-based solutions">Nature-based solutions</a> are another approach to prevent water pollution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-6">[6]</a></sup> Effective control of urban runoff includes reducing speed and quantity of flow. In the United States, <a href="/wiki/Best_management_practice_for_water_pollution" title="Best management practice for water pollution">best management practices for water pollution</a> include approaches to reduce the quantity of water and improve <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">water quality</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA-PDS_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-PDS-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction">Introduction</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Canal-pollution.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="450" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Canal-pollution.jpg/220px-Canal-pollution.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Canal-pollution.jpg/330px-Canal-pollution.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Canal-pollution.jpg/440px-Canal-pollution.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Canal-pollution.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Pollution in the <a href="/wiki/Lachine_Canal" title="Lachine Canal">Lachine Canal</a>, Canada</div></div></div>
<p>Water is typically referred to as polluted when it is impaired by <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a> contaminants. Due to these contaminants it either does not support a human use, such as <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a>, or undergoes a marked shift in its ability to support its biotic communities, such as fish. Natural phenomena such as <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Algae_bloom" title="Algae bloom">algae blooms</a>, storms, and earthquakes also cause major changes in water quality and the ecological status of water.
</p><p>Water pollution is a major global problem. It requires ongoing evaluation and revision of <a href="/wiki/Water_resource_policy" title="Water resource policy">water resource policy</a> at all levels (international down to individual aquifers and wells). It has been suggested that water pollution is the leading worldwide cause of death and diseases,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-death_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-death-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-death2_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-death2-1">[1]</a></sup> and that it accounts for the deaths of more than 14,000 people daily.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-death2_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-death2-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>India and China are two countries with high levels of water pollution: An estimated 580 people in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> die of water pollution related illness (including <a href="/wiki/Waterborne_diseases" title="Waterborne diseases">waterborne diseases</a>) every day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> About 90 percent of the <a href="/wiki/Water_resources_of_China" title="Water resources of China">water in the cities of China</a> is polluted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> As of 2007, half a billion Chinese had no access to safe drinking water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition to the acute problems of water pollution in <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing countries</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developed_countries" title="Developed countries">developed countries</a> also continue to struggle with pollution problems. For example, in a report on <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">water quality</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> in 2009, 44 percent of assessed stream miles, 64 percent of assessed lake acres, and 30 percent of assessed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bays" title="Bays">bays</a> and <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuarine</a> square miles were classified as polluted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Surface_water_pollution">Surface water pollution</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Nutrient_pollution" title="Nutrient pollution">Nutrient pollution</a></div>
<p>Surface water pollution includes pollution of rivers, lakes and oceans. A subset of surface water pollution is <a href="/wiki/Marine_pollution" title="Marine pollution">marine pollution</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marine_pollution">Marine pollution</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Marine_pollution" title="Marine pollution">Marine pollution</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AngleseyCopperStream.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="656" data-file-width="492" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/AngleseyCopperStream.jpg/170px-AngleseyCopperStream.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/AngleseyCopperStream.jpg/255px-AngleseyCopperStream.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/AngleseyCopperStream.jpg/340px-AngleseyCopperStream.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AngleseyCopperStream.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A polluted river draining an abandoned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Copper_mine" title="Copper mine">copper mine</a> on Anglesey</div></div></div>
<p>One common path of entry by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contaminant" title="Contaminant">contaminants</a> to the sea are rivers. An example is directly discharging sewage and industrial waste into the ocean. Pollution such as this occurs particularly in developing nations.  
</p><p>Large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gyre" title="Gyre">gyres</a> (<a href="/wiki/Vortex" title="Vortex">vortexes</a>) in the oceans trap floating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plastic_debris" title="Plastic debris">plastic debris</a>. Plastic debris can absorb toxic chemicals from ocean pollution, potentially poisoning any creature that eats it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Many of these long-lasting pieces end up in the stomachs of marine birds and animals. This results in obstruction of digestive pathways, which leads to reduced appetite or even starvation.
</p><p>There are a variety of secondary effects stemming not from the original pollutant, but a derivative condition. An example is <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silt</a>-bearing <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">surface runoff</a>, which can inhibit the penetration of sunlight through the water column, hampering <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> in aquatic plants.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Groundwater_pollution">Groundwater pollution</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Groundwater_pollution" title="Groundwater pollution">Groundwater pollution</a></div>
<p>Interactions between <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> and <a href="/wiki/Surface_water" title="Surface water">surface water</a> are complex. Consequently, groundwater pollution, also referred to as groundwater contamination, is not as easily classified as surface water pollution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-circ1139_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-circ1139-13">[13]</a></sup> By its very nature, groundwater <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifers</a> are susceptible to contamination from sources that may not directly affect surface water bodies. The distinction of point vs. non-point source may be irrelevant. 
</p><p>Analysis of groundwater contamination may focus on <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> characteristics and site geology, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogeology" title="Hydrogeology">hydrogeology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrology</a>, and the nature of the contaminants. Causes of groundwater pollution include: naturally-occurring (geogenic), <a href="/wiki/Sanitation#Onsite_sanitation" title="Sanitation">on-site sanitation</a> systems, <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewage</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fertilizers" title="Fertilizers">fertilizers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticide</a>, commercial and industrial leaks, <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_fracturing" title="Hydraulic fracturing">hydraulic fracturing</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfill_leachate" title="Landfill leachate">landfill leachate</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Categories_of_pollution_sources">Categories of pollution sources</span></h2>
<p>Surface water and <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> have often been studied and managed as separate resources even though they are interrelated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-circ1139_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-circ1139-13">[13]</a></sup> Surface water seeps through the soil and becomes groundwater. Conversely, groundwater can also feed surface water sources. Sources of surface water pollution are generally grouped into two categories based on their origin.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Point_sources">Point sources</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jacuecanga_Angra_dos_Reis_Rio_de_Janeiro_Brazil_Brasfels.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Jacuecanga_Angra_dos_Reis_Rio_de_Janeiro_Brazil_Brasfels.JPG/220px-Jacuecanga_Angra_dos_Reis_Rio_de_Janeiro_Brazil_Brasfels.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Jacuecanga_Angra_dos_Reis_Rio_de_Janeiro_Brazil_Brasfels.JPG/330px-Jacuecanga_Angra_dos_Reis_Rio_de_Janeiro_Brazil_Brasfels.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Jacuecanga_Angra_dos_Reis_Rio_de_Janeiro_Brazil_Brasfels.JPG/440px-Jacuecanga_Angra_dos_Reis_Rio_de_Janeiro_Brazil_Brasfels.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jacuecanga_Angra_dos_Reis_Rio_de_Janeiro_Brazil_Brasfels.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Point source pollution at a <a href="/wiki/Shipyard" title="Shipyard">shipyard</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a>, Brazil.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/United_States_regulation_of_point_source_water_pollution" title="United States regulation of point source water pollution">United States regulation of point source water pollution</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Point_source_water_pollution" title="Point source water pollution">Point source water pollution</a> refers to contaminants that enter a waterway from a single, identifiable source, such as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_pipe" title="Water pipe">pipe</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ditch" title="Ditch">ditch</a>. Examples of sources in this category include discharges from a <a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">sewage treatment plant</a>, a factory, or a city <a href="/wiki/Storm_drain" title="Storm drain">storm drain</a>. 
</p><p>The U.S. <a href="/wiki/Clean_Water_Act" title="Clean Water Act">Clean Water Act</a> (CWA) defines point source for <a href="/wiki/Regulation" title="Regulation">regulatory</a> enforcement purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The CWA definition of point source was amended in 1987 to include municipal storm sewer systems, as well as industrial storm water, such as from construction sites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-point_sources">Non-point sources</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nonpoint_source_pollution" title="Nonpoint source pollution">Nonpoint source pollution</a> refers to diffuse contamination that does not originate from a single discrete source. This type of pollution is often the cumulative effect of small amounts of contaminants gathered from a large area. A common example is the leaching out of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> compounds from fertilized agricultural lands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup> Nutrient <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">runoff</a> in <a href="/wiki/Stormwater" title="Stormwater">storm water</a> from "sheet flow" over an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agricultural_field" title="Agricultural field">agricultural field</a> or a forest are also cited as examples of non-point source pollution.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yellow_Fish.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Yellow_Fish.jpg/220px-Yellow_Fish.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Yellow_Fish.jpg/330px-Yellow_Fish.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Yellow_Fish.jpg/440px-Yellow_Fish.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yellow_Fish.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Blue drain and yellow fish symbol used by the UK Environment Agency to raise awareness of the ecological impacts of contaminating surface drainage</div></div></div><p>Contaminated storm water washed off of <a href="/wiki/Parking_lot" title="Parking lot">parking lots</a>, roads and highways, called <a href="/wiki/Urban_runoff" title="Urban runoff">urban runoff</a>, is sometimes included  under the category of non-point sources. However, because this runoff is typically channeled into storm drain systems and discharged through pipes to local surface waters, it becomes a point source.
</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contaminants_and_their_sources">Contaminants and their sources</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">Wastewater</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">Sewage</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_wastewater" title="Industrial wastewater">Industrial wastewater</a></div>
<p>The specific contaminants leading to pollution in water include a wide spectrum of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical" title="Chemical">chemicals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a>, and physical changes such as elevated temperature and discoloration. While many of the chemicals and substances that are regulated may be naturally occurring (<a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>, iron, <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a>, etc.) the <a href="/wiki/Concentration" title="Concentration">concentration</a> usually determines what is a natural component of water and what is a contaminant. High concentrations of naturally occurring substances can have negative impacts on aquatic flora and fauna.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">Oxygen</a>-depleting substances may be natural materials such as plant matter (e.g. leaves and grass) as well as man-made chemicals. Other natural and anthropogenic substances may cause <a href="/wiki/Turbidity" title="Turbidity">turbidity</a> (cloudiness) which blocks light and disrupts plant growth, and clogs the <a href="/wiki/Gill" title="Gill">gills</a> of some fish species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA-AGFact_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-AGFact-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Alteration of water's physical chemistry includes acidity (change in <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" title="Electrical conductivity">electrical conductivity</a>, temperature, and <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">eutrophication</a>. Eutrophication is an increase in the concentration of chemical nutrients in an <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> to an extent that increases the <a href="/wiki/Primary_production" title="Primary production">primary productivity</a> of the ecosystem. Depending on the degree of eutrophication, subsequent negative environmental effects such as <a href="/wiki/Anoxic_waters" title="Anoxic waters">anoxia</a> (oxygen depletion) and severe reductions in water quality may occur, affecting fish and other animal populations.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pathogens">Pathogens</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:South_Asia_Poster_03.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1980" data-file-width="1530" height="285" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/South_Asia_Poster_03.jpg/220px-South_Asia_Poster_03.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/South_Asia_Poster_03.jpg/330px-South_Asia_Poster_03.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/South_Asia_Poster_03.jpg/440px-South_Asia_Poster_03.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:South_Asia_Poster_03.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Poster to teach people in South Asia about human activities leading to the pollution of water sources</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sewer_overflow_RI_EPA.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="202" data-file-width="300" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Sewer_overflow_RI_EPA.jpg/220px-Sewer_overflow_RI_EPA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f0/Sewer_overflow_RI_EPA.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sewer_overflow_RI_EPA.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Manhole" title="Manhole">manhole</a> cover unable to contain a <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_sewer_overflow" title="Sanitary sewer overflow">sanitary sewer overflow</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dumping_of_faecal_sludge_into_the_river.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3240" data-file-width="4320" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Dumping_of_faecal_sludge_into_the_river.jpg/250px-Dumping_of_faecal_sludge_into_the_river.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Dumping_of_faecal_sludge_into_the_river.jpg/375px-Dumping_of_faecal_sludge_into_the_river.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Dumping_of_faecal_sludge_into_the_river.jpg/500px-Dumping_of_faecal_sludge_into_the_river.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dumping_of_faecal_sludge_into_the_river.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Septage" title="Septage">Fecal sludge</a> collected from pit latrines is dumped into a river at the Korogocho slum in <a href="/wiki/Nairobi" title="Nairobi">Nairobi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Disease-causing <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a> are referred to as <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a>. Pathogens can produce <a href="/wiki/Waterborne_diseases" title="Waterborne diseases">waterborne diseases</a> in either human or animal hosts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Coliform_bacteria" title="Coliform bacteria">Coliform bacteria</a>, which are not an actual cause of disease, are commonly used as a <a href="/wiki/Indicator_bacteria" title="Indicator bacteria">bacterial indicator</a> of water pollution. Other microorganisms sometimes found in contaminated surface waters that have caused human health problems include:
</p>
<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Burkholderia_pseudomallei" title="Burkholderia pseudomallei">Burkholderia pseudomallei</a></i></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Cryptosporidium_parvum" title="Cryptosporidium parvum">Cryptosporidium parvum</a></i></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Giardia_lamblia" title="Giardia lamblia">Giardia lamblia</a></i></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Salmonella" title="Salmonella">Salmonella</a></i></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Norovirus" title="Norovirus">Norovirus</a></i> and other viruses</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Helminths" title="Helminths">Parasitic worms</a> including the <a href="/wiki/Schistosoma" title="Schistosoma">Schistosoma</a> type <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></i></li></ul>
<p>High levels of pathogens may result from on-site <a href="/wiki/Sanitation" title="Sanitation">sanitation</a> systems (<a href="/wiki/Septic_tank" title="Septic tank">septic tanks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pit_latrine" title="Pit latrine">pit latrines</a>) or inadequately treated <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewage</a> discharges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Older cities with ageing infrastructure may have leaky sewage collection systems (pipes, pumps, valves), which can cause <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_sewer_overflow" title="Sanitary sewer overflow">sanitary sewer overflows</a>. Some cities also have <a href="/wiki/Combined_sewer" title="Combined sewer">combined sewers</a>, which may discharge untreated sewage during rain storms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPARTC_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPARTC-21">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">Silt</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a>) from <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewage</a> discharges also pollutes water bodies.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Muddy_USGS.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="488" data-file-width="732" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Muddy_USGS.jpg/220px-Muddy_USGS.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Muddy_USGS.jpg/330px-Muddy_USGS.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Muddy_USGS.jpg/440px-Muddy_USGS.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Muddy_USGS.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Muddy river polluted by sediment.</div></div></div>
<p>Pathogen discharges may also be caused by poorly managed livestock operations.
</p>
<h3><span id="Organic.2C_inorganic_and_macroscopic_contaminants"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Organic,_inorganic_and_macroscopic_contaminants">Organic, inorganic and macroscopic contaminants</span></h3>
<p>Contaminants may include <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inorganic" title="Inorganic">inorganic</a> substances. Many of the chemical substances are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toxic" title="Toxic">toxic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laws-aquatic_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Laws-aquatic-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>229</span></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Garbage_Collector_In_Oakley_Creek.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Garbage_Collector_In_Oakley_Creek.jpg/220px-Garbage_Collector_In_Oakley_Creek.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Garbage_Collector_In_Oakley_Creek.jpg/330px-Garbage_Collector_In_Oakley_Creek.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Garbage_Collector_In_Oakley_Creek.jpg/440px-Garbage_Collector_In_Oakley_Creek.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Garbage_Collector_In_Oakley_Creek.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A garbage collection boom in an urban-area stream in <a href="/wiki/Auckland" title="Auckland">Auckland</a>, New Zealand.</div></div></div>
<p>Organic water pollutants include:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Detergents" title="Detergents">Detergents</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Disinfection_by-product" title="Disinfection by-product">Disinfection by-products</a> found in chemically <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disinfection" title="Disinfection">disinfected</a> <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Chloroform" title="Chloroform">chloroform</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Food_processing" title="Food processing">Food processing</a> waste, which can include oxygen-demanding substances, fats and grease</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Insecticide" title="Insecticide">Insecticides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Herbicide" title="Herbicide">herbicides</a>, a huge range of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organohalide" title="Organohalide">organohalides</a> and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_compounds" title="Chemical compounds">chemical compounds</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">Petroleum</a> hydrocarbons, including fuels (<a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel fuel</a>, jet fuels, and <a href="/wiki/Fuel_oil" title="Fuel oil">fuel oil</a>) and lubricants (motor oil), and fuel <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> byproducts, from <a href="/wiki/Stormwater" title="Stormwater">storm water</a> <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">runoff</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burton_&amp;_Pitt_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burton_&amp;_Pitt-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compounds" title="Volatile organic compounds">Volatile organic compounds</a>, such as industrial <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvents</a>, from improper storage.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chlorinated_solvent" title="Chlorinated solvent">Chlorinated solvents</a>, which are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dense_non-aqueous_phase_liquids" title="Dense non-aqueous phase liquids">dense non-aqueous phase liquids</a>, may fall to the bottom of reservoirs, since they don't mix well with water and are denser.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_biphenyl" title="Polychlorinated biphenyl">Polychlorinated biphenyl</a> (PCBs)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Trichloroethylene" title="Trichloroethylene">Trichloroethylene</a></li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Perchlorate" title="Perchlorate">Perchlorate</a></li>
<li>Various chemical compounds found in personal <a href="/wiki/Hygiene" title="Hygiene">hygiene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cosmetics" title="Cosmetics">cosmetic</a> products</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Drug_pollution" title="Drug pollution">Drug pollution</a> involving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_drug" title="Pharmaceutical drug">pharmaceutical drugs</a> and their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metabolites" title="Metabolites">metabolites</a>, this can include <a href="/wiki/Antidepressant" title="Antidepressant">antidepressant</a> drugs or hormonal medicines such as <a href="/wiki/Oral_contraceptive_pill" title="Oral contraceptive pill">contraceptive pills</a>. These <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> can be small and difficult for treatment plants to remove without expensive upgrades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li></ul>

<p>Inorganic water pollutants include:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acidity" title="Acidity">Acidity</a> caused by industrial discharges (especially <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_plants" title="Power plants">power plants</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">Ammonia</a> from food processing waste</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chemical_waste" title="Chemical waste">Chemical waste</a> as industrial by-products</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">Fertilizers</a> containing nutrients--<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrates" title="Nitrates">nitrates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphates</a>—which are found in storm water runoff from agriculture, as well as commercial and residential use<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burton_&amp;_Pitt_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Burton_&amp;_Pitt-22">[22]</a></sup> (see <a href="/wiki/Nutrient_pollution" title="Nutrient pollution">nutrient pollution</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" title="Heavy metals">Heavy metals</a> from <a href="/wiki/Motor_vehicle" title="Motor vehicle">motor vehicles</a> (via <a href="/wiki/Urban_runoff" title="Urban runoff">urban storm water runoff</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burton_&amp;_Pitt_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Burton_&amp;_Pitt-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Acid_mine_drainage" title="Acid mine drainage">acid mine drainage</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secretion_of_creosote_preservative_into_aquatic_ecosystem" title="Secretion of creosote preservative into aquatic ecosystem">Secretion of creosote preservative into the aquatic ecosystem</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">Silt</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a>) in runoff from construction sites, logging, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slash_and_burn" title="Slash and burn">slash and burn</a> practices or land clearing sites.</li></ul>
<p>Macroscopic pollution – large visible items polluting the water – may be termed "floatables" in an urban storm water context, or <a href="/wiki/Marine_debris" title="Marine debris">marine debris</a> when found on the open seas, and can include such items as:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trash_(material)" title="Trash (material)">Trash</a> or garbage (e.g. paper, plastic, or <a href="/wiki/Food_waste" title="Food waste">food waste</a>) discarded by people on the ground, along with accidental or intentional dumping of rubbish, that are washed by rainfall into <a href="/wiki/Storm_drain" title="Storm drain">storm drains</a> and eventually discharged into surface waters.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">Nurdles</a>, small ubiquitous waterborne plastic pellets. <i>See</i> <a href="/wiki/Plastic_pollution" title="Plastic pollution">plastic pollution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microplastics" title="Microplastics">microplastic</a> pollution.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Shipwreck" title="Shipwreck">Shipwrecks</a>, large derelict ships.</li></ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Change_in_temperature">Change in temperature</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Thermal_pollution" title="Thermal pollution">Thermal pollution</a></div>
<p>Thermal pollution is the rise or fall in the temperature of a natural body of water caused by human influence. Thermal pollution, unlike chemical pollution, results in a change in the physical properties of water. A common cause of thermal pollution is the use of water as a <a href="/wiki/Coolant" title="Coolant">coolant</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_plants" title="Power plants">power plants</a> and industrial manufacturers. Elevated water temperatures decrease oxygen levels, which can kill fish and alter <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a> composition, reduce species <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>, and foster invasion by new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermophilic" title="Thermophilic">thermophilic</a> species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goel_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Goel-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kennish_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kennish-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laws-aquatic_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Laws-aquatic-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>375</span></sup> Urban runoff may also elevate temperature in surface waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Thermal pollution can also be caused by the release of very cold water from the base of reservoirs into warmer rivers.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement">Measurement</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Research-_water_sampling_equipment.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Research-_water_sampling_equipment.jpg/220px-Research-_water_sampling_equipment.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Research-_water_sampling_equipment.jpg/330px-Research-_water_sampling_equipment.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Research-_water_sampling_equipment.jpg/440px-Research-_water_sampling_equipment.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Research-_water_sampling_equipment.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Environmental_science" title="Environmental science">Environmental scientists</a> preparing water autosamplers.</div></div></div>
<p>Water pollution may be analyzed through several broad categories of methods: physical, chemical and biological. Most involve collection of samples, followed by specialized analytical tests. Some methods may be conducted <i><a href="/wiki/In_situ" title="In situ">in situ</a></i>, without sampling, such as temperature. Government agencies and research organizations have published standardized, validated analytical test methods to facilitate the comparability of results from disparate testing events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sampling">Sampling</span></h3>
<p>Sampling of water for physical or chemical testing can be done by several methods, depending on the accuracy needed and the characteristics of the contaminant. Many contamination events are sharply restricted in time, most commonly in association with rain events. For this reason "grab" samples are often inadequate for fully quantifying contaminant levels. Scientists gathering this type of data often employ auto-sampler devices that pump increments of water at either time or <a href="/wiki/Discharge_(hydrology)" title="Discharge (hydrology)">discharge</a> intervals.
</p><p>Sampling for biological testing involves collection of plants and animals from the surface water body. Depending on the type of assessment, the organisms may be identified for <a href="/wiki/Biosurvey" title="Biosurvey">biosurveys</a> (population counts) and returned to the water body, or they may be dissected for <a href="/wiki/Bioassay" title="Bioassay">bioassays</a> to determine <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxicity</a>.
</p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Water_quality#Sampling_and_measurement" title="Water quality">Water quality § Sampling and measurement</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_testing">Physical testing</span></h3>
<p>Common physical tests of water include temperature, solids concentrations (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Total_suspended_solids" title="Total suspended solids">total suspended solids</a> (TSS)) and turbidity.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_testing">Chemical testing</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_chemistry_analysis" title="Water chemistry analysis">water chemistry analysis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_chemistry" title="Environmental chemistry">environmental chemistry</a></div>
<p>Water samples may be examined using the principles of <a href="/wiki/Analytical_chemistry" title="Analytical chemistry">analytical chemistry</a>. Many published test methods are available for both organic and inorganic compounds. Frequently used methods include <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biochemical_oxygen_demand" title="Biochemical oxygen demand">biochemical oxygen demand</a> (BOD),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newton_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Newton-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>102</span></sup> <a href="/wiki/Chemical_oxygen_demand" title="Chemical oxygen demand">chemical oxygen demand</a> (COD),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newton_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Newton-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>104</span></sup> nutrients (<a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a> compounds), metals (including copper, <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium</a>, lead and <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>), oil and grease, total <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> hydrocarbons (TPH), and <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_testing">Biological testing</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bioindicator" title="Bioindicator">Bioindicator</a></div>
<p>Biological testing involves the use of plant, animal or microbial indicators to monitor the health of an <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_ecosystem" title="Aquatic ecosystem">aquatic ecosystem</a>. They are any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indicator_species" title="Indicator species">biological species</a> or group of species whose function, population, or status can reveal what degree of ecosystem or environmental integrity is present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karr_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Karr-29">[29]</a></sup> One example of a group of bio-indicators are the <a href="/wiki/Copepod" title="Copepod">copepods</a> and other small water <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> that are present in many water bodies. Such organisms can be monitored for changes (biochemical, physiological, or behavioral) that may indicate a problem within their ecosystem.
</p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For microbial testing of drinking water, see <a href="/wiki/Bacteriological_water_analysis" title="Bacteriological water analysis">Bacteriological water analysis</a>.</div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Control_of_pollution">Control of pollution</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Municipal_wastewater_treatment">Municipal wastewater treatment</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">Sewage treatment</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wastewater_treatment" title="Wastewater treatment">Wastewater treatment</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Deer_Island_MA.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3008" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Deer_Island_MA.JPG/220px-Deer_Island_MA.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Deer_Island_MA.JPG/330px-Deer_Island_MA.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Deer_Island_MA.JPG/440px-Deer_Island_MA.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Deer_Island_MA.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Deer_Island_Waste_Water_Treatment_Plant" title="Deer Island Waste Water Treatment Plant">Deer Island Wastewater Treatment Plant</a> serving Boston, Massachusetts and vicinity.</div></div></div>
<p>In urban areas of developed countries, municipal <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewage</a>) is typically treated by centralized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment_plants" title="Sewage treatment plants">sewage treatment plants</a>. Well-designed and operated systems (i.e., with secondary treatment steps or more advanced treatment) can remove 90 percent or more of the pollutant load in sewage. Some plants have additional systems to remove <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrients</a> and pathogens, but these more advanced treatment steps get progressively more expensive.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Nature-based_solutions" title="Nature-based solutions">Nature-based solutions</a> are also being used instead of (or in combination with) centralized treatment plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cities with <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_sewer_overflow" title="Sanitary sewer overflow">sanitary sewer overflows</a> or <a href="/wiki/Combined_sewer" title="Combined sewer">combined sewer overflows</a> employ one or more <a href="/wiki/Environmental_engineering" title="Environmental engineering">engineering</a> approaches to reduce discharges of untreated sewage, including:
</p>
<ul><li>utilizing a <a href="/wiki/Green_infrastructure" title="Green infrastructure">green infrastructure</a> approach to improve storm water management capacity throughout the system, and reduce the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydraulic" title="Hydraulic">hydraulic</a> overloading of the treatment plant<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li>repair and replacement of leaking and malfunctioning equipment<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPARTC_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-EPARTC-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li>increasing overall hydraulic capacity of the sewage collection system (often a very expensive option).</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="On-site_sanitation_and_safely_managed_sanitation">On-site sanitation and safely managed sanitation</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Sanitation" title="Sanitation">Sanitation</a></div>
<p>Households or businesses not served by a municipal treatment plant may have an individual <a href="/wiki/Septic_tank" title="Septic tank">septic tank</a>, which pre-treats the wastewater on site and infiltrates it into the soil. This can lead to <a href="/wiki/Groundwater_pollution" title="Groundwater pollution">groundwater pollution</a> if not properly done.
</p><p>Globally, about 4.5 billion people currently (in 2017) do not have <a href="/wiki/Improved_sanitation" title="Improved sanitation">safely managed sanitation</a>, according to an estimate by the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Monitoring_Programme_for_Water_Supply_and_Sanitation" title="Joint Monitoring Programme for Water Supply and Sanitation">Joint Monitoring Programme for Water Supply and Sanitation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JMP2017_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-JMP2017-31">[31]</a></sup> Lack of access to sanitation often leads to water pollution, e.g. via the practice of <a href="/wiki/Open_defecation" title="Open defecation">open defecation</a>: during rain events or floods, the <a href="/wiki/Human_feces" title="Human feces">human feces</a> are moved from the ground where they were deposited into surface waters. Simple <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pit_latrines" title="Pit latrines">pit latrines</a> may also get flooded during rain events. The use of safely managed sanitation services would prevent this type of water pollution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JMP2017_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-JMP2017-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_wastewater_treatment">Industrial wastewater treatment</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Industrial_wastewater_treatment" title="Industrial wastewater treatment">Industrial wastewater treatment</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:REDOX_DAF_unit_225_m3-h-1000_GPM.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="220" data-file-width="389" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/REDOX_DAF_unit_225_m3-h-1000_GPM.jpg/220px-REDOX_DAF_unit_225_m3-h-1000_GPM.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/REDOX_DAF_unit_225_m3-h-1000_GPM.jpg/330px-REDOX_DAF_unit_225_m3-h-1000_GPM.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6c/REDOX_DAF_unit_225_m3-h-1000_GPM.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:REDOX_DAF_unit_225_m3-h-1000_GPM.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Dissolved_air_flotation" title="Dissolved air flotation">Dissolved air flotation</a> system for treating industrial wastewater.</div></div></div>
<p>Some industrial facilities generate wastewater that is similar to domestic sewage and can be treated by sewage treatment plants. Industries that generate wastewater with high concentrations of organic matter (e.g. oil and grease), toxic pollutants (e.g. heavy metals, volatile organic compounds) or nutrients such as ammonia, need specialized treatment systems. Some industries install a pre-treatment system to remove the pollutants, and then discharge the partially treated <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a> to the municipal sewer system. Industries generating large volumes of wastewater typically operate their own treatment systems. Some industries have been successful at redesigning their manufacturing processes to reduce or eliminate pollutants, through a process called <a href="/wiki/Pollution_prevention" title="Pollution prevention">pollution prevention</a>.
</p><p>To remove heat from wastewater generated by <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">power plants</a> or <a href="/wiki/Factory" title="Factory">manufacturing plants</a> the following technologies are used:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Cooling_pond" title="Cooling pond">cooling ponds</a>, man-made bodies of water designed for cooling by <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cooling_tower" title="Cooling tower">cooling towers</a>, which transfer <a href="/wiki/Waste_heat" title="Waste heat">waste heat</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> through <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Heat_transfer" title="Heat transfer">heat transfer</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">cogeneration</a>, a process where waste heat is recycled for domestic or industrial heating purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Riparian_buffer_on_Bear_Creek_in_Story_County,_Iowa.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2100" data-file-width="1500" height="238" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Riparian_buffer_on_Bear_Creek_in_Story_County%2C_Iowa.JPG/170px-Riparian_buffer_on_Bear_Creek_in_Story_County%2C_Iowa.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Riparian_buffer_on_Bear_Creek_in_Story_County%2C_Iowa.JPG/255px-Riparian_buffer_on_Bear_Creek_in_Story_County%2C_Iowa.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Riparian_buffer_on_Bear_Creek_in_Story_County%2C_Iowa.JPG/340px-Riparian_buffer_on_Bear_Creek_in_Story_County%2C_Iowa.JPG 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Riparian_buffer_on_Bear_Creek_in_Story_County,_Iowa.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Riparian_buffer" title="Riparian buffer">Riparian buffer</a> lining a creek in <a href="/wiki/Iowa" title="Iowa">Iowa</a>.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agricultural_wastewater_treatment">Agricultural wastewater treatment</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_wastewater_treatment" title="Agricultural wastewater treatment">Agricultural wastewater treatment</a></div>
<p><b>Non point source controls</b><br/>
<a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">Sediment</a> (loose <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>) washed off fields is the largest source of <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_pollution" title="Agricultural pollution">agricultural pollution</a> in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA-AGFact_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-AGFact-16">[16]</a></sup> Farmers may utilize <a href="/wiki/Erosion_control" title="Erosion control">erosion controls</a> to reduce runoff flows and retain soil on their fields. Common techniques include <a href="/wiki/Contour_plowing" title="Contour plowing">contour plowing</a>, crop <a href="/wiki/Mulch" title="Mulch">mulching</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crop_rotation" title="Crop rotation">crop rotation</a>, planting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perennial" title="Perennial">perennial</a> crops and installing <a href="/wiki/Riparian_buffer" title="Riparian buffer">riparian buffers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NRCS-NCPS_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-NRCS-NCPS-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA-agmm_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-agmm-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>pp. 4-95–4-96</span></sup>
</p><p>Nutrients (<a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>) are typically applied to farmland as commercial <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizer</a>, animal <a href="/wiki/Manure" title="Manure">manure</a>, or spraying of municipal or industrial wastewater (effluent) or sludge. Nutrients may also enter runoff from <a href="/wiki/Crop_residue" title="Crop residue">crop residues</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> water, <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">wildlife</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deposition_(Aerosol_physics)" title="Deposition (Aerosol physics)">atmospheric deposition</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA-agmm_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-agmm-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>p. 2–9</span></sup> Farmers can develop and implement <a href="/wiki/Nutrient_management" title="Nutrient management">nutrient management</a> plans to reduce excess application of nutrients<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NRCS-NCPS_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-NRCS-NCPS-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA-agmm_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-agmm-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>pp. 4-37–4-38</span></sup> and reduce the potential for <a href="/wiki/Nutrient_pollution" title="Nutrient pollution">nutrient pollution</a>.
</p><p>To minimize pesticide impacts, farmers may use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integrated_Pest_Management" title="Integrated Pest Management">Integrated Pest Management</a> (IPM) techniques (which can include <a href="/wiki/Biological_pest_control" title="Biological pest control">biological pest control</a>) to maintain control over pests, reduce reliance on chemical pesticides, and protect water quality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Confined-animal-feeding-operation.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="250" data-file-width="350" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Confined-animal-feeding-operation.jpg/220px-Confined-animal-feeding-operation.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Confined-animal-feeding-operation.jpg/330px-Confined-animal-feeding-operation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1c/Confined-animal-feeding-operation.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Confined-animal-feeding-operation.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Feedlot in the United States</div></div></div>
<p><b>Point source wastewater treatment</b><br/>
Farms with large livestock and poultry operations, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Factory_farm" title="Factory farm">factory farms</a>, are called <i>concentrated animal feeding operations</i> or <i>feedlots</i> in the US and are being subject to increasing government regulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Animal <a href="/wiki/Slurry" title="Slurry">slurries</a> are usually treated by containment in <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_lagoon" title="Anaerobic lagoon">anaerobic lagoons</a> before disposal by spray or trickle application to grassland. <a href="/wiki/Constructed_wetland" title="Constructed wetland">Constructed wetlands</a> are sometimes used to facilitate treatment of animal wastes. Some animal slurries are treated by mixing with <a href="/wiki/Straw" title="Straw">straw</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Composting" title="Composting">composted</a> at high temperature to produce a bacteriologically sterile and friable manure for soil improvement.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Erosion_and_sediment_control_from_construction_sites">Erosion and sediment control from construction sites</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Silt_fence_EPA.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1632" data-file-width="2448" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Silt_fence_EPA.jpg/220px-Silt_fence_EPA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Silt_fence_EPA.jpg/330px-Silt_fence_EPA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Silt_fence_EPA.jpg/440px-Silt_fence_EPA.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Silt_fence_EPA.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Silt_fence" title="Silt fence">Silt fence</a> installed on a construction site.</div></div></div>
<p>Sediment from construction sites is managed by installation of:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Erosion_control" title="Erosion control">erosion controls</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Mulch" title="Mulch">mulching</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydroseeding" title="Hydroseeding">hydroseeding</a>, and</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sediment_control" title="Sediment control">sediment controls</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Sediment_basin" title="Sediment basin">sediment basins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Silt_fence" title="Silt fence">silt fences</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Discharge of toxic chemicals such as motor fuels and concrete washout is prevented by use of:
</p>
<ul><li>spill prevention and control plans, and</li>
<li>specially designed containers (e.g. for concrete washout) and structures such as overflow controls and diversion berms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span id="Control_of_urban_runoff_.28storm_water.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Control_of_urban_runoff_(storm_water)">Control of urban runoff (storm water)</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Urban_runoff" title="Urban runoff">Urban runoff</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Green_infrastructure" title="Green infrastructure">Green infrastructure</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Trounce_Pond.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Trounce_Pond.jpg/220px-Trounce_Pond.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Trounce_Pond.jpg/330px-Trounce_Pond.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Trounce_Pond.jpg/440px-Trounce_Pond.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Trounce_Pond.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Retention_basin" title="Retention basin">Retention basin</a> for controlling <a href="/wiki/Urban_runoff" title="Urban runoff">urban runoff</a></div></div></div>
<p>Effective control of urban runoff involves reducing the velocity and flow of storm water, as well as reducing pollutant discharges. Local governments use a variety of storm water management techniques to reduce the effects of urban runoff. These techniques, called <a href="/wiki/Best_management_practice_for_water_pollution" title="Best management practice for water pollution">best management practices for water pollution</a> (BMPs) in the U.S., may focus on water quantity control, while others focus on improving water quality, and some perform both functions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA-PDS_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-PDS-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Pollution prevention practices include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low-impact_development_(Canada/US)" title="Low-impact development (Canada/US)">low-impact development</a> techniques, installation of <a href="/wiki/Green_roof" title="Green roof">green roofs</a> and improved chemical handling (e.g. management of motor fuels &amp; oil, fertilizers and pesticides).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Runoff mitigation systems include <a href="/wiki/Infiltration_basin" title="Infiltration basin">infiltration basins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bioretention" title="Bioretention">bioretention</a> systems, constructed <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Retention_basin" title="Retention basin">retention basins</a> and similar devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>Thermal pollution from runoff can be controlled by storm water management facilities that absorb the runoff or direct it into <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a>, such as bioretention systems and infiltration basins. Retention basins tend to be less effective at reducing temperature, as the water may be heated by the sun before being discharged to a receiving stream.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA-PDS_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-PDS-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>p. 5–58</span></sup>
</p>
