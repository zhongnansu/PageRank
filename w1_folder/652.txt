<p><b>Serpentinite</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rock</a> composed of one or more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serpentine_group" title="Serpentine group">serpentine group</a> <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a>, the name originating from the similarity of the texture of the rock to that of the skin of a snake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schoenherr2017_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schoenherr2017-1">[1]</a></sup>  Minerals in this group are formed by <b>serpentinization</b>, a hydration and <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphic</a> transformation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultramafic" title="Ultramafic">ultramafic</a> rock from the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Mineral_alteration" title="Mineral alteration">mineral alteration</a> is particularly important at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_floor" title="Sea floor">sea floor</a> at <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">tectonic plate</a> boundaries.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_and_petrology">Formation and petrology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serpentinite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation and petrology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Serpentinization is a geological low-temperature <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphic</a> process involving heat and water in which low-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">silica</a> <a href="/wiki/Mafic" title="Mafic">mafic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultramafic" title="Ultramafic">ultramafic</a> rocks are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidized</a> (anaerobic oxidation of Fe<sup>2+</sup> by the protons of water leading to the formation of H<sub>2</sub>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrolyzed" title="Hydrolyzed">hydrolyzed</a> with water into serpentinite. <a href="/wiki/Peridotite" title="Peridotite">Peridotite</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Dunite" title="Dunite">dunite</a>, at and near the seafloor and in mountain belts is converted to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serpentine_group" title="Serpentine group">serpentine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brucite" title="Brucite">brucite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnetite" title="Magnetite">magnetite</a>, and other minerals — some rare, such as <a href="/wiki/Awaruite" title="Awaruite">awaruite</a> (Ni<sub>3</sub>Fe), and even native <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>. In the process large amounts of water are absorbed into the rock increasing the volume, reducing the density and destroying the structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LC_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-LC-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The density changes from 3.3 to 2.7 g/cm<sup>3</sup> with a concurrent volume increase on the order of 30-40%. The reaction is highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermic</a> and rock temperatures can be raised by about 260 °C (500 °F),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LC_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-LC-2">[2]</a></sup> providing an energy source for formation of non-volcanic <a href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent" title="Hydrothermal vent">hydrothermal vents</a>. The magnetite-forming chemical reactions produce <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> gas under anaerobic conditions prevailing deep in the <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a>, far from the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">Earth's atmosphere</a>. <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">Carbonates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfates</a> are subsequently reduced by hydrogen and form <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>. The hydrogen, methane, and hydrogen sulfide provide energy sources for deep sea <a href="/wiki/Chemotroph" title="Chemotroph">chemotroph</a> <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LC_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-LC-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Serpentinite_reactions">Serpentinite reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serpentinite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Serpentinite reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Serpentinite is formed from <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">olivine</a> via several reactions, some of which are complementary. Olivine is a <a href="/wiki/Solid_solution" title="Solid solution">solid solution</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>-endmember <a href="/wiki/Forsterite" title="Forsterite">forsterite</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>-endmember <a href="/wiki/Fayalite" title="Fayalite">fayalite</a>. Serpentinite reaction 1a and 1b, below, exchange silica between forsterite and fayalite to form serpentine group minerals and magnetite. These are highly exothermic reactions.
</p>

<p>After reaction, the poorly soluble reaction products (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silicic_acid" title="Silicic acid">aqueous silica</a> or dissolved <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a>) can be transported in solution out of the serpentinized zone by <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">diffusion</a> or <a href="/wiki/Advection" title="Advection">advection</a>.
</p><p>A similar suite of reactions involves <a href="/wiki/Pyroxene" title="Pyroxene">pyroxene</a>-group minerals, though less readily and with complication of the additional end-products due to the wider compositions of pyroxene and pyroxene-olivine mixes. <a href="/wiki/Talc" title="Talc">Talc</a> and magnesian <a href="/wiki/Chlorite_group" title="Chlorite group">chlorite</a> are possible products, together with the serpentine minerals <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antigorite" title="Antigorite">antigorite</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lizardite" title="Lizardite">lizardite</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chrysotile" title="Chrysotile">chrysotile</a>. The final mineralogy depends both on rock and fluid compositions, temperature, and pressure. Antigorite forms in reactions at temperatures that can exceed 600 °C (1,112 °F) during metamorphism, and it is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serpentine_group" title="Serpentine group">serpentine group</a> mineral stable at the highest temperatures. Lizardite and chrysotile can form at low temperatures very near the Earth's surface. Fluids involved in serpentinite formation commonly are highly reactive and may transport <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> and other elements into surrounding rocks; fluid reaction with these rocks may create <a href="/wiki/Metasomatism" title="Metasomatism">metasomatic</a> reaction zones enriched in calcium and called rodingites.
</p><p>In the presence of carbon dioxide, however, serpentinitization may form either magnesite (MgCO<sub>3</sub>) or generate methane (CH<sub>4</sub>). It is thought that some hydrocarbon gases may be produced by serpentinite reactions within the oceanic crust.

<p>Reaction 2a is favored if the serpentinite is Mg-poor or if there isn't enough carbon dioxide to promote <a href="/wiki/Talc" title="Talc">talc</a> formation. Reaction 2b is favored in highly magnesian compositions and low partial pressure of carbon dioxide.
</p><p>The degree to which a mass of ultramafic rock undergoes serpentinisation depends on the starting rock composition and on whether or not fluids transport <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> and other elements away during the process. If an olivine composition contains sufficient fayalite, then olivine plus water can completely metamorphose to serpentine and magnetite in a closed system. In most ultramafic rocks formed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_mantle" title="Earth's mantle">Earth's mantle</a>, however, the olivine is about 90% forsterite endmember, and for that olivine to react completely to serpentine, magnesium must be transported out of the reacting volume.
</p><p>Serpentinitization of a mass of peridotite usually destroys all previous textural evidence because the serpentine minerals are weak and behave in a very ductile fashion. However, some masses of serpentinite are less severely deformed, as evidenced by the apparent preservation of <a href="/wiki/Rock_microstructure" title="Rock microstructure">textures</a> inherited from the peridotite, and the serpentinites may have behaved in a rigid fashion.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrogen_production_by_anaerobic_oxidation_of_fayalite_ferrous_ions">Hydrogen production by anaerobic oxidation of fayalite ferrous ions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serpentinite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hydrogen production by anaerobic oxidation of fayalite ferrous ions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Serpentine is the product of the reaction between water and fayalite's ferrous (Fe<sup>2+</sup>) ions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Abiotic_methane_production_on_Mars_by_serpentinization">Abiotic methane production on Mars by serpentinization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serpentinite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Abiotic methane production on Mars by serpentinization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The presence of traces of <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Mars#Methane" title="Atmosphere of Mars">methane in the atmosphere of Mars</a> has been hypothesized to be a possible evidence for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_on_Mars_(planet)" title="Life on Mars (planet)">life on Mars</a> if methane was produced by <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacterial</a> activity. Serpentinization has been proposed as an alternative non-biological source for the observed methane traces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Serpentinization_on_Enceladus">Serpentinization on Enceladus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serpentinite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Serpentinization on Enceladus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Using data from the <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">Cassini</a> probe flybys obtained in 2010–12, scientists were able to confirm that Saturn's moon <a href="/wiki/Enceladus" title="Enceladus">Enceladus</a> likely has a liquid water ocean beneath its frozen surface. A model suggests that the ocean on Enceladus has an alkaline <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> of 11–12.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pH_2015_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-pH_2015-7">[7]</a></sup> The high pH is interpreted to be a key consequence of serpentinization of <a href="/wiki/Chondrite" title="Chondrite">chondritic rock</a>, that leads to the generation of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">H<sub>2</sub></a>, a geochemical source of energy that can support both abiotic and biological synthesis of organic molecules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pH_2015_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-pH_2015-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_agriculture">Impact on agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serpentinite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Impact on agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Soil cover over serpentinite bedrock tends to be thin or absent. Soil with serpentine is poor in calcium and other major plant nutrients, but rich in elements toxic to plants such as chromium and nickel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serpentinite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decorative_stone_in_architecture">Decorative stone in architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serpentinite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Decorative stone in architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Grades of serpentinite higher in calcite, along with the <a href="/wiki/Verd_antique" title="Verd antique">verd antique</a> (<a href="/wiki/Breccia" title="Breccia">breccia</a> form of serpentinite), have historically been used as decorative stones for their marble-like qualities. <a href="/wiki/College_Hall_(University_of_Pennsylvania)" title="College Hall (University of Pennsylvania)">College Hall</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a>, for example, is constructed out of serpentine. Popular sources in Europe before contact with the Americas were the mountainous <a href="/wiki/Piedmont" title="Piedmont">Piedmont</a> region of Italy and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Larissa,_Greece" title="Larissa, Greece">Larissa, Greece</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Carvingstone_tools.2C_oil_lamp-known_as_the_Qulliq_and_Inuit_sculpture"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Carvingstone_tools,_oil_lamp-known_as_the_Qulliq_and_Inuit_sculpture">Carvingstone tools, oil lamp-known as the Qulliq and Inuit sculpture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serpentinite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Carvingstone tools, oil lamp-known as the Qulliq and Inuit sculpture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><span id="Inuit"></span><span id="Qulliq"></span>Inuit and Aboriginal peoples of the Arctic areas and less so of southern areas used the carved bowl shaped serpentinite Qulliq or <a href="/wiki/Kudlik" title="Kudlik">Kudlik</a> lamp with wick, to burn oil or fat to heat, make light and cook with. Inuit made tools and more recently carvings of animals for commerce.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Swiss_ovenstone">Swiss ovenstone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serpentinite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Swiss ovenstone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A variety of <a href="/wiki/Chlorite_group" title="Chlorite group">chlorite</a> <a href="/wiki/Talc" title="Talc">talc</a> <a href="/wiki/Schist" title="Schist">schist</a> associated with Alpine serpentinite is found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Val_d%E2%80%99Anniviers" title="Val d’Anniviers">Val d’Anniviers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> and was used for making "ovenstones" (Ger. <i>Ofenstein</i>), a carved stone base beneath a cast iron stove.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neutron_shield_in_nuclear_reactors">Neutron shield in nuclear reactors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serpentinite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Neutron shield in nuclear reactors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Serpentinite has a significant amount of <a href="/wiki/Bound_water" title="Bound water">bound water</a>, hence it contains abundant <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> atoms able to slow down <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a> by <a href="/wiki/Elastic_collision" title="Elastic collision">elastic collision</a> (neutron <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermalization" title="Thermalization">thermalization</a> process). Because of this serpentinite can be used as dry filler inside <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> jackets in some designs of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactors</a>. For example, in <a href="/wiki/RBMK" title="RBMK">RBMK</a> series it was used for top <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiation_shielding" title="Radiation shielding">radiation shielding</a> to protect operators from escaping neutrons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Serpentine can also be added as <a href="/wiki/Construction_aggregate" title="Construction aggregate">aggregate</a> to special <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a> used in nuclear reactor shielding to increase the concrete density (2.6 g/cm<sup>3</sup>) and its <a href="/wiki/Neutron_capture" title="Neutron capture">neutron capture</a> <a href="/wiki/Cross_section_(physics)" title="Cross section (physics)">cross section</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_references">Cultural references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serpentinite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Cultural references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is the state rock of <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, USA and the California Legislature specified that serpentine was "the official State Rock and lithologic emblem."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In 2010, a bill was introduced which would have removed serpentine's special status as state rock due to it potentially containing chrysotile asbestos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The bill met with resistance from some California geologists, who noted that the chrysotile present is not hazardous unless it is mobilized in the air as dust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
