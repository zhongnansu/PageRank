<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Anaerobic organism</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Spinoloricus.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1374" data-file-width="1040" height="132" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Spinoloricus.png/100px-Spinoloricus.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Spinoloricus.png/150px-Spinoloricus.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Spinoloricus.png/200px-Spinoloricus.png 2x" width="100"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Spinoloricus.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spinoloricus_nov._sp." title="Spinoloricus nov. sp.">Spinoloricus nov. sp.</a>, a metazoan that metabolises with hydrogen, lacking <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitochondria" title="Mitochondria">mitochondria</a> and instead using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogenosomes" title="Hydrogenosomes">hydrogenosomes</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>An <b>anaerobic organism</b> or <b>anaerobe</b> is any <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organism</a> that does not require <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> for growth. It may react negatively or even die if free oxygen is present. (In contrast, an <a href="/wiki/Aerobic_organism" title="Aerobic organism">aerobic organism</a> (aerobe) is an organism that requires an oxygenated environment.)
</p><p>An anaerobic organism may be unicellular (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Protozoa" title="Protozoa">protozoans</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmc88967_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmc88967-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levinson,_W._2010_91–93_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Levinson,_W._2010_91–93-2">[2]</a></sup>) or multicellular.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid20370908_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid20370908-3">[3]</a></sup> For practical purposes, there are three categories of anaerobe: <a href="/wiki/Obligate_anaerobe" title="Obligate anaerobe">obligate anaerobes</a>, which are harmed by the presence of oxygen; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerotolerant_organism" title="Aerotolerant organism">aerotolerant organisms</a>, which cannot use oxygen for growth but tolerate its presence; and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Facultative_anaerobic_organism" title="Facultative anaerobic organism">facultative anaerobes</a>, which can grow without oxygen but use oxygen if it is present.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="First_observation">First observation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaerobic_organism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: First observation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In his letter of 14 June 1680 to The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antonie_van_Leeuwenhoek" title="Antonie van Leeuwenhoek">Antonie van Leeuwenhoek</a> described an experiment he carried out by filling two identical glass tubes about halfway with crushed pepper powder, to which
some clean rain water was added. Van Leeuwenhoek sealed one of the glass tubes by using a flame and left the other glass tube open. Several days later, he discovered in the open glass tube ‘a
great many very little animalcules, of divers sort having its own particular motion.’ Not expecting to see any life in the sealed glass tube, Van Leeuwenhoek saw to his surprise ‘a kind of living animalcules that were round and bigger than the biggest sort that I have said were in the other water.’ The conditions in the sealed tube had become quite anaerobic owing to consumption of oxygen by aerobic microorganisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gest,_Howard_2004_pp._12_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gest,_Howard_2004_pp._12-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>
In 1913 <a href="/wiki/Martinus_Beijerinck" title="Martinus Beijerinck">Martinus Beijerinck</a> repeated Van Leeuwenhoek's experiment and identified <a href="/wiki/Clostridium_butyricum" title="Clostridium butyricum">Clostridium butyricum</a> as a prominent anaerobic bacterium in the sealed pepper infusion tube liquid. Beijerinck commented: </p><blockquote><p>'We thus come to the remarkable conclusion that, beyond doubt, Van Leeuwenhoek in his experiment with the fully closed tube had cultivated and seen genuine anaerobic bacteria, which would happen again only after 200 years, namely about 1862 by Pasteur. That Leeuwenhoek, one hundred years before the discovery of oxygen and the composition of air, was not aware of the meaning of his observations is understandable. But the fact that in the closed tube he observed an increased gas pressure caused by fermentative bacteria and in addition saw the bacteria, prove in any case that he not only was a good observer, but also was able to design an experiment from which a
conclusion could be drawn.' <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gest,_Howard_2004_pp._12_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gest,_Howard_2004_pp._12-4">[4]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaerobic_organism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ana.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="180" data-file-width="300" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/76/Ana.png" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ana.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Aerobic and anaerobic <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> can be identified by growing them in test tubes of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thioglycollate_broth" title="Thioglycollate broth">thioglycollate broth</a>: <br/> 1: <a href="/wiki/Obligate_aerobe" title="Obligate aerobe">Obligate aerobes</a> need oxygen because they cannot ferment or respire anaerobically. They gather at the top of the tube where the oxygen concentration is highest. <br/> 2: <a href="/wiki/Obligate_anaerobe" title="Obligate anaerobe">Obligate anaerobes</a> are poisoned by oxygen, so they gather at the bottom of the tube where the oxygen concentration is lowest. <br/> 3: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Facultative_anaerobes" title="Facultative anaerobes">Facultative anaerobes</a> can grow with or without oxygen because they can metabolise energy aerobically or anaerobically. They gather mostly at the top because aerobic respiration generates more <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">adenosine triphosphate</a> (ATP) than either fermentation or anaerobic respiration. <br/> 4: <a href="/wiki/Microaerophile" title="Microaerophile">Microaerophiles</a> need oxygen because they cannot ferment or respire anaerobically. However, they are poisoned by high concentrations of oxygen. They gather in the upper part of the test tube but not the very top. <br/> 5: Aerotolerant organisms do not require oxygen as they metabolise energy anaerobically. Unlike obligate anaerobes however, they are not poisoned by <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>.  They can be found evenly spread throughout the test tube.</div></div></div>
<p>For practical purposes, there are three categories of anaerobe: 
</p>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Obligate_anaerobe" title="Obligate anaerobe">Obligate anaerobes</a></b>, which are harmed by the presence of oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PHK_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-PHK-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JMA_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-JMA-6">[6]</a></sup> Two examples of obligate anaerobes are <i>Clostridium botulinum</i> and the bacteria which live near hydrothermal vents on the deep-sea ocean floor.</li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerotolerant" title="Aerotolerant">Aerotolerant organisms</a></b>, which cannot use oxygen for growth, but tolerate its presence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hogg_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hogg-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Facultative_anaerobic_organism" title="Facultative anaerobic organism">Facultative anaerobes</a></b>, which can grow without oxygen but use oxygen if it is present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hogg_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hogg-7">[7]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_metabolism">Energy metabolism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaerobic_organism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Energy metabolism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some obligate anaerobes use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">fermentation</a>, while others use <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_respiration" title="Anaerobic respiration">anaerobic respiration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Aerotolerant organisms are strictly fermentative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In the presence of oxygen, facultative anaerobes use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerobic_respiration" title="Aerobic respiration">aerobic respiration</a>; without oxygen, some of them ferment; some use anaerobic respiration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hogg_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hogg-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fermentation">Fermentation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaerobic_organism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fermentation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are many anaerobic fermentative reactions.
</p><p>Fermentative anaerobic organisms mostly use the lactic acid fermentation pathway:
</p>
<dl><dd><a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>12</sub>O<sub>6</sub></a> + 2 <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_diphosphate" title="Adenosine diphosphate">ADP</a> + 2 phosphate → 2 <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactic acid</a> + 2 <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a></dd></dl>
<p>The energy released in this equation is approximately 150 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilojoule_per_mole" title="Kilojoule per mole">kJ per mol</a>, which is conserved in regenerating two ATP from ADP per <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a>. This is only 5% of the energy per sugar molecule that the typical aerobic reaction generates.
</p><p>Plants and fungi (e.g., yeasts) in general use alcohol (ethanol) fermentation when oxygen becomes limiting:
</p>
<dl><dd>C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>12</sub>O<sub>6</sub> (<a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a>) + 2 ADP + 2 phosphate → 2 C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>5</sub>OH + 2 CO<sub>2</sub>↑ + 2 ATP</dd></dl>
<p>The energy released is about 180 kJ per mol, which is conserved in regenerating two ATP from ADP per glucose.
</p><p>Anaerobic bacteria and <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a> use these and many other fermentative pathways, e.g., <a href="/wiki/Propionic_acid" title="Propionic acid">propionic acid</a> fermentation, <a href="/wiki/Butyric_acid" title="Butyric acid">butyric acid</a> fermentation, solvent fermentation, mixed acid fermentation, <a href="/wiki/Butanediol_fermentation" title="Butanediol fermentation">butanediol fermentation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stickland_fermentation" title="Stickland fermentation">Stickland fermentation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acetogenesis" title="Acetogenesis">acetogenesis</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Methanogenesis" title="Methanogenesis">methanogenesis</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culturing_anaerobes">Culturing anaerobes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaerobic_organism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Culturing anaerobes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since normal microbial culturing occurs in atmospheric air, which is an aerobic environment, the culturing of anaerobes poses a problem.  Therefore, a number of techniques are employed by microbiologists when culturing anaerobic organisms, for example, handling the bacteria in a <a href="/wiki/Glovebox" title="Glovebox">glovebox</a> filled with nitrogen or the use of other specially sealed containers, or techniques such as injection of the bacteria into a <a href="/wiki/Dicotyledon" title="Dicotyledon">dicot</a> plant, which is an environment with limited oxygen. The GasPak System is an isolated container that achieves an anaerobic environment by the reaction of water with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_borohydride" title="Sodium borohydride">sodium borohydride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_bicarbonate" title="Sodium bicarbonate">sodium bicarbonate</a> tablets to produce hydrogen gas and carbon dioxide. <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">Hydrogen</a> then reacts with oxygen gas on a palladium catalyst to produce more water, thereby removing oxygen gas. The issue with the Gaspak method is that an adverse reaction can take place where the bacteria may die, which is why a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thioglycollate_broth" title="Thioglycollate broth">thioglycollate medium</a> should be used. The thioglycollate supplies a medium mimicking that of a dicot, thus providing not only an anaerobic environment but all the nutrients needed for the bacteria to thrive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Multicellularity">Multicellularity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaerobic_organism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Multicellularity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Except for three species of anaerobic <a href="/wiki/Loricifera" title="Loricifera">loricifera</a>, all known complex multicellular life is <a href="/wiki/Aerobic_organism" title="Aerobic organism">aerobic</a>, i.e. needs oxygen to survive.
</p><p>In 2010 three species of anaerobic loricifera were discovered in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypersaline" title="Hypersaline">hypersaline</a> <a href="/wiki/Anoxic_waters" title="Anoxic waters">anoxic</a> <a href="/wiki/L%27Atalante_basin" title="L'Atalante basin">L'Atalante basin</a> at the bottom of the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>. They lack <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitochondria" title="Mitochondria">mitochondria</a> which contain the <a href="/wiki/Oxidative_phosphorylation" title="Oxidative phosphorylation">oxidative phosphorylation</a> pathway, which in all other animals combines <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> with <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> to produce metabolic energy, and thus they consume no oxygen. Instead these loricifera derive their energy from <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogenosomes" title="Hydrogenosomes">hydrogenosomes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid20370908_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid20370908-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some organisms metabolise primarily using glycogen, for example the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nereid_(worm)" title="Nereid (worm)">Nereid (worm)s</a> and some <a href="/wiki/Polychaete" title="Polychaete">polychaetes</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> or the juvenile form of the pork parasite <i><a href="/wiki/Trichinella_spiralis" title="Trichinella spiralis">Trichinella spiralis</a></i> which causes <a href="/wiki/Trichinosis" title="Trichinosis">trichinosis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roberts_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
