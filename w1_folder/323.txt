<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Decaf" redirects here. For the computer software program, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DECAF" title="DECAF">DECAF</a>.</div>
<p><b>Decaffeination</b> is the removal of <a href="/wiki/Caffeine" title="Caffeine">caffeine</a> from <a href="/wiki/Coffee" title="Coffee">coffee beans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cocoa_bean" title="Cocoa bean">cocoa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tea" title="Tea">tea</a> leaves, and other caffeine-containing materials. While <a href="/wiki/Soft_drink" title="Soft drink">soft drinks</a> which do not use caffeine as an ingredient are sometimes described as "decaffeinated", they are better termed "non-caffeinated" because decaffeinated implies that there was caffeine present at one point in time. Decaffeinated drinks contain typically 1–2% of the original caffeine content, and sometimes as much as 20%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciencedaily_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-sciencedaily-1">[1]</a></sup> Decaffeinated products are commonly termed <b>decaf</b>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decaffeination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coffee">Coffee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decaffeination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Coffee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Friedlieb_Ferdinand_Runge" title="Friedlieb Ferdinand Runge">Friedlieb Ferdinand Runge</a> performed the first isolation of pure caffeine from coffee beans in 1820. He did this after the poet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goethe" title="Goethe">Goethe</a> requested he perform an analysis on coffee beans after seeing his work on belladonna extract.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Though Runge was able to isolate the compound, he did not learn much about the chemistry of caffeine itself, nor did he seek to use the process commercially to produce decaffeinated coffee.
</p><p>The first commercially successful decaffeination process was invented by German merchant <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Roselius" title="Ludwig Roselius">Ludwig Roselius</a> and co-workers in 1903 and patented in 1906.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-decaf_101_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-decaf_101-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1903, Ludwig accidentally stumbled upon this method when his freight of coffee beans was soaked in sea water and lost much of its caffeine without losing much taste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This original decaffeination process involved steaming coffee beans with various <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a> or <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">bases</a>, then using <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a> as a solvent to remove the caffeine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Coffee decaffeinated this way was sold as <a href="/wiki/Caf%C3%A9_HAG" title="Café HAG">Kaffee HAG</a> after the company name <i>Kaffee Handels-Aktien-Gesellschaft</i> (Coffee Trading Company) in most of Europe, as <i>Café Sanka</i> in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and later as <a href="/wiki/Sanka" title="Sanka">Sanka</a> brand coffee in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">US</a> <a href="/wiki/Caf%C3%A9_HAG" title="Café HAG">Café HAG</a> and Sanka are now worldwide brands of <a href="/wiki/Kraft_Foods" title="Kraft Foods">Kraft Foods</a>. Because of health concerns regarding benzene (which is recognised today as a <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogen</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IARC_Monograph_100F_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-IARC_Monograph_100F-7">[7]</a></sup>), benzene is no longer used as a solvent commercially.
</p><p>Since its inception, methods of decaffeination similar to those first developed by Roselius have continued to dominate. While Roselius used benzene, many different solvents have since been tried after learning of the potential harmful effects of benzene. The most prevalent solvents used to date are <a href="/wiki/Dichloromethane" title="Dichloromethane">dichloromethane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethyl_acetate" title="Ethyl acetate">ethyl acetate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another variation of Roselius' method is the indirect organic solvent method. This is very similar to the process described above, only instead of treating the beans directly, water resulting from the soaking of beans is treated with solvents and the process goes on until equilibrium is reached without caffeine in the beans. This method was first mentioned in 1941, and people have made great efforts to make the process more "natural" and a true water-based process by finding ways to process the caffeine out of the water in ways that circumvents the use of organic solvents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another process, known as the Swiss Water Method, uses solely water and osmosis to decaffeinate beans. The use of water as the solvent to decaffeinate coffee was originally pioneered in Switzerland in 1933 and developed as a commercially viable method of decaffeination by Coffex S.A. in 1980.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-decaf_101_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-decaf_101-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1988, the Swiss Water Method was introduced by The Swiss Water Decaffeinated Coffee Company of <a href="/wiki/Burnaby" title="Burnaby">Burnaby</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Noted food engineer <a href="/wiki/Torunn_Atteraas_Garin" title="Torunn Atteraas Garin">Torunn Atteraas Garin</a> also developed a process to remove caffeine from coffee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Most recently, food scientists have turned to <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_carbon_dioxide" title="Supercritical carbon dioxide">supercritical carbon dioxide</a> as a means of decaffeination. Developed by Kurt Zosel, a scientist of the Max Planck Institute, it uses CO<sub>2</sub>, heated and pressurized above its <a href="/wiki/Critical_point_(thermodynamics)" title="Critical point (thermodynamics)">critical point</a>, to extract caffeine<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-decaf_101_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-decaf_101-3">[3]</a></sup> and could be useful going forward because it circumvents the use of other solvents and their possible effects entirely.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decaffeination_processes_for_coffee">Decaffeination processes for coffee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decaffeination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Decaffeination processes for coffee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the case of coffee, various methods can be used. The process is performed on unroasted (green) beans and starts with steaming of the beans. They are then rinsed with a solvent that extracts the caffeine while leaving other constituents largely unaffected. The process is repeated from 8 to 12 times until the caffeine content meets the required standard (97% of caffeine removed according to the US standard, or 99.9% caffeine-free by mass per the EU standard).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Swiss_Water_process">Swiss Water process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decaffeination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Swiss Water process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Swiss Water Process uses Green Coffee Extract (GCE) for the caffeine extraction mechanism.  Green Coffee Extract is a solution containing the water-soluble components of green coffee except for the caffeine.  The process relies on the stability of the soluble components of the GCE and the gradient pressure difference between the GCE (which is caffeine lean) and the green coffee (which is caffeine-rich). This gradient pressure causes the caffeine molecules to migrate from the green coffee into the GCE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-decaf_101_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-decaf_101-3">[3]</a></sup> Because GCE is saturated with the other water-soluble components of green coffee only the caffeine molecule migrates to the GCE; the other water-soluble coffee elements are retained in the Green Coffee.
</p><p>Once the GCE is rich with caffeine it is then percolated through carbon absorbers which attract the caffeine molecule from the GCE while leaving other green coffee elements intact in the GCE. When the GCE is once again lean of caffeine it is then used to remove additional caffeine from the green coffee. This is a continuous batch process that takes 8–10 hours to meet the final residual decaffeinated target.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organic_solvent_processes">Organic solvent processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decaffeination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Organic solvent processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Indirect_method">Indirect method</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decaffeination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Indirect method">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the indirect method, beans are first soaked in hot water for several hours, in essence making a strong pot of coffee. Then the beans are removed and either dichloromethane or ethyl acetate is used to extract the caffeine from the water. As in other methods, the caffeine can then be separated from the organic solvent by simple evaporation. The same water is recycled through this two-step process with new batches of beans. An <a href="/wiki/Solubility_equilibrium" title="Solubility equilibrium">equilibrium</a> is reached after several cycles, wherein the water and the beans have a similar composition except for the caffeine. After this point, the caffeine is the only material removed from the beans, so no coffee strength or other flavorings are lost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Because water is used in the initial phase of this process, indirect method decaffeination is sometimes referred to as "water-processed".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Triglyceride_process">Triglyceride process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decaffeination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Triglyceride process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Green coffee beans are soaked in a hot water/coffee solution to draw the caffeine to the surface of the beans. Next, the beans are transferred to another container and immersed in coffee oils that were obtained from spent coffee grounds and left to soak.
</p><p>After several hours of high temperatures, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triglycerides" title="Triglycerides">triglycerides</a> in the oils remove the caffeine, but not the flavor elements, from the beans. The beans are separated from the oils and dried. The caffeine is removed from the oils, which are reused to decaffeinate another batch of beans. This is a direct-contact method of decaffeination.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decaffeinated_coffee">Decaffeinated coffee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decaffeination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Decaffeinated coffee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Caffeine_content_of_coffee">Caffeine content of coffee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decaffeination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Caffeine content of coffee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Coffee#Caffeine_content" title="Coffee">Coffee § Caffeine content</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Caffeine_content_of_decaffeinated_coffee">Caffeine content of decaffeinated coffee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decaffeination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Caffeine content of decaffeinated coffee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To ensure product quality, manufacturers are required to test the newly decaffeinated coffee beans to make sure that caffeine concentration is relatively low. No less than 97% caffeine content reduction according to United States standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Less than 0.1% caffeine in decaffeinated coffee and less than 0.3% in decaffeinated instant coffee in Canada. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> To do so, many coffee companies choose to employ <a href="/wiki/High-performance_liquid_chromatography" title="High-performance liquid chromatography">High-performance liquid chromatography</a> to quantitatively measure how much caffeine remains in the coffee beans. However, since HPLC can be quite costly, some coffee companies are beginning to use other methods such as <a href="/wiki/Near-infrared_spectroscopy" title="Near-infrared spectroscopy">Near-infrared (NIR) spectroscopy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Although HPLC is highly accurate, NIR spectroscopy is much faster, cheaper and overall easier to use. Lastly, another method typically used to quantify remaining caffeine includes <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet%E2%80%93visible_spectroscopy" title="Ultraviolet–visible spectroscopy">Ultraviolet–visible spectroscopy</a>, which can be greatly advantageous for decaffeination processes that include supercritical CO<sub>2,</sub> as CO<sub>2</sub> does not absorb in the UV-Vis range.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>A controlled study of ten samples of prepared decaffeinated coffee from coffee shops showed that some caffeine remained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciencedaily_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-sciencedaily-1">[1]</a></sup> Fourteen to twenty cups of such decaffeinated coffee would contain as much caffeine as one cup of regular coffee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciencedaily_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-sciencedaily-1">[1]</a></sup> The 16-ounce (473-ml) cups of coffee samples contained caffeine in the range of 8.6 mg to 13.9 mg. In another study of popular brands of decaf coffees, the caffeine content varied from 3 mg to 32 mg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> An 8-ounce (237-ml) cup of regular coffee contains 95–200 mg of caffeine,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and a 12-ounce (355-milliliter) serving of <a href="/wiki/Coca-Cola" title="Coca-Cola">Coca-Cola</a> contains 36 mg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Both of these studies tested the caffeine content of store-brewed coffee, suggesting that the caffeine may be residual from the normal coffee served rather than poorly decaffeinated coffee.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (September 2018)">original research?</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decaffito">Decaffito</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decaffeination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Decaffito">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of 2009, progress toward growing coffee beans that do not contain caffeine was still continuing. The term "Decaffito" has been coined to describe this type of decaffeinated coffee, and trademarked in Brazil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-decaffito_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-decaffito-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>The prospect for Decaffito-type coffees was shown by the discovery of the naturally caffeine-free <i><a href="/wiki/Coffea_charrieriana" title="Coffea charrieriana">Coffea charrieriana</a></i>, reported in 2004. It has a deficient <a href="/wiki/Caffeine_synthase" title="Caffeine synthase">caffeine synthase</a> gene, leading it to accumulate <a href="/wiki/Theobromine" title="Theobromine">theobromine</a> instead of converting it to caffeine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid15215853_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid15215853-23">[23]</a></sup> Either this trait could be bred into other coffee plants by crossing them with <i>C. charrieriana</i>, or an equivalent effect could be achieved by <a href="/wiki/Gene_knockout" title="Gene knockout">knocking out</a> the gene for caffeine synthase in normal coffee plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decaffeinated_tea">Decaffeinated tea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decaffeination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Decaffeinated tea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Health_effects_of_tea" title="Health effects of tea">Health effects of tea</a></div>
<p>Tea may also be decaffeinated, usually by using processes analogous to the direct method or the CO<sub>2</sub> process, as described above. The process of oxidizing tea leaves to create <a href="/wiki/Black_tea" title="Black tea">black tea</a> ("red" in <a href="/wiki/Chinese_tea" title="Chinese tea">Chinese tea</a> culture) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oolong_tea" title="Oolong tea">oolong tea</a> leaves from green leaves does not affect the amount of caffeine in the tea, though tea-plant species (i.e., <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Camellia_sinensis_sinensis" title="Camellia sinensis sinensis">Camellia sinensis sinensis</a></i> vs. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Camellia_sinensis_assamica" title="Camellia sinensis assamica">Camellia sinensis assamica</a></i>) may differ in natural caffeine content. Younger leaves and buds contain more caffeine per weight than older leaves and stems.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Although the CO<sub>2</sub> process is favorable because it is convenient, nonexplosive, and nontoxic,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> a comparison between regular and decaffeinated green teas using supercritical carbon dioxide showed that most volatile, nonpolar compounds (such as <a href="/wiki/Linalool" title="Linalool">linalool</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phenylacetaldehyde" title="Phenylacetaldehyde">phenylacetaldehyde</a>), green and floral flavor compounds (such as <a href="/wiki/Hexanal" title="Hexanal">hexanal</a> and (<i>E</i>)-2-hexenal), and some unknown compounds disappeared or decreased after decaffeination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition to CO<sub>2</sub> process extraction, tea may be also decaffeinated using a hot water treatment. Optimal conditions are met by controlling water temperature, extraction time, and ratio of leaf to water, where higher temperatures at or over 100 °C, moderate extraction time of 3 minutes, and a 1:20 water to leaf weight per volume ratio removed 83% caffeine content and preserved 95% of total <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catechins" title="Catechins">catechins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catechins" title="Catechins">Catechins</a>, a type of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flavanol" title="Flavanol">flavanols</a>, contribute to the flavor of the tea and have been shown to increase the suppression of <a href="/wiki/Mutagen" title="Mutagen">mutagens</a> that may lead to cancer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Both coffee and tea have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tannins" title="Tannins">tannins</a>, which are responsible for their <a href="/wiki/Astringent" title="Astringent">astringent</a> taste, but tea has a nearly three times smaller tannin content than coffee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Thus, decaffeination of tea requires more care to maintain tannin content than decaffeination of coffee in order to preserve this flavor. Preserving tannins is desirable not only because of their flavor, but also because they have been shown to have anticarcinogenic, antimutagenic, antioxidative, and antimicrobrial properties. Specifically, tannins accelerate blood clotting, reduce blood pressure, decrease the serum lipid level, produce liver necrosis, and modulate immunoresponses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Certain processes during normal production might help to decrease the caffeine content directly, or simply lower the rate at which it is released throughout each infusion. Several instances in China where this is evident is in many cooked <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pu-erh_tea" title="Pu-erh tea">pu-erh teas</a>, as well as more heavily fired <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wuyi_Mountain" title="Wuyi Mountain">Wuyi Mountain</a> oolongs; commonly referred to as 'zhonghuo' (mid-fired) or 'zuhuo' (high-fired).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>A generally accepted statistic is that a cup of normal black (or red) tea contains 40–50 mg of caffeine, roughly half the content of a cup of coffee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although a common technique of discarding a short (30- to 60-second) steep<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> is believed to much reduce caffeine content of a subsequent brew at the cost of some loss of flavor, research suggests that a five-minute steep yields up to 70% of the caffeine, and a second steep has one-third the caffeine of the first (about 23% of the total caffeine in the leaves).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
