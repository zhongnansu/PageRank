<p><b>Npower Limited</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> (trading as <b>npower</b>) is an electricity generator and supplier of gas and electricity to homes and businesses which is based in the United Kingdom, formerly known as Innogy plc. As Innogy plc it was listed on the <a href="/wiki/London_Stock_Exchange" title="London Stock Exchange">London Stock Exchange</a> and was a constituent of the <a href="/wiki/FTSE_100_Index" title="FTSE 100 Index">FTSE 100 Index</a>.
</p><p>In March 2002, it was acquired by <a href="/wiki/RWE" title="RWE">RWE</a> of Germany, and was subsequently renamed RWE npower plc. RWE split off its renewable energy, network and retail divisions as <a href="/wiki/Innogy" title="Innogy">Innogy SE</a> in 2016, and npower transferred to the new business. It is considered as one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_Six_Energy_Suppliers_(UK)" title="Big Six Energy Suppliers (UK)">Big Six Energy Suppliers</a>, which <a href="/wiki/Dominate" title="Dominate">dominate</a> the gas and electricity market in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.
</p><p>The Npower is different from the Nigeria Npower programme, which is a scheme by the Federal government of Nigeria to alleviate poverty by employing and training Nigerian youth in skill acquisitions in different areas. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Npower_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The company established the Innogy plc from the operations of <a href="/wiki/National_Power" title="National Power">National Power</a> when the overseas operations were demerged as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Power" title="International Power">International Power</a> plc in October 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Innogy plc then went on to purchase the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regional_electricity_company" title="Regional electricity company">regional electricity company</a> <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Electricity" title="Yorkshire Electricity">Yorkshire Electricity</a> in February 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It later disposed of the distribution side of Yorkshire Electricity to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CE_Electric_UK" title="CE Electric UK">CE Electric UK</a> in exchange for the supply business of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Electric" title="Northern Electric">Northern Electric</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Innogy plc was taken over by the German energy company <a href="/wiki/RWE" title="RWE">RWE</a> in 2002, and renamed RWE npower plc with all the supply business adopting the "npower" brand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2006, npower acquired 19% of <a href="/wiki/Telecom_Plus" title="Telecom Plus">Telecom Plus</a>, a business which now provides npower with management services, such as billing, customer service, metering, debt collection and administration: under the deal npower can increase its stake in Telecom Plus up to 29%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In February 2009, Npower acquired Superior Plumbing, a business providing services to social housing and business customers across the United Kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2009, npower acquired SPI Group to add to its energy services business Npower Hometeam. SPI provides services to the social housing market and its commercial arm serves public buildings, including schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 2013, however, npower sold the two former Telecom Plus subsidiaries back to <a href="/wiki/Utility_Warehouse" title="Utility Warehouse">Utility Warehouse</a> for £218 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FT03_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FT03-10">[10]</a></sup> As a result, Utility Warehouse became one of the largest independent energy suppliers in the United Kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FT03_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-FT03-10">[10]</a></sup> with over 500,000 customers and 770,000 gas and electricity supply points to their name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>The deal also sparked commentary about the possibility of npower's parent company RWE leaving the United Kingdom, or the emergence of a "Big Seven" in place of the existing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_Six_Energy_Suppliers_(UK)" title="Big Six Energy Suppliers (UK)">Big Six Energy Suppliers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph02_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph02-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2016, the company announced losses of £106 million and 2,500 global job cuts<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> along with the fact that they had lost 351,000 customers in 2015: a high level of customer complaints were cited as reasons for the issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 1 April 2016, RWE split off its renewable energy, network and retail businesses into the newly formed <a href="/wiki/Innogy" title="Innogy">Innogy SE</a>. npower became a subsidiary of the new business and was renamed Npower Limited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The coal, natural gas and oil-fired energy power stations, which npower operated, are no longer part of npower, but of <a href="/wiki/RWE" title="RWE">RWE</a> Generation UK plc.
</p>
<h3><span id="Proposed_merger.2Fdemerger_of_retail_supply_division"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_merger/demerger_of_retail_supply_division">Proposed merger/demerger of retail supply division</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Npower_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Proposed merger/demerger of retail supply division">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In November 2017, Innogy and rival <a href="/wiki/SSE_plc" title="SSE plc">SSE</a> announced a planned merger of npower's residential and business retail business with SSE's residential energy supply and home services business in Great Britain to create a new company listed on the London Stock Exchange.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> SSE plan to divest its 65.6% holding in the business to its shareholders and Innogy would own the remainder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>The merger received preliminary regulatory clearance from the <a href="/wiki/Competition_and_Markets_Authority" title="Competition and Markets Authority">Competition and Markets Authority</a> on 30 August 2018,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and full clearance was given on 10 October 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Npower_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The company supplies gas and electricity to residential and business customers in the United Kingdom. Its energy services business provides servicing such as repairing boilers and central heating systems through its Hometeam brand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Npower_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_European_power_companies_by_carbon_intensity" title="List of European power companies by carbon intensity">List of European power companies by carbon intensity</a></div>
<p>In April 2008, npower faced allegations over the conduct of its door to door salespeople.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> An undercover investigation by a newspaper resulted in allegations of npower salespeople misleading potential customers, with suggestions that salespeople were 'routinely lying' and asking potential customers to 'sign a form without revealing it was a contract'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> npower was eventually fined £1.8 million by the energy regulator Ofgem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> npower have also been criticised for their customer service, having been rated the lowest of all energy suppliers in the <i><a href="/wiki/Which%3F" title="Which?">Which?</a></i> Switch 2010 customer satisfaction survey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>In September 2013, <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> MPs and campaign group <a href="/wiki/38_Degrees" title="38 Degrees">38 Degrees</a> accused npower of avoiding tax. npower defended itself by stating that tax paid had been lower than expected due to higher than expected capital investment in the United Kingdom, leading to capital allowances. A Bloomberg report for Greenpeace confirmed that RWE npower was the biggest investor in new energy infrastructure recently in the United Kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, groups such as 38 Degrees called for customers to boycott or switch over to other providers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2014, npower's call centre in <a href="/wiki/Fenton,_Staffordshire" title="Fenton, Staffordshire">Fenton</a> closed, resulting in a loss of 480 jobs following the announcement of the closure the previous December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In December 2015, the company was ordered to pay a £26m settlement by the energy regulator, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ofgem" title="Ofgem">Ofgem</a>, for "failing to treat customers fairly", the second such fine Ofgem has had imposed on it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In February 2017, npower announced they were increasing their prices. Ofgem announced that npower would have to justify this price increase.
</p>
