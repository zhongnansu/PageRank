<p>An <b>organochloride</b>, <b>organochlorine</b> compound, <b>chlorocarbon</b>, or <b>chlorinated hydrocarbon</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compound</a> containing at least one <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">covalently bonded</a> atom of <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> that has an effect on the chemical behavior of the <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecule</a>. The <b>chloroalkane</b> class (<a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">alkanes</a> with one or more hydrogens substituted by chlorine) provides common examples. The wide structural variety and divergent chemical properties of organochlorides lead to a broad range of names and applications. Organochlorides are very useful compounds in many applications, but some are of profound environmental concern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_and_chemical_properties">Physical and chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organochloride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Physical and chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chlorination_reaction" title="Chlorination reaction">Chlorination</a> modifies the physical properties of hydrocarbons in several ways. The compounds are typically denser than <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> due to the higher atomic weight of chlorine versus hydrogen.  Aliphatic organochlorides are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkylating_agent" title="Alkylating agent">alkylating agents</a> because chloride is a <a href="/wiki/Leaving_group" title="Leaving group">leaving group</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_occurrence">Natural occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organochloride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Natural occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many organochlorine compounds have been isolated from natural sources ranging from bacteria to humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gribble_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gribble-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gribble99_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gribble99-3">[3]</a></sup> Chlorinated organic compounds are found in nearly every class of biomolecules including <a href="/wiki/Alkaloid" title="Alkaloid">alkaloids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Terpene" title="Terpene">terpenes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flavonoid" title="Flavonoid">flavonoids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steroid" title="Steroid">steroids</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fatty_acid" title="Fatty acid">fatty acids</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gribble_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gribble-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Organochlorides, including <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_dibenzodioxins" title="Polychlorinated dibenzodioxins">dioxins</a>, are produced in the high temperature environment of forest fires, and dioxins have been found in the preserved ashes of lightning-ignited fires that predate synthetic dioxins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In addition, a variety of simple chlorinated hydrocarbons including dichloromethane, chloroform, and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride" title="Carbon tetrachloride">carbon tetrachloride</a> have been isolated from marine algae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> A majority of the <a href="/wiki/Chloromethane" title="Chloromethane">chloromethane</a> in the environment is produced naturally by biological decomposition, forest fires, and volcanoes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The natural organochloride <a href="/wiki/Epibatidine" title="Epibatidine">epibatidine</a>, an alkaloid isolated from tree frogs, has potent <a href="/wiki/Analgesic" title="Analgesic">analgesic</a> effects and has stimulated research into new pain medication. (The frogs obtain epibatidine through their diet and then sequester it on their skin. Likely dietary sources are beetles, ants, mites, and flies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-E.N._Lasley_(1999)_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-E.N._Lasley_(1999)-8">[8]</a></sup>)
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organochloride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="From_chlorine">From chlorine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organochloride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: From chlorine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkanes" title="Alkanes">Alkanes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aryl" title="Aryl">aryl</a> alkanes may be chlorinated under free radical conditions, with UV light. However, the extent of chlorination is difficult to control. Aryl chlorides may be prepared by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friedel-Crafts_halogenation" title="Friedel-Crafts halogenation">Friedel-Crafts halogenation</a>, using chlorine and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_acid" title="Lewis acid">Lewis acid</a> catalyst.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Haloform_reaction" title="Haloform reaction">haloform reaction</a>, using chlorine and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a>, is also able to generate alkyl halides from methyl ketones, and related compounds. Chloroform was formerly produced thus.
</p><p>Chlorine adds to the multiple bonds on alkenes and alkynes as well, giving di- or tetra-chloro compounds.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction_with_hydrogen_chloride">Reaction with hydrogen chloride</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organochloride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reaction with hydrogen chloride">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Alkenes react with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride" title="Hydrogen chloride">hydrogen chloride</a> (HCl) to give alkyl chlorides. For example, the industrial production of <a href="/wiki/Chloroethane" title="Chloroethane">chloroethane</a> proceeds by the reaction of <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">ethylene</a> with HCl:
</p>
<dl><dd>H<sub>2</sub>C=CH<sub>2</sub> + HCl → CH<sub>3</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>Cl</dd></dl>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Oxychlorination" title="Oxychlorination">oxychlorination</a>, hydrogen chloride instead of the more expensive chlorine for the same purpose:
</p>
<dl><dd>CH<sub>2</sub>=CH<sub>2</sub> + 2 HCl + ​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> O<sub>2</sub> → ClCH<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>Cl + <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">H<sub>2</sub>O</a>.</dd></dl>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_chlorinating_agents">Other chlorinating agents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organochloride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other chlorinating agents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Alkyl chlorides are most easily prepared by treating alcohols with <a href="/wiki/Thionyl_chloride" title="Thionyl chloride">thionyl chloride</a> (SOCl<sub>2</sub>) or <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus_pentachloride" title="Phosphorus pentachloride">phosphorus pentachloride</a> (PCl<sub>5</sub>), but also commonly with sulfuryl chloride (SO<sub>2</sub>Cl<sub>2</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus_trichloride" title="Phosphorus trichloride">phosphorus trichloride</a> (PCl<sub>3</sub>):
</p>
<dl><dd>ROH + SOCl<sub>2</sub> → RCl + SO<sub>2</sub> + HCl</dd>
<dd>3 ROH + PCl<sub>3</sub> → 3 RCl + H<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>3</sub></dd>
<dd>ROH + PCl<sub>5</sub> → RCl + POCl<sub>3</sub> + HCl</dd></dl>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organochloride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Alkyl chlorides are versatile building blocks in organic chemistry. While alkyl bromides and iodides are more reactive, alkyl chlorides tend to be less expensive and more readily available. Alkyl chlorides readily undergo attack by nucleophiles.
</p><p>Heating alkyl halides with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> or <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> gives alcohols. Reaction with <a href="/wiki/Alkoxide" title="Alkoxide">alkoxides</a> or <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Aroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Aroxide (page does not exist)">aroxides</a> give <a href="/wiki/Ether" title="Ether">ethers</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Williamson_ether_synthesis" title="Williamson ether synthesis">Williamson ether synthesis</a>; reaction with <a href="/wiki/Thiol" title="Thiol">thiols</a> give <a href="/wiki/Thioether" title="Thioether">thioethers</a>. Alkyl chlorides readily react with <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amines</a> to give substituted <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amines</a>. Alkyl chlorides are substituted by softer halides such as the <a href="/wiki/Iodide" title="Iodide">iodide</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Finkelstein_reaction" title="Finkelstein reaction">Finkelstein reaction</a>. Reaction with other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pseudohalide" title="Pseudohalide">pseudohalides</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Azide" title="Azide">azide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyanide" title="Cyanide">cyanide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thiocyanate" title="Thiocyanate">thiocyanate</a> are possible as well. In the presence of a strong base, alkyl chlorides undergo dehydrohalogenation to give <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkenes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Alkyne" title="Alkyne">alkynes</a>.
</p><p>Alkyl chlorides react with <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> to give <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grignard_reagent" title="Grignard reagent">Grignard reagents</a>, transforming an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrophilic" title="Electrophilic">electrophilic</a> compound into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nucleophilic" title="Nucleophilic">nucleophilic</a> compound. The <a href="/wiki/Wurtz_reaction" title="Wurtz reaction">Wurtz reaction</a> reductively couples two alkyl halides to couple with <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organochloride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vinyl_chloride">Vinyl chloride</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organochloride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Vinyl chloride">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The largest application of organochlorine chemistry is the production of <a href="/wiki/Vinyl_chloride" title="Vinyl chloride">vinyl chloride</a>. The annual production in 1985 was around 13 billion kilograms, almost all of which was converted into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyvinylchloride" title="Polyvinylchloride">polyvinylchloride</a> (PVC).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chloromethanes">Chloromethanes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organochloride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Chloromethanes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most low molecular weight chlorinated hydrocarbons such as <a href="/wiki/Chloroform" title="Chloroform">chloroform</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dichloromethane" title="Dichloromethane">dichloromethane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dichloroethene" title="Dichloroethene">dichloroethene</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Trichloroethane" title="Trichloroethane">trichloroethane</a> are useful solvents. These solvents tend to be relatively <a href="/wiki/Chemical_polarity" title="Chemical polarity">non-polar</a>; they are therefore immiscible with water and effective in cleaning applications such as <a href="/wiki/Degreasing" title="Degreasing">degreasing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dry_cleaning" title="Dry cleaning">dry cleaning</a>. Several billion kilograms of chlorinated methanes are produced annually, mainly by chlorination of methane:
</p>
<dl><dd>CH<sub>4</sub> + x Cl<sub>2</sub> → CH<sub>4−x</sub>Cl<sub>x</sub> + x HCl</dd></dl>
<p>The most important is dichloromethane, which is mainly used as a solvent. Chloromethane is a precursor to <a href="/wiki/Chlorosilane" title="Chlorosilane">chlorosilanes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Silicone" title="Silicone">silicones</a>. Historically significant, but smaller in scale is chloroform, mainly a precursor to <a href="/wiki/Chlorodifluoromethane" title="Chlorodifluoromethane">chlorodifluoromethane</a> (CHClF<sub>2</sub>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetrafluoroethene" title="Tetrafluoroethene">tetrafluoroethene</a> which is used in the manufacture of Teflon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pesticides">Pesticides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organochloride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Pesticides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The two main groups of organochlorine insecticides are the <a href="/wiki/DDT" title="DDT">DDT</a>-type compounds and the chlorinated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alicyclic" title="Alicyclic">alicyclics</a>.
Their mechanism of action differs slightly.
</p>
<ul><li>The DDT like compounds work on the <a href="/wiki/Peripheral_nervous_system" title="Peripheral nervous system">peripheral nervous system</a>.  At the axon's <a href="/wiki/Sodium_channel#Voltage-gated" title="Sodium channel">sodium channel</a>, they prevent gate closure after activation and membrane <a href="/wiki/Depolarization" title="Depolarization">depolarization</a>.  Sodium ions leak through the nerve membrane and create a destabilizing negative "afterpotential" with hyperexcitability of the nerve. This leakage causes repeated discharges in the neuron either spontaneously or after a single stimulus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coats_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-coats-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>255</span></sup></li></ul>
<ul><li>Chlorinated cyclodienes include <a href="/wiki/Aldrin" title="Aldrin">aldrin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dieldrin" title="Dieldrin">dieldrin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Endrin" title="Endrin">endrin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heptachlor" title="Heptachlor">heptachlor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chlordane" title="Chlordane">chlordane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Endosulfan" title="Endosulfan">endosulfan</a>. A 2- to 8-hour exposure leads to depressed <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a> (CNS) activity, followed by hyperexcitability, tremors, and then seizures. The mechanism of action is the insecticide binding at the <a href="/wiki/GABAA_receptor" title="GABAA receptor">GABA<sub>A</sub></a> site in the <a href="/wiki/Gamma-Aminobutyric_acid" title="Gamma-Aminobutyric acid">gamma-Aminobutyric acid</a> (GABA) chloride ionophore complex, which inhibits chloride flow into the nerve.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coats_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-coats-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>257</span></sup></li></ul>
<ul><li>Other examples include <a href="/wiki/Dicofol" title="Dicofol">dicofol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mirex" title="Mirex">mirex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kepone" title="Kepone">kepone</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pentachlorophenol" title="Pentachlorophenol">pentachlorophenol</a>.  These can be either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophilic" title="Hydrophilic">hydrophilic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrophobic" title="Hydrophobic">hydrophobic</a>, depending on their molecular structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann2_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann2-10">[10]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Insulators">Insulators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organochloride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Insulators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_biphenyl" title="Polychlorinated biphenyl">Polychlorinated biphenyls</a> (PCBs) were once commonly used electrical insulators and heat transfer agents.  Their use has generally been phased out due to health concerns. PCBs were replaced by polybrominated diphenyl ethers (PBDEs), which bring similar toxicity and <a href="/wiki/Bioaccumulation" title="Bioaccumulation">bioaccumulation</a> concerns.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicity">Toxicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Organochloride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Toxicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some types of organochlorides have significant toxicity to plants or animals, including humans.  Dioxins, produced when organic matter is burned in the presence of chlorine, are <a href="/wiki/Persistent_organic_pollutant" title="Persistent organic pollutant">persistent organic pollutants</a> which pose dangers when they are released into the environment, as are some insecticides (such as <a href="/wiki/DDT" title="DDT">DDT</a>).  For example, DDT, which was widely used to control insects in the mid-20th century, also accumulates in food chains, as do its metabolites <a href="/wiki/Dichlorodiphenyldichloroethylene" title="Dichlorodiphenyldichloroethylene">DDE</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dichlorodiphenyldichloroethane" title="Dichlorodiphenyldichloroethane">DDD</a>, and causes reproductive problems (e.g., eggshell thinning) in certain bird species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>  Some organochlorine compounds, such as <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_mustard" title="Sulfur mustard">sulfur mustards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_mustard" title="Nitrogen mustard">nitrogen mustards</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lewisite" title="Lewisite">Lewisite</a>, are even used as <a href="/wiki/Chemical_weapon" title="Chemical weapon">chemical weapons</a> due to their toxicity.
</p><p>However, the presence of chlorine in an organic compound does not <i>ensure</i> toxicity.  Some organochlorides are considered safe enough for consumption in foods and medicines. For example, peas and broad beans contain the natural chlorinated plant hormone <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/4-chloroindole-3-acetic_acid" title="4-chloroindole-3-acetic acid">4-chloroindole-3-acetic acid</a> (4-Cl-IAA);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and the sweetener <a href="/wiki/Sucralose" title="Sucralose">sucralose</a> (Splenda) is widely used in diet products. As of  2004<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Organochloride&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, at least 165 organochlorides had been approved worldwide for use as pharmaceutical drugs, including the natural antibiotic <a href="/wiki/Vancomycin" title="Vancomycin">vancomycin</a>, the antihistamine <a href="/wiki/Loratadine" title="Loratadine">loratadine</a> (Claritin), the antidepressant <a href="/wiki/Sertraline" title="Sertraline">sertraline</a> (Zoloft), the anti-epileptic <a href="/wiki/Lamotrigine" title="Lamotrigine">lamotrigine</a> (Lamictal), and the inhalation anesthetic <a href="/wiki/Isoflurane" title="Isoflurane">isoflurane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Rachel_Carson" title="Rachel Carson">Rachel Carson</a> brought the issue of DDT pesticide toxicity to public awareness with her 1962 book <i><a href="/wiki/Silent_Spring" title="Silent Spring">Silent Spring</a></i>. While many countries have phased out the use of some types of organochlorides such as the US ban on DDT, persistent DDT, PCBs, and other organochloride residues continue to be found in humans and mammals across the planet many years after production and use have been limited. In <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> areas, particularly high levels are found in <a href="/wiki/Marine_mammal" title="Marine mammal">marine mammals</a>. These chemicals concentrate in mammals, and are even found in human breast milk. In some species of marine mammals, particularly those that produce milk with a high fat content, males typically have far higher levels, as females reduce their concentration by transfer to their offspring through lactation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
