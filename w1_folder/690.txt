<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">Ecological footprint</a></div>
<p>This is a <b>list of countries by ecological footprint</b>. The table is based on data spanning from 1961 to 2013 from the
<a href="/wiki/Global_Footprint_Network" title="Global Footprint Network">Global Footprint Network</a>'s National Footprint Accounts published in 2016. Numbers are given in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_hectares" title="Global hectares">global hectares</a> per capita. The
world-average <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a> in 2012 was 2.84 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_hectares" title="Global hectares">global hectares</a> per person (22.1 billion in total). With a world-average <a href="/wiki/Biocapacity" title="Biocapacity">biocapacity</a> of 1.73 global hectares (gha) per person (9.2 billion in total), this leads to a global ecological deficit of 1.1 global hectares per person (7.8 billion in total).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>For humanity, having a footprint smaller than the planet's biocapacity is a necessary condition for sustainability. After all, ecological overuse is only possible temporarily. A country that consumes more, on average, than 1.73 gha per person has a resource demand that is not replicable world-wide. Vice versa, with a footprint below 1.73 gha per person, is not necessarily sustainable, because the quality of the footprint may still lead to ecological destruction. If a country does not have enough ecological resources within its own territory to cover its population's footprint, then it runs an ecological deficit and the country is termed an <i>ecological debtor</i>. Otherwise, it has an ecological reserve and it is called a <i>creditor</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
