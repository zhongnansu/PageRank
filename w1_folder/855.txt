<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Fact</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Fact_(disambiguation)" title="Fact (disambiguation)">Fact (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>A <b>fact</b> is something that is consistent with objective <a href="/wiki/Reality" title="Reality">reality</a> or can be proven with <a href="/wiki/Evidence" title="Evidence">evidence</a>. The usual test for a statement of fact is verifiability — that is, whether it can be demonstrated to correspond to <a href="/wiki/Experience" title="Experience">experience</a>. Standard reference works are often used to check facts. <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">Scientific</a> facts are verified by repeatable careful observation or measurement (by <a href="/wiki/Experiment" title="Experiment">experiments</a> or other means).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_usage">Etymology and usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fact&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word "fact" derives from the Latin <i>factum</i>, and was first used in English with the same meaning: <i>a thing done or performed</i>, a meaning now obsolete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>  The common usage of "something that has really occurred or is the case" dates from the middle of the sixteenth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fact is sometimes used synonymously with <i><a href="/wiki/Truth" title="Truth">truth</a></i>, as distinct from opinions, falsehoods, or matters of taste.  This use is found in such phrases as, "<i>It is a fact that the cup is blue"</i> or "<i>Matter of fact"</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and "... not history, nor fact, but imagination." Filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Werner_Herzog" title="Werner Herzog">Werner Herzog</a> distinguishes between the two, claiming that "Fact creates norms, and truth illumination."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fact also indicates a <i>matter under discussion</i> deemed to be true or correct, such as to emphasize a point or prove a disputed issue; (e.g., "... the <i>fact</i> of the matter is ...").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Alternatively, <i>fact</i> may also indicate an <i>allegation or stipulation</i> of something that may or may not be a <i>true fact</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> (e.g., "the author's facts are not trustworthy"). This alternate usage, although contested by some, has a long history in standard English.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fact may also indicate findings derived through a <i>process of evaluation</i>, including review of testimony, direct observation, or otherwise; as distinguishable from matters of inference or speculation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> This use is reflected in the terms "fact-find" and "fact-finder" (e.g., "set up a <a href="/wiki/Fact-finding" title="Fact-finding">fact-finding</a> commission").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Facts may be checked by reason, experiment, personal experience, or may be argued from authority.  <a href="/wiki/Roger_Bacon" title="Roger Bacon">Roger Bacon</a> wrote "If in other sciences we should arrive at certainty without doubt and truth without error, it behooves us to place the foundations of knowledge in mathematics."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_philosophy">In philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fact&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>, the concept <i>fact</i> is considered in <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ontology" title="Ontology">ontology</a>. Questions of <a href="/wiki/Objectivity_(philosophy)" title="Objectivity (philosophy)">objectivity</a> and truth are closely associated with questions of fact. A "fact" can be defined as something that is the case—that is, a <a href="/wiki/State_of_affairs_(philosophy)" title="State of affairs (philosophy)">state of affairs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Facts may be understood as information that makes a true sentence true.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Facts may also be understood as those things to which a true sentence refers. The statement "Jupiter is the largest planet in the solar system" is <i>about</i> the fact Jupiter is the largest planet in the solar system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Correspondence_and_the_slingshot_argument">Correspondence and the slingshot argument</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fact&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Correspondence and the slingshot argument">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Engel's version of the <a href="/wiki/Correspondence_theory_of_truth" title="Correspondence theory of truth">correspondence theory of truth</a> explains that what makes a sentence true is that it <i>corresponds</i> to a fact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
This theory presupposes the existence of an objective world.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Slingshot_argument" title="Slingshot argument">Slingshot argument</a> claims to show that all true statements stand for the same thing - the <a href="/wiki/Truth_value" title="Truth value">truth value</a> <i>true</i>. If this argument holds, and facts are taken to be what true statements stand for, then we reach the counter-intuitive conclusion that there is only one fact - <i>the truth</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Compound_facts">Compound facts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fact&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Compound facts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Any non-trivial true statement about <a href="/wiki/Reality" title="Reality">reality</a> is necessarily an abstraction composed of a complex of <a href="/wiki/Object_(philosophy)" title="Object (philosophy)">objects</a> and <a href="/wiki/Property_(philosophy)" title="Property (philosophy)">properties</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logic_of_relatives" title="Logic of relatives">relations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> For example, the fact described by the true statement "<a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> is the capital city of <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>" implies that there is such a place as Paris, there is such a place as France, there are such things as capital cities, as well as that France has a government, that the government of France has the power to define its capital city, and that the French government has chosen Paris to be the capital, that there is such a thing as a <i>place</i> or a <i>government</i>, and so on.  The verifiable accuracy of all of these assertions, if facts themselves, may coincide to create the fact that Paris is the capital of France.
</p><p>Difficulties arise, however, in attempting to identify the constituent parts of negative, modal, disjunctive, or moral facts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Fact.E2.80.93value_distinction"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Fact–value_distinction">Fact–value distinction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fact&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fact–value distinction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fact%E2%80%93value_distinction" title="Fact–value distinction">Fact–value distinction</a></div>
<p>Moral philosophers since <a href="/wiki/David_Hume#Ethics" title="David Hume">David Hume</a> have debated whether values are objective, and thus factual. In <i><a href="/wiki/A_Treatise_of_Human_Nature" title="A Treatise of Human Nature">A Treatise of Human Nature</a></i> Hume pointed out there is no obvious way for a series of statements about what <i>ought</i> to be the case to be derived from a series of statements of what <i>is</i> the case. Those who insist there is a logical gulf between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fact-value_distinction" title="Fact-value distinction">facts and values</a>, such that it is fallacious to attempt to derive values from facts, include <a href="/wiki/G._E._Moore" title="G. E. Moore">G. E. Moore</a>, who called attempting to do so the <a href="/wiki/Naturalistic_fallacy" title="Naturalistic fallacy">naturalistic fallacy</a>.
</p>
<h3><span id="Factual.E2.80.93counterfactual_distinction"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Factual–counterfactual_distinction">Factual–counterfactual distinction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fact&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Factual–counterfactual distinction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Counterfactual_conditional" title="Counterfactual conditional">Counterfactual conditional</a></div>
<p>Factuality—what has occurred—can also be contrasted with counterfactuality:
what <i>might have</i> occurred, but did not. A counterfactual conditional or subjunctive conditional is a conditional (or "if-then") statement indicating what <i>would be</i> the case if events had been other than they were. For example, "If Alexander had lived, his empire would have been greater than Rome." This contrasts with an indicative conditional, which indicates what <i>is</i> (in fact) the case if its antecedent <i>is</i> (in fact) true—for example, "If you drink this, it will make you well."
</p><p>Such sentences are important to <a href="/wiki/Modal_logic" title="Modal logic">modal logic</a>, especially since the development of <a href="/wiki/Possible_world" title="Possible world">possible world</a> semantics.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_science">In science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fact&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific method</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_science" title="Philosophy of science">philosophy of science</a></div>
<p>In science, a <i>fact</i> is a repeatable careful observation or measurement (by experimentation or other means), also called <a href="/wiki/Empirical_evidence" title="Empirical evidence">empirical evidence</a>.  Facts are central to building <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_theories" title="Scientific theories">scientific theories</a>.  Various forms of observation and measurement lead to fundamental questions about the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific method</a>, and the scope and validity of <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_science" title="Philosophy of science">scientific reasoning</a>.
</p><p>In the most basic sense, a scientific <i>fact</i> is an objective and verifiable observation, in contrast with a <i><a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">hypothesis</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Theory" title="Theory">theory</a></i>, which is intended to explain or interpret facts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gower000_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gower000-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scholars" title="Scholars">scholars</a> have offered significant refinements to this basic formulation.  Scientists are careful to distinguish between: 1) <i>states of affairs</i> in the external world and 2) <i>assertions</i> of fact that may be considered relevant in scientific analysis. The term is used in both senses in the philosophy of science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ravetz000_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ravetz000-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Scholars and clinical researchers in both the social and natural sciences have written about numerous questions and theories that arise in the attempt to clarify the fundamental nature of scientific fact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Pertinent issues raised by this inquiry include:
</p>
<ul><li>the process by which "established fact" becomes recognized and accepted as such;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li>whether and to what extent "fact" and "theoretic explanation" can be considered truly independent and separable from one another;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gower,_p._138_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gower,_p._138-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li>to what extent "facts" are influenced by the mere act of observation;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gower,_p._138_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gower,_p._138-25">[25]</a></sup> and</li>
<li>to what extent factual conclusions are influenced by history and consensus, rather than a strictly systematic methodology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Consistent with the idea of <a href="/wiki/Confirmation_holism" title="Confirmation holism">confirmation holism</a>, some scholars assert "fact" to be necessarily "theory-laden" to some degree. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Kuhn" title="Thomas Kuhn">Thomas Kuhn</a> points out that knowing what facts to measure, and how to measure them, requires the use of other theories. For example, the age of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossils" title="Fossils">fossils</a> is based on <a href="/wiki/Radiometric_dating" title="Radiometric dating">radiometric dating</a>, which is justified by reasoning that radioactive decay follows a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poisson_process" title="Poisson process">Poisson process</a> rather than a <a href="/wiki/Bernoulli_process" title="Bernoulli process">Bernoulli process</a>. Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Percy_Williams_Bridgman" title="Percy Williams Bridgman">Percy Williams Bridgman</a> is credited with the methodological position known as operationalism, which asserts that all observations are not only influenced, but necessarily defined by the means and assumptions used to measure them.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_scientific_method">The scientific method</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fact&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The scientific method">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Apart from the fundamental inquiry into the nature of scientific fact, there remain the practical and social considerations of how fact is investigated, established, and substantiated through the proper application of the scientific method.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Scientific facts are generally believed independent of the observer: no matter who performs a scientific experiment, all observers agree on the outcome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Nature_of_Suffering_and_the_Goals_of_Medicine_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Nature_of_Suffering_and_the_Goals_of_Medicine-28">[28]</a></sup>
In addition to these considerations, there are the social and institutional measures, such as peer review and accreditation, that are intended to promote <i>factual accuracy</i> (among other interests) in scientific study.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_history">In history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fact&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Historiography" title="Historiography">Historiography</a></div>
<p>A common rhetorical cliché states, "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_is_written_by_the_winners" title="History is written by the winners">History is written by the winners</a>." This phrase suggests but does not examine the use of facts in the writing of history.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/E._H._Carr" title="E. H. Carr">E. H. Carr</a> in his 1961 volume <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/What_is_History%3F" title="What is History?">What is History?</a></i> argues that the inherent biases from the gathering of facts makes the objective truth of any historical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perspective_(cognitive)" title="Perspective (cognitive)">perspective</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Idealistic" title="Idealistic">idealistic</a> and impossible. Facts are, "like fish in the Ocean," of which we may only happen to catch a few, only an indication of what is below the surface. Even a dragnet cannot tell us for certain what it would be like to live below the Ocean's surface. Even if we do not discard any facts (or fish) presented, we will always miss the majority; the site of our fishing, the methods undertaken, the weather and even luck play a vital role in what we will catch. Additionally, the composition of history is inevitably made up by the compilation of many different biases of fact finding - all compounded over time. He concludes that for a historian to attempt a more objective method, one must accept that history can only aspire to a conversation of the present with the past - and that one's methods of fact gathering should be openly examined.  Historical truth and facts therefore change over time, and reflect only the present consensus (if that).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_law">In law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fact&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: In law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Evidence_(law)" title="Evidence (law)">Evidence (law)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trier_of_fact" title="Trier of fact">Trier of fact</a></div>
<p>In most <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> jurisdictions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> the general concept and analysis of fact reflects fundamental principles of <a href="/wiki/Jurisprudence" title="Jurisprudence">jurisprudence</a>, and is supported by several well-established standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Estrich000_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Estrich000-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elkouri000_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Elkouri000-32">[32]</a></sup> Matters of fact have various formal definitions under common law jurisdictions.
</p><p>These include:
</p>
<ul><li>an element required in legal pleadings to demonstrate a cause of action;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bishin000_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bishin000-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yale001_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yale001-34">[34]</a></sup></li></ul>
<ul><li>the determinations of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finder_of_fact" title="Finder of fact">finder of fact</a> after evaluating admissible evidence produced in a trial or hearing;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></li>
<li>a potential ground of reversible error forwarded on appeal in an <a href="/wiki/Appellate_court" title="Appellate court">appellate court</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Houston000_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Houston000-36">[36]</a></sup> and</li>
<li>any of various matters subject to investigation by official authority to establish whether a <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">crime</a> has been perpetrated, and to establish culpability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bennett000_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett000-37">[37]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_pleadings">Legal pleadings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fact&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Legal pleadings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pleading" title="Pleading">Pleading</a></div>
<p>A party to a civil suit generally must clearly state all relevant allegations of fact that form the basis of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claim_(legal)" title="Claim (legal)">claim</a>. The requisite level of precision and particularity of these allegations varies, depending on the rules of civil procedure and jurisdiction. Parties who face uncertainties regarding facts and circumstances attendant to their side in a dispute may sometimes invoke alternative pleading.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In this situation, a party may plead separate sets of facts that (when considered together) may be contradictory or mutually exclusive. This (seemingly) logically-inconsistent presentation of facts may be necessary as a safeguard against contingencies (such as <i><a href="/wiki/Res_judicata" title="Res judicata">res judicata</a></i>) that would otherwise preclude presenting a claim or defense that depends on a particular interpretation of the underlying facts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
