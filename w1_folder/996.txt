<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Carbon capture and storage in Australia</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Carbon capture and storage</b> (<b>CCS</b>) is an approach to try to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitigation_of_global_warming" title="Mitigation of global warming">mitigate</a> global warming by capturing <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> CO<sub>2</sub> from large point sources such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_power_plant" title="Fossil fuel power plant">fossil fuel power plants</a> and storing it instead of releasing it into the atmosphere. <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">Carbon capture and storage</a> is also used to sequester CO<sub>2</sub> filtered out of natural gas from certain from natural gas fields. While typically the CO<sub>2</sub> has no value after being stored, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enhanced_Oil_Recovery" title="Enhanced Oil Recovery">Enhanced Oil Recovery</a> uses CO<sub>2</sub> to increase yield from declining oil fields.
</p><p>There are no currently-operating large-scale CCS projects in Australia, although the <a href="/wiki/Gorgon_gas_project" title="Gorgon gas project">Gorgon gas project</a> will qualify when it is fully operational. Despite multiple CCS demonstration projects at Australian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal-fired_power_station" title="Coal-fired power station">coal-fired power stations</a>, none of Australia's coal plants are currently capturing CO<sub>2</sub> or have a time frame for doing so. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Treasury" title="Australian Treasury">Australian Treasury</a> modelling finds that CCS is not expected to be commercially viable until the 2030s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC) estimates that the economic potential of CCS could be between 10% and 55% of the total carbon mitigation effort until 2100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC_CC_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC_CC-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_federal_budget,_2017" title="Australian federal budget, 2017">2017 budget</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Turnbull_Government" title="Turnbull Government">Turnbull Government</a> announced the cessation of the Low Emissions Technology Demonstration Fund in the 2017 financial year and the cessation of business case funding for the Carbon Capture and Storage Flagships program in financial year 2019.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This is on top of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_federal_budget,_2015" title="Australian federal budget, 2015">2015 budget</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/Abbott_Government" title="Abbott Government">Abbott Government</a> cut $460m from CCS research projects leaving $191.7m to continue existing projects for the next seven years. The program had already been cut by the previous Labor government and much of the funding remained unallocated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits">Benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ol><li>Fossil fuels are capable of dispatching electricity whenever there is demand.</li>
<li>Australia has significant deposits of coal allowing economic benefits for years to come without significant environmental impacts.</li>
<li>CCS can be used to capture CO2 from high-emission industrial processes such as the making of certain chemicals, steel and cement.</li>
<li>CCS, when it comes on line, is likely to make the greatest impact in reducing greenhouse gas emissions during the transition to sustainable energy supplies.</li></ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Challenges">Challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ol><li>Cost of CCS will make coal-fired electricity more expensive than wind power <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>Leakage from underground or undersea reservoirs</li>
<li>Scarcity of potential sites and capacity compared to volumes of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> needed to be sequestered on an ongoing basis</li>
<li>Existing power stations unlikely to be able to have carbon capture technology retrofitted <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>CCS currently requires up to 30% more coal than conventional plants to cover the energy needs of CCS (although R&amp;D is rapidly improving efficiencies), and that extra coal must first be mined (which has environmental effects) and transported to the plant (which takes energy)</li>
<li>Infrastructure required would take years to build</li>
<li>emissions of <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a>-causing gases like nitrogen oxides and sulfur oxides of a plant that captures CO<sub>2</sub> will be up to 40 percent greater than the total cradle-to-grave emissions of a modern plant that doesn't capture its CO<sub>2</sub> because of the extra coal burnt <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport_of_CO2">Transport of CO<sub>2</sub></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Transport of CO2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Australia, the major emissions sites are in the <a href="/wiki/Latrobe_Valley" title="Latrobe Valley">Latrobe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hunter_Region" title="Hunter Region">Hunter Valleys</a>. The Latrobe Valley has considerable potential storage within a few hundred kilometres in Bass Strait which the CarbonNet Project was investigating (see below). There are no particularly promising large storage prospects near the Hunter Valley. Geologically prospective areas include the North West Shelf (see the Gorgon Project below) and Bass Strait. Australia has very extensive basins with deep saline formations, both onshore and particularly offshore, in which large quantities of carbon dioxide can dissolve. In such formations Australia has a potential carbon dioxide storage resource equivalent to many hundreds of years of emissions at the current rate. Work is now underway to fully assess storage potential.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_involvement">Government involvement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Government involvement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In November 2008, the Australian Commonwealth Government passed the Offshore Petroleum Amendment (Greenhouse Gas Storage) Act 2008, which provides a regulatory framework for carbon dioxide storage in federal offshore waters.
</p><p>The Victorian Greenhouse Gas Geological Sequestration Act 2008 (No. 61 of 2008) received Royal Assent on 5 November 2008. It provides a dedicated legal framework enabling the onshore injection and permanent storage of greenhouse gas substances. The state government has also developed a regulatory framework for offshore storage sites (i.e. those sites falling within the 3 nautical mile extent of state jurisdiction; the Offshore Petroleum and Greenhouse Gas Storage Act 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Both Australia's state and federal governments have been major contributors to CCS research and development. Federal government CCS initiatives include the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=CO2CRC&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="CO2CRC (page does not exist)">CO2CRC</a> (founded 2003), the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Low_Emission_Technology_Demonstration_Fund&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Low Emission Technology Demonstration Fund (page does not exist)">Low Emission Technology Demonstration Fund</a> (2004-2017), funding for the <a href="/wiki/Asia-Pacific_Partnership_on_Clean_Development_and_Climate" title="Asia-Pacific Partnership on Clean Development and Climate">Asia-Pacific Partnership on Clean Development and Climate</a> (2006-2011, renewables, CCS and other), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=National_Low_Emissions_Coal_Initiative&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="National Low Emissions Coal Initiative (page does not exist)">National Low Emissions Coal Initiative</a> (founded 2008), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Global_CCS_Institute&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Global CCS Institute (page does not exist)">Global CCS Institute</a> (founded 2009), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_Capture_and_Storage_Flagships&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Carbon Capture and Storage Flagships (page does not exist)">Carbon Capture and Storage Flagships</a> (2009-2019), the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_Capture_and_Storage_Research_Development_and_Demonstration_Fund&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Carbon Capture and Storage Research Development and Demonstration Fund (page does not exist)">Carbon Capture and Storage Research Development and Demonstration Fund</a> (2015-2016) and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=National_CO2_Infrastructure_Plan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="National CO2 Infrastructure Plan (page does not exist)">National CO2 Infrastructure Plan</a> operated by Geoscience Australia (2012-2016).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Federal funding commitments to these initiatives total $3.5-3.6 billion, of which $1.3-$1.6 billion has been committed or is expected to be committed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2017, Prime Minister Malcolm Turnbull said:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>We've invested $590 million since 2009 in clean coal technology research and demonstration and yet we do not have one modern high-efficiency low-emissions coal-fired power station, let alone one with carbon capture and storage.
</p></blockquote><p>In April 2018, a parliamentary inquiry heard from energy researchers that carbon capture and storage requires a price on carbon to be viable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_projects_in_operation">Commercial projects in operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Commercial projects in operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are no large-scale commercial CCS projects within Australia. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Global_CCS_Institute&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Global CCS Institute (page does not exist)">Global CCS Institute</a> defines "large-scale" as 400,000 tonnes of CO<sub>2</sub> per annum, or 800,000 tonnes per annum for a coal plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> It previously used a one million tonnes per annum threshold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Demonstration and proposed projects and projects under construction are listed below with brief descriptions.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demonstration_projects">Demonstration projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Demonstration projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CO2CRC_Otway_Project">CO2CRC Otway Project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: CO2CRC Otway Project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The CO2CRC Otway Project in Western Victoria is a demonstration project which has injected and stored over 65,000 tonnes of carbon dioxide in a depleted natural gas reservoir 2 km below the Earth’s surface. The project was first proposed to the Board of the then Australian Petroleum Cooperative Research Centre (APCRC) in March 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> There has been no sign of leakage according to a comprehensive monitoring and verification program. A mixture of Carbon dioxide and methane gas is extracted from a well in the Bathurst field, then compressed and transported via dedicated pipeline to the Naylor field two kilometres away. The gases are then injected into the depleted gas reservoir through a dedicated injection well. A nearby well (previously used to produce natural gas) is used to monitor the injected carbon dioxide. A second stage of the project, involving evaluation of carbon dioxide storage in deep saline formations, has been highly successful and provided data on estimating CO2 storage capacity using an innovative single well test. The project is Australia's first demonstration of geosequestration and one of the world's largest geosequestration research projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> This area has active exploration for geothermal and petroleum resources and has been supported by geotechnical work completed by the public sector and the private sector.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post_combustion_capture_projects_in_the_Latrobe_Valley">Post combustion capture projects in the Latrobe Valley</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Post combustion capture projects in the Latrobe Valley">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Latrobe Valley Post Combustion Capture Project was a joint collaboration between Loy Yang Power, International Power Hazelwood, government and researchers from CSIRO’s Energy Transformed Flagship and CO2CRC (including Monash and Melbourne Universities), involving research at both Loy Yang and Hazelwood power stations. The 10.5-metre-high pilot plant at Loy Yang was designed to capture up to 1,000 tonnes of CO<sub>2</sub> per annum from the power station's exhaust-gas flues. Future trials were expected to involve the use of a range of different CO<sub>2</sub>-capture liquids. On 9 July 2008, CSIRO Energy Technology Chief Dr David Brockway announced that carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>) had been captured from power station flue gases in a post-combustion-capture (PCC) pilot plant at Loy Yang Power Station in Victoria’s Latrobe Valley. The purpose of the pilot plant is to conduct research, not to capture all the emissions from the power station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Further government projects in this area led to many geo-technical studies that review gas and liquid migration, trapping and leakage. While the Gippsland area has been described as a basin margin, this is somewhat vague. The area defines a major fold belt onshore and offshore. The key risk to injection of CO<sub>2</sub> in the area is the ability to keep gas in the ground. Multiple regional and local studies over the area have been completed by government and private companies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2016, the PICA Post-Combustion Project was announced. This is a joint collaboration between <a href="/wiki/CSIRO" title="CSIRO">CSIRO</a>, <a href="/wiki/IHI_Corporation" title="IHI Corporation">IHI Corporation</a> (Japanese technology provider) and <a href="/wiki/AGL_Energy" title="AGL Energy">AGL</a>. It will use a pilot plant to test CO<sub>2</sub>-capture liquids over a two-year period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="The_CO2CRC.2FHRL_Mulgrave_Capture_Project"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_CO2CRC/HRL_Mulgrave_Capture_Project">The CO2CRC/HRL Mulgrave Capture Project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The CO2CRC/HRL Mulgrave Capture Project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CO2CRC commissioned three carbon dioxide capture research rigs at HRL’s gasifier research facility at Mulgrave in Melbourne, Victoria. The CO2CRC rigs captured carbon dioxide from <a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">syngas</a>, the product of the brown coal gasifier, using solvent, membrane and adsorbent technologies. The capture technologies are equally applicable to syngas from brown and black coal, gas or biomass fuels. During the project, researchers evaluated each technology for efficiency and cost-effectiveness. Advanced gasifier technologies are highly suitable for carbon dioxide capture for CCS as they produce a concentrated stream of carbon dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_projects">Proposed projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Proposed projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Gorgon_gas_field.2C_Barrow_Island"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Gorgon_gas_field,_Barrow_Island">Gorgon gas field, Barrow Island</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Gorgon gas field, Barrow Island">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This project led by <a href="/wiki/Chevron_Corporation" title="Chevron Corporation">Chevron</a> will be designed to capture 3.5 Mt of carbon dioxide per annum from <a href="/wiki/Gorgon_gas_project" title="Gorgon gas project">Greater Gorgon gas fields</a> and store it in the Dupuy formation beneath the <a href="/wiki/Barrow_Island_(Western_Australia)" title="Barrow Island (Western Australia)">Barrow Island.</a> The project will be the largest carbon dioxide sequestration operation in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Chevron is liable for leaks and other damage during the project's lifetime and for 15 years afterwards,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> but in 2009 the state and federal governments agreed to indemnify Chevron against liability for the project after that time,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> with the Commonwealth in 2015 confirming it would adopt 80% of the liability and WA the remaining 20%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>When construction on the project began in 2009, it was expected to be completed by 2014<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> - including carbon capture and storage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The project ultimately started extracting gas in February 2017 but carbon capture and storage is now not expected to begin until March 2019,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> requiring a further five million tonnes of CO<sub>2</sub> to be released, because: 
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>A Chevron report to the State Government released yesterday said start-up checks this year found leaking valves, valves that could corrode and excess water in the pipeline from the LNG plant to the injection wells that could cause the pipeline to corrode.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>In May 2018, the WA Environmental Protection Authority announced an investigation into whether Gorgon could meet its storage commitments given the delays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>WWF claims that the Gorgon geosequestration project is potentially unsafe as the area has over 700 wells drilled in the area, 50 of which reach the area proposed for geosequestration of CO<sub>2</sub>. Fault lines compound the problems. Barrow Island is also an A class nature reserve of global importance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrogen_Energy_Supply_Chain_project">Hydrogen Energy Supply Chain project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Hydrogen Energy Supply Chain project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In April 2018, the federal and Victorian governments announced a brown coal-to-hydrogen project that would operate out of <a href="/wiki/AGL_Energy" title="AGL Energy">AGL Energy</a>'s <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Loy_Yang_A&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Loy Yang A (page does not exist)">Loy Yang A</a> power station. Construction is expected to begin in 2019, and hydrogen production by 2020 or 2021.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>The pilot program will not include carbon capture and storage,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> but it is expected if the project is expanded, with proponent <a href="/wiki/Kawasaki_Heavy_Industries" title="Kawasaki Heavy Industries">Kawasaki Heavy Industries</a> says that coal-to-hydrogen technology is not commercial without it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_CarbonNet_Project">The CarbonNet Project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: The CarbonNet Project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=CarbonNet&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="CarbonNet (page does not exist)">CarbonNet</a> was established by the Victorian Government in 2009 to investigate the potential for establishing a world class, large-scale, multi-user carbon capture and storage network in Victoria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> In 2012 the Australian Government selected CarbonNet as one of only two CCS flagship projects under its Clean Energy Initiative and, with the State of Victoria, awarded the project a further $100 million in joint funding to undertake feasibility. The Global CCS Institute provided $2.3 million in support.
</p><p>In 2016 it was reported that "When Australia repealed the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_pricing_in_Australia" title="Carbon pricing in Australia">carbon price</a> the project did not advance",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> but as of January 2018 the project was conducting a 17-day seismic survey of former oil wells in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gippsland_Basin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gippsland Basin (page does not exist)">Gippsland Basin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Completed_projects">Completed projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Completed projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Callide_Oxyfuel_Project.2C_Queensland"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Callide_Oxyfuel_Project,_Queensland">Callide Oxyfuel Project, Queensland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Callide Oxyfuel Project, Queensland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Callide Oxyfuel Project demonstrated carbon capture using oxyfuel combustion, but did not achieve carbon storage. The Oxyfuel boiler operated for two years and nine months, beyond the project's expected duration, and achieved "partial capture" of 75 tonnes of CO<sub>2</sub> per day (27,300 tonnes per annum). The project team assessed eight potential carbon storage sites but were unsuitable because of location, availability and geological profile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>The project was headed by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=CS_Energy_Ltd&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="CS Energy Ltd (page does not exist)">CS Energy Ltd</a> (which operates the Callide power plants) in conjunction with an international team of partners, including IHI Corporation (Japan), J-Power (Japan), Mitsui &amp; Company (Japan) Schlumberger Oilfields Australia and Xstrata Coal. The <a href="/wiki/Australian_Coal_Association" title="Australian Coal Association">Australian Coal Association</a> (through the industry fund <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Coal21&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Coal21 (page does not exist)">Coal21</a>), and the Commonwealth (through the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Low_Emission_Technology_Demonstration_Fund&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Low Emission Technology Demonstration Fund (page does not exist)">Low Emission Technology Demonstration Fund</a>, Queensland and Japanese governments provided financial support for the project, which was expected to cost A$208 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> It was a project for the <a href="/wiki/Asia-Pacific_Partnership_on_Clean_Development_and_Climate" title="Asia-Pacific Partnership on Clean Development and Climate">Asia-Pacific Partnership on Clean Development and Climate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2017, <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">chief executive officer</a> of project proponent <a href="/wiki/CS_Energy" title="CS Energy">CS Energy</a> said of the Callide project:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>We proved that technologically it’s possible to retrofit [CCS] to existing coal-fired plants, but commercially, the numbers don’t stack up …  It’s unlikely there will be [a commercial operation for CCS in Australia], I think that technology may well be bypassed … simply because of the economics. … If you could decarbonise coal by capturing and sequestering the emissions, then you’d have clean coal. It sounds easy if you say it fast enough, but it’s not that simple.
</p></blockquote>
<h3><span id="International_Power_Carbon_Capture_Plant.2C_Victoria_.28Hazelwood_2030.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="International_Power_Carbon_Capture_Plant,_Victoria_(Hazelwood_2030)">International Power Carbon Capture Plant, Victoria (Hazelwood 2030)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: International Power Carbon Capture Plant, Victoria (Hazelwood 2030)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A post-combustion capture plant operated at the International Power GDF Suez Hazelwood Power Station <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hazelwood_Power_Station,_Victoria" title="Hazelwood Power Station, Victoria">Hazelwood</a>. 
</p><p>When announced in 2007, the project was originally planned as a retro-fit of one of Hazelwood's eight generating units, which would have reduced its emissions intensity by 20% (500,000 tonnes per annum). It was offered a $50 million federal government grant from the Low Emissions Technology Development Fund and a $30 million Victorian government grant from the Energy Technology Innovation Strategy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>A pilot program with a more modest capture objective was completed. The solvent capture plant cost $10 million (including grants from state and federal governments) and began operation in 2009, capturing and chemically sequestered CO<sub>2</sub> at a nominal rate of 10,000 tonnes per annum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hazelwood Power Station closed in March 2017.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Delayed_projects">Delayed projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Delayed projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Monash_coal-to-liquids">Monash coal-to-liquids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Monash coal-to-liquids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 2 December 2008 Shell and Anglo American announced that this possible <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brown_coal" title="Brown coal">brown coal</a> project in the <a href="/wiki/Latrobe_Valley" title="Latrobe Valley">Latrobe Valley</a> will not proceed at present. They have described it as a "long term" opportunity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>The planned project was planned to have some CCS, storing the gas captured in depleted off-shore oil fields in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gippsland_Basin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gippsland Basin (page does not exist)">Gippsland Basin</a> in east <a href="/wiki/Bass_Strait" title="Bass Strait">Bass Strait</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cancelled_projects">Cancelled projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Cancelled projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="BP_Kwinana_.28WA.29_coal_to_gas_plant"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="BP_Kwinana_(WA)_coal_to_gas_plant">BP Kwinana (WA) coal to gas plant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: BP Kwinana (WA) coal to gas plant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A proposed $2 billion "hydrogen energy" coal-to-gas plant will not proceed because the geological formations off Perth, which were intended to sequester the CO<sub>2</sub>, contain gas "chimneys" that "mean it is next to impossible to establish a seal in the strata that could contain the CO2".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zerogen_power_station">Zerogen power station</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Zerogen power station">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Zerogen powerstation project near Stanwell power station in Queensland is proposed to be a 100 MW "Integrated Gasification Combined Cycle" power station with CCS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> In late 2010, the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Queensland" title="Government of Queensland">Government of Queensland</a> announced it would not fund the Zerogen project because it was not economically viable and that it would be sold off.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bdccb_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-bdccb-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fairview_project">Fairview project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_capture_and_storage_in_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Fairview project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Fairview Project, near Roma in <a href="/wiki/South_West_Queensland" title="South West Queensland">South West Queensland</a>, was intended to capture 1/3 of the CO<sub>2</sub> emissions from a 100 MW coal seam methane gas-fired power station. In 2006 it was selected to receive federal government funding,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> but as of September 2017 it does not appear on the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Global_CCS_Institute&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Global CCS Institute (page does not exist)">Global CCS Institute</a>'s list of projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p>
