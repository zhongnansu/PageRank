<p><b>SaskPower</b> is the principal <a href="/wiki/Electric_utility" title="Electric utility">electric utility</a> in <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a>, Canada. Established in 1929 by the <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan#Government_and_politics" title="Saskatchewan">provincial government</a>, it serves more than 522,000 customers and manages over $10 billion in assets. SaskPower is a major employer in the province with over 3,150 permanent full-time staff located in approximately 70 communities.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_status">Legal status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SaskPower&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Legal status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SaskPower_Building,_Regina.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/SaskPower_Building%2C_Regina.jpg/220px-SaskPower_Building%2C_Regina.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/SaskPower_Building%2C_Regina.jpg/330px-SaskPower_Building%2C_Regina.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/SaskPower_Building%2C_Regina.jpg/440px-SaskPower_Building%2C_Regina.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SaskPower_Building,_Regina.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan_Power_Building" title="Saskatchewan Power Building">SaskPower head office</a> in Regina</div></div></div>
<p>SaskPower was founded as the Saskatchewan Power Commission in 1929, becoming the Saskatchewan Power Corporation in 1949.  The abbreviated name SaskPower was officially adopted in 1987.
</p><p>Owned by the government through its holding company, the <a href="/wiki/Crown_Investments_Corporation" title="Crown Investments Corporation">Crown Investments Corporation</a>, SaskPower is governed by a <a href="#Corporate_governance">Board of Directors</a> who are accountable to the provincial government Minister Responsible for Saskatchewan Power Corporation.
</p><p>SaskPower has the exclusive right and the exclusive obligation to supply electricity in the province, except in the city of <a href="/wiki/Swift_Current" title="Swift Current">Swift Current</a> and most of the city of <a href="/wiki/Saskatoon" title="Saskatoon">Saskatoon</a>.  The Swift Current Department of Light and Power provides electrical services within the municipal boundary of Swift Current.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Saskatoon_Light_%26_Power" title="Saskatoon Light &amp; Power">Saskatoon Light &amp; Power</a> provides service to the customers within the 1958 boundaries of Saskatoon while SaskPower has responsibility for areas annexed after 1958.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Customers">Customers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SaskPower&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Customers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SaskPower_Boundary_Dam_GS.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/SaskPower_Boundary_Dam_GS.jpg/220px-SaskPower_Boundary_Dam_GS.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/SaskPower_Boundary_Dam_GS.jpg/330px-SaskPower_Boundary_Dam_GS.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/SaskPower_Boundary_Dam_GS.jpg/440px-SaskPower_Boundary_Dam_GS.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SaskPower_Boundary_Dam_GS.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Boundary Dam Power Station</div></div></div>
<p>SaskPower serves more than 522,000 customers through more than 157,000 kilometres of power lines throughout the province and covers a service territory that includes Saskatchewan's geographic area of approximately 651,000 km<sup>2</sup> (251,000 sq mi). This relatively low customer density means that while most North American electrical utilities supply an average of 12 customers per circuit kilometre, SaskPower supplies about three. In fiscal year 2015-16, total electricity revenue was $2,690 million (Canadian) on sales of 27,382 gigawatt hours of electricity.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Facilities">Facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SaskPower&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FrancoisFinlaydam2007.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/FrancoisFinlaydam2007.jpg/220px-FrancoisFinlaydam2007.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/FrancoisFinlaydam2007.jpg/330px-FrancoisFinlaydam2007.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/FrancoisFinlaydam2007.jpg/440px-FrancoisFinlaydam2007.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FrancoisFinlaydam2007.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Francois Finlay Dam and Nipawin Hydroelectric Station</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_power_stations_in_Saskatchewan" title="List of power stations in Saskatchewan">List of power stations in Saskatchewan</a></div>
<p>SaskPower has a generating capacity of 3,542 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">megawatts</a> (MW) from 17 generating facilities, including three coal-fired power stations, five natural gas stations, seven hydroelectric stations, and two wind power facilities.  SaskPower also buys power from various independent power producers (IPPs) including the North Battleford Generating Station, Cory Cogeneration Station, Spy Hill Generating Station, Morse Wind Energy Facility, Red Lily Wind Power Facility and SunBridge Wind Energy Facility. SaskPower's total available generation capacity is 4,437 MW.
</p><p>The SaskPower transmission system utilizes lines carrying 230,000 volts, 138,000 volts and 72,000 volts. SaskPower has interconnections at the Manitoba, Alberta and North Dakota borders.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rural_areas">Rural areas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SaskPower&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rural areas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Incorporated under <i>The Power Corporation Act</i> (1949), SaskPower purchased the majority of the province’s small, independent municipal electrical utilities and integrated them into a province-wide grid.  It was also responsible under <i>The Rural Electrification Act</i> (1949) for the electrification of the province’s rural areas, bringing electricity to over 66,000 farms between 1949 and 1966.  To manage the high costs of electrifying the province’s sparsely populated rural areas, SaskPower used a large-scale implementation of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single_wire_earth_return" title="Single wire earth return">single wire ground return</a> distribution system, claimed to be a pioneering effort (although some utilities in the USA had been using such a system on its rural lines). It was at the time one of the largest such systems in the world. One of the last cities in the province added to SaskPower's system was North Portal in 1971 (which had been served up to this point from Montana-Dakota Utilities' distribution system in Portal, ND just across the border).
</p>

<ul><li><b>NorthPoint Energy Solutions Inc.</b>, located in <a href="/wiki/Regina,_Saskatchewan" title="Regina, Saskatchewan">Regina, Saskatchewan</a> is a wholly owned subsidiary of SaskPower and is SaskPower's wholesale energy marketing agent.  NorthPoint began operation on November 1, 2001.  NorthPoint handles the export of power on the North American Market.</li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rural_electrification">Rural electrification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SaskPower&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Rural electrification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Rural_electrification" title="Rural electrification">Rural electrification</a></div>
<p>SaskPower was founded by an Act of the provincial legislature as the <i>Saskatchewan Power Commission</i> in 1929.  The purpose of the Commission was to research how best to create a provincial power system which would provide the province’s residents with safe, reliable electric service.
</p><p>A provincial power system was desirable for many reasons.  In the early days of electricity in the province of Saskatchewan, electricity was largely unavailable outside of larger centres.  Most electrical utilities were owned either privately or by municipalities, and none of them were interconnected.  Because each utility operated independently, rates often varied significantly between communities – anywhere from 4<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> to 45<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> cents per kilowatt hour in the mid-1920s.  The rapid growth in the province’s <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan#Demographics" title="Saskatchewan">population</a> in the first decades of the century – from 91,279 to 757,510 within 20 years – had led to a sharp increase in the demand for electricity.  Finally, the provincial government had determined that the lack of inexpensive power was hampering the development of industry in the province (Ref).
</p><p>While the Commission began purchasing independently owned electrical utilities with the goal of interconnecting them, the economic situation of the 1930s and the labour shortage caused by the Second World War delayed the creation of a provincial power system for nearly two decades.
</p><p>By 1948, the Commission operated 35 generating stations and more than 8,800 km of transmission lines. However, most farm families who had electricity generated it themselves using battery systems charged by wind turbines or gasoline- or diesel-powered generators. Across the province, only 1,500 farms were connected to the electrical grid, most of them because of their proximity to the lines that linked cities and larger towns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1949, by an Act of the Provincial Legislature, the Commission became the Saskatchewan Power Corporation.  The first task of the new Corporation was to purchase what remained of the province’s small, independent electrical utilities and to begin integrating them into a province-wide electrical grid.
</p><p>The final step in creating a truly province-wide grid was to electrify the province’s vast rural areas.  The primary hurdle to rural electrification was the very low customer density in the province – approximately one farm customer per network mile (1.6 km) – and the extremely high cost of a network of the scale required by the vast distances between customers.  After much study, the Corporation adopted a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single_wire_earth_return" title="Single wire earth return">single wire ground return</a> distribution scheme, which lowered the cost of rural electrification significantly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saskpower.com_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Saskpower.com-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first year of the program set the goal of connecting 1200 rural customers to the network.  The experience gained during the first years led to an increased rate of connections every year, leading to a peak yearly connection rate in 1956 of 7,800 customers.  By 1961, 58,000 farms were connected, and by 1966 when the program concluded, the Corporation had provided power to a total of 66,000 rural customers.  In addition, hundreds of schools, churches and community halls received electrical service during this period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saskpower.com_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Saskpower.com-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
