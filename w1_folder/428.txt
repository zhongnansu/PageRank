<p>The <b>Globally Harmonized System of Classification and Labelling of Chemicals</b> (<b>GHS</b>) is an internationally agreed-upon standard managed by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> that was set up to replace the assortment of hazardous material classification and labelling schemes previously used around the world. Core elements of the GHS include standardized hazard testing criteria, universal warning pictograms, and harmonized safety data sheets which provide users of dangerous goods with a host of information. The system acts as a complement to the <a href="/wiki/UN_number" title="UN number">UN Numbered</a> system of regulated hazmat transport. Implementation is managed through the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Secretariat" title="United Nations Secretariat">UN Secretariat</a>. Although adoption has taken time, as of 2017, the system has been enacted to significant extents in most major countries of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> This includes the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, which has implemented the United Nations' GHS into EU law as the <a href="/wiki/CLP_Regulation" title="CLP Regulation">CLP Regulation</a>, and United States <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">Occupational Safety and Health Administration</a> standards.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Globally_Harmonized_System_of_Classification_and_Labelling_of_Chemicals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before the Globally Harmonized System of Classification and Labelling of Chemicals (GHS) was created and implemented, there were many different regulations on hazard classification in use in different countries, resulting in multiple standards, classifications and labels for the same hazard. Given the extent of international trade in chemicals and the potential impact on neighboring countries when controls are not implemented, countries determined that a worldwide approach was necessary.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The GHS development began at the 1992 Rio Conference on Environment and Development by the United Nations<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> also called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_Summit_(1992)" title="Earth Summit (1992)">Earth Summit (1992)</a> when the <a href="/wiki/International_Labour_Organization" title="International Labour Organization">International Labour Organization</a> (ILO), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organisation_for_Economic_Co-operation_and_Development" title="Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development">Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development</a> (OECD), various governments and other stakeholders agreed that "A globally harmonized hazard classification and compatible labelling system, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Material_safety_data_sheets" title="Material safety data sheets">material safety data sheets</a> and easily understandable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symbols" title="Symbols">symbols</a>, should be available if feasible, by the year 2000".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-visual_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-visual-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The universal standard for all countries was to replace all the diverse classification systems; however, it has not been compulsory under UN law. The GHS provides the infrastructure for participating countries to implement a hazard classification and <a href="/wiki/Hazard_Communication_Standard" title="Hazard Communication Standard">Hazard Communication Standard</a>, which many less economically developed countries would not have had the money to create themselves.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the longer term, the GHS is expected to improve knowledge of the chronic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Health_hazard" title="Health hazard">health hazards</a> of chemicals and encourage a move towards the elimination of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hazardous_chemical" title="Hazardous chemical">hazardous chemicals</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mutagen" title="Mutagen">mutagens</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reproductive_toxicant" title="Reproductive toxicant">reproductive toxicants</a>, or their replacement with less hazardous ones.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hazard_classification">Hazard classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Globally_Harmonized_System_of_Classification_and_Labelling_of_Chemicals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hazard classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The GHS classification system is a complex system with data obtained from tests, literature, and practical experience.
</p><p>The main elements of the hazard classification criteria are summarized below:
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_hazards">Physical hazards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Globally_Harmonized_System_of_Classification_and_Labelling_of_Chemicals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physical hazards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Substances or articles are assigned to 9 different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hazard_class" title="Hazard class">hazard classes</a> largely based on the <a href="/wiki/UN_Recommendations_on_the_Transport_of_Dangerous_Goods" title="UN Recommendations on the Transport of Dangerous Goods">United Nations Dangerous Goods System</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>59–60</span></sup> Additions and changes have been necessary since the scope of the GHS includes all target audiences.
</p>
<ol><li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explosives" title="Explosives">Explosives</a></b>, which are assigned to one of six subcategories depending on the type of hazard they present, as used in the UN Dangerous Goods System.</li>
<li><b>Gases</b> are category 1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flammable" title="Flammable">flammable</a> if they start to flame in a range in air at 20 °C and a standard pressure of 101.3 kPa. Category 2 is Non flammable and non toxic gases, and category 3 is toxic gases. Substances and mixtures of this hazard class are assigned to one of two hazard categories on the basis of the outcome of the test or calculation method.</li>
<li>A <b><a href="/wiki/Flammable_liquid" title="Flammable liquid">flammable liquid</a></b> is a liquid with a <a href="/wiki/Flash_point" title="Flash point">flash point</a> of not more than 93 °C.  Substances and mixtures of this hazard class are assigned to one of four hazard categories on the basis of the flash point and <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">boiling point</a>. A pyrophoric liquid is a liquid that, even in small quantities, is liable to ignite within five minutes after coming into contact with air. Substances and mixtures of this hazard class are assigned to a single hazard category on the basis of the outcome of the UN Test N.3.</li>
<li>A <b><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Flammable_solid&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Flammable solid (page does not exist)">flammable solid</a></b> is one that is readily combustible or may cause or contribute to fire through friction.  Readily combustible solids are powdered, granular, or pasty substances which are dangerous if they can be easily ignited by brief contact with an ignition source, such as a burning match, and if the flame spreads rapidly. it is further divided into <br/>• flammable solids, <br/>• polymerizing substances and <br/>• self-reactive substances, i.e., thermally unstable solids liable to undergo a strongly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermic</a> thermal decomposition even without participation of oxygen (air) other than materials classified as explosive, <a href="/wiki/Organic_peroxide" title="Organic peroxide">organic peroxides</a> or as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidizing" title="Oxidizing">oxidizing</a>.<br/>Spontaneously combusting solids or pyrophoric solids are solids that, even in small quantities, are liable to ignite within five minutes after coming into contact with air. Substances and mixtures of this hazard class are assigned to a single hazard category on the basis of the outcome of the UN Test N.2. Self-heating substances are solids or liquids, other than a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrophoric" title="Pyrophoric">pyrophoric</a> substance, which, by reaction with air and without energy supply, are liable to self-heat. Substances and mixtures of this hazard class are assigned to one of two hazard categories on the basis of the outcome of the UN Test N.4. Substances which on contact with water emit flammable gases are liable to become spontaneously flammable or to give off flammable gases in dangerous quantities. Substances and mixtures of this hazard class are assigned to one of three hazard categories on the basis of the outcome of UN Test N.5, which measures gas evolution and speed of evolution. Flammable aerosols can be classified as Class 1 or Class 2 if they contain any component, which is classified as flammable.</li>
<li><b>Oxidizing substances and organic <a href="/wiki/Peroxide" title="Peroxide">peroxides</a> </b> contain <br/>• category 1: oxidizing substances and<br/>• category 2: organic peroxides, organic liquids or solids that contain the bivalent -O-O- structure and may be considered a derivative of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a>, where one or both of the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atoms" title="Atoms">atoms</a> have been replaced by <a href="/wiki/Substituent" title="Substituent">organic radicals</a>. The term also includes organic peroxide formulations (mixtures). <br/>Substances and mixtures of this hazard class are assigned to one of seven 'Types', A to G, on the basis of the outcome of the UN Test Series A to H.</li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toxic" title="Toxic">Toxic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infectious" title="Infectious">infectious</a> substances</b></li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">Radioactive</a> substances</b></li>
<li><b>Substances <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corrosive" title="Corrosive">corrosive</a> to metal</b> are substances or mixtures that by chemical action will materially damage or even destroy metals. These substances or mixtures are classified in a single hazard category on the basis of tests (Steel: ISO 9328 (II): 1991 - Steel type P235; Aluminum: ASTM G31-72 (1990) - non-clad types 7075-T6 or AZ5GU-T66). The GHS criteria are a <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> rate on <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum" title="Aluminum">aluminum</a> surfaces exceeding 6.25 mm per year at a test temperature of 55 °C.</li>
<li><b>Miscellaneous</b> dangerous substances which includes environmentally dangerous substances</li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_hazards">Health hazards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Globally_Harmonized_System_of_Classification_and_Labelling_of_Chemicals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Health hazards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Acute_toxicity" title="Acute toxicity">Acute toxicity</a></b> includes five GHS categories from which the appropriate elements relevant to transport, consumer, worker and environment protection can be selected. Substances are assigned to one of the five <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxicity</a> categories on the basis of LD<sub>50</sub> (oral, dermal) or LC<sub>50</sub>  (inhalation).</li>
<li><b>Skin corrosion</b> means the production of irreversible damage to the skin following the application of a test substance for up to 4 hours. Substances and mixtures in this hazard class are assigned to a single harmonized corrosion category.</li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skin_irritation" title="Skin irritation">Skin irritation</a></b> means the production of reversible damage to the skin following the application of a test substance for up to 4 hours. Substances and mixtures in this hazard class are assigned to a single <a href="/wiki/Irritation" title="Irritation">irritant</a> category. For those authorities, such as <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticide</a> regulators, wanting more than one designation for skin irritation, an additional mild irritant category is provided.</li>
<li><b>Serious eye damage</b> means the production of tissue damage in the eye, or serious physical decay of vision, following application of a test substance to the front surface of the eye, which is not fully reversible within 21 days of application. Substances and mixtures in this hazard class are assigned to a single harmonized category.</li>
<li><b>Eye irritation</b> means changes in the eye following the application of a test substance to the front surface of the eye, which are fully reversible within 21 days of application. Substances and mixtures in this hazard class are assigned to a single harmonized hazard category.  For authorities, such as pesticide regulators, wanting more than one designation for eye irritation, one of two subcategories can be selected, depending on whether the effects are reversible in 21 or 7 days.</li>
<li><b>Respiratory sensitizer</b> means a substance that induces <a href="/wiki/Hypersensitivity" title="Hypersensitivity">hypersensitivity</a> of the airways following inhalation of the substance. Substances and mixtures in this hazard class are assigned to one hazard category.</li>
<li><b>Skin sensitizer</b> means a substance that will induce an allergic response following skin contact. The definition for "skin <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sensitizer" title="wikt:sensitizer">sensitizer</a>" is equivalent to "contact sensitizer". Substances and mixtures in this hazard class are assigned to one hazard category.</li>
<li><b>Germ cell mutagenicity</b> means an agent giving rise to an increased occurrence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mutations" title="Mutations">mutations</a> in populations of cells and/or organisms. Substances and mixtures in this hazard class are assigned to one of two hazard categories. Category 1 has two subcategories.</li>
<li><b>Carcinogenicity</b> means a chemical substance or a mixture of chemical substances that induce <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a> or increase its incidence. Substances and mixtures in this hazard class are assigned to one of two hazard categories. Category 1 has two subcategories.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Reproductive_toxicity" title="Reproductive toxicity">Reproductive toxicity</a></b> includes adverse effects on sexual function and <a href="/wiki/Fertility" title="Fertility">fertility</a> in adult males and females, as well as <a href="/wiki/Developmental_toxicity" title="Developmental toxicity">developmental toxicity</a> in offspring.  Substances and mixtures with reproductive and/or developmental effects are assigned to one of two hazard categories, 'known or presumed' and 'suspected'. Category 1 has two subcategories for reproductive and developmental effects. Materials which cause concern for the health of breastfed children have a separate category: effects on or via <a href="/wiki/Lactation" title="Lactation">Lactation</a>.</li>
<li><b><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Specific_target_organ_toxicity&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Specific target organ toxicity (page does not exist)">Specific target organ toxicity</a> (STOT) </b> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> category distinguishes between single and repeated exposure for <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Target_Organ&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Target Organ (page does not exist)">Target Organ</a> Effects.  All significant health effects, not otherwise specifically included in the GHS, that can impair function, both reversible and irreversible, immediate and/or delayed are included in the non-lethal target organ/systemic toxicity class (TOST). Narcotic effects and respiratory tract irritation are considered to be target organ systemic effects following a single exposure.  Substances and mixtures of the single exposure target organ toxicity hazard class are assigned to one of three hazard categories.  Substances and mixtures of the repeated exposure target organ toxicity hazard class are assigned to one of two hazard categories.</li>
<li><b>Aspiration hazard</b> includes severe acute effects such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_pneumonia" title="Chemical pneumonia">chemical pneumonia</a>, varying degrees of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulmonary_injury" title="Pulmonary injury">pulmonary injury</a> or death following <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_aspiration" title="Pulmonary aspiration">aspiration</a>. Aspiration is the entry of a liquid or solid directly through the oral or nasal cavity, or indirectly from vomiting, into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertebrate_trachea" title="Vertebrate trachea">trachea</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lower_respiratory_system&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lower respiratory system (page does not exist)">lower respiratory system</a>.  Substances and mixtures of this hazard class are assigned to one of two hazard categories this hazard class on the basis of <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_hazards">Environmental hazards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Globally_Harmonized_System_of_Classification_and_Labelling_of_Chemicals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Environmental hazards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><b>Acute <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aquatic_toxicity" title="Aquatic toxicity">aquatic toxicity</a></b> means the intrinsic property of a material of causing injury to an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aquatic_organism" title="Aquatic organism">aquatic organism</a> in a short-term exposure. Substances and mixtures of this hazard class are assigned to one of three toxicity categories on the basis of acute toxicity data: LC<sub>50</sub> (<a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>) or EC<sub>50</sub> (<a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustacean</a>) or ErC<sub>50</sub> (for <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> or other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aquatic_plants" title="Aquatic plants">aquatic plants</a>). In some regulatory systems these acute toxicity categories may be subdivided or extended for certain sectors.</li>
<li><b>Chronic aquatic toxicity</b> means the potential or actual properties of a material to cause adverse effects to aquatic organisms during exposures that are determined in relation to the lifecycle of the organism. Substances and mixtures in this hazard class are assigned to one of four toxicity categories on the basis of acute data and environmental fate data:  LC<sub>50</sub> (fish) or EC<sub>50</sub> (crustacea) or ErC<sub>50</sub> (for algae or other aquatic plants) and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">degradation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bioaccumulation" title="Bioaccumulation">bioaccumulation</a>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_of_mixtures">Classification of mixtures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Globally_Harmonized_System_of_Classification_and_Labelling_of_Chemicals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Classification of mixtures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The GHS approach to the classification of mixtures for health and environmental hazards is also complex.  It uses a tiered approach and is dependent upon the amount of information available for the mixture itself and for its components. Principles that have been developed for the classification of mixtures, drawing on existing systems such as the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> (EU) system for classification of preparations laid down in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Directive_1999/45/EC&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Directive 1999/45/EC (page does not exist)">Directive 1999/45/EC</a>.  The process for the classification of mixtures is based on the following steps:
</p>
<ol><li>Where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toxicological" title="Toxicological">toxicological</a> or <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ecotoxicological&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ecotoxicological (page does not exist)">ecotoxicological</a> test data are available for the mixture itself, the classification of the mixture will be based on that data;</li>
<li>Where test data are not available for the mixture itself, then the appropriate <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bridging_principles&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bridging principles (page does not exist)">bridging principles</a> should be applied, which uses test data for components and/or similar mixtures;</li>
<li>If (1) test data are not available for the mixture itself, and (2) the bridging principles cannot be applied, then use the calculation or cutoff values described in the specific endpoint to classify the mixture.</li></ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Testing_requirements">Testing requirements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Globally_Harmonized_System_of_Classification_and_Labelling_of_Chemicals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Testing requirements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The GHS document does not include testing requirements for substances or mixtures.  In fact, one of the main goals of the GHS is to reduce the need for animal testing.  The GHS criteria for determining health and environmental hazards are test method neutral, allowing different approaches as long as they are scientifically sound and validated according to international procedures and criteria already referred to in existing systems.  Test data already generated for the classification of chemicals under existing systems should be accepted when classifying these chemicals under the GHS, thereby avoiding duplicative testing and the unnecessary use of test animals.  The GHS physical hazard criteria are linked to specific <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=UN_test_methods&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="UN test methods (page does not exist)">UN test methods</a>.  It is assumed that mixtures will be tested for physical hazards.
</p>

<p>Per GHS, hazards need to be communicated:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<ul><li>in more than one form (for example, placards, labels or SDS’s).</li>
<li>with <a href="/wiki/GHS_hazard_statements" title="GHS hazard statements">hazard statements</a> and <a href="/wiki/GHS_precautionary_statements" title="GHS precautionary statements">precautionary statements</a>.</li>
<li>in an easily comprehensible and standardized manner.</li>
<li>consistent with other statements to reduce confusion.</li>
<li>taking into account all existing research and any new evidence.</li></ul>
<p>Comprehensibility is challenging for a single culture and language, so global harmonization is complex. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=GHS_Purple_Book&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="GHS Purple Book (page does not exist)">GHS Purple Book</a> includes a comprehensibility-testing instrument in Annex 6. Factors that were considered in developing the GHS communication tools include:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Different philosophies in existing systems on how and what should be communicated;</li>
<li>Language differences around the world;</li>
<li>Ability to translate phrases meaningfully;</li>
<li>Ability to understand and appropriately respond to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pictograms" title="Pictograms">pictograms</a>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="GHS_label_elements">GHS label elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Globally_Harmonized_System_of_Classification_and_Labelling_of_Chemicals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: GHS label elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GHS-pictogram-silhouette.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="724" data-file-width="724" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/GHS-pictogram-silhouette.svg/220px-GHS-pictogram-silhouette.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/GHS-pictogram-silhouette.svg/330px-GHS-pictogram-silhouette.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/GHS-pictogram-silhouette.svg/440px-GHS-pictogram-silhouette.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GHS-pictogram-silhouette.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The symbol for substances hazardous to the human health as implemented by the GHS.</div></div></div>
<p>The standardized label elements included in the GHS are:
</p>
<ul><li><b>Symbols</b> (<a href="/wiki/GHS_hazard_pictograms" title="GHS hazard pictograms">GHS hazard pictograms</a>): Convey <a href="/wiki/Health" title="Health">health</a>, physical and <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environmental</a> hazard information, assigned to a GHS hazard class and category.  Pictograms include the harmonized hazard symbols plus other graphic elements, such as borders, background patterns or cozers and substances which have target organ toxicity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Also, harmful chemicals and irritants are marked with an <a href="/wiki/Exclamation_mark" title="Exclamation mark">exclamation mark</a>, replacing the European <a href="/wiki/Saltire" title="Saltire">saltire</a>. Pictograms will have a black symbol on a white background with a red diamond frame.  For transport, pictograms will have the background, symbol and colors currently used in the <i><a href="/wiki/UN_Recommendations_on_the_Transport_of_Dangerous_Goods" title="UN Recommendations on the Transport of Dangerous Goods">UN Recommendations on the Transport of Dangerous Goods</a></i>. Where a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Transport_pictogram&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Transport pictogram (page does not exist)">transport pictogram</a> appears, the GHS pictogram for the same hazard should not appear.</li>
<li><b>Signal words</b>:  "Danger" or "Warning" will be used to emphasize hazards and indicate the relative level of severity of the hazard, assigned to a GHS hazard class and category.  Some lower level hazard categories do not use signal words. Only one signal word corresponding to the class of the most severe hazard should be used on a label.</li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GHS_hazard_statement" title="GHS hazard statement">GHS hazard statement</a></b>: Standard phrases assigned to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hazard_class" title="Hazard class">hazard class</a> and category that describe the nature of the hazard.  An appropriate statement for each GHS hazard should be included on the label for products possessing more than one hazard.</li></ul>
<p>The additional label elements included in the GHS are:
</p>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/GHS_precautionary_statements" title="GHS precautionary statements">GHS precautionary statements</a></b>: Measures to minimize or prevent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adverse_effects" title="Adverse effects">adverse effects</a>.  There are four types of precautionary statements covering: prevention, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_accidents" title="Chemical accidents">response in cases of accidental spillage or exposure</a>, storage, and disposal. The precautionary statements have been linked to each GHS hazard statement and type of hazard.</li>
<li><b><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Product_identifier&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Product identifier (page does not exist)">Product identifier</a></b> (ingredient disclosure): Name or number used for a hazardous product on a label or in the SDS.  The GHS label for a substance should include the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chemical_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chemical identity (page does not exist)">chemical identity</a> of the substance.  For mixtures, the label should include the chemical identities of all ingredients that contribute to acute toxicity, skin corrosion or serious eye damage, germ cell mutagenicity, carcinogenicity, reproductive toxicity, skin or respiratory sensitization, or Systemic Target Organ Systemic (STOT), when these hazards appear on the label.</li>
<li><b>Supplier identification</b>: The name, address and telephone number should be provided on the label.</li>
<li><b>Supplemental information</b>: Non-harmonized information on the container of a hazardous product that is not required or specified under the GHS.  Supplemental information may be used to provide further detail that does not contradict or cast doubt on the validity of the standardized hazard information.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="GHS_label_format">GHS label format</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Globally_Harmonized_System_of_Classification_and_Labelling_of_Chemicals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: GHS label format">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The GHS includes directions for application of the hazard communication elements on the label. In particular, it specifies for each hazard, and for each class within the hazard, what signal word, <a href="/wiki/Pictogram" title="Pictogram">pictogram</a>, and hazard statement should be used. The GHS hazard pictograms, signal words and hazard statements should be located together on the label. The actual label format or layout is not specified in the GHS. National authorities may choose to specify where information should appear on the label or allow supplier discretion.  There has been discussion about the size of GHS pictograms and that a GHS pictogram might be confused with a transport pictogram or "diamond". Transport pictograms are different in appearance than the GHS pictograms. Annex 7 of the Purple Book explains how the GHS pictograms are expected to be proportional to the size of the label text so that generally the GHS pictograms would be smaller than the transport pictograms.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_data_sheet">Safety data sheet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Globally_Harmonized_System_of_Classification_and_Labelling_of_Chemicals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Safety data sheet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Safety_data_sheet" title="Safety data sheet">safety data sheet</a> or SDS (The GHS dropped<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (February 2017)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> the word "material" from material safety data sheet) is specifically aimed at use in the workplace. It should provide comprehensive information about the chemical product that allows employers and workers to obtain concise, relevant and accurate information in perspective to the hazards, uses and <a href="/wiki/Risk_management" title="Risk management">risk management</a> of the chemical product in the workplace. While there were some differences in existing industry recommendations and country specific requirements, there was agreement on a 16 section SDS to include the following headings in the order specified:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>Identification</li>
<li>Hazard(s) identification</li>
<li>Composition/ information on ingredients</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First-aid" title="First-aid">First-aid</a> measures</li>
<li>Fire-fighting measures</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_accidents" title="Chemical accidents">Accidental release measures</a></li>
<li>Handling and storage</li>
<li>Exposure control/ personal protection</li>
<li>Physical and chemical properties</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chemical_stability" title="Chemical stability">chemical stability</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reactivity_(chemistry)" title="Reactivity (chemistry)">reactivity</a></li>
<li>Toxicological information</li>
<li>Ecological information</li>
<li>Disposal considerations</li>
<li>Transport information</li>
<li>Regulatory information</li>
<li>Other information</li></ol>
<p>The primary difference between the GHS and the international industry recommendations is that sections 2 and 3 have been reversed in order.  The GHS SDS headings, sequence and content are similar to the <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">ISO</a>, <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ANSI" title="ANSI">ANSI</a> MSDS/SDS requirements.  The SDS should provide a clear description of the data used to identify the hazards.  A table comparing the content and format of a MSDS/SDS versus the GHS SDS is provided in Appendix A of the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">Occupational Safety and Health Administration</a> (OSHA) GHS guidance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Training">Training</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Globally_Harmonized_System_of_Classification_and_Labelling_of_Chemicals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Training">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Current training procedures for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hazard_Communication" title="Hazard Communication">Hazard Communication</a> in the United States are more detailed than the GHS training recommendations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (February 2017)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> Therefore, educating employees on the updated chemical and product classifications and related pictograms, signal words, hazard statements and precautionary measures will represent the greatest training challenge.  Training will be a key component of the overall GHS approach and should incorporate information as it is introduced into the workplace.  Employees and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emergency_responders" title="Emergency responders">emergency responders</a> will need to be trained on all new program elements, from hazard statements to pictograms. Bear in mind, if products are imported from countries that implement GHS prior to the United States and Canada, employee training may need to begin earlier than expected.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Implementation">Implementation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Globally_Harmonized_System_of_Classification_and_Labelling_of_Chemicals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Implementation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> goal was broad international adoption by 2008.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> As of 2017, it has been adopted to varying degrees in nearly all major countries. 
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GHS_pictogram_on_back_of_Axe_botte.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2310" data-file-width="2208" height="230" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/GHS_pictogram_on_back_of_Axe_botte.jpg/220px-GHS_pictogram_on_back_of_Axe_botte.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/GHS_pictogram_on_back_of_Axe_botte.jpg/330px-GHS_pictogram_on_back_of_Axe_botte.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/GHS_pictogram_on_back_of_Axe_botte.jpg/440px-GHS_pictogram_on_back_of_Axe_botte.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GHS_pictogram_on_back_of_Axe_botte.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A flammable warning symbol on the back of a European Axe deodorant spray.</div></div></div>
<p>GHS adoption by country:
</p>
<ul><li>Australia: In 2012, adopted regulation for GHS implementation, setting January 1, 2017 as the GHS implementation deadline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>Brazil: Established an implementation deadline of February 2011 for substances and June 2015 for mixtures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>Canada: In December 2011,committed to the implementation by June 1, 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>China: Established implementation deadline of December 1, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ckj_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-ckj-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>Colombia: By June, 2014  No deadline had been set up yet. the subcommittee for the implementation within the Mesa de Seguridad Química (Bureau of Chemical Safety), began communication between the government, the private sector and civil society.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>European Union: The deadline for substance classification was December 1, 2010 and for mixtures it was June 1, 2015 per regulation for GHS implementation on December 31, 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>Turkey: Published Turkish CLP regulation and SDS regulation in 2013 and 2014 respectively. The deadline for substance classification was June 1, 2015, for mixtures, it was June 1, 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>Japan: Established deadline of December 31, 2010 for products containing one of 640 designated substances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ckj_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-ckj-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>Korea: Established the GHS implementation deadline of July 1, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ckj_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-ckj-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>Malaysia: Deadline for substance and mixture was April 17, 2015 per its Industry Code of Practice on Chemicals Classification and Hazard Communication(ICOP) on 16 April 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>Pakistan: Country does not a single streamlined system for chemical labeling, although there are many rules in place. The Pakistani government has requested assistance in developing future regulations to implement GHS. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li>Philippines: The deadline for substance and mixture was March 14, 2015 per Guidelines for the Implementation of GHS in Chemical Safety Program in the Workplace in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li>Russian Federation. GHS was approved for optional use as of August 2014. Manufacturers may continue using non-GHS Russian labels through 2021, after which compliance with the system is compulsory. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>Taiwan: Full GHS implementation was scheduled for 2016 for all hazardous chemicals with physical and health hazards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>Thailand: The deadline for substances was March 13, 2013. The deadline for mixture is March 13, 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li>Vietnam: The deadline for substances was March 30, 2014. The deadline for mixtures was March 30, 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li>United States: GHS compliant labels and SDSs are required for many applications including laboratory chemicals, commercial cleaning agents, and other workplace cases regulated by previous US Occupational Health and Safety Administration (OSHA) standards. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> First widespread implementation set by OSHA was on March 26, 2012, requiring manufacturers to adopt the standard by June 1, 2015 and product distributors to adopt the standard by December 1, 2015. Workers had to be trained by December 1, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>In the US, GHS labels are not required on most hazardous consumer grade products (ex. laundry detergent) however some manufacturers which also sell the same product in Canada or Europe include GHS compliant warnings on these products too. The US Consumer Product Safety Commission is not opposed to this and has been evaluating the possibility of incorporating elements of GHS into future consumer regulations. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li>Uruguay: regulation approved in 2011, setting December 31, 2012 as deadline for pure substances and December 31, 2017 for compounds. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
<li>Mexico: GHS has been incorporated into the Official Mexican Standard as of 2015. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li></ul>
