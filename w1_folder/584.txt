<p><b>Liquefaction of gases</b> is physical conversion of a <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> into a <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a> state (<a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensation</a>).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Details">Details</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefaction_of_gases&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Details">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The processes are used for scientific, industrial and commercial purposes. Many gases can be put into a liquid state at normal <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> by simple cooling; a few, such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, require pressurization as well. <a href="/wiki/Liquefaction" title="Liquefaction">Liquefaction</a> is used for analyzing the fundamental properties of gas molecules (intermolecular forces), for storage of gases, for example: <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_petroleum_gas" title="Liquefied petroleum gas">LPG</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">refrigeration</a> and <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">air conditioning</a>. There the gas is liquefied in the <i><a href="/wiki/Condenser_(laboratory)" title="Condenser (laboratory)">condenser</a></i>, where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_of_vaporization" title="Heat of vaporization">heat of vaporization</a> is released, and evaporated in the <i><a href="/wiki/Evaporator" title="Evaporator">evaporator</a>,</i> where the heat of vaporization is absorbed. <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">Ammonia</a> was the first such <a href="/wiki/Refrigerant" title="Refrigerant">refrigerant</a>, and is still in widespread use in industrial refrigeration, but it has largely been replaced by compounds derived from <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogens</a> in residential and commercial applications.  
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">Liquid oxygen</a> is provided to hospitals for conversion to gas for patients with breathing problems, and <a href="/wiki/Liquid_nitrogen" title="Liquid nitrogen">liquid nitrogen</a> is used in the medical field for <a href="/wiki/Cryosurgery" title="Cryosurgery">cryosurgery</a>, and by inseminators to freeze <a href="/wiki/Semen" title="Semen">semen</a>. Liquefied <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> is transported for eventual solution in water, after which  it is used for water purification, sanitation of <a href="/wiki/Industrial_waste" title="Industrial waste">industrial waste</a>, sewage and swimming pools, bleaching of pulp and textiles and manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride" title="Carbon tetrachloride">carbon tetrachloride</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glycol" title="Glycol">glycol</a> and numerous other organic compounds as well as <a href="/wiki/Phosgene" title="Phosgene">phosgene</a> gas.
</p><p>Liquefaction of <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> (<a href="/wiki/Liquid_helium" title="Liquid helium"><sup>4</sup>He</a>) with the precooled <a href="/wiki/Hampson%E2%80%93Linde_cycle" title="Hampson–Linde cycle">Hampson–Linde cycle</a> led to a <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prize</a> for <a href="/wiki/Heike_Kamerlingh_Onnes" title="Heike Kamerlingh Onnes">Heike Kamerlingh Onnes</a> in 1913. At ambient pressure the boiling point of <a href="/wiki/Liquid_helium" title="Liquid helium">liquefied helium</a> is 4.22 K (−268.93 °C).  Below 2.17 K liquid <sup>4</sup>He becomes a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superfluid" title="Superfluid">superfluid</a> (<a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prize</a> 1978, <a href="/wiki/Pyotr_Kapitsa" title="Pyotr Kapitsa">Pyotr Kapitsa</a>) and shows characteristic properties such as heat conduction through <a href="/wiki/Second_sound" title="Second sound">second sound</a>, zero <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Superfluid_helium-4" title="Superfluid helium-4">fountain effect</a> among others.
</p><p>The liquefaction of gases is a complicated process that uses various compressions and expansions to achieve high pressures and very low temperatures, using, for example, <a href="/wiki/Turboexpander" title="Turboexpander">turboexpanders</a>.  
</p><p>The liquefaction of air is used to obtain <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a> and other atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Noble_gas" title="Noble gas">noble gases</a> by separating the air components by <a href="/wiki/Fractional_distillation" title="Fractional distillation">fractional distillation</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Air_separation" title="Air separation">cryogenic air separation unit</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Liquid_air">Liquid air</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefaction_of_gases&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Liquid air">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Liquid_air" title="Liquid air">Liquid air</a></div>
<h3><span id="Linde.27s_process"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Linde's_process">Linde's process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefaction_of_gases&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Linde's process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Air is liquefied by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linde_process" title="Linde process">Linde process</a>, in which air is alternately compressed, cooled, and expanded, each expansion results in a considerable reduction in temperature. With the lower temperature the molecules move more slowly and occupy less space, so the air changes phase to become liquid.
</p>
<h3><span id="Claude.27s_process"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Claude's_process">Claude's process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Liquefaction_of_gases&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Claude's process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Air can also be liquefied by <a href="/wiki/Georges_Claude" title="Georges Claude">Claude</a>'s process in which the gas is allowed to expand <a href="/wiki/Isentropic_process" title="Isentropic process">isentropically</a> twice in two chambers. While expanding, the gas has to do work as it is led through an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expansion_turbine" title="Expansion turbine">expansion turbine</a>. The gas is not yet liquid, since that would destroy the turbine. Commercial air liquefication plants bypass this problem by expanding the air at <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_fluid" title="Supercritical fluid">supercritical</a> pressures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Final liquefaction takes place by <a href="/wiki/Isenthalpic_process" title="Isenthalpic process">isenthalpic</a> expansion in a <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion_valve" title="Thermal expansion valve">thermal expansion valve</a>.
</p>
