<p>An <b>individual</b> is that which exists as a distinct entity. <b>Individuality</b> (or <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/selfhood" title="wikt:selfhood">selfhood</a>) is the state or quality of being an individual; particularly of being a person separate from other people and possessing his or her own <a href="/wiki/Maslow%27s_hierarchy_of_needs" title="Maslow's hierarchy of needs">needs</a> or <a href="/wiki/Personal_goal_setting" title="Personal goal setting">goals</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right" title="Right">rights</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moral_responsibility" title="Moral responsibility">responsibilities</a>. The exact definition of an individual is important in the fields of <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>. 
</p><p>From the 15th century and earlier (and also today within the fields of <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metaphysics" title="Metaphysics">metaphysics</a>) <i>individual</i> meant "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Divisible" title="Divisible">indivisible</a>", typically describing any numerically singular thing, but sometimes meaning "a person". From the 17th century on, <i>individual</i> indicates separateness, as in <a href="/wiki/Individualism" title="Individualism">individualism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although individuality and individualism are commonly considered to mature with age/time and experience/wealth, a <a href="/wiki/Sanity" title="Sanity">sane</a> adult <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human being</a> is usually considered by the <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a> as an "individual person" in law, even if the person denies individual <a href="/wiki/Culpability" title="Culpability">culpability</a> ("I followed instructions"). An individual person is <a href="/wiki/Accountability" title="Accountability">accountable</a> for their actions/decisions/instructions, subject to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prosecution" title="Prosecution">prosecution</a> in both national and international law, from the time that they have reached <a href="/wiki/Age_of_majority" title="Age of majority">age of majority</a>, often though not always more or less coinciding with the granting of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voting_right" title="Voting right">voting rights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tax" title="Tax">tax</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_duty" title="Military duty">military duties</a>/ individual <a href="/wiki/Right_to_keep_and_bear_arms" title="Right to keep and bear arms">right to bear arms</a> (protected only under certain constitutions). In line with <a href="/wiki/Hierarchy" title="Hierarchy">hierarchy</a>, ultimate individual human reward for <a href="/wiki/Social_status" title="Social status">success</a> and responsibility for <a href="/wiki/Failure" title="Failure">failure</a> is nonetheless found at the top of <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">human society</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Law">Law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individual&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>in <a href="/wiki/International_law" title="International law">International law</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>All human beings are born free and equal in dignity and rights. They are endowed with reason and conscience and should act towards one another in a spirit of brotherhood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>in <a href="/wiki/Law_of_the_United_States" title="Law of the United States">American law</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>A natural person is a human being. A legal person is an entity such as a company, which is regarded in law as having its own 'legal personality'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>The <i>Merriam-Webster's Dictionary of Law</i> states: A "natural person" is "A human being as distinguished from person (as a corporation) created by operation of law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  A 1910 legal dictionary states: "Individual: As a noun, this term denotes a single person as distinguished from a group or class, and also, very commonly, a private or natural person as distinguished from a partnership, corporation, or association."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy">Philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individual&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Buddhism">Buddhism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individual&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Buddhism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, the concept of the individual lies in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anatman" title="Anatman">anatman</a>, or "no-self." According to anatman, the individual is really a series of interconnected processes that, working together, give the appearance of being a single, separated whole. In this way, anatman, together with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anicca" title="Anicca">anicca</a>, resembles a kind of <a href="/wiki/Bundle_theory" title="Bundle theory">bundle theory</a>. Instead of an atomic, indivisible self distinct from reality, the individual in Buddhism is understood as an interrelated part of an ever-changing, impermanent universe (see <a href="/wiki/Interdependence" title="Interdependence">Interdependence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nondualism" title="Nondualism">Nondualism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reciprocity_(social_psychology)" title="Reciprocity (social psychology)">Reciprocity</a>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Empiricism">Empiricism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individual&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Empiricism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Early <a href="/wiki/Empiricism" title="Empiricism">empiricists</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Tufail" title="Ibn Tufail">Ibn Tufail</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Russell_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Russell-6">[6]</a></sup>  in early 12th century Islamic Spain, and <a href="/wiki/John_Locke" title="John Locke">John Locke</a> in late 17th century England, <a href="/wiki/Hayy_ibn_Yaqdhan#Empiricism,_tabula_rasa,_nature_versus_nurture" title="Hayy ibn Yaqdhan">introduced the idea</a> of the individual as a <a href="/wiki/Tabula_rasa" title="Tabula rasa">tabula rasa</a> ("blank slate"), shaped from birth by experience and education. This ties into the idea of the liberty and rights of the individual, society as a <a href="/wiki/Social_contract" title="Social contract">social contract</a> between <a href="/wiki/Rationality" title="Rationality">rational</a> individuals, and the beginnings of <a href="/wiki/Individualism" title="Individualism">individualism</a> as a doctrine.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hegel">Hegel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individual&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hegel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Georg_Wilhelm_Friedrich_Hegel" title="Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel">Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel</a> regarded history as the gradual evolution of Mind as it tests its own concepts against the external world.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Each time the mind applies its concepts to the world, the concept is revealed to be only partly true, within a certain context; thus the mind continually revises these incomplete concepts so as to reflect a fuller reality (commonly known as the process of thesis, antithesis, and synthesis). The individual comes to rise above his or her own particular viewpoint,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and grasps that he or she is a part of a greater whole<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> insofar as he or she is bound to family, a social context, and/or a political order.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Existentialism">Existentialism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individual&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Existentialism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With the rise of <a href="/wiki/Existentialism" title="Existentialism">existentialism</a>, <a href="/wiki/S%C3%B8ren_Kierkegaard" title="Søren Kierkegaard">Søren Kierkegaard</a> rejected Hegel's notion of the individual as subordinated to the forces of history. Instead, he elevated the individual's subjectivity and capacity to choose his or her own fate. Later Existentialists built upon this notion.  <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Nietzsche" title="Friedrich Nietzsche">Friedrich Nietzsche</a>, for example, examines the individual's need to define his/her own self and circumstances in his concept of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_will_to_power" title="The will to power">the will to power</a> and the heroic ideal of the <a href="/wiki/%C3%9Cbermensch" title="Übermensch">Übermensch</a>. The individual is also central to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sartre" title="Sartre">Sartre</a>'s philosophy, which emphasizes individual authenticity, responsibility, and <a href="/wiki/Free_will" title="Free will">free will</a>. In both Sartre and Nietzsche (and in <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Berdyaev" title="Nikolai Berdyaev">Nikolai Berdyaev</a>), the individual is called upon to create his or her own values, rather than rely on external, socially imposed codes of morality.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Objectivism">Objectivism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individual&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Objectivism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ayn_Rand" title="Ayn Rand">Ayn Rand</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Objectivism_(Ayn_Rand)" title="Objectivism (Ayn Rand)">Objectivism</a> regards every human as an independent, sovereign entity who possesses an inalienable right to his or her own life, a right derived from his or her nature as a rational being. Individualism and Objectivism hold that a civilized society, or any form of association, cooperation or peaceful coexistence among humans, can be achieved only on the basis of the recognition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Individual_rights" title="Individual rights">individual rights</a> — and that a group, as such, has no rights other than the individual rights of its members. The principle of individual rights is the only moral base of all groups or associations. Since only an individual man or woman can possess rights, the expression "individual rights" is a redundancy (which one has to use for purposes of clarification in today’s intellectual chaos), but the expression "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collective_rights" title="Collective rights">collective rights</a>" is a contradiction in terms. Individual rights are not subject to a public vote; a <a href="/wiki/Tyranny_of_the_majority" title="Tyranny of the majority">majority</a> has no right to vote away the rights of a <a href="/wiki/Minority_group" title="Minority group">minority</a>; the political function of rights is precisely to protect minorities from oppression by majorities (and the smallest minority on earth is the individual).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology">Biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Individual&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, the question of the individual is related to the definition of an <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organism</a>, which is an important question in biology and <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_biology" title="Philosophy of biology">philosophy of biology</a>, despite there having been little work devoted explicitly to this question. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilson_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson-9">[9]</a></sup> An individual organism is not the only kind of individual that is considered as a "unit of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Selection_(biology)" title="Selection (biology)">selection</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilson_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">Genes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genomes</a>, or groups may function as individual units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilson_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Asexual_reproduction" title="Asexual reproduction">Asexual reproduction</a> occurs in some colonial organisms so that the individuals are genetically identical. Such a colony is called a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genet_(biology)" title="Genet (biology)">genet</a>, and an individual in such a population is referred to as a ramet. The colony, rather than the individual, functions as a unit of selection. In other colonial organisms the individuals may be closely related to one another but differ as a result of <a href="/wiki/Sexual_reproduction" title="Sexual reproduction">sexual reproduction</a>.
</p>
