<p><b>Earth Overshoot Day</b> (<b>EOD</b>), previously known as <b>Ecological Debt Day</b> (<b>EDD</b>), is the calculated illustrative calendar date on which humanity’s <a href="/wiki/Resource_consumption" title="Resource consumption">resource consumption</a> for the year exceeds <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>’s capacity to regenerate those resources that year. Earth Overshoot Day is calculated by dividing the world <a href="/wiki/Biocapacity" title="Biocapacity">biocapacity</a> (the amount of <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a> generated by Earth that year), by the world <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a> (humanity's <a href="/wiki/Consumption_(economics)" title="Consumption (economics)">consumption</a> of Earth’s natural resources for that year), and multiplying by 365, the number of days in one <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian common calendar</a> year:

<p>When viewed through an <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economic</a> perspective, EOD represents the day in which humanity enters an ecological <a href="/wiki/Deficit_spending" title="Deficit spending">deficit spending</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> the term Earth Overshoot Day illustrates the level by which human population <a href="/wiki/Overshoot_(population)" title="Overshoot (population)">overshoots</a> its environment. In 2018, Earth Overshoot Day is on August 1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Earth Overshoot Day is calculated by <a href="/wiki/Global_Footprint_Network" title="Global Footprint Network">Global Footprint Network</a> and is a campaign supported by dozens of other nonprofit organizations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EOD_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-EOD-2">[2]</a></sup> Information about Global Footprint Network's calculations<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and national Ecological Footprints<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> are available online.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_Overshoot_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint_sustainability.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="522" data-file-width="850" height="246" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint_sustainability.jpg/400px-Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint_sustainability.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint_sustainability.jpg/600px-Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint_sustainability.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint_sustainability.jpg/800px-Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint_sustainability.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Human_welfare_and_ecological_footprint_sustainability.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The correlation between the development of a country using <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Index" title="Human Development Index">HDI</a> and its natural resource consumption<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Andrew_Simms" title="Andrew Simms">Andrew Simms</a> of UK <a href="/wiki/Think_tank" title="Think tank">think tank</a> <a href="/wiki/New_Economics_Foundation" title="New Economics Foundation">New Economics Foundation</a> originally developed the concept of Earth Overshoot Day. <a href="/wiki/Global_Footprint_Network" title="Global Footprint Network">Global Footprint Network</a>, a partner organization of New Economics Foundation, launches a campaign every year for Earth Overshoot Day to raise awareness of Earth’s limited resources. Global Footprint Network measures humanity’s demand for and supply of natural resources and ecological services. Global Footprint Network estimates that in less than eight months, we demand more <a href="/wiki/Renewable_resource" title="Renewable resource">renewable resources</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">CO<sub>2</sub> sequestration</a> than what the planet can provide for an entire year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EOD_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-EOD-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Throughout most of history, humanity has used nature’s resources to build cities and roads, to provide food and create products, and to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CO2_emissions" title="CO2 emissions">release carbon dioxide</a> at a rate that was well within Earth’s budget. But by the early 1970s, that critical threshold had been crossed: Human consumption began outstripping what the planet could reproduce. According to <a href="/wiki/Global_Footprint_Network" title="Global Footprint Network">Global Footprint Network</a>’s calculations, our demand for renewable ecological resources and the services they provide is now equivalent to that of more than 1.5 Earths. The data shows us on track to require the resources of two planets well before mid-2000-century.
</p><p>Advocates for Earth Overshoot Day note that the costs of <a href="/wiki/Resource_depletion" title="Resource depletion">ecological overspending</a> are becoming more evident over time. <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">Climate change</a> — a result of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gases</a> being emitted faster than they can be absorbed by forests and oceans — is the most obvious result and widespread effects. Other cited effects include: <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">shrinking forests</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Species_loss" title="Species loss">species loss</a>, fisheries collapse, higher commodity prices and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_unrest" title="Civil unrest">civil unrest</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EOD_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-EOD-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_Overshoot_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Breakthrough_Institute" title="Breakthrough Institute">Breakthrough Institute</a> regards the idea of Earth Overshoot Day and how many Earths we consume as "a nice publicity stunt".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> data, forests and fisheries are, as a whole, regenerating faster than they are depleted (but admitting that "the surplus might be more a reflection of poor UN fisheries data than healthy fisheries"), while cropland and pasture use is equal to what is available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup> Hence, Earth Overshoot Day does a poor job at measuring water and land mismanagement (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion" title="Soil erosion">soil erosion</a>) and only highlights the excess of carbon dioxide that humanity releases above what the ecosystem can absorb. In other words, the additional equivalent number of Earths that humanity requires is equivalent to land area that, if filled with carbon sinks like forests, would balance carbon dioxide emissions.
</p><p>Global Footprint Network points out that the ecological footprint makes apparent the gap between human demand and regeneration. Essentially, the sum of all demands competing for the planet's regeneration are now exceeding what the planet renews. While the accounting can be improved, and more details added, in its current applications to countries, the accounts typically underestimate human demand as not all aspects are measured (there are gaps in UN data). They also overestimate biocapacity because it is ambiguous to determine how much of current yields is enabled by reduced future yield (for instance as in the case of overuse of groundwater, or erosion). Therefore current accounts underplay the overall ecological deficit or overshoot at the global level. This is discussed in more detail in two chapters of the Routledge Handbook of Sustainability Indicators (Wackernagel et al. 2018 a and b).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Therefore, ecological footprint accounts are metrics that merely define minimal conditions for sustainability. They do not cover all material aspects of sustainability, just a basic, minimal condition for living within the regenerative capacity of the planet’s ecosystems.
</p><p>In response to the Breakthrough Institute Report, Mathis Wackernagel, founder and president of the Global Footprint Network, also points out that depletion of crop land, for instance, could be included in the Ecological Footprint accounts informing Earth Overshoot Day. But such improvements would "require data sets that do not exist within the UN data set". And such improvements would show an even larger global overshoot. Similarly, the apparent fishery surplus "might be more a reflection of poor UN fisheries data than healthy fisheries".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
