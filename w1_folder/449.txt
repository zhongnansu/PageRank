<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Blood plasma</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FreshFrozenPlasma.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2090" data-file-width="1633" height="282" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/FreshFrozenPlasma.JPG/220px-FreshFrozenPlasma.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/FreshFrozenPlasma.JPG/330px-FreshFrozenPlasma.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/FreshFrozenPlasma.JPG/440px-FreshFrozenPlasma.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FreshFrozenPlasma.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A unit of donated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fresh_plasma" title="Fresh plasma">fresh plasma</a></div></div></div>
<p><b>Blood plasma</b> is a yellowish coloured liquid component of blood that normally holds the <a href="/wiki/Blood_cell" title="Blood cell">blood cells</a> in <a href="/wiki/Whole_blood" title="Whole blood">whole blood</a> in <a href="/wiki/Suspension_(chemistry)" title="Suspension (chemistry)">suspension</a>; this makes plasma the <a href="/wiki/Extracellular_matrix" title="Extracellular matrix">extracellular matrix</a> of blood cells. It makes up about 55% of the body's total blood volume.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intravascular" title="Intravascular">intravascular</a> fluid part of <a href="/wiki/Extracellular_fluid" title="Extracellular fluid">extracellular fluid</a> (all body fluid outside cells). It is mostly water (up to 95% by volume), and contains dissolved <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a> (6–8%) (i.e.—<a href="/wiki/Serum_albumin" title="Serum albumin">serum albumins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Globulin" title="Globulin">globulins</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fibrinogen" title="Fibrinogen">fibrinogen</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clotting_factors" title="Clotting factors">clotting factors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolytes</a> (Na<sup>+</sup>, Ca<sup>2+</sup>, Mg<sup>2+</sup>, HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>, Cl<sup>−</sup>, etc.), <a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">hormones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (plasma being the main medium for excretory product transportation) and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>.
Plasma also serves as the protein reserve of the human body. It plays a vital role in an intravascular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Osmotic" title="Osmotic">osmotic</a> effect that keeps electrolyte concentration balanced and protects the body from <a href="/wiki/Infection" title="Infection">infection</a> and other blood disorders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bloodplasma_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bloodplasma-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Blood plasma is separated from the blood by <a href="/wiki/Blood_fractionation" title="Blood fractionation">spinning a tube of fresh blood</a> containing an <a href="/wiki/Anticoagulant" title="Anticoagulant">anticoagulant</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Centrifuge" title="Centrifuge">centrifuge</a> until the blood cells fall to the bottom of the tube. The blood plasma is then poured or drawn off.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maton_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Maton-4">[4]</a></sup> Blood plasma has a density of approximately 1025 kg/m<sup>3</sup>, or 1.025 g/ml.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_serum" title="Blood serum">Blood serum</a> is blood plasma without clotting factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maton_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Maton-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Plasmapheresis" title="Plasmapheresis">Plasmapheresis</a> is a medical therapy that involves blood plasma extraction, treatment, and reintegration.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Fresh_frozen_plasma" title="Fresh frozen plasma">Fresh frozen plasma</a> is on the <a href="/wiki/WHO_Model_List_of_Essential_Medicines" title="WHO Model List of Essential Medicines">WHO Model List of Essential Medicines</a>, the most important medications needed in a basic <a href="/wiki/Health_system" title="Health system">health system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WHO2015E_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2015E-6">[6]</a></sup> It is of critical importance in the treatment of many types of trauma which result in blood loss, and is therefore kept stocked universally in  all medical facilities capable of treating trauma (ex. trauma centers, hospitals, and ambulances) or that pose a risk of patient blood loss such as surgical suite facilities.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Volume">Volume</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_plasma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Volume">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Blood plasma volume may be expanded by or drained to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extravascular_fluid" title="Extravascular fluid">extravascular fluid</a> when there are changes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Starling_forces" title="Starling forces">Starling forces</a> across capillary walls. For example, when <a href="/wiki/Blood_pressure" title="Blood pressure">blood pressure</a> drops in <a href="/wiki/Shock_(circulatory)" title="Shock (circulatory)">circulatory shock</a>, Starling forces drive fluid into the interstitium, causing <a href="/wiki/Fluid_compartments#Third_spacing" title="Fluid compartments">third spacing</a>.
</p><p>Standing still for a prolonged period will cause an increase in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transcapillary_hydrostatic_pressure" title="Transcapillary hydrostatic pressure">transcapillary hydrostatic pressure</a>. As a result, approximately 12% of blood plasma volume will cross into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extravascular_compartment" title="Extravascular compartment">extravascular compartment</a>. This causes an increase in <a href="/wiki/Hematocrit" title="Hematocrit">hematocrit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serum_total_protein" title="Serum total protein">serum total protein</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_viscosity" title="Blood viscosity">blood viscosity</a> and, as a result of increased concentration of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coagulation_factors" title="Coagulation factors">coagulation factors</a>, it causes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthostatic_hypercoagulability" title="Orthostatic hypercoagulability">orthostatic hypercoagulability</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The use of blood plasma as a substitute for whole blood and for transfusion purposes was proposed in March 1918, in the correspondence columns of the British Medical Journal, by Gordon R. Ward. "Dried plasmas" in powder or strips of material format were developed and first used in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Prior to the United States' involvement in the war, liquid plasma and <a href="/wiki/Whole_blood" title="Whole blood">whole blood</a> were used. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blood_for_Britain">Blood for Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_plasma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Blood for Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The "Blood for Britain" program during the early 1940s was quite successful (and popular in the United States) based on <a href="/wiki/Charles_R._Drew" title="Charles R. Drew">Charles Drew</a>'s contribution. A large project began in August 1940 to collect blood in New York City hospitals for the export of plasma to Britain. Drew was appointed medical supervisor of the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plasma_for_Britain" title="Plasma for Britain">Plasma for Britain</a>" project. His notable contribution at this time was to transform the <a href="/wiki/Test_tube" title="Test tube">test tube</a> methods of many blood researchers into the first successful mass production techniques.
</p><p>Nevertheless, the decision was made to develop a dried plasma package for the armed forces as it would reduce breakage and make the transportation, packaging, and storage much simpler.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The resulting dried plasma package came in two tin cans containing 400 cc bottles. One bottle contained enough <a href="/wiki/Distilled_water" title="Distilled water">distilled water</a> to reconstitute the dried plasma contained within the other bottle. In about three minutes, the plasma would be ready to use and could stay fresh for around four hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> 
The Blood for Britain program operated successfully for five months, with total collections of almost 15,000 people donating blood, and with over 5,500 vials of blood plasma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plasma_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-plasma-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Following the "Plasma for Britain" invention, Drew was named director of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_Cross" title="Red Cross">Red Cross</a> <a href="/wiki/Blood_bank" title="Blood bank">blood bank</a> and assistant director of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Research_Council" title="United States National Research Council">National Research Council</a>, in charge of blood collection for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">Navy</a>. Drew argued against the armed forces directive that blood/plasma was to be separated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(human_classification)" title="Race (human classification)">race</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Blood_donation" title="Blood donation">donor</a>. Drew insisted that there was no racial difference in human blood and that the policy would lead to needless deaths as soldiers and sailors were required to wait for "same race" blood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>By the end of the war the <a href="/wiki/American_Red_Cross" title="American Red Cross">American Red Cross</a> had provided enough blood for over six million plasma packages. Most of the surplus plasma was returned to the United States for civilian use. <a href="/wiki/Serum_albumin" title="Serum albumin">Serum albumin</a> replaced dried plasma for combat use during the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plasma_donation">Plasma donation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_plasma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Plasma donation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Plasma as a <a href="/wiki/Blood_product" title="Blood product">blood product</a> prepared from <a href="/wiki/Blood_donation" title="Blood donation">blood donations</a> is used in <a href="/wiki/Blood_transfusion" title="Blood transfusion">blood transfusions</a>, typically as <a href="/wiki/Fresh_frozen_plasma" title="Fresh frozen plasma">fresh frozen plasma</a> (FFP) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plasma_Frozen_Within_24_Hours_After_Phlebotomy" title="Plasma Frozen Within 24 Hours After Phlebotomy">plasma Frozen Within 24 Hours After Phlebotomy</a> (PF24).  When donating whole blood or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Packed_red_blood_cell" title="Packed red blood cell">packed red blood cell</a> (PRBC) transfusions, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ABO_blood_type" title="ABO blood type">O-</a> is the most desirable and is considered a "universal donor," since it has neither A nor B <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antigens" title="Antigens">antigens</a> and can be safely transfused to most recipients.  Type AB+ is the "universal recipient" type for PRBC donations.  However, for plasma the situation is somewhat reversed. Blood donation centers will sometimes collect only plasma from AB donors through <a href="/wiki/Apheresis" title="Apheresis">apheresis</a>, as their plasma does not contain the antibodies that may cross react with recipient antigens.  As such, AB is often considered the "universal donor" for plasma. Special programs exist just to cater to the male AB plasma donor, because of concerns about <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transfusion_related_acute_lung_injury" title="Transfusion related acute lung injury">transfusion related acute lung injury</a> (TRALI) and female donors who may have higher leukocyte antibodies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> However, some studies show an increased risk of TRALI despite increased leukocyte antibodies in women who have been pregnant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_plasma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following fears of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Variant_Creutzfeldt-Jakob_disease" title="Variant Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease">variant Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VCJD" title="VCJD">vCJD</a>) being spread through the blood supply, the British government began to import American plasma to meet its needs. In 2002, it purchased Life Resources Incorporated to be able to secure plasma supply from the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The company became Plasma Resources UK (PRUK).  It owned <a href="/wiki/Bio_Products_Laboratory" title="Bio Products Laboratory">Bio Products Laboratory</a>. In 2013, the British government sold an 80% stake in PRUK to American hedge fund Bain Capital, in a deal estimated to be worth £230 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The sale was met with criticism in the UK, over both the oversight of the company, and the impact on British blood needs. Confusion arose as some thought that the blood of British blood donors voluntarily donating to NHS Blood and Transplant Services would be sold for profit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHSBTreassures_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHSBTreassures-17">[17]</a></sup> Currently, plasma donated in the United Kingdom is still not used due to concerns about vCJD, and is incinerated after component parts have been separated from it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHSBTreassures_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-NHSBTreassures-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthetic_blood_plasma">Synthetic blood plasma</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_plasma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Synthetic blood plasma">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Simulated_body_fluid" title="Simulated body fluid">Simulated body fluid</a> (SBF) is a solution having a similar ion concentration to that of human blood plasma. SBF is normally used for the surface modification of metallic implants, and more recently in <a href="/wiki/Gene_delivery" title="Gene delivery">gene delivery</a> application.
</p>
