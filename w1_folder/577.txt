<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Adsorption</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(chemistry)" title="Absorption (chemistry)">Absorption</a>.</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Physisorption" title="Physisorption">Physisorption</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemisorption" title="Chemisorption">Chemisorption</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Segregation_(Materials_Science)" title="Segregation (Materials Science)">Segregation (Materials Science)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BET_Multilayer_Adsorption.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="584" data-file-width="1352" height="95" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/BET_Multilayer_Adsorption.svg/220px-BET_Multilayer_Adsorption.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/BET_Multilayer_Adsorption.svg/330px-BET_Multilayer_Adsorption.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/BET_Multilayer_Adsorption.svg/440px-BET_Multilayer_Adsorption.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BET_Multilayer_Adsorption.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Brunauer, Emmett and Teller's <a href="/wiki/Adsorption#BET" title="Adsorption">model</a> of multilayer adsorption is a random distribution of molecules on the material surface.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Adsorption</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Adhesion" title="Adhesion">adhesion</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atoms" title="Atoms">atoms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> or <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a> from a gas, liquid or dissolved solid to a <a href="/wiki/Surface_science" title="Surface science">surface</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> This process creates a film of the <i>adsorbate</i> on the surface of the <i>adsorbent</i>. This process differs from <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(chemistry)" title="Absorption (chemistry)">absorption</a>, in which a <a href="/wiki/Fluid" title="Fluid">fluid</a> (the <i>absorbate</i>) is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dissolution_(chemistry)" title="Dissolution (chemistry)">dissolved</a> by or <a href="/wiki/Permeation" title="Permeation">permeates</a> a liquid or solid (the <i>absorbent</i>), respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Adsorption is a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surface_phenomenon" title="Surface phenomenon">surface phenomenon</a></i>, while absorption involves the whole volume of the material. The term <i><a href="/wiki/Sorption" title="Sorption">sorption</a></i> encompasses both processes, while <i><a href="/wiki/Desorption" title="Desorption">desorption</a></i> is the reverse of it.
</p>
<div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:50%; ;"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303512">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{margin:0.5em auto 0.8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft p,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright p{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:larger;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" “ ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" ” ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:360px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{min-width:100%;margin:0 0 0.8em!important;float:none!important}}</style>
<div class="quotebox-title" style=""><a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Pure_and_Applied_Chemistry" title="International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry">IUPAC</a> definition</div>
<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">Increase in the concentration of a substance at the interface of a condensed and a liquid or gaseous layer owing to the operation of surface forces.
<p>Note 1: Adsorption of proteins is of great importance when a material is in contact with blood or body fluids. In the case of blood, <a href="/wiki/Albumin" title="Albumin">albumin</a>, which is largely predominant, is generally adsorbed first, and then rearrangements occur in favor of other minor proteins according to surface affinity against mass law selection (<a href="/wiki/Vroman_effect" title="Vroman effect">Vroman effect</a>).
</p><p>Note 2: Adsorbed molecules are those that are resistant to washing with the same solvent medium in the case of adsorption from solutions. The washing conditions can thus modify the measurement results, particularly when the interaction energy is low.
</p>
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldbook1990_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-goldbook1990-3">[3]</a></sup></div>
</div>
<p>Similar to <a href="/wiki/Surface_tension" title="Surface tension">surface tension</a>, adsorption is a consequence of <a href="/wiki/Surface_energy" title="Surface energy">surface energy</a>. In a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bulk_material_(materials_science)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bulk material (materials science) (page does not exist)">bulk material</a>, all the bonding requirements (be they <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionic_bond" title="Ionic bond">ionic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">covalent</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metallic_bond" title="Metallic bond">metallic</a>) of the constituent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atoms" title="Atoms">atoms</a> of the material are filled by other atoms in the material. However, atoms on the surface of the adsorbent are not wholly surrounded by other adsorbent atoms and therefore can attract adsorbates. The exact nature of the bonding depends on the details of the species involved, but the adsorption process is generally classified as <a href="/wiki/Physisorption" title="Physisorption">physisorption</a> (characteristic of weak <a href="/wiki/Van_der_Waals_force" title="Van der Waals force">van der Waals forces</a>) or <a href="/wiki/Chemisorption" title="Chemisorption">chemisorption</a> (characteristic of covalent bonding). It may also occur due to electrostatic attraction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Adsorption is present in many natural, physical, biological and chemical systems and is widely used in industrial applications such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterogeneous_catalysts" title="Heterogeneous catalysts">heterogeneous catalysts</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Activated_charcoal" title="Activated charcoal">activated charcoal</a>, capturing and using <a href="/wiki/Waste_heat" title="Waste heat">waste heat</a> to provide cold water for air conditioning and other process requirements (<a href="/wiki/Adsorption#Adsorption_chillers" title="Adsorption">adsorption chillers</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthetic_resins" title="Synthetic resins">synthetic resins</a>, increasing storage capacity of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tunable_nanoporous_carbon" title="Tunable nanoporous carbon">carbide-derived carbons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_purification" title="Water purification">water purification</a>. Adsorption, <a href="/wiki/Ion_exchange" title="Ion exchange">ion exchange</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chromatography" title="Chromatography">chromatography</a> are sorption processes in which certain adsorbates are selectively transferred from the fluid phase to the surface of insoluble, rigid particles suspended in a vessel or packed in a column. Pharmaceutical industry applications, which use adsorption as a means to prolong neurological exposure to specific drugs or parts thereof,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> are lesser known.
</p><p>The word "adsorption" was coined in 1881 by German physicist <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Kayser" title="Heinrich Kayser">Heinrich Kayser</a> (1853 –1940).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotherms">Isotherms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adsorption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Isotherms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Adsorption is usually described through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotherms" title="Isotherms">isotherms</a>, that is, the amount of adsorbate on the adsorbent as a function of its pressure (if gas) or concentration (if liquid) at constant temperature. The quantity adsorbed is nearly always normalized by the mass of the adsorbent to allow comparison of different materials. To date, 15 different isotherm models were developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FooHameed2010_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FooHameed2010-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Henry_adsorption_constant" title="Henry adsorption constant">Henry adsorption constant</a></div>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Langmuir">Langmuir</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adsorption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Langmuir">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Langmuir_equation" title="Langmuir equation">Langmuir equation</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Irving_Langmuir" title="Irving Langmuir">Irving Langmuir</a> was the first to derive a scientifically based adsorption isotherm in 1918.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langmuir_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Langmuir-9">[9]</a></sup> The model applies to gases adsorbed on solid surfaces. It is a semi-empirical isotherm with a kinetic basis and was derived based on statistical thermodynamics. It is the most common isotherm equation to use due to its simplicity and its ability to fit a variety of adsorption data. It is based on four assumptions:
</p>
<ol><li>All of the adsorption sites are equivalent, and each site can only accommodate one molecule.</li>
<li>The surface is energetically homogeneous, and adsorbed molecules do not interact.</li>
<li>There are no phase transitions.</li>
<li>At the maximum adsorption, only a monolayer is formed. Adsorption only occurs on localized sites on the surface, not with other adsorbates.</li></ol>
<p>These four assumptions are seldom all true: there are always imperfections on the surface, adsorbed molecules are not necessarily inert, and the mechanism is clearly not the same for the very first molecules to adsorb to a surface as for the last. The fourth condition is the most troublesome, as frequently more molecules will adsorb to the monolayer; this problem is addressed by the <a href="#BET_isotherm">BET isotherm</a> for relatively flat (non-<a href="/wiki/Microporous_material" title="Microporous material">microporous</a>) surfaces. The Langmuir isotherm is nonetheless the first choice for most models of adsorption and has many applications in surface kinetics (usually called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Langmuir%E2%80%93Hinshelwood_kinetics" title="Langmuir–Hinshelwood kinetics">Langmuir–Hinshelwood kinetics</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="BET">BET</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adsorption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: BET">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kisliuk">Kisliuk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adsorption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Kisliuk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wiki_kisliuk_n2-tungsten.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="132" data-file-width="300" height="97" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Wiki_kisliuk_n2-tungsten.JPG/220px-Wiki_kisliuk_n2-tungsten.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/66/Wiki_kisliuk_n2-tungsten.JPG 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wiki_kisliuk_n2-tungsten.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Two adsorbate nitrogen molecules adsorbing onto a tungsten adsorbent from the precursor state around an island of previously adsorbed adsorbate (left) and via random adsorption (right)</div></div></div>
<p>In other instances, molecular interactions between gas molecules previously adsorbed on a solid surface form significant interactions with gas molecules in the gaseous phases. Hence, adsorption of gas molecules to the surface is more likely to occur around gas molecules that are already present on the solid surface, rendering the Langmuir adsorption isotherm ineffective for the purposes of modelling. This effect was studied in a system where nitrogen was the adsorbate and tungsten was the adsorbent by Paul Kisliuk (1922–2008) in 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kisliuk_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kisliuk-10">[10]</a></sup> To compensate for the increased probability of adsorption occurring around molecules present on the substrate surface, Kisliuk developed the precursor state theory, whereby molecules would enter a precursor state at the interface between the solid adsorbent and adsorbate in the gaseous phase. From here, adsorbate molecules would either adsorb to the adsorbent or desorb into the gaseous phase. The probability of adsorption occurring from the precursor state is dependent on the adsorbate’s proximity to other adsorbate molecules that have already been adsorbed. If the adsorbate molecule in the precursor state is in close proximity to an adsorbate molecule that has already formed on the surface, it has a sticking probability reflected by the size of the S<sub>E</sub> constant and will either be adsorbed from the precursor state at a rate of <i>k</i><sub>EC</sub> or will desorb into the gaseous phase at a rate of <i>k</i><sub>ES</sub>. If an adsorbate molecule enters the precursor state at a location that is remote from any other previously adsorbed adsorbate molecules, the sticking probability is reflected by the size of the S<sub>D</sub> constant.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adsorbents">Adsorbents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adsorption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Adsorbents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics_and_general_requirements">Characteristics and general requirements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adsorption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Characteristics and general requirements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Activated_Carbon.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1740" data-file-width="2419" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Activated_Carbon.jpg/220px-Activated_Carbon.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Activated_Carbon.jpg/330px-Activated_Carbon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Activated_Carbon.jpg/440px-Activated_Carbon.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Activated_Carbon.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Activated carbon is used as an adsorbent</div></div></div>
<p>Adsorbents are used usually in the form of spherical pellets, rods, moldings, or monoliths with a <a href="/wiki/Hydrodynamic_radius" title="Hydrodynamic radius">hydrodynamic radius</a> between 0.25 and 5 mm. They must have high <a href="/wiki/Abrasion_(mechanical)" title="Abrasion (mechanical)">abrasion</a> resistance, high <a href="/wiki/Thermal_stability" title="Thermal stability">thermal stability</a> and small pore diameters, which results in higher exposed surface area and hence high capacity for adsorption. The adsorbents must also have a distinct pore structure that enables fast transport of the gaseous vapors.
</p><p>Most industrial adsorbents fall into one of three classes:
</p>
<ul><li>Oxygen-containing compounds – Are typically hydrophilic and polar, including materials such as <a href="/wiki/Silica_gel" title="Silica gel">silica gel</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zeolites" title="Zeolites">zeolites</a>.</li>
<li>Carbon-based compounds – Are typically hydrophobic and non-polar, including materials such as <a href="/wiki/Activated_carbon" title="Activated carbon">activated carbon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a>.</li>
<li>Polymer-based compounds – Are polar or non-polar functional groups in a porous polymer matrix.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Silica_gel">Silica gel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adsorption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Silica gel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:THC_2003.902.070_Silica_Gel_Adsorber_for_NO2.tif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1231" data-file-width="1541" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/THC_2003.902.070_Silica_Gel_Adsorber_for_NO2.tif/lossy-page1-220px-THC_2003.902.070_Silica_Gel_Adsorber_for_NO2.tif.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/THC_2003.902.070_Silica_Gel_Adsorber_for_NO2.tif/lossy-page1-330px-THC_2003.902.070_Silica_Gel_Adsorber_for_NO2.tif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/THC_2003.902.070_Silica_Gel_Adsorber_for_NO2.tif/lossy-page1-440px-THC_2003.902.070_Silica_Gel_Adsorber_for_NO2.tif.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:THC_2003.902.070_Silica_Gel_Adsorber_for_NO2.tif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Silica gel adsorber for NO<sub>2</sub>, Fixed Nitrogen Research Laboratory, ca.1930s</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Silica_gel" title="Silica gel">Silica gel</a> is a chemically inert, nontoxic, polar and dimensionally stable (&lt; 400 °C or 750 °F) amorphous form of SiO<sub>2</sub>. It is prepared by the reaction between sodium silicate and acetic acid, which is followed by a series of after-treatment processes such as aging, pickling, etc. These after-treatment methods results in various pore size distributions.
</p><p>Silica is used for drying of process air (e.g. oxygen, natural gas) and adsorption of heavy (polar) hydrocarbons from natural gas.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zeolites">Zeolites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adsorption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Zeolites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Zeolite" title="Zeolite">Zeolites</a> are natural or synthetic crystalline aluminosilicates, which have a repeating pore network and release water at high temperature. Zeolites are polar in nature.
</p><p>They are manufactured by hydrothermal synthesis of sodium aluminosilicate or another silica source in an autoclave followed by ion exchange with certain cations (Na<sup>+</sup>, Li<sup>+</sup>, Ca<sup>2+</sup>, K<sup>+</sup>, NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup>). The channel diameter of zeolite cages usually ranges from 2 to 9 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angstrom" title="Angstrom">Å</a>. The ion exchange process is followed by drying of the crystals, which can be pelletized with a binder to form macroporous pellets.
</p><p>Zeolites are applied in drying of process air, CO<sub>2</sub> removal from natural gas, CO removal from reforming gas, air separation, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalytic_cracking" title="Catalytic cracking">catalytic cracking</a>, and catalytic synthesis and reforming.
</p><p>Non-polar (siliceous) zeolites are synthesized from aluminum-free silica sources or by dealumination of aluminum-containing zeolites. The dealumination process is done by treating the zeolite with steam at elevated temperatures, typically greater than 500 °C (930 °F). This high temperature heat treatment breaks the aluminum-oxygen bonds and the aluminum atom is expelled from the zeolite framework.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Activated_carbon">Activated carbon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adsorption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Activated carbon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Activated_carbon" title="Activated carbon">Activated carbon</a> is a highly porous, amorphous solid consisting of microcrystallites with a graphite lattice, usually prepared in small pellets or a powder. It is non-polar and cheap. One of its main drawbacks is that it reacts with oxygen at moderate temperatures (over 300 °C).</p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Demac_isoth.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="712" data-file-width="928" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/52/Demac_isoth.jpg/230px-Demac_isoth.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/52/Demac_isoth.jpg/345px-Demac_isoth.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/52/Demac_isoth.jpg/460px-Demac_isoth.jpg 2x" width="230"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Demac_isoth.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Activated carbon nitrogen isotherm showing a marked microporous type I behavior</div></div></div>
<p>Activated carbon can be manufactured from carbonaceous material, including coal (bituminous, subbituminous, and lignite), peat, wood, or nutshells (e.g., coconut). The manufacturing process consists of two phases, carbonization and activation. The carbonization process includes drying and then heating to separate by-products, including tars and other hydrocarbons from the raw material, as well as to drive off any gases generated. The process is completed by heating the material over 400 °C (750 °F) in an oxygen-free atmosphere that cannot support combustion. The carbonized particles are then "activated" by exposing them to an oxidizing agent, usually steam or carbon dioxide at high temperature. This agent burns off the pore blocking structures created during the carbonization phase and so, they develop a porous, three-dimensional graphite lattice structure. The size of the pores developed during activation is a function of the time that they spend in this stage. Longer exposure times result in larger pore sizes. The most popular aqueous phase carbons are bituminous based because of their hardness, abrasion resistance, pore size distribution, and low cost, but their effectiveness needs to be tested in each application to determine the optimal product.
</p><p>Activated carbon is used for adsorption of organic substances and non-polar adsorbates and it is also usually used for waste gas (and waste water) treatment. It is the most widely used adsorbent since most of its chemical (e.g. surface groups) and physical properties (e.g. pore size distribution and surface area) can be tuned according to what is needed. Its usefulness also derives from its large micropore (and sometimes mesopore) volume and the resulting high surface area.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_capture_and_storage">Carbon capture and storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adsorption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Carbon capture and storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Typical adsorbents used in <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a>  are <a href="/wiki/Zeolite" title="Zeolite">zeolites</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal-organic_framework" title="Metal-organic framework">MOFs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The customization of adsorbents makes them an attractive alternative to absorption. Because adsorbents can be regenerated by temperature or pressure swing, this step can be less energy intensive than <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(chemistry)" title="Absorption (chemistry)">absorption</a> regeneration methods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Major problems that are present with adsorption cost in carbon capture are: regenerating the adsorbent, mass ratio, solvent/MOF, cost of adsorbent, production of the adsorbent, lifetime of adsorbent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protein_and_surfactant_adsorption">Protein  and surfactant adsorption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adsorption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Protein and surfactant adsorption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Protein_adsorption" title="Protein adsorption">Protein adsorption</a> is a process that has a fundamental role in the field of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biomaterials" title="Biomaterials">biomaterials</a>. Indeed, biomaterial surfaces in contact with biological media, such as blood or serum, are immediately coated by proteins. Therefore, living <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> do not interact directly with the biomaterial surface, but with the adsorbed proteins layer. This protein layer mediates the interaction between biomaterials and cells, translating biomaterial physical and chemical properties into a "biological language".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In fact, <a href="/wiki/Cell_membrane" title="Cell membrane">cell membrane</a> <a href="/wiki/Receptor_(biochemistry)" title="Receptor (biochemistry)">receptors</a> bind to protein layer bioactive sites and these receptor-protein binding events are transduced, through the cell membrane, in a manner that stimulates specific intracellular processes that then determine cell adhesion, shape, growth and differentiation. Protein adsorption is influenced by many surface properties such as surface <a href="/wiki/Wetting" title="Wetting">wettability</a>, surface chemical composition <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and surface nanometre-scale morphology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
Surfactant adsorption is a similar phenomenon, but utilising surfactant molecules in the place of proteins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adsorption_chillers">Adsorption chillers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adsorption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Adsorption chillers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Adsorption_chiller_schematic.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="786" data-file-width="1314" height="132" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Adsorption_chiller_schematic.svg/220px-Adsorption_chiller_schematic.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Adsorption_chiller_schematic.svg/330px-Adsorption_chiller_schematic.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Adsorption_chiller_schematic.svg/440px-Adsorption_chiller_schematic.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Adsorption_chiller_schematic.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A schematic diagram of an adsorption chiller: (1) heat is lost through evaporation of refrigerant, (2) refrigerant vapour is adsorbed onto the solid medium, (3) refrigerant is desorbed from the solid medium section not in use, (4) refrigerant is condensed and returned to the start, (5) &amp; (6) solid medium is cycled between adsorption and desorption to regenerate it.</div></div></div>
<p>Combining an adsorbent with a refrigerant, <a href="/wiki/Adsorption_refrigeration" title="Adsorption refrigeration">adsorption chillers</a> use heat to provide a cooling effect. This heat, in the form of hot water, may come from any number of industrial sources including waste heat from industrial processes, prime heat from solar thermal installations or from the exhaust or water jacket heat of a piston engine or turbine.
</p><p>Although there are similarities between adsorption chillers and <a href="/wiki/Absorption_refrigerator" title="Absorption refrigerator">absorption refrigeration</a>, the former is based on the interaction between gases and solids. The adsorption chamber of the chiller is filled with a solid material (for example zeolite, silica gel, alumina, active carbon or certain types of metal salts), which in its neutral state has adsorbed the refrigerant. When heated, the solid desorbs (releases) refrigerant vapour, which subsequently is cooled and liquefied. This liquid refrigerant then provides a cooling effect at the evaporator from its <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_vaporization" title="Enthalpy of vaporization">enthalpy of vaporization</a>. In the final stage the refrigerant vapour is (re)adsorbed into the solid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> As an adsorption chiller requires no compressor, it is relatively quiet.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Portal_site_mediated_adsorption">Portal site mediated adsorption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adsorption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Portal site mediated adsorption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Portal site mediated adsorption is a model for site-selective activated gas adsorption in metallic catalytic systems that contain a variety of different adsorption sites. In such systems, low-coordination "edge and corner" defect-like sites can exhibit significantly lower adsorption enthalpies than high-coordination (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basal_plane" title="Basal plane">basal plane</a>) sites. As a result, these sites can serve as "portals" for very rapid adsorption to the rest of the surface. The phenomenon relies on the common "spillover" effect (described below), where certain adsorbed species exhibit high mobility on some surfaces. The model explains seemingly inconsistent observations of gas adsorption thermodynamics and kinetics in catalytic systems where surfaces can exist in a range of coordination structures, and it has been successfully applied to bimetallic catalytic systems where synergistic activity is observed.
</p><p>In contrast to pure spillover, portal site adsorption refers to surface diffusion to adjacent adsorption sites, not to non-adsorptive support surfaces.
</p><p>The model appears to have been first proposed for carbon monoxide on silica-supported platinum by Brandt <i>et al.</i> (1993).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brandt_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brandt-19">[19]</a></sup> A similar, but independent model was developed by King and co-workers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uner1997_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Uner1997-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Narayan_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Narayan-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiel_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wiel-22">[22]</a></sup> to describe hydrogen adsorption on silica-supported alkali promoted ruthenium, silver-ruthenium and copper-ruthenium bimetallic catalysts. The same group applied the model to CO hydrogenation (Fischer–Tropsch synthesis).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Uner1998_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Uner1998-23">[23]</a></sup> Zupanc <i>et al.</i> (2002) subsequently confirmed the same model for hydrogen adsorption on magnesia-supported caesium-ruthenium bimetallic catalysts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zupanc_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zupanc-24">[24]</a></sup> Trens <i>et al.</i> (2009) have similarly described CO surface diffusion on carbon-supported Pt particles of varying morphology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trens_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trens-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adsorption_spillover">Adsorption spillover</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adsorption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Adsorption spillover">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the case catalytic or adsorbent systems where a metal species is dispersed upon a support (or carrier) material (often quasi-inert oxides, such as alumina or silica), it is possible for an adsorptive species to indirectly adsorb to the support surface under conditions where such adsorption is thermodynamically unfavorable. The presence of the metal serves as a lower-energy pathway for gaseous species to first adsorb to the metal and then diffuse on the support surface. This is possible because the adsorbed species attains a lower energy state once it has adsorbed to the metal, thus lowering the activation barrier between the gas phase species and the support-adsorbed species.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_spillover" title="Hydrogen spillover">Hydrogen spillover</a> is the most common example of an adsorptive spillover. In the case of hydrogen, adsorption is most often accompanied with dissociation of molecular hydrogen (H<sub>2</sub>) to atomic hydrogen (H), followed by spillover of the hydrogen atoms present.
</p><p>The spillover effect has been used to explain many observations in <a href="/wiki/Heterogeneous_catalysis" title="Heterogeneous catalysis">heterogeneous catalysis</a> and adsorption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rozanov_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rozanov-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Polymer_adsorption">Polymer adsorption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adsorption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Polymer adsorption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Polymer_adsorption" title="Polymer adsorption">polymer adsorption</a></div>
<p>Adsorption of molecules onto polymer surfaces is central to a number of applications, including development of non-stick coatings and in various biomedical devices. Polymers may also be adsorbed to surfaces through <a href="/wiki/Polyelectrolyte_adsorption" title="Polyelectrolyte adsorption">polyelectrolyte adsorption</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adsorption_in_viruses">Adsorption in viruses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Adsorption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Adsorption in viruses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Viral_entry" title="Viral entry">Adsorption</a> is the first step in the <a href="/wiki/Viral_life_cycle" title="Viral life cycle">viral life cycle</a>. The next steps are penetration, uncoating, synthesis (transcription if needed, and translation), and release. The virus replication cycle, in this respect, is similar for all types of viruses. Factors such as transcription may or may not be needed if the virus is able to integrate its genomic information in the cell's nucleus, or if the virus can replicate itself directly within the cell's cytoplasm.
</p>
