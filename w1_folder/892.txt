<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Coal pollution mitigation</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Clean coal" redirects here. For the technology, see <a href="/wiki/Clean_coal_technology" title="Clean coal technology">Clean coal technology</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Figure_1-_Sample_Layout_of_Emissions_Controls_at_a_Coal_Power_Plant_(7315637538)_(cropped).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="520" data-file-width="963" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Figure_1-_Sample_Layout_of_Emissions_Controls_at_a_Coal_Power_Plant_%287315637538%29_%28cropped%29.jpg/330px-Figure_1-_Sample_Layout_of_Emissions_Controls_at_a_Coal_Power_Plant_%287315637538%29_%28cropped%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Figure_1-_Sample_Layout_of_Emissions_Controls_at_a_Coal_Power_Plant_%287315637538%29_%28cropped%29.jpg/495px-Figure_1-_Sample_Layout_of_Emissions_Controls_at_a_Coal_Power_Plant_%287315637538%29_%28cropped%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Figure_1-_Sample_Layout_of_Emissions_Controls_at_a_Coal_Power_Plant_%287315637538%29_%28cropped%29.jpg/660px-Figure_1-_Sample_Layout_of_Emissions_Controls_at_a_Coal_Power_Plant_%287315637538%29_%28cropped%29.jpg 2x" width="330"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Figure_1-_Sample_Layout_of_Emissions_Controls_at_a_Coal_Power_Plant_(7315637538)_(cropped).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Government_Accountability_Office" title="Government Accountability Office">Government Accountability Office</a> diagram showing emissions controls at a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_fired_power_plant" title="Coal fired power plant">coal fired power plant</a></div></div></div>
<p><b>Coal pollution mitigation</b>, often referred to by the term <b>clean coal</b>, is a series of systems and technologies that seek to mitigate the pollution and other environmental effects normally associated with the burning (though not the <a href="/wiki/Coal_mining" title="Coal mining">mining</a> or processing) of <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, which is widely regarded as the dirtiest of the common fuels for industrial processes and power generation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NatGeoMyth_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NatGeoMyth-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Approaches attempt to mitigate emissions of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) and other <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Naturally_occurring_radioactive_material" title="Naturally occurring radioactive material">radioactive materials</a>, that arise from the use of coal, mainly for electrical power generation, using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_pollution_mitigation_technology" title="Coal pollution mitigation technology">various technologies</a>. Historical efforts to reduce coal pollution focused on <a href="/wiki/Flue-gas_desulfurization" title="Flue-gas desulfurization">flue-gas desulfurization</a> starting in the 1850s and clean burn technologies.  These efforts have been very successful in countries with strong environmental regulation, such as the US, where emissions of acid-rain causing compounds and particulates have been reduced by up to 90%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> since 1995. More recent developments include <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a>, which pumps and stores CO<sub>2</sub> emissions underground, and <a href="/wiki/Integrated_gasification_combined_cycle" title="Integrated gasification combined cycle">integrated gasification combined cycle</a> (IGCC) involve <a href="/wiki/Coal_gasification" title="Coal gasification">coal gasification</a>, which provides a basis for increased efficiency and lower cost in capturing CO<sub>2</sub> emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are seven <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clean_coal_technologies" title="Clean coal technologies">technologies</a> deployed or proposed by the <a href="/wiki/National_Mining_Association" title="National Mining Association">National Mining Association</a> for deployment in the United States:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a> (CCS),</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Flue-gas_desulfurization" title="Flue-gas desulfurization">flue-gas desulfurization</a>,</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fluidized_bed_combustion" title="Fluidized bed combustion">fluidized-bed combustion</a>,</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Integrated_gasification_combined_cycle" title="Integrated gasification combined cycle">integrated gasification combined cycle</a> (IGCC),</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/LO-NOx_burner" title="LO-NOx burner">low nitrogen oxide burners</a>,</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Selective_catalytic_reduction" title="Selective catalytic reduction">selective catalytic reduction</a> (SCR), and</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrostatic_precipitators" title="Electrostatic precipitators">electrostatic precipitators</a>.</li></ul>
<p>Of the 22 clean coal demonstration projects funded by the U.S. Department of Energy since 2003, none are in operation as of February 2017, having been abandoned or delayed due to capital budget overruns or discontinued because of excessive operating expenses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulations">Regulations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_pollution_mitigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Regulations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since the 1970s, various policy and regulatory measures have driven coal pollution mitigation.  In the US, the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)" title="Clean Air Act (United States)">Clean Air Act</a> was the primary driving force in reducing particulate emissions and acid rain from coal combustion. As regulations have increased the demand for coal pollution mitigation technologies, costs have fallen and performance has improved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEA_Coal_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-IEA_Coal-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The widespread deployment of pollution-control equipment to reduce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulphur_dioxide" title="Sulphur dioxide">sulphur dioxide</a>, NO<sub>x</sub> and dust emissions is just one example that brought cleaner air to many countries. The desire to tackle rising CO<sub>2</sub> emissions to address <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> later introduced <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEA_Coal_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-IEA_Coal-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Within the United States, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_Capture_and_Storage" title="Carbon Capture and Storage">Carbon Capture and Storage</a> technologies, also sometimes referred to as carbon capture and sequestration, are mainly being developed in response to regulations by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a>—most notably the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)" title="Clean Air Act (United States)">Clean Air Act</a>—and in anticipation of legislation that seeks to mitigate <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loan_guarantees" title="Loan guarantees">Loan guarantees</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tax_incentives" title="Tax incentives">tax incentives</a> have a long history of use in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Member_state_of_the_European_Union" title="Member state of the European Union">EU countries</a> and the US to encourage the introduction of coal pollution mitigation and other technologies to reduce environmental impact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEA_Coal_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-IEA_Coal-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impact_of_coal">Environmental impact of coal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_pollution_mitigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Environmental impact of coal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_the_coal_industry" title="Environmental impact of the coal industry">Environmental impact of the coal industry</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenhouse_gasses">Greenhouse gasses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_pollution_mitigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Greenhouse gasses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">Greenhouse gas</a></div>
<p>Combustion of coal--which is mostly carbon--produces carbon dioxide as a product of combustion.  According to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a>, the burning of coal, a <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a>, is a significant contributor to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. (See the UN <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">IPCC Fourth Assessment Report</a>). For 1 ton of coal burned, 2.86 tons of carbon dioxide is created.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">Carbon sequestration</a> technology has yet to be tested on a large scale and may not be safe or successful. Sequestered CO<sub>2</sub> may eventually leak up through the ground, may lead to unexpected geological instability or may cause contamination of aquifers used for drinking water supplies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>As 25.5% of the world's electrical generation in 2004 was from coal-fired generation (see <a href="/wiki/World_energy_consumption" title="World energy consumption">world energy consumption</a>), reaching the carbon dioxide reduction targets of the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> will require modifications to how coal is used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncseonline.org_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-ncseonline.org-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Combustion_By-products">Combustion By-products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_pollution_mitigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Combustion By-products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By-products of coal combustion are compounds which are released into the atmosphere as a result of burning coal.  Coal includes contaminants such as sulfur compounds and non-combustible minerals.  When coal is burned, the minerals become ash (i.e <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particulate_matter" title="Particulate matter">particulate matter</a> or PM) and the sulfur forms sulfur dioxide (SO<sub>2</sub>).  Since air is mostly <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, combustion of coal often leads to production of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxides</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">Sulfur  dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxides</a> are primary causes of <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a>.  For many years--before greenhouse gasses were widely understood to be a threat-- it was thought that these by-products were the only drawback to using coal.  These by-products are still a problem, but they have been greatly diminished in most advanced countries due to clean air regulations.
It is possible to remove most of the sulfur dioxide (SO<sub>2</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxides</a> (NO<sub>x</sub>), and <a href="/wiki/Particulates" title="Particulates">particulate matter</a> (PM) emissions from the coal-burning process.   For example, various techniques are used in a <a href="/wiki/Coal_preparation_plant" title="Coal preparation plant">coal preparation plant</a> to reduce the amount of non-combustible matter (i.e. ash) in the coal prior to burning.  During combustion, <a href="/wiki/Fluidized_bed_combustion" title="Fluidized bed combustion">fluidized bed combustion</a> is used to reduce sulfur dioxide emissions.  After burning, particulate matter (i.e. ash and dust) can be reduced using an <a href="/wiki/Electrostatic_precipitator" title="Electrostatic precipitator">electrostatic precipitator</a> and sulfur dioxide emissions can be further reduced with <a href="/wiki/Flue-gas_desulfurization" title="Flue-gas desulfurization">Flue-gas desulfurization</a>.  Trace amounts of <a href="/wiki/Radionuclide" title="Radionuclide">radionuclides</a> are more difficult to remove.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Coal-fired power plants are the largest aggregate source of the toxic heavy metal <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>: 50 tons per year come from coal power plants out of 150 tons emitted nationally in the USA and 5000 tons globally. However, according to the United States Geological Survey, the trace amounts of mercury in coal by-products do not pose a threat to public health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-14">[14]</a></sup> A study in 2013 found that Mercury found in the fish in the Pacific Ocean could possibly be linked to coal-fired plants in Asia.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Potential_financial_impact">Potential financial impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_pollution_mitigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Potential financial impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Whether carbon capture and storage technology is adopted worldwide will “…depend less on science than on economics. Cleaning coal is very expensive.” <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cost_of_converting_a_single_coal-fired_power_plant">Cost of converting a single coal-fired power plant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_pollution_mitigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cost of converting a single coal-fired power plant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Conversion of a conventional coal-fired power plant is done by injecting the CO<sub>2</sub> into <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_carbonate" title="Ammonium carbonate">ammonium carbonate</a> after which it is then transported and deposited underground (preferably in soil beneath the sea).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Can_coal_ever_be_clean_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Can_coal_ever_be_clean-16">[16]</a></sup> This injection process however is by far the most expensive. Besides the cost of the equipment and the ammonium carbonate, the coal-fired power plant also needs to use 30% of its generated heat to do the injection (parasitic load). A test-setup has been done in the American Electric Power Mountaineer coal-burning power plant.
</p><p>One solution to reduce this thermal loss/parasitic load is to burn the pulverised load with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pure_oxygen" title="Pure oxygen">pure oxygen</a> instead of air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Can_coal_ever_be_clean_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Can_coal_ever_be_clean-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cost_implications_for_new_coal-fired_power_plants">Cost implications for new coal-fired power plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_pollution_mitigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cost implications for new coal-fired power plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Newly built coal-fired power plants can be made to immediately use gasification of the coal prior to combustion. This makes it much easier to separate off the CO<sub>2</sub> from the exhaust fumes, making the process cheaper. This gasification process is done in new coal-burning power plants such as the coal-burning power plant at <a href="/wiki/Tianjin" title="Tianjin">Tianjin</a>, called "<a href="/wiki/GreenGen" title="GreenGen">GreenGen</a>".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Costs_for_US-wide_conversion">Costs for US-wide conversion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_pollution_mitigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Costs for US-wide conversion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The projected nationwide costs for the implementing of carbon capture and storage (CCS) in coal-fired power plants in the USA (presumably using a conventional tactic, see above) can be found in the Wall Street Journal article. Credit Suisse Group says $15 billion needs to be invested in CCS over the next 10 years for it to play an important role in climate change. The <a href="/wiki/International_Energy_Agency" title="International Energy Agency">International Energy Agency</a> says $20 billion is needed. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pew_Center_on_Global_Climate_Change" title="Pew Center on Global Climate Change">Pew Center on Global Climate Change</a> says the number is as high as $30 billion. Those figures dwarf the actual investments to date.
</p><p>In the US, the Bush administration spent about $2.5 billion on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_pollution_mitigation_technology" title="Coal pollution mitigation technology">a range of mitigation technologies</a> — a large amount, but far less than the amounts previously suggested. CCS proponents say both the government and the private sector need to step up their investments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Potential_financial_benefits">Potential financial benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_pollution_mitigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Potential financial benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The coal industry in the US has the potential to make billions of dollars if clean coal technologies are pursued. It is estimated that from 2000 to 2020 the industry could make up to $15 billion in reduced fuel costs, $25 billion in avoided environmental costs, and $32 billion from exporting the equipment and licensing for use in other countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_pollution_mitigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Australia, carbon capture and storage was often referred to by then <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Australia" title="Prime Minister of Australia">Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Rudd" title="Kevin Rudd">Kevin Rudd</a> as a possible way to reduce greenhouse gas emissions. (The previous <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Australia" title="Prime Minister of Australia">Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Howard" title="John Howard">John Howard</a> had stated that <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> was a better alternative, as CCS technology may not prove to be economically feasible.)
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_pollution_mitigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2014 SaskPower a provincial-owned electric utility finished renovations on Boundary Dam’s boiler number 3 making it the worlds first post-combustion carbon capture storage facility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-19">[19]</a></sup> The renovation project ended up costing a little over $1.2 billion and can scrub out CO2 and other toxin from up to 90 percent of the flue gas that it emits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_pollution_mitigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since 2006, China keeps releasing more CO<sub>2</sub> than <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_carbon_dioxide_emissions" title="List of countries by carbon dioxide emissions">any other country</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Researchers in China are focusing on increasing efficiency of burning coal so they can get more power out of less coal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-25">[25]</a></sup> It is estimated that new high efficiency power plants could reduce CO2 emission by 7% because they won't have to burn as much coal to get the same amount of power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_pollution_mitigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Fukushima_Daiichi_nuclear_disaster" title="Fukushima Daiichi nuclear disaster">catastrophic failure of the Fukushima I Nuclear Power Plant</a> in Japan that resulted from the <a href="/wiki/2011_T%C5%8Dhoku_earthquake_and_tsunami" title="2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami">2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_reaction_to_Fukushima_Daiichi_nuclear_disaster" title="Japanese reaction to Fukushima Daiichi nuclear disaster">subsequent widespread public opposition against nuclear power</a>, high energy, lower emission (HELE) coal power plants were increasingly favored by the <a href="/wiki/Shinz%C5%8D_Abe" title="Shinzō Abe">Shinzō Abe</a>-led government to recoup lost energy capacity from the partial shutdown of nuclear power plants in Japan and to replace aging coal and oil-fired power plants, while meeting 2030 emission targets of the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Agreement" title="Paris Agreement">Paris Agreement</a>. 45 HELE power plants have been planned, purportedly to employ <a href="/wiki/Integrated_gasification_fuel_cell_cycle" title="Integrated gasification fuel cell cycle">integrated gasification fuel cell cycle</a>, a further development of integrated gasification combined cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Japan had adopted prior pilot projects on IGCC coal power plants in the early-1990s and late-2000s.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_pollution_mitigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the United States, "clean coal" was mentioned by former President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> on several occasions, including his 2007 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_of_the_Union_Address" title="State of the Union Address">State of the Union Address</a>. Bush's position was that carbon capture and storage technologies should be encouraged as one means to reduce the country's dependence on foreign oil.
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_2008" title="United States presidential election, 2008">US Presidential campaign for 2008</a>, both candidates <a href="/wiki/John_McCain" title="John McCain">John McCain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> expressed interest in the development of CCS technologies as part of an overall comprehensive energy plan. The development of pollution mitigation technologies could also create export business for the United States or any other country working on it.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/American_Recovery_and_Reinvestment_Act_of_2009" title="American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009">American Reinvestment and Recovery Act</a>, signed in 2009 by President Obama, allocated $3.4 billion for advanced carbon capture and storage technologies, including demonstration projects.
</p><p>Former Secretary of State <a href="/wiki/Hillary_Clinton" title="Hillary Clinton">Hillary Clinton</a> has said that "we should strive to have new electricity generation come from other sources, such as clean coal and renewables,” and former Energy Secretary <a href="/wiki/Steven_Chu" title="Steven Chu">Dr. Steven Chu</a> has said that “It is absolutely worthwhile to invest in carbon capture and storage", noting that even if the U.S. and Europe turned their backs on coal, developing nations like India and China would likely not.
</p><p>During the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election_debates,_2012" title="United States presidential election debates, 2012">2012 United States presidential election debate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mitt_Romney" title="Mitt Romney">Mitt Romney</a> expressed his support for clean coal, and claimed that current federal policies were hampering the coal industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-npr2012-10-03_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-npr2012-10-03-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_of_the_approach">Criticism of the approach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_pollution_mitigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Criticism of the approach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Environmentalists such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dan_Becker" title="Dan Becker">Dan Becker</a>, director of the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club" title="Sierra Club">Sierra Club</a>'s Global Warming and Energy Program, believes that the term "clean coal" is misleading: "There is no such thing as clean coal and there never will be. It's an <a href="/wiki/Oxymoron" title="Oxymoron">oxymoron</a>." The Sierra Club's Coal Campaign has launched a site refuting the clean coal statements and advertising of the coal industry.
</p><p>Complaints focus on the environmental impacts of <a href="/wiki/Coal_mining" title="Coal mining">coal extraction</a>, high costs to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">sequester carbon</a>, and uncertainty of how to manage end result <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">pollutants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radionuclide" title="Radionuclide">radionuclides</a>. In reference to sequestration of carbon, concerns exist about whether geologic storage of CO<sub>2</sub> in reservoirs, aquifers, <i>etc.</i>, is indefinite/permanent.
</p><p>The palaeontologist and influential environmental activist <a href="/wiki/Tim_Flannery" title="Tim Flannery">Tim Flannery</a> made the assertion that the concept of clean coal might not be viable for all geographical locations.
</p><p>Critics also believe that the continuing construction of coal-powered plants (whether or not they use carbon sequestration techniques) encourages unsustainable mining practices for coal, which can strip away mountains, hillsides, and natural areas. They also point out that there can be a large amount of energy required and pollution emitted in transporting the coal to the power plants.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/The_Climate_Reality_Project" title="The Climate Reality Project">The Reality Coalition</a>, a US non-profit climate organization composed of the Alliance for Climate Protection, the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club" title="Sierra Club">Sierra Club</a>, the <a href="/wiki/National_Wildlife_Federation" title="National Wildlife Federation">National Wildlife Federation</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Natural_Resources_Defense_Council" title="Natural Resources Defense Council">Natural Resources Defense Council</a> and the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Conservation_Voters" title="League of Conservation Voters">League of Conservation Voters</a>, ran a series of television commercials in 2008 and 2009. The commercials were highly critical of attempts to mitigate coal's pollution, stating that without capturing CO<sub>2</sub> emissions and storing it safely that it cannot be called "clean coal".
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a> is a major opponent of the concept, because they view emissions and wastes as not being avoided but instead transferred from one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waste_stream" title="Waste stream">waste stream</a> to another. According to <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace_USA" title="Greenpeace USA">Greenpeace USA</a>'s Executive Director <a href="/wiki/Phil_Radford" title="Phil Radford">Phil Radford</a> speaking in 2012, "even the industry figures it will take 10 or 20 years to arrive, and we need solutions sooner than that. We need to scale up renewable energy; “clean coal” is a distraction from that."
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clean_coal">Clean coal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_pollution_mitigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Clean coal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term Clean Coal in modern society often refers to the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a> process. The term has been used by advertisers, lobbyists, and politicians such as  <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prior_terminology">Prior terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coal_pollution_mitigation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Prior terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The industry term "clean coal" is increasingly used in reference to carbon capture and storage, an advanced theoretical process that would eliminate or significantly reduce carbon dioxide emissions from coal-based plants and permanently sequester them. More generally, the term has been found in modern usage to describe technologies designed to enhance both the efficiency and the environmental acceptability of coal extraction, preparation, and use.
</p><p>U.S. Senate Bill 911 in April, 1987, defined clean coal technology as follows:
</p>
<blockquote><p>"The term clean coal technology means any technology...deployed at a new or existing facility which will achieve significant reductions in air emissions of sulfur dioxide or oxides of nitrogen associated with the utilization of coal in the generation of electricity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Before being adopted in this fashion, historically "clean coal" was used to refer to clean-burning coal with low levels of impurities, though this term faded after rates of domestic coal usage dropped. The term appeared in a speech to mine workers in 1918, in context indicating coal that was "free of dirt and impurities."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In the early 20th century, prior to World War II, clean coal (also called "smokeless coal") generally referred to <a href="/wiki/Anthracite" title="Anthracite">anthracite</a> and high-grade <a href="/wiki/Bituminous_coal" title="Bituminous coal">bituminous coal</a>, used for cooking and home heating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
