<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Methanogen</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Not to be confused with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methanotrophs" title="Methanotrophs">methanotrophs</a>.</div>
<p><b>Methanogens</b> are microorganisms that produce <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metabolic" title="Metabolic">metabolic</a> byproduct in <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(environmental)" title="Hypoxia (environmental)">anoxic</a> conditions. They are prokaryotic and belong to the domain of <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a>. They are common in <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a>, where they are responsible for <a href="/wiki/Marsh_gas" title="Marsh gas">marsh gas</a>, and in the digestive tracts of animals such as <a href="/wiki/Ruminant" title="Ruminant">ruminants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a>, where they are responsible for the methane content of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Belching" title="Belching">belching</a> in ruminants and <a href="/wiki/Flatulence" title="Flatulence">flatulence</a> in humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_(ocean)" title="Marine (ocean)">marine</a> <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediments</a> the biological production of methane, also termed <a href="/wiki/Methanogenesis" title="Methanogenesis">methanogenesis</a>, is generally confined to where <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfates</a> are depleted, below the top layers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Moreover, the methanogenic archaea populations play an indispensable role in anaerobic wastewater treatments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Others are <a href="/wiki/Extremophile" title="Extremophile">extremophiles</a>, found in environments such as <a href="/wiki/Hot_spring" title="Hot spring">hot springs</a> and submarine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_vents" title="Hydrothermal vents">hydrothermal vents</a> as well as in the "solid" rock of the Earth's crust, kilometers below the surface.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_description">Physical description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Physical description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Methanogens are coccoid (spherical shaped) or bacilli (rod shaped). There are over 50 described species of methanogens, which do not form a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monophyletic" title="Monophyletic">monophyletic</a> group, although all methanogens belong to <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">Archaea</a>.  They are mostly <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_organism" title="Anaerobic organism">anaerobic organisms</a> that cannot function under aerobic conditions, but recently a species (<i><a href="/wiki/Candidatus" title="Candidatus">Candidatus</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Methanothrix_paradoxum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Methanothrix paradoxum (page does not exist)">Methanothrix paradoxum</a></i>) has been identified that can function in anoxic microsites within aerobic environments. They are very sensitive to the presence of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> even at trace level. Usually, they cannot sustain oxygen stress for a prolonged time. However, <i><a href="/wiki/Methanosarcina_barkeri" title="Methanosarcina barkeri">Methanosarcina barkeri</a></i> is exceptional in possessing a <a href="/wiki/Superoxide_dismutase" title="Superoxide dismutase">superoxide dismutase</a> (SOD) <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzyme</a>, and may survive longer than the others in the presence of O<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Some methanogens, called <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenotroph" title="Hydrogenotroph">hydrogenotrophic</a>, use <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) as a source of carbon, and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Reducing_agent" title="Reducing agent">reducing agent</a>.
</p><p>The reduction of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> into <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> in the presence of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> can be expressed as follows:
</p>
<dl><dd>CO<sub>2</sub> + 4 H<sub>2</sub> → CH<sub>4</sub> + 2H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Some of the CO<sub>2</sub> is reacted with the hydrogen to produce methane, which creates an <a href="/wiki/Electrochemical_gradient" title="Electrochemical gradient">electrochemical gradient</a> across cell membrane, used to generate <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a> through <a href="/wiki/Chemiosmosis" title="Chemiosmosis">chemiosmosis</a>. In contrast, <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> use water as their <a href="/wiki/Reducing_agent" title="Reducing agent">reducing agent</a>.
</p><p>Methanogens lack <a href="/wiki/Peptidoglycan" title="Peptidoglycan">peptidoglycan</a>, a polymer that is found in the <a href="/wiki/Cell_wall" title="Cell wall">cell walls</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">Bacteria</a> but not in those of Archaea. Some methanogens have a cell wall that is composed of <a href="/wiki/Pseudopeptidoglycan" title="Pseudopeptidoglycan">pseudopeptidoglycan</a>. Other methanogens do not, but have at least one <a href="/wiki/Paracrystalline" title="Paracrystalline">paracrystalline</a> array (S-layer) made up of <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a> that fit together like a <a href="/wiki/Jigsaw_puzzle" title="Jigsaw puzzle">jigsaw puzzle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extreme_living_areas">Extreme living areas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Extreme living areas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Methanogens play the vital ecological role in <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(environmental)" title="Hypoxia (environmental)">anaerobic environments</a> of removing excess hydrogen and fermentation products that have been produced by other forms of <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_respiration" title="Anaerobic respiration">anaerobic respiration</a>. Methanogens typically thrive in environments in which all <a href="/wiki/Electron_acceptor" title="Electron acceptor">electron acceptors</a> other than CO<sub>2</sub> (such as <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrate</a>,  ferric<a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> (Fe(III)), and <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfate</a>) have been depleted.  In deep <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basaltic</a> rocks near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mid_ocean_ridge" title="Mid ocean ridge">mid ocean ridges</a>, they can obtain their <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Serpentinite#Serpentinite_reactions" title="Serpentinite">serpentinisation</a> reaction of <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">olivine</a> as observed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lost_City_(hydrothermal_field)" title="Lost City (hydrothermal field)">hydrothermal field of Lost City</a>.
</p><p>The thermal breakdown of water and water <a href="/wiki/Radiolysis" title="Radiolysis">radiolysis</a> are other possible sources of hydrogen.
</p><p>Methanogens are key agents of remineralization of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_carbon" title="Organic carbon">organic carbon</a> in <a href="/wiki/Continental_margin" title="Continental margin">continental margin</a> sediments and other aquatic sediments with high rates of sedimentation and high sediment organic matter.  Under the correct conditions of pressure and temperature, biogenic methane can accumulate in massive deposits of <a href="/wiki/Methane_clathrate" title="Methane clathrate">methane clathrates</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kvenvolden_1995_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kvenvolden_1995-7">[7]</a></sup> which account for a significant fraction of organic carbon in continental margin sediments and represent a key reservoir of a potent greenhouse gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Milkov_2004_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Milkov_2004-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Methanogens have been found in several extreme environments on Earth – buried under kilometres of ice in <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> and living in hot, dry desert soil. They are known to be the most common archaebacteria in deep subterranean habitats. Live microbes making methane were found in a glacial ice core sample retrieved from about three kilometres under Greenland by researchers from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a>. They also found a constant metabolism able to repair macromolecular damage, at temperatures of 145 to –40 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another study<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> has also discovered methanogens in a harsh environment on Earth. Researchers studied dozens of soil and vapour samples from five different desert environments in <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Idaho" title="Idaho">Idaho</a> and <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>. Of these, five soil samples and three vapour samples from the vicinity of the <a href="/wiki/Mars_Desert_Research_Station" title="Mars Desert Research Station">Mars Desert Research Station</a> in Utah were found to have signs of viable methanogens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some scientists have proposed that the presence of methane in the <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Martian</a> atmosphere may be indicative of native methanogens on that planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Closely related to the methanogens are the anaerobic methane oxidizers, which utilize methane as a substrate in conjunction with the reduction of sulfate and nitrate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Most methanogens are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autotrophic" title="Autotrophic">autotrophic</a> producers, but those that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidize" title="Oxidize">oxidize</a> CH<sub>3</sub>COO<sup>−</sup> are classed as <a href="/wiki/Chemotroph" title="Chemotroph">chemotroph</a> instead.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparative_genomics_and_molecular_signatures">Comparative genomics and molecular signatures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Comparative genomics and molecular signatures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Comparative genomic analysis has led to the identification of 31 signature proteins which are specific for the methanogens (also known as <i>Methanoarchaeota</i>). Most of these proteins are related to methanogenesis, and they could serve as potential molecular markers for the methanogens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gupta_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gupta-14">[14]</a></sup> Additionally, 10 proteins found in all methanogens which are shared by <i><a href="/wiki/Archaeoglobus" title="Archaeoglobus">Archaeoglobus</a></i>, suggest that these two groups are related.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gupta_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gupta-14">[14]</a></sup> In phylogenetic trees, the methanogens are not monophyletic and they are generally split into three clades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gupta_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gupta-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Hence, the unique shared presence of large numbers of proteins by all methanogens could be due to lateral gene transfers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gupta_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-Gupta-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Metabolism">Metabolism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Metabolism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Methane_production">Methane production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Methane production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wastewater_treatment">Wastewater treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Wastewater treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Methanogens are widely used in anaerobic digestors to treat wastewater as well as aqueous organic pollutants. Industries have selected methanogens for their ability to perform <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biomethanation" title="Biomethanation">biomethanation</a> during wastewater decomposition thereby rendering the process sustainable and cost-effective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Bio-decomposition in the anaerobic digester involves a four-staged cooperative action performed by different microorganisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The first stage is the hydrolysis of insoluble polymerized organic matter by anaerobes such as Streptococcus and Enterobacterium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In the second stage, acidogens breakdown dissolved organic pollutants in wastewater to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatty_acids" title="Fatty acids">fatty acids</a>. In the third stage, acetogens convert fatty acids to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acetates" title="Acetates">acetates</a>. In the final stage, methanogens metabolize acetates to gaseous <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>. The byproduct methane leaves the aqueous layer and serves as an energy source to power wastewater-processing within the digestor, thus generating a self-sustaining mechanism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Methanogens also effectively decrease the concentration of organic matter in wastewater run-off and minimizes <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-doi_10.1016/j.procbio.2010.05.017_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-doi_10.1016/j.procbio.2010.05.017-30">[30]</a></sup> For instance, agricultural wastewater, highly rich in organic material, has been a major cause of aquatic ecosystem degradation. The chemical imbalances can lead to severe ramifications such as <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">eutrophication</a>. Through anaerobic digestion, the purification of wastewater can prevent unexpected blooms in water systems as well as trap <a href="/wiki/Methanogenesis" title="Methanogenesis">methanogenesis</a> within digesters. This allocates biomethane for energy production and prevents a potent greenhouse gas, methane, from being released into the atmosphere.
</p><p>The organic components of wastewater vary vastly. Chemical structures of the organic matter select for specific methanogens to perform anaerobic digestion. An example is the members of <a href="/wiki/Methanosaeta" title="Methanosaeta">Methanosaeta</a> genus dominate the digestion of palm oil mill effluent (POME) and brewery waste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-doi_10.1016/j.procbio.2010.05.017_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-doi_10.1016/j.procbio.2010.05.017-30">[30]</a></sup> Modernizing wastewater treatment systems to incorporate higher diversity of microorganisms to decrease organic content in treatment is under active research in the field of microbiological and chemical engineering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Current new generations of Staged Multi-Phase Anaerobic reactors and Upflow Sludge Bed reactor systems are designed to have innovated features to counter high loading wastewater input, extreme temperature conditions, and possible inhibitory compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
