<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Combustibility and flammability</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Flammable</b> materials are those that ignite more easily than other materials, whereas those that are harder to ignite or burn less vigorously are <b>combustible</b>.
</p><p>The degree of flammability or combustibility in air depends largely upon the chemical composition of the subject material, as well as the ratio of mass versus surface area. Take wood as an example. Finely divided wood dust can undergo <a href="/wiki/Dust_explosion" title="Dust explosion">explosive combustion</a> and produce a blast wave.  A piece of paper (made from wood) catches on fire quite easily. A heavy oak desk is much harder to ignite, even though the wood fibre is the same in all three materials. 
</p><p>Common sense (and indeed <a href="/wiki/Phlogiston_theory" title="Phlogiston theory">scientific consensus</a> until the mid-1700s) would seem to suggest that material "disappears" when burned, as only the ash is left. In fact, there is an increase in weight because the combustible material reacts (or combines) chemically with oxygen, which also has mass. The original mass of combustible material and the mass of the oxygen required for combustion equals the mass of the combustion products (ash, water, carbon dioxide, and other gases).  <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Lavoisier" title="Antoine Lavoisier">Antoine Lavoisier</a>, one of the pioneers in these early insights, stated that <i>Nothing is lost, nothing is created, everything is transformed</i>, which would later be known as the law of <a href="/wiki/Conservation_of_mass" title="Conservation of mass">conservation of mass</a>.  Lavoisier used the experimental fact that some metals gained mass when they burned to support his ideas.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combustibility_and_flammability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Historically, <i>flammable</i>, <i>inflammable</i> and <i>combustible</i> meant <i>capable of burning</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The word "inflammable" came through French from the Latin <i>inflammāre</i> = "to set fire to," where the Latin preposition "in-"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> means "in" as in "indoctrinate", rather than "not" as in "invisible" and "ineligible".
</p><p>The word "inflammable" may be erroneously thought to mean "non-flammable".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The erroneous usage of the word "inflammable" is a significant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Safety_hazard" title="Safety hazard">safety hazard</a>. Therefore, since the 1950s, efforts to put forward the use of "flammable" in place of "inflammable" were accepted by linguists, and it is now the accepted standard in American English and British English.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>  Antonyms of "flammable/inflammable" include: <i>non-flammable</i>, <i>non-inflammable</i>, <i>incombustible</i>, <i>non-combustible</i>, <i>not flammable</i>, and <i>fireproof</i>.
</p><p><i>Flammable</i> applies to materials that ignite more easily than other materials, and thus are more dangerous and more highly regulated. Less easily ignited less-vigorously burning materials are <i>combustible</i>. For example, in the United States <a href="/wiki/Flammable_liquid" title="Flammable liquid">flammable liquids</a>, by definition, have a <a href="/wiki/Flash_point" title="Flash point">flash point</a> below 100 °F (38 °C)—where combustible liquids have a flash point above 100 °F (38 °C). <a href="/wiki/HAZMAT_Class_4_Flammable_solids" title="HAZMAT Class 4 Flammable solids">Flammable solids</a> are solids that are readily combustible, or may cause or contribute to fire through friction. Readily combustible solids are powdered, granular, or pasty substances that easily ignite by brief contact with an ignition source, such as a burning match, and spread flame rapidly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OSHA_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-OSHA-6">[6]</a></sup> The technical definitions vary between countries so the United Nations created the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Globally_Harmonized_System_of_Classification_and_Labeling_of_Chemicals" title="Globally Harmonized System of Classification and Labeling of Chemicals">Globally Harmonized System of Classification and Labeling of Chemicals</a>, which defines the flash point temperature of flammable liquids as between 0 and 140 °F (60 °C) and combustible liquids between 140 °F (60 °C) and 200 °F (93 °C).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OSHA_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-OSHA-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flammability">Flammability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combustibility_and_flammability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Flammability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Flammability</b> is the ability of a substance to burn or ignite, causing <a href="/wiki/Fire" title="Fire">fire</a> or <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a>. The degree of difficulty required to cause the combustion of a substance is quantified through <a href="/wiki/Fire_test" title="Fire test">fire testing</a>. Internationally, a variety of test protocols exist to quantify flammability. The ratings achieved are used in <a href="/wiki/Building_code" title="Building code">building codes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a> requirements, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fire_code" title="Fire code">fire codes</a> and other regulations governing the use of building materials as well as the storage and handling of highly flammable substances inside and outside of structures and in surface and air transportation. For instance, changing an <a href="/wiki/Occupancy" title="Occupancy">occupancy</a> by altering the flammability of the contents requires the owner of a <a href="/wiki/Building" title="Building">building</a> to apply for a building permit to make sure that the overall <a href="/wiki/Fire_protection" title="Fire protection">fire protection</a> design basis of the facility can take the change into account.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Testing">Testing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combustibility_and_flammability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Testing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Fire_test" title="Fire test">fire test</a> can be conducted to determine the degree of flammability. Test standards used to make this determination but are not limited to the following:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Underwriters_Laboratories" title="Underwriters Laboratories">Underwriters Laboratories</a> <a class="external text" href="http://ulstandards.ul.com/standard/?id=94&amp;edition=6&amp;doctype=ulstd" rel="nofollow">UL 94 Flammability Testing</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/International_Electrotechnical_Commission" title="International Electrotechnical Commission">International Electrotechnical Commission</a> IEC 60707, 60695-11-10 and 60695-11-20</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">International Organization for Standardization</a> ISO 9772 and 9773.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/National_Fire_Protection_Association" title="National Fire Protection Association">National Fire Protection Association</a> <a class="external text" href="http://www.nfpa.org/aboutthecodes/AboutTheCodes.asp?DocNum=287" rel="nofollow">NFPA 287 Standard Test Methods for Measurement of Flammability of Materials in Cleanrooms Using a Fire Propagation Apparatus (FPA)</a></li>
<li><a class="external text" href="http://www.nfpa.org/aboutthecodes/AboutTheCodes.asp?DocNum=701" rel="nofollow">NFPA 701: Standard Methods of Fire Tests for Flame Propagation of Textiles and Films</a></li>
<li><a class="external text" href="http://www.nfpa.org/aboutthecodes/AboutTheCodes.asp?DocNum=850" rel="nofollow">NFPA 850: Recommended Practice for Fire Protection for Electric Generating Plants and High Voltage Direct Current Converter Stations</a></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Furniture_flammability">Furniture flammability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combustibility_and_flammability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Furniture flammability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Flammability of furniture is of concern as cigarettes and candle accidents can trigger domestic fires. In 1975, California began implementing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Technical_Bulletin_117" title="Technical Bulletin 117">Technical Bulletin 117</a> (TB 117), which required that materials such as <a href="/wiki/Polyurethane" title="Polyurethane">polyurethane</a> foam used to fill furniture be able to withstand a small open flame, equivalent to a candle, for at least 12 seconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TB117_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-TB117-7">[7]</a></sup> In polyurethane foam, furniture manufacturers typically meet TB 117 with additive halogenated organic <a href="/wiki/Flame_retardant" title="Flame retardant">flame retardants</a>. No other U.S. states had similar standards, but because California has such a large market, manufacturers meet TB 117 in products that they distribute across the United States. The proliferation of flame retardants, and especially halogenated organic flame retardants, in furniture across the United States is strongly linked to TB 117. When it became apparent that the risk-benefit ratio of this approach was unfavorable and industry had used falsified documentation (i.e. see <a href="/wiki/David_Heimbach" title="David Heimbach">David Heimbach</a>) for the use of flame retardants, California modified TB 117 to require that fabric covering upholstered furniture meet a smolder test replacing the open flame test.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>  Gov. <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Brown" title="Jerry Brown">Jerry Brown</a> signed the modified TB117-2013, which became effective in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gov.brown_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-gov.brown-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Flammability_2">Flammability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combustibility_and_flammability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Flammability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>For existing buildings, fire codes focus on maintaining the <a href="/wiki/Occupancy" title="Occupancy">occupancies</a> as originally intended. In other words, if a portion of a building were designed as an <a href="/wiki/Apartment" title="Apartment">apartment</a>, one could not suddenly load it with flammable liquids and turn it into a gas storage facility, because the fire load and smoke development in that one apartment would be so immense as to overtax the <a href="/wiki/Active_fire_protection" title="Active fire protection">active fire protection</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Passive_fire_protection" title="Passive fire protection">passive fire protection</a> means for the building. The handling and use of flammable substances inside a building is subject to the local fire code, which is ordinarily enforced by the local fire prevention officer.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Combustibility">Combustibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combustibility_and_flammability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Combustibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Combustibility</b> is a measure of how easily a substance bursts into flame, through <a href="/wiki/Fire" title="Fire">fire</a> or <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a>.  This is an important property to consider when a substance is used for construction or is being stored.  It is also important in processes that produce combustible substances as a by-product. Special precautions are usually required for substances that are easily combustible.  These measures may include installation of <a href="/wiki/Fire_sprinkler" title="Fire sprinkler">fire sprinklers</a> or storage remote from possible sources of ignition.
</p><p>Substances with low combustibility may be selected for construction where the fire risk must be reduced, such as apartment buildings, houses, or offices. If combustible resources are used there is greater chance of fire accidents and deaths.  <a href="/wiki/Fire-resistance_rating" title="Fire-resistance rating">Fire resistant</a> substances are preferred for building materials and furnishings.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Code_Definitions">Code Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combustibility_and_flammability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Code Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Authority_Having_Jurisdiction" title="Authority Having Jurisdiction">Authority Having Jurisdiction</a>, combustibility is defined by the local code. In the <a href="/wiki/National_Building_Code_of_Canada" title="National Building Code of Canada">National Building Code of Canada</a>, it is defined as follows:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Combustible: A material that fails to meet acceptance criteria of <a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20061003193651/http://www.ulc.ca/About_ulc/Publication_Notice_Detail.asp?PN_ID=31" rel="nofollow">CAN/ULC-S114, Standard Method of Test for Determination of Noncombustibility in Building Materials</a>.</i></li></ul>
<dl><dd>This leads to the definition of <b>noncombustible</b>:</dd></dl>
<ul><li><i>Non-combustible: means that a material meets the acceptance criteria of <a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20061003193651/http://www.ulc.ca/About_ulc/Publication_Notice_Detail.asp?PN_ID=31" rel="nofollow">CAN4-S114, "Standard Method of Test for Determination of Non-Combustibility in Building Materials</a>".</i></li></ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/British_Standards" title="British Standards">BS</a> 476-4:1970 defines a test for combusibility in which a technician heats three specimens of a material in a furnace. Non-combustibile materials are those for which none of the three specimens either:
</p>
<ul><li>Makes the temperature reading from either of two thermocouples rise by 50 degrees Celsius or more above the initial furnace temperature</li>
<li>Flame continuously for 10 seconds or more inside the furnace</li></ul>
<p>Otherwise, the material is classified as combustible.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fire_testing">Fire testing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combustibility_and_flammability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Fire testing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fire_test" title="Fire test">Fire test</a></div>
<p>Various countries have tests for determining noncombustibility of materials. Most involve the heating of a specified quantity of the test specimen for a set duration. Usually, the material cannot support combustion and must not undergo a certain loss of mass. As a rule of thumb, concrete, steel, ceramics, in other words inorganic substances pass these tests, so building codes list them as suitable and sometimes even mandate them for use in certain applications. In <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, for instance, <a href="/wiki/Firewall_(construction)" title="Firewall (construction)">firewalls</a> must be made of <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Combustible_dust">Combustible dust</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combustibility_and_flammability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Combustible dust">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dust_explosion" title="Dust explosion">Dust explosion</a></div>
<p>A number of industrial processes produce combustible dust as a by-product. The most common being <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wood_dust" title="Wood dust">wood dust</a>. Combustible dust has been defined as: <i>a solid material composed of distinct particles or pieces, regardless of size, shape, or chemical composition, which presents a fire or deflagration hazard when suspended in air or some other oxidizing medium over a range of concentrations.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OSHA-2009_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-OSHA-2009-11">[11]</a></sup> In addition to wood, combustible dusts include metals, especially magnesium, titanium and aluminum, as well as other carbon-based dusts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OSHA-2009_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-OSHA-2009-11">[11]</a></sup> There are at least a 140 known substances that produce combustible dust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSBFinalReport_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-CSBFinalReport-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>38</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> While the particles in a combustible dusts may be of any size, normally they have a diameter of less than 420 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%9Cm" title="Μm">µm</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OSHA-2009_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-OSHA-2009-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[note 1]</a></sup>  As of  2012<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Combustibility_and_flammability&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="United States Occupational Safety and Health Administration">United States Occupational Safety and Health Administration</a> has yet to adopt a comprehensive set of rules on combustible dust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>When suspended in air (or any oxidizing environment), the fine particles of combustible dust present a potential for explosions. Accumulated dust, even when not suspended in air, remains a fire hazard. The National Fire Protection Association (U.S.) specifically addresses the prevention of fires and dust explosions in agricultural and food products facilities in NFPA Code section 61,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> and other industries in NFPA Code sections 651–664.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[note 2]</a></sup>  Collectors designed to reduce airborne dust account for more than 40 percent of all dust explosions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[16]</a></sup> Other important processes are grinding and pulverizing, transporting powders, filing silos and containers (which produces powder), and the mixing and blending of powders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Investigation of 200 dust explosions and fires, between 1980 to 2005, indicated <i>approximately 100 fatalities and 600 injuries.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSBFinalReport_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-CSBFinalReport-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>105–106</span></sup> In January 2003, a polyethylene powder explosion and fire at the West Pharmaceutical Services plant in <a href="/wiki/Kinston,_North_Carolina" title="Kinston, North Carolina">Kinston, North Carolina</a> resulted in the deaths of six workers and injuries to 38 others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSBFinalReport_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-CSBFinalReport-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>104</span></sup> In February 2008 an <a href="/wiki/2008_Georgia_sugar_refinery_explosion" title="2008 Georgia sugar refinery explosion">explosion of sugar dust</a> rocked the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Sugar" title="Imperial Sugar">Imperial Sugar Company's</a> plant at <a href="/wiki/Port_Wentworth,_Georgia" title="Port Wentworth, Georgia">Port Wentworth, Georgia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[18]</a></sup> resulting in thirteen deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[19]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>A more recent industrial standard is the European EN 13501-1 - Fire classification of construction products and building elements - which roughly replaces A2 with A2/B, B1 with C, B2 with D/E and B3 with F.
</p><p>B3 or F rated materials may not be used in building unless combined with another material that reduces the flammability of those materials.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Important_characteristics">Important characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combustibility_and_flammability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Important characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flash_point">Flash point</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combustibility_and_flammability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Flash point">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A material's <a href="/wiki/Flash_point" title="Flash point">flash point</a> is a metric of how easy it is to ignite the vapor of the material as it evaporates into the atmosphere.  A lower flash point indicates higher flammability.  Materials with flash points below 100 <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">°F</a> (38 <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">°C</a>) are regulated in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> by <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">OSHA</a> as potential workplace <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hazards" title="Hazards">hazards</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vapor_pressure">Vapor pressure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Combustibility_and_flammability&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Vapor pressure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Vapor_pressure" title="Vapor pressure">vapor pressure</a> of a liquid, which varies with its temperature, is a measure of how much the vapor of the liquid tends to concentrate in the surrounding atmosphere as the liquid evaporates.  Vapor pressure is a major determinant of the flash point, with higher vapor pressures leading to lower flash points and higher flammability.</li></ul>
