<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Carbonation</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about carbonation reactions in chemistry. For carbonated beverages, see <a href="/wiki/Soft_drink#Carbonated_drinks" title="Soft drink">Soft drink § Carbonated drinks</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carbonated_water" title="Carbonated water">Carbonated water</a>.</div>
<p><b>Carbonation</b> refers to reactions of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> to give <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonates</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>  In <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, the term is sometimes used in place of <a href="/wiki/Carboxylation" title="Carboxylation">carboxylation</a>, which refers to the formation of <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxylic acids</a>. 
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_chemistry" title="Inorganic chemistry">inorganic chemistry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, carbonation is common. <a href="/wiki/Metal_hydroxide" title="Metal hydroxide">Metal hydroxides</a> (MOH) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal_oxide" title="Metal oxide">metal oxides</a> (M'O) react with CO<sub>2</sub> to give <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonates</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>MOH  +  CO<sub>2</sub>  →   M(HCO<sub>3</sub>)</dd>
<dd>M'O  +  CO<sub>2</sub>  →   M'CO<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Reinforced_concrete" title="Reinforced concrete">reinforced concrete</a> construction, the chemical reaction between <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> in the air and <a href="/wiki/Calcium_hydroxide" title="Calcium hydroxide">calcium hydroxide</a> and hydrated <a href="/wiki/Calcium_silicate" title="Calcium silicate">calcium silicate</a> in the concrete is known as <b>neutralisation</b>. 
</p>
<h2><span id="Henry.27s_Law"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Henry's_Law">Henry's Law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Henry's Law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Henry's Law states that P<sub>CO2</sub>=K<sub>B</sub>x<sub>CO2</sub> Where P<sub>CO2</sub> is the partial pressure of CO2 gas above the solution. K<sub>B</sub> is Henry's Law Constant. K<sub>B</sub> increases as temperature increases. x<sub>CO2</sub> is the mole fraction of CO2 gas in the solution. According to Henry's Law carbonation increases in a solution as temperature decreases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
