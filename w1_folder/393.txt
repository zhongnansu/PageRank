<p><b>Pteropoda</b> (<a href="/wiki/Common_name" title="Common name">common name</a> <b>pteropods</b>, from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" title="Ancient Greek language">Greek</a> meaning "wing-foot") are specialized free-swimming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelagic" title="Pelagic">pelagic</a> sea snails and sea slugs, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_(ocean)" title="Marine (ocean)">marine</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opisthobranch" title="Opisthobranch">opisthobranch</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gastropod" title="Gastropod">gastropods</a>.  The <a href="/wiki/Monophyly" title="Monophyly">monophyly</a> of Pteropoda is the subject of a lengthy debate; they have even been considered as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paraphyletic" title="Paraphyletic">paraphyletic</a> with respect to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cephalopods" title="Cephalopods">cephalopods</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>  Current consensus, guided by molecular studies, leans towards interpreting the group as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monophyletic" title="Monophyletic">monophyletic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Pteropoda encompasses the two <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clades</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thecosomata" title="Thecosomata">Thecosomata</a>, the sea butterflies, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gymnosomata" title="Gymnosomata">Gymnosomata</a>, the sea angels. The Thecosomata (<abbr title="literally">lit.</abbr> "case-body"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>) have a shell, while the Gymnosomata  ("naked body") do not. The two clades may or may not be sister taxa; if not, their similarity (in that they are both pelagic, small, and transparent, and both groups swim using wing-like flaps (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parapodia" title="Parapodia">parapodia</a>) which protrude from their bodies) may reflect adaptation to their particular lifestyle.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pteropoda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The group Pteropoda was established by <a href="/wiki/Georges_Cuvier" title="Georges Cuvier">Georges Cuvier</a> as "<i><i lang="fr" title="French language text">ptéropodes</i></i>" in 1804.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_P%C3%A9ron" title="François Péron">François Péron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Alexandre_Lesueur" title="Charles Alexandre Lesueur">Charles Alexandre Lesueur</a> thought the group to be larger, and so they also included the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opisthobranch" title="Opisthobranch">opisthobranch</a> taxa (<i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Phyllirho%C3%AB&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Phyllirhoë (page does not exist)">Phyllirhoë</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Glaucus_(gastropod)" title="Glaucus (gastropod)">Glaucus</a></i>), the <a href="/wiki/Pterotracheoidea" title="Pterotracheoidea">heteropoda</a> taxa (<i><a href="/wiki/Carinaria" title="Carinaria">Carinaria</a></i> and <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Firola&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Firola (page does not exist)">Firola</a></i>), and even the <a href="/wiki/Ctenophora" title="Ctenophora">Ctenophora</a> (<i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Callianira_(ctenophore)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Callianira (ctenophore) (page does not exist)">Callianira</a></i>). In 1810 these authors divided the whole group in two separate groups: those with a shell and those without a shell.
</p><p>In 1824 <a href="/wiki/Henri_Marie_Ducrotay_de_Blainville" title="Henri Marie Ducrotay de Blainville">Henri Marie Ducrotay de Blainville</a> named these two groups Gymnosomata and Thecosomata and named the combining order Aporobranchia instead  of Pteropoda.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He rejected the additional genera, except <i>Phyllirhoë</i> which he upgraded to a third group that he called Psilosomata. Only much later was Phyllirhoë classified within the order <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nudibranchia" title="Nudibranchia">Nudibranchia</a>.
</p><p>Other attempts were made to describe the Pteropoda. <a href="/wiki/John_Edward_Gray" title="John Edward Gray">John Edward Gray</a> divided the Pteropoda into Dactylobranchia (with just the genus <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cavolinia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cavolinia (page does not exist)">Cavolinia</a></i>) and Pterobranchia (including all the other genera).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Cuvier (and his followers) did not accept the classification by de Blainville; they preferred the original classification as described in <i><i lang="fr" title="French language text"><a href="/wiki/Le_R%C3%A8gne_Animal" title="Le Règne Animal">Le Règne Animal</a></i></i>.
</p><p>In 1829 <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Rang&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Paul Rang (page does not exist)">Paul Rang</a> followed the Cuvierian classification, but tried to include the character of having a distinct head or not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The German naturalist <a href="/wiki/Lorenz_Oken" title="Lorenz Oken">Lorenz Oken</a> went one step further and, for the  sake of symmetry, wanted each order to contain four families and  each family to contain four genera.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Andr%C3%A9_Latreille" title="Pierre André Latreille">Pierre André Latreille</a> divided the Pteropoda according to the size of their fins: "<i><i lang="fr" title="French language text">Macroptérygiens</i></i>" (including only Pneumonoderma) and "<i><i lang="fr" title="French language text">Microptérygiens</i></i>" (including all the others). In 1851 <a href="/wiki/William_Bullock_Clark" title="William Bullock Clark">William Bullock Clark</a> treated the Pteropoda as a family and emended the spelling to Pteropodidae (a name now used for a family of <a href="/wiki/Megabat" title="Megabat">fruit bats</a>)
</p><p>Finally all these attempts were abandoned and, as more and more species were described as a result of several scientific expeditions, the classification of the Pteropoda into Thecosomata and Gymnosomata was generally adopted. Many of these new species were first described by French zoologists, for example <a href="/wiki/Jean_Ren%C3%A9_Constant_Quoy" title="Jean René Constant Quoy">Jean René Constant Quoy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Paul_Gaimard" title="Joseph Paul Gaimard">Joseph Paul Gaimard</a>, Paul Rang, <a href="/wiki/Alcide_d%27Orbigny" title="Alcide d'Orbigny">Alcide d'Orbigny</a> and <a href="/wiki/Louis_Fran%C3%A7ois_Auguste_Souleyet" title="Louis François Auguste Souleyet">Louis François Auguste Souleyet</a>.
</p><p>The relationship between these two clades is not unequivocally established, but it seems that they are sister taxa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pteropoda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pteropods are found in all <a href="/wiki/Borders_of_the_oceans" title="Borders of the oceans">major oceans</a>, usually 0–10 meters below the surface (0 ft - 32.8084 ft) below the ocean surface and in all levels of latitude. Pteropods can be found lower than 10 meters, but in less amounts in terms of biomass, however pteropod distribution is more spread out deeper based on findings. This can be explained as Pteropods from tropical areas become more common in deeper areas. They are not found commonly in the deep sea, in fact, few live lower than 500 meters below sea level. <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">Continental shelves</a>, areas containing many opportunities for nutrients, and productivity are locations in which Pteropods are usually populous, according to patterns in data. Springtime is a peak season for pteropoda, as they reach higher populations, though data shows that pteropoda south of the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> are less abundant seasonally. In addition, current data suggests that, 93% of the world's pteropods are part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thecosomata" title="Thecosomata">Thecosomata</a> family, while the 7% are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gymnosomata" title="Gymnosomata">Gymnosomata</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Resistance_to_ocean_acidification">Resistance to ocean acidification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pteropoda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Resistance to ocean acidification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A study was conducted on the West Coast of the United States to see ocean acidification’s effects on pteropods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">[10]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Limacina_helicina" title="Limacina helicina">Limacina helicina</a></i> was used to test the sensitivity to decreasing pH.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">[10]</a></sup> This species of pteropod is potentially vulnerable to the corrosive waters associated with ocean acidification due to their calcium carbonate shell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The shell of a pteropod was immersed in ocean water with the projected pH level that the water will reach by the year 2100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">[10]</a></sup> After a month and a half in the water, the shell had almost completely dissolved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
