<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Amine gas treating</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Amine gas treating</b>, also known as <b>amine scrubbing</b>, <b>gas sweetening</b> and <b>acid gas removal</b>, refers to a group of processes that use aqueous solutions of various <a href="/wiki/Amine#Aliphatic_amines" title="Amine">alkylamines</a> (commonly referred to simply as <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amines</a>) to remove <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a> (H<sub>2</sub>S) and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) from gases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kohl_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kohl-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gary_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gary-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>   It is a common <a href="/wiki/Unit_process" title="Unit process">unit process</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">refineries</a>, and is also used in <a href="/wiki/Petrochemical" title="Petrochemical">petrochemical</a> plants, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_gas_processing" title="Natural gas processing">natural gas processing plants</a> and other industries.
</p><p>Processes within oil refineries or chemical processing plants that remove hydrogen sulfide are referred to as "sweetening" processes because the odor of the processed products is improved by the absence of hydrogen sulfide.  An alternative to the use of amines involves <a href="/wiki/Membrane_technology" title="Membrane technology">membrane technology</a>. However, membrane separation is less attractive due to the relatively high capital and operating costs as well as other technical factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many different amines are used in gas treating:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Diethanolamine" title="Diethanolamine">Diethanolamine</a> (DEA)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ethanolamine" title="Ethanolamine">Monoethanolamine</a> (MEA)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Methyl_diethanolamine" title="Methyl diethanolamine">Methyldiethanolamine</a> (MDEA)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Diisopropanolamine" title="Diisopropanolamine">Diisopropanolamine</a> (DIPA)</li>
<li>Aminoethoxyethanol (Diglycolamine) (DGA)</li></ul>
<p>The most commonly used amines in industrial plants are the alkanolamines DEA, MEA, and MDEA.  These amines are also used in many oil refineries to remove <a href="/wiki/Sour_gas" title="Sour gas">sour gases</a> from liquid hydrocarbons such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquified_petroleum_gas" title="Liquified petroleum gas">liquified petroleum gas</a> (LPG).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description_of_a_typical_amine_treater">Description of a typical amine treater</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amine_gas_treating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description of a typical amine treater">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gases containing H<sub>2</sub>S or both H<sub>2</sub>S and CO<sub>2</sub> are commonly referred to as <i><a href="/wiki/Sour_gas" title="Sour gas">sour gases</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Acid_gas" title="Acid gas">acid gases</a></i> in the <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a> processing industries.
</p><p>The chemistry involved in the amine treating of such gases varies somewhat with the particular amine being used. For one of the more common amines, monoethanolamine (MEA) denoted as <b>RNH<sub>2</sub></b>, the chemistry may be expressed as:
</p>
<dl><dd>RNH<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>S ⇌ RNH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span> + SH<sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<p>A typical amine gas treating process (the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Girbotol_process&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Girbotol process (page does not exist)">Girbotol process</a>, as shown in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Process_Flow_diagram" title="Process Flow diagram">flow diagram</a> below) includes an absorber unit and a regenerator unit as well as accessory equipment.  In the absorber, the downflowing amine solution absorbs H<sub>2</sub>S and CO<sub>2</sub> from the upflowing sour gas to produce a sweetened gas stream (i.e., a gas free of hydrogen sulfide and carbon dioxide) as a product and an amine solution rich in the absorbed acid gases.  The resultant "rich" amine is then routed into the regenerator (a stripper with a <a href="/wiki/Reboiler" title="Reboiler">reboiler</a>) to produce regenerated or "lean" amine that is recycled for reuse in the absorber. The stripped <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Overhead_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Overhead gas (page does not exist)">overhead gas</a> from the regenerator is concentrated H<sub>2</sub>S and CO<sub>2</sub>.
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:554px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AmineTreating.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="506" data-file-width="552" height="506" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/AmineTreating.svg/552px-AmineTreating.svg.png" width="552"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><a href="/wiki/Process_flow_diagram" title="Process flow diagram">Process flow diagram</a> of a typical amine treating process used in petroleum refineries, natural gas processing plants and other industrial facilities.</div></div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_processes">Alternative processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amine_gas_treating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Alternative processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Alternative stripper configurations include matrix, internal exchange, flashing feed, and multipressure with split feed. Many of these configurations offer more energy efficiency for specific solvents or operating conditions. Vacuum operation favors solvents with low heats of absorption while operation at normal pressure favors solvents with high heats of absorption. Solvents with high heats of absorption require less energy for stripping from temperature swing at fixed capacity. The matrix stripper recovers 40% of CO<sub>2</sub> at a higher pressure and does not have inefficiencies associated with multipressure stripper. Energy and costs are reduced since the reboiler duty cycle is slightly less than normal pressure stripper. An Internal Exchange stripper has a smaller ratio of water vapor to CO<sub>2</sub> in the overheads stream, and therefore less steam is required. The multipressure configuration with split feed reduces the flow into the bottom section, which also reduces the equivalent work. Flashing feed requires less heat input because it uses the latent heat of water vapor to help strip some of the CO<sub>2</sub> in the rich stream entering the stripper at the bottom of the column. The multipressure configuration is more attractive for solvents with a higher heats of absorption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oyenekan2007_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oyenekan2007-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Amines">Amines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amine_gas_treating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Amines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The amine concentration in the absorbent aqueous solution is an important parameter in the design and operation of an amine gas treating process. Depending on which one of the following four amines the unit was designed to use and what gases it was designed to remove, these are some typical amine concentrations, expressed as weight percent of pure amine in the aqueous solution:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kohl_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kohl-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><ul><li>Monoethanolamine: About 20 % for removing H<sub>2</sub>S and CO<sub>2</sub>, and about 32 % for removing only CO<sub>2</sub>.</li>
<li>Diethanolamine: About 20 to 25 % for removing H<sub>2</sub>S and CO<sub>2</sub></li>
<li>Methyldiethanolamine: About 30 to 55 % for removing H<sub>2</sub>S and CO<sub>2</sub></li>
<li>Diglycolamine: About 50 % for removing H<sub>2</sub>S and CO<sub>2</sub></li></ul></dd></dl>
<p>The choice of amine concentration in the circulating aqueous solution depends upon a number of factors and may be quite arbitrary. It is usually made simply on the basis of experience. The factors involved include whether the amine unit is treating raw <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> or <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_refining_processes" title="Petroleum refining processes">petroleum refinery</a> by-product gases that contain relatively low concentrations of both H<sub>2</sub>S and CO<sub>2</sub> or whether the unit is treating gases with a high percentage of CO<sub>2</sub> such as the offgas from the steam reforming process used in <a href="/wiki/Ammonia_production" title="Ammonia production">ammonia production</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">flue gases</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conventional_coal-fired_power_plant" title="Conventional coal-fired power plant">power plants</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kohl_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kohl-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Both H<sub>2</sub>S and CO<sub>2</sub> are acid gases and hence corrosive to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_steel" title="Carbon steel">carbon steel</a>. However, in an amine treating unit, CO<sub>2</sub> is the stronger acid of the two. H<sub>2</sub>S forms a film of <a href="/wiki/Iron_sulfide" title="Iron sulfide">iron sulfide</a> on the surface of the steel that acts to protect the steel. When treating gases with a high percentage of CO<sub>2</sub>, corrosion inhibitors are often used and that permits the use of higher concentrations of amine in the circulating solution.
</p><p>Another factor involved in choosing an amine concentration is the relative solubility of H<sub>2</sub>S and CO<sub>2</sub> in the selected amine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kohl_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kohl-1">[1]</a></sup> The choice of the type of amine will affect the required circulation rate of amine solution, the energy consumption for the regeneration and the ability to selectively remove either H<sub>2</sub>S alone or CO<sub>2</sub> alone if desired. For more information about selecting the amine concentration, the reader is referred to Kohl and Nielsen's book.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="MEA_and_DEA">MEA and DEA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amine_gas_treating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: MEA and DEA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>MEA and DEA are primary and secondary amines. They are very reactive and can effectively remove a high volume of gas due to a high reaction rate. However, due to stoichiometry, the loading capacity is limited to 0.5 mol CO<sub>2</sub> per mole of amine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Idem2006_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Idem2006-6">[6]</a></sup>  MEA and DEA also require a large amount of energy to strip the CO<sub>2</sub> to during regeneration, which can be up to 70% of total operating costs. They are also more corrosive and chemically unstable compared to other amines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Idem2006_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Idem2006-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amine_gas_treating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In oil refineries, that stripped gas is mostly H<sub>2</sub>S, much of which often comes from a sulfur-removing process called <a href="/wiki/Hydrodesulfurization" title="Hydrodesulfurization">hydrodesulfurization</a>.  This H<sub>2</sub>S-rich stripped gas stream is then usually routed into a <a href="/wiki/Claus_process" title="Claus process">Claus process</a> to convert it into elemental <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>. In fact, the vast majority of the 64,000,000 metric tons of sulfur produced worldwide in 2005 was byproduct sulfur from refineries and other hydrocarbon processing plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>  Another sulfur-removing process is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WSA_Process" title="WSA Process">WSA Process</a> which recovers sulfur in any form as concentrated sulfuric acid. In some plants, more than one amine absorber unit may share a common regenerator unit. 
The current emphasis on removing CO<sub>2</sub> from the flue gases emitted by fossil fuel power plants has led to much interest in using amines for removing CO<sub>2</sub>. (See also: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">Carbon capture and storage</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conventional_coal-fired_power_plant" title="Conventional coal-fired power plant">Conventional coal-fired power plant</a>.)
</p><p>In the specific case of the industrial synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>, for the <a href="/wiki/Steam_reforming" title="Steam reforming">steam reforming</a> process of hydrocarbons to produce gaseous <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, amine treating is one of the commonly used processes for removing excess carbon dioxide in the final purification of the gaseous hydrogen.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">biogas</a> production it is sometimes necessary to remove carbon dioxide from the biogas to make it comparable with the natural. The removal of the sometimes high content of hydrogen sulfide is necessary to prevent corrosion of metallic parts after burning the bio gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AbatzoglouBoivin2009_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-AbatzoglouBoivin2009-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HuertasGiraldo2011_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-HuertasGiraldo2011-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_capture_and_storage">Carbon capture and storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amine_gas_treating&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Carbon capture and storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Amines are used to remove CO<sub>2</sub> in various areas ranging from natural gas production to the food and beverage industry, and have been for over sixty years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rochelle2009_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rochelle2009-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are multiple classifications of amines, each of which has different characteristics relevant to CO<sub>2</sub> capture. For example, Monoethanolamine (MEA) reacts strongly with acid gases like CO<sub>2</sub> and has a fast reaction time and an ability to remove high percentages of CO<sub>2</sub>, even at the low CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations. Typically, Monoethanolamine (MEA) can capture 85% to 90% of the CO<sub>2</sub> from the flue gas of a coal-fired plant, which is one of the most effective solvent to capture CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-folger_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-folger-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Challenges of carbon capture using amine include:
</p>
<ul><li>Low pressure gas increases difficulty of transferring CO<sub>2</sub> from the gas into amine</li>
<li>Oxygen content of the gas can cause amine degradation and acid formation</li>
<li>CO<sub>2</sub> degradation of primary (and secondary) amines</li>
<li>High energy consumption</li>
<li>Very large facilities</li>
<li>Finding suitable location for the removed CO<sub>2</sub><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WuCarroll2011_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-WuCarroll2011-13">[13]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The partial pressure is the driving force to transfer CO<sub>2</sub> into the liquid phase. Under the low pressure, this transfer is hard to achieve without increasing the reboiler’s heat duty, which will result in higher cost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WuCarroll2011_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-WuCarroll2011-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Primary and secondary amines, for example, MEA and DEA, will react with CO<sub>2</sub> and form degradation products. O<sub>2</sub> from the inlet gas will cause degradation as well. The degraded amine is no longer able to capture CO<sub>2</sub>, which decreases the overall carbon capture efficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WuCarroll2011_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-WuCarroll2011-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Currently, variety of amine mixtures are being synthesized and tested to achieve a more desirable set of overall properties for use in CO<sub>2</sub> capture systems. One major focus is on lowering the energy required for solvent regeneration, which has a major impact on process costs. However, there are tradeoffs to consider. For example, the energy required for regeneration is typically related to the driving forces for achieving high capture capacities. Thus, reducing the regeneration energy can lower the driving force and thereby increase the amount of solvent and size of absorber needed to capture a given amount of CO<sub>2</sub>, thus, increasing the capital cost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-folger_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-folger-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
