<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Land use</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Zoning" title="Zoning">zoning</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Indiana_Dunes_Habitat_Fragmentation.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="492" data-file-width="700" height="197" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Indiana_Dunes_Habitat_Fragmentation.jpg/280px-Indiana_Dunes_Habitat_Fragmentation.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Indiana_Dunes_Habitat_Fragmentation.jpg/420px-Indiana_Dunes_Habitat_Fragmentation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Indiana_Dunes_Habitat_Fragmentation.jpg/560px-Indiana_Dunes_Habitat_Fragmentation.jpg 2x" width="280"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Indiana_Dunes_Habitat_Fragmentation.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Habitat fragmented by numerous roads near the <a href="/wiki/Indiana_Dunes_National_Lakeshore" title="Indiana Dunes National Lakeshore">Indiana Dunes National Lakeshore</a></div></div></div>
<p><b>Land use</b> involves the management and modification of <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">natural environment</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wilderness" title="Wilderness">wilderness</a> into <a href="/wiki/Built_environment" title="Built environment">built environment</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Human_settlement" title="Human settlement">settlements</a> and semi-natural habitats such as <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable fields</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pasture" title="Pasture">pastures</a>, and managed <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woods</a>. It also has been defined as "the total of arrangements, activities, and inputs that people undertake in a certain <a href="/wiki/Land_cover" title="Land cover">land cover</a> type."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation">Regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_use&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Europe_land_use_map.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="740" data-file-width="1004" height="295" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Europe_land_use_map.png/400px-Europe_land_use_map.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Europe_land_use_map.png/600px-Europe_land_use_map.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Europe_land_use_map.png/800px-Europe_land_use_map.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Europe_land_use_map.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A land use map of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>—major non-natural land uses include arable farmland (yellow) and pasture (light green).</div></div></div>
<p>Land Use practices vary considerably across the world. The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>' <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">Food and Agriculture Organization</a> Water Development Division explains that "Land use concerns the products and/or benefits obtained from use of the land as well as the land management actions (activities) carried out by humans to produce those products and benefits."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  As of the early 1990s, about 13% of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> was considered arable land, with 26% in pasture, 32% forests and woodland, and 1.5% urban areas.
</p><p>As Albert Guttenberg (1959) wrote many years ago, "'Land use' is a key term in the language of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_planning" title="City planning">city planning</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>  Commonly, political <a href="/wiki/Jurisdiction" title="Jurisdiction">jurisdictions</a> will undertake <a href="/wiki/Land-use_planning" title="Land-use planning">land-use planning</a> and regulate the use of land in an attempt to avoid <a href="/wiki/Land-use_conflict" title="Land-use conflict">land-use conflicts</a>.  Land use plans are implemented through land division and use ordinances and regulations, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zoning_regulations" title="Zoning regulations">zoning regulations</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Management_consulting" title="Management consulting">Management consulting</a> firms and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organizations" title="Non-governmental organizations">non-governmental organizations</a> will frequently seek to influence these regulations before they are codified.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_use&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In colonial America, few regulations existed to control the use of land, due to the seemingly endless amounts of it. As society shifted from rural to urban, public land regulation became important, especially to city governments trying to control industry, commerce, and housing within their boundaries. The first zoning ordinance was passed in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> in 1916,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and, by the 1930s, most states had adopted <a href="/wiki/Zoning" title="Zoning">zoning</a> laws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In the 1970s, concerns about the environment and historic preservation led to further regulation.
</p><p>Today, federal, state, and local governments regulate growth and <a href="/wiki/Land_development" title="Land development">development</a> through <a href="/wiki/Statutory_law" title="Statutory law">statutory law</a>. The majority of controls on land, however, stem from the actions of private developers and individuals. Three typical situations bringing such private entities into the court system are: suits brought by one neighbor against another; suits brought by a public official against a neighboring landowner on behalf of the public; and suits involving individuals who share ownership of a particular parcel of land. In these situations, judicial decisions and enforcement of private land-use arrangements can reinforce public regulation, and achieve forms and levels of control that regulatory zoning cannot.
</p><p>Two major federal laws have been passed in the last half century that limit the use of land significantly. These are the <a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Preservation_Act_of_1966" title="National Historic Preservation Act of 1966">National Historic Preservation Act of 1966</a> (today embodied in 16 U.S.C. 461 et seq.) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Environmental_Policy_Act_of_1969" title="National Environmental Policy Act of 1969">National Environmental Policy Act of 1969</a> (42 U.S.C. 4321 et seq.).
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Agriculture" title="United States Department of Agriculture">US Department of Agriculture</a> has identified six major types of land use in the US. Acreage statistics for each type of land use in the <a href="/wiki/Contiguous_United_States" title="Contiguous United States">contiguous 48 states</a> in 2017 were as follows:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Pasture" title="Pasture">Pasture</a>/<a href="/wiki/Rangeland" title="Rangeland">range</a>: 654 M</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">Forest</a>: 538.6 M</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Agricultural_land" title="Agricultural land">Cropland</a>: 391.5 M</li>
<li>Special use: 168.8 M</li>
<li>Miscellaneous: 68.9 M</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">Urban</a>: 69.4 M <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environment">Environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_use&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Land use and <a href="/wiki/Land_management" title="Land management">land management</a> practices have a major impact on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_resources" title="Natural resources">natural resources</a> including <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrients</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Land use information can be used to develop solutions for natural resource management issues such as salinity and <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">water quality</a>. For instance, water bodies in a region that has been <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deforested" title="Deforested">deforested</a> or having erosion will have different water quality than those in areas that are <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forested</a>. <a href="/wiki/Forest_gardening" title="Forest gardening">Forest gardening</a>, a plant-based food production system, is believed to be the oldest form of land use in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The major effect of land use on <a href="/wiki/Land_cover" title="Land cover">land cover</a> since 1750 has been <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate_region" title="Temperate region">temperate regions</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title=" Dead link since February 2010">dead link</span></a></i>]</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> More recent significant effects of land use include <a href="/wiki/Urban_sprawl" title="Urban sprawl">urban sprawl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion" title="Soil erosion">soil erosion</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soil_degradation" title="Soil degradation">soil degradation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soil_salinity" title="Soil salinity">salinization</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">desertification</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Land-use change, together with use of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>, are the major <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a> sources of carbon dioxide, a dominant <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to a report by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organisation" title="Food and Agriculture Organisation">Food and Agriculture Organisation</a>, land degradation has been exacerbated where there has been an absence of any land use planning, or of its orderly execution, or the existence of financial or legal incentives that have led to the wrong land use decisions, or one-sided central planning leading to over-utilization of the land resources - for instance for immediate production at all costs. As a consequence the result has often been misery for large segments of the local population and destruction of valuable <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>. Such narrow approaches should be replaced by a technique for the planning and management of land resources that is integrated and holistic and where land users are central. This will ensure the long-term quality of the land for human use, the prevention or resolution of social conflicts related to land use, and the conservation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecosystems" title="Ecosystems">ecosystems</a> of high <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> value.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_growth_boundaries">Urban growth boundaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_use&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Urban growth boundaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Urban_growth_boundary" title="Urban growth boundary">urban growth boundary</a> is one form of land-use regulation. For example, <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Oregon" title="Portland, Oregon">Portland, Oregon</a> is required to have an urban growth boundary which contains at least 20,000 acres (81 km<sup>2</sup>) of vacant land. Additionally, Oregon restricts the development of farmland. The regulations are controversial, but an economic analysis concluded that farmland appreciated similarly to the other land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
