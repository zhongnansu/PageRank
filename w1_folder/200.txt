<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Carbon–oxygen bond</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>carbon–oxygen bond</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_covalent_bond" title="Polar covalent bond">polar covalent bond</a> between <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Oxygen has 6 <a href="/wiki/Valence_electron" title="Valence electron">valence electrons</a> and prefers to either share two electrons in bonding with carbon, leaving the 4 nonbonding electrons in 2 <a href="/wiki/Lone_pair" title="Lone pair">lone pairs</a> :O:   or to share two pairs of electrons to form the <a href="/wiki/Carbonyl_group" title="Carbonyl group">carbonyl functional group</a>. =O: Simple representatives of these two bond types are the <sup>_</sup>OH in <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohols</a> such as the ethanol in beverages and fuels and the C=O in <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">ketones</a>  (as well as many other related <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonyl_compound" title="Carbonyl compound">carbonyl compounds</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electronegativities_and_bond_lengths">Electronegativities and bond lengths</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon%E2%80%93oxygen_bond&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Electronegativities and bond lengths">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The C–O bond is strongly polarized towards oxygen (<a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">electronegativity</a> of C <i>vs</i> O, 2.55 <i>vs</i> 3.44). <a href="/wiki/Bond_length" title="Bond length">Bond lengths</a> for paraffinic C–O bonds are in the range of 143 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Picometer" title="Picometer">pm</a> – less than <a href="/wiki/Bond_length" title="Bond length">those</a> of C–N or C–C bonds. Shortened single bonds are found with carboxylic acids (136 pm) due to partial double bond character and elongated bonds are found in <a href="/wiki/Epoxide" title="Epoxide">epoxides</a> (147 pm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crc_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-crc-5">[5]</a></sup> The C–O <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bond_strength" title="Bond strength">bond strength</a> is also larger than C–N or C–C. For example, bond strengths are 91 kilocalories (380 kJ)/mol (at 298 K) in <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>, 87 kilocalories (360 kJ)/mol in <a href="/wiki/Methylamine" title="Methylamine">methylamine</a>, and 88 kilocalories (370 kJ)/mol in <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">ethane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crc_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-crc-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Carbon and oxygen form terminal <a href="/wiki/Double_bond" title="Double bond">double bonds</a> in functional groups collectively known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonyl" title="Carbonyl">carbonyl</a> compounds to which belong such compounds as <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">ketones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">esters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxylic acids</a> and many more. Internal C=O bonds are found in positively charged <a href="/wiki/Oxonium_ion" title="Oxonium ion">oxonium ions</a>.  In <a href="/wiki/Furan" title="Furan">furans</a>, the oxygen atom contributes to pi-electron delocalization via its filled p-orbital and hence furans are <a href="/wiki/Aromaticity" title="Aromaticity">aromatic</a>.  Bond lengths of C=O bonds are around 123 pm in carbonyl compounds. The C=O bond length in carbon dioxide is 116 pm. The C=O bonds in <a href="/wiki/Acyl_halide" title="Acyl halide">acyl halides</a> have partial <a href="/wiki/Triple_bond" title="Triple bond">triple bond</a> character and are subsequently very short: 117 pm. Compounds with formal C–O triple bonds do not exist except for <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>, which has a very short, strong bond (112.8 pm). Such triple bonds have a very high bond energy, even higher than N–N triple bonds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Oxygen can also be trivalent, for example in <a href="/wiki/Triethyloxonium_tetrafluoroborate" title="Triethyloxonium tetrafluoroborate">triethyloxonium tetrafluoroborate</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon%E2%80%93oxygen_bond&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carbon–oxygen bond forming reactions are the <a href="/wiki/Williamson_ether_synthesis" title="Williamson ether synthesis">Williamson ether synthesis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nucleophilic_acyl_substitution" title="Nucleophilic acyl substitution">nucleophilic acyl substitutions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electrophilic_addition" title="Electrophilic addition">electrophilic addition</a> to alkenes. The <a href="/wiki/Patern%C3%B2%E2%80%93B%C3%BCchi_reaction" title="Paternò–Büchi reaction">Paternò–Büchi reaction</a> involves carbonyl compounds.
</p>
