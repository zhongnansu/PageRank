<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Red blood cell</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><table class="infobox" style="width:22em"><tbody><tr><th colspan="2" style="text-align:center;font-size:125%;font-weight:bold;background-color:beige"><i>Red blood cell</i></th></tr><tr><td colspan="2" style="text-align:center">
<a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Redbloodcells.jpg"><img alt="Redbloodcells.jpg" data-file-height="166" data-file-width="144" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/13/Redbloodcells.jpg" width="144"/></a><div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scanning_electron_micrograph" title="Scanning electron micrograph">Scanning electron micrograph</a> of human red blood cells (ca. 6–8 μm in diameter)</div></td></tr><tr><th colspan="2" style="text-align:center;background-color: #efefef">Details</th></tr><tr><th scope="row" style="padding-right:0.25em">Function</th><td>

<p><b>Red blood cells</b>-- also known as <b>RBCs</b>, <b>red cells</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <b>red blood corpuscles</b>, <b>haematids</b>, <b>erythroid cells</b>  or <b>erythrocytes</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>erythros</i> for "red" and <i>kytos</i> for "hollow vessel", with <i>-cyte</i> translated as "cell" in modern usage), are the most common type of <a href="/wiki/Blood_cell" title="Blood cell">blood cell</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrate</a>'s principal means of delivering <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> (O<sub>2</sub>) to the body <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">tissues</a>—via <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> flow through the <a href="/wiki/Circulatory_system" title="Circulatory system">circulatory system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  RBCs take up oxygen in the <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">lungs</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Gill" title="Gill">gills</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, and release it into tissues while squeezing through the body's <a href="/wiki/Capillary" title="Capillary">capillaries</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Cytoplasm" title="Cytoplasm">cytoplasm</a> of erythrocytes is rich in <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>-containing <a href="/wiki/Biomolecule" title="Biomolecule">biomolecule</a> that can bind oxygen and is responsible for the red color of the cells and the blood. The <a href="/wiki/Cell_membrane" title="Cell membrane">cell membrane</a> is composed of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proteins" title="Proteins">proteins</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lipids" title="Lipids">lipids</a>, and this structure provides properties essential for physiological <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cell</a> function such as <a href="/wiki/Erythrocyte_deformability" title="Erythrocyte deformability">deformability</a> and <a href="/wiki/Erythrocyte_fragility" title="Erythrocyte fragility">stability</a> while traversing the circulatory system and specifically the <a href="/wiki/Capillary" title="Capillary">capillary</a> network.
</p><p>In humans, mature red blood cells are flexible and oval biconcave disks. They lack a <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">cell nucleus</a> and most <a href="/wiki/Organelle" title="Organelle">organelles</a>, in order to accommodate maximum space for hemoglobin; they can be viewed as sacks of hemoglobin, with a <a href="/wiki/Lipid_bilayer" title="Lipid bilayer">plasma membrane</a> as the sack. Approximately 2.4 million new erythrocytes are produced per second in human adults.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sackmann_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sackmann-3">[3]</a></sup> The cells develop in the <a href="/wiki/Bone_marrow" title="Bone marrow">bone marrow</a> and circulate for about 100–120 days in the body before their components are recycled by <a href="/wiki/Macrophage" title="Macrophage">macrophages</a>.  Each circulation takes about 60 seconds (one minute).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blom20032_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blom20032-4">[4]</a></sup> Approximately a quarter of the cells in the human body are red blood cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dean_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-dean-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pierige_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-pierige-6">[6]</a></sup> Nearly half of the blood's volume (<a href="/wiki/Hematocrit" title="Hematocrit">40% to 45%</a>) is red blood cells.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Packed_red_blood_cells" title="Packed red blood cells">Packed red blood cells</a> (pRBC) are red blood cells that have been donated, processed, and stored in a <a href="/wiki/Blood_bank" title="Blood bank">blood bank</a> for <a href="/wiki/Blood_transfusion" title="Blood transfusion">blood transfusion</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vertebrates">Vertebrates</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Erythrocytes_in_vertebrates.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="877" data-file-width="497" height="353" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Erythrocytes_in_vertebrates.jpg/200px-Erythrocytes_in_vertebrates.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Erythrocytes_in_vertebrates.jpg/300px-Erythrocytes_in_vertebrates.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Erythrocytes_in_vertebrates.jpg/400px-Erythrocytes_in_vertebrates.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Erythrocytes_in_vertebrates.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>There is an immense size variation in vertebrate red blood cells, as well as a correlation between cell and nucleus size. Mammalian red blood cells, which do not contain nuclei, are considerably smaller than those of most other vertebrates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gulliver1875_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gulliver1875-7">[7]</a></sup></div></div></div><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cytological_abnormalities_in_peripheral_blood_erythrocytes_of_penguins_Pygoscelis_papua_5.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="671" data-file-width="666" height="222" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Cytological_abnormalities_in_peripheral_blood_erythrocytes_of_penguins_Pygoscelis_papua_5.jpg/220px-Cytological_abnormalities_in_peripheral_blood_erythrocytes_of_penguins_Pygoscelis_papua_5.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Cytological_abnormalities_in_peripheral_blood_erythrocytes_of_penguins_Pygoscelis_papua_5.jpg/331px-Cytological_abnormalities_in_peripheral_blood_erythrocytes_of_penguins_Pygoscelis_papua_5.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Cytological_abnormalities_in_peripheral_blood_erythrocytes_of_penguins_Pygoscelis_papua_5.jpg/441px-Cytological_abnormalities_in_peripheral_blood_erythrocytes_of_penguins_Pygoscelis_papua_5.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cytological_abnormalities_in_peripheral_blood_erythrocytes_of_penguins_Pygoscelis_papua_5.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mature red blood cells of birds have a nucleus, however in the blood of adult females of penguin <i><a href="/wiki/Gentoo_penguin" title="Gentoo penguin">Pygoscelis papua</a></i> enucleated red blood cells (<b>B</b>) have been observed, but with very low frequency.</div></div></div><p>Almost all vertebrates, including all mammals and humans, have red blood cells. Red blood cells are cells present in blood in order to transport oxygen. The only known vertebrates without red blood cells are the crocodile icefish (family <a href="/wiki/Channichthyidae" title="Channichthyidae">Channichthyidae</a>); they live in very oxygen-rich cold water and transport oxygen freely dissolved in their blood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> While they no longer use hemoglobin, remnants of hemoglobin genes can be found in their <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Vertebrate red blood cells consist mainly of <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a>, a complex <a href="/wiki/Metalloprotein" title="Metalloprotein">metalloprotein</a> containing <a href="/wiki/Heme" title="Heme">heme</a> groups whose <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> atoms temporarily bind to oxygen molecules (O<sub>2</sub>) in the lungs or gills and release them throughout the body. Oxygen can easily <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">diffuse</a> through the red blood cell's <a href="/wiki/Cell_membrane" title="Cell membrane">cell membrane</a>. Hemoglobin in the red blood cells also carries some of the waste product <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> back from the tissues; most waste carbon dioxide, however, is transported back to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar-capillary_barrier" title="Alveolar-capillary barrier">pulmonary capillaries</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">lungs</a> as <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> (HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>) dissolved in the <a href="/wiki/Blood_plasma" title="Blood plasma">blood plasma</a>. <a href="/wiki/Myoglobin" title="Myoglobin">Myoglobin</a>, a compound related to hemoglobin, acts to store oxygen in <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">muscle</a> cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The color of red blood cells is due to the heme group of hemoglobin. The <a href="/wiki/Blood_plasma" title="Blood plasma">blood plasma</a> alone is straw-colored, but the red blood cells change color depending on the state of the hemoglobin: when combined with oxygen the resulting oxyhemoglobin is scarlet, and when oxygen has been released the resulting deoxyhemoglobin is of a dark red burgundy color. However, blood can appear bluish when seen through the vessel wall and skin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pulse_oximetry" title="Pulse oximetry">Pulse oximetry</a> takes advantage of the hemoglobin color change to directly measure the <a href="/wiki/Artery" title="Artery">arterial</a> blood <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_saturation" title="Oxygen saturation">oxygen saturation</a> using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colorimetric" title="Colorimetric">colorimetric</a> techniques. Hemoglobin also has a very high affinity for <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>, forming carboxyhemoglobin which is a very bright red in color. Flushed, confused patients with a saturation reading of 100% on pulse oximetry are sometimes found to be suffering from carbon monoxide poisoning.
</p><p>Having oxygen-carrying proteins inside specialized cells (as opposed to oxygen carriers being dissolved in body fluid) was an important step in the evolution of vertebrates as it allows for less <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscous</a> blood, higher concentrations of oxygen, and better diffusion of oxygen from the blood to the tissues. The size of red blood cells varies widely among vertebrate species; red blood cell width is on average about 25% larger than <a href="/wiki/Capillary" title="Capillary">capillary</a> diameter, and it has been hypothesized that this improves the oxygen transfer from red blood cells to tissues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snyder_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-snyder-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mammals">Mammals</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gray453.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="305" data-file-width="469" height="195" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Gray453.png/300px-Gray453.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Gray453.png/450px-Gray453.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/89/Gray453.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gray453.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Typical mammalian red blood cells: (a) seen from surface; (b) in profile, forming rouleaux; (c) rendered spherical by water ; (d) rendered crenate (shrunken and spiky) by salt. (c) and (d) do not normally occur in the body. The last two shapes are due to water being transported into, and out of, the cells, by <a href="/wiki/Osmosis" title="Osmosis">osmosis</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The red blood cells of <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammal</a>s are typically shaped as biconcave disks: flattened and depressed in the center, with a dumbbell-shaped cross section, and a <a href="/wiki/Torus" title="Torus">torus</a>-shaped rim on the edge of the disk. This distinctive biconcave shape optimises the flow properties of blood in the large vessels, such as maximization of <a href="/wiki/Laminar_flow" title="Laminar flow">laminar flow</a> and minimization of platelet scatter, which suppresses their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atherogenic" title="Atherogenic">atherogenic</a> activity in those large vessels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Moreover, this shape allows for a high surface-area-to-volume (SA/V) ratio to facilitate diffusion of gases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> However, there are some exceptions concerning shape in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artiodactyl" title="Artiodactyl">artiodactyl</a> order (even-toed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ungulates" title="Ungulates">ungulates</a> including cattle, deer, and their relatives), which displays a wide variety of bizarre red blood cell morphologies: small and highly ovaloid cells in <a href="/wiki/Llama" title="Llama">llamas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Camel" title="Camel">camels</a> (family <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Camelidae" title="Camelidae">Camelidae</a>), tiny spherical cells in mouse deer (family <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tragulidae" title="Tragulidae">Tragulidae</a>), and cells which assume fusiform, lanceolate, crescentic, and irregularly polygonal and other angular forms in red deer and wapiti (family <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cervidae" title="Cervidae">Cervidae</a>). Members of this order have clearly evolved a mode of red blood cell development substantially different from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mammalian" title="Mammalian">mammalian</a> norm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gulliver1875_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gulliver1875-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Overall, mammalian red blood cells are remarkably flexible and deformable so as to squeeze through tiny <a href="/wiki/Capillary" title="Capillary">capillaries</a>, as well as to maximize their apposing surface by assuming a cigar shape, where they efficiently release their oxygen load.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Red blood cells in mammals are unique amongst vertebrates as they do not have nuclei when mature. Red blood cells of mammals cells have nuclei during early phases of <a href="/wiki/Erythropoiesis" title="Erythropoiesis">erythropoiesis</a>, but extrude them during development as they mature in order to provide more space for hemoglobin.  The red blood cells without nuclei, called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reticulocytes" title="Reticulocytes">reticulocytes</a>, go on to lose all other cellular <a href="/wiki/Organelle" title="Organelle">organelles</a> such as their <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrion" title="Mitochondrion">mitochondria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Golgi_apparatus" title="Golgi apparatus">Golgi apparatus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Endoplasmic_reticulum" title="Endoplasmic reticulum">endoplasmic reticulum</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Spleen" title="Spleen">spleen</a> acts as a reservoir of red blood cells, but this effect is somewhat limited in humans. In some other mammals such as <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">dogs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horses</a>, the spleen sequesters large numbers of red blood cells which are dumped into the blood during times of exertion stress, yielding a higher oxygen transport capacity.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Red_White_Blood_cells.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="326" data-file-width="500" height="130" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Red_White_Blood_cells.jpg/200px-Red_White_Blood_cells.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Red_White_Blood_cells.jpg/300px-Red_White_Blood_cells.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Red_White_Blood_cells.jpg/400px-Red_White_Blood_cells.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Red_White_Blood_cells.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Scanning electron micrograph of blood cells. From left to right: human red blood cell, <a href="/wiki/Platelet" title="Platelet">thrombocyte</a> (platelet), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leukocyte" title="Leukocyte">leukocyte</a>.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Human">Human</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NIK_3232-Drops_of_blood_medium.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="317" data-file-width="640" height="99" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/NIK_3232-Drops_of_blood_medium.JPG/200px-NIK_3232-Drops_of_blood_medium.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/NIK_3232-Drops_of_blood_medium.JPG/300px-NIK_3232-Drops_of_blood_medium.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/NIK_3232-Drops_of_blood_medium.JPG/400px-NIK_3232-Drops_of_blood_medium.JPG 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NIK_3232-Drops_of_blood_medium.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Two drops of blood are shown with a bright red oxygenated drop on the left and a deoxygenated drop on the right.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Erytrocyte_deoxy_to_oxy_v0.7.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="240" data-file-width="320" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Erytrocyte_deoxy_to_oxy_v0.7.gif/200px-Erytrocyte_deoxy_to_oxy_v0.7.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Erytrocyte_deoxy_to_oxy_v0.7.gif/300px-Erytrocyte_deoxy_to_oxy_v0.7.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/75/Erytrocyte_deoxy_to_oxy_v0.7.gif 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Erytrocyte_deoxy_to_oxy_v0.7.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Animation of a typical human red blood cell cycle in the circulatory system. This animation occurs at a faster rate (~20 seconds of the average 60-second cycle) and shows the red blood cell deforming as it enters capillaries, as well as the bars changing color as the cell alternates in states of oxygenation along the circulatory system.</div></div></div>
<p>A typical <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> red blood cell has a disk diameter of approximately <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1_E-6_m" title="1 E-6 m">6.2–8.2 µm</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and a thickness at the thickest point of 2–2.5 µm and a minimum thickness in the centre of 0.8–1 µm, being much smaller than most other <a href="/wiki/List_of_distinct_cell_types_in_the_adult_human_body" title="List of distinct cell types in the adult human body">human cells</a>. These cells have an average volume of about <a href="/wiki/Femto-" title="Femto-">90 fL</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> with a surface of about 136 μm<sup>2</sup>, and can swell up to a sphere shape containing 150 fL, without membrane distension.
</p><p>Adult humans have roughly 20–30 trillion red blood cells at any given time, constituting approximately 70% of all cells by number.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Women have about 4–5 million red blood cells per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liter" title="Liter">microliter</a> (cubic millimeter) of blood and men about 5–6 million; <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_high_altitude_on_humans#Acclimatization_to_altitude" title="Effects of high altitude on humans">people living at high altitudes</a> with low oxygen tension will have more. Red blood cells are thus much more common than the other blood particles: there are about 4,000–11,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_blood_cells" title="White blood cells">white blood cells</a> and about 150,000–400,000 <a href="/wiki/Platelet" title="Platelet">platelets</a> per microliter.
</p><p>Human red blood cells take on average 60 seconds to complete one cycle of circulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blom20032_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blom20032-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pierige_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-pierige-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>The blood's red color is due to the spectral properties of the <a href="/wiki/Heme" title="Heme">hemic</a> <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a>. Each human red blood cell contains approximately 270 million<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> of these <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a> molecules. Each hemoglobin molecule carries four heme groups; hemoglobin constitutes about a third of the total cell volume. Hemoglobin is responsible for the transport of more than 98% of the oxygen in the body (the remaining oxygen is carried dissolved in the <a href="/wiki/Blood_plasma" title="Blood plasma">blood plasma</a>). The red blood cells of an average adult human male store collectively about 2.5 grams of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, representing about 65% of the total iron contained in the body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Microstructure">Microstructure</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nucleus">Nucleus</span></h3>
<p>Red blood cells in mammals <i>anucleate</i> when mature, meaning that they lack a <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">cell nucleus</a>. In comparison, the red blood cells of other vertebrates have nuclei; the only known exceptions are <a href="/wiki/Salamander" title="Salamander">salamanders</a> of the genus <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Batrachoseps" title="Batrachoseps">Batrachoseps</a></i> and fish of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Maurolicus" title="Maurolicus">Maurolicus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>The elimination of the nucleus in vertebrate red blood cells has been offered as an explanation for the subsequent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-value_enigma" title="C-value enigma">accumulation of non-coding DNA in the genome</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The argument runs as follows: Efficient gas transport requires red blood cells to pass through very narrow capillaries, and this constrains their size. In the absence of nuclear elimination, the accumulation of repeat sequences is constrained by the volume occupied by the nucleus, which increases with genome size.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Nucleated_red_blood_cell" title="Nucleated red blood cell">Nucleated red blood cells</a> in mammals consist of two forms: normoblasts, which are normal erythropoietic precursors to mature red blood cells, and megaloblasts, which are abnormally large precursors that occur in <a href="/wiki/Megaloblastic_anemia" title="Megaloblastic anemia">megaloblastic anemias</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Membrane_composition">Membrane composition</span></h3>
<p>Red blood cells are deformable, flexible, are able to adhere to other cells, and are able to interface with immune cells. Their <a href="/wiki/Cell_membrane" title="Cell membrane">membrane</a> plays many roles in this. These functions are highly dependent on the membrane composition. The red blood cell membrane is composed of 3 layers: the <a href="/wiki/Glycocalyx" title="Glycocalyx">glycocalyx</a> on the exterior, which is rich in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbohydrates" title="Carbohydrates">carbohydrates</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Lipid_bilayer" title="Lipid bilayer">lipid bilayer</a> which contains many <a href="/wiki/Transmembrane_protein" title="Transmembrane protein">transmembrane proteins</a>, besides its lipidic main constituents; and the membrane skeleton, a structural network of proteins located on the inner surface of the lipid bilayer. Half of the membrane mass in human and most mammalian red blood cells are proteins.  The other half are lipids, namely <a href="/wiki/Phospholipid" title="Phospholipid">phospholipids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cholesterol" title="Cholesterol">cholesterol</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yazdanbakhsh2000_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yazdanbakhsh2000-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Membrane_lipids">Membrane lipids</span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Erythrocyte_Membrane_lipids.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="756" data-file-width="542" height="349" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Erythrocyte_Membrane_lipids.jpg/250px-Erythrocyte_Membrane_lipids.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Erythrocyte_Membrane_lipids.jpg/375px-Erythrocyte_Membrane_lipids.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Erythrocyte_Membrane_lipids.jpg/500px-Erythrocyte_Membrane_lipids.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Erythrocyte_Membrane_lipids.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The most common red blood cell cell membrane lipids, schematically disposed as they are distributed on the bilayer. Relative abundances are not at scale.</div></div></div>
<p>The red blood cell cell membrane comprises a typical <a href="/wiki/Lipid_bilayer" title="Lipid bilayer">lipid bilayer</a>, similar to what can be found in virtually all human cells. Simply put, this lipid bilayer is composed of <a href="/wiki/Cholesterol" title="Cholesterol">cholesterol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phospholipid" title="Phospholipid">phospholipids</a> in equal proportions by weight. The lipid composition is important as it defines many physical properties such as membrane permeability and fluidity. Additionally, the activity of many membrane proteins is regulated by interactions with lipids in the bilayer.
</p><p>Unlike cholesterol, which is evenly distributed between the inner and outer leaflets, the 5 major phospholipids are asymmetrically disposed, as shown below:
</p><p><b>Outer monolayer</b>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Phosphatidylcholine" title="Phosphatidylcholine">Phosphatidylcholine</a> (PC);</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sphingomyelin" title="Sphingomyelin">Sphingomyelin</a> (SM).</li></ul>
<p><b>Inner monolayer</b>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Phosphatidylethanolamine" title="Phosphatidylethanolamine">Phosphatidylethanolamine</a> (PE);</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphoinositol" title="Phosphoinositol">Phosphoinositol</a> (PI) (small amounts).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Phosphatidylserine" title="Phosphatidylserine">Phosphatidylserine</a> (PS);</li></ul>
<p>This asymmetric phospholipid distribution among the bilayer is the result of the function of several energy-dependent and energy-independent <a href="/wiki/Phospholipid" title="Phospholipid">phospholipid</a> transport proteins. Proteins called “<a href="/wiki/Flippase" title="Flippase">Flippases</a>” move phospholipids from the outer to the inner monolayer, while others called “<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floppase" title="Floppase">floppases</a>” do the opposite operation, against a concentration gradient in an energy dependent manner. Additionally, there are also “<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scramblase" title="Scramblase">scramblase</a>” proteins that move phospholipids in both directions at the same time, down their concentration gradients in an energy independent manner. There is still considerable debate ongoing regarding the identity of these membrane maintenance proteins in the red cell membrane.
</p><p>The maintenance of an asymmetric phospholipid distribution in the bilayer (such as an exclusive localization of PS and PIs in the inner monolayer) is critical for the cell integrity and function due to several reasons:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macrophages" title="Macrophages">Macrophages</a> recognize and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phagocytose" title="Phagocytose">phagocytose</a> red cells that expose PS at their outer surface. Thus the confinement of PS in the inner monolayer is essential if the cell is to survive its frequent encounters with macrophages of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reticuloendothelial_system" title="Reticuloendothelial system">reticuloendothelial system</a>, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Spleen" title="Spleen">spleen</a>.</li>
<li>Premature destruction of <a href="/wiki/Thalassemia" title="Thalassemia">thallassemic</a> and sickle red cells has been linked to disruptions of lipid asymmetry leading to exposure of PS on the outer monolayer.</li>
<li>An exposure of PS can potentiate adhesion of red cells to vascular endothelial cells, effectively preventing normal transit through the microvasculature. Thus it is important that PS is maintained only in the inner leaflet of the bilayer to ensure normal blood flow in microcirculation.</li>
<li>Both PS and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PIP2" title="PIP2">phosphatidylinositol-4,5-bisphosphate</a> (PIP2) can regulate membrane mechanical function, due to their interactions with skeletal proteins such as <a href="/wiki/Spectrin" title="Spectrin">spectrin</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Band_4.1" title="Band 4.1">protein 4.1R</a>. Recent studies have shown that binding of spectrin to PS promotes membrane mechanical stability. PIP2 enhances the binding of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Band_4.1" title="Band 4.1">protein band 4.1R</a> to <a href="/wiki/Glycophorin_C" title="Glycophorin C">glycophorin C</a> but decreases its interaction with <a href="/wiki/Band_3" title="Band 3">protein band 3</a>, and thereby may modulate the linkage of the bilayer to the membrane skeleton.</li></ul>
<p>The presence of specialized structures named "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lipid_rafts" title="Lipid rafts">lipid rafts</a>" in the red blood cell membrane have been described by recent studies. These are structures enriched in <a href="/wiki/Cholesterol" title="Cholesterol">cholesterol</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sphingolipids" title="Sphingolipids">sphingolipids</a> associated with specific membrane proteins, namely <a href="/wiki/FLOT1" title="FLOT1">flotillins</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/STOM" title="STOM">stomatins</a> (band 7), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G-proteins" title="G-proteins">G-proteins</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta-adrenergic_receptor" title="Beta-adrenergic receptor">β-adrenergic receptors</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lipid_rafts" title="Lipid rafts">Lipid rafts</a> that have been implicated in cell signaling events in nonerythroid cells have been shown in erythroid cells to mediate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta-adrenergic_receptor" title="Beta-adrenergic receptor">β2-adregenic receptor</a> signaling and increase <a href="/wiki/Cyclic_adenosine_monophosphate" title="Cyclic adenosine monophosphate">cAMP</a> levels, and thus regulating entry of <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malarial</a> parasites into normal red cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mohandas2008_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mohandas2008-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Membrane_proteins">Membrane proteins</span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RBC_Membrane_Proteins_SDS-PAGE_gel.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1013" data-file-width="869" height="256" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/RBC_Membrane_Proteins_SDS-PAGE_gel.jpg/220px-RBC_Membrane_Proteins_SDS-PAGE_gel.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/RBC_Membrane_Proteins_SDS-PAGE_gel.jpg/330px-RBC_Membrane_Proteins_SDS-PAGE_gel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/RBC_Membrane_Proteins_SDS-PAGE_gel.jpg/440px-RBC_Membrane_Proteins_SDS-PAGE_gel.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RBC_Membrane_Proteins_SDS-PAGE_gel.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Red blood cell membrane proteins separated by <a href="/wiki/Polyacrylamide_gel_electrophoresis" title="Polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis">SDS-PAGE</a> and silverstained<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The proteins of the membrane skeleton are responsible for the deformability, flexibility and durability of the red blood cell, enabling it to squeeze through capillaries less than half the diameter of the red blood cell (7–8 μm) and recovering the discoid shape as soon as these cells stop receiving compressive forces, in a similar fashion to an object made of rubber.
</p><p>There are currently more than 50 known membrane proteins, which can exist in a few hundred up to a million copies per red blood cell. Approximately 25 of these membrane proteins carry the various blood group antigens, such as the A, B and Rh antigens, among many others. These membrane proteins can perform a wide diversity of functions, such as transporting ions and molecules across the red cell membrane, adhesion and interaction with other cells such as endothelial cells, as signaling receptors, as well as other currently unknown functions. The <a href="/wiki/Blood_type" title="Blood type">blood types</a> of humans are due to variations in surface <a href="/wiki/Glycoprotein" title="Glycoprotein">glycoproteins</a> of red blood cells. Disorders of the proteins in these membranes are associated with many disorders, such as <a href="/wiki/Hereditary_spherocytosis" title="Hereditary spherocytosis">hereditary spherocytosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hereditary_elliptocytosis" title="Hereditary elliptocytosis">hereditary elliptocytosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hereditary_stomatocytosis" title="Hereditary stomatocytosis">hereditary stomatocytosis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Paroxysmal_nocturnal_hemoglobinuria" title="Paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria">paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yazdanbakhsh2000_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Yazdanbakhsh2000-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mohandas2008_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mohandas2008-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>The red blood cell membrane proteins organized according to their function:
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Surface_electrostatic_potential">Surface electrostatic potential</span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Zeta_potential" title="Zeta potential">zeta potential</a> is an electrochemical property of cell surfaces that is determined by the net electrical charge of molecules exposed at the surface of cell membranes of the cell. The normal zeta potential of the red blood cell is −15.7 milli<a href="/wiki/Volt" title="Volt">volts</a> (mV).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tokumasu2012_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tokumasu2012-32">[32]</a></sup> Much of this potential appears to be contributed by the exposed <a href="/wiki/Sialic_acid" title="Sialic acid">sialic acid</a> residues in the membrane: their removal results in zeta potential of −6.06 mV.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Secondary_functions">Secondary functions</span></h3>
<p>When red blood cells undergo shear stress in constricted vessels, they release <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a>, which causes the vessel walls to relax and dilate so as to promote normal blood flow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>When their hemoglobin molecules are deoxygenated, red blood cells release <a href="/wiki/S-Nitrosothiol" title="S-Nitrosothiol">S-nitrosothiols</a>, which also act to dilate blood vessels,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> thus directing more blood to areas of the body depleted of oxygen.
</p><p>Red blood cells can also synthesize <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a> enzymatically, using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L-arginine" title="L-arginine">L-arginine</a> as substrate, as do <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endothelial_cell" title="Endothelial cell">endothelial cells</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Exposure of red blood cells to physiological levels of shear stress activates <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide_synthase" title="Nitric oxide synthase">nitric oxide synthase</a> and export of nitric oxide,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> which may contribute to the regulation of vascular tonus.
</p><p>Red blood cells can also produce <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>, a signalling gas that acts to relax vessel walls. It is believed that the cardioprotective effects of <a href="/wiki/Garlic" title="Garlic">garlic</a> are due to red blood cells converting its sulfur compounds into hydrogen sulfide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Red blood cells also play a part in the body's <a href="/wiki/Immune_response" title="Immune response">immune response</a>: when <a href="/wiki/Lysis" title="Lysis">lysed</a> by pathogens such as bacteria, their hemoglobin releases <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_radical" title="Free radical">free radicals</a>, which break down the pathogen's cell wall and membrane, killing it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cellular_processes">Cellular processes</span></h3>
<p>As a result of not containing <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrion" title="Mitochondrion">mitochondria</a>, red blood cells use none of the oxygen they transport; instead they produce the energy carrier <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Glycolysis" title="Glycolysis">glycolysis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid_fermentation" title="Lactic acid fermentation">lactic acid fermentation</a> on the resulting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyruvate" title="Pyruvate">pyruvate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Furthermore, the <a href="/wiki/Pentose_phosphate_pathway" title="Pentose phosphate pathway">pentose phosphate pathway</a> plays an important role in red blood cells; see <a href="/wiki/Glucose-6-phosphate_dehydrogenase_deficiency" title="Glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase deficiency">glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase deficiency</a> for more.
</p><p>As red blood cells contain no nucleus, <a href="/wiki/Protein_biosynthesis" title="Protein biosynthesis">protein biosynthesis</a> is currently assumed to be absent in these cells.
</p><p>Because of the lack of nuclei and organelles, mature red blood cells do not contain <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> and cannot synthesize any <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a>, and consequently cannot divide and have limited repair capabilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kabanova2009_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kabanova2009-42">[42]</a></sup>  The inability to carry out <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protein_synthesis" title="Protein synthesis">protein synthesis</a> means that no <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">virus</a> can evolve to target mammalian red blood cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>  However, infection with <a href="/wiki/Parvovirus" title="Parvovirus">parvoviruses</a> (such as human <a href="/wiki/Parvovirus_B19" title="Parvovirus B19">parvovirus B19</a>) can affect erythroid precursors, as recognized by the presence of giant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pronormoblast" title="Pronormoblast">pronormoblasts</a> with viral particles and <a href="/wiki/Inclusion_bodies" title="Inclusion bodies">inclusion bodies</a>, thus temporarily depleting the blood of reticulocytes and causing <a href="/wiki/Anemia" title="Anemia">anemia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_cycle">Life cycle</span></h2>
<p>Human red blood cells are produced through a process named <a href="/wiki/Erythropoiesis" title="Erythropoiesis">erythropoiesis</a>, developing from committed <a href="/wiki/Stem_cell" title="Stem cell">stem cells</a> to mature red blood cells in about 7 days. When matured, in a healthy individual these cells live in blood circulation for about 100 to 120 days (and 80 to 90 days in a full term <a href="/wiki/Infant" title="Infant">infant</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> At the end of their lifespan, they are removed from circulation. In many chronic diseases, the lifespan of the red blood cells is reduced.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Creation">Creation</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Erythropoiesis" title="Erythropoiesis">Erythropoiesis</a> is the process by which new red blood cells are produced; it lasts about 7 days. Through this process red blood cells are continuously produced in the red <a href="/wiki/Bone_marrow" title="Bone marrow">bone marrow</a> of large bones. (In the <a href="/wiki/Embryo" title="Embryo">embryo</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a> is the main site of red blood cell production.) The production can be stimulated by the <a href="/wiki/Hormone" title="Hormone">hormone</a> <a href="/wiki/Erythropoietin" title="Erythropoietin">erythropoietin</a> (EPO), synthesised by the kidney. Just before and after leaving the bone marrow, the developing cells are known as <a href="/wiki/Reticulocyte" title="Reticulocyte">reticulocytes</a>; these constitute about 1% of circulating red blood cells.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Functional_lifetime">Functional lifetime</span></h3>
<p>The functional lifetime of a red blood cell is about 100–120 days, during which time the red blood cells are continually moved by the blood flow push (in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arteries" title="Arteries">arteries</a>), pull (in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Veins" title="Veins">veins</a>) and a combination of the two as they squeeze through microvessels such as capillaries. They are also recycled in the bone marrow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Senescence">Senescence</span></h3>
<p>The aging red blood cell undergoes changes in its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plasma_membrane" title="Plasma membrane">plasma membrane</a>, making it susceptible to selective recognition by <a href="/wiki/Macrophage" title="Macrophage">macrophages</a> and subsequent <a href="/wiki/Phagocytosis" title="Phagocytosis">phagocytosis</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Mononuclear_phagocyte_system" title="Mononuclear phagocyte system">mononuclear phagocyte system</a> (<a href="/wiki/Spleen" title="Spleen">spleen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lymph_node" title="Lymph node">lymph nodes</a>), thus removing old and defective cells and continually purging the blood. This process is termed <a href="/wiki/Eryptosis" title="Eryptosis">eryptosis</a>, red blood cell programmed cell death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lang2012_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lang2012-47">[47]</a></sup> This process normally occurs at the same rate of production by erythropoiesis, balancing the total circulating red blood cell count. Eryptosis is increased in a wide variety of diseases including <a href="/wiki/Sepsis" title="Sepsis">sepsis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haemolytic_uremic_syndrome" title="Haemolytic uremic syndrome">haemolytic uremic syndrome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sickle_cell_anemia" title="Sickle cell anemia">sickle cell anemia</a>, beta-<a href="/wiki/Thalassemia" title="Thalassemia">thalassemia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glucose-6-phosphate_dehydrogenase_deficiency" title="Glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase deficiency">glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase deficiency</a>, phosphate depletion, iron deficiency and <a href="/wiki/Wilson%27s_disease" title="Wilson's disease">Wilson's disease</a>. Eryptosis can be elicited by osmotic shock, oxidative stress, energy depletion as well as a wide variety of endogenous mediators and xenobiotics. Excessive eryptosis is observed in red blood cells lacking the cGMP-dependent protein kinase type I or the AMP-activated protein kinase AMPK. <a href="/wiki/Enzyme_inhibitor" title="Enzyme inhibitor">Inhibitors</a> of eryptosis include <a href="/wiki/Erythropoietin" title="Erythropoietin">erythropoietin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catecholamine" title="Catecholamine">catecholamines</a> and high concentrations of <a href="/wiki/Urea" title="Urea">urea</a>.
</p><p>Much of the resulting breakdown products are recirculated in the body. The heme constituent of hemoglobin are broken down into iron (Fe<sup>3+</sup>) and <a href="/wiki/Biliverdin" title="Biliverdin">biliverdin</a>. The biliverdin is reduced to <a href="/wiki/Bilirubin" title="Bilirubin">bilirubin</a>, which is released into the plasma and recirculated to the liver bound to <a href="/wiki/Albumin" title="Albumin">albumin</a>.  The iron is released into the plasma to be recirculated by a carrier protein called <a href="/wiki/Transferrin" title="Transferrin">transferrin</a>.  Almost all red blood cells are removed in this manner from the circulation before they are old enough to <a href="/wiki/Hemolysis" title="Hemolysis">hemolyze</a>. Hemolyzed hemoglobin is bound to a protein in plasma called <a href="/wiki/Haptoglobin" title="Haptoglobin">haptoglobin</a>, which is not excreted by the kidney.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_significance">Clinical significance</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disease">Disease</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:146px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sicklecells.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="168" data-file-width="144" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/92/Sicklecells.jpg" width="144"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption">Affected by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sickle-cell_disease" title="Sickle-cell disease">Sickle-cell disease</a>, red blood cells alter shape and threaten to damage internal organs.</div></div></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_diseases" title="Blood diseases">Blood diseases</a> involving the red blood cells include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Anemia" title="Anemia">Anemias</a> (or anaemias) are diseases characterized by low oxygen transport capacity of the blood, because of low red cell count or some abnormality of the red blood cells or the hemoglobin.</li></ul>
<dl><dd><ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron_deficiency_anemia" title="Iron deficiency anemia">Iron deficiency anemia</a> is the most common anemia; it occurs when the dietary intake or absorption of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> is insufficient, and hemoglobin, which contains iron, cannot be formed</li></ul></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sickle-cell_disease" title="Sickle-cell disease">Sickle-cell disease</a> is a genetic disease that results in abnormal hemoglobin molecules. When these release their oxygen load in the tissues, they become insoluble, leading to mis-shaped red blood cells. These sickle shaped red cells are less deformable and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_Viscoelasticity" title="Blood Viscoelasticity">viscoelastic</a> meaning that they have become rigid and can cause blood vessel blockage, pain, strokes, and other tissue damage.</li></ul></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><ul><li><a href="/wiki/Thalassemia" title="Thalassemia">Thalassemia</a> is a genetic disease that results in the production of an abnormal ratio of hemoglobin subunits.</li></ul></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><ul><li><a href="/wiki/Hereditary_spherocytosis" title="Hereditary spherocytosis">Hereditary spherocytosis</a> syndromes are a group of inherited disorders characterized by defects in the red blood cell's <a href="/wiki/Cell_membrane" title="Cell membrane">cell membrane</a>, causing the cells to be small, sphere-shaped, and fragile instead of donut-shaped and flexible. These abnormal red blood cells are destroyed by the <a href="/wiki/Spleen" title="Spleen">spleen</a>. Several other hereditary disorders of the red blood cell membrane are known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></li></ul></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pernicious_anemia" title="Pernicious anemia">Pernicious anemia</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Autoimmune_disease" title="Autoimmune disease">autoimmune disease</a> wherein the body lacks <a href="/wiki/Intrinsic_factor" title="Intrinsic factor">intrinsic factor</a>, required to absorb <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_B12" title="Vitamin B12">vitamin B<sub>12</sub></a> from food. Vitamin B<sub>12</sub> is needed for the production of hemoglobin.</li></ul></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><ul><li><a href="/wiki/Aplastic_anemia" title="Aplastic anemia">Aplastic anemia</a> is caused by the inability of the <a href="/wiki/Bone_marrow" title="Bone marrow">bone marrow</a> to produce blood cells.</li></ul></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><ul><li><a href="/wiki/Pure_red_cell_aplasia" title="Pure red cell aplasia">Pure red cell aplasia</a> is caused by the inability of the bone marrow to produce only red blood cells.</li></ul></dd></dl>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Human_Erythrocytes_OsmoticPressure_PhaseContrast_Plain.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="580" data-file-width="1280" height="100" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Human_Erythrocytes_OsmoticPressure_PhaseContrast_Plain.svg/220px-Human_Erythrocytes_OsmoticPressure_PhaseContrast_Plain.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Human_Erythrocytes_OsmoticPressure_PhaseContrast_Plain.svg/330px-Human_Erythrocytes_OsmoticPressure_PhaseContrast_Plain.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Human_Erythrocytes_OsmoticPressure_PhaseContrast_Plain.svg/440px-Human_Erythrocytes_OsmoticPressure_PhaseContrast_Plain.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Human_Erythrocytes_OsmoticPressure_PhaseContrast_Plain.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Micrographs of the effects of osmotic pressure</div></div></div>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Hemolysis" title="Hemolysis">Hemolysis</a> is the general term for excessive breakdown of red blood cells. It can have several causes and can result in <a href="/wiki/Hemolytic_anemia" title="Hemolytic anemia">hemolytic anemia</a>.</li></ul>
<dl><dd><ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a> parasite spends part of its life-cycle in red blood cells, feeds on their hemoglobin and then breaks them apart, causing fever. Both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sickle-cell_disease" title="Sickle-cell disease">sickle-cell disease</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thalassemia" title="Thalassemia">thalassemia</a> are more common in malaria areas, because these mutations convey some protection against the parasite.</li></ul></dd></dl>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Polycythemia" title="Polycythemia">Polycythemias</a> (or erythrocytoses) are diseases characterized by a surplus of red blood cells. The increased viscosity of the blood can cause a number of symptoms.</li></ul>
<dl><dd><ul><li>In <a href="/wiki/Polycythemia_vera" title="Polycythemia vera">polycythemia vera</a> the increased number of red blood cells results from an abnormality in the bone marrow.</li></ul></dd></dl>
<ul><li>Several <a href="/wiki/Microangiopathy" title="Microangiopathy">microangiopathic diseases</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Disseminated_intravascular_coagulation" title="Disseminated intravascular coagulation">disseminated intravascular coagulation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thrombotic_microangiopathies" title="Thrombotic microangiopathies">thrombotic microangiopathies</a>, present with <a href="/wiki/Pathognomonic" title="Pathognomonic">pathognomonic</a> (diagnostic) red blood cell fragments called <a href="/wiki/Schistocyte" title="Schistocyte">schistocytes</a>. These pathologies generate <a href="/wiki/Fibrin" title="Fibrin">fibrin</a> strands that sever red blood cells as they try to move past a <a href="/wiki/Thrombus" title="Thrombus">thrombus</a>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transfusion">Transfusion</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Blood_transfusion" title="Blood transfusion">Blood transfusion</a></div>
<p>Red blood cells may be given as part of a <a href="/wiki/Blood_transfusion" title="Blood transfusion">blood transfusion</a>. Blood may be <a href="/wiki/Blood_donation" title="Blood donation">donated</a> from another person, or stored by the recipient at an earlier date. Donated blood usually requires <a href="/wiki/Screening_(medicine)" title="Screening (medicine)">screening</a> to ensure that donors do not contain risk factors for the presence of blood-borne diseases, or will not suffer themselves by giving blood. Blood is usually collected and tested for common or serious <a href="/wiki/Blood-borne_disease" title="Blood-borne disease">Blood-borne diseases</a> including <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis_B" title="Hepatitis B">Hepatitis B</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis_C" title="Hepatitis C">Hepatitis C</a> and <a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">HIV</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Blood_type" title="Blood type">blood type</a> (A, B, AB, or O) or the blood product is identified. This relates to the presence of <a href="/wiki/Antigen" title="Antigen">antigens</a> on the cell's surface. After this process, the blood is stored, and within a short duration is used. Blood can be given as a whole product or the red blood cells separated as <a href="/wiki/Packed_red_blood_cells" title="Packed red blood cells">packed red blood cells</a>.
</p><p>Blood is often transfused when there is known anaemia, active bleeding, or when there is an expectation of serious blood loss, such as prior to an operation. Before blood is given, a small sample of the recipient's blood is tested with the transfusion in a process known as <a href="/wiki/Cross-matching" title="Cross-matching">cross-matching</a>. In addition to the transmission of infection, certain types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transfusion_reaction" title="Transfusion reaction">transfusion reaction</a>.
</p><p>In 2008 it was reported that human <a href="/wiki/Embryonic_stem_cell" title="Embryonic stem cell">embryonic stem cells</a> had been successfully coaxed into becoming red blood cells in the lab. The difficult step was to induce the cells to eject their nucleus; this was achieved by growing the cells on <a href="/wiki/Stromal_cell" title="Stromal cell">stromal cells</a> from the bone marrow. It is hoped that these artificial red blood cells can eventually be used for blood transfusions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tests">Tests</span></h3>
<p>Several <a href="/wiki/Blood_test" title="Blood test">blood tests</a> involve red blood cells. These include a <i>RBC count</i> (the number of red blood cells per volume of blood), calculation of the <a href="/wiki/Hematocrit" title="Hematocrit">hematocrit</a> (percentage of blood volume occupied by red blood cells), and the <a href="/wiki/Erythrocyte_sedimentation_rate" title="Erythrocyte sedimentation rate">erythrocyte sedimentation rate</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Blood_type" title="Blood type">blood type</a> needs to be determined to prepare for a <a href="/wiki/Blood_transfusion" title="Blood transfusion">blood transfusion</a> or an <a href="/wiki/Organ_transplantation" title="Organ transplantation">organ transplantation</a>.
</p><p>Many diseases involving red blood cells are diagnosed with a <a href="/wiki/Blood_film" title="Blood film">blood film</a> (or peripheral blood smear), where a thin layer of blood is smeared on a microscope slide. This may reveal abnormalities of red blood cell shape and form. When red blood cells sometimes occur as a stack, flat side next to flat side. This is known as <i><a href="/wiki/Rouleaux" title="Rouleaux">rouleaux</a> formation</i>, and it occurs more often if the levels of certain serum proteins are elevated, as for instance during <a href="/wiki/Inflammation" title="Inflammation">inflammation</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Separation_and_blood_doping">Separation and blood doping</span></h3>
<p>Red blood cells can be obtained from <a href="/wiki/Whole_blood" title="Whole blood">whole blood</a> by <a href="/wiki/Centrifugation" title="Centrifugation">centrifugation</a>, which separates the cells from the <a href="/wiki/Blood_plasma" title="Blood plasma">blood plasma</a> in a process known as <a href="/wiki/Blood_fractionation" title="Blood fractionation">blood fractionation</a>. <a href="/wiki/Packed_red_blood_cells" title="Packed red blood cells">Packed red blood cells</a>, which are made in this way from whole blood with the plasma removed, are used in <a href="/wiki/Transfusion_medicine" title="Transfusion medicine">transfusion medicine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> During <a href="/wiki/Blood_donation" title="Blood donation">plasma donation</a>, the red blood cells are pumped back into the body right away and only the plasma is collected.
</p><p>Some athletes have tried to improve their performance by <a href="/wiki/Blood_doping" title="Blood doping">blood doping</a>: first about 1 litre of their blood is extracted, then the red blood cells are isolated, frozen and stored, to be reinjected shortly before the competition. (Red blood cells can be conserved for 5 weeks at −79 °C or −110 °F) This practice is hard to detect but may endanger the human <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardiovascular_system" title="Cardiovascular system">cardiovascular system</a> which is not equipped to deal with blood of the resulting higher <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a>. Another method of blood doping involves injection with <a href="/wiki/Erythropoietin" title="Erythropoietin">erythropoietin</a> in order to stimulate production of red blood cells.  Both practices are banned by the <a href="/wiki/World_Anti-Doping_Agency" title="World Anti-Doping Agency">World Anti-Doping Agency</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>The first person to describe red blood cells was the young <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> biologist <a href="/wiki/Jan_Swammerdam" title="Jan Swammerdam">Jan Swammerdam</a>, who had used an early <a href="/wiki/Microscope" title="Microscope">microscope</a> in 1658 to study the blood of a frog.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Unaware of this work, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anton_van_Leeuwenhoek" title="Anton van Leeuwenhoek">Anton van Leeuwenhoek</a> provided another microscopic description in 1674, this time providing a more precise description of red blood cells, even approximating their size, "25,000 times smaller than a fine grain of sand".
</p><p>In 1901, <a href="/wiki/Karl_Landsteiner" title="Karl Landsteiner">Karl Landsteiner</a> published his discovery of the three main <a href="/wiki/ABO_blood_group_system" title="ABO blood group system">blood groups</a>—A, B, and C (which he later renamed to O). Landsteiner described the regular patterns in which reactions occurred when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_serum" title="Blood serum">serum</a> was mixed with red blood cells, thus identifying compatible and conflicting combinations between these blood groups. A year later Alfred von Decastello and Adriano Sturli, two colleagues of Landsteiner, identified a fourth blood group—AB.
</p><p>In 1959, by use of <a href="/wiki/X-ray_crystallography" title="X-ray crystallography">X-ray crystallography</a>, Dr. <a href="/wiki/Max_Perutz" title="Max Perutz">Max Perutz</a> was able to unravel the <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin#Structure" title="Hemoglobin">structure of hemoglobin</a>, the red blood cell protein that carries oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>The oldest intact red blood cells ever discovered were found in <a href="/wiki/%C3%96tzi" title="Ötzi">Ötzi the Iceman</a>, a natural mummy of a man who died around 3255 BCE. These cells were discovered in May 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
