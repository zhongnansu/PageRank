<p>In <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_chemistry" title="Inorganic chemistry">inorganic chemistry</a>, <b>bicarbonate</b> (<a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Pure_and_Applied_Chemistry" title="International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry">IUPAC</a>-recommended nomenclature: <b>hydrogencarbonate</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>) is an intermediate form in the <a href="/wiki/Deprotonation" title="Deprotonation">deprotonation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a>. It is a <a href="/wiki/Polyatomic_ion" title="Polyatomic ion">polyatomic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">anion</a> with the chemical formula <span class="chemf nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">H</a><a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">C</a><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>.
</p><p>Bicarbonate serves a crucial biochemical role in the physiological <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buffering_solution" title="Buffering solution">buffering</a> system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-veq_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-veq-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The term "bicarbonate" was coined in 1814 by the English chemist <a href="/wiki/William_Hyde_Wollaston" title="William Hyde Wollaston">William Hyde Wollaston</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  The prefix "bi" in "bicarbonate" comes from an outdated naming system and is based on the observation that there is twice as much <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonate</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>) per sodium ion in <a href="/wiki/Sodium_bicarbonate" title="Sodium bicarbonate">sodium bicarbonate</a> (NaHCO<sub>3</sub>) and other bicarbonates than in <a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">sodium carbonate</a> (Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>) and other carbonates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The name lives on as a <a href="/wiki/Trivial_name" title="Trivial name">trivial name</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bicarbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The bicarbonate ion (hydrogencarbonate ion) is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">anion</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Empirical_formula" title="Empirical formula">empirical formula</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">HCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> and a molecular mass of 61.01 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_mass_unit" title="Atomic mass unit">daltons</a>; it consists of one central carbon <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a> surrounded by three oxygen atoms in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trigonal_planar" title="Trigonal planar">trigonal planar</a> arrangement, with a hydrogen atom attached to one of the oxygens. It is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isoelectronic" title="Isoelectronic">isoelectronic</a> with <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">HNO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>. The bicarbonate ion carries a negative one <a href="/wiki/Formal_charge" title="Formal charge">formal charge</a> and is an <a href="/wiki/Amphoterism#Amphiprotic_molecules" title="Amphoterism">amphiprotic</a> species which has both acidic and basic properties. It is both the <a href="/wiki/Conjugate_acid" title="Conjugate acid">conjugate base</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>; and the <a href="/wiki/Conjugate_acid" title="Conjugate acid">conjugate acid</a> of <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>, the <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonate</a> ion, as shown by these <a href="/wiki/Chemical_equilibrium" title="Chemical equilibrium">equilibrium</a> reactions:
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O ⇌ <span class="chemf nowrap">HCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> + H<sub>2</sub>O + OH<sup>−</sup> ⇌ H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> + 2  OH<sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<dl><dd>H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O ⇌ <span class="chemf nowrap">HCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> + H<sub>3</sub>O<sup>+</sup> + H<sub>2</sub>O ⇌ <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> + 2 H<sub>3</sub>O<sup>+</sup>.</dd></dl>
<p>A bicarbonate salt forms when a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">positively charged ion</a> attaches to the negatively charged oxygen atoms of the ion, forming an <a href="/wiki/Ionic_compound" title="Ionic compound">ionic compound</a>. Many bicarbonates are <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">soluble</a> in <a href="/wiki/Aqueous_solution" title="Aqueous solution">water</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard temperature and pressure">standard temperature and pressure</a>; in particular, sodium bicarbonate contributes to <a href="/wiki/Total_dissolved_solids" title="Total dissolved solids">total dissolved solids</a>, a common parameter for assessing <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">water quality</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physiological_role">Physiological role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bicarbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physiological role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:452px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Riassorbimento_bicarbonati_e_respirazione_cellulare.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="560" data-file-width="540" height="467" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Riassorbimento_bicarbonati_e_respirazione_cellulare.svg/450px-Riassorbimento_bicarbonati_e_respirazione_cellulare.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Riassorbimento_bicarbonati_e_respirazione_cellulare.svg/675px-Riassorbimento_bicarbonati_e_respirazione_cellulare.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Riassorbimento_bicarbonati_e_respirazione_cellulare.svg/900px-Riassorbimento_bicarbonati_e_respirazione_cellulare.svg.png 2x" width="450"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Riassorbimento_bicarbonati_e_respirazione_cellulare.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>CO<sub>2</sub> produced as a waste product of the oxidation of sugars in the mitochondria reacts with water in a reaction catalyzed by <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_anhydrase" title="Carbonic anhydrase">carbonic anhydrase</a> to form H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>, which is in equilibrium with the cation H<sup>+</sup> and anion HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>. It is then carried to the lung, where the reverse reaction occurs and CO<sub>2</sub> gas is released. In the kidney (left), cells (green) lining the proximal tubule conserve bicarbonate by transporting it from the glomerular filtrate in the lumen (yellow) of the nephron back into the blood (red). The exact stoichiometry in the kidney is omitted for simplicity.</div></div></div><div style="clear:left;"></div>
<p>Bicarbonate (<span class="chemf nowrap">HCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>) is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkaline" title="Alkaline">alkaline</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (July 2018)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup>, and a vital component of the <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> <a href="/wiki/Buffer_solution" title="Buffer solution">buffering system</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-veq_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-veq-3">[3]</a></sup> of the human body (maintaining <a href="/wiki/Acid%E2%80%93base_homeostasis" title="Acid–base homeostasis">acid–base homeostasis</a>). 70–75% of CO<sub>2</sub> in the body is converted into <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a> (H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>), which can quickly turn into bicarbonate.
</p><p>With carbonic acid as the <a href="/wiki/Reaction_intermediate" title="Reaction intermediate">central intermediate</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemical_species" title="Chemical species">species</a>, bicarbonate – in conjunction with water, <a href="/wiki/Hydronium" title="Hydronium">hydrogen ions</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> – forms this buffering system, which is maintained at the volatile equilibrium<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-veq_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-veq-3">[3]</a></sup> required to provide prompt resistance to pH changes in both the acidic and <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">basic</a> directions. This is especially important for protecting <a href="/wiki/Tissue_(biology)" title="Tissue (biology)">tissues</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a>, where pH changes too far outside of the normal range in either direction could prove disastrous (see <a href="/wiki/Acidosis" title="Acidosis">acidosis</a> or <a href="/wiki/Alkalosis" title="Alkalosis">alkalosis</a>).
</p><p>Bicarbonate also serves much in the digestive system. It raises the internal pH of the stomach, after highly acidic digestive juices have finished in their digestion of food. Bicarbonate also acts to regulate pH in the small intestine. It is released from the <a href="/wiki/Pancreas" title="Pancreas">pancreas</a> in response to the hormone <a href="/wiki/Secretin" title="Secretin">secretin</a> to neutralize the acidic <a href="/wiki/Chyme" title="Chyme">chyme</a> entering the duodenum from the stomach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bicarbonate_in_the_environment">Bicarbonate in the environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bicarbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Bicarbonate in the environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bicarbonate is the dominant form of <a href="/wiki/Total_inorganic_carbon" title="Total inorganic carbon">dissolved inorganic carbon</a> in sea water,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and in most fresh waters. As such it is an important sink in the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a>.
</p><p>In freshwater ecology, strong <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthetic</a> activity by freshwater plants in daylight releases gaseous <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> into the water and at the same time produces bicarbonate ions. These shift the pH upward until in certain circumstances the degree of alkalinity can become toxic to some organisms or can make other chemical constituents such as <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> toxic. In darkness, when no photosynthesis occurs, respiration processes release carbon dioxide, and no new bicarbonate ions are produced, resulting in a rapid fall in pH.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bicarbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The most common salt of the bicarbonate ion is <a href="/wiki/Sodium_bicarbonate" title="Sodium bicarbonate">sodium bicarbonate</a>, NaHCO<sub>3</sub>, which is commonly known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baking_soda" title="Baking soda">baking soda</a>. When heated or exposed to an <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acid</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a> (<a href="/wiki/Vinegar" title="Vinegar">vinegar</a>), sodium bicarbonate releases <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. This is used as a <a href="/wiki/Leavening_agent" title="Leavening agent">leavening agent</a> in <a href="/wiki/Baking" title="Baking">baking</a>.
</p><p>The flow of bicarbonate ions from rocks weathered by the carbonic acid in rainwater is an important part of the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ammonium_bicarbonate" title="Ammonium bicarbonate">Ammonium bicarbonate</a> is used in <a href="/wiki/Digestive_biscuit" title="Digestive biscuit">digestive biscuit</a> manufacture.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnostics">Diagnostics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bicarbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diagnostics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diagnostic_medicine" title="Diagnostic medicine">diagnostic medicine</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_value" title="Blood value">blood value</a> of bicarbonate is one of several indicators of the state of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Acid%E2%80%93base_physiology&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Acid–base physiology (page does not exist)">acid–base physiology</a> in the body. It is measured, along with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chloride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>, to assess <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolyte</a> levels in an <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Electrolyte_panel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Electrolyte panel (page does not exist)">electrolyte panel</a> test (which has <a href="/wiki/Current_Procedural_Terminology" title="Current Procedural Terminology">Current Procedural Terminology</a>, CPT, code 80051).
</p><p>The parameter <i>standard bicarbonate concentration</i> (SBC<sub>e</sub>) is the bicarbonate concentration in the blood at a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PaCO2" title="PaCO2">P<sub>a</sub>CO<sub>2</sub></a> of 40 mmHg (5.33 kPa), full oxygen saturation and 36 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
