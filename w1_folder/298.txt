<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Baker's yeast</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:277px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:20100911_232323_Yeast_Live.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2222" data-file-width="2222" height="275" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/20100911_232323_Yeast_Live.jpg/275px-20100911_232323_Yeast_Live.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/20100911_232323_Yeast_Live.jpg/413px-20100911_232323_Yeast_Live.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/20100911_232323_Yeast_Live.jpg/550px-20100911_232323_Yeast_Live.jpg 2x" width="275"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:20100911_232323_Yeast_Live.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_cerevisiae" title="Saccharomyces cerevisiae">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</a></i>, the yeast commonly used as baker's yeast. Gradation marks are 1 <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">µm</a> apart</div></div></div>
<p><b>Baker's yeast</b> is the common name for the strains of  <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">yeast</a> commonly used as a <a href="/wiki/Leavening_agent" title="Leavening agent">leavening agent</a> in <a href="/wiki/Baking" title="Baking">baking</a> <a href="/wiki/Bread" title="Bread">bread</a> and bakery products, where it converts the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(food)" title="Fermentation (food)">fermentable</a> <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugars</a> present in the <a href="/wiki/Dough" title="Dough">dough</a> into <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>. Baker's yeast is of the species <i><a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_cerevisiae" title="Saccharomyces cerevisiae">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-387-38563-0-cite4_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-387-38563-0-cite4-1">[1]</a></sup> which is the same species (but a different <a href="/wiki/Strain_(biology)" title="Strain (biology)">strain</a>) commonly used in alcoholic fermentation, which is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brewer%27s_yeast" title="Brewer's yeast">brewer's yeast</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Baker's yeast is also a single-cell microorganism found on and around the human body.
</p><p>The use of steamed or boiled <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">potatoes</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-urlReport_on_Vienna_bread_-_Google_Books_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-urlReport_on_Vienna_bread_-_Google_Books-3">[3]</a></sup> water from potato boiling,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-urlA_hand-book_of_industrial_organic_..._-_Google_Books_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-urlA_hand-book_of_industrial_organic_..._-_Google_Books-4">[4]</a></sup> or sugar in a bread dough provides food for the growth of yeasts; however, too much sugar will dehydrate them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-306-47363-1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-306-47363-1-5">[5]</a></sup> Yeast growth is inhibited by both <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">salt</a> and sugar, but more so by salt than sugar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-387-38563-0-cite2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-387-38563-0-cite2-6">[6]</a></sup> Some sources say <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fats</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Butter" title="Butter">butter</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_(food)" title="Egg (food)">eggs</a>, slow down yeast growth;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn1-58816-398-9_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn1-58816-398-9-7">[7]</a></sup> others say the effect of fat on dough remains unclear, presenting evidence that small amounts of fat are beneficial for baked bread volume.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-387-38563-0-cite3_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-387-38563-0-cite3-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Saccharomyces exiguus</i> (also known as <i>S. minor</i>) is a wild yeast found on plants, fruits, and grains that is occasionally used for baking; however, in general it is not used in a pure form but comes from being propagated in a <a href="/wiki/Sourdough" title="Sourdough">sourdough</a> starter.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baker%27s_yeast&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Compressed_fresh_yeast_-_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1330" data-file-width="2000" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Compressed_fresh_yeast_-_1.jpg/220px-Compressed_fresh_yeast_-_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Compressed_fresh_yeast_-_1.jpg/330px-Compressed_fresh_yeast_-_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Compressed_fresh_yeast_-_1.jpg/440px-Compressed_fresh_yeast_-_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Compressed_fresh_yeast_-_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A block of compressed fresh yeast in its wrapper</div></div></div>
<p>It is not known when yeast was first used to bake bread; the earliest definite records come from Ancient Egypt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Egypt_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Egypt-9">[9]</a></sup> Researchers speculate that a mixture of flour meal and water was left longer than usual on a warm day and the yeasts that occur in natural contaminants of the flour caused it to ferment before baking. The resulting bread would have been lighter and tastier than the previous hard <a href="/wiki/Flatbread" title="Flatbread">flatbreads</a>. It is generally assumed that the earliest forms of leavening were likely very similar to modern sourdough; the leavening action of yeast would have been discovered from its action on flatbread doughs, and would have been either cultivated separately or transferred from batch to batch by means of previously mixed ("old") dough. Also, the development of leavened bread seems to have developed in close proximity to the development of <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a> brewing, and <a href="/wiki/Barm" title="Barm">barm</a> from the beer fermentation process can also be used in bread making.
</p><p>Without an understanding of <a href="/wiki/Microbiology" title="Microbiology">microbiology</a>, early bakers would have had little ability to directly control yeast cultures, but still kept locally interesting cultures by reusing doughs and starters to leaven later batches. However, it became possible to isolate and propagate favored yeast strains in the same manner as was done in the beer industry, and it eventually became practical to propagate yeast in a slurry with a composition similar to beer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wort_(brewing)" title="Wort (brewing)">wort</a>, usually including <a href="/wiki/Malt#Uses" title="Malt">malted barley</a> and wheat flour. Such cultures (sometimes referred to in old American cookery as "emptins", from their origins as the dregs of beer or cider fermentation) would become the ancestors of modern baker's yeast, as, in general, they were carefully maintained to avoid what would later be discovered to be bacterial contamination, including using preservatives such as <a href="/wiki/Hops" title="Hops">hops</a> as well as boiling the growth medium.
</p><p>In the 19th century, bread bakers obtained their yeast from beer brewers, and this led to sweet-fermented breads such as the Imperial "<a href="/wiki/Kaiser_roll" title="Kaiser roll">Kaiser-Semmel</a>" roll,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-urlReport_on_Vienna_bread,_page_86_-_Google_Books_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-urlReport_on_Vienna_bread,_page_86_-_Google_Books-10">[10]</a></sup> which in general lacked the sourness created by the acidification typical of <i><a href="/wiki/Lactobacillus" title="Lactobacillus">Lactobacillus</a></i>. However, beer brewers slowly switched from top-fermenting to bottom-fermenting yeast (both S. cerevisiae) and this created a shortage of yeast for making bread, so the <a href="/wiki/Vienna_bread" title="Vienna bread">Vienna Process</a> was developed in 1846.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-521-77917-0_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-521-77917-0-11">[11]</a></sup> While the innovation is often popularly credited for using steam in baking ovens, leading to a different crust characteristic, it is notable for including procedures for high milling of grains (see Vienna grits<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-urlReport_on_Vienna_bread,_page_31-32_-_Google_Books_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-urlReport_on_Vienna_bread,_page_31-32_-_Google_Books-12">[12]</a></sup>), cracking them incrementally instead of mashing them with one pass; as well as better processes for growing and harvesting top-fermenting yeasts, known as press-yeast.
</p><p>Refinements in microbiology following the work of <a href="/wiki/Louis_Pasteur" title="Louis Pasteur">Louis Pasteur</a> led to more advanced methods of culturing pure strains. In 1879, Great Britain introduced specialized growing vats for the production of <i>S. cerevisiae</i>, and in the United States around the turn of the century centrifuges were used for concentrating the yeast,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-521-32749-0_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-521-32749-0-13">[13]</a></sup>  making modern commercial yeast possible, and turning yeast production into a major industrial endeavor. The slurry yeast made by small bakers and grocery shops became cream yeast, a suspension of live yeast cells in growth medium, and then compressed yeast, the fresh cake yeast that became the standard leaven for bread bakers in much of the Westernized world during the early 20th century.
</p><p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fleischmann%27s_Yeast" title="Fleischmann's Yeast">Fleischmann's</a> developed a <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/granulate" title="wiktionary:granulate">granulated</a> active dry yeast for the United States armed forces, which did not require refrigeration and had a longer shelf-life and better temperature tolerance than fresh yeast; it is still the standard yeast for US military recipes. The company created yeast that would rise twice as fast, cutting down on baking time. <a href="/wiki/Lesaffre" title="Lesaffre">Lesaffre</a> would later create instant yeast in the 1970s, which has gained considerable use and market share at the expense of both fresh and dry yeast in their various applications.
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dry_yeast.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Dry_yeast.jpg/220px-Dry_yeast.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Dry_yeast.jpg/330px-Dry_yeast.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Dry_yeast.jpg/440px-Dry_yeast.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dry_yeast.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Active dried yeast, a granulated form in which yeast is commercially sold</div></div></div>
<p>Baker's yeast is available in a number of different forms, the main differences being the moisture contents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-387-38563-0_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-387-38563-0-14">[14]</a></sup> Though each version has certain advantages over the others, the choice of which form to use is largely a question of the requirements of the recipe at hand and the training of the cook preparing it. Dry yeast forms are good choices for longer-term storage, often lasting more than a year at room temperatures without significant loss of viability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In general, with occasional allowances for liquid content and temperature, the different forms of commercial yeast are considered interchangeable.
</p>
<ul><li><b>Cream yeast</b> is the closest form to the yeast slurries of the 19th century, in essence being a suspension of yeast cells in liquid, siphoned off from the growth medium. Its primary use is in industrial bakeries with special high-volume dispensing and mixing equipment, and it is not readily available to small bakeries or home cooks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reinhart,_Peter_2001_61_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reinhart,_Peter_2001_61-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Compressed yeast</b> is, in essence, cream yeast with most of the liquid removed. It is a soft solid, beige in color, and best known in the consumer form as small, foil-wrapped cubes of <b>cake yeast</b>. It is also available in larger-block form for bulk usage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gisslen_2008_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gisslen_2008-17">[17]</a></sup> It is highly perishable; though formerly widely available for the consumer market, it has become less common in supermarkets in some countries due to its poor keeping properties, having been superseded in some such markets by active dry and instant yeast. It is still widely available for commercial use, and is somewhat more tolerant of low temperatures than other forms of commercial yeast; however, even there, instant yeast has made significant market inroads.</li>
<li><b>Active dry yeast</b> is the form of yeast most commonly available to non-commercial bakers in the United States. It consists of coarse oblong granules of yeast, with live yeast cells encapsulated in a thick jacket of dry, dead cells with some growth medium. Under most conditions, active dry yeast must first be <a href="/wiki/Proofing_(baking_technique)" title="Proofing (baking technique)">proofed</a> or rehydrated. It can be stored at room temperature for a year, or frozen for more than a decade, which means that it has better keeping qualities than other forms, but it is generally considered more sensitive than other forms to thermal shock when actually used in recipes.</li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:20100911_165854_YeastGrain.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/20100911_165854_YeastGrain.jpg/220px-20100911_165854_YeastGrain.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/20100911_165854_YeastGrain.jpg/330px-20100911_165854_YeastGrain.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/20100911_165854_YeastGrain.jpg/440px-20100911_165854_YeastGrain.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:20100911_165854_YeastGrain.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A single grain of active dry yeast. The numbered ticks on the scale are 230 <a href="/wiki/Micrometer" title="Micrometer">µm</a> apart</div></div></div>
<ul><li><b>Instant yeast</b> appears similar to active dry yeast, but has smaller granules with substantially higher percentages of live cells per comparable unit volumes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reinhart,_Peter_2001_61_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Reinhart,_Peter_2001_61-16">[16]</a></sup> It is more perishable than active dry yeast but also does not require rehydration, and can usually be added directly to all but the driest doughs. In general, instant yeast has a small amount of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ascorbic_acid" title="Ascorbic acid">ascorbic acid</a> added as a preservative. Some producers provide specific variants for doughs with high sugar contents, and such yeasts are more generally known as <b>osmotolerant</b> yeasts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Rapid-rise yeast</b> is a variety of dried yeast (usually a form of instant yeast) that is of a smaller granular size, thus it dissolves faster in dough, and it provides greater carbon dioxide output to allow faster rising.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-07-048703-0_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-07-048703-0-19">[19]</a></sup> There is considerable debate as to the value of such a product; while most baking experts believe it reduces the flavor potential of the finished product, <i><a href="/wiki/Cook%27s_Illustrated" title="Cook's Illustrated">Cook's Illustrated</a></i> magazine, among others, feels that, at least for direct-rise recipes, it makes little difference. Rapid-rise yeast is often marketed specifically for use in <a href="/wiki/Bread_machine" title="Bread machine">bread machines</a>.</li>
<li><b>Deactivated yeast</b> is dead yeast which has no leavening value and is not interchangeable with other yeast types. Typically used for pizza and pan bread doughs, it is used at a rate of 0.1% of the flour weight, though manufacturer specifications may vary. It is a powerful reducing agent used to increase the extensibility of a dough.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>For most commercial uses, yeast of any form is packaged in bulk (blocks or freezer bags for fresh yeast; vacuum-packed brick bags for dry or instant); however, yeast for home use is often packaged in pre-measured doses, either small squares for compressed yeast or sealed packets for dry or instant. For active dry and instant yeast, in general a single dose (reckoned for the average bread recipe of between 500 g and 1000 g of dough) is about 2.5 <a href="/wiki/Teaspoon" title="Teaspoon">tsp</a> (~12 mL) or about 7 g (<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>4</sub></span> oz), though comparatively lesser amounts are used when the yeast is used in a <a href="/wiki/Pre-ferment" title="Pre-ferment">pre-ferment</a>. In general, a yeast flavor in the baked bread is not noticeable when the <a href="/wiki/Baker_percentage" title="Baker percentage">bakers' percent</a> of added yeast is less than 2.5%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p475isbn1-85573-553-9_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-p475isbn1-85573-553-9-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_research">Use in research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baker%27s_yeast&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Use in research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Model_organism">Model organism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baker%27s_yeast&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Model organism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because it is readily available and easy to culture, baker's yeast has long been used in chemical, biological, and genetic research as a <a href="/wiki/Model_organism" title="Model organism">model organism</a>. In 1996, after 6 years of work, <i>S. cerevisiae</i> became the first <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryote</a> to have its entire <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full_genome_sequencing" title="Full genome sequencing">sequenced</a>. It has over 12 million <a href="/wiki/Base_pair" title="Base pair">base pairs</a> and around 6000 <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a>. Since then, it has remained in the forefront of genetic research. For example, most of our knowledge of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cell_division_cycle" title="Cell division cycle">cell division cycle</a> was worked out from experiments with yeast.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organic_synthesis">Organic synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baker%27s_yeast&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Organic synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BY-reduction_of_carbonyls.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="100" data-file-width="488" height="45" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/BY-reduction_of_carbonyls.svg/220px-BY-reduction_of_carbonyls.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/BY-reduction_of_carbonyls.svg/330px-BY-reduction_of_carbonyls.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/BY-reduction_of_carbonyls.svg/440px-BY-reduction_of_carbonyls.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BY-reduction_of_carbonyls.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Reduction of a carbonyl to a hydroxyl with baker's yeast.</div></div></div>
<p>Baker's yeast contains <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> that can <a href="/wiki/Reducing_agent" title="Reducing agent">reduce</a> a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonyl" title="Carbonyl">carbonyl</a> group into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl" title="Hydroxyl">hydroxyl</a> group in fairly high yield, thus making it useful for <a href="/wiki/Biotransformation" title="Biotransformation">biotransformations</a> in <a href="/wiki/Organic_synthesis" title="Organic synthesis">organic syntheses</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> It is known to reduce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organometallic" title="Organometallic">organometallic</a> carbonyl compounds in very high yield.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Handbookofreagents_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Handbookofreagents-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Baker's yeast can also be used to produce <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> via <a href="/wiki/Brewing#Fermenting" title="Brewing">fermentation</a> for use in chemical synthesis, although doing so in some places requires permits.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_production">Industrial production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baker%27s_yeast&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Industrial production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The baking industry relies on industrial production of its ingredients, including baking yeasts. Much effort has been put into developing and marketing yeasts that will perform reliably in mass production. Since the end of the nineteenth century, baker's yeast has been produced by companies that specialize in its production.
</p><p>The main ingredients for industrial production are yeast cultures, <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">cane</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beet" title="Beet">beet</a>; but a number of minerals, nitrogen and vitamins are also needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EFIG_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-EFIG-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fermentation happens in several phases, which vary depending on the manufacturer:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EFIG_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-EFIG-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>pure cultures in a laboratory flask for 2 to 4 days, then batch fermentations for 13 to 24 hours (anaerobic);</li>
<li>intermediate and stock fermentation with gradual feeding and constant aeration;</li>
<li>pitch and trade fermentation with large air supplies for up to 15 hours;</li>
<li>filtration, blending, extrusion and cutting, drying.</li></ul>
<p>The yeast grows from hundreds kg in the intermediate and stock fermentor to tens of thousands kg in the trade fermentor, where most yeast is produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EFIG_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-EFIG-24">[24]</a></sup> The earlier stages produce more ethanol and other alcohols, while in the final stages ethanol production is suppressed up to 95 % by controlling the amount of oxygen and sugar, in order to increase the yeast production instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EFIG_24-3"><a href="#cite_note-EFIG-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>The industry is highly concentrated, with 5 companies holding up to 80 % of the worldwide market for dry yeast as of 2006. While dry yeast is exported over long distances and mostly sold in the developing countries, industrial customers often prefer to supply fresh yeast from local facilities, with a single wholesaler having up to 90 % of liquid yeast market in UK in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LesaffreEC_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-LesaffreEC-26">[26]</a></sup> In USA companies like <a href="/wiki/Lesaffre" title="Lesaffre">Lesaffre Group</a>, AB Vista, <a href="/wiki/DSM_(company)" title="DSM (company)">DSM</a>, GB Plange and <a href="/wiki/AB_Mauri" title="AB Mauri">AB Mauri</a>, produced hundreds of thousands of metric tons of yeast in 2012.
</p>
