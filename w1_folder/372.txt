<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Positive feedback</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>

See also <a href="/wiki/Viral_video" title="Viral video">viral video</a>.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Positive feedback</b> is a process that occurs in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feedback_loop" title="Feedback loop">feedback loop</a> in which the effects of a small disturbance on a system include an increase in the magnitude of the perturbation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zuckerman_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-zuckerman-1">[1]</a></sup>
That is, <i><b>A</b> produces more of <b>B</b> which in turn produces more of <b>A</b></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-culturalanthropology2nded_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-culturalanthropology2nded-2">[2]</a></sup> In contrast, a system in which the results of a change act to reduce or counteract it has <a href="/wiki/Negative_feedback" title="Negative feedback">negative feedback</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zuckerman_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-zuckerman-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theorymodelling_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-theorymodelling-3">[3]</a></sup> Both concepts play an important role in science and engineering, including biology, chemistry, and <a href="/wiki/Cybernetics" title="Cybernetics">cybernetics</a>.
</p><p>Mathematically, positive feedback is defined as a positive <a href="/wiki/Loop_gain" title="Loop gain">loop gain</a> around a closed loop of cause and effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zuckerman_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-zuckerman-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theorymodelling_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-theorymodelling-3">[3]</a></sup>
That is, positive feedback is <a href="/wiki/Phase_(waves)" title="Phase (waves)">in phase with</a> the input, in the sense that it adds to make the input larger.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
Positive feedback tends to cause <a href="/wiki/Control_theory#Stability" title="Control theory">system instability</a>. When the loop gain is positive and above 1, there will typically be <a href="/wiki/Exponential_growth" title="Exponential growth">exponential growth</a>, increasing <a href="/wiki/Oscillation" title="Oscillation">oscillations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaotic behavior</a> or other divergences from <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/equilibrium" title="wikt:equilibrium">equilibrium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theorymodelling_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-theorymodelling-3">[3]</a></sup> System parameters will typically accelerate towards extreme values, which may damage or destroy the system, or may end with the system <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latch_(electronics)" title="Latch (electronics)">latched</a> into a new stable state. Positive feedback may be controlled by signals in the system being <a href="/wiki/Filter_(signal_processing)" title="Filter (signal processing)">filtered</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Damping" title="Damping">damped</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Maxima_and_minima" title="Maxima and minima">limited</a>, or it can be cancelled or reduced by adding negative feedback.
</p><p>Positive feedback is used in <a href="/wiki/Digital_electronics" title="Digital electronics">digital electronics</a> to force voltages away from intermediate voltages into '0' and '1' states. On the other hand, <a href="/wiki/Thermal_runaway" title="Thermal runaway">thermal runaway</a> is a type of positive feedback that can destroy <a href="/wiki/P%E2%80%93n_junction" title="P–n junction">semiconductor junctions</a>. Positive feedback in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_reactions" title="Chemical reactions">chemical reactions</a> can increase the rate of reactions, and in some cases can lead to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explosives" title="Explosives">explosions</a>. Positive feedback in mechanical design causes <a href="/wiki/Tipping_point_(physics)" title="Tipping point (physics)">tipping-point</a>, or 'over-centre', mechanisms to snap into position, for example in <a href="/wiki/Miniature_snap-action_switch" title="Miniature snap-action switch">switches</a> and <a href="/wiki/Locking_pliers" title="Locking pliers">locking pliers</a>. Out of control, it can cause <a href="/wiki/Tacoma_Narrows_Bridge_(1940)" title="Tacoma Narrows Bridge (1940)">bridges to collapse</a>. Positive feedback in economic systems can cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic_boom" title="Economic boom">boom-then-bust cycles</a>.   A familiar example of positive feedback is the loud squealing or howling sound produced by <a href="/wiki/Audio_feedback" title="Audio feedback">audio feedback</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_address" title="Public address">public address systems</a>: the microphone picks up sound from its own loudspeakers, amplifies it, and sends it through the speakers again.
</p>

The damaged blood vessel wall releases chemicals that initiate the formation of a blood clot through platelet congregation. As more platelets gather, more chemicals are released that speed up the process. The process gets faster and faster until the blood vessel wall is completely sealed and the positive feedback loop has ended.  The exponential form of the graph illustrates the positive feedback mechanism.</div></div></div>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positive_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Positive feedback enhances or amplifies an effect by it having an influence on the process which gave rise to it. For example, when part of an electronic output signal returns to the input, and is in phase with it, the system <a href="/wiki/Gain_(electronics)" title="Gain (electronics)">gain</a> is increased.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The feedback from the outcome to the originating process can be direct, or it can be via other state variables.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theorymodelling_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-theorymodelling-3">[3]</a></sup> Such systems can give rich qualitative behaviors, but whether the feedback is instantaneously positive or negative in sign has an extremely important influence on the results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theorymodelling_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-theorymodelling-3">[3]</a></sup> Positive feedback reinforces and negative feedback moderates the original process. <i>Positive</i> and <i>negative</i> in this sense refer to loop gains greater than or less than zero, and do not imply any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Value_judgement" title="Value judgement">value judgements</a> as to the desirability of the outcomes or effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  A key feature of positive feedback is thus that small disturbances get bigger. When a change occurs in a system, positive feedback causes further change, in the same direction.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic">Basic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positive_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Basic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ideal_feedback_model.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="280" data-file-width="680" height="91" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Ideal_feedback_model.svg/220px-Ideal_feedback_model.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Ideal_feedback_model.svg/330px-Ideal_feedback_model.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Ideal_feedback_model.svg/440px-Ideal_feedback_model.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ideal_feedback_model.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A basic feedback system can be represented by this block diagram. <p>When AB &gt; 1, the system is unstable, so does not have a well-defined gain; the gain may be called infinite.
</p><p>Thus depending on the feedback, state changes can be convergent, or divergent.  The result of positive feedback is to <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/augment" title="wikt:augment">augment</a> changes, so that small perturbations may result in big changes.
</p><p>A system in equilibrium in which there is positive feedback to any change from its current state may be unstable, in which case the equilibrium is said to be in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unstable_equilibrium" title="Unstable equilibrium">unstable equilibrium</a>. The magnitude of the forces that act to move such a system away from its equilibrium are an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Increasing_function" title="Increasing function">increasing function</a> of the "distance" of the state from the equilibrium.
</p><p>Positive feedback does not necessarily imply instability of an equilibrium, for example stable <i>on</i> and <i>off</i> states may exist in positive-feedback architectures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hysteresis">Hysteresis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positive_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hysteresis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hysteresis" title="Hysteresis">Hysteresis</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hysteresis_sharp_curve.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="427" data-file-width="427" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Hysteresis_sharp_curve.svg/220px-Hysteresis_sharp_curve.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Hysteresis_sharp_curve.svg/330px-Hysteresis_sharp_curve.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Hysteresis_sharp_curve.svg/440px-Hysteresis_sharp_curve.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hysteresis_sharp_curve.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hysteresis causes the output value to depend on the history of the input</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Op-Amp_Schmitt_Trigger.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="150" data-file-width="300" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Op-Amp_Schmitt_Trigger.svg/220px-Op-Amp_Schmitt_Trigger.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Op-Amp_Schmitt_Trigger.svg/330px-Op-Amp_Schmitt_Trigger.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Op-Amp_Schmitt_Trigger.svg/440px-Op-Amp_Schmitt_Trigger.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Op-Amp_Schmitt_Trigger.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In a <a href="/wiki/Schmitt_trigger" title="Schmitt trigger">Schmitt trigger</a> circuit, feedback to the non-inverting input of an amplifier pushes the output directly away from the applied voltage towards the maximum or minimum voltage the amplifier can generate.</div></div></div>
<p>In the real world, positive feedback loops typically do not cause ever-increasing growth, but are modified by limiting effects of some sort. According to <a href="/wiki/Donella_Meadows" title="Donella Meadows">Donella Meadows</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>"Positive feedback loops are sources of growth, explosion, erosion, and collapse in systems. A system with an unchecked positive loop ultimately will destroy itself. That’s why there are so few of them. Usually a negative loop will kick in sooner or later."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meadows_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-meadows-10">[10]</a></sup></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p>Hysteresis, in which the starting point affects where the system ends up, can be generated by positive feedback. When the gain of the feedback loop is above 1, then the output moves away from the input: if it is above the input, then it moves towards the nearest positive limit, while if it is below the input then it moves towards the nearest negative limit.
</p><p>Once it reaches the limit, it will be stable. However, if the input goes past the limit,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> then the feedback will change sign<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (June 2012)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata"> – <a href="/wiki/Talk:Positive_feedback#Dubious" title="Talk:Positive feedback">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> and the output will move in the opposite direction until it hits the opposite limit. The system therefore shows <a href="/wiki/Bistability" title="Bistability">bistable</a> behaviour.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positive_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The terms <i>positive</i> and <i>negative</i> were first applied to feedback before <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. The idea of positive feedback was already current in the 1920s with the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Regenerative_circuit" title="Regenerative circuit">regenerative circuit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mindell_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-mindell-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="#CITEREFFriisJensen1924">Friis &amp; Jensen (1924)</a> described regeneration in a set of electronic amplifiers as a case where <i>the "feed-back" action is positive</i> in contrast to negative feed-back action, which they mention only in passing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-friis_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-friis-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Harold_Stephen_Black" title="Harold Stephen Black">Harold Stephen Black</a>'s classic 1934 paper first details the use of negative feedback in electronic amplifiers. According to Black:
</p>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>"Positive feed-back increases the gain of the amplifier, negative feed-back reduces it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-black_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-black-13">[13]</a></sup></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p>According to <a href="#CITEREFMindell2002">Mindell (2002)</a> confusion in the terms arose shortly after this:
</p>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>"...Friis and Jensen had made the same distinction Black used between 'positive feed-back' and 'negative feed-back', based not on the sign of the feedback itself but rather on its effect on the amplifier’s gain. In contrast, Nyquist and Bode, when they built on Black’s work, referred to negative feedback as that with the sign reversed. Black had trouble convincing others of the utility of his invention in part because confusion existed over basic matters of definition."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mindell_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-mindell-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">(<span>p121</span>)</sup></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_and_applications">Examples and applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positive_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Examples and applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_electronics">In electronics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positive_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In electronics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Regenerartive_Receiver-S7300056.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Regenerartive_Receiver-S7300056.JPG/220px-Regenerartive_Receiver-S7300056.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Regenerartive_Receiver-S7300056.JPG/330px-Regenerartive_Receiver-S7300056.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Regenerartive_Receiver-S7300056.JPG/440px-Regenerartive_Receiver-S7300056.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Regenerartive_Receiver-S7300056.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A vintage style regenerative radio receiver. Due to the controlled use of positive feedback, sufficient amplification can be derived from a single <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_tube" title="Vacuum tube">vacuum tube</a> or valve (centre).</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Regenerative_circuit" title="Regenerative circuit">Regenerative circuits</a> were invented and patented in 1914<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> for the amplification and reception of very weak radio signals. Carefully controlled positive feedback around a single <a href="/wiki/Transistor" title="Transistor">transistor</a> amplifier can multiply its <a href="/wiki/Gain_(electronics)" title="Gain (electronics)">gain</a> by 1,000 or more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Therefore, a signal can be amplified 20,000 or even 100,000 times in one stage, that would normally have a gain of only 20 to 50. The problem with regenerative amplifiers working at these very high gains is that they easily become unstable and start to oscillate. The radio operator has to be prepared to tweak the amount of feedback fairly continuously for good reception. Modern radio receivers use the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superheterodyne" title="Superheterodyne">superheterodyne</a> design, with many more amplification stages, but much more stable operation and no positive feedback.
</p><p>The oscillation that can break out in a regenerative radio circuit is used in <a href="/wiki/Electronic_oscillator" title="Electronic oscillator">electronic oscillators</a>. By the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuned_circuit" title="Tuned circuit">tuned circuits</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Piezoelectricity" title="Piezoelectricity">piezoelectric</a> <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystal</a> (commonly <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a>), the signal that is amplified by the positive feedback remains linear and <a href="/wiki/Sine_wave" title="Sine wave">sinusoidal</a>. There are several designs for such <a href="/wiki/Harmonic_oscillator" title="Harmonic oscillator">harmonic oscillators</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Armstrong_oscillator" title="Armstrong oscillator">Armstrong oscillator</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hartley_oscillator" title="Hartley oscillator">Hartley oscillator</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colpitts_oscillator" title="Colpitts oscillator">Colpitts oscillator</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Wien_bridge_oscillator" title="Wien bridge oscillator">Wien bridge oscillator</a>. They all use positive feedback to create oscillations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many electronic circuits, especially amplifiers, incorporate <a href="/wiki/Negative_feedback" title="Negative feedback">negative feedback</a>. This reduces their gain, but improves their linearity, <a href="/wiki/Input_impedance" title="Input impedance">input impedance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Output_impedance" title="Output impedance">output impedance</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(signal_processing)" title="Bandwidth (signal processing)">bandwidth</a>, and stabilises all of these parameters, including the closed-loop gain. These parameters also become less dependent on the details of the amplifying device itself, and more dependent on the feedback components, which are less likely to vary with manufacturing tolerance, age and temperature. The difference between positive and negative feedback for <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">AC</a> signals is one of <a href="/wiki/Phase_(waves)" title="Phase (waves)">phase</a>: if the signal is fed back out of phase, the feedback is negative and if it is in phase the feedback is positive. One problem for amplifier designers who use negative feedback is that some of the components of the circuit will introduce <a href="/wiki/Phase_(waves)#Phase_shift" title="Phase (waves)">phase shift</a> in the feedback path. If there is a frequency (usually a high frequency) where the phase shift reaches 180°, then the designer must ensure that the amplifier gain at that frequency is very low (usually by <a href="/wiki/Low-pass_filter" title="Low-pass filter">low-pass filtering</a>). If the <a href="/wiki/Loop_gain" title="Loop gain">loop gain</a> (the product of the amplifier gain and the extent of the positive feedback) at any frequency is greater than one, then the amplifier will oscillate at that frequency (<a href="/wiki/Barkhausen_stability_criterion" title="Barkhausen stability criterion">Barkhausen stability criterion</a>). Such oscillations are sometimes called <a href="/wiki/Parasitic_oscillation" title="Parasitic oscillation">parasitic oscillations</a>. An amplifier that is stable in one set of conditions can break into parasitic oscillation in another. This may be due to changes in temperature, supply voltage, adjustment of front-panel controls, or even the proximity of a person or other conductive item.
</p><p>Amplifiers may oscillate gently in ways that are hard to detect without an <a href="/wiki/Oscilloscope" title="Oscilloscope">oscilloscope</a>, or the oscillations may be so extensive that only a very distorted or no required signal at all gets through, or that damage occurs. Low frequency parasitic oscillations have been called 'motorboating' due to the similarity to the sound of a low-revving exhaust note.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Smitt_hysteresis_graph.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="121" data-file-width="136" height="196" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Smitt_hysteresis_graph.svg/220px-Smitt_hysteresis_graph.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Smitt_hysteresis_graph.svg/330px-Smitt_hysteresis_graph.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Smitt_hysteresis_graph.svg/440px-Smitt_hysteresis_graph.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Smitt_hysteresis_graph.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The effect of using a Schmitt trigger (B) instead of a comparator (A)</div></div></div>
<p>Many common <a href="/wiki/Digital_electronics" title="Digital electronics">digital electronic</a> circuits employ positive feedback. While normal simple boolean <a href="/wiki/Logic_gate" title="Logic gate">logic gates</a> usually rely simply on gain to push digital signal voltages away from intermediate values to the values that are meant to represent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boolean_logic" title="Boolean logic">boolean</a> '0' and '1', but many more complex gates use feedback. When an input voltage is expected to vary in an <a href="/wiki/Analogue_electronics" title="Analogue electronics">analogue</a> way, but sharp thresholds are required for later digital processing, the <a href="/wiki/Schmitt_trigger" title="Schmitt trigger">Schmitt trigger</a> circuit uses positive feedback to ensure that if the input voltage creeps gently above the threshold, the output is forced smartly and rapidly from one logic state to the other. One of the corollaries of the Schmitt trigger's use of positive feedback is that, should the input voltage move gently down again past the same threshold, the positive feedback will hold the output in the same state with no change. This effect is called <a href="/wiki/Hysteresis" title="Hysteresis">hysteresis</a>: the input voltage has to drop past a different, lower threshold to 'un-latch' the output and reset it to its original digital value. By reducing the extent of the positive feedback, the hysteresis-width can be reduced, but it can not entirely be eradicated. The Schmitt trigger is, to some extent, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latch_(electronics)" title="Latch (electronics)">latching</a> circuit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Positive_feedback_bistable_switch.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="87" data-file-width="217" height="88" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/fd/Positive_feedback_bistable_switch.svg/220px-Positive_feedback_bistable_switch.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/fd/Positive_feedback_bistable_switch.svg/330px-Positive_feedback_bistable_switch.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/fd/Positive_feedback_bistable_switch.svg/440px-Positive_feedback_bistable_switch.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Positive_feedback_bistable_switch.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Positive feedback is a mechanism by which an output is enhanced, such as protein levels. However, in order to avoid any fluctuation in the protein level, the mechanism is inhibited stochastically (I), therefore when the concentration of the activated protein (A) is past the threshold ([I]), the loop mechanism is activated and the concentration of A increases exponentially if d[A]=k [A]</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:R-S_mk2.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="365" data-file-width="500" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/R-S_mk2.gif/220px-R-S_mk2.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/R-S_mk2.gif/330px-R-S_mk2.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/R-S_mk2.gif/440px-R-S_mk2.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:R-S_mk2.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illustration of an R-S ('reset-set') flip-flop made from two digital <a href="/wiki/NOR_gate" title="NOR gate">nor</a> gates with positive feedback. Red and black mean logical '1' and '0', respectively.</div></div></div>
<p>An electronic <a href="/wiki/Flip-flop_(electronics)" title="Flip-flop (electronics)">flip-flop</a>, or "latch", or "bistable <a href="/wiki/Multivibrator" title="Multivibrator">multivibrator</a>", is a circuit that due to high positive feedback is not stable in a balanced or intermediate state. Such a bistable circuit is the basis of one <a href="/wiki/Bit" title="Bit">bit</a> of electronic <a href="/wiki/Computer_memory" title="Computer memory">memory</a>.  The flip-flop uses a pair of amplifiers, transistors, or  logic gates connected to each other so that positive feedback maintains the state of the circuit in one of two unbalanced stable states after the input signal has been removed, until a suitable alternative signal is applied to change the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Computer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Random_access_memory" title="Random access memory">random access memory</a> (RAM) can be made in this way, with one latching circuit for each bit of memory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Thermal_runaway" title="Thermal runaway">Thermal runaway</a> occurs in electronic systems because some aspect of a circuit is allowed to pass more current when it gets hotter, then the hotter it gets, the more current it passes, which heats it some more and so it passes yet more current. The effects are usually catastrophic for the device in question. If devices have to be used near to their maximum power-handling capacity, and thermal runaway is possible or likely under certain conditions, improvements can usually be achieved by careful design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Technics_SL-1210MK2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Technics_SL-1210MK2.jpg/220px-Technics_SL-1210MK2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Technics_SL-1210MK2.jpg/330px-Technics_SL-1210MK2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Technics_SL-1210MK2.jpg/440px-Technics_SL-1210MK2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Technics_SL-1210MK2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A phonograph turntable is prone to acoustic feedback</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sound_recording_and_reproduction" title="Sound recording and reproduction">Audio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Video" title="Video">video</a> systems can demonstrate positive feedback. If a <a href="/wiki/Microphone" title="Microphone">microphone</a> picks up the amplified sound output of <a href="/wiki/Loudspeaker" title="Loudspeaker">loudspeakers</a> in the same circuit, then howling and screeching sounds of <a href="/wiki/Audio_feedback" title="Audio feedback">audio feedback</a> (at up to the maximum power capacity of the amplifier) will be heard, as random noise is re-amplified by positive feedback and <a href="/wiki/Filter_(signal_processing)" title="Filter (signal processing)">filtered</a> by the characteristics of the audio system and the room.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Audio_and_live_music">Audio and live music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positive_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Audio and live music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Audio_feedback" title="Audio feedback">Audio feedback</a> (also known as acoustic feedback, simply as feedback, or the Larsen effect) is a special kind of positive feedback which occurs when a sound loop exists between an audio input (for example, a <a href="/wiki/Microphone" title="Microphone">microphone</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guitar_pickup" title="Guitar pickup">guitar pickup</a>) and an audio output (for example, a loudly-amplified <a href="/wiki/Loudspeaker" title="Loudspeaker">loudspeaker</a>). In this example, a signal received by the microphone is <a href="/wiki/Amplifier" title="Amplifier">amplified</a> and passed out of the loudspeaker. The sound from the loudspeaker can then be received by the microphone again, amplified further, and then passed out through the loudspeaker again.  The <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a> of the resulting sound is determined by resonance frequencies in the microphone, amplifier, and loudspeaker, the acoustics of the room, the directional pick-up and emission patterns of the microphone and loudspeaker, and the distance between them. For small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PA_system" title="PA system">PA systems</a> the sound is readily recognized as a loud squeal or screech.
</p><p>Feedback is almost always considered undesirable when it occurs with a singer's or public speaker's microphone at an event using a <a href="/wiki/Sound_reinforcement_system" title="Sound reinforcement system">sound reinforcement system</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PA_system" title="PA system">PA system</a>. <a href="/wiki/Audio_engineer" title="Audio engineer">Audio engineers</a> use various electronic devices, such as equalizers and, since the 1990s, automatic feedback detection devices to prevent these unwanted squeals or screeching sounds, which detract from the audience's enjoyment of the event. On the other hand, since the 1960s, <a href="/wiki/Electric_guitar" title="Electric guitar">electric guitar</a> players in <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock music</a> bands using loud <a href="/wiki/Guitar_amplifier" title="Guitar amplifier">guitar amplifiers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Distortion_(music)" title="Distortion (music)">distortion</a> effects have intentionally created guitar feedback to create a desirable musical effect.  "I Feel Fine" by the Beatles marks one of the earliest examples of the use of feedback as a recording effect in popular music. It starts with a single, percussive <a href="/wiki/Audio_feedback" title="Audio feedback">feedback</a> note produced by plucking the A string on Lennon's guitar. Artists such as the Kinks and the Who had already used feedback live, but Lennon remained proud of the fact that the Beatles were perhaps the first group to deliberately put it on vinyl. In one of his last interviews, he said, "I defy anybody to find a record—unless it's some old blues record in 1922—that uses feedback that way."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>The principles of audio feedback were first discovered by Danish scientist <a href="/wiki/S%C3%B8ren_Absalon_Larsen" title="Søren Absalon Larsen">Søren Absalon Larsen</a>. Microphones are not the only transducers subject to this effect. <a href="/wiki/Phonograph" title="Phonograph">Record deck</a> <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_cartridge" title="Magnetic cartridge">pickup cartridges</a> can do the same, usually in the low frequency range below about 100 Hz, manifesting as a low rumble. <a href="/wiki/Jimi_Hendrix" title="Jimi Hendrix">Jimi Hendrix</a> was an innovator in the intentional use of guitar feedback in his <a href="/wiki/Guitar_solo" title="Guitar solo">guitar solos</a> to create unique sound effects. He helped develop the controlled and musical use of audio feedback in <a href="/wiki/Electric_guitar" title="Electric guitar">electric guitar</a> playing,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and later <a href="/wiki/Brian_May" title="Brian May">Brian May</a> was a famous proponent of the technique.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Video">Video</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positive_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Video">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Similarly, if a <a href="/wiki/Video_camera" title="Video camera">video camera</a> is pointed at a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_monitor" title="Video monitor">monitor</a> screen that is displaying the camera's own signal, then repeating patterns can be formed on the screen by positive feedback. This video feedback effect was used in the opening sequences to the <a href="/wiki/Doctor_Who_(season_1)" title="Doctor Who (season 1)">first</a> <a href="/wiki/Doctor_Who_(season_10)" title="Doctor Who (season 10)">ten</a> series of the television program <i><a href="/wiki/Doctor_Who" title="Doctor Who">Doctor Who</a></i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Switches">Switches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positive_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Switches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_switch" title="Electrical switch">electrical switches</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Bimetallic_strip" title="Bimetallic strip">bimetallic strip</a> based thermostats, the switch usually has hysteresis in the switching action. In these cases hysteresis is mechanically achieved via positive feedback within a tipping point mechanism. The positive feedback action minimises the length of time arcing occurs for during the switching and also holds the contacts in an open or closed state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_biology">In biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positive_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: In biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Positive_Feedback-_Childbirth_(1).svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="798" data-file-width="512" height="343" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Positive_Feedback-_Childbirth_%281%29.svg/220px-Positive_Feedback-_Childbirth_%281%29.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Positive_Feedback-_Childbirth_%281%29.svg/330px-Positive_Feedback-_Childbirth_%281%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Positive_Feedback-_Childbirth_%281%29.svg/440px-Positive_Feedback-_Childbirth_%281%29.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Positive_Feedback-_Childbirth_(1).svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Positive feedback is the amplification of a body’s response to a stimulus. For example, in childbirth, when the head of the fetus pushes up against the cervix (1) it stimulates a nerve impulse from the cervix to the brain (2). When the brain is notified, it signals the pituitary gland to release a hormone called <a href="/wiki/Oxytocin" title="Oxytocin">oxytocin</a>(3). Oxytocin is then carried via the bloodstream to the <a href="/wiki/Uterus" title="Uterus">uterus</a> (4) causing contractions, pushing the fetus towards the cervix eventually inducing childbirth.</div></div></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="In_physiology">In physiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positive_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: In physiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A number of examples of positive feedback systems may be found in <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiology</a>.
</p>
<ul><li>One example is the onset of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contraction_(childbirth)" title="Contraction (childbirth)">contractions</a> in <a href="/wiki/Childbirth" title="Childbirth">childbirth</a>, known as the <a href="/wiki/Ferguson_reflex" title="Ferguson reflex">Ferguson reflex</a>. When a contraction occurs, the hormone <a href="/wiki/Oxytocin" title="Oxytocin">oxytocin</a> causes a nerve stimulus, which stimulates the <a href="/wiki/Hypothalamus" title="Hypothalamus">hypothalamus</a> to produce more oxytocin, which increases uterine contractions. This results in contractions increasing in <a href="/wiki/Amplitude" title="Amplitude">amplitude</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guyton1991_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guyton1991-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">(<span>pp924–925</span>)</sup></li>
<li>Another example is the process of <a href="/wiki/Coagulation" title="Coagulation">blood clotting</a>. The loop is initiated when injured tissue releases signal chemicals that activate platelets in the blood. An activated platelet releases chemicals to activate more platelets, causing a rapid cascade and the formation of a blood clot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guyton1991_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guyton1991-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">(<span>pp392–394</span>)</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lactation" title="Lactation">Lactation</a> also involves positive feedback in that as the baby suckles on the nipple there is a nerve response into the spinal cord and up into the hypothalamus of the brain, which then stimulates the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pituitary" title="Pituitary">pituitary</a> gland to produce more <a href="/wiki/Prolactin" title="Prolactin">prolactin</a> to produce more milk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guyton1991_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-Guyton1991-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">(<span>p926</span>)</sup></li>
<li>A spike in <a href="/wiki/Estrogen" title="Estrogen">estrogen</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Follicular_phase" title="Follicular phase">follicular phase</a> of the menstrual cycle causes <a href="/wiki/Ovulation" title="Ovulation">ovulation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guyton1991_26-3"><a href="#cite_note-Guyton1991-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">(<span>p907</span>)</sup></li>
<li>The generation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nerve_signal" title="Nerve signal">nerve signals</a> is another example, in which the membrane of a nerve fibre causes slight leakage of sodium ions through sodium channels, resulting in a change in the membrane potential, which in turn causes more opening of channels, and so on. So a slight initial leakage results in an explosion of sodium leakage which creates the nerve <a href="/wiki/Action_potential" title="Action potential">action potential</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guyton1991_26-4"><a href="#cite_note-Guyton1991-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">(<span>p59</span>)</sup></li>
<li>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Excitation%E2%80%93contraction_coupling" title="Excitation–contraction coupling">excitation–contraction coupling</a> of the heart, an increase in intracellular calcium ions to the cardiac myocyte is detected by ryanodine receptors in the membrane of the sarcoplasmic reticulum which transport calcium out into the cytosol in a positive feedback physiological response.</li></ul>
<p>In most cases, such feedback loops culminate in counter-signals being released that suppress or break the loop. Childbirth contractions stop when the baby is out of the mother's body. Chemicals break down the blood clot. Lactation stops when the baby no longer nurses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guyton1991_26-5"><a href="#cite_note-Guyton1991-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="In_gene_regulation">In gene regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positive_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: In gene regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Positive feedback is a well studied phenomenon in gene regulation, where it is most often associated with <a href="/wiki/Bistability" title="Bistability">bistability</a>. Positive feedback occurs when a gene activates itself directly or indirectly via a double negative feedback loop. Genetic engineers have constructed and tested simple positive feedback networks in bacteria to demonstrate the concept of bistability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hasty2002_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hasty2002-27">[27]</a></sup> A classic example of positive feedback is the <a href="/wiki/Lac_operon" title="Lac operon">lac operon</a> in <i>E. coli</i>. Positive feedback plays an integral role in cellular differentiation, development, and cancer progression, and therefore, positive feedback in gene regulation can have significant physiological consequences. Random motions in <a href="/wiki/Molecular_dynamics" title="Molecular dynamics">molecular dynamics</a> coupled with positive feedback can trigger interesting effects, such as create population of phenotypically different cells from the same parent cell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Veening2008_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Veening2008-28">[28]</a></sup> This happens because noise can become amplified by positive feedback. Positive feedback can also occur in other forms of <a href="/wiki/Cell_signaling" title="Cell signaling">cell signaling</a>, such as enzyme kinetics or metabolic pathways.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christoph2001_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Christoph2001-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="In_evolutionary_biology">In evolutionary biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positive_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: In evolutionary biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Positive feedback loops have been used to describe aspects of the dynamics of change in biological <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a>.  For example, beginning at the macro level, <a href="/wiki/Alfred_J._Lotka" title="Alfred J. Lotka">Alfred J. Lotka</a> (1945) argued that the evolution of the species was most essentially a matter of selection that fed back energy flows to capture more and more energy for use by living systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lotka1945_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lotka1945-30">[30]</a></sup>  At the human level, <a href="/wiki/Richard_D._Alexander" title="Richard D. Alexander">Richard D. Alexander</a> (1989) proposed that social competition between and within human groups fed back to the selection of intelligence thus constantly producing more and more refined human intelligence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alexander1989_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alexander1989-31">[31]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Crespi" title="Bernard Crespi">Crespi</a> (2004) discussed several other examples of positive feedback loops in evolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crespi2004_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crespi2004-32">[32]</a></sup>  The analogy of <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_arms_race" title="Evolutionary arms race">Evolutionary arms races</a> provide further examples of positive feedback in biological systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blindwatchmaker_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blindwatchmaker-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Phanerozoic_Biodiversity.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="464" data-file-width="784" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Phanerozoic_Biodiversity.svg/300px-Phanerozoic_Biodiversity.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Phanerozoic_Biodiversity.svg/450px-Phanerozoic_Biodiversity.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Phanerozoic_Biodiversity.svg/600px-Phanerozoic_Biodiversity.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Phanerozoic_Biodiversity.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>During the Phanerozoic the <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> shows a steady but not monotonic increase from near zero to several thousands of genera.</div></div></div>
<p>It has been shown that changes in <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Phanerozoic" title="Phanerozoic">Phanerozoic</a> correlate much better with hyperbolic model (widely used in <a href="/wiki/Demography" title="Demography">demography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Macrosociology" title="Macrosociology">macrosociology</a>) than with <a href="/wiki/Exponential_growth" title="Exponential growth">exponential</a> and <a href="/wiki/Logistic_function" title="Logistic function">logistic</a> models (traditionally used in <a href="/wiki/Population_biology" title="Population biology">population biology</a> and extensively applied to <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> as well). The latter models imply that changes in diversity are guided by a first-order positive feedback (more ancestors, more descendants) and/or a <a href="/wiki/Negative_feedback" title="Negative feedback">negative feedback</a> arising from resource limitation. Hyperbolic model implies a second-order positive feedback. The hyperbolic pattern of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_population_growth" title="World population growth">world population growth</a> has been demonstrated (see below) to arise from a second-order positive feedback between the population size and the rate of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Technological_growth" title="Technological growth">technological growth</a>. The hyperbolic character of biodiversity growth can be similarly accounted for by a positive feedback between the diversity and community structure complexity. It has been suggested that the similarity between the curves of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> and human population probably comes from the fact that both are derived from the interference of the hyperbolic trend (produced by the positive feedback) with cyclical and stochastic dynamics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Immune_system">Immune system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positive_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Immune system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cytokine_storm" title="Cytokine storm">cytokine storm</a>, or <b>hypercytokinemia</b> is a potentially fatal immune reaction consisting of a positive feedback loop between <a href="/wiki/Cytokine" title="Cytokine">cytokines</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immune_cell" title="Immune cell">immune cells</a>, with highly elevated levels of various cytokines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-osterholm_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-osterholm-36">[36]</a></sup> In normal immune function, positive feedback loops can be utilized to enhance the action of B lymphocytes. When a B cell binds its antibodies to an antigen and becomes activated, it begins releasing antibodies and secreting a complement protein called C3. Both C3 and a B cell's antibodies can bind to a pathogen, and when a B cell has its antibodies bind to a pathogen with C3, it speeds up that B cell's secretion of more antibodies and more C3, thus creating a positive feedback loop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cell_death">Cell death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positive_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Cell death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Apoptosis" title="Apoptosis">Apoptosis</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Caspase" title="Caspase">caspase</a>-mediated process of cellular death, whose aim is the removal of long-lived or damaged cells. A failure of this process has been implicated in prominent conditions such as <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a> or <a href="/wiki/Parkinson%27s_disease" title="Parkinson's disease">Parkinson's disease</a>. The very core of the apoptotic process is the auto-activation of caspases, which may be modeled via a positive-feedback loop. This positive feedback exerts an auto-activation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Effector_caspase" title="Effector caspase">effector caspase</a> by means of intermediate caspases. When isolated from the rest of apoptotic pathway, this positive-feedback presents only one stable steady state, regardless of the number of intermediate activation steps of the effector caspase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">[9]</a></sup> When this core process is complemented with inhibitors and enhancers of caspases effects, this process presents bistability, thereby modeling the alive and dying states of a cell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_psychology">In psychology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positive_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: In psychology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Winner (1996) described gifted children as driven by positive feedback loops involving setting their own learning course, this feeding back satisfaction, thus further setting their learning goals to higher levels and so on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Winner1996_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Winner1996-39">[39]</a></sup>  Winner termed this positive feedback loop as a "rage to master."  Vandervert (2009a, 2009b) proposed that the <a href="/wiki/Child_prodigy" title="Child prodigy">child prodigy</a> can be explained in terms of a positive feedback loop between the output of thinking/performing in <a href="/wiki/Working_memory" title="Working memory">working memory</a>, which then is fed to the <a href="/wiki/Cerebellum" title="Cerebellum">cerebellum</a> where it is streamlined, and then fed back to working memory thus steadily increasing the quantitative and qualitative output of working memory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vandervert2009a_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vandervert2009a-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vandervert2009b_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vandervert2009b-41">[41]</a></sup>  Vandervert also argued that this working memory/cerebellar positive feedback loop was responsible for <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a> evolution in working memory.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_economics">In economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positive_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: In economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Markets_with_social_influence">Markets with social influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positive_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Markets with social influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Product recommendations and information about past purchases have been shown to influence consumers choices significantly whether it is for music, movie, book, technological, and other type of products. Social influence often induces a rich-get-richer phenomenon (<a href="/wiki/Matthew_effect" title="Matthew effect">Matthew effect</a>) where popular products tend to become even more popular.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-altszyler2017_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-altszyler2017-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Market_dynamics">Market dynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positive_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Market dynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>According to the theory of <a href="/wiki/Reflexivity_(social_theory)" title="Reflexivity (social theory)">reflexivity</a> advanced by <a href="/wiki/George_Soros" title="George Soros">George Soros</a>, price changes are driven by a positive feedback process whereby investors' expectations are influenced by price movements so their behaviour acts to reinforce movement in that direction until it becomes unsustainable, whereupon the feedback drives prices in the opposite direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Systemic_risk">Systemic risk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positive_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Systemic risk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Systemic_risk" title="Systemic risk">Systemic risk</a> is the risk that an amplification or leverage or positive feedback process presents to a system. This is usually unknown, and under certain conditions this process can amplify exponentially and rapidly lead to destructive or chaotic behavior.  A <a href="/wiki/Ponzi_scheme" title="Ponzi scheme">Ponzi scheme</a> is a good example of a positive-feedback system: funds from new investors are used to pay out unusually high returns, which in turn attract more new investors, causing rapid growth toward collapse. <a href="/wiki/W._Brian_Arthur" title="W. Brian Arthur">W. Brian Arthur</a> has also studied and written on positive feedback in the economy (e.g. W. Brian Arthur, 1990).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hyman_Minsky" title="Hyman Minsky">Hyman Minsky</a> proposed a theory that certain credit expansion practices could make a market economy into "a deviation amplifying system" that could suddenly collapse,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-working_paper_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-working_paper-45">[45]</a></sup> sometimes called a "<a href="/wiki/Minsky_moment" title="Minsky moment">Minsky moment</a>".
</p><p>Simple systems that clearly separate the inputs from the outputs are not prone to <a href="/wiki/Systemic_risk" title="Systemic risk">systemic risk</a>.  This risk is more likely as the complexity of the system increases, because it becomes more difficult to see or analyze all the possible combinations of variables in the system even under careful stress testing conditions.  The more efficient a complex system is, the more likely it is to be prone to systemic risks, because it takes only a small amount of deviation to disrupt the system.  Therefore, well-designed complex systems generally have built-in features to avoid this condition, such as a small amount of friction, or resistance, or inertia, or time delay to decouple the outputs from the inputs within the system. These factors amount to an inefficiency, but they are necessary to avoid instabilities.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/2010_Flash_Crash" title="2010 Flash Crash">2010 Flash Crash</a> incident was blamed on the practice of <a href="/wiki/High-frequency_trading" title="High-frequency trading">high-frequency trading</a> (HFT),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flashreport_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-flashreport-46">[46]</a></sup> although whether HFT really increases systemic risk remains controversial.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_population_growth">Human population growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positive_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Human population growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_population_growth" title="Human population growth">human population growth</a></div>
<p>Agriculture and human population can be considered to be in a positive feedback mode,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2003_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown2003-47">[47]</a></sup> which means that one drives the other with increasing intensity. It is suggested that this positive feedback system will end sometime with a catastrophe, as modern agriculture is using up all of the easily available phosphate and is resorting to highly efficient monocultures which are more susceptible to <a href="/wiki/Systemic_risk" title="Systemic risk">systemic risk</a>.
</p><p>Technological innovation and human population can be similarly considered, and this has been offered as an explanation for the apparent <a href="/wiki/Hyperbolic_growth" title="Hyperbolic growth">hyperbolic growth</a> of the human population in the past, instead of a simpler <a href="/wiki/Exponential_growth" title="Exponential growth">exponential growth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
It is proposed that the growth rate is accelerating because of second-order positive feedback between population and technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mgc_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-mgc-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">(<span>p133–160</span>)</sup> Technological growth increases the carrying capacity of land for people, which leads to a growing population, and this in turn drives further technological growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mgc_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-mgc-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">(<span>p146</span>)</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Prejudice.2C_social_institutions_and_poverty"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Prejudice,_social_institutions_and_poverty">Prejudice, social institutions and poverty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positive_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Prejudice, social institutions and poverty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gunnar_Myrdal" title="Gunnar Myrdal">Gunnar Myrdal</a> described a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vicious_circle" title="Vicious circle">vicious circle</a> of increasing inequalities, and poverty, which is known as "<a href="/wiki/Circular_cumulative_causation" title="Circular cumulative causation">circular cumulative causation</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_meteorology">In meteorology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positive_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: In meteorology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">Drought</a> intensifies through positive feedback. A lack of rain decreases soil moisture, which kills plants and/or causes them to release less water through <a href="/wiki/Transpiration" title="Transpiration">transpiration</a>. Both factors limit <a href="/wiki/Evapotranspiration" title="Evapotranspiration">evapotranspiration</a>, the process by which water vapor is added to the atmosphere from the surface, and add dry dust to the atmosphere, which absorbs water. Less water vapor means both low <a href="/wiki/Dew_point" title="Dew point">dew point</a> temperatures and more efficient daytime heating, decreasing the chances of humidity in the atmosphere leading to cloud formation. Lastly, without clouds, there cannot be rain, and the loop is complete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_climatology">In climatology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positive_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: In climatology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_feedback" title="Climate change feedback">Climate change feedback</a></div>
<p>Climate "forcings" may push a climate system in the direction of warming or cooling,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> for example, increased atmospheric concentrations of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> cause warming at the surface. Forcings are external to the climate system and feedbacks are internal processes of the system. Some feedback mechanisms act in relative isolation to the rest of the climate system while others are tightly coupled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Forcings, feedbacks and the dynamics of the climate system determine how much and how fast the climate changes. The main positive feedback in <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> is the tendency of warming to increase the amount of water vapor in the atmosphere, which in turn leads to further warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> The main negative feedback comes from the <a href="/wiki/Stefan%E2%80%93Boltzmann_law" title="Stefan–Boltzmann law">Stefan–Boltzmann law</a>, the amount of heat radiated from the Earth into space is proportional to the fourth power of the temperature of Earth's surface and atmosphere.
</p><p>Other examples of positive feedback subsystems in climatology include:
</p>
<ul><li>A warmer atmosphere will melt ice and this changes the <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> which further warms the atmosphere.</li>
<li>Methane hydrates can be unstable so that a warming ocean could release more <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, which is also a greenhouse gas.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">Peat</a>, occurring naturally in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peat_bog" title="Peat bog">peat bogs</a>, contains carbon.  When peat dries it <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Decomposes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Decomposes (page does not exist)">decomposes</a>, and may additionally burn. Peat also releases <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a>.</li>
<li>Global warming affects the cloud distribution. Clouds at higher altitudes result in more infrared radiation reflected back at the Earth's surface than immediately reflected back into space.</li></ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC) <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">Fourth Assessment Report</a> states that "Anthropogenic warming could lead to some effects that are abrupt or irreversible, depending upon the rate and magnitude of the climate change."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_sociology">In sociology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positive_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: In sociology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Self-fulfilling_prophecy" title="Self-fulfilling prophecy">self-fulfilling prophecy</a> is a social positive feedback loop between beliefs and behavior: if enough people believe that something is true, their behavior can make it true, and observations of their behavior may in turn increase belief. A classic example is a <a href="/wiki/Bank_run" title="Bank run">bank run</a>.
</p><p>Another sociological example of positive feedback is the <a href="/wiki/Network_effect" title="Network effect">network effect</a>. When more people are encouraged to join a network this increases the reach of the network therefore the network expands ever more quickly. A <a href="/wiki/Viral_video" title="Viral video">viral video</a> is an example of the network effect in which <a href="/wiki/Hyperlink" title="Hyperlink">links</a> to a popular video are shared and redistributed, ensuring that more people see the video and then re-publish the links. This is the basis for many social phenomena, including <a href="/wiki/Ponzi_scheme" title="Ponzi scheme">Ponzi schemes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chain_letter" title="Chain letter">chain letters</a>. In many cases population size is the limiting factor to the feedback effect.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_chemistry">In chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positive_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: In chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>If a chemical reaction causes <a href="/wiki/Exothermic_reaction" title="Exothermic reaction">the release of heat</a>, and the reaction itself <a href="/wiki/Reaction_rate" title="Reaction rate">happens faster</a> at higher temperatures, then there is a high likelihood of positive feedback. If the heat produced is not removed from the reactants fast enough, <a href="/wiki/Thermal_runaway" title="Thermal runaway">thermal runaway</a> can occur and very quickly lead to a chemical <a href="/wiki/Explosion" title="Explosion">explosion</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Conservation">In Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Positive_feedback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: In Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many wildlife are hunted for their parts which can be quite valuable. The closer to extinction that targeted species become, the higher the price there is on their parts. This is an example of positive feedback.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p>
