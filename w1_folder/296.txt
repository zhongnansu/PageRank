<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Pop Rocks</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Pop Rocks</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Candy" title="Candy">candy</a>, owned by Zeta Espacial S.A.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Pop Rocks ingredients include <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lactose" title="Lactose">lactose</a> (milk sugar), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flavoring" title="Flavoring">flavoring</a>. It differs from typical hard candy in that it creates a small popping  reaction when it dissolves in one's mouth.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_history">Background and history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pop_Rocks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background and history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The concept was patented by <a href="/wiki/General_Foods" title="General Foods">General Foods</a> research <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">chemists</a> Leon T. Kremzner and <a href="/wiki/William_A._Mitchell" title="William A. Mitchell">William A. Mitchell</a> on December 12, 1961 (U.S. patent #3,012,893),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> but the candy was not offered to the public until 1975 by <a href="/wiki/General_Foods" title="General Foods">General Foods</a>, which in 1983 withdrew it, citing its lack of success in the marketplace, and its relatively short <a href="/wiki/Shelf_life" title="Shelf life">shelf life</a>.
</p><p>Distribution was initially controlled to ensure freshness; but with its increasing popularity, unauthorized redistribution from market to market resulted in out-of-date product reaching consumers. After that, Kraft Foods licensed the Pop Rocks brand  to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zeta_Espacial&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zeta Espacial (page does not exist)">Zeta Espacial</a> S.A. who continued manufacturing the product under Kraft's license. Eventually Zeta Espacial S.A. became the brand's owner and sole manufacturer. Pop Rocks is distributed in the U.S. by Pop Rocks Inc. (Atlanta, Georgia) and by Zeta Espacial S.A. (Barcelona, Spain) in the rest of the world. Zeta Espacial S.A. also sells popping candy internationally under other brands including Peta Zetas, Fizz Wiz and Magic Gum.
</p><p>In 2008, Dr. Marvin J. Rudolph, who led the group assigned to bring Pop Rocks out of the laboratory and into the manufacturing plant, wrote a history of Pop Rocks development.  The book, titled <i>Pop Rocks: The Inside Story of America's Revolutionary Candy</i>, was based on interviews with food technologists, engineers, marketing managers, and members of Billy Mitchell's family, along with the author's experience. In the book, Dr. Rudolph points out that the Turkish company HLEKS Popping Candy flooded the market with popping candy in the year 2000.
</p><p>A similar product, <i>Cosmic Candy</i>, previously called <i>Space Dust</i>, was in powdered form and was also manufactured by General Foods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2012, Cadbury Schweppes Pty. Ltd. (in Australia) began producing a chocolate product named "Marvellous Creations Jelly Popping Candy Beanies" which contains popping candy, jelly beans and beanies (candy covered chocolate).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> By 2013 Whittakers (New Zealand) had also released a local product (white chocolate with a local carbonated drink "Lemon and Paeroa' or "L&amp;P" for short). Prominent British chef <a href="/wiki/Heston_Blumenthal" title="Heston Blumenthal">Heston Blumenthal</a> has also made several desserts incorporating popping candy, both for the peculiar sensory experience of the popping and for the nostalgia value of using an ingredient popular in the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacturing">Manufacturing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pop_Rocks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Manufacturing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The candy is made by mixing its ingredients and heating them until they melt into a syrup, then exposing the mixture to pressurized <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> gas (about 600 <a href="/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch" title="Pounds per square inch">pounds per square inch</a> or 40 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">bar</a>) and allowing it to cool. The process causes tiny high-pressure bubbles to be trapped inside the candy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> When it is placed in the mouth and comes into contact with saliva the candy breaks and dissolves, releasing the carbon dioxide from the bubbles, resulting in a popping and sizzling sound and leaving a slight tingling sensation. It also releases carbon dioxide when coming in contact with any liquid. The bubbles in the candy pieces can be viewed using a microscope.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_legend">Urban legend</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pop_Rocks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Urban legend">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rumors persisted that eating Pop Rocks and drinking <a href="/wiki/Soft_drink" title="Soft drink">soda</a> would cause a person's stomach to boil and explode.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snopes_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-snopes-10">[10]</a></sup> This was, in part, caused by the false assumption that Pop Rocks contain an acid/base mixture (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baking_soda" title="Baking soda">baking soda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vinegar" title="Vinegar">vinegar</a>) which produces large volumes of gas when mixed through chewing and saliva.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> One of these <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">myths</a> involved a character named <a href="/wiki/Little_Mikey" title="Little Mikey">Mikey</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Life_(cereal)" title="Life (cereal)">Life cereal</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_commercial" title="Television commercial">commercials</a>. Mikey, played by child actor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Gilchrist_(actor)" title="John Gilchrist (actor)">John Gilchrist</a>, was falsely rumored to have died after eating a Pop Rocks and <a href="/wiki/Coca-Cola" title="Coca-Cola">Coca-Cola</a> mixture—namely, a six-pack of Coca-Cola and six pouches of Pop Rocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snopes_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-snopes-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Though the confection had been extensively tested and found safe, the carbonated candy still alarmed residents in <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>. The Food and Drug Administration set up a <a href="/wiki/Hotline" title="Hotline">hotline</a> there to assure anxious parents that the fizzing candy would not cause their children to choke.  General Foods was battling the "exploding kid" rumors as early as 1979. General Foods sent letters to school principals,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rudolph_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-rudolph-12">[12]</a></sup> created an open letter to parents,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rudolph2_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-rudolph2-13">[13]</a></sup> took out advertisements in major publications and sent the confection's inventor on the road to explain that a Pop Rocks package contains less gas (namely, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, the same gas used in all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonated_beverages" title="Carbonated beverages">carbonated beverages</a>) than half a can of soda.
</p><p>Because of the unique flavor of the legend, and the duration of its perpetuation, the story has appeared in many other forms of media and fiction. On the <a href="/wiki/List_of_MythBusters_pilot_episodes#Pop_Rocks" title="List of MythBusters pilot episodes">very first episode of <i>MythBusters</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/Adam_Savage" title="Adam Savage">Adam Savage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Hyneman" title="Jamie Hyneman">Jamie Hyneman</a> put the Mikey rumor to the test by mixing six packs of Pop Rocks and a six-pack of cola inside a pig's stomach, complete with enough hydrochloric acid to simulate the acid inside a human stomach. Despite the pig stomach growing to three times its initial size, it did not blow up even after time was allotted for digestion. In another stomach used as an experimental counterpart, only a large amount of sodium bicarbonate along with acid and soda (and without any Pop Rocks) was able to cause a gastric rupture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The broadcast included interview clips with Pop Rocks Inc. vice president Fernando Arguis explaining the candy and the myth, and Adam later alluded to the myth at a presentation at <a href="/wiki/Rensselaer_Polytechnic_Institute" title="Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute">Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute</a> by showing that Pop Rocks and soda—albeit in a smaller amount—in his own stomach was not fatal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
