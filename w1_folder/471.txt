<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Kaya identity</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>Kaya identity</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Identity_(mathematics)" title="Identity (mathematics)">identity</a> stating that the total emission level of the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> can be expressed as the product of four factors: human <a href="/wiki/World_population" title="World population">population</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GDP" title="GDP">GDP</a> <a href="/wiki/Per_capita" title="Per capita">per capita</a>, <a href="/wiki/Energy_intensity" title="Energy intensity">energy intensity</a> (per unit of GDP), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_intensity" title="Carbon intensity">carbon intensity</a> (emissions per unit of energy consumed).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is a concrete form of the more general <a href="/wiki/I_%3D_PAT" title="I = PAT">I = PAT</a> equation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipccEmissionScenarios_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ipccEmissionScenarios-2">[2]</a></sup> relating factors that determine the level of <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">human impact</a> on climate. The Kaya identity is both simple and tricky, as it can be reduced to only two terms, but it is developed so that the carbon emission calculation becomes easy, as per the available data, or generally in which format the data is available.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kaya_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Kaya identity was developed by Japanese energy economist Yoichi Kaya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>  It is the subject of his book <i>Environment, Energy, and Economy: strategies for sustainability</i> co-authored with Keiichi Yokobori as the output of the <i>Conference on Global Environment, Energy, and Economic Development (1993 : Tokyo, Japan).</i>
</p><p>Kaya identity is expressed in the form:

</p><p><i>F</i> is global CO2 emissions from human sources

</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_IPCC_reports">Use in IPCC reports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kaya_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Use in IPCC reports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Kaya identity plays a core role in the development of future emissions <a href="/wiki/Scenario" title="Scenario">scenarios</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">IPCC</a> <a href="/wiki/Special_Report_on_Emissions_Scenarios" title="Special Report on Emissions Scenarios">Special Report on Emissions Scenarios</a>. The scenarios set out a range of assumed conditions for future development of each of the four inputs. Population growth projections are available independently from demographic research; GDP per capita trends are available from economic statistics and econometrics; similarly for energy intensity and emission levels. The projected carbon emissions can drive <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_models" title="Climate models">climate models</a> to predict future CO<sub>2</sub> concentration and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipccEmissionScenarios_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ipccEmissionScenarios-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_other_scientific_analysis">Use in other scientific analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kaya_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Use in other scientific analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Kaya identity is reviewed in a 2002 paper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2007 article<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> uses the Kaya Identity in its analysis of recent trends in carbon emissions, and finds: 
</p>
<blockquote><p>... cessation or reversal of earlier declining trends in the energy intensity of gross domestic product (GDP) (energy/GDP) and the carbon intensity of energy (emissions/energy), coupled with continuing increases in population and per-capita GDP. Nearly constant or slightly increasing trends in the carbon intensity of energy have been recently observed in both developed and developing regions. No region is decarbonizing its energy supply.</p></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kaya_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Bill_Gates" title="Bill Gates">Bill Gates</a> used a form of the Identity, without attribution, at a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TED_Talk" title="TED Talk">TED Talk</a> called <a class="external text" href="https://www.ted.com/talks/bill_gates/transcript?language=en" rel="nofollow">Innovating to zero!</a>. Writing in <i><a href="/wiki/ThinkProgress" title="ThinkProgress">ThinkProgress</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_J._Romm" title="Joseph J. Romm">Joseph J. Romm</a> disputed the validity of Gates' arguments, as well as clarifying the key idea behind the identity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critique">Critique</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kaya_identity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Critique">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Kaya identity and its applications in climate control policies has been criticized by <a href="/wiki/Mario_Bunge" title="Mario Bunge">Mario Bunge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bunge2012_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bunge2012-8">[8]</a></sup> Bunge points out that the Kaya identity is a tautology, because it is nothing but a rewrite of the identity :<span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\displaystyle F=F}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
<mi>F</mi>
<mo>=</mo>
<mi>F</mi>
</mstyle>
</mrow>
<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle F=F}</annotation>
</semantics>
</math></span><img alt="{\displaystyle F=F}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b1f8368f7def49eafa6c132b275382d3e39648c6" style="vertical-align: -0.338ex; width:6.58ex; height:2.176ex;"/></span>, i.e., "Carbon is carbon". Bunge concludes that the Kaya identity is impervious to empirical data, i.e., the Kaya identity cannot be tested empirically and thus cannot be used for predictions. 
In addition, Bunge proposes a tentative formula for the volume of global carbon emission that is not just a simple identity. Bunge suggests means to control climate and finally states "it might also help if economics students were to take Logic 101".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bunge2012_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bunge2012-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
