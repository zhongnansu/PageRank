<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Parts-per notation</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fluorescein_(2).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2830" data-file-width="2264" height="275" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Fluorescein_%282%29.jpg/220px-Fluorescein_%282%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Fluorescein_%282%29.jpg/330px-Fluorescein_%282%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Fluorescein_%282%29.jpg/440px-Fluorescein_%282%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fluorescein_(2).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Fluorescein" title="Fluorescein">Fluorescein</a> aqueous solutions, diluted from 1–10,000 parts-per-million</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>, the <b>parts-per notation</b> is a set of pseudo-units to describe small values of miscellaneous <a href="/wiki/Dimensionless_quantity" title="Dimensionless quantity">dimensionless quantities</a>, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Mole_fraction" title="Mole fraction">mole fraction</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mass_fraction_(chemistry)" title="Mass fraction (chemistry)">mass fraction</a>. Since these <a href="/wiki/Fraction_(mathematics)" title="Fraction (mathematics)">fractions</a> are quantity-per-quantity measures, they are pure numbers with no associated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Units_of_measurement" title="Units of measurement">units of measurement</a>. Commonly used are <b>ppm</b> (parts-per-million, <span class="nowrap">10<sup>−6</sup></span>), <b>ppb</b> (parts-per-billion, <span class="nowrap">10<sup>−9</sup></span>), <b>ppt</b> (parts-per-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trillion_(short_scale)" title="Trillion (short scale)">trillion</a>, <span class="nowrap">10<sup>−12</sup></span>) and <b>ppq</b> (parts-per-quadrillion, <span class="nowrap">10<sup>−15</sup></span>). This notation is not part of the SI system and its meaning is ambiguous.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parts-per_notation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Parts-per notation is often used describing dilute solutions in <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, for instance, the relative abundance of dissolved minerals or pollutants in water. The unit “1 ppm” can be used for a mass fraction if a water-borne pollutant is present at one-millionth of a <a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">gram</a> per gram of sample solution. When working with aqueous solutions, it is common to assume that the density of water is 1.00 g/mL. Therefore, it is common to equate 1 kilogram of water with 1 L of water. Consequently, 1 ppm corresponds to 1 mg/L and 1 ppb corresponds to 1 μg/L.
</p><p>Similarly, parts-per notation is used also in <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a> to express the value of various proportional phenomena. For instance, a special metal alloy might expand 1.2 <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">micrometers</a> per <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">meter</a> of length for every <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">degree Celsius</a> and this would be expressed as “<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_thermal_expansion" title="Coefficient of thermal expansion">α</a></i> = 1.2 ppm/°C.” Parts-per notation is also employed to denote the change, stability, or <a href="/wiki/Standard_deviation" title="Standard deviation">uncertainty</a> in measurements. For instance, the accuracy of land-survey distance measurements when using a <a href="/wiki/Laser_rangefinder" title="Laser rangefinder">laser rangefinder</a> might be 1 millimeter per kilometer of distance; this could be expressed as “<a href="/wiki/Accuracy_and_precision" title="Accuracy and precision">Accuracy</a> = 1 ppm.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Parts-per notations are all <a href="/wiki/Dimensionless_quantity" title="Dimensionless quantity">dimensionless quantities</a>: in mathematical expressions, the units of measurement always cancel. In fractions like “2 nanometers per meter” (2 n<s>m</s>/<s>m</s> = 2 nano = 2 × 10<sup>−9</sup> = 2 ppb = 2 × 0.000<span style="margin-left:0.2em">000<span style="margin-left:0.2em">001)</span></span> so the <a href="/wiki/Quotient" title="Quotient">quotients</a> are pure-number <a href="/wiki/Coefficient" title="Coefficient">coefficients</a> with positive values less than 1. When parts-per notations, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Percent" title="Percent">percent</a> symbol (%), are used in regular prose (as opposed to mathematical expressions), they are still pure-number dimensionless quantities. However, they generally take the literal “parts per” meaning of a comparative ratio (e.g., “2 ppb” would generally be interpreted as “two parts in a billion parts”).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIPM_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-BIPM-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Parts-per notations may be expressed in terms of any unit of the same measure. For instance, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_thermal_expansion" title="Coefficient of thermal expansion">coefficient of thermal expansion</a> of a certain <a href="/wiki/Brass" title="Brass">brass</a> alloy, <i>α</i> = 18.7 ppm/°C, may be expressed as 18.7 (<a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">µm</a>/m)/°C, or as 18.7 (µ<a href="/wiki/Inch" title="Inch">in</a>/in)/°C; the numeric value representing a relative proportion does not change with the adoption of a different unit of measure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Similarly, a <a href="/wiki/Metering_pump" title="Metering pump">metering pump</a> that injects a trace chemical into the main process line at the proportional flow rate <i><a href="/wiki/Volumetric_flow_rate" title="Volumetric flow rate">Q</a><sub>p</sub></i> = 125 ppm, is doing so at a rate that may be expressed in a variety of volumetric units, including 125 µL/L, 125 µ<a href="/wiki/Gallon" title="Gallon">gal</a>/gal, 125 cm<sup>3</sup>/<a href="/wiki/Cubic_metre" title="Cubic metre">m<sup>3</sup></a>, etc.
</p>
<h3><span id="In_nuclear_magnetic_resonance_.28NMR.29_spectroscopy"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="In_nuclear_magnetic_resonance_(NMR)_spectroscopy">In nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parts-per_notation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance_spectroscopy" title="Nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy">nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy</a> (NMR), <a href="/wiki/Chemical_shift" title="Chemical shift">chemical shift</a> is usually expressed in ppm. It represents the difference of a measured frequency in parts per million from the reference frequency. The reference frequency depends on the instrument's magnetic field and the element being measured. It is usually expressed in MHz. Typical chemical shifts are rarely more than a few hundred Hz from the reference frequency, so chemical shifts are conveniently expressed in ppm (Hz/MHz). Parts-per notation gives a dimensionless quantity that does not depend on the instrument's field strength.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parts-per_expressions">Parts-per expressions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parts-per_notation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Parts-per expressions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Visualisation_parts_per.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="683" data-file-width="512" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Visualisation_parts_per.svg/200px-Visualisation_parts_per.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Visualisation_parts_per.svg/300px-Visualisation_parts_per.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Visualisation_parts_per.svg/400px-Visualisation_parts_per.svg.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Visualisation_parts_per.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Visualisation of 1%, 1‰, 1‱ and 1 ppm as the fraction of the red cube to its respective block <span class="nowrap"><span class="plainlinks"><a class="external text" href="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Visualisation_parts_per.svg/3072px-Visualisation_parts_per.svg.png">(larger version)</a></span></span></div></div></div>
<ul><li><i>One part per <a href="/wiki/100_(number)" title="100 (number)">hundred</a></i> is generally represented by the <a href="/wiki/Percent_sign" title="Percent sign">percent sign</a> (%) and denotes one part <a href="/wiki/Percentage" title="Percentage">per 100 parts</a>, one part in 10<sup>2</sup>, and a value of 1 × 10<sup>−2</sup>. This is equivalent to about fifteen minutes out of one day.</li></ul>
<p><span id="Thousand"></span>
</p>
<ul><li><i>One part per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thousand" title="Thousand">thousand</a></i> should generally be spelled out in full and <b>not</b> as "ppt" (which is usually understood to represent "parts per trillion"). It may also be denoted by the <a href="/wiki/Per_mille" title="Per mille">permille</a> sign (‰). Note however, that specific disciplines such as oceanography, as well as educational exercises, do use the "ppt" abbreviation. "One part per thousand" denotes one part per 1000 parts, one part in 10<sup>3</sup>, and a value of 1 × 10<sup>−3</sup>. This is equivalent to about one and a half minutes out of one day.</li>
<li><i>One part per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ten_thousand" title="Ten thousand">ten thousand</a></i> is denoted by the <a href="/wiki/Basis_point" title="Basis point">permyriad</a> sign (‱). In contrast, in <a href="/wiki/Finance" title="Finance">finance</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Basis_point" title="Basis point">basis point</a> is a quantity with dimensions of (time<sup>−1</sup>) and is typically used to denote changes in or differences between percentage interest rates. For instance, a change in an interest rate from 5.15% per annum to 5.35% per annum could be denoted as a change of 20 basis points. Although rarely used in science (ppm is typically used instead), one permyriad has an unambiguous value of one part per <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7004100000000000000♠</span>10<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span></span> parts, one part in 10<sup>4</sup>, and a value of 1 × 10<sup>−4</sup>. This is equivalent to about nine seconds out of one day.</li></ul>
<p><span id="ppm"></span>
</p>
<ul><li><i>One part per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Million" title="Million">million</a></i> (<b>ppm</b>) denotes one part per <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7006100000000000000♠</span>1<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span></span> parts, one part in 10<sup>6</sup>, <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;"><span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7006100000000000000♠</span>1<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span></span></span></span> × 100% = 0.0001% (or 1% = <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7004100000000000000♠</span>10<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span></span> ppm), and a value of 1 × 10<sup>−6</sup>. This is equivalent to about 32 seconds out of a year; in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distance_measurement" title="Distance measurement">distance measurement</a>, it is equivalent to 1 mm of error per km of distance traversed.</li></ul>
<p><span id="ppb"></span>
</p>
<ul><li><i>One part per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1000000000_(number)" title="1000000000 (number)">billion</a></i> (<b>ppb</b>) denotes one part per <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7009100000000000000♠</span>1<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span></span> parts, one part in 10<sup>9</sup>, <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;"><span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7009100000000000000♠</span>1<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span></span></span></span> × 100% = <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6993100000000000000♠</span>0.000<span style="margin-left:.25em;">0001</span></span>% (or 1% = <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7007100000000000000♠</span>10<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span></span> ppb) and a value of 1 × 10<sup>−9</sup>. This is equivalent to about three seconds out of a <a href="/wiki/Century" title="Century">century</a>.</li></ul>
<p><span id="ppt"></span>
</p>
<ul><li><i>One part per <a href="/wiki/Orders_of_magnitude_(numbers)#1012" title="Orders of magnitude (numbers)">trillion</a></i> (<b>ppt</b>) denotes one part per <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7012100000000000000♠</span>1<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span></span> parts, one part in 10<sup>12</sup>, and a value of 1 × 10<sup>−12</sup>. This is equivalent to about three seconds out of every hundred thousand years.</li></ul>
<p><span id="ppq"></span>
</p>
<ul><li><i>One part per <a href="/wiki/Orders_of_magnitude_(numbers)#1015" title="Orders of magnitude (numbers)">quadrillion</a></i> (<b>ppq</b>) denotes one part per <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7015100000000000000♠</span>1<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span></span> parts, one part in 10<sup>15</sup>, and a value of 1 × 10<sup>−15</sup>. This is equivalent to about two and a half minutes out of the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_the_Earth" title="Age of the Earth">age of the Earth</a> (4.5 billion years). Although relatively uncommon in analytical chemistry, measurements at the ppq level are sometimes performed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parts-per_notation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the <a href="/wiki/International_Bureau_of_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Bureau of Weights and Measures">International Bureau of Weights and Measures</a> (an international standards organization known also by its <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a>-language initials BIPM) recognizes the use of parts-per notation, it is not formally part of the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a> (SI).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIPM_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-BIPM-2">[2]</a></sup> Note that although “<a href="/wiki/Percentage" title="Percentage">percent</a>” (%) is not formally part of the SI, both the BIPM and the <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">ISO</a> take the position that <i>“in mathematical expressions, the internationally recognized symbol % (percent) may be used with the SI to represent the number 0.01”</i> for dimensionless quantities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIPM_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-BIPM-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Pure_and_Applied_Physics" title="International Union of Pure and Applied Physics">IUPAP</a>, <i>“a continued source of annoyance to unit purists has been the continued use of percent, ppm, ppb, and ppt.”</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUPAP_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-IUPAP-6">[6]</a></sup> Although <a href="#SI-compliant_expressions">SI-compliant expressions</a> should be used as an alternative, the parts-per notation remains nevertheless widely used in technical disciplines. The main problems with the parts-per notation are the following:
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Long_and_short_scales">Long and short scales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parts-per_notation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Long and short scales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because the <a href="/wiki/Names_of_large_numbers" title="Names of large numbers">named numbers</a> starting with a “<a href="/wiki/Long_and_short_scales" title="Long and short scales">billion</a>” have different values in different countries, the BIPM suggests avoiding the use of “ppb” and “ppt” to prevent misunderstanding. The U.S. <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">National Institute of Standards and Technology</a> (NIST) takes the stringent position, stating that <i>“the language-dependent terms [ . . . ] are not acceptable for use with the SI to express the values of quantities.”</i><span style="margin-left:0.3em"><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></span>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thousand_vs._trillion">Thousand vs. trillion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parts-per_notation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Thousand vs. trillion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although "ppt" usually means "parts per trillion", it occasionally means "parts per thousand". Unless the meaning of "ppt" is defined explicitly, it has to be determined from the context.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mass_fraction_vs._mole_fraction_vs._volume_fraction">Mass fraction vs. mole fraction vs. volume fraction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parts-per_notation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mass fraction vs. mole fraction vs. volume fraction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another problem of the parts-per notation is that it may refer to <a href="/wiki/Mass_fraction_(chemistry)" title="Mass fraction (chemistry)">mass fraction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mole_fraction" title="Mole fraction">mole fraction</a> or <a href="/wiki/Volume_fraction" title="Volume fraction">volume fraction</a>. Since it is usually not stated which quantity is used, it is better to write the unit as kg/kg, mol/mol or m<sup>3</sup>/m<sup>3</sup> (even though they are all dimensionless).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The difference is quite significant when dealing with gases and it is very important to specify which quantity is being used. For example, the conversion factor between a mass fraction of 1 ppb and a mole fraction of 1 ppb is about 4.7 for the greenhouse gas <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CFC-11" title="CFC-11">CFC-11</a> in air. For volume fraction, the suffix "V" or "v" is sometimes appended to the parts-per notation (e.g., ppmV, ppbv, pptv).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Unfortunately, ppbv and pptv are also often used for mole fractions (which is identical to volume fraction only for ideal gases).
</p><p>To distinguish the mass fraction from volume fraction or mole fraction, the letter w (standing for weight) is sometimes added to the abbreviation (e.g., ppmw, ppbw).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>The usage of the parts-per notation is generally quite fixed inside most specific branches of science, leading some researchers to draw the conclusion that their own usage (mass/mass, mol/mol, volume/volume, or others) is the only correct one. This, in turn, leads them to not specify their usage in their publications, and others may therefore misinterpret their results. For example, <a href="/wiki/Electrochemistry" title="Electrochemistry">electrochemists</a> often use volume/volume, while <a href="/wiki/Chemical_engineering" title="Chemical engineering">chemical engineers</a> may use mass/mass as well as volume/volume. Many academic papers of otherwise excellent level fail to specify their usage of the parts-per notation.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uno">Uno</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parts-per_notation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Uno">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because of the cumbersome nature of expressing certain dimensionless quantities per SI guidelines, the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Pure_and_Applied_Physics" title="International Union of Pure and Applied Physics">International Union of Pure and Applied Physics</a> (IUPAP) in 1999 proposed the adoption of the special name "uno" (symbol: U) to represent the number 1 in dimensionless quantities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUPAP_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-IUPAP-6">[6]</a></sup> This symbol is not to be confused with the always-italicized symbol for the variable "uncertainty" (symbol: <i>U</i>). This unit name "uno" and its symbol could be used in combination with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_prefix" title="SI prefix">SI prefixes</a> to express the values of dimensionless quantities that are much less—or even <i>greater</i>—than one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>

<p>In 2004, a report to the <a href="/wiki/International_Committee_for_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Committee for Weights and Measures">International Committee for Weights and Measures</a> (known also by its French-language initials CIPM) stated that response to the proposal of the uno "had been almost entirely negative" and the principal proponent "recommended dropping the idea".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> To date, the uno has not been adopted by any <a href="/wiki/Standards_organization" title="Standards organization">standards organization</a> and it appears unlikely it will ever become an officially sanctioned way to express low-value (high-ratio) dimensionless quantities. The proposal was instructive, however, as to the perceived shortcomings of the current options for denoting dimensionless quantities.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Improper_applications">Improper applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Parts-per_notation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Improper applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Parts-per notation may properly be used only to express true dimensionless quantities; that is, the units of measurement <i>must</i> cancel in expressions like "1 mg/kg" so that the quotients are pure numbers with values less than 1. Mixed-unit quantities such as "a <a href="/wiki/Radon" title="Radon">radon</a> concentration of 15 p<a href="/wiki/Curie" title="Curie">Ci</a>/<a href="/wiki/Litre" title="Litre">L</a>" are not dimensionless quantities and may not be expressed using any form of parts-per notation, such as "15 ppt". Other examples of measures that are not dimensionless quantities are as follows:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particulate" title="Particulate">Particulate matter</a> in the air: 50 µg/m<sup>3</sup>; not 50 ppb.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Stepper_motor" title="Stepper motor">stepper motor</a>/gear system that produces a motion of 1 µm/pulse; not 1 ppm</li>
<li>Mercury vapor concentration in air: 0.6 ng/<a href="/wiki/Litre" title="Litre">L</a>; not 0.6 ppt</li></ul>
<p>Note however, that it is not uncommon to express aqueous concentrations—particularly in drinking-water reports intended for the general public—using parts-per notation (2.1 ppm, 0.8 ppb, etc.) and further, for those reports to state that the notations denote milligrams per <a href="/wiki/Litre" title="Litre">liter</a> or micrograms per liter. Although "2.1 mg/L" is not a dimensionless quantity, it is assumed in scientific circles that "2.1 mg/kg" (2.1 ppm) is the true measure because one liter of water has a mass of about one kilogram. The goal in all <a href="/wiki/Technical_writing" title="Technical writing">technical writing</a> (including drinking-water reports for the general public) is to clearly communicate to the intended audience with minimal confusion. Drinking water is intuitively a volumetric quantity in the public’s mind so measures of contamination expressed on a per-liter basis are considered to be easier to grasp. Still, it is technically possible, for example, to "dissolve" more than one liter of a very hydrophilic chemical in 1 liter of water; parts-per notation would be confusing when describing its solubility in water (greater than a million parts per million), so one would simply state the volume (or mass) that will dissolve into a liter, instead.
</p><p>When reporting air-borne rather than water-borne densities, a slightly different convention is used since air is approximately 1000 times less dense than water. In water, 1 µg/m<sup>3</sup> is roughly equivalent to parts-per-trillion whereas in air, it is roughly equivalent to parts-per-billion. Note also, that in the case of air, this convention is much less accurate. Whereas one liter of water is almost exactly 1 kg, one cubic meter of air is often taken as 1.143 kg—much less accurate, but still close enough for many practical uses.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">This is a simplified explanation. Laser rangefinders typically have a measurement <a href="/wiki/Granularity" title="Granularity">granularity</a> of one to ten millimeters; thus, the complete specification for distance measurement accuracy might read as follows:  Accuracy: ±(1 mm + 1 ppm). Consequently, a distance measurement of only a few meters would still have an accuracy of ±1 mm in this example.<sub> </sub></span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-BIPM-2"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-BIPM_2-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-BIPM_2-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-BIPM_2-2"><sup><i><b>c</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">BIPM: <a class="external text" href="http://www.bipm.org/en/publications/si-brochure/section5-3-7.html" rel="nofollow">5.3.7 <i>Stating values of dimensionless quantities, or quantities of dimension one</i></a><sub> </sub></span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-3"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-3">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">In the particular case of coefficient of thermal expansion, the change to inches (one of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_customary_units" title="United States customary units">U.S. customary units</a>) is typically also accompanied by a change to <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">degrees Fahrenheit</a>. Since a Fahrenheit-sized interval of temperature is only ​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>5</sup>⁄<sub>9</sub></span> that of a Celsius-sized interval, the value is typically expressed as 10.4 (µin/in)/°F rather than 18.7 (µin/in)/°C.<sub> </sub></span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-4"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-4">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">
Measurements of <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_dibenzodioxins" title="Polychlorinated dibenzodioxins">dioxin</a> are routinely made at the <i>sub</i>-ppq level. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">U.S. Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA) currently sets a hard limit of 30 ppq for dioxin in drinking water but once recommended a voluntary limit of 0.013 ppq. Also, radioactive contaminants in drinking water, which are quantified by measuring their radiation, are often reported in terms of ppq; 0.013 ppq is equivalent to the thickness of a sheet of paper versus a journey of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7005146000000000000♠</span>146<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span></span> trips around the world.<sub> </sub></span>
</li>

</li>

<li id="cite_note-12"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-12">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Compliance with the SI regarding the percent symbol (%) is limited in this chart. According to the BIPM’s <a class="external text" href="http://www.bipm.org/en/publications/si-brochure/section5-3-3.html" rel="nofollow">SI brochure: Subsection 5.3.3, <i>Formatting the value of a quantity</i></a>, a space is always used to separate the unit symbol from the numeric value. Notable exceptions are the unit symbols for degree, minute, and second for plane angle, °, ′, and ″ (e.g., a latitude of <span class="nowrap">47° 38′ 8.8″).</span> However, according to <a class="external text" href="http://www.bipm.org/en/publications/si-brochure/section5-3-7.html" rel="nofollow">5.3.7 <i>Stating values of dimensionless quantities, or quantities of dimension one</i></a>, the exception does not apply to the “%” symbol; it states as follows: <i>“When it [the percent symbol] is used, a space separates the number and the symbol %.”</i> This practice has not been well adopted with regard to the % symbol, is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:%25" title="Wikipedia:%">contrary to Wikipedia’s <i>Manual of Style</i></a>, and is not observed here.<sub> </sub></span>
</li>
