<p><b>Chemical looping combustion</b> (<b>CLC</b>) is a technological process typically employing a dual <a href="/wiki/Fluidized_bed" title="Fluidized bed">fluidized bed</a> system. The CLC operated with an interconnected moving bed with a fluidized bed system has also been employed as a technology process. In CLC, a metal oxide is employed as a bed material providing the oxygen for combustion in the <a href="/wiki/Fluidized_bed_combustion" title="Fluidized bed combustion">fuel reactor</a>. The reduced metal is then transferred to the second bed (<a href="/wiki/Fluidized_bed_reactor" title="Fluidized bed reactor">air reactor</a>) and re-oxidized before being reintroduced back to the fuel reactor completing the loop. Fig 1 shows a simplified diagram of the CLC process. Fig 2 shows an example of a dual fluidized bed circulating reactor system and a moving bed-fluidized bed circulating reactor system.
</p><p>Isolation of the fuel from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a> simplifies the number of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reactions</a> in <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a>. Employing <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> without <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> and the trace gases found in air eliminates the primary source for the formation of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxide</a> (NO<sub><var>x</var></sub>), produces a <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">flue gas</a> composed primarily of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a>; other trace <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pollutants" title="Pollutants">pollutants</a> depend on the <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a> selected.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemical_looping_combustion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chemical looping combustion (CLC) uses two or more reactions to perform the oxidation of hydrocarbon based fuels. In its simplest form, an oxygen carrying species (normally a metal) is first oxidized in air forming an oxide. This oxide is then reduced using a hydrocarbon as reducer in a second reaction. <p>CLC was first studied as a way to produce <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> from fossil fuels, using two interconnected fluidized beds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Later it was proposed as a system for increasing power station efficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The gain in efficiency is possible due to the enhanced reversibility of the two redox reactions; in traditional single stage combustion, the release of a fuel’s energy occurs in a highly irreversible manner - departing considerably from equilibrium. In CLC, if an appropriate oxygen carrier is chosen, both redox reactions can be made to occur almost reversibly and at relatively low temperatures. Theoretically, this allows a power station using CLC to approach the ideal work output for an internal combustion engine without exposing components to excessive working temperatures.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermodynamics">Thermodynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemical_looping_combustion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Thermodynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:366px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CLC_MCG.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="540" data-file-width="720" height="273" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/CLC_MCG.png/364px-CLC_MCG.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/CLC_MCG.png/546px-CLC_MCG.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0b/CLC_MCG.png 2x" width="364"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CLC_MCG.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fig 3. Sankey diagram of energy fluxes in a reversible CLC system.</div></div></div><p>Fig 3 illustrates the energy exchanges in a CLC system graphically, and shows a <a href="/wiki/Sankey_diagram" title="Sankey diagram">Sankey diagram</a> of the energy fluxes occurring in a reversible CLC based engine. Studying Fig 1, a <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine" title="Heat engine">heat engine</a> is arranged to receive heat at high temperature from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermic</a> oxidation reaction. After converting part of this energy to work, the heat engine rejects the remaining energy as heat. Almost all of this heat rejection can be absorbed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endothermic" title="Endothermic">endothermic</a> reduction reaction occurring in the reducer. This arrangement requires the redox reactions to be exothermic and endothermic respectively, but this is normally the case for most metals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Some additional heat exchange with the environment is required to satisfy the <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">second law</a>; theoretically, for a reversible process, the heat exchange is related to the standard state entropy change, ΔS<sup>o</sup>, of the primary hydrocarbon oxidation reaction as follows:
</p><dl><dd>Q<sub>o</sub> = T<sub>o</sub>ΔS<sup>o</sup></dd></dl>
<p>However, for most hydrocarbons, ΔS<sup>o</sup> is a small value and, as a result, an engine of high overall efficiency is theoretically possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CO2_capture">CO<sub>2</sub> capture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemical_looping_combustion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: CO2 capture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although proposed as a means of increasing efficiency, in recent years, interest has been shown in CLC as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_capture" title="Carbon capture">carbon capture</a> technique.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Carbon capture is facilitated by CLC because the two redox reactions generate two intrinsically separated flue gas streams: a stream from the air reactor, consisting of atmospheric <span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> and residual <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>, but sensibly free of CO<sub>2</sub>; and a stream from the fuel reactor predominately containing CO<sub>2</sub> and <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O</span> with very little diluent nitrogen. The air reactor flue gas can be discharged to the atmosphere causing minimal CO<sub>2</sub> pollution. The reducer exit gas contains almost all of the CO<sub>2</sub> generated by the system and CLC therefore can be said to exhibit 'inherent carbon capture', as water vapor can easily be removed from the second flue gas via condensation, leading to a stream of almost pure CO<sub>2</sub>. This gives CLC clear benefits when compared with competing carbon capture technologies, as the latter generally involve a significant energy penalty associated with either post combustion scrubbing systems or the work input required for air separation plants. This has led to CLC being proposed as an energy efficient carbon capture technology,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> able to capture nearly all of the CO<sub>2</sub>, for example, from a Coal Direct Chemical Looping (CDCL) plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> A continuous 200-hour demonstration results of a 25 kW<sub>th</sub> CDCL sub-pilot unit indicated nearly 100% coal conversion to CO<sub>2</sub> with no carbon carryover to the air reactor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology_development">Technology development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemical_looping_combustion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Technology development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>First operation of chemical-looping combustion with gaseous fuels was demonstrated in 2003,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and later with solid fuels in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Total operational experience in 34 pilots of 0.3 to 3 MW is more than 9000 h.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-19">[19]</a></sup> Oxygen carrier materials used in operation include monometallic oxides of nickel, copper, manganese and iron, as well as various combined oxides including manganese oxides.combined with calcium, iron and silica. Also natural ores have been in use, especially for solid fuels, including iron ores, manganese ores and ilmenite.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cost_and_energy_penalty">Cost and energy penalty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemical_looping_combustion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cost and energy penalty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A detailed technology assessment of chemical-looping combustion of solid fuel, i.e. coal, for a 1000 MW<sub>th</sub> power plant shows that the added CLC reactor costs as compared to a normal circulating fluidized bed boiler are small, because of the similarities of the technologies. Major costs are instead CO<sub>2</sub> compression, needed in all CO<sub>2</sub> capture technologies, and oxygen production. Molecular oxygen production may also be needed in certain CLC configuration for polishing the product gas from the fuel reactor. In all the added costs were estimated to 20 €/tonne of CO<sub>2</sub> whereas the energy penalty was 4%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants_and_related_technologies">Variants and related technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemical_looping_combustion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Variants and related technologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A variant of CLC is Chemical-Looping Combustion with Oxygen Uncoupling (CLOU) where an oxygen carrier is used that releases gas-phase oxygen in the fuel reactor, e.g. CuO/<span class="chemf nowrap">Cu<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>O.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> This is helpful for achieving high gas conversion, and especially when using solid fuels, where slow steam gasification of char can be avoided. CLOU operation with solid fuels shows high performance<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Chemical Looping can also be used to produce hydrogen in <a href="/wiki/Chemical_looping_reforming_and_gasification" title="Chemical looping reforming and gasification">Chemical-Looping Reforming</a> (CLR) processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In one configuration of the CLR process, hydrogen is produced from coal and/or natural gas using a moving bed fuel reactor integrated with a steam reactor and a fluidized bed air reactor. This configuration of CLR can produce greater than 99% purity H<sub>2</sub> without the need for CO<sub>2</sub> separation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Comprehensive overviews of the field are given in recent reviews on chemical looping technologies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>In summary CLC can achieve both an increase in power station efficiency simultaneously with low energy penalty carbon capture.  Challenges with CLC include operation of dual fluidized bed (maintaining carrier fluidization while avoiding crushing and attrition), and maintaining carrier stability over many cycles.
</p>
