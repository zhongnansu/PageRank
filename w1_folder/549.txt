<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Carbon–hydrogen bond activation</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:258px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chfunctionalizationscheme.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="234" data-file-width="680" height="88" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Chfunctionalizationscheme.jpg/256px-Chfunctionalizationscheme.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Chfunctionalizationscheme.jpg/384px-Chfunctionalizationscheme.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Chfunctionalizationscheme.jpg/512px-Chfunctionalizationscheme.jpg 2x" width="256"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chfunctionalizationscheme.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>General scheme for C-H functionalization</div></div></div>
<p><b>Carbon–hydrogen bond functionalization</b> (<b>C–H functionalization</b>) is a type of reaction in which a <a href="/wiki/Carbon%E2%80%93hydrogen_bond" title="Carbon–hydrogen bond">carbon–hydrogen bond</a> is cleaved and replaced with a carbon-X bond (where X is usually carbon, oxygen, or nitrogen).  The term usually implies that a transition metal is involved in the C-H cleavage process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sanford_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sanford-2">[2]</a></sup> Reactions classified by the term typically involve the <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a> first to react with a metal catalyst to create an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organometallic" title="Organometallic">organometallic</a> complex in which the hydrocarbon is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coordination_chemistry" title="Coordination chemistry">coordinated</a> to the inner-sphere of a metal, either via an intermediate "alkane or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arene_compound" title="Arene compound">arene</a> complex" or as a <a href="/wiki/Transition_state" title="Transition state">transition state</a> leading to a "M−C" intermediate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldman_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-goldman-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The intermediate of this first step (known as <b>C-H activation</b> and sometimes used interchangeably with C-H functionalization) can then undergo subsequent reactions to produce the functionalized product. Important to this definition is the requirement that during the C–H cleavage event, the hydrocarbyl species remains associated in the inner-sphere and under the influence of "M".
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:338px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CHmechs.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="350" data-file-width="465" height="253" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/CHmechs.jpg/336px-CHmechs.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fd/CHmechs.jpg 1.5x" width="336"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CHmechs.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Some mechanisms for C-H activation</div></div></div>
<p>Mechanisms for C-H activations fall under three general categories: (i) <a href="/wiki/Oxidative_addition" title="Oxidative addition">oxidative addition</a>, in which a metal center inserts into a carbon-hydrogen bond, which cleaves the bond and <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">oxidizes</a> the metal, producing an intermediate that can undergo reductive elimination to yield the organometallic reactive intermediate (ii) <i>electrophilic activation</i>, in which a generally electron rich substrate undergoes an SeAr-type mechanism (iii) <a href="/wiki/Sigma-bond_metathesis" title="Sigma-bond metathesis">Sigma-bond metathesis</a>, which proceeds through a "four-centered" transition state in which bonds break and form in a single step: the target hydrocarbon bond breaks as the carbon bonds to the metal and the hydrogen bonds to one of the metal’s <a href="/wiki/Ligand" title="Ligand">ligands</a>, which causes bond breakage between the ligand and the metal.
</p><p>C–H bonds, which are traditionally considered unreactive, can be cleaved by coordination.  Much research has been devoted to the design and synthesis of new reagents and catalysts that can effect C–H activation.  C-H activation chemistry has the potential to transform the chemical world through the development of novel synthetic methods. C-H activation could enable the conversion of cheap and abundant alkanes into valuable <a href="/wiki/Functional_group" title="Functional group">functionalized</a> organic compounds and the efficient structural editing of already complex molecules (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Natural_product" title="Natural product">natural product</a> synthesis).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Selective activation of a specific C-H bond poses a great challenge.  In addition to a high bond dissociation energy, C-H bonds have very low polarity because these two elements have similar electronegativities.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historic_overview">Historic overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon%E2%80%93hydrogen_bond_activation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historic overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first C–H activation reaction is often attributed to <a href="/wiki/Otto_Dimroth" title="Otto Dimroth">Otto Dimroth</a>, who in 1902, reported that <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a> reacted with <a href="/wiki/Mercury(II)_acetate" title="Mercury(II) acetate">mercury(II) acetate</a> (See: <a href="/wiki/Organomercury" title="Organomercury">organomercury</a>), but some scholars<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (December 2013)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> do not view this reaction as being true C–H activation.  Many electrophilic metal centers undergo this reaction. <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Chatt" title="Joseph Chatt">Joseph Chatt</a> has been credited by many to be the first to perform the first C-H activation reaction in 1965<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> with the insertion of <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium" title="Ruthenium">ruthenium</a>, in the form of RuCl<sub>2</sub>(dmpe)<sub>2</sub> (where <a href="/wiki/1,2-Bis(dimethylphosphino)ethane" title="1,2-Bis(dimethylphosphino)ethane">dmpe</a> = 1,2-Bis(dimethylphosphino)ethane), into the C-H bond of <a href="/wiki/Naphthalene" title="Naphthalene">naphthalene</a>. However, in 1955,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Shunsuke Murahashi reported a <a href="/wiki/Cobalt" title="Cobalt">cobalt</a>-catalyzed <a href="/wiki/Chelation" title="Chelation">chelation</a>-assisted C-H functionalization of 2-phenylisoindolin-1-one from (<i>E</i>)-N,1-diphenylmethanimine.
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:389px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cobalt_C-H_activation.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="255" data-file-width="912" height="108" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Cobalt_C-H_activation.png/387px-Cobalt_C-H_activation.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Cobalt_C-H_activation.png/581px-Cobalt_C-H_activation.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Cobalt_C-H_activation.png/774px-Cobalt_C-H_activation.png 2x" width="387"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cobalt_C-H_activation.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cobalt-catalyzed C-H activation</div></div></div></div>
<p>In 1969, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_E._Shilov" title="Alexander E. Shilov">A.E. Shilov</a> reported that <a href="/wiki/Potassium_tetrachloroplatinate" title="Potassium tetrachloroplatinate">potassium tetrachloroplatinate</a> induced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotope_scrambling" title="Isotope scrambling">isotope scrambling</a> between <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heavy_water" title="Heavy water">heavy water</a>.  The pathway was proposed to involve binding of methane to Pt(II). In 1972, the Shilov group was able to produce <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methyl_chloride" title="Methyl chloride">methyl chloride</a> in a similar reaction involving a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stoichiometric" title="Stoichiometric">stoichiometric</a> amount of <a href="/wiki/Potassium_tetrachloroplatinate" title="Potassium tetrachloroplatinate">potassium tetrachloroplatinate</a>, catalytic <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hexachloroplatinate" title="Potassium hexachloroplatinate">potassium hexachloroplatinate</a>, methane and water. Due to the fact that Shilov worked and published in the Soviet Union during the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> era, his work was largely ignored by Western scientists. This so-called <a href="/wiki/Shilov_system" title="Shilov system">Shilov system</a> is today one of the few true catalytic systems for <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">alkane</a> functionalizations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goldman_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-goldman-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>In some cases, discoveries in C-H activation were being made in conjunction with those of <a href="/wiki/Coupling_reaction" title="Coupling reaction">cross coupling</a>. In 1969,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Yuzo Fujiwara reported the synthesis of (<i>E</i>)-1,2-diphenylethene from <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Styrene" title="Styrene">styrene</a> with Pd(OAc)<sub>2</sub> and Cu(OAc)<sub>2</sub>, a procedure very similar to that of cross coupling. On the category of oxidative addition, <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Green_(chemist)" title="Malcolm Green (chemist)">M. L. H. Green</a> in 1970 reported on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photochemical" title="Photochemical">photochemical</a> insertion of <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a> (as a Cp<sub>2</sub>WH<sub>2</sub> complex) in a <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a> C–H bond<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/George_M._Whitesides" title="George M. Whitesides">George M. Whitesides</a> in 1979 was the first to carry out an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intramolecular" title="Intramolecular">intramolecular</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aliphatic" title="Aliphatic">aliphatic</a> C–H activation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:390px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fujiwarachfunctionalization.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="89" data-file-width="481" height="72" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Fujiwarachfunctionalization.png/388px-Fujiwarachfunctionalization.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0c/Fujiwarachfunctionalization.png 1.5x" width="388"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fujiwarachfunctionalization.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fujiwara's palladium- and copper-catalyzed C-H functionalization</div></div></div></div>
<p>The next breakthrough was reported independently by two research groups in 1982. <a href="/wiki/Robert_G._Bergman" title="Robert G. Bergman">R. G. Bergman</a> reported the first transition metal-mediated intermolecular C–H activation of unactivated and completely saturated hydrocarbons by oxidative addition. Using a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photochemical" title="Photochemical">photochemical</a> approach, photolysis of Cp*Ir(PMe<sub>3</sub>)H<sub>2</sub>, where Cp* is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pentamethylcyclopentadienyl" title="Pentamethylcyclopentadienyl">pentamethylcyclopentadienyl</a> ligand, led to the coordinatively unsaturated species Cp*Ir(PMe<sub>3</sub>) which reacted via oxidative addition with <a href="/wiki/Cyclohexane" title="Cyclohexane">cyclohexane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neopentane" title="Neopentane">neopentane</a> to form the corresponding hydridoalkyl complexes, Cp*Ir(PMe<sub>3</sub>)HR,  where R = cyclohexyl and neopentyl, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> W.A.G. Graham found that the same hydrocarbons react with Cp*Ir(CO)<sub>2</sub> upon irradiation to afford the related alkylhydrido complexes Cp*Ir(CO)HR, where R = cyclohexyl and neopentyl, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In the latter example, the reaction is presumed to proceed via the oxidative addition of alkane to a 16-electron iridium(I) intermediate, Cp*Ir(CO), formed by irradiation of Cp*Ir(CO)<sub>2</sub>.
</p>
<dl><dd><div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:342px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CHactRGB%2BWAGimproved.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="407" data-file-width="1777" height="78" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/CHactRGB%2BWAGimproved.png/340px-CHactRGB%2BWAGimproved.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/CHactRGB%2BWAGimproved.png/510px-CHactRGB%2BWAGimproved.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/CHactRGB%2BWAGimproved.png/680px-CHactRGB%2BWAGimproved.png 2x" width="340"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CHactRGB%2BWAGimproved.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>C–H activation by Bergman et al. (left) and Graham et al.</div></div></div></div></dd></dl>
<p>The selective activation and functionalization of alkane C–H bonds was reported using a <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a> complex outfitted with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pentamethylcyclopentadienyl" title="Pentamethylcyclopentadienyl">pentamethylcyclopentadienyl</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal_nitrosyl" title="Metal nitrosyl">nitrosyl</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allyl" title="Allyl">allyl</a> and neopentyl ligands, Cp*W(NO)(η<sup>3</sup>-allyl)(CH<sub>2</sub>CMe<sub>3</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PentaneActivation.png" title="C–H activation of pentane, as seen in Ledgzdins et al., J. Am. Chem. Soc. 2007; 129, 5372–3."><img alt="C–H activation of pentane, as seen in Ledgzdins et al., J. Am. Chem. Soc. 2007; 129, 5372–3." data-file-height="710" data-file-width="2162" height="131" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/PentaneActivation.png/400px-PentaneActivation.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/PentaneActivation.png/600px-PentaneActivation.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/PentaneActivation.png/800px-PentaneActivation.png 2x" width="400"/></a></div></div></dd></dl>
<p>In one example involving this system, the alkane <a href="/wiki/Pentane" title="Pentane">pentane</a> is selectively converted to the <a href="/wiki/Halocarbon" title="Halocarbon">halocarbon</a> <i>1-iodopentane</i>. This transformation was achieved via the thermolysis of Cp*W(NO)(η<sup>3</sup>-allyl)(CH<sub>2</sub>CMe<sub>3</sub>) in pentane at <a href="/wiki/Room_temperature" title="Room temperature">room temperature</a>, resulting in elimination of <a href="/wiki/Neopentane" title="Neopentane">neopentane</a> by a pseudo-first-order process, generating an undetectable electronically and sterically unsaturated <a href="/wiki/Electron_counting" title="Electron counting">16-electron</a> intermediate that is coordinated by an <a href="/wiki/Hapticity" title="Hapticity">η<sup>2</sup></a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Butadiene" title="Butadiene">butadiene</a> ligand. Subsequent intermolecular activation of a pentane solvent molecule then yields an <a href="/wiki/Electron_counting" title="Electron counting">18-electron</a> complex possessing an <i>n</i>-pentyl ligand. In a separate step, reaction with <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a> at −60 °C liberates 1-iodopentane from the complex.
</p><p>Arene C–H bonds are readily activated by metal complexes, as illustrated by <a href="/wiki/Directed_ortho_metalation" title="Directed ortho metalation">directed ortho metalation</a>.  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N,N-dimethylbenzylamine" title="N,N-dimethylbenzylamine">N,N-dimethylbenzylamine</a> in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclometalation" title="Cyclometalation">cyclometalation</a> readily by many transition metals:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scope">Scope</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon%E2%80%93hydrogen_bond_activation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Scope">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon%E2%80%93hydrogen_bond_activation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Two pathways achieve C-H activation: innate C-H activation or guided C-H activation. The figure depicts the two oxidation products possible, depending on the reagents used to functionalize a specific C-H bond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bruckl_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bruckl-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Guided_vs_Innate_activation_Figure_1_Redone.png" title="Guided vs. Innate C-H Activation"><img alt="Guided vs. Innate C-H Activation" data-file-height="785" data-file-width="4247" height="120" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Guided_vs_Innate_activation_Figure_1_Redone.png/650px-Guided_vs_Innate_activation_Figure_1_Redone.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Guided_vs_Innate_activation_Figure_1_Redone.png/975px-Guided_vs_Innate_activation_Figure_1_Redone.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Guided_vs_Innate_activation_Figure_1_Redone.png/1300px-Guided_vs_Innate_activation_Figure_1_Redone.png 2x" width="650"/></a></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Selectivity_of_C-H_activation">Selectivity of C-H activation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon%E2%80%93hydrogen_bond_activation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Selectivity of C-H activation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Among the various factors that affect selectivity in C-H activation, the more sterically accessible C-H bond will have more selectivity at that position.
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Strain_release_figure.png" title="Selective C-H activation due to strain release"><img alt="Selective C-H activation due to strain release" data-file-height="653" data-file-width="2747" height="95" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Strain_release_figure.png/400px-Strain_release_figure.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Strain_release_figure.png/600px-Strain_release_figure.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Strain_release_figure.png/800px-Strain_release_figure.png 2x" width="400"/></a></div></div>
<p>In the figure above, the equitorial C-H bond in cis-1,2-dimethylcyclohexane is selectively oxidized over the axial C-H bond. During oxidation, the axial methyl group becomes planar, decreasing the 1,3-diaxial interactions in the transition state, which stabilizes the transition state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>An alkene C–H bond activation with a <a href="/wiki/Rhodium" title="Rhodium">rhodium</a> catalyst is demonstrated in the synthesis of this strained bicyclic enamine:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BergMannCHActivation2008.svg" title="C–H bond activation Yotphan 2008"><img alt="C–H bond activation Yotphan 2008" data-file-height="333" data-file-width="643" height="207" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/BergMannCHActivation2008.svg/400px-BergMannCHActivation2008.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/BergMannCHActivation2008.svg/600px-BergMannCHActivation2008.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/BergMannCHActivation2008.svg/800px-BergMannCHActivation2008.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a></div></div></dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Innate_selectivity">Innate selectivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon%E2%80%93hydrogen_bond_activation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Innate selectivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Innate selectivity is observed for reactions that functionalize C-H bonds that lack an influence of directing forces, thus only relying on the natural reactivity of the molecule.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bruckl_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bruckl-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Inductive_effect" title="Inductive effect">Inductive</a> (through-bond) effects can explain the innate selectivity of a C-H bond in a molecule through the examination of the electronic nature of the bonds. The presence and proximity of electron-withdrawing groups (EWGs) or electron-donating groups (EDGs) can heavily influence the electron density of a C-H bond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newhouse_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Newhouse-22">[22]</a></sup> The reactivity trend for nonmetal insertion is tertiary&gt;secondary&gt;primary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Steric effects can also influence the selectivity of C-H bond activation: bulky groups can decrease the rate of functionalizing an adjacent C-H bond.
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Inductive_Effects_Figure_1_Redone.png" title="Influence of inductive effects on selectivity of C-H bond activation"><img alt="Influence of inductive effects on selectivity of C-H bond activation" data-file-height="845" data-file-width="2075" height="122" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Inductive_Effects_Figure_1_Redone.png/300px-Inductive_Effects_Figure_1_Redone.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Inductive_Effects_Figure_1_Redone.png/450px-Inductive_Effects_Figure_1_Redone.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Inductive_Effects_Figure_1_Redone.png/600px-Inductive_Effects_Figure_1_Redone.png 2x" width="300"/></a></div></div>
<p>For oxaziridine (left), the 1st site is oxidized preferably due not only to distance from the electron-withdrawing OBz group but also because of its substitution—the carbon is tertiary—and so it is more electron rich. Similarly, the secondary methylene position in trifluoromethyldioxirane (TFDO, right) that is farthest away to the electron-withdrawing group is selectively oxidized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newhouse_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Newhouse-22">[22]</a></sup> Note that here, like with oxaziridine, substitution at the carbon also greatly affects selectivity; the secondary methylene position is preferred over the terminal methylene position because the former has a secondary carbon while the latter has a primary one.
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Steric_Effects_Figure_1_Redone.png" title="Steric Effects"><img alt="Steric Effects" data-file-height="817" data-file-width="1099" height="112" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Steric_Effects_Figure_1_Redone.png/150px-Steric_Effects_Figure_1_Redone.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Steric_Effects_Figure_1_Redone.png/225px-Steric_Effects_Figure_1_Redone.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Steric_Effects_Figure_1_Redone.png/300px-Steric_Effects_Figure_1_Redone.png 2x" width="150"/></a></div></div>
<p>In the oxidation of cyclohexane compound, the tertiary site on the ring is preferential over the tertiary site of the isopropyl substituent. The two methyl groups hinder the oxidation on the isopropyl group, making the less hindered cyclohexyl site more favorable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newhouse_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Newhouse-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Guided_selectivity">Guided selectivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon%E2%80%93hydrogen_bond_activation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Guided selectivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In contrast to innate selectivity, guided selectivity results from external reagents or directing groups affecting the nature of specific C-H bonds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bruckl_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bruckl-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Guided_Figure_1.png" title="Guided Example 1"><img alt="Guided Example 1" data-file-height="782" data-file-width="2608" height="90" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Guided_Figure_1.png/300px-Guided_Figure_1.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Guided_Figure_1.png/450px-Guided_Figure_1.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Guided_Figure_1.png/600px-Guided_Figure_1.png 2x" width="300"/></a></div></div>
<p>The figure above demonstrates the use of a <a href="/wiki/Pyridine" title="Pyridine">pyridine</a> directing group to activate selectively a C-H bond to form a C-<a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogen</a> bond. Pyridine derivatives are commonly used for ortho-selective C-H functionalization. Such reactions use metals like palladium to catalyze sp<sup>2</sup> C-H activation. A similar system uses pyridine to acetoxylate the C-H bond, forming a C-<a href="/wiki/Acetoxy_group" title="Acetoxy group">OAc</a> bond, instead of a C-X halogen bond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sanford_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sanford-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sanford_Figure_1_Rxn.png" title="Pd-catalyzed C-H activation reaction"><img alt="Pd-catalyzed C-H activation reaction" data-file-height="868" data-file-width="2374" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Sanford_Figure_1_Rxn.png/300px-Sanford_Figure_1_Rxn.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Sanford_Figure_1_Rxn.png/450px-Sanford_Figure_1_Rxn.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Sanford_Figure_1_Rxn.png/600px-Sanford_Figure_1_Rxn.png 2x" width="300"/></a></div></div>
<p>The mechanism for the pyridine based Pd-catalyzed C-H activation reactions involves a catalytic cycle in which the ligand directs the molecule to interact with Pd to form a metallacycle intermediate. The intermediate is oxidized to form a Pd<sup>IV</sup> species, followed by reductive elimination to form the C-O bond and release the product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sanford_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sanford-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sanford_Figure_2_mechanism.png" title="Pd-catalyzed mechanism for C-H activation"><img alt="Pd-catalyzed mechanism for C-H activation" data-file-height="2659" data-file-width="2634" height="353" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Sanford_Figure_2_mechanism.png/350px-Sanford_Figure_2_mechanism.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Sanford_Figure_2_mechanism.png/525px-Sanford_Figure_2_mechanism.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Sanford_Figure_2_mechanism.png/700px-Sanford_Figure_2_mechanism.png 2x" width="350"/></a></div></div>
<p>See <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meta-selective_C-H_functionalization" title="Meta-selective C-H functionalization">Meta-selective C-H functionalization</a> for more examples of directed C-H activation.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction_conditions">Reaction conditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon%E2%80%93hydrogen_bond_activation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reaction conditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many C–H bond activations proceed under rather harsh reaction conditions (high temperature, strongly acidic or basic conditions, strong oxidant, etc.), significantly limiting their utility. However, mild methods have been developed, significantly expanding the scope of these transformations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Organocatalysis" title="Organocatalysis">Organocatalysis</a> is another important approach to facilitating C-H activation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Case_Study:_Borylation">Case Study: Borylation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon%E2%80%93hydrogen_bond_activation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Case Study: Borylation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Transforming C-H bonds into C-B bonds through <a href="/wiki/Borylation" title="Borylation">borylation</a> has been thoroughly investigated due to their utility in synthesis (i.e. for cross-coupling reactions). <a href="/wiki/John_F._Hartwig" title="John F. Hartwig">J. F. Hartwig</a> reported a highly regioselective arene and alkane borylation catalyzed by a rhodium complex.  In the case of alkanes, exclusive terminal functionalization was observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hartwig_figure_1_borylation.png" title="Hartwig borylation"><img alt="Hartwig borylation" data-file-height="494" data-file-width="3202" height="77" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Hartwig_figure_1_borylation.png/500px-Hartwig_figure_1_borylation.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Hartwig_figure_1_borylation.png/750px-Hartwig_figure_1_borylation.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Hartwig_figure_1_borylation.png/1000px-Hartwig_figure_1_borylation.png 2x" width="500"/></a></div></div>
<p>Later, ruthenium catalysts were discovered to have higher activity and functional group compatibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ru_cat._borylation.png" title="Ru catalyst based borylation"><img alt="Ru catalyst based borylation" data-file-height="454" data-file-width="1844" height="74" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Ru_cat._borylation.png/300px-Ru_cat._borylation.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Ru_cat._borylation.png/450px-Ru_cat._borylation.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Ru_cat._borylation.png/600px-Ru_cat._borylation.png 2x" width="300"/></a></div></div>
<p>Other borylation catalysts have also been developed, including iridium-based catalysts, which successfully activate C-H bonds with high compatibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>For more information, consult <a href="/wiki/Borylation" title="Borylation">borylation</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon%E2%80%93hydrogen_bond_activation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>No applications have been commercialized for homogeneously catalyzed C-H activations.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_gas">Natural gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon%E2%80%93hydrogen_bond_activation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Natural gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methane_Functionalization" title="Methane Functionalization">Methane Functionalization</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">Naturally occurring</a> <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> is not utilized as a chemical feedstock, despite its abundance and low cost. Current technology makes prodigious use of methane by steam reforming to produce syngas, a mixture of carbon monoxide and hydrogen. This syngas is then used in Fischer-Tropsch reactions to make longer carbon chain products or methanol, one of the most important industrial chemical feedstocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>   An intriguing methods to convert these hydrocarbons involves C-H activation. <a href="/wiki/Roy_A._Periana" title="Roy A. Periana">Periana</a>, for example, reported that complexes containing late transition metals, such as <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">Pt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palladium" title="Palladium">Pd</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">Au</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">Hg</a>, react with <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> (CH<sub>4</sub>) in H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> to yield <a href="/wiki/Methyl_bisulfate" title="Methyl bisulfate">methyl bisulfate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Natural_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Natural-34">[34]</a></sup>  The process has not however been implemented commercially.
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MethaneOxidationinH2SO4.png" title="C–H Bond Activation Periana 1998"><img alt="C–H Bond Activation Periana 1998" data-file-height="323" data-file-width="1038" height="86" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/MethaneOxidationinH2SO4.png/275px-MethaneOxidationinH2SO4.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/MethaneOxidationinH2SO4.png/413px-MethaneOxidationinH2SO4.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/MethaneOxidationinH2SO4.png/550px-MethaneOxidationinH2SO4.png 2x" width="275"/></a></div></div><p>Another approach is the use of Fe an Cu containing metallozeolites, employing highly reactive oxygen species such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha-Oxygen" title="Alpha-Oxygen">Alpha-Oxygen</a>. However, these systems operate stoichiometrically due to strong product adsorption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_product_synthesis">Natural product synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon%E2%80%93hydrogen_bond_activation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Natural product synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The development of methodology for C-H activation has significantly impacted natural product synthesis, at least in the academic realm. Ideally, synthetic routes contain minimal steps, while maximizing yield. C-H activation has enabled researchers to activate C-H bonds in highly functionalized molecules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Natural_Product_Synth_Figure_1.png" title="Example of synthesis of core leading to multiple natural products"><img alt="Example of synthesis of core leading to multiple natural products" data-file-height="795" data-file-width="3197" height="99" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Natural_Product_Synth_Figure_1.png/400px-Natural_Product_Synth_Figure_1.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Natural_Product_Synth_Figure_1.png/600px-Natural_Product_Synth_Figure_1.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Natural_Product_Synth_Figure_1.png/800px-Natural_Product_Synth_Figure_1.png 2x" width="400"/></a></div></div>
<p>The product in the above reaction is a common scaffold for multiple natural products, including hapalindole Q and ambiguine H. The core structure can be made through a C-C bond (blue bond in product) formation via C-H activation. The innate reactivity of the indole and enolate leads to the formation of the C-C bond to form the indole-carvone intermediate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bruckl_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-Bruckl-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id=".28.2B.29-Lithospermic_acid_via_C-H_activation"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="(+)-Lithospermic_acid_via_C-H_activation">(+)-Lithospermic acid via C-H activation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon%E2%80%93hydrogen_bond_activation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: (+)-Lithospermic acid via C-H activation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The total synthesis of lithospermic acid employs guided C-H functionalization late stage to a highly functionalized system. The directing group, a <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)" title="Chirality (chemistry)">chiral</a> nonracemic imine, is capable of performing an intramolecular alkylation, which allows for the rhodium-catalyzed conversion of imine to the dihydrobenzofuran.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Natural_Product_Synth_Ellman_Figure_1.png" title="Key step in synthesis of lithospermic acid"><img alt="Key step in synthesis of lithospermic acid" data-file-height="1182" data-file-width="6966" height="127" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Natural_Product_Synth_Ellman_Figure_1.png/750px-Natural_Product_Synth_Ellman_Figure_1.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Natural_Product_Synth_Ellman_Figure_1.png/1125px-Natural_Product_Synth_Ellman_Figure_1.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Natural_Product_Synth_Ellman_Figure_1.png/1500px-Natural_Product_Synth_Ellman_Figure_1.png 2x" width="750"/></a></div></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Calothrixin_A_and_B_via_C-H_activation">Calothrixin A and B via C-H activation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon%E2%80%93hydrogen_bond_activation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Calothrixin A and B via C-H activation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The total synthesis of calothrixin A and B features an intramolecular Pd-catalyzed cross coupling reaction via C-H activation, an example of a guided C-H activation. Cross coupling occurs between aryl C-I and C-H bonds to form a C-C bond (highlighted in red).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Natural_Product_Synth_Nagarajan_Figure_1.png" title="Key step in total synthesis of Calothrixin A and B"><img alt="Key step in total synthesis of Calothrixin A and B" data-file-height="1053" data-file-width="6967" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Natural_Product_Synth_Nagarajan_Figure_1.png/750px-Natural_Product_Synth_Nagarajan_Figure_1.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Natural_Product_Synth_Nagarajan_Figure_1.png/1125px-Natural_Product_Synth_Nagarajan_Figure_1.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Natural_Product_Synth_Nagarajan_Figure_1.png/1500px-Natural_Product_Synth_Nagarajan_Figure_1.png 2x" width="750"/></a></div></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mescaline_analogue_via_C-H_activation">Mescaline analogue via C-H activation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon%E2%80%93hydrogen_bond_activation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Mescaline analogue via C-H activation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The synthesis of a Mescaline analogue, which has interesting biological properties, employs, specifically, the <a href="/wiki/Rhodium" title="Rhodium">rhodium</a>-catalyzed enantioselective annulation of an aryl imine via a C-H activation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
