<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Bohr effect</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Not to be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Bohr_equation" title="Bohr equation">Bohr equation</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Christian_Bohr_u016a.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1363" data-file-width="1057" height="284" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Christian_Bohr_u016a.jpg/220px-Christian_Bohr_u016a.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Christian_Bohr_u016a.jpg/330px-Christian_Bohr_u016a.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Christian_Bohr_u016a.jpg/440px-Christian_Bohr_u016a.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Christian_Bohr_u016a.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Christian Bohr, who was credited with the discovery of the effect in 1904.</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Bohr effect</b> is a physiological phenomenon first described in 1904 by the Danish physiologist <a href="/wiki/Christian_Bohr" title="Christian Bohr">Christian Bohr</a>: <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a>'s oxygen binding affinity (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxygen%E2%80%93haemoglobin_dissociation_curve" title="Oxygen–haemoglobin dissociation curve">oxygen–haemoglobin dissociation curve</a>) is inversely related both to acidity and to the concentration of carbon dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-1">[1]</a></sup> That is, the Bohr effect refers to the shift in the oxygen dissociation curve caused by changes in the concentration of carbon dioxide or the pH of the environment. Since carbon dioxide reacts with water to form <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a>, an increase in CO<sub>2</sub> results in a decrease in blood <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voet_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Voet-2">[2]</a></sup> resulting in hemoglobin proteins releasing their load of oxygen. Conversely, a decrease in carbon dioxide provokes an increase in pH, which results in hemoglobin picking up more oxygen.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Experimental_discovery">Experimental discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bohr_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Experimental discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:293px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bohr_effect.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1005" data-file-width="1398" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Bohr_effect.png/291px-Bohr_effect.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Bohr_effect.png/437px-Bohr_effect.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Bohr_effect.png/582px-Bohr_effect.png 2x" width="291"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bohr_effect.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The original dissociation curves from Bohr's experiments, showing a decrease in oxygen affinity as the partial pressure of carbon dioxide increases. The curves were obtained using whole dog blood, with the exception of the dashed curve, for which horse blood was used.</div></div></div><p>In the early 1900s, Christian Bohr was a professor at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Copenhagen" title="University of Copenhagen">University of Copenhagen</a> in Denmark, already well known for his work in the field of respiratory physiology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:5-3">[3]</a></sup> He had spent the last two decades studying the solubility of oxygen, carbon dioxide, and other gases in various liquids,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-4">[4]</a></sup> and had conducted extensive research on haemoglobin and its affinity for oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-:5-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1903, he began working closely with <a href="/wiki/Karl_Albert_Hasselbalch" title="Karl Albert Hasselbalch">Karl Hasselbalch</a> and <a href="/wiki/August_Krogh" title="August Krogh">August Krogh</a>, two of his associates at the university, in an attempt to experimentally replicate the work of <a href="/wiki/Gustav_von_H%C3%BCfner" title="Gustav von Hüfner">Gustav von Hüfner</a>, using whole blood instead of haemoglobin solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-1">[1]</a></sup> Hüfner had suggested that the oxygen-haemoglobin binding curve was <a href="/wiki/Hyperbolic_function" title="Hyperbolic function">hyperbolic</a> in shape,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> but after extensive experimentation, the Copenhagen group determined that the curve was in fact <a href="/wiki/Sigmoid_function" title="Sigmoid function">sigmoidal</a>. Furthermore, in the process of plotting out numerous dissociation curves, it soon became apparent that high partial pressures of carbon dioxide caused the curves to shift to the right.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-4">[4]</a></sup> Further experimentation while varying the CO<sub>2</sub> concentration quickly provided conclusive evidence, confirming the existence of what would soon become known as the Bohr effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-:2-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bohr_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is some more debate over whether Bohr was actually the first to discover the relationship between CO<sub>2</sub> and oxygen affinity, or whether the Russian physiologist Bronislav Verigo beat him to it, allegedly discovering the effect in 1898, six years before Bohr.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> While this has never been proven, Verigo did in fact publish a paper on the haemoglobin-CO<sub>2</sub> relationship in 1892.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> His proposed model was flawed, and Bohr harshly criticized it in his own publications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-:2-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another challenge to Bohr's discovery comes from within his lab. Though Bohr was quick to take full credit, his associate Krogh, who invented the apparatus used to measure gas concentrations in the experiments,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> maintained throughout his life that he himself had actually been the first to demonstrate the effect. Though there is some evidence to support this, retroactively changing the name of a well-known phenomenon would be extremely impractical, so it remains known as the Bohr effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-:3-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physiological_role">Physiological role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bohr_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physiological role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Bohr effect increases the efficiency of oxygen transportation through the blood. After hemoglobin binds to oxygen in the lungs due to the high oxygen concentrations, the Bohr effect facilitates its release in the tissues, particularly those tissues in most need of oxygen. When a tissue's metabolic rate increases, so does its carbon dioxide waste production. When released into the bloodstream, carbon dioxide forms <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> and protons through the following reaction:

<p>Although this reaction usually proceeds very slowly, the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_anhydrase" title="Carbonic anhydrase">carbonic anhydrase</a> (which is present in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_blood_cells" title="Red blood cells">red blood cells</a>) drastically speeds up the conversion to bicarbonate and protons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voet_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Voet-2">[2]</a></sup> This causes the pH of the blood to decrease, which promotes the dissociation of oxygen from haemoglobin, and allows the surrounding tissues to obtain enough oxygen to meet their demands. In areas where oxygen concentration is high, such as the lungs, binding of oxygen causes haemoglobin to release protons, which recombine with bicarbonate to eliminate carbon dioxide during <a href="/wiki/Exhalation" title="Exhalation">exhalation</a>. These opposing protonation and deprotonation reactions occur <a href="/wiki/Steady_state_(chemistry)" title="Steady state (chemistry)">at an equal rate</a>, resulting in little overall change in blood pH.
</p><p>
The Bohr effect enables the body to adapt to changing conditions and makes it possible to supply extra oxygen to tissues that need it the most. For example, when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muscles" title="Muscles">muscles</a> are undergoing strenuous activity, they require large amounts of oxygen to conduct <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">cellular respiration</a>, which generates CO<sub>2</sub> (and therefore HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup> and H<sup>+</sup>) as byproducts. These waste products lower the pH of the blood, which increases oxygen delivery to the active muscles. Carbon dioxide is not the only molecule that can trigger the Bohr effect. If muscle cells aren't receiving enough oxygen for cellular respiration, they resort to <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid_fermentation" title="Lactic acid fermentation">lactic acid fermentation</a>, which releases <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactic acid</a> as a byproduct. This increases the acidity of the blood far more than CO<sub>2</sub> alone, which reflects the cells' even greater need for oxygen. In fact, under anaerobic conditions, muscles generate lactic acid so quickly that pH of the blood passing through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muscles" title="Muscles">muscles</a> will drop to around 7.2, which causes haemoglobin to begin releasing roughly 10% more oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voet_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Voet-2">[2]</a></sup></p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bohr_Effect_Magnitude_vs_Body_Size.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="591" data-file-width="469" height="277" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Bohr_Effect_Magnitude_vs_Body_Size.png/220px-Bohr_Effect_Magnitude_vs_Body_Size.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Bohr_Effect_Magnitude_vs_Body_Size.png/330px-Bohr_Effect_Magnitude_vs_Body_Size.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Bohr_Effect_Magnitude_vs_Body_Size.png/440px-Bohr_Effect_Magnitude_vs_Body_Size.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bohr_Effect_Magnitude_vs_Body_Size.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The magnitude of the Bohr effect is given by <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\textstyle {\scriptstyle \Delta \log(P_{50}) \over \Delta {\text{pH}}}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism">Mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bohr_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Allosteric_interactions">Allosteric interactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bohr_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Allosteric interactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:280px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hemoglobin_t-r_state_ani.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="301" data-file-width="400" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Hemoglobin_t-r_state_ani.gif/278px-Hemoglobin_t-r_state_ani.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/ba/Hemoglobin_t-r_state_ani.gif 1.5x" width="278"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hemoglobin_t-r_state_ani.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Haemoglobin changes conformation from a high-affinity R state (oxygenated) to a low-affinity T state (deoxygenated) to improve oxygen uptake and delivery.</div></div></div><p>The Bohr effect hinges around allosteric interactions between the <a href="/wiki/Heme" title="Heme">hemes</a> of the haemoglobin <a href="/wiki/Tetrameric_protein" title="Tetrameric protein">tetramer</a>, a mechanism first proposed by Max Perutz in 1970.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Haemoglobin exists in two conformations: a high-affinity R state and a low-affinity T state. When oxygen concentration levels are high, as in the lungs, the R state is favored, enabling the maximum amount of oxygen to be bound to the hemes. In the capillaries, where oxygen concentration levels are lower, the T state is favored, in order to facilitate the delivery of oxygen to the tissues. The Bohr effect is dependent on this allostery, as increases in CO<sub>2</sub> and H<sup>+</sup> help stabilize the T state and ensure greater oxygen delivery to muscles during periods of elevated cellular respiration. This is evidenced by the fact that <a href="/wiki/Myoglobin" title="Myoglobin">myoglobin</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Monomer" title="Monomer">monomer</a> with no allostery, does not exhibit the Bohr effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voet_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Voet-2">[2]</a></sup> Haemoglobin mutants with weaker allostery may exhibit a reduced Bohr effect. For example, in Hiroshima variant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haemoglobinopathy" title="Haemoglobinopathy">haemoglobinopathy</a>, allostery in haemoglobin is reduced, and the Bohr effect is diminished. As a result, during periods of exercise, the mutant haemoglobin has a higher affinity for oxygen and tissue may suffer minor <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(medical)" title="Hypoxia (medical)">oxygen starvation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="T-state_stabilization">T-state stabilization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bohr_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: T-state stabilization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When hemoglobin is in its T state, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N-terminal" title="N-terminal">N-terminal</a> amino groups of the α-subunits and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-terminal" title="C-terminal">C-terminal</a> <a href="/wiki/Histidine" title="Histidine">histidine</a> of the β-subunits are protonated, giving them a positive charge and allowing these residues to participate in <a href="/wiki/Ionic_bonding" title="Ionic bonding">ionic interactions</a> with carboxyl groups on nearby residues. These interactions help hold the haemoglobin in the T state. Decreases in pH (increases in acidity) stabilize this state even more, since a decrease in pH makes these residues even more likely to be protonated, strengthening the ionic interactions. In the R state, the ionic pairings are absent, meaning that the R state's stability increases when the pH increases, as these residues are less likely to stay deprotonated in a more basic environment. The Bohr effect works by simultaneously destabilizing the high-affinity R state and stabilizing the low-affinity T state, which leads to an overall decrease in oxygen affinity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voet_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Voet-2">[2]</a></sup> This can be visualized on an <a href="/wiki/Oxygen%E2%80%93hemoglobin_dissociation_curve" title="Oxygen–hemoglobin dissociation curve">oxygen-haemoglobin dissociation curve</a> by shifting the whole curve to the right.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_monoxide">Carbon monoxide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bohr_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Carbon monoxide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another special case of the Bohr effect occurs when <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> is present. This molecule serves as a <a href="/wiki/Competitive_inhibition" title="Competitive inhibition">competitive inhibitor</a> for oxygen, and binds to haemoglobin to form <a href="/wiki/Carboxyhemoglobin" title="Carboxyhemoglobin">carboxyhaemoglobin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-13">[13]</a></sup> haemoglobin's affinity for CO is about 250 times stronger than its affinity for O<sub>2</sub>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> meaning that it is very unlikely to dissociate, and once bound, it blocks the binding of O<sub>2</sub> to that subunit. At the same time, CO is structurally similar enough to O<sub>2</sub> to cause carboxyhaemoglobin to favor the R state, raising the oxygen affinity of the remaining unoccupied subunits. This combination significantly reduces the delivery of oxygen to the tissues of the body, which is what makes carbon monoxide so <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide_poisoning" title="Carbon monoxide poisoning">toxic</a>. This toxicity is reduced slightly by an increase in the strength of the Bohr effect in the presence of carboxyhaemoglobin. This increase is ultimately due to differences in interactions between heme groups in carboxyhaemoglobin relative to oxygenated haemoglobin. It is most pronounced when the oxygen concentration is extremely low, as a last-ditch effort when the need for oxygen delivery becomes critical. However, the physiological implications of this phenomenon remain unclear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
