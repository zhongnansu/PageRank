<p>The <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> <b>olivine</b> (<span class="nowrap"><small> </small><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> <a href="/wiki/Silicate_minerals" title="Silicate minerals">silicate</a> with the formula (<a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">Mg</a><sup>2+</sup>, <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">Fe</a><sup>2+</sup>)<sub>2</sub><a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">Si</a><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a><sub>4</sub>. Thus it is a type of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nesosilicates" title="Nesosilicates">nesosilicate or orthosilicate</a>. It is a common mineral in Earth's subsurface but weathers quickly on the surface.
</p><p>The ratio of magnesium to iron varies between the two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endmember_(mineralogy)" title="Endmember (mineralogy)">endmembers</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Solid_solution" title="Solid solution">solid solution</a> series: <a href="/wiki/Forsterite" title="Forsterite">forsterite</a> (Mg-endmember: <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">Mg</a><sub>2</sub><a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">Si</a><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a><sub>4</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Fayalite" title="Fayalite">fayalite</a> (Fe-endmember: <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">Fe</a><sub>2</sub><a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">Si</a><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a><sub>4</sub>). Compositions of olivine are commonly expressed as <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">molar</a> percentages of forsterite (Fo) and fayalite (Fa) (e.g., Fo<sub>70</sub>Fa<sub>30</sub>). Forsterite's melting temperature is unusually high at atmospheric pressure, almost 1,900 °C (3,450 °F), while fayalite's is much lower (about 1,200 °C [2,190 °F]). Melting temperature varies smoothly between the two endmembers, as do other properties. Olivine incorporates only minor amounts of elements other than <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>. <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">Manganese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a> commonly are the additional elements present in highest concentrations.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Olivine_in_polarizing_light.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="860" data-file-width="980" height="193" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Olivine_in_polarizing_light.jpg/220px-Olivine_in_polarizing_light.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Olivine_in_polarizing_light.jpg/330px-Olivine_in_polarizing_light.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Olivine_in_polarizing_light.jpg/440px-Olivine_in_polarizing_light.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Olivine_in_polarizing_light.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Olivine in polarizing light</div></div></div>
<p>Olivine gives its name to the group of minerals with a related structure (the <b>olivine group</b>)—which includes <a href="/wiki/Tephroite" title="Tephroite">tephroite</a> (<a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">Mn</a><sub>2</sub>SiO<sub>4</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Monticellite" title="Monticellite">monticellite</a> (<a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">Ca</a>MgSiO<sub>4</sub>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kirschsteinite" title="Kirschsteinite">kirschsteinite</a> (CaFeSiO<sub>4</sub>).
</p><p>Olivine's crystal structure incorporates aspects of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthorhombic" title="Orthorhombic">orthorhombic</a> P <a href="/wiki/Bravais_lattice" title="Bravais lattice">Bravais lattice</a>, which arise from each silica (SiO<sub>4</sub>) unit being joined by metal divalent cations with each oxygen in SiO<sub>4</sub> bound to 3 metal ions. It has a <a href="/wiki/Spinel" title="Spinel">spinel</a>-like structure similar to magnetite but uses one quadrivalent and two divalent cations M<sub>2</sub><sup>2+</sup> M<sup>4+</sup>O<sub>4</sub> instead of two trivalent and one divalent cations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Olivine <a href="/wiki/Gemstone" title="Gemstone">gemstones</a> are called <a href="/wiki/Peridot" title="Peridot">peridot</a> and chrysolite.
</p><p>Olivine rock is usually harder than surrounding rock and stands out as distinct ridges in the terrain. These ridges are often dry with little soil. Drought resistant <a href="/wiki/Scots_pine" title="Scots pine">scots pine</a> is one of few trees that thrive on olivine rock. Olivine pine forest is unique to Norway. It is rare and found on dry olivine ridges in the fjord districts of Sunnmøre and Nordfjord. Olivine rock is hard and base-rich. The habitat is endangered by mining and road construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Identification_and_paragenesis">Identification and paragenesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olivine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Identification and paragenesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Papakolea_Beach_sand_high_mag_052915.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Papakolea_Beach_sand_high_mag_052915.jpg/220px-Papakolea_Beach_sand_high_mag_052915.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Papakolea_Beach_sand_high_mag_052915.jpg/330px-Papakolea_Beach_sand_high_mag_052915.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Papakolea_Beach_sand_high_mag_052915.jpg/440px-Papakolea_Beach_sand_high_mag_052915.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Papakolea_Beach_sand_high_mag_052915.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Olivine grains that eroded from <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava</a> on <a href="/wiki/Papakolea_Beach" title="Papakolea Beach">Papakolea Beach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a></div></div></div>

<p>Olivine is named for its typically olive-green color (thought to be a result of traces of <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">nickel</a>), though it may alter to a reddish color from the oxidation of iron.
</p><p>Translucent olivine is sometimes used as a <a href="/wiki/Gemstone" title="Gemstone">gemstone</a> called <a href="/wiki/Peridot" title="Peridot">peridot</a> (<i>péridot</i>, the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> word for olivine). It is also called chrysolite (or <i>chrysolithe</i>, from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> words for <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> and stone). Some of the finest gem-quality olivine has been obtained from a body of <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> rocks on <a href="/wiki/Zabargad_Island" title="Zabargad Island">Zabargad Island</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a>.
</p><p>Olivine occurs in both <a href="/wiki/Mafic" title="Mafic">mafic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultramafic" title="Ultramafic">ultramafic</a> <a href="/wiki/Igneous_rock" title="Igneous rock">igneous rocks</a> and as a primary mineral in certain <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphic rocks</a>. Mg-rich olivine crystallizes from <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> that is rich in magnesium and low in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">silica</a>. That magma crystallizes to mafic rocks such as <a href="/wiki/Gabbro" title="Gabbro">gabbro</a> and <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basalt</a>. Ultramafic rocks such as <a href="/wiki/Peridotite" title="Peridotite">peridotite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dunite" title="Dunite">dunite</a> can be residues left after extraction of magmas, and typically they are more enriched in olivine after extraction of partial melts. Olivine and high pressure structural variants constitute over 50% of the Earth's upper mantle, and olivine is one of the Earth's most common minerals by volume.  The <a href="/wiki/Metamorphism" title="Metamorphism">metamorphism</a> of impure <a href="/wiki/Dolomite" title="Dolomite">dolomite</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rocks</a> with high magnesium and low silica content also produces Mg-rich olivine, or <a href="/wiki/Forsterite" title="Forsterite">forsterite</a>.
</p><p>Fe-rich olivine is relatively much less common, but it occurs in <a href="/wiki/Igneous_rock" title="Igneous rock">igneous rocks</a> in small amounts in rare <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rhyolite" title="Rhyolite">rhyolites</a>, and extremely Fe-rich olivine can exist stably with <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tridymite" title="Tridymite">tridymite</a>. In contrast, Mg-rich olivine does not occur stably with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">silica</a> minerals, as it would react with them to form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthopyroxene" title="Orthopyroxene">orthopyroxene</a> ((Mg,Fe)<sub>2</sub>Si<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>).
</p><p>Mg-rich olivine is stable to pressures equivalent to a depth of about 410 km (250 mi) within Earth. Because it is thought to be the most abundant mineral in Earth’s mantle at shallower depths, the properties of olivine have a dominant influence upon the <a href="/wiki/Rheology" title="Rheology">rheology</a> of that part of Earth and hence upon the solid flow that drives <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a>. Experiments have documented that olivine at high pressures (e.g. 12 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPa" title="GPa">GPa</a>, the pressure at depths of about 360 km (220 mi)) can contain at least as much as about 8900 parts per million (weight) of water, and that such water contents drastically reduce the resistance of olivine to solid flow; moreover, because olivine is so abundant, more water may be dissolved in olivine of the mantle than contained in Earth's oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraterrestrial_occurrences">Extraterrestrial occurrences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olivine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Extraterrestrial occurrences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mg-rich olivine has also been discovered in <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">meteorites</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> on the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MareBasalt1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-MareBasalt1-13">[13]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> falling into infant stars,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> as well as on asteroid <a href="/wiki/25143_Itokawa" title="25143 Itokawa">25143 Itokawa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>  Such meteorites include <a href="/wiki/Chondrite" title="Chondrite">chondrites</a>, collections of debris from the early <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Pallasite" title="Pallasite">pallasites</a>, mixes of iron-nickel and olivine.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Spectral_signature" title="Spectral signature">spectral signature</a> of olivine has been seen in the dust disks around young stars. The tails of comets (which formed from the dust disk around the young <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>) often have the spectral signature of olivine, and the presence of olivine was verified in samples of a comet from the <a href="/wiki/Stardust_(spacecraft)#Sample_analysis" title="Stardust (spacecraft)">Stardust spacecraft</a> in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Comet-like (magnesium-rich) olivine has also been detected in the <a href="/wiki/Planetesimal" title="Planetesimal">planetesimal</a> belt around the star <a href="/wiki/Beta_Pictoris" title="Beta Pictoris">Beta Pictoris</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deVries2012_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-deVries2012-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Crystal_structure">Crystal structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olivine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Crystal structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Atomic_structure_of_olivine_1.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="258" data-file-width="300" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Atomic_structure_of_olivine_1.png/220px-Atomic_structure_of_olivine_1.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cc/Atomic_structure_of_olivine_1.png 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Atomic_structure_of_olivine_1.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><b>Figure 1:</b> The atomic scale structure of olivine looking along the <i>a</i> axis. Oxygen is shown in red, silicon in pink, and magnesium/iron in blue. A projection of the unit cell is shown by the black rectangle.</div></div></div>
<p>Minerals in the olivine group crystallize in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthorhombic" title="Orthorhombic">orthorhombic</a> system (<a href="/wiki/Space_group" title="Space group">space group</a> P<i>bnm</i>) with isolated silicate tetrahedra, meaning that olivine is a <a href="/wiki/Silicate_minerals" title="Silicate minerals">nesosilicate</a>.  In an alternative view, the atomic structure can be described as a hexagonal, close-packed array of oxygen <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> with half of the octahedral sites occupied with magnesium or iron ions and one-eighth of the tetrahedral sites occupied by silicon ions.
</p><p>There are three distinct oxygen sites (marked O1, O2 and O3 in figure 1), two distinct metal sites (M1 and M2) and only one distinct silicon site.  O1, O2, M2 and Si all lie on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mirror_plane" title="Mirror plane">mirror planes</a>, while M1 exists on an inversion center. O3 lies in a general position.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="High_pressure_polymorphs">High pressure polymorphs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olivine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: High pressure polymorphs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the high temperatures and pressures found at depth within the Earth the olivine structure is no longer stable. Below depths of about 410 km (250 mi) olivine undergoes an exothermic <a href="/wiki/Phase_transition" title="Phase transition">phase transition</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Silicate_minerals" title="Silicate minerals">sorosilicate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wadsleyite" title="Wadsleyite">wadsleyite</a> and, at about 520 km (320 mi) depth, wadsleyite transforms exothermically into <a href="/wiki/Ringwoodite" title="Ringwoodite">ringwoodite</a>, which has the <a href="/wiki/Spinel" title="Spinel">spinel</a> structure. At a depth of about 660 km (410 mi), ringwoodite decomposes into <a href="/wiki/Silicate_perovskite" title="Silicate perovskite">silicate perovskite</a> ((Mg,Fe)SiO<sub>3</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Ferropericlase" title="Ferropericlase">ferropericlase</a> ((Mg,Fe)O) in an endothermic reaction. These phase transitions lead to a discontinuous increase in the density of the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> that can be observed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seismic" title="Seismic">seismic</a> methods. They are also thought to influence the dynamics of <a href="/wiki/Mantle_convection" title="Mantle convection">mantle convection</a> in that the exothermic transitions reinforce flow across the phase boundary, whereas the endothermic reaction hampers it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>The pressure at which these phase transitions occur depends on temperature and iron content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>  At 800 °C (1,070 K; 1,470 °F), the pure magnesium end member, forsterite, transforms to wadsleyite at 11.8 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">gigapascals</a> (116,000 <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">atm</a>) and to ringwoodite at pressures above 14 GPa (138,000 atm).  Increasing the iron content decreases the pressure of the phase transition and narrows the <a href="/wiki/Wadsleyite" title="Wadsleyite">wadsleyite</a> stability field.  At about 0.8 <a href="/wiki/Mole_fraction" title="Mole fraction">mole fraction</a> fayalite, olivine transforms directly to ringwoodite over the pressure range 10.0 to 11.5 GPa (99,000–113,000 atm).  Fayalite transforms to <span class="chemf nowrap">Fe<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>SiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> spinel at pressures below 5 GPa (49,000 atm). Increasing the temperature increases the pressure of these phase transitions.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Weathering">Weathering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olivine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Weathering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Iddingsite.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Iddingsite.JPG/220px-Iddingsite.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Iddingsite.JPG/330px-Iddingsite.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Iddingsite.JPG/440px-Iddingsite.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Iddingsite.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Olivine altered to iddingsite within a <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> <a href="/wiki/Xenolith" title="Xenolith">xenolith</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Olivine is one of the weaker common minerals on the surface according to the <a href="/wiki/Goldich_dissolution_series" title="Goldich dissolution series">Goldich dissolution series</a>.  It alters into <a href="/wiki/Iddingsite" title="Iddingsite">iddingsite</a> (a combination of clay minerals, iron oxides and <a href="/wiki/Ferrihydrite" title="Ferrihydrite">ferrihydrites</a>) readily in the presence of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>  Artificially increasing the weathering rate of olivine e.g. by dispersing fine-grained olivine on beaches has been proposed as a cheap way to sequester CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The presence of iddingsite on Mars would suggest that liquid water once existed there, and might enable scientists to determine when there was last liquid water on the planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mining">Mining</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olivine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mining">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Norway">Norway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olivine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Norway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Norway is the main source of olivine in Europe, particularly in an area stretching from <a href="/wiki/%C3%85heim" title="Åheim">Åheim</a> to <a href="/wiki/Tafjord" title="Tafjord">Tafjord</a>, and from <a href="/wiki/Hornindal" title="Hornindal">Hornindal</a> to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Flems%C3%B8y&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Flemsøy (page does not exist)">Flemsøy</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Sunnm%C3%B8re" title="Sunnmøre">Sunnmøre</a> district. There is also olivine in <a href="/wiki/Eid,_Norway" title="Eid, Norway">Eid</a> municipality. About 50 % of the world's olivine for industrial use is produced in Norway. At Svarthammaren in <a href="/wiki/Norddal" title="Norddal">Norddal</a> olivine was mined from around 1920 to 1979, with a daily output up to 600 metric tons. Olivine was also obtained from the construction site of the hydro power stations in Tafjord. At Robbervika in Norddal municipality an open-pit mine has been in operation since 1984. The characteristic red color is reflected in several local names with "red" such as <i>Raudbergvik</i> (Red rock bay) or <i>Raudnakken</i> (Red ridge).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sunnylvsfjord_MS-Midnatsol.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1392" data-file-width="1744" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Sunnylvsfjord_MS-Midnatsol.jpg/220px-Sunnylvsfjord_MS-Midnatsol.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Sunnylvsfjord_MS-Midnatsol.jpg/330px-Sunnylvsfjord_MS-Midnatsol.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Sunnylvsfjord_MS-Midnatsol.jpg/440px-Sunnylvsfjord_MS-Midnatsol.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sunnylvsfjord_MS-Midnatsol.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Open-pit mining at <a href="/wiki/Sunnylvsfjorden" title="Sunnylvsfjorden">Sunnylvsfjorden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurtigruten" title="Hurtigruten">Hurtigruten</a> ship passing.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hans_Str%C3%B8m" title="Hans Strøm">Hans Strøm</a> in 1766 described the olivine's typical red color on the surface and the blue color within. Strøm wrote that in Norddal district large quantities of olivine were broken from the bedrock and used as <a href="/wiki/Sharpening_stone" title="Sharpening stone">sharpening stones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kallskaret&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kallskaret (page does not exist)">Kallskaret</a> near Tafjord is a nature reserve with olivine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olivine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A worldwide search is on for cheap processes to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">sequester CO<sub>2</sub></a> by mineral reactions, called <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_weathering" title="Enhanced weathering">enhanced weathering</a>. Removal by reactions with olivine is an attractive option, because it is widely available and reacts easily with the (acid) CO<sub>2</sub> from the atmosphere. When olivine is <a href="/wiki/Comminution" title="Comminution">crushed</a>, it weathers completely within a few years, depending on the grain size. All the CO<sub>2</sub> that is produced by burning one liter of oil can be sequestered by less than one liter of olivine. The reaction is exothermic but slow. To recover the heat produced by the reaction to produce electricity, a large volume of olivine must be thermally well-isolated. The end-products of the reaction are <a href="/wiki/Silicon_dioxide" title="Silicon dioxide">silicon dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_carbonate" title="Magnesium carbonate">magnesium carbonate</a>, and small amounts of iron oxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>Olivine is used as a substitute for <a href="/wiki/Dolomite" title="Dolomite">dolomite</a> in steel works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Olivine is also used to tap blast furnaces in the steel industry, acting as a plug, removed in each steel run.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The aluminium foundry industry uses olivine sand to cast objects in aluminium. Olivine sand requires less water than silica sands while still holding the mold together during handling and pouring of the metal. Less water means less gas (steam) to vent from the mold as metal is poured into the mold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>In Finland, olivine is marketed as an ideal rock for <a href="/wiki/Sauna" title="Sauna">sauna</a> stoves because of its comparatively high density and resistance to weathering under repeated heating and cooling.
</p>
