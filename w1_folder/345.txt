<p><b>Isobutanol</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAC_nomenclature" title="IUPAC nomenclature">IUPAC nomenclature</a>: <b>2-methylpropan-1-ol</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> (CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>CHCH<sub>2</sub>OH (sometimes <a href="/wiki/Skeletal_formula#Pseudoelement_symbols" title="Skeletal formula">represented</a> as <b><i>i</i>-BuOH</b>). This colorless, flammable liquid with a characteristic smell is mainly used as a solvent. Its <a href="/wiki/Isomer" title="Isomer">isomers</a>, the other <a href="/wiki/Butanol" title="Butanol">butanols</a>, include <a href="/wiki/N-Butanol" title="N-Butanol"><i>n</i>-butanol</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2-butanol" title="2-butanol">2-butanol</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tert-Butanol" title="Tert-Butanol"><i>tert</i>-butanol</a>, all of which are important industrially.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isobutanol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Isobutanol is produced by the <a href="/wiki/Carbonylation" title="Carbonylation">carbonylation</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propylene" title="Propylene">propylene</a>.  Two methods are practiced industrially, <a href="/wiki/Hydroformylation" title="Hydroformylation">hydroformylation</a> is more common and generates a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Isobutyraldehyde" title="Isobutyraldehyde">isobutyraldehydes</a>, which are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogenate" title="Hydrogenate">hydrogenated</a> to the alcohols and then separated. Reppe carbonylation is also practiced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biosynthesis_of_Isobutanol">Biosynthesis of Isobutanol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isobutanol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Biosynthesis of Isobutanol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Higher-chain alcohols have energy densities close to gasoline, are not as volatile or corrosive as ethanol, and do not readily absorb water. Furthermore, branched-chain alcohols, such as isobutanol, have higher-octane numbers, resulting in less knocking in engines. Although produced naturally during the fermentation of carbohydrates and may also be a byproduct of the decay process of organic matter, Isobutanol or C5 alcohols have never been produced from a renewable source with yields high enough to make them viable as a gasoline substitute before the 2008 Nature article that produced over 20g/L isobutanol from glucose in E.coli.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>To modify an organism to produce these compounds usually results in toxicity in the cell.  This difficulty was bypassed by leveraging the native metabolic networks in E. coli but altered its intracellular chemistry using genetic engineering to produce these alcohols. Key pathways in E. coli were modified to produce several higher-chain alcohols from glucose, including isobutanol, 1-butanol, 2-methyl-1-butanol, 3-methyl-1-butanol, and 2-phenylethanol. This strategy exploits the E. coli host's highly active amino acid biosynthetic pathway by shifting part of it to alcohol production. It is proposed that these unusual alcohols can be produced as efficiently as the biosynthesis of ethanol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p><i><b>Escherichia coli</b></i>
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">Escherichia coli</a></i>, or <i>E. coli</i>, is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gram-negative" title="Gram-negative">Gram-negative</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bacillus_(shape)" title="Bacillus (shape)">rod-shaped</a> <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>.  <i>E. coli</i> is the microorganism most likely to move on to commercial production of isobutanol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_Review_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nature_Review-7">[7]</a></sup> In its engineered form <i>E. coli</i> produces the highest yields of isobutanol of any microorganism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">[6]</a></sup> Methods such as <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_network_modelling#Metabolic_network_simulation" title="Metabolic network modelling">elementary mode analysis</a> have been used to improve the <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolic efficiency</a> of <i>E. coli</i> so that larger quantities of isobutanol may be produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elucidating_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Elucidating-8">[8]</a></sup> <i>E. coli</i> is an ideal isobutanol bio-synthesizer for several reasons:
</p>
<ul><li><i>E. coli</i> is an organism for which several tools of genetic manipulation exist, and it is an organism for which an extensive body of scientific literature exists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_Review_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nature_Review-7">[7]</a></sup> This wealth of knowledge allows <i>E. coli</i> to be easily modified by scientists.</li>
<li><i>E. coli</i> has the capacity to use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lignocellulose" title="Lignocellulose">lignocellulose</a> (waste plant matter left over from agriculture) in the synthesis of isobutanol. The use of lignocellulose prevents <i>E. coli</i> from using plant matter meant for human consumption, and prevents any food-fuel price relationship which would occur from the biosynthesis of isobutanol by <i>E. coli</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_Review_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nature_Review-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>Genetic modification has been used to broaden the scope of lignocellulose which can be used by <i>E. coli</i>. This has made <i>E. coli</i> a useful and diverse isobutanol bio-synthesizer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_new_carbon_catabolite_repression_mutation_of_Escherichia_coli,_mlc*,_and_its_use_for_producing_isobutanol_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-A_new_carbon_catabolite_repression_mutation_of_Escherichia_coli,_mlc*,_and_its_use_for_producing_isobutanol-9">[9]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The primary drawback of <i>E. coli</i> is that it is susceptible to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacteriophages" title="Bacteriophages">bacteriophages</a> when being grown. This susceptibility could potentially shut down entire bioreactors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_Review_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Nature_Review-7">[7]</a></sup> Furthermore, the native reaction pathway for isobutanol in <i>E. coli</i> functions optimally at a limited concentration of isobutanol in the cell. To reduce the sensitivity of <i>E. coli</i> in high concentrations, mutants of the <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> involved in synthesis can be generated by random <a href="/wiki/Mutagenesis" title="Mutagenesis">mutagenesis</a>. By chance, some mutants may prove to be more tolerant of isobutanol which will enhance the overall yield of the synthesis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Clostridium</b>
</p><p>While cellulosic biomass like corn stover and switchgrass is abundant and cheap, it is much more difficult to utilize than corn and sugar cane. This is due in large part because of recalcitrance, or a plant's natural defenses to being chemically dismantled. Adding to the complexity is the fact biofuel production that involves several steps—pretreatment, enzyme treatment and fermentation—is more costly than a method that combines biomass utilization and the fermentation of sugars to biofuel into a single process.
</p><p>To make the conversion possible, researchers had to develop a strain of Clostridium cellulolyticum, a native cellulose-degrading microbe, that could synthesize isobutanol directly from cellulose.  This proof of concept research sets the stage for studies that will likely involve genetic manipulation of other consolidated bioprocessing microorganisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cyanobacteria">Cyanobacteria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isobutanol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cyanobacteria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">Cyanobacteria</a>, are a <a href="/wiki/Phylum" title="Phylum">phylum</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photosynthetic" title="Photosynthetic">photosynthetic</a> bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cyanobacteria_wiki_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-cyanobacteria_wiki-12">[12]</a></sup> Cyanobacteria are suited for isobutanol biosynthesis when genetically engineered to produce isobutanol and its corresponding aldehydes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Isobutanol producing species of cyanobacteria offer several advantages as biofuel synthesizers:
</p>
<ul><li>Cyanobacteria grow faster than plants<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cyanobacteria_Review_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cyanobacteria_Review-14">[14]</a></sup> and also absorb sunlight more efficiently than plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_instigator_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_instigator-15">[15]</a></sup> This means they can be replenished at a faster rate than the plant matter used for other biofuel biosynthesizers.</li>
<li>Cyanobacteria can be grown on non-arable land (land not used for farming).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cyanobacteria_Review_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cyanobacteria_Review-14">[14]</a></sup> This prevents <a href="/wiki/Food_vs._fuel" title="Food vs. fuel">competition between food sources and fuel sources</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cyanobacteria_Review_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cyanobacteria_Review-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>The supplements necessary for the growth of Cyanobacteria are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_Dioxide" title="Carbon Dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">H<sub>2</sub>O</a>, and sunlight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_instigator_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_instigator-15">[15]</a></sup> This presents two advantages:
<ul><li>Because CO<sub>2</sub> is derived from the atmosphere, Cyanobacteria do not need plant matter to synthesize isobutanol (in other organisms which synthesize isobutanol, plant matter is the source of the carbon necessary to synthetically assemble isobutanol).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_instigator_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-The_instigator-15">[15]</a></sup> Since plant matter is not used by this method of isobutanol production, the necessity to source plant matter from food sources and create a food-fuel price relationship is avoided.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cyanobacteria_Review_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-Cyanobacteria_Review-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>Because CO<sub>2</sub> is absorbed from the atmosphere by Cyanobacteria, the possibility of <a href="/wiki/Bioremediation" title="Bioremediation">bioremediation</a> (in the form of Cyanobacteria removing excess CO<sub>2</sub> from the atmosphere) exists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_instigator_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-The_instigator-15">[15]</a></sup></li></ul></li></ul>
<p>The primary drawbacks of Cyanobacteria are:
</p>
<ul><li>Cyanobacteria are sensitive to environmental conditions when being grown. Cyanobacteria suffer greatly from sunlight of inappropriate <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> and intensity, CO<sub>2</sub> of inappropriate concentration, or H<sub>2</sub>O of inappropriate <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a> though a wealth of cyanobacteria are able to grow in brackish and marine waters. These factors are generally hard to control, and present a major obstacle in cyanbacterial production of isobutanol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thug_life_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thug_life-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li>Cyanobacteria <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioreactors" title="Bioreactors">bioreactors</a> require high energy to operate. Cultures require constant mixing, and the harvesting of biosynthetic products is energy intensive. This reduces the efficiency of isobutanol production via Cyanobacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thug_life_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thug_life-16">[16]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bacillus_subtilis"><i>Bacillus subtilis</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isobutanol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Bacillus subtilis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Bacillus_subtilis" title="Bacillus subtilis">Bacillus subtilis</a></i> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gram-positive" title="Gram-positive">gram-positive</a> rod-shaped bacteria. <i><a href="/wiki/Bacillus_subtilis" title="Bacillus subtilis">Bacillus subtilis</a></i> offers many of the same advantages and disadvantages of <i>E. coli</i>, but it is less prominently used and does not produce isobutanol in quantities as large as <i>E. coli</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_Review_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Nature_Review-7">[7]</a></sup> Similar to <i>E. coli</i>, <i>Bacillus subtilis</i> is capable of producing isobutanol from lignocellulose, and is easily manipulated by common genetic techniques.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_Review_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-Nature_Review-7">[7]</a></sup> Elementary mode analysis has also been used to improve the isobutanol-synthesis <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_pathway" title="Metabolic pathway">metabolic pathway</a> used by <i>Bacillus subtilis</i>, leading to higher yields of isobutanol being produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EMA_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-EMA-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Saccharomyces_cerevisiae"><i>Saccharomyces cerevisiae</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isobutanol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Saccharomyces cerevisiae">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_cerevisiae" title="Saccharomyces cerevisiae">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</a></i>, or <i>S. cerevisiae</i> is a species of <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">yeast</a>. <i>S. cerevisiae</i> naturally produces isobutanol in small quantities via its <a href="/wiki/Valine" title="Valine">valine</a> biosynthetic pathway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ehrlich_pathway_enhance_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ehrlich_pathway_enhance-18">[18]</a></sup> <i>S. cerevisiae</i> is an ideal candidate for isobutanol biofuel production for several reasons:
</p>
<ul><li><i>S. cerevisiae</i> can be grown at low <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH levels</a>, helping prevent contamination during growth in industrial bioreactors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_Review_7-6"><a href="#cite_note-Nature_Review-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>S. cerevisiae</i> cannot be affected by bacteriophages because it is a <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryote</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_Review_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-Nature_Review-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>Extensive scientific knowledge about <i>S. cerevisiae</i> and its biology already exists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_Review_7-8"><a href="#cite_note-Nature_Review-7">[7]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Overexpression" title="Overexpression">Overexpression</a> of the enzymes in the valine biosynthetic pathway of  <i>S. cerevisiae</i> has been used to improve isobutanol yields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ehrlich_pathway_enhance_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ehrlich_pathway_enhance-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cytosol_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-cytosol-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-overexpression_of_2-keto_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-overexpression_of_2-keto-20">[20]</a></sup> <i>S. cerevisiae</i>, however, has proved difficult to work with because of its inherent biology: 
</p>
<ul><li>As a eukaryote, <i>S. cerevisiae</i>is genetically more complex than <i>E. coli</i> or <i>B. subtilis</i>, and is harder to genetically manipulate as a result.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_Review_7-9"><a href="#cite_note-Nature_Review-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>S. cerevisiae</i> has the <a href="/wiki/Ethanol#Fermentation" title="Ethanol">natural ability to produce ethanol</a>. This natural ability can "overpower" and consequently inhibit isobutanol production by <i>S. cerevisiae</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_Review_7-10"><a href="#cite_note-Nature_Review-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><i>S. cerevisiae</i> cannot use five carbon sugars to produce isobutanol. The inability to use five-carbon sugars restricts <i>S. cerevisiae</i> from using lignocellulose, and means <i>S. cerevisiae</i> must use plant matter intended for human consumption to produce isobutanol. This results in an unfavorable food/fuel price relationship when isobutanol is produced by <i>S. cerevisiae</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_Review_7-11"><a href="#cite_note-Nature_Review-7">[7]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ralstonia_eutropha"><i>Ralstonia eutropha</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isobutanol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ralstonia eutropha">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ralstonia_eutropha" title="Ralstonia eutropha">Ralstonia eutropha</a></i> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gram-negative" title="Gram-negative">gram-negative</a> <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> bacterium of the <a href="/wiki/Betaproteobacteria" title="Betaproteobacteria">betaproteobacteria</a> class. <i>Ralstonia eutropha</i> is capable of converting electrical energy into isobutanol. This conversion is completed in several steps: 
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anodes" title="Anodes">Anodes</a> are placed in a mixture of H<sub>2</sub>O and CO<sub>2</sub>.</li>
<li>An electric current is run through the anodes, and through an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrochemical" title="Electrochemical">electrochemical</a> process H<sub>2</sub>O and CO<sub>2</sub> are combined to synthesize <a href="/wiki/Formic_acid" title="Formic acid">formic acid</a>.</li>
<li>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacterial_culture" title="Bacterial culture">culture</a> of <i>Ralstonia eutropha</i> (composed of a <a href="/wiki/Strain_(biology)" title="Strain (biology)">strain</a> tolerant to electricity) is kept within the H<sub>2</sub>O and CO<sub>2</sub> mixture.</li>
<li>The culture of <i>Ralstonia eutropha</i> then converts formic acid from the mixture into isobutanol.</li>
<li>The biosynthesized isobutanol is then separated from the mixture, and can be used as a biofuel.</li></ul>
<p>This method of isobutanol production offers a way to chemically store energy produced from <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_energy" title="Sustainable energy">sustainable sources</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Solar_to_Isobutanol_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Solar_to_Isobutanol-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isobutanol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Isobutanol has a variety of technical and industrial applications:
</p>
<ul><li>feedstock in the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Isobutyl_acetate" title="Isobutyl acetate">isobutyl acetate</a>, which is used in the production of <a href="/wiki/Lacquer" title="Lacquer">lacquer</a> and similar <a href="/wiki/Coating" title="Coating">coatings</a>, and in the food industry as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flavoring" title="Flavoring">flavoring agent</a></li>
<li>precursor of derivative esters – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isobutyl" title="Isobutyl">isobutyl</a> <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">esters</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Diisobutyl_phthalate" title="Diisobutyl phthalate">diisobutyl phthalate</a> (DIBP) are used as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plasticizers" title="Plasticizers">plasticizers</a> in plastics, rubbers, and other dispersions</li>
<li>precursor of <a href="/wiki/P-Xylene" title="P-Xylene">p-xylene</a>, a building block for <a href="/wiki/Plastic_bottle" title="Plastic bottle">plastic bottles</a>, textiles and clothing.</li>
<li>paint solvent</li>
<li>varnish remover</li>
<li>ink ingredient</li>
<li>paint additive, to reduce <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a>, improve brush flow, and retard formation of oil residues (<i>blush</i>) on painted surfaces</li>
<li>gasoline additive, to reduce <a href="/wiki/Carburetor_icing" title="Carburetor icing">carburetor icing</a></li>
<li>automotive polish additive</li>
<li>automotive paint cleaner additive</li>
<li>chemical extractant in production of organic compounds</li>
<li>mobile phase in <a href="/wiki/Chromatography#Thin_layer_chromatography_.28TLC.29" title="Chromatography">thin layer chromatography</a>.</li>
<li>potential gasoline alternative</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second-generation_biofuel">Second-generation biofuel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isobutanol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Second-generation biofuel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Isobutanol can be used as a <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuel</a> substitute for <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> in the current petroleum <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a>. Isobutanol has not yet been put into mainstream use as a biofuel and would serve as a replacement for <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>. Ethanol is a <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">first-generation biofuel</a>, and is used primarily as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gasoline_additive" title="Gasoline additive">gasoline additive</a> in the petroleum infrastructure. Isobutanol is a <a href="/wiki/Second-generation_biofuels" title="Second-generation biofuels">second-generation biofuel</a> with several qualities that resolve issues presented by ethanol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_Review_7-12"><a href="#cite_note-Nature_Review-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Isobutanol's properties make it an attractive biofuel:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_Review_7-13"><a href="#cite_note-Nature_Review-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>relatively high <a href="/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">energy density</a>, 98% of that of gasoline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Studies_on_the_production_of_branched-chain_alcohols_in_engineered_Ralstonia_eutropha_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Studies_on_the_production_of_branched-chain_alcohols_in_engineered_Ralstonia_eutropha-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li>does not readily absorb water from air, preventing the <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> of engines and pipelines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_Review_7-14"><a href="#cite_note-Nature_Review-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>can be mixed at any proportion with gasoline,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Screened_Butanol-Tolerant_Enterococcus_faecium_Capable_of_Butanol_Production_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Screened_Butanol-Tolerant_Enterococcus_faecium_Capable_of_Butanol_Production-23">[23]</a></sup> meaning the fuel can "drop into" the existing petroleum infrastructure as a replacement fuel or major additive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_Review_7-15"><a href="#cite_note-Nature_Review-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>can be produced from plant matter not connected to food supplies, preventing a fuel-price/food-price relationship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_Review_7-16"><a href="#cite_note-Nature_Review-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elucidating_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Elucidating-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_new_carbon_catabolite_repression_mutation_of_Escherichia_coli,_mlc*,_and_its_use_for_producing_isobutanol_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-A_new_carbon_catabolite_repression_mutation_of_Escherichia_coli,_mlc*,_and_its_use_for_producing_isobutanol-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EMA_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-EMA-17">[17]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_and_regulation">Safety and regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isobutanol&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Safety and regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Isobutanol is one of the least <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toxic" title="Toxic">toxic</a> of the butanols with an <a href="/wiki/Median_lethal_dose" title="Median lethal dose">LD<sub style="font-size:-1">50</sub></a> of 2460 mg/kg (rat, oral).
</p><p>In March 2009, the <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> government announced a ban on isobutanol use in <a href="/wiki/Cosmetics" title="Cosmetics">cosmetics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
