<p><b>Whiteflies</b> are small <a href="/wiki/Hemiptera" title="Hemiptera">Hemipterans</a> that typically feed on the undersides of plant leaves. They comprise the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <b>Aleyrodidae</b>, the only family in the superfamily <b>Aleyrodoidea</b>. More than <a href="/wiki/List_of_whitefly_species" title="List of whitefly species">1550 species</a> have been described.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description_and_taxonomy">Description and taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whitefly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description and taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Aleyrodidae are a family in the suborder <a href="/wiki/Sternorrhyncha" title="Sternorrhyncha">Sternorrhyncha</a> and at present comprise the entire superfamily Aleyrodoidea, related to the superfamily <a href="/wiki/Psylloidea" title="Psylloidea">Psylloidea</a>. The family often occurs in older literature as "Aleurodidae",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-412-61390-5_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-412-61390-5-2">[2]</a></sup> but that is a junior synonym and accordingly incorrect in terms of the international standards for zoological nomenclature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Aleyrodidae are small insects, most species with a wingspan of less than 3 mm and a body length of 1 mm to 2mm. Many are so small that their size complicates their control in greenhouses because they can only be excluded by screening with very fine mesh; in fact they can enter mesh so fine that many of their natural enemies cannot come in after them, so that unchecked whitefly populations in greenhouses rapidly become overwhelming. Some "giant whitefly" species exist, some of which may exceed 5 mm in size. This sometimes is associated with sexual dimorphism in which one sex is markedly larger than the other. Such dimorphism is common in the Sternorrhyncha, in which the males of most scale insects for example are tiny compared to the female. Remarkably however, in some giant tropical species the males are much larger than the females.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MJH_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-MJH-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Neomaskellia_bergii_from_CSIRO.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="203" data-file-width="300" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Neomaskellia_bergii_from_CSIRO.jpg/220px-Neomaskellia_bergii_from_CSIRO.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b3/Neomaskellia_bergii_from_CSIRO.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Neomaskellia_bergii_from_CSIRO.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><center>Neomaskellia bergii, illustrating the wing venation and showing the vasiform orifice above the tip of the abdomen</center></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aleyrodidae_Udamoselis_Venation_Enderlein_1909.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2771" data-file-width="3005" height="203" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Aleyrodidae_Udamoselis_Venation_Enderlein_1909.jpg/220px-Aleyrodidae_Udamoselis_Venation_Enderlein_1909.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Aleyrodidae_Udamoselis_Venation_Enderlein_1909.jpg/330px-Aleyrodidae_Udamoselis_Venation_Enderlein_1909.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Aleyrodidae_Udamoselis_Venation_Enderlein_1909.jpg/440px-Aleyrodidae_Udamoselis_Venation_Enderlein_1909.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aleyrodidae_Udamoselis_Venation_Enderlein_1909.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><center>Venation in a large species of Udamoselis is more complex</center></div></div></div>
<p>Like most of the mobile Sternorrhyncha, adult Aleyrodidae have well-developed antennae, which in most species in this family are seven-segmented.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JHComstock_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-JHComstock-5">[5]</a></sup> As in many Hemiptera, there are two ocelli, which generally in the Aleyrodidae are placed at the anterior margins of the compound eyes. The compound eyes themselves however, are rather remarkable; there are many examples in entomology in which the upper and lower regions of the compound eye differ both functionally and anatomically, and in some families that are adapted to living on water surfaces, such as the <a href="/wiki/Whirligig_beetle" title="Whirligig beetle">Gyrinidae</a>, they even may be divided into upper and lower eyes for vision in air and under water. However, although it is not clear why Aleyrodidae should need any such adaptation, many have a distinct constriction between the upper and lower halves of the compound eyes in both sexes. In some species there is a complete separation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JHComstock_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-JHComstock-5">[5]</a></sup> The degree of separation is useful in recognising the species;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> for instance, one way to tell adult <i>Bemisia</i> from <i>Trialeurodes</i> is that the upper and lower parts of the compound eyes are connected by a single ommatidium in Bemisia, while in Trialeurodes they are completely separate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Both sexes  have functional mouthparts and two pairs of membranous, functional wings; the rear wings are neither much reduced, nor modified into any such hooked or <a href="/wiki/Halteres" title="Halteres">haltere</a>-like structures as occur in some other Hemiptera such as many of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coccoidea" title="Coccoidea">Coccoidea</a>. The wing <a href="/wiki/Insect_wing#Venation" title="Insect wing">venation</a> is reduced, like that of the Psyllidae, only generally much more so. In many genera there is only one conspicuous and unbranched vein in each wing; however, wings of larger species such as <i>Udamoselis</i> have less reduced venation, though their veins still are simple and few.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MJH_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-MJH-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The insects and their wings are variously marked or mottled according to species, and many species are covered with fine wax powder, giving most species a floury, dusted appearance, hence names such as Aleyrodidae, Aleurodidae and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aleuroduplidens" title="Aleuroduplidens">Aleuroduplidens</a></i>; the root refers to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" title="Ancient Greek language">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc">αλευρώδης</span> (aleurodes) meaning "floury".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jaeger_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jaeger-8">[8]</a></sup> However, not all species are white; for example, <i><a href="/wiki/Aleurocanthus_woglumi" title="Aleurocanthus woglumi">Aleurocanthus woglumi</a></i> is slaty black.
</p><p>The legs of are well developed and fairly long, but <a href="/wiki/Gracility" title="Gracility">gracile</a>, and in contrast to Psyllidae, not adapted to leaping. The tarsi have two segments of roughly equal length. The <a href="/wiki/Arthropod_leg" title="Arthropod leg">pretarsus</a> has paired claws, with an <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_entomology_terms#D.E2.80.93F" title="Glossary of entomology terms">empodium</a> between—in some species the empodium is a bristle, but in others it is a pad.
</p><p>The digestive system of the Aleyrodidae is typical of the Sternorrhyncha, including a <a href="/wiki/Hemiptera#Herbivores" title="Hemiptera">filter chamber</a>, and all active stages of the Aleyrodidae accordingly produce large quantities of honeydew; the anus is adapted to presentation of honeydew to symbiotic species, mainly ants; the honeydew emerges from the anus, which is inside an opening called the <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vasiform" title="wiktionary:vasiform">vasiform</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/orifice" title="wiktionary:orifice">orifice</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location#Dorsal_and_ventral" title="Anatomical terms of location">dorsal</a> surface of the <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location#Cranial_and_caudal" title="Anatomical terms of location">caudal</a> segment of the abdomen. This orifice is large and is covered by an <a href="/wiki/Operculum_(animal)" title="Operculum (animal)">operculum</a> The entire structure is characteristic of the Aleyrodidae and within the family it is taxonomically diagnostic because it varies in shape according to the species. Within the orifice beneath the operculum there is a tongue-like lingula. It appears to be involved in the expulsion of honeydew, and in fact at one time was wrongly assumed to be the organ that produced the honeydew. In some species it generally protrudes from beneath the operculum, but in others it normally is hidden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-412-61390-5_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-412-61390-5-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reproduction_and_metamorphosis">Reproduction and metamorphosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whitefly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reproduction and metamorphosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The eggs of Aleyrodidae generally are laid near each other on the food plant, usually on a leaf, in spiral patterns or arcs, sometimes in parallel arcs. The egg is elongated, with one narrow end produced into a pedicel, which in some species is longer than the rest of the egg. After fertilisation the pedicel shrivels into a stalk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-412-61390-5_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-412-61390-5-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The details vary, but at least some species can reproduce <a href="/wiki/Parthenogenesis" title="Parthenogenesis">parthenogenically</a> by <a href="/wiki/Parthenogenesis" title="Parthenogenesis">automixis</a>. However, apparently all males are parthenogenically produced by <a href="/wiki/Arrhenotoky" title="Arrhenotoky">arrhenotoky</a>. The female however, can mate with her own male offspring, and thereafter produce eggs of both sexes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>There generally are four larval <a href="/wiki/Instar" title="Instar">instars</a>. All the instars are more or less in the shape of a flattened ellipse fringed with bristles and waxy filaments. The first instar has functional legs, though short. Once it has inserted its <a href="/wiki/Hemiptera#Mouthparts" title="Hemiptera">stylets</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Phloem" title="Phloem">phloem</a> to feed, it settles down and no longer uses its legs, and they degenerate after the first <a href="/wiki/Ecdysis" title="Ecdysis">ecdysis</a>. From then until it emerges as an adult, it remains attached to the plant by its mouthparts. The final instar feeds for a while, then undergoes changes within its skin, ceasing feeding and growing a new skin, forming what amounts to pupa. In doing so the insect does not shed the larval skin, which it retains as a protective <a href="/wiki/Pupa#Puparium" title="Pupa">puparium</a> and which dries out. Meanwhile, the pupa within this skin develops into a <a href="/wiki/Pupa#Emergence" title="Pupa">pharate</a> adult that usually is visible through the wall of the puparium. The puparium splits open as the <a href="/wiki/Imago" title="Imago">imago</a> forces its way out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-412-61390-5_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-412-61390-5-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JHComstock_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-JHComstock-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>This pupal stage is analogous to the pupal forms of the <a href="/wiki/Endopterygota" title="Endopterygota">Endopterygota</a> and it raises questions of terminology and concept. Some authorities argue that there is little functional, and no logically cogent basis for the distinction between the terms "larva" and "nymph". Some have long been in favour of dropping the term nymph entirely, and certainly apply the term "larvae" to the Aleyrodidae.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-412-61390-5_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-412-61390-5-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JHComstock_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-JHComstock-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Agricultural_threat">Agricultural threat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whitefly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Agricultural threat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In warm or tropical climates and especially in greenhouses, whiteflies present major problems in <a href="/wiki/Crop_protection" title="Crop protection">crop protection</a>. Worldwide economic losses are estimated at hundreds of millions of dollars annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Capinera2008_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Capinera2008-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Prominent pest species include:
</p>
<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Aleurocanthus_woglumi" title="Aleurocanthus woglumi">Aleurocanthus woglumi</a></i>, citrus blackfly, which, in spite of its color, is a whitefly that attacks citrus</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Aleyrodes_proletella" title="Aleyrodes proletella">Aleyrodes proletella</a></i>, cabbage whitefly, is a pest of various <a href="/wiki/Brassica" title="Brassica">Brassica</a> crops.</li>
<li><i>Bemisia tabaci</i>, <a href="/wiki/Silverleaf_whitefly" title="Silverleaf whitefly">silverleaf whitefly</a>, is a pest of many agricultural and ornamental crops.</li>
<li><i>Trialeurodes vaporariorum</i>, <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_whitefly" title="Greenhouse whitefly">greenhouse whitefly</a>, a major pest of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouse</a> fruit, vegetables, and ornamentals</li></ul>
<p>Although several species of whitefly may cause some crop losses simply by sucking sap when they are very numerous, the major harm they do is indirect. Firstly, like many other sap-sucking Hemiptera, they secrete large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Honeydew_(secretion)" title="Honeydew (secretion)">honeydew</a> that support unsightly or harmful infestations of <a href="/wiki/Sooty_mold" title="Sooty mold">sooty mold</a>. Secondly,they inject saliva that may harm the plant more than either the mechanical damage of feeding or the growth of the fungi. However, by far their major importance as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crop_pest" title="Crop pest">crop pests</a> is their transmission of diseases of plants.
</p><p>The most prominent <a href="/wiki/Vector_(epidemiology)" title="Vector (epidemiology)">disease vectors</a> among the Aleyrodidae are a species complex in the genus <i>Bemisia</i>. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bemisia_tabaci" title="Bemisia tabaci">Bemisia tabaci</a></i> and <i>B. argentifolii</i> transmit <a href="/wiki/African_cassava_mosaic_virus" title="African cassava mosaic virus">African cassava mosaic</a>, bean golden mosaic, bean dwarf mosaic, <a href="/wiki/Bean_calico_mosaic_virus" title="Bean calico mosaic virus">bean calico mosaic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tomato_yellow_leaf_curl_virus" title="Tomato yellow leaf curl virus">tomato yellow leaf curl</a>, tomato mottle, and other <a href="/wiki/Begomovirus" title="Begomovirus">Begomoviruses</a>, in the family <a href="/wiki/Geminiviridae" title="Geminiviridae">Geminiviridae</a>.  The worldwide spread of emerging biotypes, such as <i>B. tabaci</i> biotype B, also known as, 'B. argentifolii', and a new biotype Q, continue to cause severe crop losses which are expected to increase, demanding matching increases in pesticide use on many crops (tomatoes, beans, cassava, cotton, cucurbits, potatoes, sweet potatoes).  Efforts to develop environmentally friendly integrated pest management systems, with the goal of reducing <a href="/wiki/Insecticide" title="Insecticide">insecticide</a> use, aim to re-establish the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecological_equilibrium" title="Ecological equilibrium">ecological equilibrium</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Predator" title="Predator">predators</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parasitoid" title="Parasitoid">parasitoids</a>, and microbial controls that were once in place.  New crop varieties are also being developed with increased tolerance to whiteflies, and to the plant diseases carried by them.  A major problem is that whiteflies and the viruses they carry can infect many host plants, including agricultural crops, palms,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and weeds.  These problems are complicated by difficulties in classifying and detecting new whitefly biotypes and begomoviruses.  Proper diagnosis of plant diseases depends on using sophisticated molecular techniques to detect and characterize the viruses and whiteflies which are present in a crop. A team of researchers, extension agents and growers working together are needed to follow disease development, using dynamic modeling, to understand the incidence of disease spread.
</p><p>In 1997, tomato yellow leaf-curl begomovirus was discovered in Florida, USA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This is the worst viral disease transmitted by the whitefly, <i>Bemisia argentifolii</i>.  The whitefly has also been shown to transmit almost 60 other viral plant diseases.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Damage_by_feeding">Damage by feeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whitefly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Damage by feeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Whiteflies feed by tapping into the <a href="/wiki/Phloem" title="Phloem">phloem</a> of plants, introducing toxic <a href="/wiki/Saliva" title="Saliva">saliva</a> and decreasing the plants' overall <a href="/wiki/Turgor_pressure" title="Turgor pressure">turgor pressure</a>. Since whiteflies congregate in large numbers, susceptible plants can be quickly overwhelmed. Further harm is done by <a href="/wiki/Mold" title="Mold">mold</a> growth encouraged by the <a href="/wiki/Honeydew_(secretion)" title="Honeydew (secretion)">honeydew</a> whiteflies secrete. This may also seriously impede the ability of farms to process cotton harvests.
</p><p>Whiteflies share a modified form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metamorphosis_(biology)" title="Metamorphosis (biology)">hemimetabolous metamorphosis</a>, in that the immature stages begin life as mobile individuals, but soon attach to host plants. The stage before the adult is called a <b>pupa</b>, though it shares little in common with the <a href="/wiki/Pupa" title="Pupa">pupal</a> stage of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holometabolous" title="Holometabolous">holometabolous</a> insects.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Control">Control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whitefly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Orius_insidiosus_from_USDA_2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2700" data-file-width="2044" height="291" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Orius_insidiosus_from_USDA_2.jpg/220px-Orius_insidiosus_from_USDA_2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Orius_insidiosus_from_USDA_2.jpg/330px-Orius_insidiosus_from_USDA_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Orius_insidiosus_from_USDA_2.jpg/440px-Orius_insidiosus_from_USDA_2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Orius_insidiosus_from_USDA_2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Anthocoridae" title="Anthocoridae">Pirate bug</a> feeding on whitefly larvae</div></div></div>
<p>Whitefly <a href="/wiki/Pest_control" title="Pest control">control</a> is difficult and complex, as whiteflies rapidly develop resistance to chemical <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a>. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Agriculture" title="United States Department of Agriculture">USDA</a> recommends "an integrated program that focuses on prevention and relies on cultural and biological control methods when possible."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
While an initial pesticide application may be necessary to control heavy infestations, repeated applications may lead to strains of whiteflies that are resistant to pesticides,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> so only use of selective insecticides is advised.  Specific insecticide information and guidance for the fig whitefly is available from the University of Florida.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Care should be taken to ensure that the insecticide used will not kill the natural predators of whiteflies. For effective use of biological method after application of pesticide, plant washing is advised prior to release of predators or parasitoids.
</p><p>Pesticides used for whitefly control usually contain <a href="/wiki/Neonicotinoid" title="Neonicotinoid">neonicotinoid</a> compounds as active ingredients: <a href="/wiki/Clothianidin" title="Clothianidin">clothianidin</a> (commercial), <a href="/wiki/Dinotefuran" title="Dinotefuran">dinotefuran</a> (over-the-counter and commercial), <a href="/wiki/Imidacloprid" title="Imidacloprid">imidacloprid</a> (over-the-counter and commercial) and <a href="/wiki/Thiamethoxam" title="Thiamethoxam">thiamethoxam</a> (commercial). Neonicotinoids can be harmful if ingested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Rotation of insecticides from different families may be effective at preventing the building of tolerance to the product. Clothianidin and dinotefuran are of the same family. Spraying the leaves using <a href="/wiki/Insecticidal_soap" title="Insecticidal soap">insecticidal soap</a> is another, environmentally friendly, option.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQs_about_Whiteflies_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAQs_about_Whiteflies-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nonchemical_means">Nonchemical means</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whitefly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nonchemical means">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Biological methods have also been proposed to control whitefly infestation, and may be paired with chemical methods. Washing the plant, especially the undersides of leaves, may help reduce the number of the pests on the plants and make their management by other methods more effective. Whiteflies are also attracted by the color yellow, so yellow sticky paper can serve as traps to monitor infestations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Dead leaves or leaves that have been mostly eaten by whiteflies can be removed and burned or carefully placed in closed bins to avoid reinfestation and spreading of the disease.
</p><p>Early detection in combination with hosing or vacuuming of diseased portions as well as removal of any section that is heavily infested. Pesticide use is not ideal in the case of controlling whitefly and widespread contamination can be costly; it is best to avoid this problem with aggressive preventive measures.
</p><p>Several predators and parasitoids may be effective in controlling whitefly infestations, including <a href="/wiki/Chrysopidae" title="Chrysopidae">green lacewings</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ladybird" title="Ladybird">ladybirds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthocoridae" title="Anthocoridae">minute pirate bugs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geocoris" title="Geocoris">big-eyed bugs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nabidae" title="Nabidae">damsel bugs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Phytoseiidae" title="Phytoseiidae">phytoseiid mites</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQs_about_Whiteflies_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAQs_about_Whiteflies-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Integrated management of whiteflies can as well be done using <a href="/wiki/Biopesticide" title="Biopesticide">biopesticides</a> based on microbials such as <i><a href="/wiki/Beauveria_bassiana" title="Beauveria bassiana">Beauveria bassiana</a></i> (effective on larvae and adults) or <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paecilomyces_fumosoroseus" title="Paecilomyces fumosoroseus">Isaria fumosorosea</a></i>.
</p><p>Green lacewing larvae have voracious appetites, so will attack whiteflies, as well as other pests, including <a href="/wiki/Aphid" title="Aphid">aphids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mealybug" title="Mealybug">mealybugs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spider_mites" title="Spider mites">spider mites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leafhopper" title="Leafhopper">leafhopper</a> larvae, <a href="/wiki/Moth" title="Moth">moth</a> eggs, <a href="/wiki/Scale_insect" title="Scale insect">scales</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thrips" title="Thrips">thrips</a>. They also can attack other insects, including caterpillars.  They are available as eggs from commercial insectaries, and will stay in a larval stage after they hatch for one to three weeks.  The adult insects can fly and will feed only on pollen, honey, and nectar to reproduce.  Repeated application may be necessary and the eggs could be eaten before they hatch by their natural predators, such as ants or mature green lacewings.
</p><p>Ladybirds are also used. They eat mostly insect eggs, but will also feed on <a href="/wiki/Beetle" title="Beetle">beetle</a> <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a>, aphids, scale insects, and young <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caterpillars" title="Caterpillars">caterpillars</a>. Adults are often collected when in a dormant state in the wild and shipped for use in pest control; however, they may not stay in the location where they are released.  They do live for about a year and will continuously lay eggs and reproduce. Spraying the bugs' wings with a sticky substance before release may hinder their ability to fly.
</p><p>Some promising claims have been made that mesh or film that excludes ultraviolet of certain wavelengths from a greenhouse interfere severely with the ability of whitefly and various other greenhouse pests, to find their food plants. It is not yet clear, assuming that the effect is substantially of value, how readily pests in such circumstances might develop behavioural tolerance to such control measures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Companion_plants">Companion plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Whitefly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Companion plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_companion_plants" title="List of companion plants">List of companion plants</a></div>
<p>A number of plants can be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intercrop" title="Intercrop">intercropped</a> with vegetables, in a garden setting, serving as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Companion_plant" title="Companion plant">companion plants</a> to protect against whiteflies.
</p><p>For example, <a href="/wiki/Tropaeolum" title="Tropaeolum">nasturtiums</a> are thought to provide a defense to gooseberries or tomatoes. They provide root chemicals that deter whiteflies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Zinnia" title="Zinnia">Zinnias</a>, conversely, attract predators that consume whiteflies, including hummingbirds and predatory wasps and flies. Other plants with a similar function include the <a href="/wiki/Hummingbird_bush" title="Hummingbird bush">hummingbird bush</a><sup class="plainlinks noprint Inline-Template" style="vertical-align:text-top;white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="external text" href="http://dispenser.info.tm/~dispenser/cgi-bin/dab_solver.py?page=Whitefly&amp;editintro=Template:Disambiguation_needed/editintro&amp;client=Template:Dn" rel="nofollow"><span title="Link needs disambiguation. (November 2017)">disambiguation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pineapple_sage" title="Pineapple sage">pineapple sage</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bee_balm" title="Bee balm">bee balm</a>. Each of these plants also conceals the scent of nearby plants, making their detection by some pest insects more difficult, as do most other mints.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
