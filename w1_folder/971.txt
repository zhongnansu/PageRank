<p><b>FutureGen</b> is a project (now suspended) to demonstrate <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">capture and sequestration</a> of waste carbon dioxide from a coal-fired electrical generating station. The project (renamed FutureGen 2.0) was retrofitting a shuttered coal-fired power plant in <a href="/wiki/Meredosia,_Illinois" title="Meredosia, Illinois">Meredosia, Illinois</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Oxy-fuel_combustion_process" title="Oxy-fuel combustion process">oxy-combustion</a> generators.  The waste CO<sub>2</sub> would be piped approximately 30 miles (48 km) to be sequestered in underground saline formations. FutureGen was a partnership between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_government" title="United States government">United States government</a> and an alliance of primarily coal-related corporations. Costs were estimated at US$1.65 billion, with $1.0 billion provided by the Federal Government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TechRev15Sept2014_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-TechRev15Sept2014-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MITCCST_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-MITCCST-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FGAFAQ_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FGAFAQ-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>First announced by President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> in 2003, construction started in 2014 after restructuring, canceling, relocating, and restarting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TechRev15Sept2014_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-TechRev15Sept2014-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRSHistory_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-CRSHistory-4">[4]</a></sup> Citing an inability to commit and spend the funds by deadlines in 2015, the Department of Energy withdrew funds and suspended FutureGen 2.0 in February, 2015. The government also cited the Alliance's inability to raise the requisite amount of private funding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bloombergsuspend_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-bloombergsuspend-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>FutureGen 2.0 would have been the most comprehensive Department of Energy Carbon Capture and Storage demonstration project, involving all phases from combustion to sequestration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRSHistory_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-CRSHistory-4">[4]</a></sup> FutureGen's initial plan involved <a href="/wiki/Integrated_gasification_combined_cycle" title="Integrated gasification combined cycle">integrated gasification combined cycle</a> technology to produce both electricity and hydrogen. Early in the project it was to be sited in Mattoon, IL.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CRSHistory_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-CRSHistory-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Original_project">Original project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FutureGen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Original project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The original incarnation of FutureGen was as a public-private partnership to build the world's first near zero-emissions coal-fueled power plant. The 275-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">megawatt</a> plant would be intended to prove the feasibility of producing electricity and hydrogen from coal while capturing and permanently storing carbon dioxide underground. The Alliance intended to build the plant in <a href="/wiki/Mattoon_Township,_Coles_County,_Illinois" title="Mattoon Township, Coles County, Illinois">Mattoon Township, Coles County, Illinois</a> northwest of Mattoon, Illinois, subject to necessary approvals (issuing a “Record of Decision”) by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">Department of Energy</a> (DOE) as part of the <a href="/wiki/National_Environmental_Policy_Act" title="National Environmental Policy Act">National Environmental Policy Act</a> (NEPA) process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>FutureGen was to be designed, developed and operated by the FutureGen Industrial Alliance, a non-profit consortium of coal mining and electric utility companies formed to partner with the DOE on the FutureGen project. The project was still in the development stage when its funding was cancelled in January 2008. The Alliance decision of the location of the host site, subject to DOE's completing NEPA environmental reviews, was announced in December 2007 after a two-year bidding and review process. Construction was scheduled to begin in 2009, with full-scale plant operations to begin in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The estimated gross project cost, including construction and operations, and excluding offsetting revenue, was $1.65 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sequestration.mit.edu_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-sequestration.mit.edu-8">[8]</a></sup> The project was governed by a legally binding cooperative agreement between DOE and the Alliance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Under the agreement, DOE was to provide 74% of the project’s cost, with private industry contributing the other 26%. The DOE also planned to solicit the financial support and participation of international governments in the FutureGen project, since by 2020 more than 60% of man-made greenhouse gas emissions are expected to come from developing countries. Foreign financial support was to offset a portion of DOE’s cost-share. As of January 2008, the foreign governments of <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, India, Australia, South Korea, and Japan had expressed interest in participating and sharing the cost of the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>FutureGen was to sequester carbon dioxide emissions at a rate of one million <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metric_ton" title="Metric ton">metric tons</a> per year for four years, which is the scale a <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a> (MIT) report cites as appropriate for proving sequestration. The MIT report also states that “the priority objective with respect to coal should be the successful large-scale demonstration of the technical, economic, and environmental performance of the technologies that make up all of the major components of a large-scale integrated <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">CCS</a> system — capture, transportation and storage.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> An injection field test similar to this was done in Norway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2009 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_Post" title="Washington Post">Washington Post</a> reported that U.S. Secretary of Energy <a href="/wiki/Steven_Chu" title="Steven Chu">Steven Chu</a> expressed support for continuing the project using stimulus funds (after some changes that have not yet been specified) and making it a part of a larger portfolio of research plants developed in collaboration with other countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Following the successful completion of the first phase, in February 2013, the Energy Department announced the beginning of Phase II of the project development with a new cooperative agreement between the FutureGen Industrial Alliance and the Department of Energy. This means that the FutureGen project has government support as it moves into its third phase, deployment of the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sequestration.mit.edu_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-sequestration.mit.edu-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Site_selection">Site selection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FutureGen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Site selection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Site_selection" title="Site selection">Site selection</a> for the FutureGen facility was based on a competitive process which began in May 2006. Seven states responded<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> to the Site Request for Proposals with a total of 12 proposals. Proposals were reviewed against a set of environmental, technical, regulatory, and financial criteria with input from external technical advisors on power plant design and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">carbon sequestration</a>. In July 2006, four candidate sites were selected for further review, including an environmental impact analysis as required by NEPA.
</p><p>
DOE issued its Final <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_statement" title="Environmental impact statement">Environmental impact statement</a> (EIS) on November 8, 2007, which concluded that all four sites were acceptable from an environmental impact standpoint and all would move forward in the site evaluation process.  EPA published a Notice of Availability (NOA) for the EIS in the Federal Register on November 16, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The DOE is required by federal law to wait at least 30 days after the NOA release before issuing its final Record of Decision (ROD).  The waiting period legally closed on December 17, 2007.  DOE chose not to issue the ROD and advised the FutureGen Alliance to delay the final site selection announcement, which was scheduled to occur at the end of the 30-day waiting period. The Alliance chose to move ahead with the announcement, citing time, money, and a commitment to proposers to select the final site by year-end. "Every month of delay can add $10 million to the project's cost, solely due to inflation," said Michael Mudd, the Alliance's chief executive. </p><center>

<p>The FutureGen Alliance announced the selection of <a href="/wiki/Mattoon,_Illinois" title="Mattoon, Illinois">Mattoon, Illinois</a> as the host site on December 18, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> According to the EIS, Mattoon, IL the site is located about 3.5 miles (5.6 km) northwest of downtown Mattoon in the eastern part of Mattoon township section 8 on 1.8 km<sup>2</sup> (440 acres) of former farm land. The carbon sequestration area is about 8,000 feet (2.4 km) below the ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In July 2007, Illinois Public Act 095-0018 became law giving the state of Illinois ownership of and liability for the sequestered gases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology">Technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FutureGen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Original FutureGen project was intended to combine and test several new technologies in a single location, including <a href="/wiki/Coal#Gasification" title="Coal">coal gasification</a>, emissions controls, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_production" title="Hydrogen production">hydrogen production</a>, electricity generation, and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon dioxide capture and storage</a> (CCS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integrated_Gasification_Combined_Cycle" title="Integrated Gasification Combined Cycle">Integrated Gasification Combined Cycle</a> (IGCC) was the core technology behind FutureGen. IGCC power plants use two turbines – a gas and a steam turbine – to produce electric power more efficiently than pulverized coal plants. IGCC plants also make it easier to capture carbon dioxide for carbon sequestration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>FutureGen was to capture carbon dioxide produced during the gasification process and pump it into deep rock formations thousands of feet under ground. FutureGen specifically targeted rock formations containing saline water, as these are one of the most abundant types of geologic formations that can be used to store carbon dioxide worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> A study by the Global Energy Technology Strategy Program estimates the storage capacity of these saline rock formations in the U.S. to be 2,970 gigatons of carbon dioxide, compared to a capacity of 77 gigatons of carbon dioxide for all other types of reservoirs, such as depleted gas fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Focusing on rock formations with saline water was intended to help ensure that the lessons learned from the project are broadly transferable throughout the U.S. and around the world.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Challenges">Challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FutureGen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Maintaining the project schedule and keeping costs down were two major challenges with which the DOE and the FutureGen Alliance grappled. The project had remained on schedule with the announcement of the host site before the end of 2007; however, a desire by DOE to restructure the project’s financial arrangement has brought the project to a halt.
</p><p>In December 2007, the DOE Acting Deputy Assistant Secretary for Fossil Energy James Slutz stated that projected cost overruns for the project "require a reassessment of FutureGen's design."  And that "This will require restructuring FutureGen to maximize the role of private-sector innovation, facilitate the most productive public-private partnership, and prevent further cost escalation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Illinois_wins,_official_warned_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Illinois_wins,_official_warned-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>The FutureGen Alliance wrote a letter to the Department of Energy’s Under Secretary C.H. “Bud” Albright Jr. stating that overall inflation and the rising cost of raw materials and engineering services are driving costs up on energy projects around the world. According to <a href="/wiki/James_L._Connaughton" title="James L. Connaughton">James L. Connaughton</a>, chairman of the White House <a href="/wiki/Council_on_Environmental_Quality" title="Council on Environmental Quality">Council on Environmental Quality</a>, the market for steel, concrete and power plant components has “just gone through the roof globally”, and much of the reason is the construction of hundreds of new conventional coal plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>On January 11, 2008, the FutureGen Alliance sent a letter to the DOE offering to lower the government's portion of the project's costs.  The initial plans had called for DOE to pay based on a percentage of the total cost, and their portion had risen from about $620 million to about $1.33 billion.  The letter indicated that DOE's portion would now be $800 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Risk_management" title="Risk management">Risk management</a> was a significant portion of the cost of the first FutureGen experimental implementation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> FutureGen involved many complex never-before-solved technology problems. The risks also included significant health risks, if the untested-technology systems failed to work correctly.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Funding_cancellation">Funding cancellation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FutureGen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Funding cancellation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On January 29, 2008, the U.S. Department of Energy announced that it would pull its funding for the project, mostly due to higher than expected costs. The move is likely to delay the project as other members seek the additional funds that the DOE was to provide. The sudden concern over cost after an Illinois site was chosen over those in Texas raised questions about the motives for the cancellation. Local and state officials in Illinois, including then Governor <a href="/wiki/Rod_Blagojevich" title="Rod Blagojevich">Rod Blagojevich</a>, expressed frustration at the move, especially in light of the money and resources that the state had spent to attract the project. Democratic Senator <a href="/wiki/Dick_Durbin" title="Dick Durbin">Dick Durbin</a> of Illinois accused Energy Secretary <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Bodman" title="Samuel Bodman">Samuel Bodman</a> of "cruel deception" of Illinoisans by "creating false hope in a FutureGen project which he has no intention of funding or supporting."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Durbin claimed that "when the city of Mattoon, Illinois, was chosen over possible locations in Texas, the secretary of energy set out to kill FutureGen." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChT_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-ChT-30">[30]</a></sup> Mattoon mayor David Cline said "one could question the motivation of the Department of Energy which was ready to move forward with the project until a site other than Texas was chosen."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChT_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-ChT-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2009, Congressional auditors determined that the DOE had miscalculated the government portion of the project's cost, overstating the amount by a half billion dollars.  As a result, the Bush administration cited the project as having nearly doubled in cost when, in reality, it had increased by 39%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Secretary Bodman stated that with restructuring the FutureGen project, DOE plans "to equip multiple new clean-coal power plants with advanced CCS technology, instead of one demonstration plant. That will provide more electricity from multiple clean-coal plants, sequestering at least twice as much CO<sub>2</sub> and providing for wider use and more rapid commercialization."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Revised_plan_FutureGen_2.0">Revised plan FutureGen 2.0</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FutureGen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Revised plan FutureGen 2.0">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plans_for_continuing_FutureGen">Plans for continuing FutureGen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FutureGen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Plans for continuing FutureGen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite the cancellation of funding by the DOE, the FutureGen Alliance continued to move forward with the project, opening an office in Mattoon and planning to buy the land for the plant in August 2008, in partnership with a local group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the 2008 U.S. presidential campaigns, Sen. <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> pledged his support to <a href="/wiki/Clean_coal_technology" title="Clean coal technology">clean coal technologies</a>, with plans to develop five commercial-scale coal plants equipped with CCS technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 2008, Fred Palmer, senior vice president at <a href="/wiki/Peabody_Energy" title="Peabody Energy">Peabody Energy</a> shared his outlook on FutureGen with the <a href="/wiki/American_Coalition_for_Clean_Coal_Electricity" title="American Coalition for Clean Coal Electricity">American Coalition for Clean Coal Electricity</a> (ACCCE), saying that the FutureGen Alliance would "Make a concerted effort in the Obama administration to reinstate the project and get this built as originally planned."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>On June 12, 2009, the DOE announced a restart of design work for the FutureGen project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-doe12june2009_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-doe12june2009-37">[37]</a></sup> "Following the completion of the detailed cost estimate and fundraising activities," the press release states, "the Department of Energy and the FutureGen Alliance will make a decision either to move forward or to discontinue the project early in 2010."
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Revised_project:_FutureGen_2.0">Revised project: FutureGen 2.0</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FutureGen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Revised project: FutureGen 2.0">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On August 5, 2010, the DOE announced a retooling of the FutureGen project, dubbed FutureGen 2.0.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-doe05aug2010_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-doe05aug2010-38">[38]</a></sup>  The revised plan includes retrofitting a shuttered coal-fired power plant in <a href="/wiki/Meredosia,_Illinois" title="Meredosia, Illinois">Meredosia, Illinois</a> to demonstrate advanced oxy-combustion technology, and piping the carbon dioxide 175 miles to Mattoon for underground storage.  Due to these changes, leaders in Mattoon decided to drop out of the FutureGen project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Illinois sites vying for the underground storage portion of the project were in Christian, Douglas, Fayette, and Morgan counties, after sites in Adams and Pike counties were cut in December 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>  In February 2011, <a href="/wiki/Morgan_County,_Illinois" title="Morgan County, Illinois">Morgan County</a> was chosen for the sequestration site.
</p><p>In Sept, 2014 FutureGen received the first-ever EPA permits for four class VI carbon dioxide sequestration wells in Morgan County, with plans to store 1.1 million metric tons per year for 20 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stl02sept2014_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-stl02sept2014-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epafuturegen_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-epafuturegen-42">[42]</a></sup>
Also in 2014 FutureGen survived a lawsuit from Illinois Electric utility ComEd, which challenged the state's ability to impose a surcharge on all customers to pay for FutureGen electricity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ccb22july14_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-ccb22july14-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to critics, including the Illinois Policy Institute, the plan presents major environmental and fiscal pitfalls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sjr20dec2011_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-sjr20dec2011-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="FutureGen_2.0_funding_cancellation">FutureGen 2.0 funding cancellation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=FutureGen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: FutureGen 2.0 funding cancellation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Department of Energy ordered suspension of FutureGen 2.0 in February, 2015. The funds, appropriated by the <a href="/wiki/American_Recovery_and_Reinvestment_Act_of_2009" title="American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009">American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009</a>, needed to be committed by July 1 and spent by Sept 30, 2015. The government also cited the Alliance's inability to raise the requisite amount of private funding. Energy Secretary Ernest Moniz explained at a press conference “If you look between now and July 1, without them having closed their financing, and try as we might, we just don’t see how it gets over the finish line.” At the time of suspension the power plant part of the project had spent $116.5 million and the sequestration part had spent $86 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bloombergsuspend_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-bloombergsuspend-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
