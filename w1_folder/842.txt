<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Land loss</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Common_physical_and_anthropogenic_causes_of_coastal_land_loss.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2522" data-file-width="1827" height="304" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Common_physical_and_anthropogenic_causes_of_coastal_land_loss.png/220px-Common_physical_and_anthropogenic_causes_of_coastal_land_loss.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Common_physical_and_anthropogenic_causes_of_coastal_land_loss.png/330px-Common_physical_and_anthropogenic_causes_of_coastal_land_loss.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Common_physical_and_anthropogenic_causes_of_coastal_land_loss.png/440px-Common_physical_and_anthropogenic_causes_of_coastal_land_loss.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Common_physical_and_anthropogenic_causes_of_coastal_land_loss.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Summary table of the common physical and anthropogenic causes of coastal land loss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morton2003_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morton2003-1">[1]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p><b>Land loss</b> is the term typically used to refer to the conversion of coastal land to open water by natural processes and human activities. The term <i>land loss</i> includes <a href="/wiki/Coastal_erosion" title="Coastal erosion">coastal erosion</a>. It is much broader term than coastal erosion because land loss also includes land converted to open water around the edges of estuaries and interior bays and lakes and by subsidence of coastal plain wetlands. The most important causes of land loss in coastal plains are <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>, inadequate sediment supply to <a href="/wiki/Beach" title="Beach">beaches</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Subsidence" title="Subsidence">subsidence</a>, and global <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level rise</a>.The mixture of processes responsible for most of the land loss will vary occurring the specific part of a coastal plain being examined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morton2003_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Morton2003-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BritschOthers1990a_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-BritschOthers1990a-2">[2]</a></sup> The definition of and loss does not include the loss of coastal lands to agriculture, urbanization, or other development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BarrasOthers1993_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-BarrasOthers1993-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although seemingly related, <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetland</a> loss, is defined differently than land loss. Commonly, <b>wetland loss</b> is defined as the conversion of vegetated wetlands into either uplands or drained areas; nonvegetated wetlands (e.g., mudflats); or (submerged habitats (open water). According to this, and similar definitions, wetland loss includes both land loss and <a href="/wiki/Land_consumption" title="Land consumption">land consumption</a> as components of it. In historic times, both wetland and land loss typically are the result of a varying, often controversial mixture of natural and anthropogenic factors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BoeschOthers1994a_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-BoeschOthers1994a-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChanOthers2007a_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ChanOthers2007a-5">[5]</a></sup> There are other definitions of wetland loss commonly used. For example, some researchers defined wetland loss as "the substantial removal of wetland from its ecologic role under natural conditions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CraigOthers1979a_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-CraigOthers1979a-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_loss_and_deltas">Land loss and deltas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_loss&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Land loss and deltas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because of a highly variable combination of <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level rise</a>, sediment starvation, coastal erosion, wetland deterioration, various types of <a href="/wiki/Subsidence" title="Subsidence">subsidence</a>, and various human activities, land loss within delta plains is a significant global problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morton2003_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Morton2003-1">[1]</a></sup> The large delta plains of the world, including the <a href="/wiki/Danube_Delta" title="Danube Delta">Danube</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ganges_Delta" title="Ganges Delta">Ganges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ganges_Delta" title="Ganges Delta">Brahmaputra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indus_River_Delta" title="Indus River Delta">Indus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mahanadi_River_Delta" title="Mahanadi River Delta">Mahanadi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mangoky_River" title="Mangoky River">Mangoky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mackenzie_Bay,_Canada" title="Mackenzie Bay, Canada">McKenzie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River_Delta" title="Mississippi River Delta">Mississippi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Niger_Delta" title="Niger Delta">Niger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nile_Delta" title="Nile Delta">Nile</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shatt_el_Arab" title="Shatt el Arab">Shatt el Arab</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volga_Delta" title="Volga Delta">Volga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yellow_River" title="Yellow River">Yellow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yukon%E2%80%93Kuskokwim_Delta" title="Yukon–Kuskokwim Delta">Yukon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zambezi" title="Zambezi">Zambezi</a> deltas, have all suffered significant land loss as the result of either coastal erosion, internal conversion of wetlands to open water, or a combination of both. For the 15 deltas studied by Coleman and others,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ColemanOthers2008_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ColemanOthers2008-7">[7]</a></sup> these deltas experienced a total irreversible land loss of 5,104 km<sup>2</sup> (1,971 sq mi) of wetlands between the early 1980s and 2002. During this period, the total average land loss for all of these deltas was about 41 km<sup>2</sup> (16 sq mi) per year. In case of the Mississippi River Delta, they found that in a 12-year period, some 253 km<sup>2</sup> (98 sq mi) of wetlands had been converted to new open water at a rate of 21 km<sup>2</sup> (8.1 sq mi) per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ColemanOthers2008_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-ColemanOthers2008-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
