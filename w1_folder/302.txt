<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Carbonated water</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:260px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Drinking_glass_00118.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="240" data-file-width="258" height="240" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/59/Drinking_glass_00118.gif" width="258"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Drinking_glass_00118.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A glass of sparkling water</div></div></div>
<table class="infobox" style="width:22em;width: 210px; float: right; clear: right; margin:0 0 1.5em 1.5em"><caption>External audio</caption><tbody><tr><td colspan="2" style="text-align:center;text-align: left">
<img alt="" data-file-height="128" data-file-width="128" height="16" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Nuvola_apps_arts.svg/16px-Nuvola_apps_arts.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Nuvola_apps_arts.svg/24px-Nuvola_apps_arts.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Nuvola_apps_arts.svg/32px-Nuvola_apps_arts.svg.png 2x" width="16"/> <a class="external text" href="https://www.sciencehistory.org/distillations/podcast/fizzy-water" rel="nofollow">“Fizzy Water”</a>, <i>Distillations</i> Podcast Episode 217, <a href="/wiki/Science_History_Institute" title="Science History Institute">Science History Institute</a></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p><b>Carbonated water</b> (<b>bubbly water</b>, <b>fizzy water</b>, or <b>soda water</b>) is <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> into which <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> gas under pressure has been dissolved, either by technology or by a natural geologic source. Many kinds of water are carbonated, including  sparkling <a href="/wiki/Mineral_water" title="Mineral water">mineral water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Club_soda" title="Club soda">club soda</a>, sparkling water or 'seltzer water' (US), <a href="/wiki/Tonic_water" title="Tonic water">tonic water</a>, and soda water. Mineral water, club soda and soda water typically contain dissolved minerals such as <a href="/wiki/Potassium_bicarbonate" title="Potassium bicarbonate">potassium bicarbonate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_bicarbonate" title="Sodium bicarbonate">sodium bicarbonate</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Potassium_sulfate" title="Potassium sulfate">potassium sulfate</a> which are meant to enhance its flavor, though sparkling water is water combined only with carbon dioxide with no other additives.  Tonic water includes <a href="/wiki/Quinine" title="Quinine">quinine</a>. <a href="/wiki/Carbonation" title="Carbonation">Carbonation</a> is the process that causes the water to become <a href="/wiki/Effervescence" title="Effervescence">effervescent</a>. All forms of carbonated water are available in ready-to-drink bottles and cans but some can also be prepared at home with a soda maker.
</p><p>It is thought the first person to aerate water with carbon dioxide was <a href="/wiki/William_Brownrigg" title="William Brownrigg">William Brownrigg</a> in 1740, although he never published a paper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Carbonated water was independently accidentally invented by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">Joseph Priestley</a> in 1767 when he discovered a method of infusing water with carbon dioxide after suspending a bowl of water above a beer vat at a brewery in <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>, England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henry_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Henry-2">[2]</a></sup> He wrote of the "peculiar satisfaction" he found in drinking it, and in 1772 he published a paper entitled <i>Impregnating Water with Fixed Air</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Priestley_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Priestley-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Priestley’s apparatus, which featured a bladder between the generator and the absorption tank to regulate the flow of carbon dioxide, was soon joined by a wide range of others, but it wasn’t until 1781 that carbonated water began being produced on a large scale with the establishment of companies specialized in producing artificial mineral water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henry_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Henry-2">[2]</a></sup> The first factory was built by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Henry_(apothecary)" title="Thomas Henry (apothecary)">Thomas Henry</a> of <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>, England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henry_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Henry-2">[2]</a></sup> Henry replaced the bladder in Priestley’s system with large bellows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henry_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Henry-2">[2]</a></sup> While Priestley is regarded as “the father of the <a href="/wiki/Soft_drink" title="Soft drink">soft drink</a>”, he did not benefit financially from his invention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henry_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Henry-2">[2]</a></sup> He did however receive scientific recognition for his invention of carbonated water when, in 1772, the Council of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> “were moved to reward its discoverer with the <a href="/wiki/Copley_Medal" title="Copley Medal">Copley Medal</a>”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbonation">Carbonation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonated_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Carbonation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Carbonation" title="Carbonation">Carbonation</a></div>
<p>Whether homemade or store-bought, soda water may be identical to plain carbonated water or it may contain a small amount of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Table_salt" title="Table salt">table salt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_citrate" title="Sodium citrate">sodium citrate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_bicarbonate" title="Sodium bicarbonate">sodium bicarbonate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium_bicarbonate" title="Potassium bicarbonate">potassium bicarbonate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium_citrate" title="Potassium citrate">potassium citrate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium_sulfate" title="Potassium sulfate">potassium sulfate</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Disodium_phosphate" title="Disodium phosphate">disodium phosphate</a>, depending on the bottler. These additives are often included to emulate the slightly salty taste soda water developed years ago from first using them as preservatives. Naturally occurring processes also produce effervescent <a href="/wiki/Mineral_water" title="Mineral water">mineral water</a> similar to carbonated water in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artesian_well" title="Artesian well">artesian wells</a>, such as in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mihalkovo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mihalkovo (page does not exist)">Mihalkovo</a> in the Bulgarian <a href="/wiki/Rhodope_Mountains" title="Rhodope Mountains">Rhodope Mountains</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Med%C5%BEitlija" title="Medžitlija">Medžitlija</a> in <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Macedonia" title="Republic of Macedonia">Macedonia</a>, or most notably in <a href="/wiki/Selters_(Taunus)" title="Selters (Taunus)">Selters</a> in the German <a href="/wiki/Taunus" title="Taunus">Taunus</a> mountains.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_effects">Health effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonated_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Health effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By itself, carbonated water appears to have little impact on health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> While carbonated water is somewhat acidic, this acidity is quickly neutralized by <a href="/wiki/Saliva" title="Saliva">saliva</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Soft drinks are about 100 times more erosive to teeth than sparkling mineral water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edition.cnn.com_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-edition.cnn.com-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Carbonated water may increase <a href="/wiki/Irritable_bowel_syndrome" title="Irritable bowel syndrome">irritable bowel syndrome</a> symptoms of bloating and gas due to the release of carbon dioxide in the digestive tract.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edition.cnn.com_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-edition.cnn.com-8">[8]</a></sup> It does not appear to have an effect on <a href="/wiki/Gastroesophageal_reflux_disease" title="Gastroesophageal reflux disease">gastroesophageal reflux disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> There is tentative evidence that carbonated water may help with <a href="/wiki/Constipation" title="Constipation">constipation</a> among people who have had a <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Soda water is defined in US law as a food of <a href="/wiki/Minimal_nutritional_value" title="Minimal nutritional value">minimal nutritional value</a>, even if minerals, vitamins, or artificial sweeteners have been added to it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">Carbon dioxide</a> gas dissolved in water at a low concentration (0.2–1.0%) creates <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a> (H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CO2Equilibrium_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-CO2Equilibrium-14">[14]</a></sup> according to the following reaction:
</p>
<dl><dd>H<sub>2</sub>O(l) + CO<sub>2</sub>(g) ⇌ H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>(aq)</dd></dl>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acid</a> gives carbonated water a slightly tart flavor (this is partly due to the <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a>). The <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> level between 3 and 4<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ph_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-ph-15">[15]</a></sup> is approximately in between <a href="/wiki/Apple_juice" title="Apple juice">apple juice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orange_juice" title="Orange juice">orange juice</a> in acidity, but much less acidic than the acid in the stomach. The human body robustly maintains pH equilibrium via <a href="/wiki/Acid%E2%80%93base_homeostasis" title="Acid–base homeostasis">acid–base homeostasis</a> and will not be affected by consumption of plain carbonated water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edition.cnn.com_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-edition.cnn.com-8">[8]</a></sup> If an <a href="/wiki/Alkalinity" title="Alkalinity">alkaline</a> <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salt</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Sodium_bicarbonate" title="Sodium bicarbonate">sodium bicarbonate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium_bicarbonate" title="Potassium bicarbonate">potassium bicarbonate</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Potassium_citrate" title="Potassium citrate">potassium citrate</a> is added to the water, its acidity is reduced.
</p><p>The amount of a gas like carbon dioxide that can be dissolved in water is described by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry%27s_Law" title="Henry's Law">Henry's Law</a>. Water is chilled, optimally to just above freezing, in order to permit the maximum amount of carbon dioxide to dissolve in it. Higher gas pressure and lower temperature cause more gas to dissolve in the liquid. When the temperature is raised or the pressure is reduced (as happens when a container of carbonated water is opened), carbon dioxide escapes from the solution, in the form of bubbles. This is known as <a href="/wiki/Effervescence" title="Effervescence">effervescence</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonated_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PSM_V05_D400_Joseph_Priestley.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2582" data-file-width="2045" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/PSM_V05_D400_Joseph_Priestley.jpg/150px-PSM_V05_D400_Joseph_Priestley.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/PSM_V05_D400_Joseph_Priestley.jpg/225px-PSM_V05_D400_Joseph_Priestley.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/PSM_V05_D400_Joseph_Priestley.jpg/300px-PSM_V05_D400_Joseph_Priestley.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PSM_V05_D400_Joseph_Priestley.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">Joseph Priestley</a> pioneered a method of carbonation in the 18th century.</div></div></div>
<p>Many alcoholic drinks, such as <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Champagne" title="Champagne">champagne</a>, were carbonated through the fermentation process for centuries. In 1662 <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Merret" title="Christopher Merret">Christopher Merret</a> was creating 'sparkling wine'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/William_Brownrigg" title="William Brownrigg">William Brownrigg</a> was apparently the first to produce artificial carbonated water, in the early 1740s, by using carbon dioxide taken from mines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In 1750 the Frenchman <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Fran%C3%A7ois_Venel" title="Gabriel François Venel">Gabriel François Venel</a> also produced artificial carbonated water, though he misunderstood the nature of the gas that caused the carbonation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1764, Irish chemist Dr. Macbride infused water with carbon dioxide as part of a series of experiments on fermentation and putrefaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> In 1766 <a href="/wiki/Henry_Cavendish" title="Henry Cavendish">Henry Cavendish</a> devised an aerating apparatus that would inspire Joseph Priestley to carry out his own experiments with regards to carbonated waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109_AERIEL_ACID_Text_Homan_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-109_AERIEL_ACID_Text_Homan-21">[21]</a></sup> Cavendish was also aware of Brownrigg's observations at this time and published a paper on his own experiments on a nearby source of mineral water at the beginning of January in the next year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Priestley_Joseph_pneumatic_trough.jpg"><img alt="Engraving of assorted scientific equipment, such as a pneumatic trough. A dead mouse rests under one glass canister." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="875" data-file-width="1063" height="181" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Priestley_Joseph_pneumatic_trough.jpg/220px-Priestley_Joseph_pneumatic_trough.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Priestley_Joseph_pneumatic_trough.jpg/330px-Priestley_Joseph_pneumatic_trough.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Priestley_Joseph_pneumatic_trough.jpg/440px-Priestley_Joseph_pneumatic_trough.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Priestley_Joseph_pneumatic_trough.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Equipment used by Priestley in his experiments on gases and the carbonation of water</div></div></div>
<p>In 1767, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">Joseph Priestley</a> discovered a method of infusing water with carbon dioxide when he suspended a bowl of water above a beer vat at a local brewery in <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>, England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The air blanketing the fermenting beer—called 'fixed air'—was known to kill mice suspended in it. Priestley found water thus treated had a pleasant taste, and he offered it to friends as a cool, refreshing drink. At that time, as even now, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_bicarbonate" title="Sodium bicarbonate">sodium bicarbonate</a> was used in medicines and for making baking powder. Known as 'soda bicarb', it was produced by bubbling carbon-dioxide through a solution of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">sodium carbonate</a> obtained from the ashes of plants. In 1772, Priestley published a paper titled <i>Impregnating Water with Fixed Air</i> in which he describes dripping "oil of vitriol" (<a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>) onto <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a> to produce carbon dioxide gas, and encouraging the gas to dissolve into an agitated bowl of water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Priestley_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Priestley-3">[3]</a></sup> Priestley referred to his invention of soda water as being his "happiest" discovery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Priestley’s apparatus, which featured a bladder between the generator and the absorption tank to regulate the flow of carbon dioxide, was soon joined by a wide range of others, but it wasn’t until 1781 that carbonated water began being produced on a large scale with the establishment of companies specialized in producing artificial mineral water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henry_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Henry-2">[2]</a></sup> The first factory was built by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Henry_(apothecary)" title="Thomas Henry (apothecary)">Thomas Henry</a> of <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>, England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henry_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Henry-2">[2]</a></sup> Henry replaced the bladder in Priestley’s system with large bellows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henry_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-Henry-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Johann_Jacob_Schweppe" title="Johann Jacob Schweppe">J. J. Schweppe</a> developed a process to manufacture bottled carbonated mineral water based on the discovery of Priestley, founding the <a href="/wiki/Schweppes" title="Schweppes">Schweppes</a> Company in Geneva in 1783. Schweppes regards Priestley as “the father of our industry”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In 1792 he moved to London to develop the business there. In 1799 Augustine Thwaites founded Thwaites' Soda Water in Dublin. A <i>London Globe</i> article claims that this company was the first to patent and sell "Soda Water" under that name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The name soda water arose from the observation by Richard Bewley, an 18th-century English chemist from <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a>, that the addition of a little sodium carbonate to the water greatly facilitated the absorption of carbon dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Modern carbonated water is made by passing pressurized carbon dioxide through water. The pressure increases the <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">solubility</a> and allows more carbon dioxide to <a href="/wiki/Solvation" title="Solvation">dissolve</a> than would be possible under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_atmospheric_pressure" title="Standard atmospheric pressure">standard atmospheric pressure</a>. When the bottle is opened, the pressure is released, allowing the gas to come out of the solution, forming the characteristic bubbles.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonated_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Belfast_Evening_Post,_August_7,_1786.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="722" data-file-width="533" height="298" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Belfast_Evening_Post%2C_August_7%2C_1786.jpg/220px-Belfast_Evening_Post%2C_August_7%2C_1786.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Belfast_Evening_Post%2C_August_7%2C_1786.jpg/330px-Belfast_Evening_Post%2C_August_7%2C_1786.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Belfast_Evening_Post%2C_August_7%2C_1786.jpg/440px-Belfast_Evening_Post%2C_August_7%2C_1786.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Belfast_Evening_Post,_August_7,_1786.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Belfast Evening Post, Belfast, Ireland, August 7, 1786</div></div></div>
<p>In the United States, carbonated water was known as <i>soda water</i> until World War II, due to the sodium salts it contained. These were added as flavoring and acidity regulators with the intent of mimicking the taste of natural <a href="/wiki/Mineral_water" title="Mineral water">mineral water</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>, it was sometimes called "two cents plain", a reference to its being the cheapest drink at <a href="/wiki/Soda_fountain" title="Soda fountain">soda fountains</a> (i.e. without the addition of three cents' worth of flavored syrup).
</p><p>In the 1950s, terms such as <i>sparkling water</i> and <i>seltzer water</i> gained favor. The term <i>seltzer water</i> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genericized_trademark" title="Genericized trademark">genericized trademark</a> that derives from the German town <a href="/wiki/Selters_(Taunus)" title="Selters (Taunus)">Selters</a>, which is renowned for its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mineral_springs" title="Mineral springs">mineral springs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Naturally carbonated water, <i><a href="/wiki/Selters" title="Selters">Selters</a></i>, has been commercially bottled and shipped from this town since the 18th century or earlier. Generally, <i>seltzer water</i> has no added sodium salts, while <i>club soda</i> still retains some of the sodium salts that once were used. The term "Seltzer water" is virtually unknown in Britain and most Commonwealth countries, although Australians of a certain age remember Selza Saline powder in metal tins with lids which had to be opened for each use with the back of a spoon, sold during the 1950s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United Kingdom and Canada today, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drink_mixers" title="Drink mixers">drink mixers</a> sold as <i>soda water</i> or <i>club soda</i> contain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bicarbonate_of_soda" title="Bicarbonate of soda">bicarbonate of soda</a>, which gives them a specific flavor and differentiates them from carbonated water. It is popularly used for mixed drinks such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whisky_and_soda" title="Whisky and soda">whisky and soda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Campari" title="Campari">Campari</a> soda.
</p>
<h3><span id="Social_popularity.2C_decline.2C_and_renaissance"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_popularity,_decline,_and_renaissance">Social popularity, decline, and renaissance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonated_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Social popularity, decline, and renaissance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Whisky_%26_Soda.jpg"><img alt="Soda syphone and whisky" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="765" height="294" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Whisky_%26_Soda.jpg/220px-Whisky_%26_Soda.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Whisky_%26_Soda.jpg/330px-Whisky_%26_Soda.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Whisky_%26_Soda.jpg/440px-Whisky_%26_Soda.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Whisky_%26_Soda.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Whisky and soda</div></div></div>
<p>The arrival of carbonated water onto the cocktail scene changed the way people drank. Instead of drinking liquor straight/neat (without a mixer), soda water and carbonated soft drinks helped dilute alcohol, and made having a drink more socially acceptable. Whisky and sodas can be seen in many British TV series and films from the 1960s and earlier and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soda_siphon" title="Soda siphon">soda siphon</a> is ubiquitous in many movies made before 1970. Only a small amount of soda water is added; a 'splash', and 'Scotch and a splash' was a common bar order. Whisky and soda was commonly drunk without ice in the UK.
</p><p>Social drinking changed with the counter-culture movement of the 1970s and the arrival of new bottled and canned beverages in the 1980s, and soda water has declined in popularity. Soda siphons are still bought by the more traditional bar trade and are available at the bar in many upmarket establishments, but in the UK there are now only two wholesalers of soda-water in traditional glass siphons, and an estimated market of around 120,000 siphons per year (2009). Worldwide, preferences are for beverages in recyclable <a href="/wiki/Plastic_container" title="Plastic container">plastic containers</a>.
</p><p>Home soda siphons and soda water are enjoying a renaissance in the 21st century as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retro" title="Retro">retro</a> items become fashionable. Contemporary soda siphons are commonly made of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum" title="Aluminum">aluminum</a>, although <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a> siphons are available. The valve-heads of today are made of plastic, with metal valves, and replaceable <a href="/wiki/O-ring" title="O-ring">o-ring</a> seals. Older siphons are in demand on on-line auction sites. Carbonated water, without the acidity regulating addition of soda, is currently seen as fashionable although home production (see above) is mainly eschewed in favor of commercial products.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Products">Products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonated_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Home">Home</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonated_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Home">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Soda_siphons">Soda siphons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonated_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Soda siphons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SiphonSeltzerAnchorBWks001.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="606" height="317" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/SiphonSeltzerAnchorBWks001.jpg/200px-SiphonSeltzerAnchorBWks001.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/SiphonSeltzerAnchorBWks001.jpg/300px-SiphonSeltzerAnchorBWks001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/SiphonSeltzerAnchorBWks001.jpg/400px-SiphonSeltzerAnchorBWks001.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SiphonSeltzerAnchorBWks001.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A soda siphon circa 1922</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Soda_syphon" title="Soda syphon">Soda syphon</a></div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soda_siphon" title="Soda siphon">soda siphon</a>, or seltzer bottle—a glass or metal pressure vessel with a release valve and spout for dispensing pressurized soda water—was a common sight in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bar_(establishment)" title="Bar (establishment)">bars</a> and in early- to mid-20th-century homes where it became a symbol of middle-class affluence.
</p><p>The gas pressure in a siphon drives soda water up through a tube inside the siphon when a valve lever at the top is depressed. Commercial soda siphons came pre-charged with water and gas, and were returned to the retailer for exchange when empty. A deposit scheme ensured they were not otherwise thrown away.
</p><p>Home soda siphons can carbonate flat water through the use of a small disposable steel bulb containing carbon dioxide. The bulb is pressed into the valve assembly at the top of the siphon, the gas injected, then the bulb withdrawn. Soda water made in this way tends not to be as carbonated as commercial soda water because water from the refrigerator is not chilled as much as possible, and the pressure of carbon dioxide is limited to that available from the cartridge rather than the high-pressure pumps in a commercial carbonation plant.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gasogene">Gasogene</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonated_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Gasogene">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seltzogene.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="581" height="344" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Seltzogene.jpg/200px-Seltzogene.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Seltzogene.jpg/300px-Seltzogene.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Seltzogene.jpg/400px-Seltzogene.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Seltzogene.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Late Victorian seltzogene made by British Syphon</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gasogene" title="Gasogene">gasogene</a> (or gazogene, or seltzogene) is a late <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian</a> device for producing carbonated water. It consists of two linked glass globes: the lower contained water or other drink to be made sparkling, the upper a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Tartaric_acid" title="Tartaric acid">tartaric acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_bicarbonate" title="Sodium bicarbonate">sodium bicarbonate</a> that reacts to produce <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. The produced gas pushes the liquid in the lower container up a tube and out of the device. The globes are surrounded by a <a href="/wiki/Wicker" title="Wicker">wicker</a> or wire protective mesh, as they have a tendency to explode.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Codd-neck_bottles">Codd-neck bottles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonated_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Codd-neck bottles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:77px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Codd-neck_Soda_Water_Bottle_from_Kerala.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3497" data-file-width="957" height="274" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Codd-neck_Soda_Water_Bottle_from_Kerala.png/75px-Codd-neck_Soda_Water_Bottle_from_Kerala.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Codd-neck_Soda_Water_Bottle_from_Kerala.png/113px-Codd-neck_Soda_Water_Bottle_from_Kerala.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Codd-neck_Soda_Water_Bottle_from_Kerala.png/150px-Codd-neck_Soda_Water_Bottle_from_Kerala.png 2x" width="75"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Codd-neck_Soda_Water_Bottle_from_Kerala.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Codd-neck_bottle" title="Codd-neck bottle">Codd-neck bottle</a>'s special shape is designed to contain a marble which seals in the carbonation.</div></div></div>
<p>In 1872, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> <a href="/wiki/Bottling_company" title="Bottling company">soft drink maker</a> <a href="/wiki/Hiram_Codd" title="Hiram Codd">Hiram Codd</a> of <a href="/wiki/Camberwell" title="Camberwell">Camberwell</a>, London, designed and patented the <a href="/wiki/Codd-neck_bottle" title="Codd-neck bottle">Codd-neck bottle</a>, designed specifically for <a href="/wiki/Carbonation" title="Carbonation">carbonated</a> drinks. The <i>Codd-neck bottle</i> encloses a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marbles" title="Marbles">marble</a> and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubber" title="Rubber">rubber</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washer_(mechanical)" title="Washer (mechanical)">washer/gasket</a> in the neck. The bottles were filled upside down, and pressure of the <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> in the bottle forced the marble against the washer, sealing in the carbonation. The bottle was pinched into a special shape to provide a chamber into which the marble was pushed to open the bottle. This prevented the marble from blocking the neck as the drink was poured.
</p><p>Soon after its introduction, the bottle became extremely popular with the soft drink and <a href="/wiki/Brewing" title="Brewing">brewing</a> industries mainly in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australasia" title="Australasia">Australasia</a>, though some <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a> drinkers disdained the use of the bottle. One <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymology</a> of the term <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/codswallop" title="wikt:codswallop">codswallop</a></i> originates from beer sold in Codd bottles, though this is generally dismissed as a <a href="/wiki/Folk_etymology" title="Folk etymology">folk etymology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>The bottles were regularly produced for many decades, but gradually declined in usage. Since children smashed the bottles to retrieve the marbles, they are relatively rare and have become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collector_item" title="Collector item">collector items</a>, particularly in the UK. They could be found at retail shops and restaurants in many parts of the world until recently.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (December 2015)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> Due to the risk of explosion and injuries from fragmented glass pieces, use of this type of bottle is no longer encouraged in most countries. The Codd-neck design is still used for the <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> soft drink <a href="/wiki/Ramune" title="Ramune">Ramune</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indian</a> drink called <a href="/wiki/Banta" title="Banta">Banta</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Soda_makers">Soda makers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonated_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Soda makers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Trinkwasserbesprudler.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="1616" height="372" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Trinkwasserbesprudler.jpg/220px-Trinkwasserbesprudler.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Trinkwasserbesprudler.jpg/330px-Trinkwasserbesprudler.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Trinkwasserbesprudler.jpg/440px-Trinkwasserbesprudler.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Trinkwasserbesprudler.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A typical all-in-one soda maker for home use found in supermarkets. A refillable carbon dioxide canister and a high pressure bottle are often included.</div></div></div>
<p>Soda makers or soda carbonators are appliances that carbonate water with multiple-use carbon dioxide canisters. Soda makers may reach a higher level of carbonation than home soda siphons.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A variety of systems are produced by manufacturers and hobbyists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mineral_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-mineral-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-instr_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-instr-34">[34]</a></sup> The commercial units may be sold with concentrated syrup for making flavored soft drinks.
</p><p>One major producer of soda carbonators is <a href="/wiki/SodaStream" title="SodaStream">SodaStream</a>. Their products were popular during the 1970s and 1980s in the United Kingdom, and are associated with nostalgia for that period and have experienced a comeback since.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-waitrose_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-waitrose-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-observer_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-observer-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial">Commercial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonated_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Commercial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Soda_gun.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="348" data-file-width="702" height="109" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Soda_gun.jpg/220px-Soda_gun.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Soda_gun.jpg/330px-Soda_gun.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Soda_gun.jpg/440px-Soda_gun.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Soda_gun.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A modern bar <a href="/wiki/Soda_gun" title="Soda gun">soda gun</a></div></div></div>
<p>The process of dissolving carbon dioxide in water is called <a href="/wiki/Carbonation" title="Carbonation">carbonation</a>. Commercial soda water in siphons is made by chilling filtered plain water to 8 °C (46 °F) or below, optionally adding a <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> or <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a> based alkaline compound such as <a href="/wiki/Sodium_bicarbonate" title="Sodium bicarbonate">sodium bicarbonate</a> to reduce acidity, and then pressurizing the water with carbon dioxide. The gas dissolves in the water, and a top-off fill of carbon dioxide is added to pressurize the siphon to approximately 120 pounds per square inch (830 kPa), some 30 to 40 psi (210–280 kPa) higher than is present in fermenting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Champagne_(wine)" title="Champagne (wine)">champagne</a> bottles.
</p><p>In many modern <a href="/wiki/Restaurant" title="Restaurant">restaurants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drinking_establishment" title="Drinking establishment">drinking establishments</a>, soda water is manufactured on-site using devices known as carbonators. Carbonators use mechanical pumps to pump water into a pressurized chamber where it is combined with <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> from pressurized tanks at approximately 100 psi (690 kPa). The pressurized, carbonated water then flows to taps or to mixing heads where it is then mixed with flavorings as it is dispensed.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Combined_usage">Combined usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonated_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Combined usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbonated_beverages">Carbonated beverages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonated_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Carbonated beverages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Soft_drink#Health_concerns" title="Soft drink">Soft drink § Health concerns</a></div>
<p>Plain carbonated water is often consumed as an alternative to soft drinks. However, a <a href="/wiki/Soft_drink" title="Soft drink">soft drink</a> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beverage" title="Beverage">beverage</a> that typically contains carbonated water, a <a href="/wiki/Sweetness" title="Sweetness">sweetener</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Flavor" title="Flavor">flavoring</a>. Flavored carbonated water is also commercially available like <a href="/wiki/Cola" title="Cola">cola</a>. Carbonated water is often <a href="/wiki/Drink" title="Drink">consumed</a> mixed with <a href="/wiki/Juice" title="Juice">fruit juice</a>. It differs from sodas in that it contains flavors (usually sour fruit flavors such as <a href="/wiki/Lemon" title="Lemon">lemon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lime_(fruit)" title="Lime (fruit)">lime</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cherry" title="Cherry">cherry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orange_(fruit)" title="Orange (fruit)">orange</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Raspberry" title="Raspberry">raspberry</a>) and usually a sweetener. Many people cut up fresh fruit and put in their carbonated water.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alcoholic_beverages">Alcoholic beverages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonated_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Alcoholic beverages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Carbonated water is a <a href="/wiki/Diluent" title="Diluent">diluent</a> mixed with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distilled_beverage" title="Distilled beverage">alcoholic beverages</a> where it is used to top-off the drink and provide a degree of 'fizz'.
</p><p>Adding soda water to 'short' drinks such as spirits dilutes them and makes them 'long' not to be confused with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_drinks" title="Long drinks">long drinks</a> such as those made with <a href="/wiki/Vermouth" title="Vermouth">vermouth</a>. Carbonated water also works well in short drinks made with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whiskey" title="Whiskey">whiskey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brandy" title="Brandy">brandy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Campari" title="Campari">Campari</a>. Soda water may be used to dilute drinks based on cordials such as orange <a href="/wiki/Squash_(drink)" title="Squash (drink)">squash</a>. Soda water is a necessary ingredient in many cocktails, such as <a href="/wiki/Whisky" title="Whisky">whisky</a> and soda or <a href="/wiki/Campari" title="Campari">Campari</a> and soda.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cooking">Cooking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonated_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Cooking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Carbonated water is increasingly popular in cooking to provide a lighter texture to doughs and batters as compared to regular water. Kevin Ryan, a food scientist at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Illinois_at_Urbana%E2%80%93Champaign" title="University of Illinois at Urbana–Champaign">University of Illinois at Urbana–Champaign</a>, says the effervescent bubbles when mixed with dough provide the light texture (also known as Tempura). Pockets of carbon dioxide gas are introduced into the dough and further expand when cooking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stain_remover">Stain remover</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonated_water&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Stain remover">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The popular belief that carbonated water is a good remover of clothing stains, particularly those of red wine, is based on hearsay and anecdotal evidence. The dissolved gas in water acts as a temporary <a href="/wiki/Surfactant" title="Surfactant">surfactant</a>. There is no underlying chemical reason why carbonated water would be superior to plain water in stain removal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
