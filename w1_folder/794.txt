<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Water resource management</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Water resource management</b> is the activity of planning, developing, distributing and managing the optimum use of <a href="/wiki/Water_resources" title="Water resources">water resources</a>. It is a sub-set of <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle_management" title="Water cycle management">water cycle management</a>. Ideally, water resource management planning has regard to all the competing demands for <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> and seeks to allocate water on an equitable basis to satisfy all uses and demands. As with other <a href="/wiki/Resource_management" title="Resource management">resource management</a>, this is rarely possible in practice.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_resource_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Earth_water_distribution.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="657" data-file-width="512" height="372" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Earth_water_distribution.svg/290px-Earth_water_distribution.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Earth_water_distribution.svg/435px-Earth_water_distribution.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Earth_water_distribution.svg/580px-Earth_water_distribution.svg.png 2x" width="290"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Earth_water_distribution.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Visualisation of the distribution (by volume) of water on Earth. Each tiny cube (such as the one representing biological water) corresponds to approximately 1,000 cubic kilometres (240 cu mi) of water, with a mass of approximately <a href="/wiki/Orders_of_magnitude_(mass)#1012_to_1017_kg" title="Orders of magnitude (mass)">1 trillion tonnes</a> (2000 times that of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Pyramid_of_Giza" title="Great Pyramid of Giza">Great Pyramid of Giza</a> or 5 times that of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Kariba" title="Lake Kariba">Lake Kariba</a>, arguably the heaviest man-made object). The entire block comprises 1 million tiny cubes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Water is an essential resource for all life on the planet. Of the water resources on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> only three percent of it is fresh and two-thirds of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a> is locked up in <a href="/wiki/Ice_cap" title="Ice cap">ice caps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a>. Of the remaining one percent, a fifth is in remote, inaccessible areas and much seasonal rainfall in <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoonal</a> deluges and floods cannot easily be used. As time advances, water is becoming scarcer and having access to clean, safe, <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a> is limited among countries. At present only about 0.08 percent of all the world’s fresh water<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  is exploited by mankind in ever increasing demand for <a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">sanitation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_sports" title="Water sports">leisure</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>. Due to the small percentage of water remaining, optimizing the fresh water we have left from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_resources" title="Natural resources">natural resources</a> has been a continuous difficulty in several locations worldwide.
</p><p>Much effort in water resource management is directed at optimizing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_use" title="Water use">use of water</a> and in minimizing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_impact" title="Environmental impact">environmental impact</a> of water use on the natural environment. The observation of water as an integral part of the <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> is based on integrated water resource management, where the quantity and quality of the ecosystem help to determine the nature of the natural resources.
</p><p>As a limited resource, water supply sometimes supposes a challenge. This fact is assumed by the project <a class="external text" href="http://desafioglobal.org/" rel="nofollow">DESAFIO</a> (the acronym for <i>Democratisation of Water and Sanitation Governance by Means of Socio-Technical Innovations</i>), which has been developed along 30 months and funded by the European Union’s Seventh Framework Programme for research, technological development and demonstration. This project faced a difficult task for developing areas: eliminating structural social inequity in the access to indispensable water and public health services. The <a class="external text" href="http://desafioglobal.org/" rel="nofollow">DESAFIO</a> engineers worked on a water treatment system run with solar power and filters which provides safe water to a very poor community in the state of Minas Gerais.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Successful management of any resources requires accurate knowledge of the resource available, the uses to which it may be put, the competing demands for the resource, measures to and processes to evaluate the significance and worth of competing demands and mechanisms to translate policy decisions into actions on the ground.
</p><p>For water as a resource, this is particularly difficult since sources of water can cross many national boundaries and the uses of water include many that are difficult to assign <a href="/wiki/Commodification_of_nature" title="Commodification of nature">financial value</a> to and may also be difficult to manage in conventional terms. Examples include rare species or ecosystems or the very long term value of ancient <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> reserves.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">Agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_resource_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">Agriculture</a> is the largest user of the world's freshwater resources, consuming 70 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> As the <a href="/wiki/World_population" title="World population">world population</a> rises it consumes more food (currently exceeding 6%, it is expected to reach 9% by 2050), the industries and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urban_development" title="Urban development">urban developments</a> expand, and the emerging <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuel</a> crops trade also demands a share of freshwater resources, <a href="/wiki/Water_scarcity" title="Water scarcity">water scarcity</a> is becoming an important issue. An assessment of water resource management in agriculture was conducted in 2007 by the <a href="/wiki/International_Water_Management_Institute" title="International Water Management Institute">International Water Management Institute</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> to see if the world had sufficient water to provide food for its growing population or not .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It assessed the current availability of water for agriculture on a global scale and mapped out locations suffering from water scarcity. It found that a fifth of the world's people, more than 1.2 billion, live in areas of <a href="/wiki/Physical_water_scarcity" title="Physical water scarcity">physical water scarcity</a>, where there is not enough water to meet all their demands. A further 1.6 billion people live in areas experiencing <a href="/wiki/Economic_water_scarcity" title="Economic water scarcity">economic water scarcity</a>, where the lack of investment in water or insufficient human capacity make it impossible for authorities to satisfy the demand for water.
</p><p>The report found that it would be possible to produce the food required in future, but that continuation of today's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_production" title="Food production">food production</a> and environmental trends would lead to crises in many parts of the world. Regarding food production, the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> targets agricultural food production and water resource management as an increasingly <a href="/wiki/Global_issue" title="Global issue">global issue</a> that is fostering an important and growing debate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The authors of the book <i>Out of Water: From abundance to Scarcity and How to Solve the World's Water Problems</i>, which laid down a six-point plan for solving the world's water problems. These are: 1) Improve data related to water; 2) Treasure the environment; 3) Reform <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_governance" title="Water governance">water governance</a>; 4) Revitalize agricultural water use; 5) Manage urban and industrial demand; and 6) Empower the poor and women in water resource management. To avoid a global water crisis, farmers will have to strive to increase <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_productivity" title="Agricultural productivity">productivity</a> to meet growing demands for food, while industry and cities find ways to use water more efficiently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Managing_water_in_urban_settings">Managing water in urban settings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_resource_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Managing water in urban settings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As the <a href="/wiki/Carrying_capacity" title="Carrying capacity">carrying capacity</a> of the Earth increases greatly due to technological advances, <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">urbanization</a> in modern times occurs because of economic opportunity. This rapid urbanization happens worldwide but mostly in new rising economies and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_countries" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a>. Cities in Africa and Asia are growing fastest with 28 out of 39 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megacities" title="Megacities">megacities</a> (a city or urban area with more than 10 million inhabitants) worldwide in these developing nations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>  The number of megacities will continue to rise reaching approximately 50 in 2025. With <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_economies" title="Developing economies">developing economies</a> <a href="/wiki/Water_scarcity" title="Water scarcity">water scarcity</a> is a very common and very prevalent issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Global freshwater resources dwindle in the eastern hemisphere either than at the poles, and with the majority of urban development millions live with insufficient fresh water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> This is caused by <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">polluted</a> freshwater resources, <a href="/wiki/Overexploitation" title="Overexploitation">overexploited</a> <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> resources, insufficient harvesting capacities in the surrounding rural areas, poorly constructed and maintained water supply systems, high amount of informal water use and insufficient technical and water management capacities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the areas surrounding urban centres, <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> must compete with industry and municipal users for safe <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_supplies" title="Water supplies">water supplies</a>, while traditional water sources are becoming <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">polluted</a> with <a href="/wiki/Urban_runoff" title="Urban runoff">urban runoff</a>. As cities offer the best opportunities for selling produce, farmers often have no alternative to using polluted water to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irrigate" title="Irrigate">irrigate</a> their crops. Depending on how developed a city’s <a href="/wiki/Wastewater_treatment" title="Wastewater treatment">wastewater treatment</a> is, there can be significant health hazards related to the use of this water. Wastewater from cities can contain a mixture of pollutants. There is usually wastewater from kitchens and toilets along with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainwater_runoff" title="Rainwater runoff">rainwater runoff</a>. This means that the water usually contains excessive levels of nutrients and salts, as well as a wide range of <a href="/wiki/Pathogen" title="Pathogen">pathogens</a>. <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" title="Heavy metals">Heavy metals</a> may also be present, along with traces of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibiotics" title="Antibiotics">antibiotics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Endocrine_disruptor" title="Endocrine disruptor">endocrine disruptors</a>, such as oestrogens.
</p><p>Developing world countries tend to have the lowest levels of wastewater treatment. Often, the water that farmers use for irrigating crops is contaminated with pathogens from <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewage</a>. The pathogens of most concern are bacteria, viruses and <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Parasitic_worms" title="Parasitic worms">parasitic worms</a>, which directly affect farmers’ health and indirectly affect consumers if they eat the contaminated crops. Common illnesses include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diarrhoea" title="Diarrhoea">diarrhoea</a>, which kills 1.1 million people annually and is the second most common cause of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Infant_death&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Infant death (page does not exist)">infant deaths</a>. Many <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a> outbreaks are also related to the reuse of poorly treated wastewater. Actions that reduce or remove contamination, therefore, have the potential to save a large number of lives and improve livelihoods. Scientists have been working to find ways to reduce contamination of food using a method called the 'multiple-barrier approach'.
</p><p>This involves analysing the food production process from growing crops to selling them in markets and eating them, then considering where it might be possible to create a barrier against contamination. Barriers include: introducing safer irrigation practices; promoting on-farm wastewater treatment; taking actions that cause pathogens to die off; and effectively washing crops after harvest in markets and restaurants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Urban_Decision_Support_System_.28UDSS.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_Decision_Support_System_(UDSS)">Urban Decision Support System (UDSS)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_resource_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Urban Decision Support System (UDSS)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Urban <a href="/wiki/Decision_support_system" title="Decision support system">Decision Support System</a> (UDSS)  – is a wireless device with a mobile app that uses sensors attached to water appliances in urban residences to collect data about water usage and is an example of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Data-driven_urban_water_management&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Data-driven urban water management (page does not exist)">data-driven urban water management</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The system was developed with a European Commission investment of 2.46 Million Euros<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> to improve the water consumption behaviour of households. Information about every mechanism – dishwashers, showers, washing machines, taps – is wirelessly recorded and sent to the UDSS App on the user’s mobile device. The UDSS is then able to analyse and show homeowners which of their appliances are using the most water, and which behaviour or habits of the households are not encouraged in order to reduce the water usage, rather than simply giving a total usage figure for the whole property, which will allow people to manage their consumption more economically. The UDSS is based on university research in the field of <a href="/wiki/Management_science" title="Management science">Management Science</a>, at <a href="/wiki/Loughborough_University" title="Loughborough University">Loughborough University</a> School of Business and Economics, particularly Decision Support System in household water benchmarking, led by <a class="external text" href="http://www.lboro.ac.uk/departments/sbe/staff/profiles/yanglili/yang-lili.html" rel="nofollow">Dr Lili Yang</a>, (Reader)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_of_water_resources">Future of water resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_resource_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Future of water resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of the biggest concerns for our water-based resources in the future is the sustainability of the current and even future water resource allocation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> As <a href="/wiki/Water_scarcity" title="Water scarcity">water becomes more scarce</a>, the importance of how it is managed grows vastly. Finding a balance between what is needed by humans and what is needed in the environment is an important step in the sustainability of water resources. Attempts to create sustainable freshwater systems have been seen on a national level in countries such as <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, and such commitment to the environment could set a model for the rest of the world.
</p><p>The field of water resources management will have to continue to adapt to the current and future issues facing the allocation of water. With the growing uncertainties of global <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> and the long term impacts of management actions,the decision-making will be even more difficult. It is likely that ongoing climate change will lead to situations that have not been encountered. As a result, alternative management strategies are sought for in order to avoid setbacks in the allocation of water resources.
</p>
