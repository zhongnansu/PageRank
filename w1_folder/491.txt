<p>The <b>National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</b> (<b>NOAA</b>, <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="/./: syllable break">.</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span> like <i><a href="/wiki/Noah_(name)" title="Noah (name)">Noah</a></i>) is an American scientific agency within the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Commerce" title="United States Department of Commerce">United States Department of Commerce</a> that focuses on the conditions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceans" title="Oceans">oceans</a>, major waterways, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a>. 
</p><p>NOAA warns of dangerous <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a>, charts seas, guides the use and protection of ocean and coastal resources, and conducts research to provide understanding and improve stewardship of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" title="Environment (biophysical)">environment</a>.
</p><p>NOAA was officially formed in 1970<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and in 2017 had over 11,000 civilian employees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BestPlacesToWork.org_Agency_Report_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-BestPlacesToWork.org_Agency_Report-3">[3]</a></sup> Its research and operations are further supported by 321 <a href="/wiki/Uniformed_services_of_the_United_States" title="Uniformed services of the United States">uniformed service</a> members who make up the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA_Commissioned_Corps" title="NOAA Commissioned Corps">NOAA Commissioned Corps</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since October 2017, NOAA has been headed by <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Gallaudet" title="Timothy Gallaudet">Timothy Gallaudet</a>, as acting <a href="/wiki/Under_Secretary_of_Commerce_for_Oceans_and_Atmosphere" title="Under Secretary of Commerce for Oceans and Atmosphere">Under Secretary of Commerce for Oceans and Atmosphere</a> and NOAA interim administrator.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Purpose_and_function">Purpose and function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Purpose and function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NOAA_WP-3D_Orions.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2357" data-file-width="2942" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/NOAA_WP-3D_Orions.jpg/250px-NOAA_WP-3D_Orions.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/NOAA_WP-3D_Orions.jpg/375px-NOAA_WP-3D_Orions.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/NOAA_WP-3D_Orions.jpg/500px-NOAA_WP-3D_Orions.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NOAA_WP-3D_Orions.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Two NOAA <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_WP-3D_Orion" title="Lockheed WP-3D Orion">WP-3D Orions</a></div></div></div>
<p>NOAA plays several specific roles in society, the benefits of which extend beyond the US economy and into the larger global community:
</p>
<ul><li><i>A Supplier of Environmental Information Products</i>. NOAA supplies to its customers and partners information pertaining to the state of the oceans and the atmosphere. This is clear through the production of weather warnings and forecasts via the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a>, but NOAA's information products extend to climate, ecosystems and commerce as well.</li>
<li><i>A Provider of Environmental Stewardship Services</i>. NOAA is a steward of U.S. coastal and marine environments. In coordination with federal, state, local, tribal and international authorities, NOAA manages the use of these environments, regulating fisheries and marine sanctuaries as well as protecting threatened and endangered marine species.</li>
<li><i>A Leader in Applied Scientific Research</i>. NOAA is intended to be a source of accurate and objective scientific information in the four particular areas of national and global importance identified above: ecosystems, climate, weather and water, and commerce and transportation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The five "fundamental activities" are:
</p>
<ul><li>Monitoring and observing Earth systems with instruments and data collection networks.</li>
<li>Understanding and describing Earth systems through research and analysis of that data.</li>
<li>Assessing and predicting the changes of these systems over time.</li>
<li>Engaging, advising, and informing the public and partner organizations with important information.</li>
<li>Managing resources for the betterment of society, economy and environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>NOAA traces its history back to multiple agencies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> some of which were among the oldest in the federal government:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Coast_and_Geodetic_Survey" title="U.S. Coast and Geodetic Survey">United States Coast and Geodetic Survey</a>, formed in 1807</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">Weather Bureau of the United States</a>, formed in 1870</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/United_States_Fish_Commission" title="United States Fish Commission">Bureau of Commercial Fisheries</a>, formed in 1871 (research fleet only)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA_Commissioned_Corps" title="NOAA Commissioned Corps">Coast and Geodetic Survey Corps</a>, formed in 1917</li></ul>
<p>Another direct predecessor of NOAA was the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Science_Services_Administration" title="Environmental Science Services Administration">Environmental Science Services Administration</a> (ESSA), into which several existing scientific agencies such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Coast_and_Geodetic_Survey" title="U.S. Coast and Geodetic Survey">United States Coast and Geodetic Survey</a>, the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">Weather Bureau</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA_Commissioned_Corps" title="NOAA Commissioned Corps">uniformed Corps</a> were absorbed in 1965.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>NOAA was established within the Department of Commerce via the Reorganization Plan No. 4<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and formed on October 3, 1970 after U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a> proposed creating a new agency to serve a national need for "better protection of life and property from natural hazards …for a better understanding of the total environment…[and] for exploration and development leading to the intelligent use of our marine resources." 
</p><p>In 2007, NOAA celebrated 200 years of service in its role as successor to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Coast_and_Geodetic_Survey" title="U.S. Coast and Geodetic Survey">United States Survey of the Coast</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 2013, NOAA closed 600 weather stations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="NOAA_Administrator">NOAA Administrator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: NOAA Administrator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since October 25, 2017 <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Gallaudet" title="Timothy Gallaudet">Timothy Gallaudet</a>, Assistant Secretary of Commerce for Oceans and Atmosphere, has served as acting <a href="/wiki/Under_Secretary_of_Commerce_for_Oceans_and_Atmosphere" title="Under Secretary of Commerce for Oceans and Atmosphere">Under Secretary of Commerce for Oceans and Atmosphere</a> at the US Department of Commerce and NOAA's interim administrator. 
</p><p>Gallaudet succeeded Benjamin Friedman, who served as NOAA's interim administrator since the end of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obama_Administration" title="Obama Administration">Obama Administration</a> on January 20, 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In October 2017, <a href="/wiki/Barry_Lee_Myers" title="Barry Lee Myers">Barry Lee Myers</a>, CEO of <a href="/wiki/AccuWeather" title="AccuWeather">AccuWeather</a>, was proposed to be the agency's administrator by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trump_Administration" title="Trump Administration">Trump Administration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="NOAA_Services">NOAA Services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: NOAA Services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>NOAA works toward its mission through six major line offices, the National Environmental Satellite, Data and Information Service (NESDIS), the <a href="/wiki/National_Marine_Fisheries_Service" title="National Marine Fisheries Service">National Marine Fisheries Service</a> (NMFS), the <a href="/wiki/National_Ocean_Service" title="National Ocean Service">National Ocean Service</a> (NOS), the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> (NWS), the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Research" title="Office of Oceanic and Atmospheric Research">Office of Oceanic and Atmospheric Research</a> (OAR) and the Office of Marine &amp; Aviation Operations (OMAO).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA-17">[17]</a></sup> and in addition more than a dozen staff offices, including the Office of the Federal Coordinator for Meteorology, the <a href="/wiki/NOAA_Central_Library" title="NOAA Central Library">NOAA Central Library</a>, the Office of Program Planning and Integration (PPI).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Weather_Service">National Weather Service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: National Weather Service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:US-NationalWeatherService-Logo.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="720" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/US-NationalWeatherService-Logo.svg/200px-US-NationalWeatherService-Logo.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/US-NationalWeatherService-Logo.svg/300px-US-NationalWeatherService-Logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/US-NationalWeatherService-Logo.svg/400px-US-NationalWeatherService-Logo.svg.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:US-NationalWeatherService-Logo.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Seal of the National Weather Service</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> (NWS) is tasked with providing "weather, hydrologic and climate forecasts and warnings for the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, its territories, adjacent waters and ocean areas, for the protection of life and property and the enhancement of the national economy." This is done through a collection of national and regional centers, 13 river forecast centers (RFCs), and more than 120 local weather forecast offices (WFOs). They are charged with issuing weather and river <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">forecasts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Severe_weather_terminology_(United_States)" title="Severe weather terminology (United States)">advisories, watches, and warnings</a> on a daily basis. They issue more than 734,000 weather and 850,000 river forecasts, and more than 45,000 <a href="/wiki/Severe_weather_terminology_(United_States)" title="Severe weather terminology (United States)">severe weather warnings</a> annually. NOAA data is also relevant to the issues of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">ozone depletion</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The NWS operates <a href="/wiki/NEXRAD" title="NEXRAD">NEXRAD</a>, a nationwide network of Doppler <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">weather radars</a> which can detect <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> and their velocities. Many of their products are broadcast on <a href="/wiki/NOAA_Weather_Radio" title="NOAA Weather Radio">NOAA Weather Radio</a>, a network of radio <a href="/wiki/Transmitter" title="Transmitter">transmitters</a> that broadcasts weather forecasts, severe weather statements, watches and warnings 24 hours a day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Ocean_Service">National Ocean Service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: National Ocean Service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Ocean_Service" title="National Ocean Service">National Ocean Service</a> (NOS) focuses on ensuring that ocean and coastal areas are safe, healthy, and productive. NOS scientists, natural resource managers, and specialists serve America by ensuring safe and efficient marine transportation, promoting innovative solutions to protect coastal communities, and conserving marine and coastal places.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The National Ocean Service is composed of eight program offices: the Center for Operational Oceanographic Products and Services,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> the Coastal Services Center,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> the National Centers for Coastal Ocean Science,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Coast_Survey" title="Office of Coast Survey">Office of Coast Survey</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> the Office of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Geodetic_Survey" title="National Geodetic Survey">National Geodetic Survey</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> the Office of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Marine_Sanctuaries" title="National Marine Sanctuaries">National Marine Sanctuaries</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> the Office of Ocean and Coastal Resource Management<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Response_and_Restoration" title="Office of Response and Restoration">Office of Response and Restoration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are two NOS programs, namely the <a href="/wiki/Mussel_Watch_Program" title="Mussel Watch Program">Mussel Watch Contaminant Monitoring Program</a> and the NOAA Integrated Ocean Observing System (IOOS) and two staff offices, the International Program Office and the Management and Budget Office.
</p>
<h4><span id="National_Environmental_Satellite.2C_Data.2C_and_Information_Service"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Environmental_Satellite,_Data,_and_Information_Service">National Environmental Satellite, Data, and Information Service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: National Environmental Satellite, Data, and Information Service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NOAAEngineerAtWork.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="480" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/NOAAEngineerAtWork.jpg/170px-NOAAEngineerAtWork.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/NOAAEngineerAtWork.jpg/255px-NOAAEngineerAtWork.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/NOAAEngineerAtWork.jpg/340px-NOAAEngineerAtWork.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NOAAEngineerAtWork.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>NOAA engineer at work</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Environmental_Satellite,_Data,_and_Information_Service" title="National Environmental Satellite, Data, and Information Service">National Environmental Satellite, Data, and Information Service</a> (NESDIS) was created by NOAA to operate and manage the US environmental satellite programs, and manage NWS data and those of other government agencies and departments.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> NESDIS's <a href="/wiki/National_Centers_for_Environmental_Information" title="National Centers for Environmental Information">National Centers for Environmental Information</a> (NCEI) archives data collected by the NOAA, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" title="U.S. Navy">U.S. Navy</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force" title="U.S. Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">Federal Aviation Administration</a>, and meteorological services around the world and comprises the Center for Weather and Climate (previously NOAA's <a href="/wiki/National_Climatic_Data_Center" title="National Climatic Data Center">National Climatic Data Center</a>) and the Center for Coasts, Oceans, and Geophysics (created by a merger of NOAA's National Coastal Data Development Center (NCDDC), <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanographic_Data_Center" title="National Oceanographic Data Center">National Oceanographic Data Center</a> (NODC) and the <a href="/wiki/National_Geophysical_Data_Center" title="National Geophysical Data Center">National Geophysical Data Center</a> (NGDC)).
</p><p>In 1960 <a href="/wiki/TIROS-1" title="TIROS-1">TIROS-1</a>, NOAA's first owned and operated geostationary satellite was launched. 
Since 1966 NESDIS has managed polar orbiting satellites (POES) and since 1974 it has operated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geosynchronous_satellites" title="Geosynchronous satellites">geosynchronous satellites</a> (GOES) . In 1979 NOAA's first <a href="/wiki/Polar_Operational_Environmental_Satellites" title="Polar Operational Environmental Satellites">polar-orbiting environmental satellite</a> was launched. Current operational satellites include <a href="/wiki/NOAA-15" title="NOAA-15">NOAA-15</a>, <a href="/wiki/NOAA-18" title="NOAA-18">NOAA-18</a>, <a href="/wiki/NOAA-19" title="NOAA-19">NOAA-19</a>, <a href="/wiki/GOES_13" title="GOES 13">GOES 13</a>, <a href="/wiki/GOES_14" title="GOES 14">GOES 14</a>, <a href="/wiki/GOES_15" title="GOES 15">GOES 15</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jason-2" title="Jason-2">Jason-2</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DSCOVR" title="DSCOVR">DSCOVR</a>. In 1983, NOAA assumed operational responsibility for <a href="/wiki/Landsat_program" title="Landsat program">Landsat</a> satellite system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
Since May 1998, NESIDS has operated the <a href="/wiki/Defense_Meteorological_Satellite_Program" title="Defense Meteorological Satellite Program">Defense Meteorological Satellite Program</a> (DMSP) satellites on behalf of the <a href="/wiki/557th_Weather_Wing" title="557th Weather Wing">Air Force Weather Agency</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>New generations of satellites are developed to succeed the current polar orbiting and geosynchronous satellites, the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Polar_Satellite_System" title="Joint Polar Satellite System">Joint Polar Satellite System</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GOES-R" title="GOES-R">GOES-R</a>, which is scheduled for launch in March 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>NESDIS runs the Office of Projects, Planning, and Analysis (OPPA)] formerly the Office of Systems Development,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> the Office of Satellite Ground Systems (formerly the Office of Satellite Operations)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> the Office of Satellite and Project Operations,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> the Center for Satellite Applications and Research (STAR)],<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> the Joint Polar Satellite System Program Office<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_Operational_Environmental_Satellite" title="Geostationary Operational Environmental Satellite">GOES-R</a> Program Office, the International &amp; Interagency Affairs Office, the Office of Space Commercialization<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> and the Office of System Architecture and Advanced Planning.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Marine_Fisheries_Service">National Marine Fisheries Service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: National Marine Fisheries Service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Marine_Fisheries_Service" title="National Marine Fisheries Service">National Marine Fisheries Service</a> (NMFS), also known as NOAA Fisheries, was initiated in 1871 with a primary goal of the research, protection, management, and restoration of commercial and recreational <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fisheries" title="Fisheries">fisheries</a> and their habitat, and protected species. NMFS operates twelve headquarters offices, five regional offices, six fisheries science centers, and more than 20 laboratories throughout the United States and U.S. territories, which are the sites of research and management of marine resources. NMFS also operates the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration_Fisheries_Office_for_Law_Enforcement" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration Fisheries Office for Law Enforcement">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration Fisheries Office of Law Enforcement</a> in <a href="/wiki/Silver_Spring,_Maryland" title="Silver Spring, Maryland">Silver Spring, Maryland</a>, which is the primary site of marine resource law enforcement.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Office_of_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Research">Office of Oceanic and Atmospheric Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Office of Oceanic and Atmospheric Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Research" title="Office of Oceanic and Atmospheric Research">Office of Oceanic and Atmospheric Research</a></div>
<p>NOAA's research, conducted through the Office of Oceanic and Atmospheric Research (OAR), is the driving force behind NOAA environmental products and services that protect life and property and promote economic growth. Research, conducted in OAR laboratories and by extramural programs, focuses on enhancing our understanding of environmental phenomena such as tornadoes, hurricanes, climate variability, solar flares, changes in the ozone, <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_dispersion_modeling" title="Atmospheric dispersion modeling">air pollution transport and dispersion</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> El Niño/La Niña events, fisheries productivity, ocean currents, deep sea thermal vents, and coastal ecosystem health. NOAA research also develops innovative technologies and observing systems.
</p><p>The NOAA Research network consists of seven internal research laboratories, extramural research at 30 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_Grant_Colleges" title="Sea Grant Colleges">Sea Grant</a> university and research programs, six undersea research centers, a research grants program through the Climate Program Office, and 13 cooperative institutes with academia. Through NOAA and its academic partners, thousands of scientists, engineers, technicians, and graduate students participate in furthering our knowledge of natural phenomena that affect the lives of us all.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Air_Resources_Laboratory" title="Air Resources Laboratory">Air Resources Laboratory</a> (ARL) is one of the laboratories in the Office of Oceanic and Atmospheric Research. It studies processes and develops models relating to climate and air quality, including the transport, <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_dispersion_modeling" title="Atmospheric dispersion modeling">dispersion</a>, transformation and removal of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pollutants" title="Pollutants">pollutants</a> from the ambient atmosphere. The emphasis of the ARL's work is on data interpretation, technology development and transfer. The specific goal of ARL research is to improve and eventually to institutionalize prediction of trends, dispersion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_pollution_dispersion_terminology" title="Air pollution dispersion terminology">air pollutant plumes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_quality" title="Air quality">air quality</a>, atmospheric deposition, and related variables.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Self-published_sources" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on a self-published source. (December 2017)">self-published source</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Oceanographic_and_Meteorological_Laboratory" title="Atlantic Oceanographic and Meteorological Laboratory">Atlantic Oceanographic and Meteorological Laboratory</a> (AOML), is part of NOAA's Office of Oceanic and Atmospheric Research, located in <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>. AOML's research spans hurricanes, coastal ecosystems, oceans and human health, climate studies, global carbon systems, and ocean observations. AOML's organizational structure consists of an Office of the Director and three scientific research divisions (Physical Oceanography, Ocean Chemistry and Ecosystems, and Hurricane Research). The Office of the Director oversees the Laboratory's scientific programs, as well as its financial, administrative, computer, outreach/education, and facility management services. Research programs are augmented by the Cooperative Institute for Marine and Atmospheric Studies (CIMAS), a joint enterprise with the University of Miami's Rosenstiel School of Marine and Atmospheric Science. CIMAS enables AOML and university scientists to collaborate on research areas of mutual interest and facilitates the participation of students and visiting scientists. AOML is a member of a unique community of marine research and educational institutions located on Virginia Key in Miami, Florida.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In 1977 the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Marine_Environmental_Laboratory" title="Pacific Marine Environmental Laboratory">Pacific Marine Environmental Laboratory</a> (PMEL) deployed the first successful moored equatorial current meter – the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Atmosphere_Ocean_project" title="Tropical Atmosphere Ocean project">Tropical Atmosphere Ocean</a>, TAO, array. In 1984 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_Ocean-Global_Atmosphere_program" title="Tropical Ocean-Global Atmosphere program">Tropical Ocean-Global Atmosphere program</a> (TOGA) program began.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Office_of_Marine_and_Aviation_Operations">Office of Marine and Aviation Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Office of Marine and Aviation Operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Office of Marine and Aviation Operations is responsible for the fleet of NOAA ships, aircraft, and diving operations. It has the largest research fleet of the Federal government. Its personnel is made up of civilians and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA_Commissioned_Corps" title="NOAA Commissioned Corps">NOAA Commissioned Corps</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The office is headed by a NOAA Corps two-star <a href="/wiki/Rear_admiral_(United_States)#Rear_admiral" title="Rear admiral (United States)">rear admiral</a>, who also commands the Corps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Integrated_Drought_Information_System">National Integrated Drought Information System</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: National Integrated Drought Information System">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Integrated_Drought_Information_System" title="National Integrated Drought Information System">National Integrated Drought Information System</a> is a program within NOAA with an interagency mandate to coordinate and integrate drought research, building upon existing federal, tribal, state, and local partnerships in support of creating a national drought early warning information system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flag">Flag</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Flag">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NOAA_Flag.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="418" data-file-width="691" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/NOAA_Flag.svg/260px-NOAA_Flag.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/NOAA_Flag.svg/390px-NOAA_Flag.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/NOAA_Flag.svg/520px-NOAA_Flag.svg.png 2x" width="260"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NOAA_Flag.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration flag, flown as a distinguishing mark by all commissioned NOAA ships.</div></div></div>
<p>The NOAA flag is a modification of the flag of one of its predecessor organizations, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_and_Geodetic_Survey" title="United States Coast and Geodetic Survey">United States Coast and Geodetic Survey</a>. The Coast and Geodetic Survey's flag, authorized in 1899 and in use until 1970, was blue, with a white circle centered in it and a red triangle centered within the circle. It symbolized the use of <a href="/wiki/Triangulation" title="Triangulation">triangulation</a> in <a href="/wiki/Surveying" title="Surveying">surveying</a>, and was flown by ships of the Survey.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>When NOAA was established in 1970 and the Coast and Geodetic Survey's assets became a part of NOAA, NOAA based its own flag on that of the Coast and Geodetic Survey. The NOAA flag is in essence the Coast and Geodetic Survey flag, with the NOAA logo—a circle divided by the silhouette of a seabird into an upper dark blue and a lower light blue section, but with the "NOAA" legend omitted—centered within the red triangle. NOAA ships in <a href="/wiki/Ship_commissioning" title="Ship commissioning">commission</a> display the NOAA flag; those with only one <a href="/wiki/Mast_(sailing)" title="Mast (sailing)">mast</a> fly it immediately beneath the ship's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commissioning_pennant" title="Commissioning pennant">commissioning pennant</a> or the personal flag of a civilian official or <a href="/wiki/Flag_officer" title="Flag officer">flag officer</a> if one is aboard the ship, while multimasted vessels fly it at the masthead of the forwardmost mast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> NOAA ships fly the same <a href="/wiki/Ensign" title="Ensign">ensign</a> as <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> ships but fly the NOAA flag as a distinguishing mark to differentiate themselves from Navy ships.
</p>
