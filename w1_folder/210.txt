<p>In chemistry, a <b>carbonate</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salt</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a> (H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">[2]</a></sup> characterized by the presence of the <b>carbonate ion</b>, a <a href="/wiki/Polyatomic_ion" title="Polyatomic ion">polyatomic ion</a> with the formula of <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>. The name may also mean refer to a <a href="/wiki/Carbonate_ester" title="Carbonate ester">carbonate ester</a>, an organic compound containing the <b>carbonate <a href="/wiki/Functional_group" title="Functional group">group</a></b> C(=O)(O–)<sub>2</sub>.
</p><p>The term is also used as a verb, to describe <a href="/wiki/Carbonation" title="Carbonation">carbonation</a>: the process of raising the concentrations of carbonate and <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> ions in water to produce <a href="/wiki/Carbonated_water" title="Carbonated water">carbonated water</a> and other carbonated beverages – either by the addition of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> gas under pressure, or by dissolving carbonate or bicarbonate salts into the water.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mineralogy" title="Mineralogy">mineralogy</a>, the term "carbonate" can refer both to <a href="/wiki/Carbonate_minerals" title="Carbonate minerals">carbonate minerals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbonate_rock" title="Carbonate rock">carbonate rock</a> (which is made of chiefly carbonate minerals), and both are dominated by the carbonate ion, <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>. Carbonate minerals are extremely varied and ubiquitous in chemically precipitated <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rock</a>. The most common are <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a> or <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>, CaCO<sub>3</sub>, the chief constituent of <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> (as well as the main component of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mollusc" title="Mollusc">mollusc</a> shells and <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">coral</a> skeletons); <a href="/wiki/Dolomite" title="Dolomite">dolomite</a>, a calcium-magnesium carbonate CaMg(CO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>; and <a href="/wiki/Siderite" title="Siderite">siderite</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>(II) carbonate, FeCO<sub>3</sub>, an important <a href="/wiki/Iron_ore" title="Iron ore">iron ore</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">Sodium carbonate</a> ("soda" or "natron") and <a href="/wiki/Potassium_carbonate" title="Potassium carbonate">potassium carbonate</a> ("potash") have been used since antiquity for cleaning and preservation, as well as for the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>. Carbonates are widely used in industry, e.g. in iron smelting, as a raw material for <a href="/wiki/Portland_cement" title="Portland cement">Portland cement</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lime_(mineral)" title="Lime (mineral)">lime</a> manufacture, in the composition of <a href="/wiki/Ceramic_glaze" title="Ceramic glaze">ceramic glazes</a>, and more.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_bonding">Structure and bonding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure and bonding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The carbonate ion is the simplest <a href="/wiki/Oxocarbon_anion" title="Oxocarbon anion">oxocarbon anion</a>. It consists of one <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> atom surrounded by three <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> atoms, in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trigonal_planar" title="Trigonal planar">trigonal planar</a> arrangement, with <i>D</i><sub>3h</sub> <a href="/wiki/Molecular_symmetry#Point_Group" title="Molecular symmetry">molecular symmetry</a>. It has a molecular mass of 60.01 <a href="/wiki/Molar_mass" title="Molar mass">g/mol</a> and carries a total <a href="/wiki/Formal_charge" title="Formal charge">formal charge</a> of −2. It is the <a href="/wiki/Conjugate_acid" title="Conjugate acid">conjugate base</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">hydrogen carbonate (bicarbonate)</a> ion, <span class="chemf nowrap">HCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>, which is the conjugate base of <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>, <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Lewis_structure" title="Lewis structure">Lewis structure</a> of the carbonate ion has two (long) single bonds to negative oxygen atoms, and one short double bond to a neutral oxygen.
</p><p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carbonate-ion-localised-2D.png" title="Simple, localised Lewis structure of the carbonate ion"><img alt="Simple, localised Lewis structure of the carbonate ion" data-file-height="776" data-file-width="1100" height="71" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Carbonate-ion-localised-2D.png/100px-Carbonate-ion-localised-2D.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Carbonate-ion-localised-2D.png/150px-Carbonate-ion-localised-2D.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Carbonate-ion-localised-2D.png/200px-Carbonate-ion-localised-2D.png 2x" width="100"/></a>
</p><p>This structure is incompatible with the observed symmetry of the ion, which implies that the three bonds are equally long and that the three oxygen atoms are equivalent. As in the case of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isoelectronic" title="Isoelectronic">isoelectronic</a> <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrate</a> ion, the symmetry can be achieved by a <a href="/wiki/Resonance_(chemistry)" title="Resonance (chemistry)">resonance</a> between three structures:
</p><p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carbonate-ion-resonance-2D.png" title="Resonance structures of the carbonate ion"><img alt="Resonance structures of the carbonate ion" data-file-height="244" data-file-width="1100" height="89" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Carbonate-ion-resonance-2D.png/400px-Carbonate-ion-resonance-2D.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Carbonate-ion-resonance-2D.png/600px-Carbonate-ion-resonance-2D.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Carbonate-ion-resonance-2D.png/800px-Carbonate-ion-resonance-2D.png 2x" width="400"/></a>
</p><p>This resonance can be summarized by a model with fractional bonds and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delocalization" title="Delocalization">delocalized</a> charges:
</p><p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carbonate-ion-delocalised-partial-charges-2D.png" title="Delocalisation and partial charges on the carbonate ion"><img alt="Delocalisation and partial charges on the carbonate ion" data-file-height="733" data-file-width="1100" height="80" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Carbonate-ion-delocalised-partial-charges-2D.png/120px-Carbonate-ion-delocalised-partial-charges-2D.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Carbonate-ion-delocalised-partial-charges-2D.png/180px-Carbonate-ion-delocalised-partial-charges-2D.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Carbonate-ion-delocalised-partial-charges-2D.png/240px-Carbonate-ion-delocalised-partial-charges-2D.png 2x" width="120"/></a> <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carbonate-3D-vdW.png" title="Space-filling model of the carbonate ion"><img alt="Space-filling model of the carbonate ion" data-file-height="1030" data-file-width="1100" height="75" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Carbonate-3D-vdW.png/80px-Carbonate-3D-vdW.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Carbonate-3D-vdW.png/120px-Carbonate-3D-vdW.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Carbonate-3D-vdW.png/160px-Carbonate-3D-vdW.png 2x" width="80"/></a>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Metal carbonates generally decompose on heating, liberating carbon dioxide from the long term carbon cycle to the short term carbon cycle and leaving behind an oxide of the metal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-2">[2]</a></sup> This process is called <a href="/wiki/Calcination" title="Calcination">calcination</a>, after <i>calx</i>, the Latin name of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quicklime" title="Quicklime">quicklime</a> or <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">calcium oxide</a>, CaO, which is obtained by roasting limestone in a <a href="/wiki/Lime_kiln" title="Lime kiln">lime kiln</a>.
</p><p>A carbonate salt forms when a positively charged ion, <span class="chemf nowrap">M<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>, <span class="chemf nowrap">M<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>, or <span class="chemf nowrap">M<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span>, associates with the negatively charged oxygen atoms of the ion by forming <a href="/wiki/Electrostatics" title="Electrostatics">electrostatic attractions</a> with them, forming an ionic <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">compound</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 <span class="chemf nowrap">M<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> → <span class="chemf nowrap">M<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><span class="chemf nowrap">M<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> + <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> → <span class="chemf nowrap">MCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span></dd></dl>
<dl><dd>2 <span class="chemf nowrap">M<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> + 3 <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> → <span class="chemf nowrap">M<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>(CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span></dd></dl>
<p>Most carbonate salts are <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">insoluble</a> in <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard temperature and pressure">standard temperature and pressure</a>, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solubility_constant" title="Solubility constant">solubility constants</a> of less than 1 × 10<sup>−8</sup>. Exceptions include <a href="/wiki/Lithium_carbonate" title="Lithium carbonate">lithium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">sodium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium_carbonate" title="Potassium carbonate">potassium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_carbonate" title="Ammonium carbonate">ammonium</a> carbonates, as well as many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uranium_carbonate" title="Uranium carbonate">uranium carbonates</a>.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Aqueous_solution" title="Aqueous solution">aqueous solution</a>, carbonate, bicarbonate, carbon dioxide, and carbonic acid exist together in a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_equilibrium" title="Chemical equilibrium">dynamic equilibrium</a>. In strongly basic conditions, the carbonate ion predominates, while in weakly basic conditions, the <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> ion is prevalent. In more acid conditions, aqueous <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, CO<sub>2</sub>(aq), is the main form, which, with water, H<sub>2</sub>O, is in equilibrium with carbonic acid – the equilibrium lies strongly towards carbon dioxide. Thus <a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">sodium carbonate</a> is basic, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_bicarbonate" title="Sodium bicarbonate">sodium bicarbonate</a> is weakly basic, while carbon dioxide itself is a weak acid.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Carbonated_water" title="Carbonated water">Carbonated water</a> is formed by dissolving CO<sub>2</sub> in water under pressure. When the partial pressure of CO<sub>2</sub> is reduced, for example when a can of soda is opened, the equilibrium for each of the forms of carbonate (carbonate, bicarbonate, carbon dioxide, and carbonic acid) shifts until the concentration of CO<sub>2</sub> in the solution is equal to the solubility of CO<sub>2</sub> at that temperature and pressure. In living systems an enzyme, <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_anhydrase" title="Carbonic anhydrase">carbonic anhydrase</a>, speeds the interconversion of CO<sub>2</sub> and carbonic acid.
</p><p>Although the carbonate salts of most metals are insoluble in water, the same is not true of the bicarbonate salts. In solution this equilibrium between carbonate, bicarbonate, carbon dioxide and carbonic acid changes consonant to changing temperature and pressure conditions. In the case of metal ions with insoluble carbonates, e.g. CaCO<sub>3</sub>, formation of insoluble compounds results. This is an explanation for the buildup of scale inside pipes caused by <a href="/wiki/Hard_water" title="Hard water">hard water</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbonate_in_the_Inorganic_nomenclature">Carbonate in the Inorganic nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Carbonate in the Inorganic nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Systematic additive IUPAC name for carbonate anion is trioxidocarbonate(2−).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-redbook_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-redbook-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>127</span></sup> Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Cyanide" title="Cyanide">cyanide</a> anion CN<sup>−</sup> is named nitridocarbonate(1−).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-redbook_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-redbook-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>291</span></sup> Following this logic, e.g. carbonate(4−) in the systematic additive nomenclature would mean <a href="/wiki/Carbide" title="Carbide">carbide</a> anion, but it is not the case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-redbook_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-redbook-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>287</span></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organic_carbonates">Organic carbonates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Organic carbonates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Carbonate_ester" title="Carbonate ester">Carbonate ester</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a> a carbonate can also refer to a <a href="/wiki/Functional_group" title="Functional group">functional group</a> within a larger molecule that contains a carbon atom bound to three oxygen atoms, one of which is double bonded. These compounds are also known as organocarbonates or carbonate esters, and have the general formula ROCOOR′, or RR′CO<sub>3</sub>. Important organocarbonates include <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_carbonate" title="Dimethyl carbonate">dimethyl carbonate</a>, the cyclic compounds <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_carbonate" title="Ethylene carbonate">ethylene carbonate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Propylene_carbonate" title="Propylene carbonate">propylene carbonate</a>, and the phosgene replacement, <a href="/wiki/Triphosgene" title="Triphosgene">triphosgene</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_significance">Biological significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Biological significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It works as a <a href="/wiki/Buffer_solution" title="Buffer solution">buffer</a> in the blood as follows: when pH is low, the concentration of hydrogen ions is too high, so one exhales CO<sub>2</sub>. This will cause the equation to shift left,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag needs further explanation. (January 2018)">further explanation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> essentially decreasing the concentration of H<sup>+</sup> ions, causing a more basic pH.
</p><p>When pH is too high, the concentration of hydrogen ions in the blood is too low, so the kidneys excrete bicarbonate (<span class="chemf nowrap">HCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>). This causes the equation to shift right, essentially increasing the concentration of hydrogen ions, causing a more acidic pH.
</p><p>Three important reversible reactions control the above pH balance:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>(aq) ⇌ H<sup>+</sup>(aq) +  <span class="chemf nowrap">HCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>(aq)</li></ol>
<ol><li>H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>(aq) ⇌ CO<sub>2</sub>(aq)  + H<sub>2</sub>O(l)</li></ol>
<ol><li>CO<sub>2</sub>(aq) ⇌ CO<sub>2</sub>(g)</li></ol>
<p>Exhaled CO<sub>2</sub>(g) depletes CO<sub>2</sub>(aq), which in turn consumes H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>, causing the aforementioned shift left in the first reaction by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Le_Ch%C3%A2telier%27s_principle" title="Le Châtelier's principle">Le Châtelier's principle</a>. By the same principle, when the pH is too high, the kidneys excrete bicarbonate (<span class="chemf nowrap">HCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>) into urine as urea via the <a href="/wiki/Urea_cycle" title="Urea cycle">urea cycle</a> (or Krebs–Henseleit ornithine cycle). By removing the bicarbonate, more H<sup>+</sup> is generated from carbonic acid (H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>), which comes from CO<sub>2</sub>(g) produced by <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">cellular respiration</a>.
</p><p>Crucially, this same buffer operates in the oceans. It is a major factor in climate change and the long-term carbon cycle, due to the large number of marine organisms (especially coral) which are formed of calcium carbonate. Increased solubility of carbonate through increased temperatures results in lower production of marine <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a> and increased concentration of atmospheric carbon dioxide. This, in turn, increases Earth temperature and is a part of the carbon cycle largely ignored by the global news media. The tonnage of <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> is on a geological scale and may all be redissolved into the sea and released to the atmosphere, increasing CO<sub>2</sub> levels even more.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Presence_outside_Earth">Presence outside Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Presence outside Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is generally thought that the presence of carbonates in <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rock</a> is strong evidence for the presence of liquid water. Recent observations of the <a href="/wiki/Planetary_nebula" title="Planetary nebula">planetary nebula</a> <a href="/wiki/NGC_6302" title="NGC 6302">NGC 6302</a> shows evidence for carbonates in space,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> where aqueous alteration similar to that on Earth is unlikely. Other minerals have been proposed which would fit the observations.
</p><p>Until recently <a href="/wiki/Carbonates_on_Mars" title="Carbonates on Mars">carbonate deposits</a> have not been found on Mars via remote sensing or in situ missions, even though Martian meteorites contain small amounts. Groundwater may have existed at both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gusev_crater" title="Gusev crater">Gusev</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Meridiani_Planum" title="Meridiani Planum">Meridiani Planum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
