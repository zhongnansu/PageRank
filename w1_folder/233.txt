<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Phototroph</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about phototrophism, obtaining energy from light. For the <a href="/wiki/Tropism" title="Tropism">tropism</a> that governs growth toward or away from a light source, see <a href="/wiki/Phototropism" title="Phototropism">Phototropism</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dead_tree_river.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Dead_tree_river.jpg/220px-Dead_tree_river.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Dead_tree_river.jpg/330px-Dead_tree_river.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Dead_tree_river.jpg/440px-Dead_tree_river.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dead_tree_river.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Terrestrial and aquatic phototrophs: plants grow on a fallen log floating in algae-rich water</div></div></div>
<p><b>Phototrophs</b> (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" title="Ancient Greek language">Gr</a></i>: φῶς, φωτός = light, τροϕή = nourishment) are the <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a> that carry out photon capture to acquire energy.  They use the <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> from <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> to carry out various cellular metabolic processes.  It is a common misconception that phototrophs are obligatorily <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photosynthetic" title="Photosynthetic">photosynthetic</a>.  Many, but not all, phototrophs often photosynthesize: they <a href="/wiki/Anabolism" title="Anabolism">anabolically</a> convert <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> into organic material to be utilized structurally, functionally, or as a source for later <a href="/wiki/Catabolism" title="Catabolism">catabolic</a> processes (e.g. in the form of starches, sugars and fats).  All phototrophs either use <a href="/wiki/Electron_transport_chain" title="Electron transport chain">electron transport chains</a> or direct <a href="/wiki/Proton_pump" title="Proton pump">proton pumping</a> to establish an electro-chemical gradient which is utilized by <a href="/wiki/ATP_synthase" title="ATP synthase">ATP synthase</a>, to provide the molecular energy currency for the cell. 
Phototrophs can be either <a href="/wiki/Autotroph" title="Autotroph">autotrophs</a> or <a href="/wiki/Heterotroph" title="Heterotroph">heterotrophs</a>. As their electron and hydrogen donors are inorganic compounds [Na2S2O3 (PSB) and H2S (GSB)] they can be also called as lithotrophs, and so, some photoautotrophs are also called photolithoautotrophs. Examples of phototroph organisms:  <i><a href="/wiki/Rhodobacter_capsulatus" title="Rhodobacter capsulatus">Rhodobacter capsulatus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Chromatium" title="Chromatium">Chromatium</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Chlorobium" title="Chlorobium">Chlorobium</a></i> etc.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phototroph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Originally used with a different meaning, the term took its current definition after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Lwoff" title="André Lwoff">Lwoff</a> and collaborators (1946).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Photoautotroph">Photoautotroph</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phototroph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Photoautotroph">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most of the well-recognized phototrophs are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autotrophic" title="Autotrophic">autotrophic</a>, also known as <b>photoautotrophs</b>, and can <a href="/wiki/Carbon_fixation" title="Carbon fixation">fix carbon</a>. They can be contrasted with <a href="/wiki/Chemotroph" title="Chemotroph">chemotrophs</a> that obtain their energy by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Electron_donor" title="Electron donor">electron donors</a> in their environments. Photoautotrophs are capable of synthesizing their own food from inorganic substances using light as an energy source. Green plants and photosynthetic bacteria are photoautotrophs. Photoautotrophic organisms are sometimes referred to as <b>holophytic</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Such organisms derive their energy for food synthesis from light and are capable of using carbon dioxide as their principal source of carbon.
</p><p>Oxygenic photosynthetic organisms use <a href="/wiki/Chlorophyll" title="Chlorophyll">chlorophyll</a> for light-energy capture and oxidize water, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen-evolving_complex" title="Oxygen-evolving complex">"splitting"</a> it into molecular oxygen.  In contrast, anoxygenic photosynthetic bacteria have a substance called <a href="/wiki/Bacteriochlorophyll" title="Bacteriochlorophyll">bacteriochlorophyll</a> - which absorbs predominantly at non-optical wavelengths - for light-energy capture, live in aquatic environments, and will, using light, oxidize chemical substances such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a> rather than water.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phototroph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In an <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecological</a> context, phototrophs are often the food source for neighboring heterotrophic life. In terrestrial environments, <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> are the predominant variety, while aquatic environments include a range of phototrophic organisms such as <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Kelp" title="Kelp">kelp</a>), other <a href="/wiki/Protist" title="Protist">protists</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Euglena" title="Euglena">euglena</a>), <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">cyanobacteria</a>). The depth to which sunlight or artificial light can penetrate into water, so that photosynthesis may occur, is known as the <a href="/wiki/Photic_zone" title="Photic zone">photic zone</a>.
</p><p>Cyanobacteria, which are prokaryotic organisms which carry out oxygenic photosynthesis, occupy many environmental conditions, including fresh water, seas, <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lichen" title="Lichen">lichen</a>. Cyanobacteria carry out plant-like photosynthesis because the <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">organelle</a> in plants that carries out photosynthesis is derived from an <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> endosymbiotic cyanobacterium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This bacterium can use water as a source of <a href="/wiki/Electron_transport_chain" title="Electron transport chain">electrons</a> in order to perform CO<sub>2</sub> <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduction</a> reactions. Evolutionarily, cyanobacteria's ability to survive in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxygenic_photosynthesis" title="Oxygenic photosynthesis">oxygenic</a> conditions, which are considered toxic to most <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_organism" title="Anaerobic organism">anaerobic</a> bacteria, might have given the bacteria an adaptive advantage which could have allowed the cyanobacteria to populate more efficiently.
</p><p>A <i>photolithoautotroph</i> is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autotrophic" title="Autotrophic">autotrophic</a> organism that uses light energy, and an <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic</a> <a href="/wiki/Electron_donor" title="Electron donor">electron donor</a> (e.g., H<sub>2</sub>O, H<sub>2</sub>, H<sub>2</sub>S), and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> as its <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> source. Examples include plants.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Photoheterotroph">Photoheterotroph</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phototroph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Photoheterotroph">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Photoheterotroph" title="Photoheterotroph">Photoheterotroph</a></div>
<p>In contrast to photoautotrophs, <b>photoheterotrophs</b> are organisms that depend solely on light for their energy and principally on organic compounds for their carbon. Photoheterotrophs produce <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a> through <a href="/wiki/Photophosphorylation" title="Photophosphorylation">photophosphorylation</a> but use environmentally obtained <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a> to build structures and other bio-molecules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-campbell564_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-campbell564-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
