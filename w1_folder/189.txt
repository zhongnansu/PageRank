<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Limewater</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about the saturated solution of Ca(OH)<sub>2</sub><i><sub>(aq)</sub></i>. For the juice of a lime, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lime_juice" title="Lime juice">lime juice</a>. For lime-flavoured or lime-infused water, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flavoured_water" title="Flavoured water">flavoured water</a>. For the limewash wall colourant, see <a href="/wiki/Whitewash" title="Whitewash">whitewash</a>. For Ca(OH)<sub>2</sub>, see <a href="/wiki/Calcium_hydroxide" title="Calcium hydroxide">calcium hydroxide</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Limewater.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Limewater.jpg/220px-Limewater.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Limewater.jpg/330px-Limewater.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Limewater.jpg/440px-Limewater.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Limewater.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Limewater</div></div></div>
<p><b>Limewater</b> is the common name for a diluted solution of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_hydroxide" title="Calcium hydroxide">calcium hydroxide</a>. Calcium hydroxide, Ca(OH)<sub>2</sub>, is sparsely soluble in water (1.5 g/L at 25 °C<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>). Pure limewater is clear and colorless, with a slight earthy smell and an alkaline bitter taste of calcium hydroxide. The term lime refers to the alkaline <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lime_(mineral)" title="Lime (mineral)">mineral</a>, and is unrelated to the acidic <a href="/wiki/Lime_(fruit)" title="Lime (fruit)">fruit</a>.
</p><p>Limewater is prepared by stirring calcium hydroxide in pure water and filtering off the excess undissolved Ca(OH)<sub>2</sub>. When excess calcium hydroxide is added to limewater, a suspension of calcium hydroxide particles results, giving it a milky aspect, in which case it has the common name of <b>milk of lime</b>. Milk of lime or a saturated solution of lime (limewater) has a <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> of 12.4. It is basic in nature.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limewater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:142px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HCl_%2B_Ca(CO3)2_expt.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="965" data-file-width="607" height="223" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/HCl_%2B_Ca%28CO3%292_expt.png/140px-HCl_%2B_Ca%28CO3%292_expt.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/HCl_%2B_Ca%28CO3%292_expt.png/210px-HCl_%2B_Ca%28CO3%292_expt.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/HCl_%2B_Ca%28CO3%292_expt.png/280px-HCl_%2B_Ca%28CO3%292_expt.png 2x" width="140"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HCl_%2B_Ca(CO3)2_expt.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The experiment of hydrochloric acid and calcium carbonate in the left test tube.</div></div></div>
<p>Carbon dioxide passed into limewater gives a milky solution, due to <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">precipitation</a> of an insoluble <a href="/wiki/Suspension_(chemistry)" title="Suspension (chemistry)">suspension</a> of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>Ca(OH)<sub>2</sub><i>(aq)</i> + CO<sub>2</sub><i>(g)</i> → CaCO<sub>3</sub><i>(s)</i> + H<sub>2</sub>O<i>(l)</i></dd></dl>
<p>If excess CO<sub>2</sub> is added, the following reaction takes place:
</p>
<dl><dd>CaCO<sub>3</sub><i>(s)</i> + H<sub>2</sub>O<i>(l)</i> + CO<sub>2</sub><i>(g)</i> → <a href="/wiki/Calcium_bicarbonate" title="Calcium bicarbonate">Ca(HCO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub></a><i>(aq)</i></dd></dl>
<p>The milkiness disappears since calcium bicarbonate is water-soluble.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limewater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The above <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_properties" title="Chemical properties">chemical properties</a> are commonly used for testing the presence of carbon dioxide in gaseous samples in school laboratories, and refining of <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a> in a process called <a href="/wiki/Carbonatation" title="Carbonatation">carbonatation</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industry">Industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limewater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Waste gases from industries containing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulphur_dioxide" title="Sulphur dioxide">sulphur dioxide</a> can be cleaned by bubbling through limewater, a process called <a href="/wiki/Sulfation" title="Sulfation">sulfation</a>, in which the toxic sulphur dioxide is trapped as a precipitate:
</p>
<dl><dd>Ca(OH)<sub>2</sub><i>(aq)</i> + SO<sub>2</sub><i>(g)</i> → CaSO<sub>3</sub><i>(s)</i> + H<sub>2</sub>O<i>(l)</i></dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_treatment">Water treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limewater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Water treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Limewater is used in a process known as <a href="/wiki/Lime_softening" title="Lime softening">lime softening</a> to reduce water hardness.  It is also used as a neutralizing agent in municipal waste water treatment.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arts">Arts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limewater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Arts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In buon <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">fresco</a> painting, limewater is used as the colour solvent to apply on fresh plaster. Historically, it is known as the paint <a href="/wiki/Whitewash" title="Whitewash">whitewash</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dying_Gaul_(casting_in_Pushkin_museum)_04_by_shakko.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3072" data-file-width="2304" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Dying_Gaul_%28casting_in_Pushkin_museum%29_04_by_shakko.jpg/220px-Dying_Gaul_%28casting_in_Pushkin_museum%29_04_by_shakko.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Dying_Gaul_%28casting_in_Pushkin_museum%29_04_by_shakko.jpg/330px-Dying_Gaul_%28casting_in_Pushkin_museum%29_04_by_shakko.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Dying_Gaul_%28casting_in_Pushkin_museum%29_04_by_shakko.jpg/440px-Dying_Gaul_%28casting_in_Pushkin_museum%29_04_by_shakko.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dying_Gaul_(casting_in_Pushkin_museum)_04_by_shakko.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Closeup of cast of <i>The Dying Gaul</i>, showing distinctive hairstyle, supposedly derived from washing in limewater.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_care_and_adornment">Personal care and adornment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limewater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Personal care and adornment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Treating one's hair with limewater causes it to stiffen and bleach, with the added benefit of killing any lice or mites living there. According to <a href="/wiki/Diodorus_Siculus" title="Diodorus Siculus">Diodorus Siculus</a>, he described the <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celtic</a> people as such;
"Their aspect is terrifying... They are very tall in stature, with rippling muscles under clear white skin. Their hair is blond, but not naturally so: they bleach it, to this day, artificially, washing it in lime and combing it back from their foreheads. They look like wood-demons, their hair thick and shaggy like a horse's mane. Some of them are clean-shaven, but others - especially those of high rank, shave their cheeks but leave a moustache that covers the whole mouth...".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_preparation">Food preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limewater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Food preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Limewater is used in the preparation of maize for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corn_tortillas" title="Corn tortillas">corn tortillas</a> and other culinary purposes using a process known as <a href="/wiki/Nixtamalization" title="Nixtamalization">nixtamalization</a>. This unlocks proteins in the cornmeal, making it more nutritious for the consumer. Eating maize-based foods without nixtamalization or <a href="/wiki/Complete_protein" title="Complete protein">supplementation with legumes</a> or meat leads to a nutritional deficiency known as <a href="/wiki/Pellagra" title="Pellagra">pellagra</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limewater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Limewater is widely used by marine aquarists as a primary supplement of <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alkalinity" title="Alkalinity">alkalinity</a> for reef aquariums. <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">Corals</a> of order <a href="/wiki/Scleractinia" title="Scleractinia">Scleractinia</a> build their <a href="/wiki/Endoskeleton" title="Endoskeleton">endoskeletons</a> from <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">aragonite</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorph</a> of calcium carbonate). When used for this purpose, limewater is usually referred to as <i>Kalkwasser</i>. It is also used in tanning and making <a href="/wiki/Parchment" title="Parchment">parchment</a>. The lime is used as a dehairing agent based on its alkaline properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
