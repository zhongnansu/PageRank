<p>The <b>Thomson Corporation</b> was one of the world's largest information companies. It was established in 1989 following a merger between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Thomson_Organisation" title="International Thomson Organisation">International Thomson Organisation</a> Ltd (ITOL) and Thomson Newspapers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_of_The_Thomson_Corporation_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-History_of_The_Thomson_Corporation-1">[1]</a></sup> In 2008, it purchased <a href="/wiki/Reuters_Group" title="Reuters Group">Reuters Group</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Thomson_Reuters" title="Thomson Reuters">Thomson Reuters</a>. The Thomson Corporation was active in financial services, healthcare sectors, law, science and technology research and tax and accounting sectors. The company operated through five segments (2007 onwards): <a href="/wiki/Thomson_Financial" title="Thomson Financial">Thomson Financial</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomson_Healthcare" title="Thomson Healthcare">Thomson Healthcare</a>, <b>Thomson Legal</b>, <a href="/wiki/Thomson_Scientific" title="Thomson Scientific">Thomson Scientific</a> and <b>Thomson Tax &amp; Accounting</b>.
</p><p>Until 2007, Thomson was also a major worldwide provider of higher education textbooks, academic information solutions and reference materials. On 26 October 2006, Thomson announced the proposed sale of its <b>Thomson Learning</b> assets. In May 2007, Thomson Learning was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Apax_Partners" title="Apax Partners">Apax Partners</a> and subsequently renamed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cengage_Learning" title="Cengage Learning">Cengage Learning</a> in July. The Thomson Learning brand was used to the end of August 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Subsequently, on 15 October 2007, <a href="/wiki/Educational_Testing_Service" title="Educational Testing Service">Educational Testing Service</a> (ETS) finalized acquisition of Thomson's <a href="/wiki/Prometric" title="Prometric">Prometric</a>. Thomson sold its global network of testing centres in 135 countries, for a reported $435 million. Prometric now operates as a wholly owned subsidiary of ETS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 15 May 2007, the Thomson Corporation reached an agreement with Reuters to combine the two companies, a deal valued at $17.2 billion. On 17 April 2008 the new company was created under the name of Thomson Reuters. The chief executive officer of Thomson Reuters is Jim Smith, and the <a href="/wiki/Chairman" title="Chairman">chairman</a> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Kenneth_Roy_Thomson,_3rd_Baron_Thomson_of_Fleet" title="David Kenneth Roy Thomson, 3rd Baron Thomson of Fleet">David Thomson</a>, formerly of the Thomson Corporation. Although it was officially a Canadian company and remained Canadian owned, Thomson was run from its operational headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Stamford,_Connecticut" title="Stamford, Connecticut">Stamford, Connecticut</a>, in the United States.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomson_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Thomson had grown from a single <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> newspaper, the <i><a href="/wiki/Timmins_Daily_Press" title="Timmins Daily Press">Timmins Daily Press</a></i>, acquired in 1934 by <a href="/wiki/Roy_Thomson,_1st_Baron_Thomson_of_Fleet" title="Roy Thomson, 1st Baron Thomson of Fleet">Roy Thomson, 1st Baron Thomson of Fleet</a>, into a global media concern. The Baron acquired his first non-Canadian newspaper the  <i><a href="/wiki/Evening_Independent" title="Evening Independent">Independent</a></i> of St. Petersburg, Florida in 1952 and the next year it expanded to the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. It once owned several prominent newspapers in the UK, including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Scotsman" title="The Scotsman">The Scotsman</a></i>, and it owned <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Television" title="Scottish Television">Scottish Television</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1960s, Thomson's publishing realm expanded further to include Thomson Publication (UK), a consumer magazine and book publishing house, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i>. In 1965, Thomson Newspapers, Ltd. was formed as a publicly traded company in Canada. Roy Thomson's prolific endeavors in publishing had earned him a hereditary title, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_Thomson_of_Fleet" title="Lord Thomson of Fleet">Lord Thomson of Fleet</a>. Yet, Thomson's interests moved beyond publishing with the creation of Thomson Travel and acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Britannia_Airways" title="Britannia Airways">Britannia Airways</a> in 1965 and 1971,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and a foray into a consortium exploring the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> for oil and gas. Thomson used its oil profits to buy small newspapers in the United States, starting with the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Brush-Moore_Newspapers" title="Brush-Moore Newspapers">Brush-Moore Newspapers</a> in 1967 for $72 million, at the time the largest sale of newspapers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time1_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-time1-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>By the end of the 1970s, Thomson Newspapers' circulation in the United States had surpassed the 1 million mark. The merger of Thomson Newspapers and the <a href="/wiki/International_Thomson_Organization" title="International Thomson Organization">International Thomson Organization</a> in 1989 created the Thomson Corporation.
</p><p>Over the years, the company has withdrawn from its holdings in the oil and gas business, the travel industry and department stores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iantorch_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-iantorch-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>When <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Thomson,_2nd_Baron_Thomson_of_Fleet" title="Kenneth Thomson, 2nd Baron Thomson of Fleet">Kenneth Thomson</a> took over from his father Roy in 1976, the company was worth about $500 million. At Kenneth's death in June 2006, the company was valued at about $29.3 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iantorch_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-iantorch-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transition_to_business_information">Transition to business information</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomson_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Transition to business information">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1978, the acquisition of Wadsworth Publishing provided Thomson with its first entry into specialised information, college textbooks and professional books.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iantorch_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-iantorch-8">[8]</a></sup> (In 2007, Thomson Learning, including the Wadsworth imprint, was sold and renamed as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cengage_Learning" title="Cengage Learning">Cengage Learning</a>.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Starting in the mid-1990s, Thomson invested further in specialised information services (but this time providing them in digital format) and began selling off its newspapers. That was about the time Richard J. Harrington, an accountant, became chief executive officer of the company. One of the first moves came when Thomson spent $3.4 billion to acquire the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_Publishing_Company" title="West Publishing Company">West Publishing Company</a>, a legal information provider in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eagan,_MN" title="Eagan, MN">Eagan, MN</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iantorch_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-iantorch-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>In recent years, Thomson provided much of the specialised information content the world's financial, legal, research and medical organizations rely on every day to make business-critical decisions and drive innovation. While it remained a <a href="/wiki/Publishing" title="Publishing">publishing</a> company, early and aggressive investment in electronic delivery had become a key company goal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iantorch_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-iantorch-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>"Except for its educational division, which still publishes a substantial number of conventional textbooks, Thomson had the good fortune to move into these businesses as customers were demanding electronic delivery of their information," according to a 3 July 2006 article in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a>. "In some markets, Thomson was able to move past other players who were more cautious about digital conversion."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iantorch_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-iantorch-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Brands">Brands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomson_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Brands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some of Thomson's brands are better known than the company name itself. Its brands include Thomson ONE, <a href="/wiki/Westlaw" title="Westlaw">Westlaw</a>, <a href="/wiki/FindLaw" title="FindLaw">FindLaw</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BarBri" </p><p>In October 2006, the company confirmed it would sell the Thomson Learning market group in three parts. The first part, corporate education and training (NETg), has agreed to be sold to <a href="/wiki/Skillsoft" title="Skillsoft">Skillsoft</a> for $285 million. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apax" title="Apax">Apax</a> announced its acquisition of Thomson's higher education business on 11 May 2007, for $7.5 billion in cash assets.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. </p><p>Thomson had divested many of its traditional media assets – or combined them with digital products – and had moved toward a larger reliance on information technology services and products.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Restatements">Restatements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomson_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Restatements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 1 January 2004, Thomson adopted a new accounting standard, which required restatement of all prior periods. The company restated its financial reports accordingly.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_governance">Corporate governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomson_Corporation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Corporate governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Members of the last <a href="/wiki/Board_of_directors" title="Board of directors">board of directors</a> of Thomson were as follows: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_K.R._Thomson" title="David K.R. Thomson">David K.R. Thomson</a> (chairman of the board since 2002), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=W._Geoffrey_Beattie&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="W. Geoffrey Beattie (page does not exist)">W. Geoffrey Beattie</a>, Richard Harrington, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ron_D._Barbaro&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ron D. Barbaro (page does not exist)">Ron D. Barbaro</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mary_Cirillo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mary Cirillo (page does not exist)">Mary Cirillo</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Daleo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Robert Daleo (page does not exist)">Robert Daleo</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Steven_Denning&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Steven Denning (page does not exist)">Steven Denning</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Maureen_Darkes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Maureen Darkes (page does not exist)">Maureen Darkes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Martin_(professor)" title="Roger Martin (professor)">Roger Martin</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Vance_Opperman&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Vance Opperman (page does not exist)">Vance Opperman</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Thompson_(TD_banker)" title="John Thompson (TD banker)">John M. Thompson</a>, Peter Thomson, <a href="/wiki/Richard_M._Thomson" title="Richard M. Thomson">Richard Thomson</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_A._Tory" title="John A. Tory">John A. Tory</a>.
</p><p>The Thomson family owned 70% of the company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iantorch_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-iantorch-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>When <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Thomson,_2nd_Baron_Thomson_of_Fleet" title="Kenneth Thomson, 2nd Baron Thomson of Fleet">Kenneth Thomson</a> died in June 2006, control of the family fortune passed on to <a href="/wiki/David_Thomson,_3rd_Baron_Thomson_of_Fleet" title="David Thomson, 3rd Baron Thomson of Fleet">David K.R. Thomson</a> under a plan put together decades earlier by company founder <a href="/wiki/Roy_Thomson,_1st_Baron_Thomson_of_Fleet" title="Roy Thomson, 1st Baron Thomson of Fleet">Roy Thomson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iantorch_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-iantorch-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>"David, my grandson, will have to take his part in the running of the organisation and David's son, too," <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roy_Herbert_Thomson" title="Roy Herbert Thomson">Roy</a> wrote in his 1975 autobiography. "With the fortune that we will leave to them go also responsibilities. These Thomson boys that come after Ken are not going to be able, even if they want to, to shrug off these responsibilities."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iantorch_8-8"><a href="#cite_note-iantorch-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Thomson family controlled the Thomson Corporation through a family-owned entity, <a href="/wiki/The_Woodbridge_Company" title="The Woodbridge Company">the Woodbridge Company</a>, based in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>. (Along with 70% of Thomson Corporation, Woodbridge also owns a 40% stake in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CTVglobemedia" title="CTVglobemedia">CTVglobemedia</a>, which now owns the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Globe_and_Mail" title="The Globe and Mail">Globe and Mail</a></i> daily newspaper in Toronto and <a href="/wiki/CTV_Television_Network" title="CTV Television Network">CTV</a>, Canada's largest commercial TV network.) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_K.R._Thomson" title="David K.R. Thomson">David K.R. Thomson</a> and his brother, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Thomson_(businessman)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Peter Thomson (businessman) (page does not exist)">Peter Thomson</a>, became co-chairmen of Woodbridge after their father's death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iantorch_8-9"><a href="#cite_note-iantorch-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
