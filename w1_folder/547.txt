<p>According to the classical definition, a <b>superacid</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acid</a> with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acidity" title="Acidity">acidity</a> greater than that of 100% pure <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conant_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Conant-1">[1]</a></sup> which has a <a href="/wiki/Hammett_acidity_function" title="Hammett acidity function">Hammett acidity function</a> (<i>H</i><sub>0</sub>) of −12. According to the modern definition, a superacid is a medium in which the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_potential" title="Chemical potential">chemical potential</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a> is higher than in pure <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krossing_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Krossing-2">[2]</a></sup> Commercially available superacids include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trifluoromethanesulfonic_acid" title="Trifluoromethanesulfonic acid">trifluoromethanesulfonic acid</a> (CF<sub>3</sub>SO<sub>3</sub>H), also known as triflic acid, and <a href="/wiki/Fluorosulfuric_acid" title="Fluorosulfuric acid">fluorosulfuric acid</a> (HSO<sub>3</sub>F), both of which are about a thousand times stronger (i.e. have more negative <i>H</i><sub>0</sub> values) than sulfuric acid.  Most strong superacids are prepared by the combination of a strong <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_acid" title="Lewis acid">Lewis acid</a> and a strong <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Br%C3%B8nsted_acid" title="Brønsted acid">Brønsted acid</a>. The strongest superacid of this kind is <a href="/wiki/Fluoroantimonic_acid" title="Fluoroantimonic acid">fluoroantimonic acid</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superacid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>superacid</i> was originally coined by <a href="/wiki/James_Bryant_Conant" title="James Bryant Conant">James Bryant Conant</a> in 1927 to describe acids that were stronger than conventional <a href="/wiki/Mineral_acid" title="Mineral acid">mineral acids</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conant_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Conant-1">[1]</a></sup> This definition was refined by <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Gillespie" title="Ronald Gillespie">Ronald Gillespie</a> in 1971, as any acid with an <i>H</i><sub>0</sub> value lower than that of 100% sulfuric acid (−11.93, or roughly speaking,  −12).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_A._Olah" title="George A. Olah">George A. Olah</a> prepared the so-called <a href="/wiki/Magic_acid" title="Magic acid">magic acid</a>, so-named for its ability to attack <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrocarbons" title="Hydrocarbons">hydrocarbons</a>, by mixing <a href="/wiki/Antimony_pentafluoride" title="Antimony pentafluoride">antimony pentafluoride</a> (SbF<sub>5</sub>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluorosulfonic_acid" title="Fluorosulfonic acid">fluorosulfonic acid</a> (FSO<sub>3</sub>H).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Olah1968_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Olah1968-4">[4]</a></sup> The name was coined after a candle was placed in a sample of magic acid after a Christmas party. The candle dissolved, showing the ability of the acid to protonate <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">alkanes</a>, which under normal acidic conditions do not protonate to any extent.
</p><p>At 140 °C (284 °F), FSO<sub>3</sub>H–SbF<sub>5</sub> protonates <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> to give the tertiary-butyl <a href="/wiki/Carbocation" title="Carbocation">carbocation</a>, a reaction that begins with the <a href="/wiki/Protonation" title="Protonation">protonation</a> of methane:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Olah1968_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Olah1968-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>CH<sub>4</sub>  +  H<sup>+</sup>  →  <span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">5</sub></span></span></dd>
<dd><span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">5</sub></span></span>  →  <span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>  +  H<sub>2</sub></dd>
<dd><span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>  +  3 CH<sub>4</sub>  →  (CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>C<sup>+</sup>  +  3H<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Common uses of superacids include providing an environment to create, maintain, and characterize <a href="/wiki/Carbocation" title="Carbocation">carbocations</a>. Carbocations are intermediates in numerous useful reactions such as those forming plastics and in the production of <a href="/wiki/Octane_rating" title="Octane rating">high-octane</a> <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature_and_mechanism">Nomenclature and mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superacid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nomenclature and mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fluoroantimonic_acid" title="Fluoroantimonic acid">Fluoroantimonic acid</a>, nominally (<span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>FSbF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span>), is 10<sup>16</sup> times stronger than 100% sulfuric acid,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and can produce solutions with a <i>H</i><sub>0</sub> lower than –21.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Fluoroantimonic acid is a combination of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">hydrogen fluoride</a> (HF) and <a href="/wiki/Antimony_pentafluoride" title="Antimony pentafluoride">antimony pentafluoride</a> (SbF<sub>5</sub>).  In this system, HF releases its proton (H<sup>+</sup>) concomitant with the binding of F<sup>−</sup> by the antimony pentafluoride.  The resulting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">anion</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">SbF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span>) is both a poor <a href="/wiki/Nucleophile" title="Nucleophile">nucleophile</a> and a weak <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">base</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a> from superacids has been popularly said to become "naked", accounting for the system's extreme acidity. In the physical sense, the acidic "proton" is never completely free (as an unbound proton) but is bound to the anion, albeit weakly.  The proton hops from anion to anion in superacids via the <a href="/wiki/Grotthuss_mechanism" title="Grotthuss mechanism">Grotthuss mechanism</a>, just as happens to acidic "protons" in water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The extreme acidity of the acids is due to the ease with which this proton is transferred to substances that cannot normally be "protonated" (such as hydrocarbons), due to the very high stability of the conjugate-base anion (such as <span class="chemf nowrap">SbF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span>) after it donates the proton.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superacid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Petrochemistry" title="Petrochemistry">petrochemistry</a>, superacidic media are used as catalysts, especially for <a href="/wiki/Alkylation" title="Alkylation">alkylations</a>.  Typical catalysts are sulfated oxides of <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zirconium" title="Zirconium">zirconium</a> or specially treated alumina or <a href="/wiki/Zeolite" title="Zeolite">zeolites</a>.  The <a href="/wiki/Solid_acid" title="Solid acid">solid acids</a> are used for alkylating benzene with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethene" title="Ethene">ethene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Propene" title="Propene">propene</a> as well as difficult <a href="/wiki/Acylation" title="Acylation">acylations</a>, e.g. of <a href="/wiki/Chlorobenzene" title="Chlorobenzene">chlorobenzene</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superacid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The following values show the <a href="/wiki/Hammett_acidity_function" title="Hammett acidity function">Hammett acidity function</a> for several superacids, the strongest being <a href="/wiki/Fluoroantimonic_acid" title="Fluoroantimonic acid">fluoroantimonic acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Increased acidity is indicated by smaller (in this case, more negative) values of <i>H</i><sub>0</sub>.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Fluoroantimonic_acid" title="Fluoroantimonic acid">Fluoroantimonic acid</a> (<i>H</i><sub>0</sub> between −21 and −23)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Magic_acid" title="Magic acid">Magic acid</a> (<i>H</i><sub>0</sub> = −19.2)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Carborane_acid" title="Carborane acid">Carborane acids</a> (<i>H</i><sub>0</sub> ≤ −18, indirectly determined and depends on substituents)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fluorosulfuric_acid" title="Fluorosulfuric acid">Fluorosulfuric acid</a> (<i>H</i><sub>0</sub> = −15.1)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">Hydrogen fluoride</a> (<i>H</i><sub>0</sub> = −15.1)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Triflic_acid" title="Triflic acid">Triflic acid</a> (<i>H</i><sub>0</sub> = −14.9)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">Sulfuric acid</a> (<i>H</i><sub>0</sub> = −11.9)</li></ul>
