<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Supercritical drying</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Drying.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="310" data-file-width="335" height="204" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Drying.svg/220px-Drying.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Drying.svg/330px-Drying.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Drying.svg/440px-Drying.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Drying.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Supercritical drying (red arrow) goes beyond the <a href="/wiki/Critical_point_(thermodynamics)" title="Critical point (thermodynamics)">critical point</a> of the working fluid in order to avoid the direct liquid–gas transition seen in ordinary drying (green arrow) or the two phase changes in <a href="/wiki/Freeze-drying" title="Freeze-drying">freeze-drying</a> (blue arrow).</div></div></div>
<p><b>Supercritical drying</b> is a process to remove <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a> in a precise and controlled way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TsotsasMujumdar2011_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-TsotsasMujumdar2011-1">[1]</a></sup>  It is useful in the production of <a href="/wiki/Microelectromechanical_systems" title="Microelectromechanical systems">microelectromechanical systems</a> (MEMS), the drying of <a href="/wiki/Spice" title="Spice">spices</a>, the production of <a href="/wiki/Aerogel" title="Aerogel">aerogel</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Decaffeination" title="Decaffeination">decaffeination</a> of coffee and in the preparation of biological specimens for <a href="/wiki/Scanning_electron_microscope" title="Scanning electron microscope">scanning electron microscopy</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phase_diagram">Phase diagram</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_drying&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Phase diagram">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As the substance in a liquid body crosses <a href="/wiki/Vapor%E2%80%93liquid_equilibrium" title="Vapor–liquid equilibrium">the boundary from liquid to gas</a> (see green arrow in <a href="/wiki/Phase_diagram" title="Phase diagram">phase diagram</a>), the liquid changes into <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> at a finite rate, while the amount of liquid decreases. When this happens within a heterogeneous environment, <a href="/wiki/Surface_tension" title="Surface tension">surface tension</a> in the liquid body pulls against any solid structures the liquid might be in contact with. Delicate structures such as <a href="/wiki/Cell_wall" title="Cell wall">cell walls</a>, the dendrites in <a href="/wiki/Silica_gel" title="Silica gel">silica gel</a>, and the tiny machinery of microelectromechanical devices, tend to be broken apart by this surface tension as the liquid–gas–solid junction moves by.
</p><p>To avoid this, the sample can be brought via two possible alternate paths from the liquid phase to the gas phase without crossing the liquid–gas boundary on the phase diagram. In <a href="/wiki/Freeze-drying" title="Freeze-drying">freeze-drying</a>, this means going around to the left (low temperature, low pressure; blue arrow). However, some structures are disrupted even by the <a href="/wiki/Sublimation_(phase_transition)" title="Sublimation (phase transition)">solid–gas boundary</a>. Supercritical drying, on the other hand, goes around the line to the right, on the high-temperature, high-pressure side (red arrow). This route from liquid to gas does not cross any <a href="/wiki/Phase_transition" title="Phase transition">phase boundary</a>, instead passing through the <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_fluid" title="Supercritical fluid">supercritical</a> region, where the distinction between gas and liquid ceases to apply. Densities of the liquid phase and vapor phase become equal at critical point of drying.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fluids">Fluids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_drying&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fluids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carbon_dioxide_pressure-temperature_phase_diagram.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="700" data-file-width="742" height="283" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Carbon_dioxide_pressure-temperature_phase_diagram.svg/300px-Carbon_dioxide_pressure-temperature_phase_diagram.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Carbon_dioxide_pressure-temperature_phase_diagram.svg/450px-Carbon_dioxide_pressure-temperature_phase_diagram.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Carbon_dioxide_pressure-temperature_phase_diagram.svg/600px-Carbon_dioxide_pressure-temperature_phase_diagram.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Carbon_dioxide_pressure-temperature_phase_diagram.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Phase_diagram" title="Phase diagram">Phase diagram</a> showing the supercritical region (light blue) of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Fluids suitable for supercritical drying include <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (<a href="/wiki/Critical_point_(thermodynamics)" title="Critical point (thermodynamics)">critical point</a> 304.25 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a> at 7.39 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">MPa</a> or 31.1 <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">°C</a> at 1072 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pound-force_per_square_inch" title="Pound-force per square inch">psi</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Freon" title="Freon">freon</a> (≈300 K at 3.5–4 MPa or 25–0 °C at 500–600 psi). <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">Nitrous oxide</a> has similar physical behavior to carbon dioxide, but is a powerful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidizer" title="Oxidizer">oxidizer</a> in its supercritical state. Supercritical <a href="/wiki/Properties_of_water" title="Properties of water">water</a> is inconvenient due to possible heat damage to a sample at its critical point temperature (647 K, 374 °C) and corrosiveness of water at such high temperatures and pressures (22.064 MPa, 3,212 psi).
</p><p>In most such processes, <a href="/wiki/Acetone" title="Acetone">acetone</a> is first used to wash away all water, exploiting the complete <a href="/wiki/Miscibility" title="Miscibility">miscibility</a> of these two fluids. The acetone is then washed away with high pressure liquid carbon dioxide, the industry standard now that <a href="/wiki/Freon" title="Freon">freon</a> is unavailable. The liquid carbon dioxide is then heated until its temperature goes beyond the critical point, at which time the pressure can be gradually released, allowing the gas to escape and leaving a dried product.
</p>
