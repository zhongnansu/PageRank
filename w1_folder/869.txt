<p>A <b>coalition government</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_(government)" title="Cabinet (government)">cabinet</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_system" title="Parliamentary system">parliamentary</a> government in which multiple  <a href="/wiki/Political_party" title="Political party">political parties</a> cooperate, reducing the dominance of any one party within that "coalition". The usual reason for this arrangement is that no party on its own can achieve a majority in the <a href="/wiki/Parliament" title="Parliament">parliament</a>. A coalition government might also be created in a time of national difficulty or crisis (for example, during wartime or economic crisis) to give a government the high degree of perceived <a href="/wiki/Legitimacy_(political)" title="Legitimacy (political)">political legitimacy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Collective_identity" title="Collective identity">collective identity</a> it desires while also playing a role in diminishing internal political strife. In such times, parties have formed <b>all-party coalitions</b> (<a href="/wiki/National_unity_government" title="National unity government">national unity governments</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grand_coalition" title="Grand coalition">grand coalitions</a>). If a coalition collapses, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motion_of_Confidence" title="Motion of Confidence">confidence vote</a> is held or a <a href="/wiki/Motion_of_no_confidence" title="Motion of no confidence">motion of no confidence</a> is taken.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Practice">Practice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalition_government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Practice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Original_research" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" data-file-height="40" data-file-width="40" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/40px-Ambox_important.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/60px-Ambox_important.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/80px-Ambox_important.svg.png 2x" width="40"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section  <b>possibly contains <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research">original research</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Coalition_government&amp;action=edit">improve it</a> by <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verifying</a> the claims made and adding <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#Inline_citations" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">inline citations</a>. Statements consisting only of original research should be removed.</span> <small><i>(June 2009)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>When a general election does not produce a clear majority for a single party, parties either form coalition cabinets, supported by a parliamentary majority, or <a href="/wiki/Minority_government" title="Minority government">minority cabinets</a> which may consist of one or more parties. Cabinets based on a group of parties that command a majority in parliament tend to be more stable and long-lived than minority cabinets. While the former are prone to internal struggles, they have less reason to fear votes of no confidence. <a href="/wiki/Majority_government" title="Majority government">Majority governments</a> based on a single party are typically even more stable, as long as their majority can be maintained.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalition_government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_with_coalition_governments" title="List of countries with coalition governments">List of countries with coalition governments</a></div>
<p>Countries which often operate with coalition cabinets include: the <a href="/wiki/Nordic_countries" title="Nordic countries">Nordic countries</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Benelux" title="Benelux">Benelux</a> countries, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kosovo" title="Kosovo">Kosovo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>. <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> has been ruled by a coalition of the four strongest parties in parliament from 1959 to 2008, called the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magic_Formula" title="Magic Formula">Magic Formula</a>". Between 2010 and 2015, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> also operated a formal coalition between the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrat</a> parties, but this was unusual: the UK usually has a single-party majority government.
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalition_government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/National_Government_(United_Kingdom)" title="National Government (United Kingdom)">National Government (United Kingdom)</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_coalition_government_(disambiguation)" title="United Kingdom coalition government (disambiguation)">United Kingdom coalition government (disambiguation)</a></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Politics of the United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, coalition governments (sometimes known as "national governments") usually have only been formed at times of national crisis. All six coalition governments in the last 120 years have involved the Liberal and Conservative parties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The most prominent was the <a href="/wiki/National_Government_(United_Kingdom)" title="National Government (United Kingdom)">National Government</a> of 1931 to 1940. There were multi-party coalitions during both <a href="/wiki/World_war" title="World war">world wars</a>. Apart from this, when no party has had a majority, minority governments normally have been formed with one or more opposition parties agreeing to vote in favour of the legislation which governments need to function: for instance the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a> government of <a href="/wiki/James_Callaghan" title="James Callaghan">James Callaghan</a> came to an agreement with the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberals</a> in 1977 when it lost the narrow majority it had gained in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_October_1974" title="United Kingdom general election, October 1974">October 1974 election</a>. However, in the run-up to the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1997" title="United Kingdom general election, 1997">1997 general election</a>, Labour opposition leader <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a> was in talks with <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrat</a> leader <a href="/wiki/Paddy_Ashdown" title="Paddy Ashdown">Paddy Ashdown</a> about forming a coalition government if Labour failed to win a majority at the election; but there proved to be no need for a coalition as Labour won the election by a <a href="/wiki/Landslide_victory" title="Landslide victory">landslide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2010" title="United Kingdom general election, 2010">2010 general election</a> resulted in a <a href="/wiki/Hung_parliament" title="Hung parliament">hung parliament</a> (Britain's first for <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_February_1974" title="United Kingdom general election, February 1974">36 years</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservatives</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a>, which had won the largest number of seats, formed a <a href="/wiki/Cameron%E2%80%93Clegg_coalition" title="Cameron–Clegg coalition">coalition</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrats</a> in order to gain a parliamentary majority, ending 13 years of Labour government. This was the first time that the Conservatives and Lib Dems had made a power-sharing deal at Westminster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> It was also the first full coalition in Britain since 1945, having been formed 70 years virtually to the day after the establishment of <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a>'s wartime coalition,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> although there had been a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lib-Lab_pact" title="Lib-Lab pact">Lib-Lab pact</a>", an agreement stopping well short of a coalition, between the Labour and Liberal parties, from March 1977 until July 1978, after a series of by-election defeats had eroded Labour's majority of three seats which had been gained at the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_October_1974" title="United Kingdom general election, October 1974">October 1974 election</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalition_government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Germany, for instance, coalition government is the norm, as it is rare for either the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Union_of_Germany" title="Christian Democratic Union of Germany">Christian Democratic Union of Germany</a> together with their partners the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Social_Union_in_Bavaria" title="Christian Social Union in Bavaria">Christian Social Union in Bavaria</a> (CDU/CSU), or the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Party of Germany">Social Democratic Party of Germany</a> (SPD), to win an unqualified majority in a national election. Thus, at the federal level, governments are formed with at least two parties. For example, <a href="/wiki/Helmut_Kohl" title="Helmut Kohl">Helmut Kohl</a>'s CDU governed for years in coalition with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Free Democratic Party of Germany">Free Democratic Party</a> (FDP); from 1998 to 2005 <a href="/wiki/Gerhard_Schr%C3%B6der" title="Gerhard Schröder">Gerhard Schröder</a>'s SPD was in power with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Green_Party" title="German Green Party">Greens</a>; and from 2009 <a href="/wiki/Angela_Merkel" title="Angela Merkel">Angela Merkel</a>, CDU/CSU was in power with the FDP.
</p><p>"Grand coalitions" of the two large parties also occur, but these are relatively rare, as large parties usually prefer to associate with small ones. However, if none of the larger parties can receive enough votes to form their preferred coalition, a grand coalition might be their only choice for forming a government. This was the situation in Germany in 2005 when Angela Merkel became Chancellor: in early <a href="/wiki/German_federal_election,_2005" title="German federal election, 2005">elections</a>, the CDU/CSU did not garner enough votes to form a majority coalition with the FDP; similarly the SPD and Greens did not have enough votes to continue with their formerly ruling coalition. A grand coalition government was subsequently forged between the CDU/CSU and the SPD. Partnerships like these typically involve carefully structured cabinets. The CDU/CSU ended up holding the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_Germany_(Federal_Republic)" title="Chancellor of Germany (Federal Republic)">Chancellory</a> while the SPD took the majority of cabinet posts. Parties frequently make statements ahead of elections which coalitions they categorically reject, similar to election promises or shadow cabinets in other countries.
</p><p>In Germany, coalitions rarely consist of more than two parties (CDU and CSU, two allies which always form a single <a href="/wiki/Caucus" title="Caucus">caucus</a>, are in this regard considered a single party). However, in the 2010s coalitions on the state level increasingly included three different parties, often <a href="/wiki/Free_Democratic_Party_(Germany)" title="Free Democratic Party (Germany)">FDP</a>, Greens and one of the major parties or "red red green" coalitions of <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Party of Germany">SPD</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linkspartei" title="Linkspartei">Linkspartei</a> and Greens.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_of_coalitions">Examples of coalitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalition_government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Examples of coalitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalition_government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In federal <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Australia" title="Politics of Australia">Australian politics</a>, the conservative <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_of_Australia" title="Liberal Party of Australia">Liberal</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_Party_of_Australia" title="National Party of Australia">National</a>, <a href="/wiki/Country_Liberal_Party" title="Country Liberal Party">Country Liberal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liberal_National_Party_of_Queensland" title="Liberal National Party of Queensland">Liberal National</a> parties are united in a coalition, known simply as <a href="/wiki/Coalition_(Australia)" title="Coalition (Australia)">the Coalition</a>. The Coalition has become so stable, at least at the federal level, that in practice the lower house of Parliament has become a <a href="/wiki/Two-party_system" title="Two-party system">two-party</a> house, with the Coalition and the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Labor_Party" title="Australian Labor Party">Labor Party</a> being the major parties. This coalition is also found in the states of <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a>. In <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a> the Liberal and National parties compete separately, while in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Territory" title="Northern Territory">Northern Territory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a> the two parties have merged, forming the Country Liberal Party, in 1978, and the Liberal National Party, in 2008, respectively.
</p><p>The other federal coalition has been:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/United_Australia_Party" title="United Australia Party">United Australia</a>–<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Country_Party" title="National Country Party">Country</a>–Independents coalition in <a href="/wiki/Australian_federal_election,_1940" title="Australian federal election, 1940">1940</a></li>
<li>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tasmanian_Government" title="Tasmanian Government">Tasmania</a>:
<ul><li>Liberal–Greens coalition in <a href="/wiki/Tasmanian_state_election,_1996" title="Tasmanian state election, 1996">1996</a>.</li>
<li>Labor–Greens coalitions formed in <a href="/wiki/Tasmanian_state_election,_1989" title="Tasmanian state election, 1989">1989</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tasmanian_state_election,_2010" title="Tasmanian state election, 2010">2010</a>.</li></ul></li>
<li>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ACT_Government" title="ACT Government">Australian Capital Territory</a>:
<ul><li>Liberal–<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_(politician)" title="Independent (politician)">Independent</a> coalition in <a href="/wiki/Australian_Capital_Territory_general_election,_1998" title="Australian Capital Territory general election, 1998">1998</a>.</li>
<li>Labor–Greens coalitions in <a href="/wiki/Australian_Capital_Territory_general_election,_2001" title="Australian Capital Territory general election, 2001">2001</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australian_Capital_Territory_general_election,_2008" title="Australian Capital Territory general election, 2008">2008</a>.</li></ul></li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Belgium">Belgium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalition_government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Belgium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Belgium" title="Politics of Belgium">Belgium</a>, where there are separate <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>-speaking and <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>-speaking parties for each political grouping, coalition cabinets of up to six parties are common.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalition_government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Canada" title="Politics of Canada">Canada</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Great_Coalition" title="Great Coalition">Great Coalition</a> was formed in 1864 by the <a href="/wiki/Clear_Grits" title="Clear Grits">Clear Grits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parti_bleu" title="Parti bleu">Parti bleu</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Liberal-Conservative_Party" title="Liberal-Conservative Party">Liberal-Conservative Party</a>. During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">First World War</a>, Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Robert_Borden" title="Robert Borden">Robert Borden</a> attempted to form a coalition with the opposition Liberals to broaden support for controversial conscription legislation. The Liberal Party refused the offer but some of their members did <a href="/wiki/Crossing_the_floor" title="Crossing the floor">cross the floor</a> and join the government. Although sometimes referred to as a coalition government, according to the definition above, it was not. It was disbanded after the end of the war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>As a result of the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_general_election,_1919" title="Ontario general election, 1919">1919 Ontario election</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Farmers_of_Ontario" title="United Farmers of Ontario">United Farmers of Ontario</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labour_Party_of_Canada" title="Labour Party of Canada">Labour Party</a>, together with three independent MLAs, formed a coalition that governed <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a> until 1923.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, the governing Liberals formed a coalition with the opposition Conservatives in order to prevent the surging, left-wing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cooperative_Commonwealth_Federation" title="Cooperative Commonwealth Federation">Cooperative Commonwealth Federation</a> from taking power in the <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia_general_election,_1941" title="British Columbia general election, 1941">British Columbia general election, 1941</a>. Liberal premier <a href="/wiki/Duff_Pattullo" title="Duff Pattullo">Duff Pattullo</a> refused to form a coalition with the third-place Conservatives, so his party removed him. The Liberal–Conservative coalition introduced a winner-take-all preferential voting system (the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alternative_Vote" title="Alternative Vote">Alternative Vote</a>") in the hopes that their supporters would rank the other party as their second preference; however, this strategy did not take CCF second preferences into account. In the <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia_general_election,_1952" title="British Columbia general election, 1952">British Columbia general election, 1952</a>, to the surprise of many, the right-wing populist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BC_Social_Credit_Party" title="BC Social Credit Party">BC Social Credit Party</a> won a minority. They were able to win a majority in the subsequent election as Liberal and Conservative supporters shifted their anti-CCF vote to Social Credit.
</p><p>Manitoba has had more formal coalition governments than any other province. Following gains by the United Farmer's/Progressive movement elsewhere in the country, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Farmers_of_Manitoba" title="United Farmers of Manitoba">United Farmers of Manitoba</a> unexpectedly won the 1921 election. Like their counterparts in Ontario, they had not expected to win and did not have a leader. They asked <a href="/wiki/John_Bracken" title="John Bracken">John Bracken</a>, a professor in animal husbandry, to become leader and premier. Bracken changed the party's name to the <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Party_of_Manitoba" title="Progressive Party of Manitoba">Progressive Party of Manitoba</a>. During the Great Depression, Bracken survived at a time when other premiers were being defeated by forming a coalition government with the Manitoba Liberals (eventually, the two parties would merge into the <a href="/wiki/Manitoba_Liberal_Party#Liberal-Progressive_Party:_Merger_with_the_Progressives" title="Manitoba Liberal Party">Liberal-Progressive Party of Manitoba</a>, and decades later, the party would change its name to the <a href="/wiki/Manitoba_Liberal_Party" title="Manitoba Liberal Party">Manitoba Liberal Party</a>). In 1940, Bracken formed a wartime coalition government with almost every party in the Manitoba Legislature (the Conservatives, CCF, and Social Credit; however, the CCF broke with the coalition after a few years over policy differences). The only party not included was the small, communist <a href="/wiki/Labor-Progressive_Party" title="Labor-Progressive Party">Labor-Progressive Party</a>, which had a handful of seats.
</p><p>In Saskatchewan, NDP premier <a href="/wiki/Roy_Romanow" title="Roy Romanow">Roy Romanow</a> formed a formal coalition with the <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan_Liberal_Party" title="Saskatchewan Liberal Party">Saskatchewan Liberals</a> in 1999 after being reduced to a minority. After two years, the newly elected Liberal leader <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jim_Melanchuk&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jim Melanchuk (page does not exist)">Jim Melanchuk</a> chose to withdraw from the coalition; however, 2 out of 3 members of his caucus disagreed with him and left the Liberals to run as New Democrats in the upcoming election. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saskatchewan_NDP" title="Saskatchewan NDP">Saskatchewan NDP</a> was re-elected with a majority under its new leader <a href="/wiki/Lorne_Calvert" title="Lorne Calvert">Lorne Calvert</a>, while the Saskatchewan Liberals lost their remaining seats and have not been competitive in the province since.
</p><p>According to historian <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Moore_(Canadian_historian)" title="Christopher Moore (Canadian historian)">Christopher Moore</a>, coalition governments in Canada became much less possible in 1919, when the leaders of parties were no longer chosen by elected MPs but instead began to be chosen by party members. That kind of leadership selection process had never been tried in any parliament system before and remains uncommon in the parliaments of the world today. According to Moore, as long as that kind of leadership selection process remains in place and concentrates power in the hands of the leader, as opposed to backbenchers, then coalition governments will be very difficult to form. Moore shows that the diffusion of power within a party tends to also lead to a diffusion of power in the parliament in which that party operates, thereby making coalitions more likely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moore-1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moore-1-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2008_Canadian_parliamentary_dispute" title="2008 Canadian parliamentary dispute">2008 Canadian parliamentary dispute</a>, two of Canada's opposition parties signed an agreement to form what would become the country's second coalition government since <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_confederation" title="Canadian confederation">Confederation</a> if the minority Conservative government was defeated on a vote of non-confidence,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> unseating <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Harper" title="Stephen Harper">Stephen Harper</a> as Prime Minister. The agreement outlined a formal coalition consisting of two opposition parties, the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_of_Canada" title="Liberal Party of Canada">Liberal Party</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Democratic_Party_(Canada)" title="New Democratic Party (Canada)">New Democratic Party</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Bloc_Qu%C3%A9b%C3%A9cois" title="Bloc Québécois">Bloc Québécois</a> agreed to support the proposed coalition on confidence matters for 18 months. In the end, parliament was <a href="/wiki/Prorogation_in_Canada" title="Prorogation in Canada">prorogued</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Governor_General_of_Canada" title="Governor General of Canada">Governor General</a>, and the coalition dispersed following the election.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Denmark">Denmark</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalition_government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Denmark">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Denmark, all governments from 1982 until the June 2015 elections have been coalitions. The first coalition in Danish political history was formed in 1929 by <a href="/wiki/Thorvald_Stauning" title="Thorvald Stauning">Thorvald Stauning</a> and consisted of the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democrats_(Denmark)" title="Social Democrats (Denmark)">Social Democrats</a> (Staunings own party) and the <a href="/wiki/Danish_Social_Liberal_Party" title="Danish Social Liberal Party">Social Liberals</a>. Since then, a number of parties have participated in coalitions.
</p><p>Excluding the post-<a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">WW2</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_Vilhelm_Buhl_II" title="Cabinet of Vilhelm Buhl II">Liberation Cabinet's</a> member parties, the following parties have done so: The <a href="/wiki/Centre_Democrats_(Denmark)" title="Centre Democrats (Denmark)">Centre Democrats</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democrats_(Denmark)" title="Christian Democrats (Denmark)">Christian People's Party</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_People%27s_Party_(Denmark)" title="Conservative People's Party (Denmark)">Conservative People's Party</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Justice_Party_of_Denmark" title="Justice Party of Denmark">Justice Party</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democrats_(Denmark)" title="Social Democrats (Denmark)">Social Democrats</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_People%27s_Party_(Denmark)" title="Socialist People's Party (Denmark)">Socialist People's Party</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Danish_Social_Liberal_Party" title="Danish Social Liberal Party">Social Liberal Party</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Venstre_(Denmark)" title="Venstre (Denmark)">Venstre</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Finland">Finland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalition_government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Finland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Finland" title="Politics of Finland">Finland</a>, no party has had an absolute majority in the parliament since independence, and multi-party coalitions have been the norm. Finland experienced its most stable government (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paavo_Lipponen%27s_first_cabinet" title="Paavo Lipponen's first cabinet">Lipponen I</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paavo_Lipponen%27s_second_cabinet" title="Paavo Lipponen's second cabinet">II</a>) since <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finland%27s_declaration_of_independence" title="Finland's declaration of independence">independence</a> with a five-party governing coalition, a so-called "rainbow government". The Lipponen cabinets set the stability record and were unusual in the respect that both moderate (SDP) and radical left wing (Left Alliance) parties sat in the government with the major right-wing party (National Coalition). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jyrki_Katainen%27s_cabinet" title="Jyrki Katainen's cabinet">Katainen cabinet</a> was also a rainbow coalition of a total of five parties.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalition_government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Since India's Independence on 15 August 1947, <a href="/wiki/Indian_National_Congress" title="Indian National Congress">Indian National Congress</a>, the major political party instrumental in <a href="/wiki/Indian_independence_movement" title="Indian independence movement">Indian independence movement</a>, ruled the nation. The first Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Jawaharlal_Nehru" title="Jawaharlal Nehru">Jawaharlal Nehru</a>, second PM <a href="/wiki/Lal_Bahadur_Shastri" title="Lal Bahadur Shastri">Lal Bahadur Shastri</a> and the third PM <a href="/wiki/Indira_Gandhi" title="Indira Gandhi">Indira Gandhi</a>, all were from the Congress party. However, <a href="/wiki/Raj_Narain" title="Raj Narain">Raj Narain</a>, who had unsuccessfully contested election against Indira from the constituency of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rae_Bareilly" title="Rae Bareilly">Rae Bareilly</a> in 1971, lodged a case, alleging electoral malpractices. In June 1975, Indira was found guilty and barred by High Court from holding public office for six years. In response, an ungracious Emergency was declared under the pretext of national security. The <a href="/wiki/Indian_general_election,_1977" title="Indian general election, 1977">next election's result</a> was that <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_India" title="Politics of India">India</a>'s first-ever coalition government was formed at the national level under the Prime Ministership of <a href="/wiki/Morarji_Desai" title="Morarji Desai">Morarji Desai</a>, which was also the first non-Congress national government, which existed from 24 March 1977 to 15 July 1979, headed by the <a href="/wiki/Janata_Party" title="Janata Party">Janata Party</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UK_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-UK-8">[8]</a></sup> an amalgam of political parties opposed to Emergency imposed between 1975 and 1977. As the popularity of Janata Party dwindled, Morarji Desai had to resign and <a href="/wiki/Charan_Singh" title="Charan Singh">Charan Singh</a>, a rival of Desai became the fifth PM. However, due to lack of support, this coalition government did not complete its five-year term.
</p><p>Congress returned to the                                                                                                                                                                                          power in 1980 under Indira Gandhi, and later under <a href="/wiki/Rajiv_Gandhi" title="Rajiv Gandhi">Rajiv Gandhi</a> as the 6th PM. However, the next <a href="/wiki/Indian_general_election,_1989" title="Indian general election, 1989">general election of 1989</a> once again brought a coalition government under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Front_(India)" title="National Front (India)">National Front</a>, which lasted until 1991, with two Prime Ministers, the second one being supported by Congress. The 1991 election resulted in a Congress led stable <a href="/wiki/Minority_government" title="Minority government">minority government</a> for five years. The next 11th <a href="/wiki/Lok_Sabha" title="Lok Sabha">parliament</a> produced three Prime Ministers in two years and forced the country back to the polls in 1998. The first successful coalition government in India which completed the whole 5-year term was the <a href="/wiki/Bharatiya_Janata_Party" title="Bharatiya Janata Party">Bharatiya Janata Party</a> (BJP) led <a href="/wiki/National_Democratic_Alliance_(India)" title="National Democratic Alliance (India)">National Democratic Alliance</a> with <a href="/wiki/Atal_Bihari_Vajpayee" title="Atal Bihari Vajpayee">Atal Bihari Vajpayee</a> as PM from 1999 to 2004. Then another coalition, Congress led <a href="/wiki/United_Progressive_Alliance" title="United Progressive Alliance">United Progressive Alliance</a>, consisting of 13 separate parties ruled India for two terms from 2004 to 2014 with <a href="/wiki/Manmohan_Singh" title="Manmohan Singh">Manmohan Singh</a> as PM. However, in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_general_election,_2014" title="Indian general election, 2014">16th general election</a> in May 2014, BJP secured majority on its own (first party to do so since 1984 election) and National Democratic Alliance again came into power, with <a href="/wiki/Narendra_Modi" title="Narendra Modi">Narendra Modi</a> as Prime Minister and more.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Indonesia">Indonesia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalition_government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Indonesia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>As a result of the <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Suharto" title="Fall of Suharto">toppling of Suharto</a>, political freedom is significantly increased. Compared to only three parties allowed to exist in the <a href="/wiki/New_Order_(Indonesia)" title="New Order (Indonesia)">New Order</a> era, a total of 48 political parties participated in the <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_legislative_election,_1999" title="Indonesian legislative election, 1999">1999 election</a>, a total of 24 parties in the <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_legislative_election,_2004" title="Indonesian legislative election, 2004">2004 election</a>, 38 parties in the <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_legislative_election,_2009" title="Indonesian legislative election, 2009">2009 election</a>, and 15 parties in the <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_legislative_election,_2014" title="Indonesian legislative election, 2014">2014 election</a>. There are no majority winner of those elections and coalition governments are inevitable. The current government is a coalition of seven parties led by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indonesian_Democratic_Party_%E2%80%93_Struggle" title="Indonesian Democratic Party – Struggle">PDIP</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Party_of_the_Functional_Groups" title="Party of the Functional Groups">Golkar</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ireland">Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalition_government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>, coalition governments are quite common; not since 1977 has a single party been able to form a majority government. Coalitions are the typically formed of two or more parties always consisting of one of the two biggest parties, <a href="/wiki/Fianna_F%C3%A1il" title="Fianna Fáil">Fianna Fáil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fine_Gael" title="Fine Gael">Fine Gael</a>, and one or more smaller parties or independent members of parliament. The current government consists of a minority Fine Gael government, supported by a <a href="/wiki/Confidence_and_supply" title="Confidence and supply">confidence and supply</a> arrangement with Fianna Fáil.
</p><p>Ireland's first coalition government was formed in <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_1948" title="Irish general election, 1948">1948</a>. Ireland has had consecutive coalition governments since the <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_1989" title="Irish general election, 1989">1989 general election</a>, excluding two brief Fianna Fáil minority administrations in 1994 and 2011 that followed the withdrawal of their coalition partners from government. Before 1989, Fianna Fáil had opposed participation in coalition governments, preferring single-party minority government instead.
</p><p>Irish coalition governments have traditionally been based on one of two large blocs in <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1il_%C3%89ireann" title="Dáil Éireann">Dáil Éireann</a>: either Fianna Fáil in coalition with smaller parties or independents, or Fine Gael and the Labour Party in coalition, sometimes with smaller parties. The only exception to these traditional alliances was the <a href="/wiki/23rd_Government_of_Ireland" title="23rd Government of Ireland">23rd Government of Ireland</a>, comprising Fianna Fáil and the Labour Party, which ruled between 1993 and 1994. The <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_31st_D%C3%A1il" title="Government of the 31st Dáil">Government of the 31st Dáil</a>, though a traditional Fine Gael–Labour coalition, resembles a <a href="/wiki/Grand_coalition" title="Grand coalition">grand coalition</a>, due to the collapse of the Fianna Fáil to third place among parties in Dáil Éireann.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Israel">Israel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalition_government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Israel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A similar situation exists in <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Israel" title="Politics of Israel">Israel</a>, which typically has at least 10 parties holding representation in the <a href="/wiki/Knesset" title="Knesset">Knesset</a>. The only faction to ever gain the majority of Knesset seats was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alignment_(political_party)" title="Alignment (political party)">Alignment</a>, an alliance of the <a href="/wiki/Israeli_Labor_Party" title="Israeli Labor Party">Labor Party</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mapam" title="Mapam">Mapam</a> that held an absolute majority for a brief period from 1968 to 1969. Historically, control of the Israeli government has alternated between periods of rule by the right-wing <a href="/wiki/Likud" title="Likud">Likud</a> in coalition with several right-wing and religious parties and periods of rule by the center-left Labor in coalition with several left-wing parties. <a href="/wiki/Ariel_Sharon" title="Ariel Sharon">Ariel Sharon</a>'s formation of the centrist <a href="/wiki/Kadima" title="Kadima">Kadima</a> party in 2006 drew support from former Labor and Likud members, and Kadima ruled in coalition with several other parties.
</p><p>Israel also formed a <a href="/wiki/National_unity_government" title="National unity government">national unity government</a> from <a href="/wiki/Israeli_legislative_election,_1984" title="Israeli legislative election, 1984">1984–1988</a>. The premiership and foreign ministry portfolio were held by the head of each party for two years, and they switched roles in 1986.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalition_government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Post-World War II <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Japan" title="Politics of Japan">Japan</a> has historically been dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democratic_Party_(Japan)" title="Liberal Democratic Party (Japan)">Liberal Democratic Party</a>, but there was a brief coalition government formed after the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_general_election,_1993" title="Japanese general election, 1993">1993 election</a> following LDP's first loss of its overall <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_of_Representatives_of_Japan" title="House of Representatives of Japan">House of Representatives</a> majority since 1955, winning only 223 out of 511 seats. The LDP government was replaced by an eight-party coalition government, which consisted of all of the previous opposition parties excluding the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_Communist_Party" title="Japanese Communist Party">Japanese Communist Party</a>, who together controlled 243 seats. Every Japanese government since then has been a coalition government in one way or another.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Zealand">New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalition_government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Electoral_system_of_New_Zealand#MMP_in_New_Zealand" title="Electoral system of New Zealand">Electoral system of New Zealand § MMP in New Zealand</a></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MMP_representation" title="MMP representation">MMP</a> was introduced in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1996" title="New Zealand general election, 1996">the 1996 election</a>. 
In order to get into parliament, parties need to get a total of 50% of the 121 seats in parliament – 61. Since no parties have ever gotten a full majority, they must form coalitions with other parties. For example, in 2017, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(New_Zealand)" title="Labour Party (New Zealand)">Labour</a> got 46 seats, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_First" title="New Zealand First">New Zealand First</a> got nine, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_Party_(New_Zealand)" title="Green Party (New Zealand)">Green Party</a> got eight, which adds up to a total of 63 seats.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalition_government&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Coalition_government&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span> <small><i>(May 2014)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Advocates of proportional representation suggest that a coalition government leads to more consensus-based politics, as a government comprising differing parties (often based on different ideologies) need to compromise about governmental policy. Another stated advantage is that a coalition government better reflects the popular opinion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constituency" title="Constituency">electorate</a> within a country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moore-1_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Moore-1-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Those who disapprove of coalition governments believe that such governments have a tendency to be fractious and prone to disharmony, as their component parties hold differing beliefs and thus may not always agree on policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Sometimes the results of an election mean that the coalitions which are mathematically most probable are ideologically infeasible, for example in <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a> or <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>. A second difficulty might be the ability of minor parties to play "<a href="/wiki/Kingmaker" title="Kingmaker">kingmaker</a>" and, particularly in close elections, gain far more power in exchange for their support than the size of their vote would otherwise justify.
</p><p>Coalition governments have also been criticized<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (May 2014)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> for sustaining a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consensus" title="Consensus">consensus</a> on issues when disagreement and the consequent discussion would be more fruitful. To forge a consensus, the leaders of ruling coalition parties can agree to silence their disagreements on an issue to unify the coalition against the opposition. The coalition partners, if they control the parliamentary majority, can collude to make the parliamentary discussion on the issue irrelevant by consistently disregarding the arguments of the opposition and voting against the opposition's proposals — even if there is disagreement within the ruling parties about the issue.
</p><p>Powerful parties can also act in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oligocracy" title="Oligocracy">oligocratic</a> way to form an alliance to stifle the growth of emerging parties. Of course, such an event is rare in coalition governments when compared to <a href="/wiki/Two-party_system" title="Two-party system">two-party systems</a>, which typically exist because of stifling of the growth of emerging parties, often through discriminatory <a href="/wiki/Nomination_rules" title="Nomination rules">nomination rules</a> regulations and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plurality_voting_system" title="Plurality voting system">plurality voting systems</a>, and so on.
</p><p>A single, more powerful party can shape the policies of the coalition disproportionately. Smaller or less powerful parties can be intimidated to not openly disagree. In order to maintain the coalition, they would have to vote against their own party's platform in the parliament. If they do not, the party has to leave the government and loses executive power. However, this is contradicted by the "kingmaker" factor mentioned above.
</p>
