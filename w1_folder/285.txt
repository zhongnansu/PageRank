<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Anaerobic respiration</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Anaerobic respiration</b> is <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">respiration</a> using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_acceptors" title="Electron acceptors">electron acceptors</a> other than <a href="/wiki/Allotropes_of_oxygen" title="Allotropes of oxygen">molecular oxygen</a> (O<sub>2</sub>). Although oxygen is not used as the final electron acceptor, the process still uses a respiratory electron transport chain called physolmere; it is respiration without oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerobic_organisms" title="Aerobic organisms">aerobic organisms</a> undergoing respiration, electrons are shuttled to an <a href="/wiki/Electron_transport_chain" title="Electron transport chain">electron transport chain</a>, and the final electron acceptor is oxygen. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_oxygen" title="Molecular oxygen">Molecular oxygen</a> is a highly <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_agent" title="Oxidizing agent">oxidizing agent</a> and, therefore, is an excellent electron acceptor. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anaerobes" title="Anaerobes">anaerobes</a>, other less-oxidizing substances such as <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfate</a> (SO<sub>4</sub><sup>2−</sup>), <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrate</a> (NO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulphur" title="Sulphur">sulphur</a> (S), or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fumarate" title="Fumarate">fumarate</a> are used. These terminal electron acceptors have smaller <a href="/wiki/Reduction_potential" title="Reduction potential">reduction potentials</a> than O<sub>2</sub>, meaning that less energy is released per oxidized molecule. Therefore, generally speaking, anaerobic respiration is less efficient than aerobic.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_compared_with_fermentation">As compared with fermentation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaerobic_respiration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: As compared with fermentation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are two important microbial methane formation pathways, through carbonate reduction (respiration), and acetate fermentation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cellular respiration (both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerobic_respiration" title="Aerobic respiration">aerobic</a> and anaerobic) utilizes highly reduced chemical compounds such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NADH" title="NADH">NADH</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FADH2" title="FADH2">FADH2</a> (for example produced during <a href="/wiki/Glycolysis" title="Glycolysis">glycolysis</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid_cycle" title="Citric acid cycle">citric acid cycle</a>) to establish an <a href="/wiki/Electrochemical_gradient" title="Electrochemical gradient">electrochemical gradient</a> (often a proton gradient) across a membrane, resulting in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_potential" title="Electrical potential">electrical potential</a> or ion <a href="/wiki/Concentration" title="Concentration">concentration</a> difference across the membrane. The reduced chemical compounds are oxidized by a series of respiratory <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integral_membrane_proteins" title="Integral membrane proteins">integral membrane proteins</a> with sequentially increasing reduction potentials with the final electron acceptor being <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> (in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerobic_respiration" title="Aerobic respiration">aerobic respiration</a>) or another chemical substance (in anaerobic respiration). A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proton_motive_force" title="Proton motive force">proton motive force</a> drives <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protons" title="Protons">protons</a> down the gradient (across the membrane) through the proton channel of <a href="/wiki/ATP_synthase" title="ATP synthase">ATP synthase</a>. The resulting current drives ATP synthesis from <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_diphosphate" title="Adenosine diphosphate">ADP</a> and inorganic phosphate.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">Fermentation</a>, in contrast, does not utilize an electrochemical gradient. Fermentation instead only uses <a href="/wiki/Substrate-level_phosphorylation" title="Substrate-level phosphorylation">substrate-level phosphorylation</a> to produce ATP. The electron acceptor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NAD%2B" title="NAD+">NAD+</a> is regenerated from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NADH" title="NADH">NADH</a> formed in oxidative steps of the fermentation pathway by the reduction of oxidized compounds. These oxidized compounds are often formed during the fermentation pathway itself, but may also be external. For example, in homofermentative lactic acid bacteria, NADH formed during the oxidation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate" title="Glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate">glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate</a> is oxidized back to NAD+ by the reduction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyruvate" title="Pyruvate">pyruvate</a> to <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactic acid</a> at a later stage in the pathway. In <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">yeast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acetaldehyde" title="Acetaldehyde">acetaldehyde</a> is reduced to <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> to regenerate NAD+.  The two processes thus generate ATP in very different ways, and the terms should not be treated as synonyms.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_importance">Ecological importance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaerobic_respiration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ecological importance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anaerobic respiration is a critical component of the global <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_cycle" title="Nitrogen cycle">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_cycle" title="Sulfur cycle">sulfur</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon</a> cycles through the reduction of the oxyanions of nitrogen, sulfur, and carbon to more-reduced compounds.  The <a href="/wiki/Biogeochemical_cycle" title="Biogeochemical cycle">biogeochemical cycling</a> of these compounds, which depends upon anaerobic respiration, significantly impacts the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. Anaerobic respiration occurs in many environments, including freshwater and marine sediments, soil, subsurface aquifers, deep subsurface environments, and biofilms. Even environments, such as soil, that contain oxygen also have micro-environments that lack oxygen due to the slow diffusion characteristics of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> gas.
</p><p>An example of the ecological importance of anaerobic respiration is the use of nitrate as a terminal electron acceptor, or dissimilatory <a href="/wiki/Denitrification" title="Denitrification">denitrification</a>, which is the main route by which fixed <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation" title="Nitrogen fixation">nitrogen</a> is returned to the atmosphere as molecular nitrogen gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Another example is <a href="/wiki/Methanogenesis" title="Methanogenesis">methanogenesis</a>, a form of carbonate respiration, that is used to produce <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> gas by <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_digestion" title="Anaerobic digestion">anaerobic digestion</a>. Biogenic methane is used as a sustainable alternative to fossil fuels. On the negative side, uncontrolled methanogenesis in landfill sites releases large volumes of methane into the atmosphere, where it acts as a powerful <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulfate-reducing_bacteria" title="Sulfate-reducing bacteria">Sulfate respiration</a> produces <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>, which is responsible for the characteristic 'rotten egg' smell of coast wetlands and has the capacity to precipitate heavy metal ions from solution, leading to the deposition of <a href="/wiki/Sulfide_minerals" title="Sulfide minerals">sulfidic metal ores</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_relevance">Economic relevance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaerobic_respiration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Economic relevance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dissimilatory <a href="/wiki/Denitrification" title="Denitrification">denitrification</a> is widely used in the removal of <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrite" title="Nitrite">nitrite</a> from municipal wastewater. An excess of nitrate can lead to <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">eutrophication</a> of waterways into which treated water is released. Elevated nitrite levels in drinking water can lead to problems due to its toxicity. Denitrification converts both compounds into harmless nitrogen gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<p><br/>
Specific types of anaerobic respiration are also critical in <a href="/wiki/Bioremediation" title="Bioremediation">bioremediation</a>, which uses microorganisms to convert toxic chemicals into less-harmful molecules to clean up contaminated beaches, aquifers, lakes, and oceans. For example, toxic <a href="/wiki/Arsenate" title="Arsenate">arsenate</a> or <a href="/wiki/Selenate" title="Selenate">selenate</a> can be reduced to less toxic compounds by various anaerobic bacteria via anaerobic respiration. The reduction of <a href="/wiki/Organochloride" title="Organochloride">chlorinated chemical pollutants</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Vinyl_chloride" title="Vinyl chloride">vinyl chloride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride" title="Carbon tetrachloride">carbon tetrachloride</a>, also occurs through anaerobic respiration.
</p><p>Anaerobic respiration is useful in generating electricity in <a href="/wiki/Microbial_fuel_cell" title="Microbial fuel cell">microbial fuel cell</a>s, which employ bacteria that respire solid electron acceptors (such as oxidized iron) to transfer electrons from reduced compounds to an electrode. This process can simultaneously degrade organic carbon waste and generate electricity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
