<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Active laser medium</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>active laser medium</b> (also called <b>gain medium</b> or <b>lasing medium</b>) is the source of optical <a href="/wiki/Gain_(laser)" title="Gain (laser)">gain</a> within a <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">laser</a>.  The gain results from the <a href="/wiki/Stimulated_emission" title="Stimulated emission">stimulated emission</a> of electronic or molecular transitions to a lower energy state from a higher energy state
previously populated by a <a href="/wiki/Laser_pumping" title="Laser pumping">pump source</a>.
</p><p>Examples of active laser media include:
</p>
<ul><li>Certain <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystals</a>, typically doped with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rare_earth_element" title="Rare earth element">rare-earth</a> <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Neodymium" title="Neodymium">neodymium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ytterbium" title="Ytterbium">ytterbium</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Erbium" title="Erbium">erbium</a>) or <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metal</a> ions (<a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a>); most often <a href="/wiki/Yttrium_aluminium_garnet" title="Yttrium aluminium garnet">yttrium aluminium garnet</a> (<a href="/wiki/Yttrium" title="Yttrium">Y</a><sub>3</sub><a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">Al</a><sub>5</sub><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a><sub>12</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Yttrium_orthovanadate" title="Yttrium orthovanadate">yttrium orthovanadate</a> (YVO<sub>4</sub>), or <a href="/wiki/Sapphire" title="Sapphire">sapphire</a> (Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and not often <a href="/wiki/Caesium_cadmium_bromide" title="Caesium cadmium bromide">Caesium cadmium bromide</a> (<a href="/wiki/Caesium" title="Caesium">Cs</a><a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">Cd</a><a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">Br</a><sub>3</sub>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">Glasses</a>, e.g. silicate or phosphate glasses, doped with laser-active ions;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">Gases</a>, e.g. mixtures of <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neon" title="Neon">neon</a> (HeNe), <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, or metal vapors;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">Semiconductors</a>, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Gallium_arsenide" title="Gallium arsenide">gallium arsenide</a> (GaAs), <a href="/wiki/Indium_gallium_arsenide" title="Indium gallium arsenide">indium gallium arsenide</a> (InGaAs), or <a href="/wiki/Gallium_nitride" title="Gallium nitride">gallium nitride</a> (GaN).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>Liquids, in the form of dye solutions as used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dye_lasers" title="Dye lasers">dye lasers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>In order to fire a laser, the active gain medium must be in a nonthermal energy distribution known as a <a href="/wiki/Population_inversion" title="Population inversion">population inversion</a>.  The preparation of this state requires an external energy source and is known as <a href="/wiki/Laser_pumping" title="Laser pumping">laser pumping</a>.  Pumping may be achieved with electrical currents (e.g. semiconductors, or gases via <a href="/wiki/Glow_discharge" title="Glow discharge">high-voltage discharges</a>) or with light, generated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discharge_lamp" title="Discharge lamp">discharge lamps</a> or by other lasers (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semiconductor_laser" title="Semiconductor laser">semiconductor lasers</a>).  More exotic gain media can be pumped by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_reactions" title="Chemical reactions">chemical reactions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">nuclear fission</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> or with high-energy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_beam" title="Electron beam">electron beams</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rp_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-rp-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Example_of_a_model_of_gain_medium">Example of a model of gain medium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Active_laser_medium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Example of a model of gain medium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LaserLevels1.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="660" height="303" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/LaserLevels1.png/200px-LaserLevels1.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/LaserLevels1.png/300px-LaserLevels1.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/LaserLevels1.png/400px-LaserLevels1.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LaserLevels1.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fig.1. Simplified scheme of levels a gain medium</div></div></div>
<p>An universal model valid for all laser types does not exist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siegman_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-siegman-8">[8]</a></sup> 
The simplest model includes two systems of sub-levels: upper and lower.  Within each sub-level system, the fast transitions ensure that thermal equilibrium is reached quickly, leading to the <a href="/wiki/Maxwell%E2%80%93Boltzmann_statistics" title="Maxwell–Boltzmann statistics">Maxwell–Boltzmann statistics</a> of excitations among sub-levels in each system <i>(fig.1)</i>.  The upper level is assumed to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metastable" title="Metastable">metastable</a>. Also, gain and refractive index are assumed independent of a particular way of excitation.
</p><p>For good performance of the gain medium, the separation between sub-levels should be larger than working temperature; then, at pump frequency <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\displaystyle ~\omega _{\rm {p}}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

</p><p>In the case of <a href="/wiki/Amplifier" title="Amplifier">amplification</a> of optical signals, the lasing frequency is called <i>signal frequency.</i> However, the same term is used even in the laser <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oscillators" title="Oscillators">oscillators</a>, when amplified radiation is used to transfer energy rather than information.
