<p><b>Gas</b> is one of <a href="/wiki/State_of_matter" title="State of matter">the four fundamental states of matter</a> (the others being <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a>). A pure gas may be made up of individual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atoms" title="Atoms">atoms</a> (e.g. a <a href="/wiki/Noble_gas" title="Noble gas">noble gas</a> like <a href="/wiki/Neon" title="Neon">neon</a>), <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">elemental</a> molecules made from one type of atom (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>), or <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">compound</a> molecules made from a variety of atoms (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>). A gas <a href="/wiki/Mixture" title="Mixture">mixture</a> would contain a variety of pure gases much like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">air</a>. What distinguishes a gas from liquids and solids is the vast separation of the individual gas particles. This separation usually makes a colorless gas invisible to the human observer. The interaction of gas particles in the presence of electric and <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_field" title="Gravitational field">gravitational fields</a> are considered negligible as indicated by the constant velocity vectors in the image.
</p><p>The gaseous state of matter is found between the liquid and plasma states,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> the latter of which provides the upper temperature boundary for gases. Bounding the lower end of the temperature scale lie degenerative quantum gases<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> which are gaining increasing attention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> High-density atomic gases super cooled to incredibly low temperatures are classified by their statistical behavior as either a <a href="/wiki/Bose_gas" title="Bose gas">Bose gas</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Fermi_gas" title="Fermi gas">Fermi gas</a>. For a comprehensive listing of these exotic states of matter see <a href="/wiki/List_of_states_of_matter" title="List of states of matter">list of states of matter</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Elemental_gases">Elemental gases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Elemental gases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The only <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_elements" title="Chemical elements">chemical elements</a> that are stable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diatomic" title="Diatomic">diatomic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homonuclear" title="Homonuclear">homonuclear</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecules" title="Molecules">molecules</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard temperature and pressure">STP</a> are <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> (H<sub>2</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> (N<sub>2</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> (O<sub>2</sub>), and two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halogens" title="Halogens">halogens</a>: <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a> (F<sub>2</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> (Cl<sub>2</sub>). When grouped together with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monatomic" title="Monatomic">monatomic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noble_gases" title="Noble gases">noble gases</a> – <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> (He), <a href="/wiki/Neon" title="Neon">neon</a> (Ne), <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a> (Ar), <a href="/wiki/Krypton" title="Krypton">krypton</a> (Kr), <a href="/wiki/Xenon" title="Xenon">xenon</a> (Xe), and <a href="/wiki/Radon" title="Radon">radon</a> (Rn) – these gases are called "elemental gases".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-style ambox-Cleanup" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" data-file-height="48" data-file-width="48" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/40px-Edit-clear.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/60px-Edit-clear.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/80px-Edit-clear.svg.png 2x" width="40"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This article may <b>require <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Cleanup" title="Wikipedia:Cleanup">cleanup</a></b> to meet Wikipedia's <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style">quality standards</a>. The specific problem is: <b>"pronounced like ch in "loch"" -- and how is that ch pronounced (IPA?)? Sources give "[lɒk]" and "[lɒx]" as IPA for loch. Is it [<a href="/wiki/Voiceless_velar_fricative" title="Voiceless velar fricative">x</a>]?</b><span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Gas&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> if you can.</span> <small><i>(March 2018)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The word <i>gas</i> was first used by the early 17th-century <a href="/wiki/Flemish_people" title="Flemish people">Flemish</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">chemist</a> <a href="/wiki/Jan_Baptist_van_Helmont" title="Jan Baptist van Helmont">Jan Baptist van Helmont</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He identified <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, the first known gas other than air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ley196606_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ley196606-5">[5]</a></sup> Van Helmont's word appears to have been simply a phonetic transcription of the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> word χάος <i><a href="/wiki/Chaos_(cosmogony)#Terminology" title="Chaos (cosmogony)">Chaos</a></i> – the <i>g</i> in Dutch being pronounced like <i>ch</i> in "loch" – in which case Van Helmont was simply following the established <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">alchemical</a> usage first attested in the works of <a href="/wiki/Paracelsus" title="Paracelsus">Paracelsus</a>. According to Paracelsus's terminology, <i>chaos</i> meant something like "ultra-rarefied water".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-et_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-et-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>An alternative story<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> is that Van Helmont's word is corrupted from <i>gahst</i> (or <i>geist</i>), signifying a ghost or spirit. This was because certain gases suggested a supernatural origin, such as from their ability to cause death, extinguish flames, and to occur in "mines, bottom of wells, churchyards and other lonely places".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">Physical characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Purplesmoke.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Purplesmoke.jpg/220px-Purplesmoke.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Purplesmoke.jpg/330px-Purplesmoke.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Purplesmoke.jpg/440px-Purplesmoke.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Purplesmoke.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Drifting <a href="/wiki/Smoke" title="Smoke">smoke</a> particles provide clues to the movement of the surrounding gas.</div></div></div>
<p>Because most gases are difficult to observe directly, they are described through the use of four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_properties" title="Physical properties">physical properties</a> or <a href="/wiki/Macroscopic_scale" title="Macroscopic scale">macroscopic</a> characteristics: <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volume_(thermodynamics)" title="Volume (thermodynamics)">volume</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Number_of_particles" title="Number of particles">number of particles</a> (chemists group them by <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">moles</a>) and temperature. These four characteristics were repeatedly observed by scientists such as <a href="/wiki/Robert_Boyle" title="Robert Boyle">Robert Boyle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Charles" title="Jacques Charles">Jacques Charles</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Dalton" title="John Dalton">John Dalton</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Gay-Lussac" title="Joseph Gay-Lussac">Joseph Gay-Lussac</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amedeo_Avogadro" title="Amedeo Avogadro">Amedeo Avogadro</a> for a variety of gases in various settings. Their detailed studies ultimately led to a mathematical relationship among these properties expressed by the <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas_law" title="Ideal gas law">ideal gas law</a> (see simplified models section below).
</p><p>Gas particles are widely separated from one another, and consequently, have weaker intermolecular bonds than liquids or solids. These <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intermolecular_forces" title="Intermolecular forces">intermolecular forces</a> result from electrostatic interactions between gas particles. Like-charged areas of different gas particles repel, while oppositely charged regions of different gas particles attract one another; gases that contain permanently charged <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ions" title="Ions">ions</a> are known as <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasmas</a>. Gaseous compounds with <a href="/wiki/Chemical_polarity" title="Chemical polarity">polar covalent</a> bonds contain permanent charge imbalances and so experience relatively strong intermolecular forces, although the molecule while the compound's net charge remains neutral.  Transient, randomly induced charges exist across non-polar <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">covalent bonds</a> of molecules and electrostatic interactions caused by them are referred to as <a href="/wiki/Van_der_Waals_force" title="Van der Waals force">Van der Waals forces</a>. The interaction of these intermolecular forces varies within a substance which determines many of the physical properties unique to each gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> A comparison of <i>boiling points</i> for compounds formed by ionic and covalent bonds leads us to this conclusion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The drifting smoke particles in the image provides some insight into low-pressure gas behavior.
</p><p>Compared to the other states of matter, gases have low <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> and <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a>. <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">Pressure</a> and temperature influence the particles within a certain volume. This variation in particle separation and speed is referred to as <i>compressibility</i>. This particle separation and size influences optical properties of gases as can be found in the following <a href="/wiki/List_of_refractive_indices" title="List of refractive indices">list of refractive indices</a>. Finally, gas particles spread apart or <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">diffuse</a> in order to homogeneously distribute themselves throughout any container.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Macroscopic">Macroscopic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Macroscopic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CFD_Shuttle.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="387" data-file-width="516" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/CFD_Shuttle.jpg/220px-CFD_Shuttle.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/CFD_Shuttle.jpg/330px-CFD_Shuttle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/CFD_Shuttle.jpg/440px-CFD_Shuttle.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CFD_Shuttle.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Shuttle imagery of re-entry phase</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Gas_kinetics" title="Gas kinetics">Gas kinetics</a></div>
<p>When observing a gas, it is typical to specify a frame of reference or <a href="/wiki/Length_scale" title="Length scale">length scale</a>. A <i>larger</i> length scale corresponds to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macroscopic" title="Macroscopic">macroscopic</a> or global point of view of the gas. This region (referred to as a volume) must be sufficient in size to contain a large sampling of gas particles. The resulting statistical analysis of this sample size produces the <b>"average"</b> behavior (i.e. velocity, temperature or pressure) of all the gas particles within the region. In contrast, a <i>smaller</i> length scale corresponds to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microscopic" title="Microscopic">microscopic</a> or particle point of view.
</p><p>Macroscopically, the gas characteristics measured are either in terms of the gas particles themselves (velocity, pressure, or temperature) or their surroundings (volume). For example, Robert Boyle studied <a href="/wiki/Pneumatic_chemistry" title="Pneumatic chemistry">pneumatic chemistry</a> for a small portion of his career. One of his experiments related the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macroscopic" title="Macroscopic">macroscopic</a> properties of pressure and volume of a gas. His experiment used a J-tube <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manometer" title="Manometer">manometer</a> which looks like a <a href="/wiki/Test_tube" title="Test tube">test tube</a> in the shape of the letter J. Boyle trapped an <a href="/wiki/Inert_gas" title="Inert gas">inert</a> gas in the closed end of the test tube with a column of <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>, thereby making the number of particles and the temperature constant. He observed that when the pressure was increased in the gas, by adding more mercury to the column, the trapped gas' volume decreased (this is known as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inverse_proportion" title="Inverse proportion">inverse</a> relationship). Furthermore, when Boyle multiplied the pressure and volume of each observation, the <a href="/wiki/Product_(mathematics)" title="Product (mathematics)">product</a> was constant. This relationship held for every gas that Boyle observed leading to the law, (PV=k), named to honor his work in this field.
</p><p>There are many mathematical tools available for analyzing gas properties. As gases are subjected to extreme conditions, these tools become more complex, from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euler_equations" title="Euler equations">Euler equations</a>  for inviscid flow to the <a href="/wiki/Navier%E2%80%93Stokes_equations" title="Navier–Stokes equations">Navier–Stokes equations</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> that fully account for viscous effects. These equations are adapted to the conditions of the gas system in question. Boyle's lab equipment allowed the use of <a href="/wiki/Algebra" title="Algebra">algebra</a> to obtain his analytical results. His results were possible because he was studying gases in relatively low pressure situations where they behaved in an "ideal" manner. These ideal relationships apply to safety calculations for a variety of flight conditions on the materials in use. The high technology equipment in use today was designed to help us safely explore the more exotic operating environments where the gases no longer behave in an "ideal" manner. This advanced math, including statistics and <a href="/wiki/Multivariable_calculus" title="Multivariable calculus">multivariable calculus</a>, makes possible the solution to such complex dynamic situations as space vehicle reentry. An example is the analysis of the space shuttle reentry pictured to ensure the material properties under this loading condition are appropriate.  In this flight regime, the gas is no longer behaving ideally.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pressure">Pressure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pressure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">Pressure</a></div>
<p>The symbol used to represent <i>pressure</i> in equations is <b>"p"</b> or <b>"P"</b> with SI units of <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">pascals</a>.
</p><p>When describing a container of gas, the term <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> (or absolute pressure) refers to the average force per unit area that the gas exerts on the surface of the container. Within this volume, it is sometimes easier to visualize the gas particles moving in straight lines until they collide with the container (see diagram at top of the article). The force imparted by a gas particle into the container during this collision is the change in <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a> of the particle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> During a collision only the <a href="/wiki/Normal_(geometry)" title="Normal (geometry)">normal</a> component of velocity changes. A particle traveling parallel to the wall does not change its momentum. Therefore, the average force on a surface must be the average change in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear_momentum" title="Linear momentum">linear momentum</a> from all of these gas particle collisions.
</p><p>Pressure is the sum of all the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Normal_component" title="Normal component">normal components</a> of force exerted by the particles impacting the walls of the container divided by the surface area of the wall.
</p>

</p><p>The speed of a gas particle is proportional to its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absolute_temperature" title="Absolute temperature">absolute temperature</a>. The volume of the balloon in the video shrinks when the trapped gas particles slow down with the addition of extremely cold nitrogen. The temperature of any <a href="/wiki/Physical_system" title="Physical system">physical system</a> is related to the motions of the particles (molecules and atoms) which make up the [gas] system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Statistical_mechanics" title="Statistical mechanics">statistical mechanics</a>, temperature is the measure of the average kinetic energy stored in a particle. The methods of storing this energy are dictated by the <a href="/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom_(physics_and_chemistry)" title="Degrees of freedom (physics and chemistry)">degrees of freedom</a> of the particle itself (<a href="/wiki/Energy_level#Molecules" title="Energy level">energy modes</a>). Kinetic energy added (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endothermic" title="Endothermic">endothermic</a> process) to gas particles by way of collisions produces linear, rotational, and vibrational motion. In contrast, a molecule in a solid can only increase its vibrational modes with the addition of heat as the lattice crystal structure prevents both linear and rotational motions. These heated gas molecules have a greater speed range which constantly varies due to constant collisions with other particles. The speed range can be described by the <a href="/wiki/Maxwell%E2%80%93Boltzmann_distribution" title="Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution">Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution</a>. Use of this distribution implies <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas" title="Ideal gas">ideal gases</a> near <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium" title="Thermodynamic equilibrium">thermodynamic equilibrium</a> for the system of particles being considered.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Specific_volume">Specific volume</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Specific volume">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Specific_volume" title="Specific volume">Specific volume</a></div>
<p>The symbol used to represent <i>specific volume</i> in equations is <b>"v"</b> with SI units of cubic meters per kilogram.
</p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_volume" title="Gas volume">Gas volume</a></div>
<p>The symbol used to represent <b>volume</b> in equations is <b>"V"</b> with SI units of cubic meters.
</p><p>When performing a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermodynamic" title="Thermodynamic">thermodynamic</a> analysis, it is typical to speak of <a href="/wiki/Intensive_and_extensive_properties" title="Intensive and extensive properties">intensive and extensive properties</a>. Properties which depend on the amount of gas (either by mass or volume) are called <i>extensive</i> properties, while properties that do not depend on the amount of gas are called <i>intensive</i> properties. <b>Specific volume</b> is an example of an <i>intensive</i> property because it is the ratio of volume occupied by a <i>unit of mass</i> of a gas that is identical throughout a system at equilibrium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> 1000 atoms a gas occupy the same space as any other 1000 atoms for any given temperature and pressure. This concept is easier to visualize for solids such as iron which are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incompressible" title="Incompressible">incompressible</a> compared to gases.  Since a gas fills any container in which it is placed, <b>volume</b> is an <i>extensive property</i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Density">Density</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Density">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">Density</a></div>
<p>The symbol used to represent <b>density</b> in equations is <b>ρ</b> (rho) with SI units of kilograms per cubic meter. This term is the <a href="/wiki/Multiplicative_inverse" title="Multiplicative inverse">reciprocal</a> of specific volume.
</p><p>Since gas molecules can move freely within a container, their mass is normally characterized by <b>density</b>. Density is the amount of mass per unit volume of a substance, or the inverse of specific volume. For gases, the density can vary over a wide range because the particles are free to move closer together when constrained by pressure or volume. This variation of density is referred to as <a href="/wiki/Compressibility" title="Compressibility">compressibility</a>. Like pressure and temperature, density is a <a href="/wiki/State_variable" title="State variable">state variable</a> of a gas and the change in density during any process is governed by the laws of thermodynamics. For a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluid_statics" title="Fluid statics">static gas</a>, the density is the same throughout the entire container. Density is therefore a <a href="/wiki/Scalar_(physics)" title="Scalar (physics)">scalar quantity</a>. It can be shown by <b>kinetic theory</b> that the density is <i>inversely</i> proportional to the size of the container in which a fixed mass of gas is confined. In this case of a fixed mass, the density decreases as the volume increases.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Microscopic">Microscopic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Microscopic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>If one could observe a gas under a powerful microscope, one would see a collection of particles (molecules, atoms, ions, electrons, etc.) without any definite shape or volume that are in more or less random motion. These neutral gas particles only change direction when they collide with another particle or with the sides of the container. In an ideal gas, these collisions are perfectly elastic. This particle or <a href="/wiki/Microscopic_scale" title="Microscopic scale">microscopic</a> view of a gas is described by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kinetic-molecular_theory" title="Kinetic-molecular theory">Kinetic-molecular theory</a>. The assumptions behind this theory can be found in the postulates section of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kinetic_Theory" title="Kinetic Theory">Kinetic Theory</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kinetic_theory">Kinetic theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Kinetic theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases" title="Kinetic theory of gases">Kinetic theory of gases</a></div>
<p><b>Kinetic theory</b> provides insight into the macroscopic properties of gases by considering their molecular composition and motion. Starting with the definitions of <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> one can use the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_law_(physics)" title="Conservation law (physics)">conservation of momentum</a> and geometric relationships of a cube to relate macroscopic system properties of temperature and pressure to the microscopic property of kinetic energy per molecule. The theory provides averaged values for these two properties.
</p><p>The theory also explains how the gas system responds to change. For example, as a gas is heated from absolute zero, when it is (in theory) perfectly still, its <a href="/wiki/Internal_energy" title="Internal energy">internal energy</a> (temperature) is increased. As a gas is heated, the particles speed up and its temperature rises. This results in greater numbers of collisions with the container per unit time due to the higher particle speeds associated with elevated temperatures.  The pressure increases in proportion to the number of collisions per unit time.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brownian_motion">Brownian motion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Brownian motion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Diffusion_animation.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="344" data-file-width="400" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Diffusion_animation.gif/220px-Diffusion_animation.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Diffusion_animation.gif/330px-Diffusion_animation.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fe/Diffusion_animation.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Diffusion_animation.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Random motion of gas particles results in <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">diffusion</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Brownian_motion" title="Brownian motion">Brownian motion</a></div>
<p>Brownian motion is the mathematical model used to describe the random movement of particles suspended in a fluid. The gas particle animation, using pink and green particles, illustrates how this behavior results in the spreading out of gases (<a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a>). These events are also described by <a href="/wiki/Particle" title="Particle">particle theory</a>.
</p><p>Since it is at the limit of (or beyond) current technology to observe individual gas particles (atoms or molecules), only theoretical calculations give suggestions about how they move, but their motion is different from Brownian motion because Brownian motion involves a smooth drag due to the frictional force of many gas molecules, punctuated by violent collisions of an individual (or several) gas molecule(s) with the particle. The particle (generally consisting of millions or billions of atoms) thus moves in a jagged course, yet not so jagged as would be expected if an individual gas molecule were examined.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intermolecular_forces">Intermolecular forces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Intermolecular forces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:3D_model_hydrogen_bonds_in_water.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="397" data-file-width="400" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/3D_model_hydrogen_bonds_in_water.svg/220px-3D_model_hydrogen_bonds_in_water.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/3D_model_hydrogen_bonds_in_water.svg/330px-3D_model_hydrogen_bonds_in_water.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/3D_model_hydrogen_bonds_in_water.svg/440px-3D_model_hydrogen_bonds_in_water.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:3D_model_hydrogen_bonds_in_water.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>When gases are compressed, intermolecular forces like those shown here start to play a more active role.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Van_der_Waals_force" title="Van der Waals force">van der Waals force</a> and <a href="/wiki/Intermolecular_force" title="Intermolecular force">Intermolecular force</a></div>
<p>As discussed earlier, momentary attractions (or repulsions) between particles have an effect on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_dynamics" title="Gas dynamics">gas dynamics</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Physical_chemistry" title="Physical chemistry">physical chemistry</a>, the name given to these intermolecular forces is <i>van der Waals force</i>. These forces play a key role in determining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_properties" title="Physical properties">physical properties</a> of a gas such as <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mass_flow_rate" title="Mass flow rate">flow rate</a> (see physical characteristics section). Ignoring these forces in certain conditions allows a <a href="/wiki/Real_gas" title="Real gas">real gas</a> to be treated like an <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas" title="Ideal gas">ideal gas</a>. This assumption allows the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ideal_gas_laws" title="Ideal gas laws">ideal gas laws</a> which greatly simplifies calculations.
</p><p>Proper use of these gas relationships requires the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kinetic-molecular_theory" title="Kinetic-molecular theory">kinetic-molecular theory</a> (KMT). When gas particles experience <a href="/wiki/Intermolecular_force" title="Intermolecular force">intermolecular forces</a> they gradually influence one another as the spacing between them is reduced (the hydrogen bond model illustrates one example). In the absence of any charge, at some point when the spacing between gas particles is greatly reduced they can no longer avoid collisions between themselves at normal gas temperatures. Another case for increased collisions among gas particles would include a fixed volume of gas, which upon heating would contain very fast particles. This means that these ideal equations provide reasonable results except for extremely high pressure (compressible) or high temperature (ionized) conditions. All of these excepted conditions allow energy transfer to take place within the gas system. The absence of these internal transfers is what is referred to as ideal conditions in which the energy exchange occurs only at the boundaries of the system. Real gases experience some of these collisions and intermolecular forces. When these collisions are statistically negligible (incompressible), results from these ideal equations are still meaningful. If the gas particles are compressed into close proximity they behave more like a liquid (see <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">fluid dynamics</a>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Simplified_models">Simplified models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Simplified models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Equation_of_state" title="Equation of state">Equation of state</a></div>
<p>An <i>equation of state</i> (for gases) is a mathematical model used to roughly describe or predict the state properties of a gas. At present, there is no single equation of state that accurately predicts the properties of all gases under all conditions. Therefore, a number of much more accurate equations of state have been developed for gases in specific temperature and pressure ranges. The "gas models" that are most widely discussed are "perfect gas", "ideal gas" and "real gas". Each of these models has its own set of assumptions to facilitate the analysis of a given thermodynamic system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Each successive model expands the temperature range of coverage to which it applies.
</p>

</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Real_gas">Real gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Real gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MountRedoubtEruption.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="689" data-file-width="1024" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/MountRedoubtEruption.jpg/220px-MountRedoubtEruption.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/MountRedoubtEruption.jpg/330px-MountRedoubtEruption.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/MountRedoubtEruption.jpg/440px-MountRedoubtEruption.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MountRedoubtEruption.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>21 April 1990 eruption of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Redoubt" title="Mount Redoubt">Mount Redoubt</a>, Alaska, illustrating real gases not in thermodynamic equilibrium.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Real_gas" title="Real gas">Real gas</a></div>
<p>Each one of the assumptions listed below adds to the complexity of the problem's solution. As the density of a gas increases with rising pressure, the intermolecular forces play a more substantial role in gas behavior which results in the ideal gas law no longer providing "reasonable" results. At the upper end of the engine temperature ranges (e.g. combustor sections – 1300 K), the complex fuel particles absorb internal energy by means of rotations and vibrations that cause their specific heats to vary from those of diatomic molecules and noble gases. At more than double that temperature, electronic excitation and dissociation of the gas particles begins to occur causing the pressure to adjust to a greater number of particles (transition from gas to <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Finally, all of the thermodynamic processes were presumed to describe uniform gases whose velocities varied according to a fixed distribution. Using a non-equilibrium situation implies the flow field must be characterized in some manner to enable a solution. One of the first attempts to expand the boundaries of the ideal gas law was to include coverage for different <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_process" title="Thermodynamic process">thermodynamic processes</a> by adjusting the equation to read <i>pV<sup>n</sup> = constant</i> and then varying the <i>n</i> through different values such as the <a href="/wiki/Heat_capacity_ratio" title="Heat capacity ratio">specific heat ratio</a>, <i>γ</i>.
</p><p><b>Real gas effects</b> include those adjustments made to account for a greater range of gas behavior:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Compressibility_factor" title="Compressibility factor">Compressibility effects</a> (<i>Z</i> allowed to vary from 1.0)</li>
<li>Variable <a href="/wiki/Heat_capacity" title="Heat capacity">heat capacity</a> (specific heats vary with temperature)</li>
<li>Van der Waals forces (related to compressibility, can substitute other equations of state)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Non-equilibrium_thermodynamics" title="Non-equilibrium thermodynamics">Non-equilibrium thermodynamic effects</a></li>
<li>Issues with molecular <a href="/wiki/Dissociation_(chemistry)" title="Dissociation (chemistry)">dissociation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elementary_reaction" title="Elementary reaction">elementary reactions</a> with variable composition.</li></ul>
<p>For most applications, such a detailed analysis is excessive. Examples where real gas effects would have a significant impact would be on the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_reentry" title="Atmospheric reentry">re-entry</a> where extremely high temperatures and pressures were present or the gases produced during geological events as in the image of the 1990 eruption of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Redoubt" title="Mount Redoubt">Mount Redoubt</a>.
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Boyle_air_pump.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1116" data-file-width="706" height="269" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Boyle_air_pump.jpg/170px-Boyle_air_pump.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Boyle_air_pump.jpg/255px-Boyle_air_pump.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Boyle_air_pump.jpg/340px-Boyle_air_pump.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Boyle_air_pump.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Boyle's equipment</div></div></div>

<p>Boyle's Law was perhaps the first expression of an equation of state. In 1662 <a href="/wiki/Robert_Boyle" title="Robert Boyle">Robert Boyle</a> performed a series of experiments employing a J-shaped glass tube, which was sealed on one end. Mercury was added to the tube, trapping a fixed quantity of air in the short, sealed end of the tube. Then the volume of gas was carefully measured as additional mercury was added to the tube. The pressure of the gas could be determined by the difference between the mercury level in the short end of the tube and that in the long, open end. The image of Boyle's Equipment shows some of the exotic tools used by Boyle during his study of gases.
</p><p>Through these experiments, Boyle noted that the pressure exerted by a gas held at a constant temperature varies inversely with the volume of the gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>  For example, if the volume is halved, the pressure is doubled; and if the volume is doubled, the pressure is halved. <h3><span id="Avogadro.27s_law"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Avogadro's_law">Avogadro's law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Avogadro's law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Avogadro%27s_law" title="Avogadro's law">Avogadro's law</a></div>
<p>In 1811, Amedeo Avogadro verified that equal volumes of pure gases contain the same number of particles. His theory was not generally accepted until 1858 when another Italian chemist Stanislao Cannizzaro was able to explain non-ideal exceptions. For his work with gases a century prior, the number that bears his name <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avogadro%27s_constant" title="Avogadro's constant">Avogadro's constant</a> represents the number of atoms found in 12 grams of elemental carbon-12 (6.022×10<sup>23</sup> mol<sup>−1</sup>). This specific number of gas particles, at standard temperature and pressure (ideal gas law) occupies 22.40 liters, which is referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Molar_volume" title="Molar volume">molar volume</a>.
</p><p>Avogadro's law states that the volume occupied by an ideal gas is proportional to the number of <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">moles</a> (or molecules) present in the container. <h3><span id="Dalton.27s_law"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Dalton's_law">Dalton's law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Dalton's law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Daltons_symbols.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="928" data-file-width="1067" height="191" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Daltons_symbols.gif/220px-Daltons_symbols.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Daltons_symbols.gif/330px-Daltons_symbols.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Daltons_symbols.gif/440px-Daltons_symbols.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Daltons_symbols.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/John_Dalton" title="John Dalton">Dalton</a>'s notation.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dalton%27s_law" title="Dalton's law">Dalton's law</a></div>
<p>In 1801, <a href="/wiki/John_Dalton" title="John Dalton">John Dalton</a> published the <b>Law of Partial Pressures</b> from his work with ideal gas law relationship: The pressure of a mixture of non reactive gases is equal to the sum of the pressures of all of the constituent gases alone. Mathematically, this can be represented for <i>n</i> species as:
</p>
<dl><dd><b>Pressure<sub>total</sub> = Pressure<sub>1</sub> + Pressure<sub>2</sub> + ... + Pressure<sub><i>n</i></sub></b></dd></dl>
<p>The image of Dalton's journal depicts symbology he used as shorthand to record the path he followed. Among his key journal observations upon mixing unreactive "elastic fluids" (gases) were the following:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Unlike liquids, heavier gases did not drift to the bottom upon mixing.</li>
<li>Gas particle identity played no role in determining final pressure (they behaved as if their size was negligible).</li></ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Compressibility">Compressibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Compressibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Compressibility_Factor_of_Air_75-200_K.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="731" data-file-width="1240" height="130" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Compressibility_Factor_of_Air_75-200_K.png/220px-Compressibility_Factor_of_Air_75-200_K.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Compressibility_Factor_of_Air_75-200_K.png/330px-Compressibility_Factor_of_Air_75-200_K.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Compressibility_Factor_of_Air_75-200_K.png/440px-Compressibility_Factor_of_Air_75-200_K.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Compressibility_Factor_of_Air_75-200_K.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Compressibility factors for air.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Compressibility_factor" title="Compressibility factor">Compressibility factor</a></div>
<p>Thermodynamicists use this factor (<i>Z</i>) to alter the ideal gas equation to account for compressibility effects of real gases. This factor represents the ratio of actual to ideal specific volumes. It is sometimes referred to as a "fudge-factor" or correction to expand the useful range of the ideal gas law for design purposes. <i>Usually</i> this <i>Z</i> value is very close to unity. The compressibility factor image illustrates how Z varies over a range of very cold temperatures.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reynolds_number">Reynolds number</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Reynolds number">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Reynolds_number" title="Reynolds number">Reynolds number</a></div>
<p>In fluid mechanics, the Reynolds number is the ratio of inertial forces (<i>v<sub>s</sub>ρ</i>) to viscous forces (<i>μ/L</i>). It is one of the most important dimensionless numbers in fluid dynamics and is used, usually along with other dimensionless numbers, to provide a criterion for determining dynamic similitude. As such, the Reynolds number provides the link between modeling results (design) and the full-scale actual conditions. It can also be used to characterize the flow.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Viscosity">Viscosity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Viscosity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vortex-street-1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4574" data-file-width="2549" height="305" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Vortex-street-1.jpg/170px-Vortex-street-1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Vortex-street-1.jpg/255px-Vortex-street-1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Vortex-street-1.jpg/340px-Vortex-street-1.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vortex-street-1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Satellite view of weather pattern in vicinity of <a href="/wiki/Juan_Fern%C3%A1ndez_Islands" title="Juan Fernández Islands">Robinson Crusoe Islands</a> on 15 September 1999, shows a turbulent cloud pattern called a <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_vortex_street" title="Kármán vortex street">Kármán vortex street</a></div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">Viscosity</a></div>
<p>Viscosity, a physical property, is a measure of how well adjacent molecules stick to one another. A solid can withstand a shearing force due to the strength of these sticky intermolecular forces. A fluid will continuously deform when subjected to a similar load. While a gas has a lower value of viscosity than a liquid, it is still an observable property. If gases had no viscosity, then they would not stick to the surface of a wing and form a boundary layer. A study of the <a href="/wiki/Delta_wing" title="Delta wing">delta wing</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Schlieren_photography" title="Schlieren photography">Schlieren</a> image reveals that the gas particles stick to one another (see Boundary layer section).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Turbulence">Turbulence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Turbulence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Schlierenfoto_Mach_17_Delta_-_NASA.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="452" data-file-width="394" height="195" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Schlierenfoto_Mach_17_Delta_-_NASA.jpg/170px-Schlierenfoto_Mach_17_Delta_-_NASA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Schlierenfoto_Mach_17_Delta_-_NASA.jpg/255px-Schlierenfoto_Mach_17_Delta_-_NASA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Schlierenfoto_Mach_17_Delta_-_NASA.jpg/340px-Schlierenfoto_Mach_17_Delta_-_NASA.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Schlierenfoto_Mach_17_Delta_-_NASA.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Delta_wing" title="Delta wing">Delta wing</a> in wind tunnel. The shadows form as the indices of refraction change within the gas as it compresses on the leading edge of this wing.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">Turbulence</a></div>
<p>In fluid dynamics, <b>turbulence</b> or turbulent flow is a flow regime characterized by chaotic, stochastic property changes. This includes low momentum diffusion, high momentum convection, and rapid variation of pressure and velocity in space and time. The satellite view of weather around Robinson Crusoe Islands illustrates one example.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Boundary_layer">Boundary layer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Boundary layer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Boundary_layer" title="Boundary layer">Boundary layer</a></div>
<p>Particles will, in effect, "stick" to the surface of an object moving through it. This layer of particles is called the <b>boundary layer</b>. At the surface of the object, it is essentially static due to the friction of the surface. The object, with its boundary layer is effectively the new shape of the object that the rest of the molecules "see" as the object approaches. This boundary layer <i>can</i> separate from the surface, essentially creating a new surface and completely changing the flow path. The classical example of this is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stall_(flight)#Formal_definition" title="Stall (flight)">stalling airfoil</a>. The delta wing image clearly shows the boundary layer thickening as the gas flows from right to left along the leading edge.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Maximum_entropy_principle">Maximum entropy principle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Maximum entropy principle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Principle_of_maximum_entropy" title="Principle of maximum entropy">Principle of maximum entropy</a></div>
<p>As the total number of degrees of freedom approaches infinity, the system will be found in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macrostate" title="Macrostate">macrostate</a> that corresponds to the highest <a href="/wiki/Multiplicity_(mathematics)" title="Multiplicity (mathematics)">multiplicity</a>. In order to illustrate this principle, observe the skin temperature of a frozen metal bar. Using a thermal image of the skin temperature, note the temperature distribution on the surface. This initial observation of temperature represents a "<a href="/wiki/Microstate" title="Microstate">microstate</a>". At some future time, a second observation of the skin temperature produces a second microstate. By continuing this observation process, it is possible to produce a series of microstates that illustrate the thermal history of the bar's surface. Characterization of this historical series of microstates is possible by choosing the macrostate that successfully classifies them all into a single grouping.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermodynamic_equilibrium">Thermodynamic equilibrium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Thermodynamic equilibrium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium" title="Thermodynamic equilibrium">Thermodynamic equilibrium</a></div>
<p>When energy transfer ceases from a system, this condition is referred to as thermodynamic equilibrium. Usually this condition implies the system and surroundings are at the same temperature so that heat no longer transfers between them. It also implies that external forces are balanced (volume does not change), and all chemical reactions within the system are complete. The timeline varies for these events depending on the system in question. A container of ice allowed to melt at room temperature takes hours, while in semiconductors the heat transfer that occurs in the device transition from an on to off state could be on the order of a few nanoseconds.
</p>
