<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Transparency and translucency</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Translucence_(disambiguation)" title="Translucence (disambiguation)">Translucence (disambiguation)</a>, <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Transparency_(disambiguation)" title="Transparency (disambiguation)">Transparency (disambiguation)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transparent_(TV_series)" title="Transparent (TV series)">Transparent (TV series)</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Translucent_(manga)" title="Translucent (manga)">Translucent (manga)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dichroic_filters.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="411" data-file-width="627" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Dichroic_filters.jpg/220px-Dichroic_filters.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Dichroic_filters.jpg/330px-Dichroic_filters.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Dichroic_filters.jpg/440px-Dichroic_filters.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dichroic_filters.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Dichroic_filter" title="Dichroic filter">Dichroic filters</a> are created using optically transparent materials.</div></div></div>
<p>In the field of <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">optics</a>, <b>transparency</b> (also called <b>pellucidity</b> or <b>diaphaneity</b>) is the <a href="/wiki/Physical_property" title="Physical property">physical property</a> of allowing light to pass through the material without being scattered. On a macroscopic scale (one where the dimensions investigated are much, much larger than the wavelength of the <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a> in question), the photons can be said to follow <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snell%27s_Law" title="Snell's Law">Snell's Law</a>. <b>Translucency</b> (also called <b>translucence</b> or <b>translucidity</b>) is a superset of transparency: it allows light to pass through, but does not necessarily (again, on the macroscopic scale) follow Snell's law; the photons can be scattered at either of the two interfaces, or internally, where there is a change in index of <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">refraction</a>. In other words, a translucent medium allows the transport of light while a transparent medium not only allows the transport of light but allows for image formation. Transparent materials appear clear, with the overall appearance of one color, or any combination leading up to a brilliant <a href="/wiki/Spectrum" title="Spectrum">spectrum</a> of every color. The opposite property of translucency is <a href="/wiki/Opacity_(optics)" title="Opacity (optics)">opacity</a>.
</p><p>When light encounters a material, it can interact with it in several different ways. These interactions depend on the <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> of the light and the nature of the material. Photons interact with an object by some combination of reflection, absorption and transmission.
Some materials, such as <a href="/wiki/Plate_glass" title="Plate glass">plate glass</a> and clean <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, transmit much of the light that falls on them and reflect little of it; such materials are called optically transparent. Many liquids and aqueous solutions are highly transparent. Absence of structural defects (voids, cracks, etc.) and molecular structure of most liquids are mostly responsible for excellent optical transmission.
</p><p>Materials which do not <a href="/wiki/Transmittance" title="Transmittance">transmit</a> light are called <a href="/wiki/Opacity_(optics)" title="Opacity (optics)">opaque</a>. Many such substances have a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_composition" title="Chemical composition">chemical composition</a> which includes what are referred to as <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(electromagnetic_radiation)" title="Absorption (electromagnetic radiation)">absorption</a> centers. Many substances are selective in their absorption of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_Light" title="Solar Light">white light</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequencies" title="Frequencies">frequencies</a>. They absorb certain portions of the <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible spectrum</a> while reflecting others. The frequencies of the spectrum which are not absorbed are either reflected or transmitted for our physical observation. This is what gives rise to <a href="/wiki/Color" title="Color">color</a>. The attenuation of light of all frequencies and wavelengths is due to the combined mechanisms of absorption and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_scattering_in_liquids_and_solids" title="Light scattering in liquids and solids">scattering</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Transparency can provide almost perfect <a href="/wiki/Camouflage" title="Camouflage">camouflage</a> for animals  able to achieve it. This is easier in dimly-lit or turbid <a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">seawater</a> than in good illumination. Many <a href="/wiki/Marine_biology" title="Marine biology">marine animals</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Jellyfish" title="Jellyfish">jellyfish</a> are highly transparent.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction">Introduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transparency_and_translucency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Introduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With regard to the absorption of light, primary material considerations include:
</p>
<ul><li>At the electronic level, absorption in the <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> and visible (UV-Vis) portions of the spectrum depends on whether the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_orbital" title="Atomic orbital">electron orbitals</a> are spaced (or "quantized") such that they can absorb a <a href="/wiki/Quantum" title="Quantum">quantum</a> of light (or <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a>) of a specific <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a>, and does not violate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Selection_rules" title="Selection rules">selection rules</a>. For example, in most glasses, electrons have no available energy levels above them in range of that associated with visible light, or if they do, they violate selection rules, meaning there is no appreciable absorption in pure (undoped) glasses, making them ideal transparent materials for windows in buildings.</li>
<li>At the atomic or molecular level, physical absorption in the infrared portion of the spectrum depends on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequencies" title="Frequencies">frequencies</a> of atomic or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_vibrations" title="Molecular vibrations">molecular vibrations</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_bonds" title="Chemical bonds">chemical bonds</a>, and on <a href="/wiki/Selection_rule" title="Selection rule">selection rules</a>. Nitrogen and oxygen are not greenhouse gases because there is no absorption because there is no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_dipole_moment" title="Molecular dipole moment">molecular dipole moment</a>.</li></ul>
<p>With regard to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_scattering_in_liquids_and_solids" title="Light scattering in liquids and solids">scattering of light</a>, the most critical factor is the length scale of any or all of these structural features relative to the wavelength of the light being scattered. Primary material considerations include:
</p>
<ul><li>Crystalline structure: whether or not the atoms or molecules exhibit the 'long-range order' evidenced in crystalline solids.</li>
<li>Glassy structure: scattering centers include fluctuations in density or composition.</li>
<li>Microstructure: scattering centers include internal surfaces such as grain boundaries, <a href="/wiki/Crystallographic_defect" title="Crystallographic defect">crystallographic defects</a> and microscopic pores.</li>
<li>Organic materials: scattering centers include fiber and cell structures and boundaries.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Light_scattering_in_solids">Light scattering in solids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transparency_and_translucency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Light scattering in solids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_scattering" title="Light scattering">Light scattering</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Diffuse_refl.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="683" data-file-width="660" height="259" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Diffuse_refl.gif/250px-Diffuse_refl.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Diffuse_refl.gif/375px-Diffuse_refl.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Diffuse_refl.gif/500px-Diffuse_refl.gif 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Diffuse_refl.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>General mechanism of <b>diffuse reflection</b></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Diffuse_reflection" title="Diffuse reflection">Diffuse reflection</a> - Generally, when light strikes the surface of a (non-metallic and non-glassy) solid material, it bounces off in all directions due to multiple reflections by the microscopic irregularities <i>inside</i> the material (e.g., the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grain_boundaries" title="Grain boundaries">grain boundaries</a> of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polycrystalline" title="Polycrystalline">polycrystalline</a> material, or the <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cell</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fiber" title="Fiber">fiber</a> boundaries of an organic material), and by its surface, if it is rough. Diffuse reflection is typically characterized by omni-directional reflection angles. Most of the objects visible to the naked eye are identified via diffuse reflection. Another term commonly used for this type of reflection is "light scattering". Light scattering from the surfaces of objects is our primary mechanism of physical observation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-z_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-z-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-y_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-y-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Light scattering in liquids and solids depends on the wavelength of the light being scattered. Limits to spatial scales of visibility (using white light) therefore arise, depending on the frequency of the light wave and the physical <a href="/wiki/Dimension" title="Dimension">dimension</a> (or spatial scale) of the scattering center. Visible light has a wavelength scale on the order of a half a <a href="/wiki/Micrometer" title="Micrometer">micrometer</a> (one millionth of a meter). Scattering centers (or particles) as small as one micrometer have been observed directly in the light <a href="/wiki/Microscope" title="Microscope">microscope</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Brownian_motion" title="Brownian motion">Brownian motion</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transparent_ceramics">Transparent ceramics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transparency_and_translucency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Transparent ceramics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Optical transparency in polycrystalline materials is limited by the amount of light which is scattered by their microstructural features. Light scattering depends on the wavelength of the light. Limits to spatial scales of visibility (using white light) therefore arise, depending on the frequency of the light wave and the physical dimension of the scattering center. For example, since visible light has a wavelength scale on the order of a micrometer, scattering centers will have dimensions on a similar spatial scale. Primary scattering centers in polycrystalline materials include microstructural defects such as pores and grain boundaries. In addition to pores, most of the interfaces in a typical metal or ceramic object are in the form of <a href="/wiki/Grain_boundary" title="Grain boundary">grain boundaries</a> which separate tiny regions of crystalline order. When the size of the scattering center (or grain boundary) is reduced below the size of the wavelength of the light being scattered, the scattering no longer occurs to any significant extent.
</p><p>In the formation of polycrystalline materials (metals and ceramics) the size of the crystalline grains is determined largely by the size of the crystalline particles present in the raw material during formation (or pressing) of the object. Moreover, the size of the grain boundaries scales directly with particle size. Thus a reduction of the original particle size well below the wavelength of visible light (about 1/15 of the light wavelength or roughly 600/15 = 40 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanometer" title="Nanometer">nanometers</a>) eliminates much of light scattering, resulting in a translucent or even transparent material.
</p><p>Computer modeling of light transmission through translucent ceramic alumina has shown that microscopic pores trapped near grain boundaries act as primary scattering centers. The volume fraction of porosity had to be reduced below 1% for high-quality optical transmission (99.99 percent of theoretical density). This goal has been readily accomplished and amply demonstrated in laboratories and research facilities worldwide using the emerging chemical processing methods encompassed by the methods of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sol-gel" title="Sol-gel">sol-gel</a> chemistry and <a href="/wiki/Nanotechnology" title="Nanotechnology">nanotechnology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Backlit_mushroom.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1104" data-file-width="1472" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Backlit_mushroom.jpg/220px-Backlit_mushroom.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Backlit_mushroom.jpg/330px-Backlit_mushroom.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Backlit_mushroom.jpg/440px-Backlit_mushroom.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Backlit_mushroom.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Translucency of a material being used to highlight the structure of a photographic subject</div></div></div>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transparent_ceramic" title="Transparent ceramic">Transparent ceramics</a> have created interest in their applications for high energy lasers, transparent armor windows, nose cones for heat seeking missiles, radiation detectors for non-destructive testing, high energy physics, space exploration, security and medical imaging applications. Large <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">laser</a> elements made from transparent ceramics can be produced at a relatively low cost. These components are free of internal <a href="/wiki/Stress_(mechanics)" title="Stress (mechanics)">stress</a> or intrinsic <a href="/wiki/Birefringence" title="Birefringence">birefringence</a>, and allow relatively large doping levels or optimized custom-designed doping profiles. This makes ceramic laser elements particularly important for high-energy lasers.
</p><p>The development of transparent panel products will have other potential advanced applications including high strength, impact-resistant materials that can be used for domestic windows and skylights. Perhaps more important is that walls and other applications will have improved overall strength, especially for high-shear conditions found in high seismic and wind exposures. If the expected improvements in mechanical properties bear out, the traditional limits seen on glazing areas in today's building codes could quickly become outdated if the window area actually contributes to the shear resistance of the wall.
</p><p>Currently available infrared transparent materials typically exhibit a trade-off between optical performance, mechanical strength and price. For example, <a href="/wiki/Sapphire" title="Sapphire">sapphire</a> (crystalline <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alumina" title="Alumina">alumina</a>) is very strong, but it is expensive and lacks full transparency throughout the 3–5 micrometer mid-infrared range. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yttria" title="Yttria">Yttria</a> is fully transparent from 3–5 micrometers, but lacks sufficient strength, hardness, and thermal shock resistance for high-performance aerospace applications. Not surprisingly, a combination of these two materials in the form of the <a href="/wiki/Yttrium_aluminium_garnet" title="Yttrium aluminium garnet">yttrium aluminium garnet</a> (YAG) is one of the top performers in the field.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Absorption_of_light_in_solids">Absorption of light in solids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transparency_and_translucency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Absorption of light in solids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When light strikes an object, it usually has not just a single frequency (or wavelength) but many. Objects have a tendency to selectively absorb, reflect or transmit light of certain frequencies. That is, one object might reflect green light while absorbing all other frequencies of visible light. Another object might selectively transmit blue light while absorbing all other frequencies of visible light. The manner in which visible light interacts with an object is dependent upon the frequency of the light, the nature of the atoms in the object, and often the nature of the <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> of the object.
</p><p>Some materials allow much of the light that falls on them to be transmitted through the material without being reflected. Materials that allow the transmission of light waves through them are called optically transparent. Chemically pure (undoped) window glass and clean river or spring water are prime examples of this.
</p><p>Materials which do not allow the transmission of any light wave frequencies are called <a href="/wiki/Opacity_(optics)" title="Opacity (optics)">opaque</a>. Such substances may have a chemical composition which includes what are referred to as absorption centers. Most materials are composed of materials which are selective in their absorption of light frequencies. Thus they absorb only certain portions of the visible spectrum. The frequencies of the spectrum which are not absorbed are either reflected back or transmitted for our physical observation. In the visible portion of the spectrum, this is what gives rise to color.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbbb_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbbb-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aaaa_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-aaaa-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Absorption centers are largely responsible for the appearance of specific wavelengths of visible light all around us. Moving from longer (0.7 micrometer) to shorter (0.4 micrometer) wavelengths: red, orange, yellow, green and blue (ROYGB) can all be identified by our senses in the appearance of color by the selective absorption of specific light wave frequencies (or wavelengths). Mechanisms of selective light wave absorption include:
</p>
<ul><li>Electronic: Transitions in electron <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_levels" title="Energy levels">energy levels</a> within the atom (e.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pigments" title="Pigments">pigments</a>). These transitions are typically in the ultraviolet (UV) and/or visible portions of the spectrum.</li>
<li>Vibrational: <a href="/wiki/Resonance" title="Resonance">Resonance</a> in atomic/molecular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vibrational_modes" title="Vibrational modes">vibrational modes</a>. These transitions are typically in the infrared portion of the spectrum.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="UV-Vis:_Electronic_transitions">UV-Vis: Electronic transitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transparency_and_translucency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: UV-Vis: Electronic transitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In electronic absorption, the frequency of the incoming light wave is at or near the energy levels of the electrons within the atoms which compose the substance. In this case, the electrons will absorb the energy of the light wave and increase their energy state, often moving outward from the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">nucleus</a> of the atom into an outer shell or <a href="/wiki/Atomic_orbital" title="Atomic orbital">orbital</a>.
</p><p>The atoms that bind together to make the molecules of any particular substance contain a number of electrons (given by the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> Z in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periodic_chart" title="Periodic chart">periodic chart</a>). Recall that all light waves are electromagnetic in origin. Thus they are affected strongly when coming into contact with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negatively_charged" title="Negatively charged">negatively charged</a> electrons in matter. When <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photons" title="Photons">photons</a> (individual packets of light energy) come in contact with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valence_electrons" title="Valence electrons">valence electrons</a> of atom, one of several things can and will occur:
</p>
<ul><li>A molecule absorbs the photon, some of the energy may be lost via <a href="/wiki/Luminescence" title="Luminescence">luminescence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fluorescence" title="Fluorescence">fluorescence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phosphorescence" title="Phosphorescence">phosphorescence</a>.</li>
<li>A molecule absorbs the photon which results in reflection or scattering.</li>
<li>A molecule cannot absorb the energy of the photon and the photon continues on its path. This results in transmission (provided no other absorption mechanisms are active).</li></ul>
<p>Most of the time, it is a combination of the above that happens to the light that hits an object. The states in different materials vary in the range of energy that they can absorb. Most glasses, for example, block ultraviolet (UV) light. What happens is the electrons in the glass absorb the energy of the photons in the UV range while ignoring the weaker energy of photons in the visible light spectrum. But there are also existing special <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> types, like special types of <a href="/wiki/Borosilicate_glass" title="Borosilicate glass">borosilicate glass</a> or quartz that are UV-permeable and thus allow a high transmission of ultra violet light.
</p><p>Thus, when a material is illuminated, individual photons of light can make the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valence_electrons" title="Valence electrons">valence electrons</a> of an atom transition to a higher electronic <a href="/wiki/Energy_level" title="Energy level">energy level</a>. The photon is destroyed in the process and the absorbed radiant energy is transformed to electric potential energy. Several things can happen then to the absorbed energy: it may be re-emitted by the electron as <a href="/wiki/Radiant_energy" title="Radiant energy">radiant energy</a> (in this case the overall effect is in fact a scattering of light), dissipated to the rest of the material (i.e. transformed into <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a>), or the electron can be freed from the atom (as in the <a href="/wiki/Photoelectric_effect" title="Photoelectric effect">photoelectric</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compton_effect" title="Compton effect">Compton</a> effects).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrared:_Bond_stretching">Infrared: Bond stretching</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transparency_and_translucency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Infrared: Bond stretching">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1D_normal_modes_(280_kB).gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="275" data-file-width="275" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/1D_normal_modes_%28280_kB%29.gif/250px-1D_normal_modes_%28280_kB%29.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9b/1D_normal_modes_%28280_kB%29.gif 1.5x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1D_normal_modes_(280_kB).gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Normal modes of vibration in a crystalline solid</div></div></div>
<p>The primary physical mechanism for storing mechanical energy of motion in condensed matter is through <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Thermal_energy" title="Thermal energy">thermal energy</a>. Thermal energy manifests itself as energy of motion. Thus, heat is motion at the atomic and molecular levels. The primary mode of motion in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystalline" title="Crystalline">crystalline</a> substances is <a href="/wiki/Vibration" title="Vibration">vibration</a>. Any given atom will vibrate around some <a href="/wiki/Mean" title="Mean">mean</a> or average <a href="/wiki/Position_(vector)" title="Position (vector)">position</a> within a crystalline structure, surrounded by its nearest neighbors. This vibration in two dimensions is equivalent to the <a href="/wiki/Oscillation" title="Oscillation">oscillation</a> of a clock’s pendulum. It swings back and forth <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symmetrical" title="Symmetrical">symmetrically</a> about some mean or average (vertical) position. Atomic and molecular vibrational frequencies may average on the order of 10<sup>12</sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cycles_per_second" title="Cycles per second">cycles per second</a> (<a href="/wiki/Terahertz_radiation#Natural" title="Terahertz radiation">Terahertz radiation</a>).
</p><p>When a light wave of a given frequency strikes a material with particles having the same or (resonant) vibrational frequencies, then those particles will absorb the energy of the light wave and transform it into thermal energy of vibrational motion. Since different atoms and molecules have different natural frequencies of vibration, they will selectively absorb different frequencies (or portions of the spectrum) of infrared light. Reflection and transmission of light waves occur because the frequencies of the light waves do not match the natural resonant frequencies of vibration of the objects. When infrared light of these frequencies strikes an object, the energy is reflected or transmitted.
</p><p>If the object is transparent, then the light waves are passed on to neighboring atoms through the bulk of the material and re-emitted on the opposite side of the object. Such frequencies of light waves are said to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transmit" title="Transmit">transmitted</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transparency_in_insulators">Transparency in insulators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transparency_and_translucency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Transparency in insulators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An object may be not transparent either because it reflects the incoming light or because it absorbs the incoming light. Almost all solids reflect a part and absorb a part of the incoming light.
</p><p>When light falls onto a block of <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a>, it encounters atoms that are tightly packed in a regular <a href="/wiki/Lattice_model_(physics)" title="Lattice model (physics)">lattice</a> and a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_of_electrons" title="Sea of electrons">sea of electrons</a>" moving randomly between the atoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-X_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-X-11">[11]</a></sup> In metals, most of these are non-bonding electrons (or free electrons) as opposed to the bonding electrons typically found in covalently bonded or ionically bonded non-metallic (insulating) solids. In a metallic bond, any potential bonding electrons can easily be lost by the atoms in a crystalline structure. The effect of this delocalization is simply to exaggerate the effect of the "sea of electrons". As a result of these electrons, most of the incoming light in metals is reflected back, which is why we see a <a href="/wiki/Reflection_(physics)" title="Reflection (physics)">shiny</a> metal surface.
</p><p>Most <a href="/wiki/Insulator_(electricity)" title="Insulator (electricity)">insulators</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Dielectric" title="Dielectric">dielectric</a> materials) are held together by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionic_bond" title="Ionic bond">ionic bonds</a>. Thus, these materials do not have free <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conduction_electrons" title="Conduction electrons">conduction electrons</a>, and the bonding electrons reflect only a small fraction of the incident wave. The remaining frequencies (or wavelengths) are free to propagate (or be transmitted). This class of materials includes all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceramic_materials" title="Ceramic materials">ceramics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glasses</a>.
</p><p>If a dielectric material does not include light-absorbent additive molecules (pigments, dyes, colorants), it is usually transparent to the spectrum of visible light. <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Colour_centre_(disambiguation)" title="Colour centre (disambiguation)">Color centers</a> (or dye molecules, or "dopants") in a dielectric absorb a portion of the incoming light. The remaining frequencies (or wavelengths) are free to be reflected or transmitted. This is how colored glass is produced.
</p><p>Most liquids and aqueous solutions are highly transparent. For example, water, cooking oil, rubbing alcohol, air, and natural gas are all clear. Absence of structural defects (voids, cracks, etc.) and molecular structure of most liquids are chiefly responsible for their excellent optical transmission. The ability of liquids to "heal" internal defects via viscous flow is one of the reasons why some fibrous materials (e.g., paper or fabric) increase their apparent transparency when wetted. The liquid fills up numerous voids making the material more structurally homogeneous.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Light scattering in an ideal defect-free <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystalline" title="Crystalline">crystalline</a> (non-metallic) solid which provides <i>no scattering centers</i> for incoming light will be due primarily to any effects of anharmonicity within the ordered lattice. Light <a href="/wiki/Transmission_coefficient#Optics" title="Transmission coefficient">transmission</a> will be highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direction_(geometry)" title="Direction (geometry)">directional</a> due to the typical <a href="/wiki/Anisotropy" title="Anisotropy">anisotropy</a> of crystalline substances, which includes their <a href="/wiki/Symmetry_group" title="Symmetry group">symmetry group</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bravais_lattice" title="Bravais lattice">Bravais lattice</a>. For example, the seven different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystalline" title="Crystalline">crystalline</a> forms of <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a> silica (<a href="/wiki/Silicon_dioxide" title="Silicon dioxide">silicon dioxide</a>, SiO<sub>2</sub>) are all clear, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transparent_materials" title="Transparent materials">transparent materials</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Optical_waveguides">Optical waveguides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transparency_and_translucency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Optical waveguides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Optical-fibre.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="255" data-file-width="550" height="102" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Optical-fibre.svg/220px-Optical-fibre.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Optical-fibre.svg/330px-Optical-fibre.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Optical-fibre.svg/440px-Optical-fibre.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Optical-fibre.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Propagation of light through a multi-mode optical fiber</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Laser_in_fibre.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Laser_in_fibre.jpg/220px-Laser_in_fibre.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Laser_in_fibre.jpg/330px-Laser_in_fibre.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Laser_in_fibre.jpg/440px-Laser_in_fibre.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Laser_in_fibre.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A laser beam bouncing down an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acrylic_glass" title="Acrylic glass">acrylic</a> rod, illustrating the total internal reflection of light in a multimode optical fiber</div></div></div>
<p>Optically transparent materials focus on the response of a material to incoming light waves of a range of wavelengths. Guided light wave transmission via frequency selective waveguides involves the emerging field of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiber_optics" title="Fiber optics">fiber optics</a> and the ability of certain glassy compositions to act as a <a href="/wiki/Optical_medium" title="Optical medium">transmission medium</a> for a range of frequencies simultaneously (<a href="/wiki/Multi-mode_optical_fiber" title="Multi-mode optical fiber">multi-mode optical fiber</a>) with little or no <a href="/wiki/Adjacent-channel_interference" title="Adjacent-channel interference">interference</a> between competing wavelengths or frequencies. This resonant mode of energy and data transmission via electromagnetic (light) wave propagation is relatively lossless.
</p><p>An optical fiber is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cylindrical" title="Cylindrical">cylindrical</a> dielectric waveguide that transmits light along its axis by the process of <a href="/wiki/Total_internal_reflection" title="Total internal reflection">total internal reflection</a>. The fiber consists of a <a href="/wiki/Core_(optical_fiber)" title="Core (optical fiber)">core</a> surrounded by a <a href="/wiki/Cladding_(fiber_optics)" title="Cladding (fiber optics)">cladding</a> layer. To confine the optical signal in the core, the <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive index</a> of the core must be greater than that of the cladding. The refractive index is the parameter reflecting the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a> in a material. (Refractive index is the ratio of the speed of light in vacuum to the speed of light in a given medium. The refractive index of vacuum is therefore 1.) The larger the refractive index, the more slowly light travels in that medium. Typical values for core and cladding of an optical fiber are 1.48 and 1.46, respectively.
</p><p>When light traveling in a dense medium hits a boundary at a steep angle, the light will be completely reflected. This effect, called <a href="/wiki/Total_internal_reflection" title="Total internal reflection">total internal reflection</a>, is used in optical fibers to confine light in the core. Light travels along the fiber bouncing back and forth off of the boundary. Because the light must strike the boundary with an angle greater than the <a href="/wiki/Total_internal_reflection" title="Total internal reflection">critical angle</a>, only light that enters the fiber within a certain range of angles will be propagated. This range of angles is called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acceptance_cone" title="Acceptance cone">acceptance cone</a> of the fiber. The size of this acceptance cone is a function of the refractive index difference between the fiber's core and cladding. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical" title="Optical">Optical</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waveguides" title="Waveguides">waveguides</a> are used as components in integrated optical circuits (e.g. combined with lasers or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diodes" title="Light-emitting diodes">light-emitting diodes</a>, LEDs) or as the transmission medium in local and long haul <a href="/wiki/Optical_communication" title="Optical communication">optical communication</a> systems.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanisms_of_attenuation">Mechanisms of attenuation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Transparency_and_translucency&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Mechanisms of attenuation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_scattering" title="Light scattering">Light scattering</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Zblan_transmit.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="634" data-file-width="1000" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Zblan_transmit.jpg/220px-Zblan_transmit.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Zblan_transmit.jpg/330px-Zblan_transmit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Zblan_transmit.jpg/440px-Zblan_transmit.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Zblan_transmit.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Light attenuation by ZBLAN and silica fibers</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Attenuation" title="Attenuation">Attenuation</a> in <a href="/wiki/Optical_fiber" title="Optical fiber">fiber optics</a>, also known as transmission loss, is the reduction in intensity of the light beam (or signal) with respect to distance traveled through a transmission medium. Attenuation coefficients in fiber optics usually use units of dB/km through the medium due to the very high quality of transparency of modern optical transmission media. The medium is usually a fiber of silica glass that confines the incident light beam to the inside. Attenuation is an important factor limiting the transmission of a signal across large distances. In optical fibers the main attenuation source is scattering from molecular level irregularities (<a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">Rayleigh scattering</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> due to structural disorder and compositional fluctuations of the <a href="/wiki/Amorphous_solid" title="Amorphous solid">glass structure</a>. This same phenomenon is seen as one of the limiting factors in the transparency of infrared missile domes<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Further attenuation is caused by light absorbed by residual materials, such as metals or water ions, within the fiber core and inner cladding. Light leakage due to bending, splices, connectors, or other outside forces are other factors resulting in attenuation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Many <a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">marine</a> animals that float near the surface are highly transparent, giving them almost perfect <a href="/wiki/Camouflage" title="Camouflage">camouflage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HerringTransparency_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-HerringTransparency-16">[16]</a></sup> However, transparency is difficult for bodies made of materials that have different <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive indices</a> from seawater. Some marine animals such as <a href="/wiki/Jellyfish" title="Jellyfish">jellyfish</a> have gelatinous bodies, composed mainly of water; their thick <a href="/wiki/Mesoglea" title="Mesoglea">mesogloea</a> is acellular and highly transparent. This conveniently makes them <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyant</a>, but it also makes them large for their muscle mass, so they cannot swim fast, making this form of camouflage a costly trade-off with mobility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HerringTransparency_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-HerringTransparency-16">[16]</a></sup> Gelatinous <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">planktonic</a> animals are between 50 and 90 percent transparent. A transparency of 50 percent is enough to make an animal invisible to a predator such as <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a> at a depth of 650 metres (2,130 ft); better transparency is required for <a href="/wiki/Invisibility" title="Invisibility">invisibility</a> in shallower water, where the light is brighter and predators can see better. For example, a cod can see prey that are 98 percent transparent in optimal lighting in shallow water. Therefore, sufficient transparency for camouflage is more easily achieved in deeper waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HerringTransparency_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-HerringTransparency-16">[16]</a></sup> For the same reason, transparency in air is even harder to achieve, but a partial example is found in the 
<a href="/wiki/Glass_frog" title="Glass frog">glass frogs</a> of the South American rain forest, which have translucent skin and pale greenish limbs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Several Central American species of clearwing (<a href="/wiki/Ithomiini" title="Ithomiini">ithomiine</a>) butterflies and many <a href="/wiki/Dragonfly" title="Dragonfly">dragonflies</a> and allied <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> also have wings which are mostly transparent, a form of <a href="/wiki/Crypsis" title="Crypsis">crypsis</a> that provides some protection from predators.
</p>
