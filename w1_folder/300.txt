<p><b>Sodium bicarbonate</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAC_name" title="IUPAC name">IUPAC name</a>: <b>sodium hydrogen carbonate</b>), commonly known as <b>baking soda</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> with the formula <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">Na</a><a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">HCO<sub>3</sub></a>. It is a <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salt</a> composed of a <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> cation (Na<sup>+</sup>) and a <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> anion (HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>-</sup>). Sodium bicarbonate is a white solid that is <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystalline</a>, but often appears as a fine powder. It has a slightly salty, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkaline" title="Alkaline">alkaline</a> taste resembling that of washing soda (<a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">sodium carbonate</a>). The natural mineral form is <a href="/wiki/Nahcolite" title="Nahcolite">nahcolite</a>. It is a component of the mineral <a href="/wiki/Natron" title="Natron">natron</a> and is found dissolved in many <a href="/wiki/Mineral_spring" title="Mineral spring">mineral springs</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature">Nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_bicarbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because it has long been known and is widely used, the salt has many related names such as <b>baking soda</b>, <b>bread soda</b>, <b>cooking soda</b>, and <b>bicarbonate of soda</b>. In colloquial usage, the names sodium bicarbonate and bicarbonate of soda are often truncated; forms such as sodium bicarb, bicarb soda, bicarbonate, and bicarb are common. The word <i>saleratus</i>, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a> <i>sal æratus</i> meaning "aerated salt", was widely used in the 19th century for both sodium bicarbonate and <a href="/wiki/Potassium_bicarbonate" title="Potassium bicarbonate">potassium bicarbonate</a>.
</p><p>The prefix "bi" in "bicarbonate" comes from an outdated naming system and is based on the observation that twice as much <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonate</a> (CO<sub>3</sub>) per sodium IS in sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO<sub>3</sub>) as in <a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">sodium carbonate</a> (Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>). The modern chemical formulas of these compounds express their precise chemical compositions (which were unknown when the names sodium carbonate and sodium bicarbonate were coined) and show the same ratio the other way around:  half as much sodium per carbonate is in NaHCO<sub>3</sub> as in Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> (disodium carbonate).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_bicarbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cooking">Cooking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_bicarbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cooking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Leavening_agent" title="Leavening agent">Leavening agent</a></div>
<p>In cooking, baking soda is primarily used in <a href="/wiki/Baking" title="Baking">baking</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Leavening_agent" title="Leavening agent">leavening agent</a>. It reacts with acidic components in batters, releasing carbon dioxide, which causes expansion of the batter and forms the characteristic texture and grain in pancakes, cakes, <a href="/wiki/Quick_bread" title="Quick bread">quick breads</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soda_bread" title="Soda bread">soda bread</a>, and other baked and fried foods. Acidic compounds that induce this reaction include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphates" title="Phosphates">phosphates</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cream_of_tartar" title="Cream of tartar">cream of tartar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lemon" title="Lemon">lemon juice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yogurt" title="Yogurt">yogurt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buttermilk" title="Buttermilk">buttermilk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cocoa_solids" title="Cocoa solids">cocoa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vinegar" title="Vinegar">vinegar</a>. Baking soda may be used together with <a href="/wiki/Sourdough" title="Sourdough">sourdough</a>, which is acidic, making a lighter product with a less acidic taste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Heat can also by itself cause sodium bicarbonate to act as a raising agent in baking because of thermal decomposition, releasing carbon dioxide. When used this way on its own, without the presence of an acidic component (whether in the batter or by the use of a baking powder containing acid), only half the available CO<sub>2</sub> is released. Additionally, in the absence of acid, thermal decomposition of sodium bicarbonate also produces <a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">sodium carbonate</a>, which is strongly alkaline and gives the baked product a bitter, "soapy" taste and a yellow color. To avoid an over-acidic taste from added acid, nonacid ingredients such as whole milk or <a href="/wiki/Dutch_process_chocolate" title="Dutch process chocolate">Dutch-processed</a> <a href="/wiki/Cocoa_solids" title="Cocoa solids">cocoa</a> are often added to baked foods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Carbon dioxide production from exposure to heat starts at temperatures above 80 °C (180 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 NaHCO<sub>3</sub> → Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O + CO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Since the reaction occurs slowly at room temperature, mixtures (cake batter, etc.) can be allowed to stand without rising until they are heated in the oven.
</p><p>Sodium bicarbonate was sometimes used in cooking green vegetables, as it gives them a bright green colour—which has been described as artificial-looking—due to its reacting with <a href="/wiki/Chlorophyll" title="Chlorophyll">chlorophyll</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Chlorophyllin" title="Chlorophyllin">chlorophyllin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Srilakshmi2003_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Srilakshmi2003-12">[12]</a></sup> However, this tends to affect taste, texture and nutritional content, and is no longer common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Baking soda is still used, though, in the traditional British <a href="/wiki/Mushy_peas" title="Mushy peas">mushy peas</a> recipe for soaking the peas. It is also used in Asian and Latin American cuisine to <a href="/wiki/Meat_tenderness#Tenderizing" title="Meat tenderness">tenderize</a> meats. Baking soda may react with acids in food, including <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_C" title="Vitamin C">vitamin C</a> (<small>L</small>-ascorbic acid). It is also used in breading, such as for fried foods, to enhance crispness and allow passages for steam to escape, so the breading is not blown off during cooking.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Baking_powder">Baking powder</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_bicarbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Baking powder">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Baking_powder" title="Baking powder">Baking powder</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Baking_powder" title="Baking powder">Baking powder</a>, also sold for cooking, contains around 30% of bicarbonate, and various acidic ingredients which are activated by the addition of water, without the need for additional acids in the cooking medium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
Many forms of baking powder contain sodium bicarbonate combined with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calcium_acid_phosphate" title="Calcium acid phosphate">calcium acid phosphate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_aluminium_phosphate" title="Sodium aluminium phosphate">sodium aluminium phosphate</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cream_of_tartar" title="Cream of tartar">cream of tartar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Baking soda is alkaline; the acid used in baking powder avoids a metallic taste when the chemical change during baking creates sodium carbonate.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pest_control">Pest control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_bicarbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pest control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sodium bicarbonate can be used to kill <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cockroaches" title="Cockroaches">cockroaches</a>. Once consumed, it causes internal organs of cockroaches to burst due to gas collection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sodium bicarbonate can be an effective way of controlling fungal growth,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and in the United States is registered by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Biopesticide" title="Biopesticide">biopesticide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Alkalinity.2FpH_increase"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Alkalinity/pH_increase">Alkalinity/pH increase</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_bicarbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Alkalinity/pH increase">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sodium bicarbonate can be administered to pools, spas, and garden ponds to raise the total alkalinity. This will also raise the <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> level and make maintaining proper pH easier. In the event that the pH is high, sodium bicarbonate should not be used to adjust the pH.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pyrotechnics">Pyrotechnics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_bicarbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Pyrotechnics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sodium bicarbonate is one of the main components of the common <a href="/wiki/Black_snake_(firework)" title="Black snake (firework)">"black snake" firework</a>. The effect is caused by the thermal decomposition, which produces carbon dioxide gas to produce a long <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snake</a>-like ash as a combustion product of the other main component, <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mild_disinfectant">Mild disinfectant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_bicarbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mild disinfectant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It has weak <a href="/wiki/Disinfectant" title="Disinfectant">disinfectant</a> properties,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Malik_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Malik-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and it may be an effective <a href="/wiki/Fungicide" title="Fungicide">fungicide</a> against some organisms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zamani_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zamani-24">[24]</a></sup> Because baking soda will absorb musty smells, it has become a reliable method for used-book sellers when making books less malodorous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fire_extinguisher">Fire extinguisher</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_bicarbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Fire extinguisher">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sodium bicarbonate can be used to extinguish small grease or electrical fires by being thrown over the fire, as heating of sodium bicarbonate releases carbon dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arm_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-arm-26">[26]</a></sup> However, it should not be applied to fires in <a href="/wiki/Deep_fryer" title="Deep fryer">deep fryers</a>; the sudden release of gas may cause the grease to splatter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arm_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-arm-26">[26]</a></sup> Sodium bicarbonate is used in BC <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fire_extinguishers#Dry_chemical" title="Fire extinguishers">dry chemical</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fire_extinguishers" title="Fire extinguishers">fire extinguishers</a> as an alternative to the more corrosive <a href="/wiki/Diammonium_phosphate" title="Diammonium phosphate">diammonium phosphate</a> in ABC extinguishers. The alkaline nature of sodium bicarbonate makes it the only dry chemical agent, besides <a href="/wiki/Purple-K" title="Purple-K">Purple-K</a>, that was used in large-scale fire suppression systems installed in commercial kitchens. Because it can act as an alkali, the agent has a mild <a href="/wiki/Saponification" title="Saponification">saponification</a> effect on hot grease, which forms a smothering, soapy foam.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neutralization_of_acids_and_bases">Neutralization of acids and bases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_bicarbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Neutralization of acids and bases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sodium bicarbonate is <a href="/wiki/Amphoterism" title="Amphoterism">amphoteric</a>, reacting with <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">acids and bases</a>. It reacts violently with acids, releasing CO<sub>2</sub> gas as a reaction product. It is commonly used to neutralize unwanted acid solutions or acid spills in chemical laboratories.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_uses_and_health">Medical uses and health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_bicarbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Medical uses and health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Intravenous_sodium_bicarbonate" title="Intravenous sodium bicarbonate">Intravenous sodium bicarbonate</a></div>
<p>Sodium bicarbonate mixed with water can be used as an <a href="/wiki/Antacid" title="Antacid">antacid</a> to treat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acid_indigestion" title="Acid indigestion">acid indigestion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heartburn" title="Heartburn">heartburn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Its reaction with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stomach_acid" title="Stomach acid">stomach acid</a> produces salt, water, and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>NaHCO<sub>3</sub> + HCl → NaCl + H<sub>2</sub>O + CO<sub>2</sub>(g)</dd></dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Intravenous_sodium_bicarbonate" title="Intravenous sodium bicarbonate">Intravenous sodium bicarbonate</a> in an aqueous solution is sometimes used for cases of <a href="/wiki/Acidosis" title="Acidosis">acidosis</a>, or when insufficient sodium or bicarbonate ions are in the blood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In cases of respiratory acidosis, the infused bicarbonate ion drives the carbonic acid/bicarbonate buffer of plasma to the left, and thus raises the pH. For this reason, sodium bicarbonate is used in medically supervised <a href="/wiki/Cardiopulmonary_resuscitation" title="Cardiopulmonary resuscitation">cardiopulmonary resuscitation</a>. Infusion of bicarbonate is indicated only when the blood pH is markedly low (&lt; 7.1–7.0).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>HCO<sub>3</sub> is used for treatment of <a href="/wiki/Hyperkalemia" title="Hyperkalemia">hyperkalemia</a>, as it will drive K<sup>+</sup> back into cells during periods of acidosis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Since sodium bicarbonate can cause <a href="/wiki/Alkalosis" title="Alkalosis">alkalosis</a>, it is sometimes used to treat aspirin overdoses. Aspirin requires an acidic environment for proper absorption, and the basic environment diminishes aspirin absorption in the case of an overdose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Sodium bicarbonate has also been used in the treatment of <a href="/wiki/Tricyclic_antidepressant_overdose" title="Tricyclic antidepressant overdose">tricyclic antidepressant overdose</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid9142034_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid9142034-32">[32]</a></sup> It can also be applied topically as a paste, with three parts baking soda to one part water, to relieve some kinds of insect bites and stings (as well as accompanying swelling).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sodium bicarbonate has been found to have no effect on the blood pressure of several types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rat_model" title="Rat model">rat models</a> susceptible to salt-sensitive <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">hypertension</a>, in contrast with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a>. This was ascribed to the high concentration of chloride, rather than the sodium content in dietary salts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sodium bicarbonate can be used to treat an allergic reaction to plants such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poison_ivy" title="Poison ivy">poison ivy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poison_oak" title="Poison oak">poison oak</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poison_sumac" title="Poison sumac">poison sumac</a> to relieve some of the associated itching.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>Bicarbonate of soda can also be useful in removing splinters from the skin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some alternative practitioners, such as <a href="/wiki/Tullio_Simoncini" title="Tullio Simoncini">Tullio Simoncini</a>, have promoted baking soda as a <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a> cure, which the American Cancer Society has warned against due to both its unproven effectiveness and potential danger in use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sodium bicarbonate can be added to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Local_anaesthetics" title="Local anaesthetics">local anaesthetics</a>, to speed up the onset of their effects and make their injection less painful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> It is also a component of <a href="/wiki/Moffett%27s_solution" title="Moffett's solution">Moffett's solution</a>, used in <a href="/wiki/Nose" title="Nose">nasal</a> <a href="/wiki/Surgery" title="Surgery">surgery</a>.
</p><p>As early as the 1920s, bicarbonate was found to cause increased bone strength in patients who were losing calcium in their urine. In 1968, diets producing too much acid were thought to put bones at risk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> Experiments by Anthony Sebastian of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_San_Francisco" title="University of California, San Francisco">University of California, San Francisco</a> starting in the late 20th century found that the body was breaking down bones and muscles to release carbonates, phosphates, and ammonia, which neutralize acid. Adding bicarbonate to the diet (he used potassium bicarbonate) reduced loss of calcium in postmenopausal women, amounting to the equivalent of "an arm-and-a-leg's worth" of bone if this continued for two decades.
</p><p>A wide variety of applications follows from its neutralization properties, including reducing the spread of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_phosphorus" title="White phosphorus">white phosphorus</a> from incendiary bullets inside an afflicted soldier's wounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources_(medicine)" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources (medicine)"><span title="Material near this tag needs references to reliable medical sources. (November 2011)">medical citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Antacid" title="Antacid">Antacid</a> (such as baking soda) solutions have been prepared and used by protesters to alleviate the effects of exposure to <a href="/wiki/Tear_gas" title="Tear gas">tear gas</a> during protests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (October 2017)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>In a recent study published in the <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_of_Immunology" title="Journal of Immunology">Journal of Immunology</a></i>, oral baking soda was found to activate a splenic anti-inflammatory pathway that seems to reduce the destructive inflammation of autoimmune diseases such as rheumatoid arthritis.  Proinflammatory molecules were reduced and anti-inflammatory molecules were released. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>Similarly to its use in baking, sodium bicarbonate is used together with a mild acid such as tartaric acid as the excipient in effervescent tablets: when such a tablet is dropped in a glass of water, the carbonate leaves the reaction medium as carbon dioxide gas ( HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>-</sup> + H<sup>+</sup> → H<sub>2</sub>O + CO<sub>2</sub>↑ or, more precisely, HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>-</sup> + H<sub>3</sub>O<sup>+</sup> → 2 H<sub>2</sub>O + CO<sub>2</sub>↑ ) leaving the medication dissolved in the water together with the resulting salt (in this example, sodium tartrate).
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_hygiene">Personal hygiene</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_bicarbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Personal hygiene">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Toothpaste" title="Toothpaste">Toothpaste</a> containing sodium bicarbonate has in several studies been shown to have a better whitening<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kleber_1998_72–5_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kleber_1998_72–5-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kleber_1998_72–5_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kleber_1998_72–5-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> and plaque removal effect<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
than toothpastes without it.
</p><p>Sodium bicarbonate is also used as an ingredient in some mouthwashes. It has anticaries and abrasive properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> It works as a mechanical cleanser on the teeth and gums, neutralizes the production of acid in the mouth, and also acts as an <a href="/wiki/Antiseptic" title="Antiseptic">antiseptic</a> to help prevent infections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Sodium bicarbonate in combination with other ingredients can be used to make a dry or wet <a href="/wiki/Deodorant" title="Deodorant">deodorant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Sodium bicarbonate may be used as a buffering agent, combined with table salt, when creating a solution for <a href="/wiki/Nasal_irrigation" title="Nasal irrigation">nasal irrigation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is used in eye hygiene to treat <a href="/wiki/Blepharitis" title="Blepharitis">blepharitis</a>. This is done by addition of a teaspoon of sodium bicarbonate to cool water that was recently boiled, followed by gentle scrubbing of the eyelash base with a cotton swab dipped in the solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Veterinary_uses">Veterinary uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_bicarbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Veterinary uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Sodium bicarbonate is used as a cattle feed supplement, in particular as a <a href="/wiki/Buffering_agent" title="Buffering agent">buffering agent</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Rumen" title="Rumen">rumen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_sports">In sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_bicarbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: In sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Small amounts of sodium bicarbonate have been shown to be useful as a supplement for athletes in speed-based events, such as middle-distance running, lasting about 1–7 minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ergogenic_Aids_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ergogenic_Aids-59">[59]</a></sup> However, overdose is a serious risk because sodium bicarbonate is slightly toxic;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> and gastrointestinal irritation is of particular concern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ergogenic_Aids_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ergogenic_Aids-59">[59]</a></sup> Additionally, this practice causes a significant increase in dietary sodium.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cleaning_agent">Cleaning agent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_bicarbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Cleaning agent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sodium bicarbonate is used in a process for removing paint and corrosion called <a href="/wiki/Sodablasting" title="Sodablasting">sodablasting</a>; the process is particularly suitable for cleaning aluminium panels which can be distorted by other types of abrasives.
</p><p>A paste made from baking soda with minimal water is recommended by a manufacturer as a gentle scouring powder,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arm_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-arm-26">[26]</a></sup> and is useful in removing surface rust, as the rust forms a water-soluble compound when in a concentrated alkaline solution;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InorgChem_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-InorgChem-61">[61]</a></sup> cold water should be used, as hot-water solutions can corrode steel.
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> Sodium bicarbonate attacks the thin unreactive protective oxide layer that forms on aluminium, making it unsuitable for cleaning this otherwise very reactive metal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PhilMus_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-PhilMus-63">[63]</a></sup> A solution in warm water will remove the <a href="/wiki/Tarnish" title="Tarnish">tarnish</a> from silver when the silver is in contact with a piece of <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_foil" title="Aluminium foil">aluminium foil</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PhilMus_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-PhilMus-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
Baking soda is commonly added to washing machines as a replacement for water softener and to remove odors from clothes. It is also effective in removing heavy tea and coffee stains from cups when diluted with warm water. Also, baking soda can be used as a multipurpose odor remover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">Manhattan Project</a> to develop the nuclear bomb in the early 1940s, the chemical toxicity of uranium was an issue. Uranium oxides were found to stick very well to cotton cloth, and did not wash out with soap or laundry detergent. However, the uranium would wash out with a 2% solution of sodium bicarbonate. Clothing can become contaminated with toxic dust of <a href="/wiki/Depleted_uranium" title="Depleted uranium">depleted uranium</a> (DU), which is very dense, hence used for counterweights in a civilian context, and in armour-piercing projectiles. DU is not removed by normal laundering; washing with about 6 ounces (170 g) of baking soda in 2 gallons (7.5 l) of water will help to wash it out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Orcutt_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-Orcutt-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_bicarbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sodium bicarbonate is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoteric" title="Amphoteric">amphoteric</a> compound. Aqueous solutions are very mildly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkaline" title="Alkaline">alkaline</a> due to the formation of <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxide</a> ion:
</p>
<dl><dd>HCO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span> + H<sub>2</sub>O → <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> + OH<sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<p>Sodium bicarbonate can be used as a wash to remove any acidic impurities from a "crude" liquid, producing a purer sample. Reaction of sodium bicarbonate and an <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acid</a> produces a salt and carbonic acid, which readily decomposes to carbon dioxide and water:
</p>
<dl><dd>NaHCO<sub>3</sub> + HCl → NaCl + H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub></dd>
<dd>H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> → H<sub>2</sub>O + CO<sub>2</sub>(g)</dd></dl>
<p>Sodium bicarbonate reacts with <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a> (found in <a href="/wiki/Vinegar" title="Vinegar">vinegar</a>), producing <a href="/wiki/Sodium_acetate" title="Sodium acetate">sodium acetate</a>, water, and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>NaHCO<sub>3</sub> + CH<sub>3</sub>COOH → CH<sub>3</sub>COONa + H<sub>2</sub>O + CO<sub>2</sub>(g)</dd></dl>
<p>Sodium bicarbonate reacts with <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">bases</a> such as sodium hydroxide to form carbonates:
</p>
<dl><dd>NaHCO<sub>3</sub> + NaOH → Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Sodium bicarbonate reacts with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carboxyl_group" title="Carboxyl group">carboxyl groups</a> in proteins to give a brisk <a href="/wiki/Effervescence" title="Effervescence">effervescence</a> from the formation of <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>. This reaction is used to test for the presence of carboxylic groups in protein.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal_decomposition">Thermal decomposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_bicarbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Thermal decomposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Above 50 °C (122 °F), sodium bicarbonate gradually decomposes into sodium carbonate, water, and carbon dioxide. The conversion is fast at 200 °C (392 °F):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frostburg_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-frostburg-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 NaHCO<sub>3</sub> → Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O + CO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Most bicarbonates undergo this <a href="/wiki/Dehydration_reaction" title="Dehydration reaction">dehydration reaction</a>. Further heating converts the carbonate into the <a href="/wiki/Sodium_oxide" title="Sodium oxide">oxide</a> (above 850 °C/1,560 °F):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frostburg_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-frostburg-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> → Na<sub>2</sub>O + CO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>These conversions are relevant to the use of NaHCO<sub>3</sub> as a fire-suppression agent ("BC powder") in some dry-powder <a href="/wiki/Fire_extinguisher" title="Fire extinguisher">fire extinguishers</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_bicarbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1791, French chemist <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Leblanc" title="Nicolas Leblanc">Nicolas Leblanc</a> produced sodium carbonate, also known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soda_ash" title="Soda ash">soda ash</a>. In 1846, two New York bakers, <a href="/wiki/John_Dwight_(manufacturer)" title="John Dwight (manufacturer)">John Dwight</a> and <a href="/wiki/Austin_Church" title="Austin Church">Austin Church</a>, established the first factory in the United States to produce baking soda from sodium carbonate and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Saleratus</i>, potassium or sodium bicarbonate, is mentioned in the novel <i><a href="/wiki/Captains_Courageous" title="Captains Courageous">Captains Courageous</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Rudyard_Kipling" title="Rudyard Kipling">Rudyard Kipling</a> as being used extensively in the 1800s in commercial fishing to prevent freshly caught fish from spoiling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_bicarbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Solvay_process" title="Solvay process">Solvay process</a></div>
<p>NaHCO<sub>3</sub> is mainly prepared by the <a href="/wiki/Solvay_process" title="Solvay process">Solvay process</a>, which is the reaction of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> in water. <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">Calcium carbonate</a> is used as the source of CO<sub>2</sub> and the resultant calcium oxide is used to recover the ammonia from the <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">ammonium chloride</a>. The product shows a low purity (75%). Pure product is obtained from sodium carbonate, water, and carbon dioxide as reported in one of the following reactions. It is produced on the scale of about 100,000 tonnes/year (as of 2001).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p><p>NaHCO<sub>3</sub> may be obtained by the reaction of carbon dioxide with an <a href="/wiki/Aqueous_solution" title="Aqueous solution">aqueous solution</a> of sodium hydroxide. The initial reaction produces sodium carbonate:
</p>
<dl><dd>CO<sub>2</sub> + 2 NaOH → Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Further addition of carbon dioxide produces sodium bicarbonate, which at sufficiently high concentration will precipitate out of solution:
</p>
<dl><dd>Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> + CO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O → 2 NaHCO<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Commercial quantities of baking soda are also produced by a similar method: soda ash, mined in the form of the ore <a href="/wiki/Trona" title="Trona">trona</a>, is dissolved in water and treated with carbon dioxide. Sodium bicarbonate precipitates as a solid from this method:
</p>
<dl><dd>Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> + CO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O → 2 NaHCO<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mining">Mining</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_bicarbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Mining">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Naturally occurring deposits of nahcolite (NaHCO<sub>3</sub>) are found in the <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a>-age (55.8–33.9 Mya) <a href="/wiki/Green_River_Formation" title="Green River Formation">Green River Formation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Piceance_Basin" title="Piceance Basin">Piceance Basin</a> in <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>. Nahcolite was deposited as beds during periods of high evaporation in the basin. It is commercially mined using common underground mining techniques such as bore, drum, and longwall mining in a fashion very similar to coal mining.
</p><p>Limited amounts of product are further obtained by solution mining, pumping heated water through previously mined nahcolite beds and reconstituting the dissolved nahcolite above ground through a natural cooling crystallization process. Currently, only Genesis Alkali (formerly Tronox, formerly FMC) in the Green River Wyoming basin has successfully commercially solution mined the product.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_bicarbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film">Film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sodium_bicarbonate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sodium bicarbonate, as "bicarbonate of soda", was a frequent source of punch lines for <a href="/wiki/Groucho_Marx" title="Groucho Marx">Groucho Marx</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marx_brothers" title="Marx brothers">Marx brothers</a> movies. In <i><a href="/wiki/Duck_Soup_(1933_film)" title="Duck Soup (1933 film)">Duck Soup</a></i>, Marx plays the leader of a nation at war. In one scene, he receives a message from the battlefield that his general is reporting a gas attack, and Groucho tells his aide: "Tell him to take a teaspoonful of bicarbonate of soda and a half a glass of water."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> In <i><a href="/wiki/A_Night_at_the_Opera_(film)" title="A Night at the Opera (film)">A Night at the Opera</a></i>, Groucho's character addresses the opening night crowd at an opera by saying of the lead tenor: "Signor Lassparri comes from a very famous family. His mother was a well-known bass singer. His father was the first man to stuff spaghetti with bicarbonate of soda, thus causing and curing indigestion at the same time."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p>
