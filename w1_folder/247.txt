<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Critical point (thermodynamics)</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>, a <b>critical point</b> (or <b>critical state</b>) is the end point of a phase <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equilibrium_(thermodynamics)" title="Equilibrium (thermodynamics)">equilibrium</a> curve. The most prominent example is the liquid-vapor critical point, the end point of the pressure-temperature curve that designates conditions under which a <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a> and its <a href="/wiki/Vapor" title="Vapor">vapor</a> can coexist. At higher temperatures, the gas cannot be liquefied by pressure alone. At the critical point, defined by a <i>critical temperature</i> <i>T</i><sub>c</sub> and a <i>critical pressure</i> <i>p</i><sub>c</sub>, <a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">phase</a> boundaries vanish. Other examples include the liquid–liquid critical points in mixtures.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Liquid-vapor_critical_point">Liquid-vapor critical point</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Critical_point_(thermodynamics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Liquid-vapor critical point">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Critical_point_(thermodynamics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Phase-diag2.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="443" data-file-width="530" height="276" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Phase-diag2.svg/330px-Phase-diag2.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Phase-diag2.svg/495px-Phase-diag2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Phase-diag2.svg/660px-Phase-diag2.svg.png 2x" width="330"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Phase-diag2.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The liquid-vapor critical point in a pressure–temperature <a href="/wiki/Phase_diagram" title="Phase diagram">phase diagram</a> is at the high-temperature extreme of the liquid–gas phase boundary. The dotted green line shows the anomalous behavior of water.</div></div></div>
<p>For simplicity and clarity, the generic notion of <i>critical point</i> is best introduced by discussing a specific example, the liquid-vapor critical point. This was the first critical point to be discovered, and it is still the best known and most studied one.
</p><p>The figure to the right shows the schematic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PT_diagram" title="PT diagram">PT diagram</a> of a <i>pure substance</i> (as opposed to mixtures, which have additional state variables and richer phase diagrams, discussed below). The commonly known <a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">phases</a> <i>solid</i>, <i>liquid</i> and <i>vapor</i> are separated by phase boundaries, i.e. pressure-temperature combinations where two phases can coexist. At the <a href="/wiki/Triple_point" title="Triple point">triple point</a>, all three phases can coexist. However, the liquid-vapor boundary terminates in an endpoint at some <i>critical temperature</i> <i>T</i><sub>c</sub> and <i>critical pressure</i> <i>p</i><sub>c</sub>. This is the <i>critical point</i>.
</p><p>In water, the critical point occurs at around 647 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a> (374 <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">°C</a> or 705 <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">°F</a>) and 22.064 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">MPa</a> (3200 <a href="/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch" title="Pounds per square inch">psia</a> or 218 <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">atm</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steam_2007_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Steam_2007-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <i>vicinity</i> of the critical point, the physical properties of the liquid and the vapor change dramatically, with both phases becoming ever more similar. For instance, liquid water under normal conditions is nearly incompressible, has a low thermal expansion coefficient, has a high dielectric constant, and is an excellent solvent for electrolytes. Near the critical point, all these properties change into the exact opposite: water becomes compressible, expandable, a poor dielectric, a bad solvent for electrolytes, and prefers to mix with nonpolar gases and organic molecules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>At</i> the critical point, only one phase exists. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_of_vaporization" title="Heat of vaporization">heat of vaporization</a> is zero. There is a <a href="/wiki/Stationary_point" title="Stationary point">stationary</a> <a href="/wiki/Inflection_point" title="Inflection point">inflection point</a> in the constant-temperature line (<i>critical isotherm</i>) on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PV_diagram" title="PV diagram">PV diagram</a>. This means that at the critical point:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atkins_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Atkins-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<p><i>Above</i> the critical point there exists a state of matter that is continuously connected with (can be transformed without phase transition into) both the liquid and the gaseous state. It is called <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_fluid" title="Supercritical fluid">supercritical fluid</a>. The common textbook knowledge that all distinction between liquid and vapor disappears beyond the critical point has been challenged by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Fisher" title="Michael Fisher">Fisher</a> and <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Widom" title="Benjamin Widom">Widom</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> who identified a p,T-line that separates states with different asymptotic statistical properties (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fisher-Widom_line" title="Fisher-Widom line">Fisher-Widom line</a>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Critical_point_(thermodynamics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Critical_carbon_dioxide.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2272" data-file-width="1704" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Critical_carbon_dioxide.jpg/220px-Critical_carbon_dioxide.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Critical_carbon_dioxide.jpg/330px-Critical_carbon_dioxide.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Critical_carbon_dioxide.jpg/440px-Critical_carbon_dioxide.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Critical_carbon_dioxide.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">Carbon dioxide</a> exuding <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a> while cooling from supercritical to critical temperature</div></div></div>
<p>The existence of a critical point was first discovered by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Cagniard_de_la_Tour" title="Charles Cagniard de la Tour">Charles Cagniard de la Tour</a> in 1822<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and named by <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Mendeleev" title="Dmitri Mendeleev">Dmitri Mendeleev</a> in 1860<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Andrews_(scientist)" title="Thomas Andrews (scientist)">Thomas Andrews</a> in 1869.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Cagniard showed that CO<sub>2</sub> could be liquefied at 31 °C at a pressure of 73 atm, but not at a slightly higher temperature, even under pressures as high as 3,000 atm.
</p>

<h2><span id="Mixtures:_liquid.E2.80.93liquid_critical_point"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Mixtures:_liquid–liquid_critical_point">Mixtures: liquid–liquid critical point</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Critical_point_(thermodynamics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mixtures: liquid–liquid critical point">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LCST-UCST_plot.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="430" data-file-width="430" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/LCST-UCST_plot.svg/330px-LCST-UCST_plot.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/LCST-UCST_plot.svg/495px-LCST-UCST_plot.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/LCST-UCST_plot.svg/660px-LCST-UCST_plot.svg.png 2x" width="330"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LCST-UCST_plot.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A plot of typical polymer solution phase behavior including two critical points: an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LCST" title="LCST">LCST</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Upper_critical_solution_temperature" title="Upper critical solution temperature">UCST</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Liquid-liquid_critical_point" title="Liquid-liquid critical point">liquid–liquid critical point</a> of a solution, which occurs at the <i>critical solution temperature</i>, occurs at the limit of the two-phase region of the phase diagram. In other words, it is the point at which an infinitesimal change in some thermodynamic variable (such as temperature or pressure) will lead to separation of the mixture into two distinct liquid phases, as shown in the polymer–solvent phase diagram to the right. Two types of liquid–liquid critical points are the <a href="/wiki/Upper_critical_solution_temperature" title="Upper critical solution temperature">upper critical solution temperature</a> (UCST), which is the hottest point at which cooling will induce phase separation, and the <a href="/wiki/Lower_critical_solution_temperature" title="Lower critical solution temperature">lower critical solution temperature</a> (LCST), which is the coldest point at which heating will induce phase separation.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mathematical_definition">Mathematical definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Critical_point_(thermodynamics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mathematical definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From a theoretical standpoint, the liquid–liquid critical point represents the temperature-concentration extremum of the <a href="/wiki/Spinodal" title="Spinodal">spinodal</a> curve (as can be seen in the figure to the right). Thus, the liquid–liquid critical point in a two-component system must satisfy two conditions: the condition of the spinodal curve (the <i>second</i> derivative of the <a href="/wiki/Gibbs_free_energy" title="Gibbs free energy">free energy</a> with respect to concentration must equal zero), and the extremum condition (the <i>third</i> derivative of the free energy with respect to concentration must also equal zero or the derivative of the spinodal temperature with respect to concentration must equal zero).
</p>
