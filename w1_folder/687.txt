<p>The <b>Beer–Lambert law</b>, also known as <b>Beer's law</b>, the <b>Lambert–Beer law</b>, or the <b>Beer–Lambert–Bouguer law</b> relates the <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(electromagnetic_radiation)" title="Absorption (electromagnetic radiation)">attenuation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> to the properties of the material through which the light is travelling. The law is commonly applied to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_analysis" title="Chemical analysis">chemical analysis</a> measurements and used in understanding attenuation in <a href="/wiki/Physical_optics" title="Physical optics">physical optics</a>, for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photons" title="Photons">photons</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutrons" title="Neutrons">neutrons</a> or rarefied gases. In <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_physics" title="Mathematical physics">mathematical physics</a>, this law arises as a solution of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bhatnagar-Gross-Krook" title="Bhatnagar-Gross-Krook">BGK equation</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beer%E2%80%93Lambert_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The law was discovered by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Bouguer" title="Pierre Bouguer">Pierre Bouguer</a> before 1729.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is often attributed to <a href="/wiki/Johann_Heinrich_Lambert" title="Johann Heinrich Lambert">Johann Heinrich Lambert</a>, who cited Bouguer's <i>Essai d'optique sur la gradation de la lumière</i> (Claude Jombert, Paris, 1729)—and even quoted from it—in his <i><a href="/wiki/Photometria" title="Photometria">Photometria</a></i> in 1760.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Lambert's law stated that absorbance of a material sample is directly proportional to its thickness (path length). Much later, <a href="/wiki/August_Beer" title="August Beer">August Beer</a> discovered another attenuation relation in 1852. Beer's law stated that absorbance is proportional to the concentrations of the attenuating species in the material sample.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The modern derivation of the Beer–Lambert law combines the two laws and correlates the absorbance to both the concentrations of the attenuating species and the thickness of the material sample.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Validity">Validity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beer%E2%80%93Lambert_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Validity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Under certain conditions Beer–Lambert law fails to maintain a linear relationship between attenuation and concentration of <a href="/wiki/Analyte" title="Analyte">analyte</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> These deviations are classified into three categories:
</p>
<ol><li>Real—fundamental deviations due to the limitations of the law itself.</li>
<li>Chemical—deviations observed due to specific chemical species of the sample which is being analyzed.</li>
<li>Instrument—deviations which occur due to how the attenuation measurements are made.</li></ol>
<p>There are at least six conditions that need to be fulfilled in order for Beer–Lambert law to be valid. These are:
</p>
<ol><li>The attenuators must act independently of each other.</li>
<li>The attenuating medium must be homogeneous in the interaction volume.</li>
<li>The attenuating medium must not scatter the radiation—no <a href="/wiki/Turbidity" title="Turbidity">turbidity</a>—unless this is accounted for as in <a href="/wiki/Differential_optical_absorption_spectroscopy" title="Differential optical absorption spectroscopy">DOAS</a>.</li>
<li>The incident radiation must consist of parallel rays, each traversing the same length in the absorbing medium.</li>
<li>The incident radiation should preferably be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monochromatic" title="Monochromatic">monochromatic</a>, or have at least a width that is narrower than that of the attenuating transition. Otherwise a spectrometer as detector for the power is needed instead of a photodiode which has not a selective wavelength dependence.</li>
<li>The incident flux must not influence the atoms or molecules; it should only act as a non-invasive probe of the species under study. In particular, this implies that the light should not cause optical saturation or optical pumping, since such effects will deplete the lower level and possibly give rise to stimulated emission.</li></ol>
<p>If any of these conditions are not fulfilled, there will be deviations from Beer–Lambert law.
</p><p>The Beer–Lambert law is not compatible with <a href="/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations" title="Maxwell's equations">Maxwell's equations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup> Being strict, the law does not describe the transmittance through a medium, but the propagation within that medium. It can be made compatible with Maxwell's equations if the transmittance of a sample with solute is ratioed against the transmittance of the pure solvent which explains why it works so well in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectrophotometry,_ultraviolet" title="Spectrophotometry, ultraviolet">spectrophotometry</a>. As this is not possible for pure media, the uncritical employment of the Beer–Lambert law can easily generate errors of the order of 100% or more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup> In such cases it is necessary to apply the <a href="/wiki/Transfer-matrix_method_(optics)" title="Transfer-matrix method (optics)">Transfer-matrix method</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_analysis_by_spectrophotometry">Chemical analysis by spectrophotometry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beer%E2%80%93Lambert_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Chemical analysis by spectrophotometry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Therefore, measurements at two wavelengths yields two equations in two unknowns and will suffice to determine the amount concentrations <i>c</i><sub>1</sub> and <i>c</i><sub>2</sub> as long as the molar attenuation coefficient of the two components, <i>ε</i><sub>1</sub> and <i>ε</i><sub>2</sub> are known at both wavelengths. Mixtures containing more than two components can be analyzed in the same way, using a minimum of <i>N</i> wavelengths for a mixture containing <i>N</i> components.
</p><p>The law is used widely in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infra-red_spectroscopy" title="Infra-red spectroscopy">infra-red spectroscopy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Near-infrared_spectroscopy" title="Near-infrared spectroscopy">near-infrared spectroscopy</a> for analysis of <a href="/wiki/Polymer_degradation" title="Polymer degradation">polymer degradation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">oxidation</a> (also in biological tissue) as well as to measure the <a href="/wiki/Concentration" title="Concentration">concentration</a> of various compounds in different <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> samples. The <a href="/wiki/Carbonyl_group" title="Carbonyl group">carbonyl group</a> attenuation at about 6 micrometres can be detected quite easily, and degree of oxidation of the <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a> calculated.
</p>
<h2><span id="Beer.E2.80.93Lambert_law_in_the_atmosphere"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Beer–Lambert_law_in_the_atmosphere">Beer–Lambert law in the atmosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beer%E2%80%93Lambert_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Beer–Lambert law in the atmosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

This equation can be used to retrieve <i>τ</i><sub>a</sub>, the aerosol <a href="/wiki/Optical_depth" title="Optical depth">optical thickness</a>, which is necessary for the correction of satellite images and also important in accounting for the role of aerosols in climate.
</p>
