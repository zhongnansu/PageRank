<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Coalbed methane</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Coalbed methane</b> (<b>CBM</b> or <b>coal-bed methane</b>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bg_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-bg-1">[1]</a></sup> <b>coalbed gas</b>, <b>coal seam gas</b> (<b>CSG</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bg_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-bg-1">[1]</a></sup>), or <b>coal-mine methane</b> (<b>CMM</b>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is a form of <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> extracted from <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> beds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In recent decades it has become an important source of energy in United States, Canada, Australia, and other countries.
</p><p>The term refers to <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorbed</a> into the solid matrix of the coal. It is called 'sweet gas' because of its lack of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>. The presence of this gas is well known from <a href="/wiki/Firedamp" title="Firedamp">its occurrence</a> in underground coal mining, where it presents a serious safety risk. Coalbed methane is distinct from a typical <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> or other conventional gas reservoir, as the methane is stored within the coal by a process called <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a>. The methane is in a near-liquid state, lining the inside of pores within the coal (called the matrix). The open fractures in the coal (called the cleats) can also contain free gas or can be saturated with water.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Unlike much natural gas from conventional reservoirs, coalbed methane contains very little heavier hydrocarbons such as <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a> or <a href="/wiki/Butane" title="Butane">butane</a>, and no <a href="/wiki/Natural-gas_condensate" title="Natural-gas condensate">natural-gas condensate</a>. It often contains up to a few percent <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalbed_methane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Coalbed methane grew out of venting methane from coal seams. Some coal beds have long been known to be "gassy," and as a safety measure, boreholes were drilled into the seams from the surface, and the methane allowed to vent before mining.
</p><p>Coalbed methane as a natural-gas resource received a major push from the US federal government in the late 1970s. Federal price controls were discouraging natural gas drilling by keeping natural gas prices below market levels; at the same time, the government wanted to encourage more gas production. The US Department of Energy funded research into a number of unconventional gas sources, including coalbed methane. Coalbed methane was exempted from federal price controls, and was also given a federal tax credit.
</p><p>In Australia, commercial extraction of coal seam gas began in 1996 in the <a href="/wiki/Bowen_Basin" title="Bowen Basin">Bowen Basin</a> of <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reservoir_properties">Reservoir properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalbed_methane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reservoir properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gas contained in coal bed methane is mainly methane and trace quantities of <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">ethane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and few other gases. Intrinsic properties of coal as found in nature determine the amount of gas that can be recovered.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Porosity">Porosity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalbed_methane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Porosity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Coalbed methane reservoirs are considered as a dual-porosity reservoirs. Dual porosity reservoirs are reservoirs in which porosity related to cleats (natural fractures) are responsible for flow behavior and reservoir porosity of the matrix is responsible for the storage of gas. The <a href="/wiki/Porosity" title="Porosity">porosity</a> of a coalbed methane reservoir can vary from 10%-20 %; However, the cleat porosity of the reservoir is estimated to be in the range of 0.1%-1 % <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adsorption_capacity">Adsorption capacity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalbed_methane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Adsorption capacity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Adsorption capacity of coal is defined as the volume of gas adsorbed per unit mass of coal usually expressed in SCF (<i>standard cubic feet</i>, the volume at standard pressure and temperature conditions) gas/ton of coal. The capacity to adsorb depends on the rank and quality of coal. The range is usually between 100 and 800 SCF/ton for most coal seams found in the US. Most of the gas in coal beds is in the adsorbed form. When the reservoir is put into production, water in the fracture spaces is pumped off first. This leads to a reduction of pressure enhancing desorption of gas from the matrix.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fracture_permeability">Fracture permeability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalbed_methane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fracture permeability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As discussed before, the fracture permeability acts as the major channel for the gas to flow. The higher the permeability, the higher the gas production. For most coal seams found in the US, the permeability lies in the range of 0.1–50 milliDarcys. The permeability of fractured reservoirs changes with the stress applied to them. Coal displays a stress-sensitive permeability and this process plays an important role during stimulation and production operations<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dx.doi.org_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-dx.doi.org-7">[7]</a></sup> Fracture permeability in Coalbed methane reservoir tends to increase with depletion of gas; in contrast to conventional reservoirs. This unique behavior is because of shrinking of coal, when methane is released from its matrix, which results in opening up of fractures and increased permeability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> It is also believed that due to shrinkage of coal matrix at lower reservoir pressures, there is a loss of horizontal stress in the reservoir which induces in-situ failure of coal. Such a failure has been attributed to sudden decrease in the fracture permeability of the reservoir<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dx.doi.org_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-dx.doi.org-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thickness_of_formation_and_initial_reservoir_pressure">Thickness of formation and initial reservoir pressure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalbed_methane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Thickness of formation and initial reservoir pressure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The thickness of the formation may not be directly proportional to the volume of gas produced in some areas.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>For example, it has been observed in the <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_Kansas#Subsurface_geology" title="Geology of Kansas">Cherokee Basin</a> in Southeast Kansas that a well with a single zone of 1 to 2 feet (0.3 to 0.6 m) of pay can produce excellent gas rates, whereas an alternative formation with twice the thickness can produce next to nothing. Some coal (and shale) formations may have high gas concentrations regardless of the formation's thickness, probably due to other factors of the area's geology.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The pressure difference between the well block and the sand face should be as high as possible as is the case with any producing reservoir in general.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_properties">Other properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalbed_methane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Other affecting parameters include coal density, initial gas-phase concentration, critical gas saturation, irreducible water saturation, relative permeability to water and gas at conditions of Sw = 1.0 and Sg = 1-Swirreducible respectively.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraction">Extraction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalbed_methane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Extraction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CBM_Production_Curve.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="375" data-file-width="525" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/CBM_Production_Curve.png/220px-CBM_Production_Curve.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/CBM_Production_Curve.png/330px-CBM_Production_Curve.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/CBM_Production_Curve.png/440px-CBM_Production_Curve.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CBM_Production_Curve.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Typical production profile of a coalbed methane well (USGS)</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_bed_methane_extraction" title="Coal bed methane extraction">Coal bed methane extraction</a></div>
<p>To extract the gas, a steel-encased hole is drilled into the coal seam 100 to 1,500 metres (330 to 4,920 ft) below ground. As the pressure within the coal seam declines due to natural production or the pumping of water from the coalbed, both gas and <i>produced water</i> come to the surface through tubing. Then the gas is sent to a compressor station and into natural gas pipelines. The produced water is either reinjected into isolated formations, released into streams, used for irrigation, or sent to evaporation ponds. The water typically contains dissolved solids such as <a href="/wiki/Sodium_bicarbonate" title="Sodium bicarbonate">sodium bicarbonate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chloride</a> but varies depending on the formation geology.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Coalbed methane wells often produce at lower gas rates than conventional reservoirs, typically peaking at near 300,000 cubic feet (8,500 m<sup>3</sup>) per day (about 0.100 m³/s), and can have large initial costs. The production profiles of CBM wells are typically characterized by a "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Negative_decline&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Negative decline (page does not exist)">negative decline</a>" in which the gas production rate initially increases as the water is pumped off and gas begins to desorb and flow. A dry CBM well is similar to a standard gas well.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The methane desorption process follows a curve (of gas content vs. reservoir pressure) called a <a href="/wiki/Adsorption#Langmuir" title="Adsorption">Langmuir isotherm</a>. The isotherm can be analytically described by a maximum gas content (at infinite pressure), and the pressure at which half that gas exists within the coal. These parameters (called the Langmuir volume and Langmuir pressure, respectively) are properties of the coal, and vary widely. A coal in <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a> and a coal in <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a> may have radically different Langmuir parameters, despite otherwise similar coal properties.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>As production occurs from a coal reservoir, the changes in pressure are believed to cause changes in the porosity and permeability of the coal. This is commonly known as matrix shrinkage/swelling. As the gas is desorbed, the pressure exerted by the gas inside the pores decreases, causing them to shrink in size and restricting gas flow through the coal. As the pores shrink, the overall matrix shrinks as well, which may eventually increase the space the gas can travel through (the cleats), increasing gas flow.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The potential of a particular coalbed as a CBM source depends on the following criteria. Cleat density/intensity: cleats are joints confined within coal sheets. They impart permeability to the coal seam. A high cleat density is required for profitable exploitation of CBM. Also important is the maceral composition: <a href="/wiki/Maceral" title="Maceral">maceral</a> is a microscopic, homogeneous, petrographic entity of a corresponding sedimentary rock. A high vitrinite composition is ideal for CBM extraction, while inertinite hampers the same.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The rank of coal has also been linked to CBM content: a vitrinite reflectance of 0.8–1.5% has been found to imply higher productivity of the coalbed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The gas composition must be considered, because natural gas appliances are designed for gas with a heating value of about 1,000 BTU (<a href="/wiki/British_thermal_unit" title="British thermal unit">British thermal units</a>) per cubic foot, or nearly pure methane. If the gas contains more than a few percent non-flammable gases such as <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> or <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, either these will have to be removed or it will have to be blended with higher-BTU gas to achieve <i>pipeline quality</i>. If the methane composition of the coalbed gas is less than 92%, it may not be commercially marketable.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impacts">Environmental impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalbed_methane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Environmental impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Methane">Methane</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalbed_methane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Methane">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As with all carbon based fossil fuels, burning coalbed methane releases carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>) into the atmosphere. Its effect as greenhouse gas was firstly analyzed by chemist and physicist <a href="/wiki/Svante_Arrhenius" title="Svante Arrhenius">Svante Arrhenius</a>. CBM production also entails leaks of fugitive methane into the atmosphere. Methane is rated as having 72 times the effect on global warming per unit of mass than CO<sub>2.</sub> over 20 years, reducing to 25 times over 100 years and 7.5 times over 500 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TableOfWarmingPotentials_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-TableOfWarmingPotentials-10">[10]</a></sup> Analysis of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life-cycle_greenhouse_gas_emissions_of_energy_sources" title="Life-cycle greenhouse gas emissions of energy sources">life-cycle greenhouse gas emissions of energy sources</a> indicates that generating electricity from CBM, as with conventional natural gas, has less than half the greenhouse gas effect of coal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States, methane escaping from coal during mining amounts to 10 percent of total <a href="/wiki/Methane_emissions" title="Methane emissions">methane emissions</a>. Recovery of coal mine methane in advance of mining is seen as a major opportunity to reduce methane emissions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrastructure">Infrastructure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalbed_methane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Infrastructure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CBM wells are connected by a network of roads, pipelines, and compressor stations. Over time, wells may be spaced more closely in order to extract the remaining methane.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Produced_water">Produced water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalbed_methane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Produced water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Produced_water" title="Produced water">produced water</a> brought to the surface as a byproduct of gas extraction varies greatly in quality from area to area, but may contain undesirable concentrations of dissolved substances such as <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a>, naturally present chemicals, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_(chemistry)" title="Heavy metal (chemistry)">heavy metals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radionuclide" title="Radionuclide">radionuclides</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABC-CSGbyNumbers_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-CSGbyNumbers-12">[12]</a></sup> In many producing regions the water is treated, such as through a <a href="/wiki/Reverse_osmosis_plant" title="Reverse osmosis plant">Reverse Osmosis plant</a> and used beneficially for irrigation, water for livestock, urban and industrial uses, or dust suppression.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Pilliga_Scrub">Pilliga Scrub</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalbed_methane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Pilliga Scrub">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 2012 Eastern Star Gas was fined for "discharging polluting water containing high levels of salt into Bohena Creek" in the Pilliga Scrub.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPAmedia12070601_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPAmedia12070601-13">[13]</a></sup> There were "16 spills or leaks of contaminated water" including "serious spills of saline water into woodland and a creek."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MiningAust_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-MiningAust-14">[14]</a></sup> In 2012, a NSW Legislative Council<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSW-Parl-Inq_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-NSW-Parl-Inq-15">[15]</a></sup> inquiry criticised the use of open holding ponds, recommending that "the NSW Government ban the open storage of produced water."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSW-Parl-Inq_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-NSW-Parl-Inq-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GlobalMail_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-GlobalMail-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Powder_River_Basin">Powder River Basin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalbed_methane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Powder River Basin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Not all coalbed methane produced water is saline or otherwise undesirable. Water from coalbed methane wells in the <a href="/wiki/Powder_River_Basin" title="Powder River Basin">Powder River Basin</a> of Wyoming, US, commonly meets federal drinking water standards, and is widely used in the area to water livestock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Its use for irrigation is limited by its relatively high <a href="/wiki/Sodium_adsorption_ratio" title="Sodium adsorption ratio">sodium adsorption ratio</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Groundwater">Groundwater</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalbed_methane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Groundwater">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Depending on aquifer connectivity, water withdrawal may depress aquifers over a large area and affect groundwater flows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In Australia, the CBM industry estimates extraction of 126,000 million litres (3.3<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>10</sup> US gallons) to 280,000 million litres (7.4<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>10</sup> US gallons) of groundwater per year; while the <a href="/wiki/National_Water_Commission" title="National Water Commission">National Water Commission</a> estimates extraction above 300,000 million litres (7.9<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>10</sup> US gallons) a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABC-CSGbyNumbers_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-CSGbyNumbers-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coalbed_methane_producing_areas">Coalbed methane producing areas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalbed_methane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Coalbed methane producing areas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalbed_methane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Coal_in_Australia" title="Coal in Australia">Coal in Australia</a></div>
<p>Coal Seam Gas resources are in the major coal basins in Queensland and New South Wales, with further potential resources in South Australia. Commercial recovery of coal seam gas (CSG) began in Australia in 1996. As of 2014, coal seam gas, from Queensland and New South Wales, made up about ten percent of Australia's gas production. Demonstrated reserves were estimated to be 33 trillion cubic feet (35 905 petajoules) as of January 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>

<li><a href="/wiki/Surat_Basin" title="Surat Basin">Surat Basin</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sydney_Basin" title="Sydney Basin">Sydney Basin</a></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalbed_methane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Canada, <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a> is estimated to have approximately 90 trillion cubic feet (2.5 trillion cubic metres) of coalbed gas. <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>, in 2013, was the only province with commercial coalbed methane wells and is estimated to have approximately 170 trillion cubic feet (4.8 trillion cubic metres) of economically recoverable coalbed methane, with overall reserves totaling up to 500 trillion cubic feet (14 trillion cubic metres).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Coalbed methane is considered a <a href="/wiki/Non-renewable_resource" title="Non-renewable resource">non-renewable resource</a>, although the <a href="/wiki/Alberta_Research_Council" title="Alberta Research Council">Alberta Research Council</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alberta_Geological_Survey" title="Alberta Geological Survey">Alberta Geological Survey</a> and others have argued coalbed methane is a <a href="/wiki/Renewable_resource" title="Renewable resource">renewable resource</a> because the bacterial action that formed the methane is ongoing.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This is subject to debate since it has also been shown that the dewatering that accompanies CBM production destroys the conditions needed for the bacteria to produce methane<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and the rate of formation of additional methane is undetermined.  This debate is currently causing a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right_of_ownership" title="Right of ownership">right of ownership</a> issue in the <a href="/wiki/Provinces_and_territories_of_Canada" title="Provinces and territories of Canada">Canadian province</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>, as only non-renewable resources can legally be owned by the province.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Western_Canadian_Sedimentary_Basin" title="Western Canadian Sedimentary Basin">Western Canadian Sedimentary Basin</a></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalbed_methane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although gas in place in Britain's coal fields has been estimated to be 2,900 billion cubic meters, it may be that as little as one percent might be economically recoverable. Britain's CBM potential is largely untested. Some methane is extracted by coal mine venting operations, and burned to generate electricity. Assessment by private industry of coalbed methane wells independent of mining began in 2008, when 55 onshore exploration licences were issued, covering 7,000 square kilometers of potential coalbed methane areas. <a href="/wiki/IGas_Energy" title="IGas Energy">IGas Energy</a> became the first in the UK to commercially extract coalbed methane separate from mine venting; as of 2012, the Igas coalbed methane wells at Doe Green, extracting gas for electrical generation, were the only commercial CBM wells in the UK.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalbed_methane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Coalbed_methane_in_the_United_States" title="Coalbed methane in the United States">Coalbed methane in the United States</a></div>
<p>United States coalbed methane production in 2011 was 1.76 trillion cubic feet (TCF), 7.3 percent of all US dry gas production that year.  The 2011 production was down from the peak of 1.97 TCF in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Most CBM production came from the Rocky Mountain states of Colorado, Wyoming, and New Mexico.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalbed_methane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Kazakhstan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kazakhstan could witness the development of a large coalbed methane (CBM) sector over the coming decades, according to industry professionals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Preliminary research suggests there may be as much as 900 billion m3 of gas in Kazakhstan’s main coalfields – 85% of all reserves in Kazakhstan.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Coalbed_methane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With the completion of the drilling of 23 vertical production wells by <a href="/wiki/Great_Eastern_Energy" title="Great Eastern Energy">Great Eastern Energy</a> (GEECL), coalbed methane would be available in India for commercial sale purpose from 14 July 2007 priced at ₹ 30 per kg for CNG. Initially 90% of the CBM would be distributed among vehicles as CNG gas.
GEECL is also setting up the first CBM station in Southeast Asia and the same will be located in India in the city of Asansol in West Bengal. GEECL is the first company whose first field development plan has been approved.Essar is also producing and investing in CBM.
</p><p>Prashant Modi, President and Chief Operating Officer of GEECL, said, "We are proud to be India’s first private sector company that has ventured into Coal Bed Methane exploration, production, marketing and distribution. With the nation requiring higher energy sources to sustain its development pace, we are confident that CBM will play an important role as one of the prime energy source for the future generations."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
