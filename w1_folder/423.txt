<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Metabolite</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>metabolite</b> is the intermediate end product of <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a>. The term <i>metabolite</i> is usually restricted to <a href="/wiki/Small_molecule" title="Small molecule">small molecules</a>. Metabolites have various functions, including fuel, structure, signaling, stimulatory and inhibitory effects on <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a>, catalytic activity of their own (usually as a <a href="/wiki/Cofactor_(biochemistry)" title="Cofactor (biochemistry)">cofactor</a> to an enzyme), defense, and interactions with other organisms (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Biological_pigment" title="Biological pigment">pigments</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Odorant" title="Odorant">odorants</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pheromone" title="Pheromone">pheromones</a>). A <b>primary metabolite</b> is directly involved in normal "growth", development, and reproduction. <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">Ethylene</a> is an example of a primary metabolite produced in large-scale by <a href="/wiki/Industrial_microbiology" title="Industrial microbiology">industrial microbiology</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Secondary_metabolite" title="Secondary metabolite">secondary metabolite</a> is not directly involved in those processes, but usually has an important <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecological</a> function. Examples include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibiotics" title="Antibiotics">antibiotics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigments</a> such as resins and terpenes etc. Some antibiotics use primary metabolites as precursors, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Actinomycin" title="Actinomycin">actinomycin</a> which is created from the primary metabolite, <a href="/wiki/Tryptophan" title="Tryptophan">tryptophan</a>. Some sugars are metabolites, such as fructose or glucose, which are both present in the metabolic pathways.

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Metabolome" title="Metabolome">metabolome</a> forms a large network of metabolic reactions, where outputs from one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enzymatic" title="Enzymatic">enzymatic</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reaction</a> are inputs to other chemical reactions.
</p><p>Metabolites from <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compounds</a>, whether inherent or pharmaceutical, are formed as part of the natural biochemical process of degrading and eliminating the compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  The rate of degradation of a compound is an important determinant of the duration and intensity of its action.  Profiling metabolites of pharmaceutical compounds, <a href="/wiki/Drug_metabolism" title="Drug metabolism">drug metabolism</a>, is an important part of <a href="/wiki/Drug_discovery" title="Drug discovery">drug discovery</a>, leading to an understanding of any undesirable <a href="/wiki/Side_effect" title="Side effect">side effects</a>.
</p>
