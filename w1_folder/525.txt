<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Carbon–hydrogen bond</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>carbon-hydrogen bond</b> (<b>C–H bond</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">bond</a> between <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> atoms that can be found in many <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-March_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-March-1">[1]</a></sup> This bond is a covalent bond meaning that carbon shares its outer valence electrons with up to four hydrogens.  This completes both of their outer shells making them stable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Carbon–hydrogen bonds have a <a href="/wiki/Bond_length" title="Bond length">bond length</a> of about 1.09 <a href="/wiki/%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="Ångström">Å</a> (1.09 × 10<sup>−10</sup> m) and a <a href="/wiki/Bond_energy" title="Bond energy">bond energy</a> of about 413 <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">kJ</a>/<a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mol</a> (see table below).  Using <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">Pauling's scale</a>—C (2.55) and H (2.2)—the electronegativity difference between these two atoms is 0.35. Because of this small difference in electronegativities, the C−H bond is generally regarded as being non-polar. In <a href="/wiki/Structural_formula" title="Structural formula">structural formulas</a> of molecules, the hydrogen atoms are often omitted. Compound classes consisting solely of C–H bonds and <a href="/wiki/Carbon%E2%80%93carbon_bond" title="Carbon–carbon bond">C–C</a> bonds are <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">alkanes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkenes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alkyne" title="Alkyne">alkynes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aromatic_hydrocarbon" title="Aromatic hydrocarbon">aromatic hydrocarbons</a>. Collectively they are known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrocarbons" title="Hydrocarbons">hydrocarbons</a>.
</p><p>In October 2016, astronomers reported that the very basic chemical ingredients of <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a>—the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon-hydrogen_bond" title="Carbon-hydrogen bond">carbon-hydrogen molecule</a> (CH, or <a href="/wiki/Methylidyne_radical" title="Methylidyne radical">methylidyne radical</a>), the carbon-hydrogen positive ion (CH+) and the carbon ion (C+)—are the result, in large part, of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_light" title="Ultraviolet light">ultraviolet light</a> from <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>, rather than in other ways, such as the result of turbulent events related to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supernovae" title="Supernovae">supernovae</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Young_star" title="Young star">young stars</a>, as thought earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20161012_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20161012-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon%E2%80%93hydrogen_bond&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Carbon%E2%80%93hydrogen_bond_activation" title="Carbon–hydrogen bond activation">Carbon–hydrogen bond activation</a></div>
<p>The C−H bond in general is very strong, so it is relatively unreactive. In several compound classes, collectively called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_acid" title="Carbon acid">carbon acids</a>, the C−H bond can be sufficiently acidic for proton removal. Unactivated C−H bonds are found in alkanes and are not adjacent to a <a href="/wiki/Heteroatom" title="Heteroatom">heteroatom</a> (O, N, Si, etc.). Such bonds usually only participate in <a href="/wiki/Radical_substitution" title="Radical substitution">radical substitution</a>.  Many enzymes are known, however, to affect these reactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the C−H bond is one of the strongest, it varies over 30% in magnitude for fairly stable organic compounds, even in the absence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heteroatoms" title="Heteroatoms">heteroatoms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
