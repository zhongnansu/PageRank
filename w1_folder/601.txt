<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Methanogenesis</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Methanogenesis</b> or <b>biomethanation</b> is the formation of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microbe" title="Microbe">microbes</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Methanogen" title="Methanogen">methanogens</a>. Organisms capable of producing methane have been identified only from the <a href="/wiki/Domain_(biology)" title="Domain (biology)">domain</a> <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">Archaea</a>, a group <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetics" title="Phylogenetics">phylogenetically</a> distinct from both <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryotes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, although many live in close association with anaerobic bacteria. The production of methane is an important and widespread form of microbial <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(environmental)" title="Hypoxia (environmental)">anoxic</a> environments, it is the final step in the decomposition of <a href="/wiki/Biomass_(ecology)" title="Biomass (ecology)">biomass</a>.  Methanogenesis is responsible for significant amounts of natural gas accumulations, the remainder being thermogenic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biochemistry">Biochemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biochemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Methanogenesis_cycle.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2714" data-file-width="3413" height="254" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Methanogenesis_cycle.png/320px-Methanogenesis_cycle.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Methanogenesis_cycle.png/480px-Methanogenesis_cycle.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Methanogenesis_cycle.png/640px-Methanogenesis_cycle.png 2x" width="320"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Methanogenesis_cycle.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cycle for methanogenesis, showing intermediates.</div></div></div>
<p>Methanogenesis in microbes is a form of <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_respiration" title="Anaerobic respiration">anaerobic respiration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RT_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-RT-4">[4]</a></sup> Methanogens do not use oxygen to respire; in fact, oxygen inhibits the growth of methanogens.  The terminal <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_agent#Electron_acceptor" title="Oxidizing agent">electron acceptor</a> in methanogenesis is not oxygen, but carbon.  The carbon can occur in a small number of organic compounds, all with low molecular weights.  The two best described pathways involve the use of <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a> or inorganic <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> as terminal electron acceptors:
</p>
<dl><dd><a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> + 4 H<sub>2</sub> → <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">CH<sub>4</sub></a> + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>CH<sub>3</sub>COOH → CH<sub>4</sub> + CO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>During anaerobic respiration of carbohydrates, H<sub>2</sub> and acetate are formed in a ratio of 2:1 or lower, so H<sub>2</sub> contributes only ca. 33% to methanogenesis, with acetate contributing the greater proportion. In some circumstances, for instance in the <a href="/wiki/Rumen" title="Rumen">rumen</a>, where acetate is largely absorbed into the bloodstream of the host, the contribution of H<sub>2</sub> to methanogenesis is greater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, depending on pH and temperature, methanogenesis has been shown to use carbon from other small organic compounds, such as <a href="/wiki/Formic_acid" title="Formic acid">formic acid</a> (formate), <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methylamines" title="Methylamines">methylamines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tetramethylammonium" title="Tetramethylammonium">tetramethylammonium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfide" title="Dimethyl sulfide">dimethyl sulfide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Methanethiol" title="Methanethiol">methanethiol</a>.  The catabolism of the methyl compounds is mediated by methyl transferases to give methyl coenzyme M.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RT_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-RT-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_mechanism">Proposed mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Proposed mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The biochemistry of methanogenesis involves the following coenzymes and cofactors: <a href="/wiki/Coenzyme_F420" title="Coenzyme F420">F420</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coenzyme_B" title="Coenzyme B">coenzyme B</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coenzyme_M" title="Coenzyme M">coenzyme M</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methanofuran" title="Methanofuran">methanofuran</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methanopterin" title="Methanopterin">methanopterin</a>.
</p><p>The mechanism for the conversion of <span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>–S</span> bond into methane involves a ternary complex of methyl coenzyme M and coenzyme B fit into a channel terminated by the axial site on nickel of the cofactor F430.  One proposed mechanism invokes electron transfer from Ni(I) (to give Ni(II)), which initiates formation of <span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>.  Coupling of the coenzyme M <a href="/wiki/Thiyl_radical" title="Thiyl radical">thiyl radical</a> (RS<sup>.</sup>) with HS coenzyme B releases a proton and re-reduces Ni(II) by one-electron, regenerating Ni(I).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reverse_methanogenesis">Reverse methanogenesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reverse methanogenesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some organisms can oxidize methane, functionally reversing the process of methanogenesis, also referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_oxidation_of_methane" title="Anaerobic oxidation of methane">anaerobic oxidation of methane</a> (AOM). Organisms performing AOM have been found in multiple marine and freshwater environments including methane seeps, hydrothermal vents, coastal sediments and sulfate-methane transition zones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  These organisms may accomplish reverse methanogenesis using a nickel-containing protein similar to <a href="/wiki/Coenzyme-B_sulfoethylthiotransferase" title="Coenzyme-B sulfoethylthiotransferase">methyl-coenzyme M reductase</a> used by methanogenic archaea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Reverse methanogenesis occurs according to the reaction:
</p>
<dl><dd>SO<sub>4</sub><sup>2−</sup> +  CH<sub>4</sub>   →   HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>  + HS<sup>−</sup> + H<sub>2</sub>O<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Importance_in_carbon_cycle">Importance in carbon cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Importance in carbon cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Methanogenesis is the final step in the decay of organic matter.  During the decay process, <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_agent#Electron_acceptor" title="Oxidizing agent">electron acceptors</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ferric" title="Ferric">ferric</a> <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfate</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrate</a>) become depleted, while <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> (H<sub>2</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> accumulate.  Light organics produced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">fermentation</a> also accumulate.  During advanced stages of organic decay, all electron acceptors become depleted except carbon dioxide.  Carbon dioxide is a product of most catabolic processes, so it is not depleted like other potential electron acceptors.
</p><p>Only methanogenesis and fermentation can occur in the absence of electron acceptors other than carbon.  Fermentation only allows the breakdown of larger organic compounds, and produces small organic compounds.  Methanogenesis effectively removes the semi-final products of decay: hydrogen, small organics, and carbon dioxide.  Without methanogenesis, a great deal of carbon (in the form of fermentation products) would accumulate in anaerobic environments.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_occurrence">Natural occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Natural occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_ruminants">In ruminants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In ruminants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CSIRO_ScienceImage_1898_Testing_Sheep_for_Methane_Production.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="630" data-file-width="945" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/CSIRO_ScienceImage_1898_Testing_Sheep_for_Methane_Production.jpg/220px-CSIRO_ScienceImage_1898_Testing_Sheep_for_Methane_Production.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/CSIRO_ScienceImage_1898_Testing_Sheep_for_Methane_Production.jpg/330px-CSIRO_ScienceImage_1898_Testing_Sheep_for_Methane_Production.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/CSIRO_ScienceImage_1898_Testing_Sheep_for_Methane_Production.jpg/440px-CSIRO_ScienceImage_1898_Testing_Sheep_for_Methane_Production.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CSIRO_ScienceImage_1898_Testing_Sheep_for_Methane_Production.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Testing Australian sheep for exhaled methane production (2001), <a href="/wiki/CSIRO" title="CSIRO">CSIRO</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Enteric_fermentation" title="Enteric fermentation">Enteric fermentation</a> occurs in the gut of some animals, especially ruminants. In the <a href="/wiki/Rumen" title="Rumen">rumen</a>, anaerobic organisms, including methanogens, digest cellulose into forms nutritious to the animal.  Without these microorganisms, animals such as cattle would not be able to consume grasses. The useful products of methanogenesis are absorbed by the gut, but methane is released from the animal mainly by belching (eructation). The average cow emits around 250 liters of methane per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>  In this way, ruminants contribute about 25% of anthropogenic <a href="/wiki/Methane_emissions" title="Methane emissions">methane emissions</a>. One method of methane production control in ruminants is by feeding them <a href="/wiki/3-Nitrooxypropanol" title="3-Nitrooxypropanol">3-nitrooxypropanol</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_humans">In humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some humans produce <a href="/wiki/Flatulence" title="Flatulence">flatus</a> that contains methane.  In one study of the <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">feces</a> of nine adults, five of the samples contained <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a> capable of producing methane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>  Similar results are found in samples of gas obtained from within the <a href="/wiki/Rectum" title="Rectum">rectum</a>.
</p><p>Even among humans whose flatus does contain methane, the amount is in the range of 10% or less of the total amount of gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_plants">In plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many experiments have suggested that <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaf</a> tissues of living plants emit methane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Other research has indicated that the plants are not actually generating methane; they are just absorbing methane from the soil and then emitting it through their leaf tissues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soils">Soils</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Soils">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Methanogens are observed in anoxic soil environments, contributing to the degradation of organic matter.  This organic matter may be placed by humans through landfill, buried as sediment on the bottom of lakes or oceans as sediments, and as residual organic matter from sediments that have formed into sedimentary rocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_global_warming">Role in global warming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Role in global warming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_methane" title="Atmospheric methane">Atmospheric methane</a> is an important <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_potential" title="Global warming potential">global warming potential</a> 25 times greater than carbon dioxide (averaged over 100 years),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and methanogenesis in <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a> and the decay of organic material is thus a considerable contributor to global warming. It may not be a net contributor in the sense that it works on organic material which used up atmospheric carbon dioxide when it was created, but its overall effect is to convert the carbon dioxide into methane which is a much more potent greenhouse gas.
</p><p>Methanogenesis can also be beneficially exploited, to treat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_waste" title="Organic waste">organic waste</a>, to produce useful compounds, and the methane can be collected and used as <a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">biogas</a>, a fuel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> It is the primary pathway whereby most organic matter disposed of via <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfill</a> is broken down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extra-terrestrial_life">Extra-terrestrial life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Extra-terrestrial life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Methane#Extraterrestrial_methane" title="Methane">Methane § Extraterrestrial methane</a></div>
<p>The presence of atmospheric methane has a role in the scientific search for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extra-terrestrial_life" title="Extra-terrestrial life">extra-terrestrial life</a>. The justification is that methane in the atmosphere will eventually dissipate, unless something is replenishing it. If methane is detected (by using a <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">spectrometer</a> for example) this may indicate that life is, or recently was, present.
This was debated<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> when methane was discovered in the Martian atmosphere by M.J. Mumma of NASA's Goddard Flight Center, and verified by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars_Express_Orbiter" title="Mars Express Orbiter">Mars Express Orbiter</a> (2004)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and in <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>'s atmosphere by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huygens_probe" title="Huygens probe">Huygens probe</a> (2005).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> This debate was furthered with the discovery of 'transient', 'spikes of methane' on Mars by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curiosity_Rover" title="Curiosity Rover">Curiosity Rover</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is also argued that <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_methane" title="Atmospheric methane">atmospheric methane</a> can come from volcanoes or other fissures in the planet's crust and that without an <a href="/wiki/Isotopic_signature" title="Isotopic signature">isotopic signature</a>, the origin or source may be difficult to identify.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 13 April 2017, NASA confirmed that the dive of the <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">Cassini orbiter</a> spacecraft on 28 October 2015 discovered the <a href="/wiki/Enceladus" title="Enceladus">Enceladus</a> plume which has all the ingredients for methanogenesis-based life forms to feed from. Previous results, published in March 2015, suggested hot water is interacting with rock beneath the sea; the new findings support that conclusion and add that the rock appears to be reacting chemically. From these observations scientists have determined that nearly 98 percent of the gas in the plume is water, about 1 percent is hydrogen and the rest is a mixture of other molecules including carbon dioxide, methane and ammonia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
