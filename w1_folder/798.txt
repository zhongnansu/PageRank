<p>
<b>John Anthony Allan</b> (born 2 January 1937), sometime cited as Tony Allan, is a British <a href="/wiki/Geographer" title="Geographer">geographer</a>. He was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Stockholm_Water_Prize" title="Stockholm Water Prize">Stockholm Water Prize</a> in 2008 for his revolutionary <a href="/wiki/Virtual_water" title="Virtual water">virtual water</a> concept. Although being an emeritus of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/School_of_Oriental_and_African_Studies" title="School of Oriental and African Studies">School of Oriental and African Studies</a> and <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College_London" title="King's College London">King's College London</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_London" title="University of London">University of London</a>, he still acts as a teaching Professor at <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College_London" title="King's College London">King's College London</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Anthony_Allan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Allan received a B.A. from <a href="/wiki/Durham_University" title="Durham University">Durham University</a> and a PhD from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/School_of_Oriental_and_African_Studies" title="School of Oriental and African Studies">School of Oriental and African Studies</a> in London.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Anthony_Allan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Having been a long time water analyst with emphasis on the Middle East, he coined the term <a href="/wiki/Virtual_water" title="Virtual water">virtual water</a> in 1993 after having been inspired by Gideon Fishelson from Tel Aviv University, who criticised his government over the amount of water that had been used to produce and export citrus fruits to the European Union. Allan then researched trade figures of Middle Eastern states to conclude that this water-scarce region was only able to survive through large quantities of food imports in grain, livestock etc. Thus, the region was not dependent on its own scarce water resources but could purchase water already embedded in agricultural produce. It was this logic that enabled Tony Allan to challenge the then prevailing thesis that the wars in the next century will be fought over water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In essence he explained how the importation of water embedded mostly in cereals into water-scarce regions of the world via trade explained why wars over water were both unnecessary and unlikely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This opened up a plethora of new research directions for his many graduate students working in water constrained parts of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> His seminal work entitled The Middle East Water Question: Hydropolitics and the Global Economy <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> captured a lifetime of research and has become a cornerstone for policy-makers and researchers. A significant contribution to this paradigm shift has been the emergence of the concept of benefit-sharing as opposed to water-sharing,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> or the management of transboundary waters as a public good.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stockholm_Water_Prize">Stockholm Water Prize</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Anthony_Allan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Stockholm Water Prize">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In March 2008, Allan was announced to receive the Stockholm Water Prize, which is the equivalent to an environmental Nobel Prize according to some commentators. The international nominated Allan for it due to 'his unique, pioneering and long lasting work in education and raising the awareness internationally of interdisciplinary relationships between agricultural production, water use, economies and political processes'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
