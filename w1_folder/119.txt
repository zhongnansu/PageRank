<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Fluorocarbon</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Fluorocarbons</b>,  sometimes referred to as <b>perfluorocarbons</b> or <b>PFCs</b>, are, strictly speaking, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organofluorine" title="Organofluorine">organofluorine</a> compounds with the formula C<sub>x</sub>F<sub>y</sub>, i.e. they contain only <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-1">[1]</a></sup> though the terminology is not strictly followed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lemal_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lemal-2">[2]</a></sup>
Compounds with the prefix <b>perfluoro-</b> are hydrocarbons, including those with heteroatoms, wherein all C-H bonds have been replaced by C-F bonds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murphy_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Murphy-3">[3]</a></sup>
Fluorocarbons can be perfluoroalkanes, fluoroalkenes and fluoroalkynes and perfluoroaromatic compounds. Fluorocarbons and their derivatives are used as <a href="/wiki/Fluoropolymer" title="Fluoropolymer">fluoropolymers</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Refrigerants" title="Refrigerants">refrigerants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvents</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anesthetic" title="Anesthetic">anesthetics</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Perfluorocarbons or PFCs are, strictly speaking, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organofluorine" title="Organofluorine">organofluorine</a> compounds with the formula C<sub>x</sub>F<sub>y</sub>, i.e. they contain only <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-1">[1]</a></sup> though the terminology is not strictly followed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lemal_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lemal-2">[2]</a></sup>
Compounds with the prefix <b>perfluoro-</b> are <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a>, including those with <a href="/wiki/Heteroatom" title="Heteroatom">heteroatoms</a>, wherein all C-H bonds have been replaced by C-F bonds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murphy_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Murphy-3">[3]</a></sup> Fluorocarbons can be divided into perfluoroalkanes, fluoroalkenes and fluoroalkynes or perfluoroaromatic compounds.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Perfluoroalkanes">Perfluoroalkanes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Perfluoroalkanes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Perfluoroalkanes are very stable because of the strength of the <a href="/wiki/Carbon%E2%80%93fluorine_bond" title="Carbon–fluorine bond">carbon–fluorine bond</a>, one of the strongest in organic chemistry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hagan_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-hagan-4">[4]</a></sup>
Its strength is a result of the electronegativity of fluorine imparting partial ionic character through <a href="/wiki/Partial_charge" title="Partial charge">partial charges</a> on the carbon and fluorine atoms, which shorten and strengthen the bond through favorable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Covalent" title="Covalent">covalent</a> interactions. Additionally, multiple carbon–fluorine bonds increase the strength and stability of other nearby carbon–fluorine bonds on the same <a href="/wiki/Geminal" title="Geminal">geminal</a> carbon, as the carbon has a higher positive partial charge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lemal_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lemal-2">[2]</a></sup> Furthermore, multiple carbon–fluorine bonds also strengthen the "skeletal" carbon–carbon bonds from the <a href="/wiki/Inductive_effect" title="Inductive effect">inductive effect</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lemal_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lemal-2">[2]</a></sup> Therefore, <a href="/wiki/Saturation_(chemistry)" title="Saturation (chemistry)">saturated</a> fluorocarbons are more chemically and thermally stable than their corresponding hydrocarbon counterparts, and indeed any other organic compound. They are susceptible to attack by very strong reductants, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Birch_reduction" title="Birch reduction">Birch reduction</a> and very specialized organometallic complexes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kip_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-kip-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fluorocarbons are colorless and have high density, up to over twice that of water. They are not miscible with most organic solvents (e.g., ethanol, acetone, ethyl acetate, and chloroform), but are miscible with some hydrocarbons (e.g., hexane in some cases). They have very low solubility in water, and water has a very low solubility in them (on the order of 10 ppm). They have low <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive indices</a>.
</p>

<p>As the high <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">electronegativity</a> of fluorine reduces the <a href="/wiki/Polarizability" title="Polarizability">polarizability</a> of the atom,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lemal_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Lemal-2">[2]</a></sup> fluorocarbons are only weakly susceptible to the fleeting dipoles that form the basis of the <a href="/wiki/London_dispersion_force" title="London dispersion force">London dispersion force</a>. As a result, fluorocarbons have low intermolecular attractive forces and are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lipophobic" title="Lipophobic">lipophobic</a> in addition to being <a href="/wiki/Hydrophobe" title="Hydrophobe">hydrophobic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-polar" title="Non-polar">non-polar</a>. Reflecting the weak <a href="/wiki/Intermolecular_force" title="Intermolecular force">intermolecular forces</a> these compounds exhibit low <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosities</a> when compared to liquids of similar <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">boiling points</a>, low <a href="/wiki/Surface_tension" title="Surface tension">surface tension</a> and low <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_of_vaporization" title="Heat of vaporization">heats of vaporization</a>. The low <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Attractive_force&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Attractive force (page does not exist)">attractive forces</a> in fluorocarbon liquids make them <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compressible" title="Compressible">compressible</a> (low <a href="/wiki/Bulk_modulus" title="Bulk modulus">bulk modulus</a>) and able to dissolve gas relatively well. Smaller fluorocarbons are extremely <a href="/wiki/Volatility_(chemistry)" title="Volatility (chemistry)">volatile</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lemal_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Lemal-2">[2]</a></sup> There are five perfluoroalkane gases; <a href="/wiki/Tetrafluoromethane" title="Tetrafluoromethane">tetrafluoromethane</a> (bp −128 °C), <a href="/wiki/Hexafluoroethane" title="Hexafluoroethane">hexafluoroethane</a> (bp −78.2 °C), <a href="/wiki/Octafluoropropane" title="Octafluoropropane">octafluoropropane</a> (bp −36.5 °C), <a href="/wiki/Perfluorobutane" title="Perfluorobutane">perfluoro-n-butane</a> (bp −2.2 °C) and perfluoro-iso-butane (bp −1 °C). Nearly all other fluoroalkanes are liquids; the most notable exception is perfluorocyclohexane, which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sublimation_(chemistry)" title="Sublimation (chemistry)">sublimes</a> at 51 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Fluorocarbons also have low <a href="/wiki/Surface_energy" title="Surface energy">surface energies</a> and high <a href="/wiki/Dielectric" title="Dielectric">dielectric</a> strengths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lemal_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Lemal-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacture">Manufacture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Manufacture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The development of the fluorocarbon industry coincided with <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBee_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-McBee-14">[14]</a></sup> Prior to that, fluorocarbons were prepared by reaction of fluorine with the hydrocarbon, i.e., direct fluorination. Because C-C bonds are readily cleaved by fluorine, direct fluorination mainly affords smaller perfluorocarbons, such as tetrafluoromethane, hexafluoroethane, and octafluoropropane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fowler_process">Fowler process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fowler process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A major breakthrough that allowed the large scale manufacture of fluorocarbons was the <a href="/wiki/Fowler_process" title="Fowler process">Fowler process</a>. In this process, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cobalt_trifluoride" title="Cobalt trifluoride">cobalt trifluoride</a> is used as the source of fluorine. Illustrative is the synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Perfluorohexane" title="Perfluorohexane">perfluorohexane</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>14</sub> + 28 CoF<sub>3</sub> → C<sub>6</sub>F<sub>14</sub> + 14 HF + 28 CoF<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>The resulting cobalt difluoride is then regenerated, sometimes in a separate reactor:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 CoF<sub>2</sub> + F<sub>2</sub> → 2 CoF<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Industrially, both steps are combined, for example in the manufacture of the Flutec range of fluorocarbons by F2 chemicals Ltd, using a vertical stirred bed reactor, with hydrocarbon introduced at the bottom, and fluorine introduced halfway up the reactor. The fluorocarbon vapor is recovered from the top.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrochemical_fluorination">Electrochemical fluorination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Electrochemical fluorination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Electrochemical_fluorination" title="Electrochemical fluorination">Electrochemical fluorination</a> (ECF) (also known as the Simons' process) involves <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> of a substrate dissolved in <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">hydrogen fluoride</a>. As fluorine is itself manufactured by the electrolysis of hydrogen fluoride, ECF is a rather more direct route to fluorocarbons. The process proceeds at low voltage (5 – 6 V) so that free fluorine is not liberated. The choice of substrate is restricted as ideally it should be soluble in hydrogen fluoride. Ethers and tertiary amines are typically employed. To make perfluorohexane, trihexylamine is used, for example:
</p>
<dl><dd>N(C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>13</sub>)<sub>3</sub> + 45 HF → 3 C<sub>6</sub>F<sub>14</sub> + NF<sub>3</sub> + 42 H<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>The perfluorinated amine will also be produced:
</p>
<dl><dd>N(C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>13</sub>)<sub>3</sub> + 39 HF → N(C<sub>6</sub>F<sub>13</sub>)<sub>3</sub> + 39H<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_and_health_concerns">Environmental and health concerns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Environmental and health concerns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fluoroalkanes are generally inert and non-toxic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yamanouchi_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yamanouchi-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fluoroalkanes are not <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">ozone depleting</a>, as they contain no chlorine or bromine atoms, and they are sometimes used as replacements for ozone-depleting chemicals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
The term fluorocarbon is used rather loosely to include any chemical containing fluorine and carbon, including <a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon" title="Chlorofluorocarbon">chlorofluorocarbons</a>, which are ozone depleting. Fluoroalkanes are sometimes confused with <a href="/wiki/Fluorosurfactant" title="Fluorosurfactant">fluorosurfactants</a>, which significantly bioaccumulate.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Perfluoroalkanes do not bioaccumulate;<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> those used in medical procedures are rapidly excreted from the body, primarily via expiration with the rate of excretion as a function of the vapour pressure; the half-life for <a href="/wiki/Octafluoropropane" title="Octafluoropropane">octafluoropropane</a> is less than 2 minutes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-platts_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-platts-20">[20]</a></sup> compared to about a week for perfluorodecalin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Geyer_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Geyer-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Low-boiling perfluoroalkanes are potent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gases</a>, in part due to their very long atmospheric lifetime, and their use is covered by the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>The aluminium smelting industry has been a major source of atmospheric perfluorocarbons (<a href="/wiki/Tetrafluoromethane" title="Tetrafluoromethane">tetrafluoromethane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hexafluoroethane" title="Hexafluoroethane">hexafluoroethane</a> especially), produced as  by-product of the electrolysis process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> However, the industry has been actively involved in reducing emissions in recent years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As they are inert, perfluoroalkanes have essentially no chemical uses, but their physical properties have led to their use in many diverse applications. These include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Perfluorocarbon_tracer" title="Perfluorocarbon tracer">Perfluorocarbon tracer</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Liquid_dielectric" title="Liquid dielectric">Liquid dielectric</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chemical_vapor_deposition" title="Chemical vapor deposition">Chemical vapor deposition</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Organic_Rankine_cycle" title="Organic Rankine cycle">Organic Rankine cycle</a></li>
<li>Fluorous biphasic catalysis <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li>Cosmetics<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>As well as several medical uses:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Liquid_breathing" title="Liquid breathing">Liquid breathing</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Blood_substitute" title="Blood substitute">Blood substitute</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Contrast-enhanced_ultrasound" title="Contrast-enhanced ultrasound">Contrast-enhanced ultrasound</a></li>
<li>Eye surgery <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Imamura_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Imamura-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li>Tattoo removal <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li></ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polymerization">Polymerization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Polymerization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fluoroalkenes polymerize more exothermically than normal alkenes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lemal_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-Lemal-2">[2]</a></sup> Unsaturated fluorocarbons have a driving force towards sp<sup>3</sup> hybridization due to the electronegative fluorine atoms seeking a greater share of bonding electrons with reduced s character in orbitals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lemal_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-Lemal-2">[2]</a></sup> The most famous member of this class is <a href="/wiki/Tetrafluoroethylene" title="Tetrafluoroethylene">tetrafluoroethylene</a>, which is used to manufacture <a href="/wiki/Polytetrafluoroethylene" title="Polytetrafluoroethylene">polytetrafluoroethylene</a> (PTFE), better known under the trade name <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teflon" title="Teflon">Teflon</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_and_health_concerns_2">Environmental and health concerns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Environmental and health concerns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fluoroalkenes and fluorinated alkynes are reactive and many are toxic for example <a href="/wiki/Perfluoroisobutene" title="Perfluoroisobutene">perfluoroisobutene</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Fluoroalkenes are not <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">ozone depleting</a>, as they contain no chlorine or bromine atoms. They are too reactive to be greenhouse gases.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
To produce <a href="/wiki/Polytetrafluoroethylene" title="Polytetrafluoroethylene">polytetrafluoroethylene</a> various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluorinated_surfactant" title="Fluorinated surfactant">fluorinated surfactants</a> are used, which bioaccumulate.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Perfluoroaromatic_compounds">Perfluoroaromatic compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fluorocarbon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Perfluoroaromatic compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Perfluoroaromatic compounds contain only carbon and fluorine, like other fluorocarbons, but also contain an aromatic ring. The three most important examples are <a href="/wiki/Hexafluorobenzene" title="Hexafluorobenzene">hexafluorobenzene</a>, octafluorotoluene, and octafluoronaphthalene.

<p>Perfluoroaromatic compounds can be manufactured via the Fowler process, like fluoroalkanes, but the conditions must be adjusted to prevent full fluorination. They can also be made by heating the corresponding perchloroaromatic compound with potassium fluoride at high temperature (typically 500 °C), during which the chlorine atoms are replaced by fluorine atoms. A third route is defluorination of the fluoroalkane; for example, octafluorotoluene can be made from <a href="/wiki/Perfluoromethylcyclohexane" title="Perfluoromethylcyclohexane">perfluoromethylcyclohexane</a> by heating to 500 °C with a nickel or iron catalyst.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Perfluoroaromatic compounds are relatively volatile for their molecular weight, with melting and boiling points similar to the corresponding aromatic compound, as the table below shows. They have high density and are non-flammable. For the most part, they are clear, colorless liquids (octafluoronaphthalene, a yellow solid, is an exception). Unlike the perfluoralkanes, they tend to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miscible" title="Miscible">miscible</a> with common solvents.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
