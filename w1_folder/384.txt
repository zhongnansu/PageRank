<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Autotroph</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Any organism that produces complex organic compounds from simple substances present in its surroundings, generally using energy from light or inorganic chemical reactions</div>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Auto-and_heterotrophs.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3167" data-file-width="2722" height="349" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Auto-and_heterotrophs.png/300px-Auto-and_heterotrophs.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Auto-and_heterotrophs.png/450px-Auto-and_heterotrophs.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Auto-and_heterotrophs.png/600px-Auto-and_heterotrophs.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Auto-and_heterotrophs.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Overview of cycle between autotrophs and <a href="/wiki/Heterotroph" title="Heterotroph">heterotrophs</a>. <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">Photosynthesis</a> is the main means by which plants, algae and many bacteria produce organic compounds and oxygen from carbon dioxide and water (<span style="color:green;">green arrow</span>).</div></div></div>
<p>An <b>autotroph</b>  or <b>producer</b>, is an organism that produces complex <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fats</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>) from simple substances present in its surroundings, generally using energy from light (<a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>) or inorganic chemical reactions (<a href="/wiki/Chemosynthesis" title="Chemosynthesis">chemosynthesis</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20160912_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20160912-1">[1]</a></sup>  They are the <a href="/wiki/Primary_production" title="Primary production">producers</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chain</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> on land or <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> in water (in contrast to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterotrophs" title="Heterotrophs">heterotrophs</a> as consumers of autotrophs).  They do not need a living source of energy or organic <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>. Autotrophs can <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduce</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> to make organic compounds for biosynthesis and also create a store of chemical energy.  Most autotrophs use water as the <a href="/wiki/Reducing_agent" title="Reducing agent">reducing agent</a>, but some can use other hydrogen compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>.  Some autotrophs, such as green plants and algae, are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phototrophs" title="Phototrophs">phototrophs</a>, meaning that they convert electromagnetic energy from sunlight  into chemical energy in the form of <a href="/wiki/Photochemical_carbon_dioxide_reduction" title="Photochemical carbon dioxide reduction">reduced carbon</a>.
</p><p>Autotrophs can be <a href="/wiki/Phototroph" title="Phototroph">photoautotrophs</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chemotroph" title="Chemotroph">chemoautotrophs</a>. Phototrophs use light as an energy source, while chemotrophs use electron donors as a source of energy, whether from organic or inorganic sources; however in the case of autotrophs, these electron donors come from inorganic chemical sources. Such chemotrophs are <a href="/wiki/Lithotroph" title="Lithotroph">lithotrophs</a>. Lithotrophs use inorganic compounds, such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>, elemental <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">ammonium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iron(II)_oxide" title="Iron(II) oxide">ferrous iron</a>, as reducing agents for <a href="/wiki/Biosynthesis" title="Biosynthesis">biosynthesis</a> and chemical energy storage. Photoautotrophs and lithoautotrophs use a portion of the <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a> produced during photosynthesis or the oxidation of inorganic compounds to reduce <a href="/wiki/Nicotinamide_adenine_dinucleotide_phosphate" title="Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate">NADP<sup>+</sup></a> to NADPH to form organic compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Autotroph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Greek term <i>autotroph</i> was coined by the German botanist <a href="/wiki/Albert_Bernhard_Frank" title="Albert Bernhard Frank">Albert Bernhard Frank</a> in 1892.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">Variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Autotroph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some organisms rely on <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a> as a source of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, but are able to use <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inorganic_compounds" title="Inorganic compounds">inorganic compounds</a> as a source of energy. Such organisms are not defined as autotrophic, but rather as heterotrophic. An organism that obtains carbon from organic compounds but obtains energy from light is called a <i><b><a href="/wiki/Photoheterotroph" title="Photoheterotroph">photoheterotroph</a></b></i>, while an organism that obtains carbon from organic compounds but obtains energy from the oxidation of inorganic compounds is termed a <i><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemoheterotroph" title="Chemoheterotroph">chemoheterotroph</a></b></i>, <i><b>chemolithoheterotroph</b></i>, or <i><b><a href="/wiki/Lithotroph#Lithoheterotrophs_versus_lithoautotrophs" title="Lithotroph">lithoheterotroph</a></b></i>.
</p><p>Evidence suggests that some fungi may also obtain energy from <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a>. Such <a href="/wiki/Radiotrophic_fungus" title="Radiotrophic fungus">radiotrophic fungi</a> were found growing inside a reactor of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chernobyl_nuclear_power_plant" title="Chernobyl nuclear power plant">Chernobyl nuclear power plant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Autotroph&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Colpfl27a.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="1536" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Colpfl27a.jpg/200px-Colpfl27a.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Colpfl27a.jpg/300px-Colpfl27a.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Colpfl27a.jpg/400px-Colpfl27a.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Colpfl27a.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Green fronds of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maidenhair_fern" title="Maidenhair fern">maidenhair fern</a>, a photoautotroph</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Primary_production" title="Primary production">Primary production</a></div>
<p>Autotrophs are fundamental to the food chains of all <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a> in the world. They take energy from the environment in the form of sunlight or inorganic chemicals and use it to create energy-rich molecules such as carbohydrates. This mechanism is called <a href="/wiki/Primary_production" title="Primary production">primary production</a>. Other organisms, called <a href="/wiki/Heterotroph" title="Heterotroph">heterotrophs</a>, take in autotrophs as <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> to carry out functions necessary for their life. Thus, heterotrophs — all <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>, almost all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fungi" title="Fungi">fungi</a>, as well as most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacterium" title="Bacterium">bacteria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protozoa" title="Protozoa">protozoa</a> — depend on autotrophs, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primary_producer" title="Primary producer">primary producers</a>, for the energy and raw materials they need. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterotrophs" title="Heterotrophs">Heterotrophs</a> obtain energy by breaking down organic molecules (carbohydrates, fats, and proteins) obtained in food. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carnivorous" title="Carnivorous">Carnivorous</a> organisms rely on autotrophs indirectly, as the <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrients</a> obtained from their heterotroph prey come from autotrophs they have consumed.
</p><p>Most ecosystems are supported by the autotrophic <a href="/wiki/Primary_production" title="Primary production">primary production</a> of plants that capture <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a> initially released by the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a>. Plants can only use a fraction of this energy for <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>, approximately 1% is used by autotrophs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The process of <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> <a href="/wiki/Water_splitting" title="Water splitting">splits a water molecule</a> (H<sub>2</sub>O), releasing oxygen (O<sub>2</sub>) into the atmosphere, and <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reducing</a> carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>) to release the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_atoms" title="Hydrogen atoms">hydrogen atoms</a> that fuel the <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolic</a> process of <a href="/wiki/Primary_production" title="Primary production">primary production</a>. Plants convert and store the energy of the photon into the chemical bonds of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simple_sugars" title="Simple sugars">simple sugars</a> during photosynthesis. These plant sugars are <a href="/wiki/Polymerization" title="Polymerization">polymerized</a> for storage as long-chain <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a>, including other sugars, starch, and cellulose; glucose is also used to make <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>. When autotrophs are eaten by <a href="/wiki/Heterotroph" title="Heterotroph">heterotrophs</a>, i.e., consumers such as animals, the <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fats</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a> contained in them become energy sources for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterotrophs" title="Heterotrophs">heterotrophs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-macronutrients_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-macronutrients-6">[6]</a></sup> Proteins can be made using <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfates</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphates</a> in the soil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odum05_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Odum05-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
