<p>The <b>joule</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/dʒ/: 'j' in 'jam'">dʒ</span><span title="/uː/: 'oo' in 'goose'">uː</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span></span>/</a></span></span>; symbol: J) is a <a href="/wiki/SI_derived_unit" title="SI derived unit">derived unit</a> of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> in the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is equal to the energy transferred to (or <a href="/wiki/Work_(physics)" title="Work (physics)">work</a> done on) an object when a <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">force</a> of one <a href="/wiki/Newton_(unit)" title="Newton (unit)">newton</a> acts on that object in the direction of its motion through a distance of one <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metre</a> (1 newton metre or N⋅m). It is also the energy dissipated as heat when an electric <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_(electricity)" title="Current (electricity)">current</a> of one <a href="/wiki/Ampere" title="Ampere">ampere</a> passes through a <a href="/wiki/Electrical_resistance_and_conductance" title="Electrical resistance and conductance">resistance</a> of one <a href="/wiki/Ohm" title="Ohm">ohm</a> for one second. It is named after the English physicist <a href="/wiki/James_Prescott_Joule" title="James Prescott Joule">James Prescott Joule</a> (1818–1889).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In terms firstly of base <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">SI units</a> and then in terms of other SI units:

<p>where kg is the <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kilogram</a>, m is the <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metre</a>, s is the <a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">second</a>, N is the <a href="/wiki/Newton_(unit)" title="Newton (unit)">newton</a>, Pa is the <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">pascal</a>, W is the <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">watt</a>, C is the <a href="/wiki/Coulomb" title="Coulomb">coulomb</a>, and V is the <a href="/wiki/Volt" title="Volt">volt</a>.
</p><p>One joule can also be defined as:
</p>
<ul><li>The work required to move an <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">electric charge</a> of one coulomb through an <a href="/wiki/Voltage" title="Voltage">electrical potential difference</a> of one volt, or one <i>coulomb-volt</i> (C⋅V). This relationship can be used to define the volt.</li>
<li>The work required to produce one watt of <a href="/wiki/Power_(physics)" title="Power (physics)">power</a> for one second, or one <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Watt-second" title="Watt-second">watt-second</a></i> (W⋅s) (compare <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilowatt-hour" title="Kilowatt-hour">kilowatt-hour</a> –  3.6 megajoules). This relationship can be used to define the watt.</li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">SI</a> unit is named after <a href="/wiki/James_Prescott_Joule" title="James Prescott Joule">James Prescott Joule</a>. As with every International System of Units (SI) unit named for a person, the first letter of its <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbol</a> is <a href="/wiki/Letter_case" title="Letter case">upper case</a> (J). However, when an SI unit is spelled out in English, it is treated as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_noun" title="Common noun">common noun</a> and should always begin with a <a href="/wiki/Letter_case" title="Letter case">lower case</a> letter (<i>joule</i>)—except in a situation where <i>any</i> word in that position would be capitalized, such as at the beginning of a sentence or in material using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Title_case" title="Title case">title case</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Confusion_with_newton_metre">Confusion with newton metre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Confusion with newton metre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Newton_metre" title="Newton metre">newton metre</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mechanics" title="Mechanics">mechanics</a>, the concept of <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">force</a> (in some direction) has a close analog in the concept of <a href="/wiki/Torque" title="Torque">torque</a> (about some angle):

<p>A result of this similarity is that the SI unit for torque is the <a href="/wiki/Newton_metre" title="Newton metre">newton metre</a>, which works out <a href="/wiki/Algebra" title="Algebra">algebraically</a> to have the same <a href="/wiki/Dimensional_analysis" title="Dimensional analysis">dimensions</a> as the joule. But they are not interchangeable. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CGPM" title="CGPM">CGPM</a> has given the unit of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> the name <i>joule</i>, but has not given the unit of torque any special name, hence it is simply the newton metre (N⋅m) – a compound name derived from its constituent parts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIPM2_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-BIPM2-5">[5]</a></sup> The use of newton metres for torque and joules for energy is helpful to avoid misunderstandings and miscommunications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIPM2_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-BIPM2-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The distinction may be seen also in the fact that energy is a <a href="/wiki/Scalar_(physics)" title="Scalar (physics)">scalar</a> – the <a href="/wiki/Dot_product" title="Dot product">dot product</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_vector" title="Euclidean vector">vector</a> force and a vector displacement. By contrast, torque is a vector – the <a href="/wiki/Cross_product" title="Cross product">cross product</a> of a distance vector and a force vector. Torque and energy are related to one another by the equation
</p>

<p>where <i>E</i> is energy, <i>τ</i> is (the <a href="/wiki/Magnitude_(mathematics)#Euclidean_vector_space" title="Magnitude (mathematics)">vector magnitude</a> of) torque, and <i>θ</i> is the angle swept (in <a href="/wiki/Radian" title="Radian">radians</a>). Since radians are dimensionless, it follows that torque and energy have the same dimensions.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Practical_examples">Practical examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Practical examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One joule in everyday life represents approximately:
</p>
<ul><li>The energy required to lift a medium sized tomato up 1 metre (3 ft 3 in) (assume the tomato has a mass of approximately 100 grams (3.5 oz)).</li>
<li>The energy released when that same tomato falls back down one metre.</li>
<li>The energy required to accelerate a 1 kg mass at 1 m⋅s<sup>−2</sup> through a distance of 1 m.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> required to raise the temperature of 1 g of water by 0.24 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>The typical energy released as heat by a person at rest every 1/60 s (approximately 17 ms).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> of a 50 kg human moving very slowly (0.2 m/s or 0.72 km/h).</li>
<li>The kinetic energy of a 56 g tennis ball moving at 6 m/s (22 km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>The kinetic energy of an object with mass 1 kg moving at <span class="nowrap">√<span style="border-top:1px solid; padding:0 0.1em;">2</span></span> ≈ 1.4 m/s.</li>
<li>The amount of electricity required to light a 1 W <a href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diode" title="Light-emitting diode">LED</a> for 1 s.</li></ul>
<p>Since the joule is also a watt-second and the common unit for electricity sales to homes is the kW⋅h (kilowatt-hour), a kW⋅h is thus 1000 W × 3600 s = 3.6 MJ (megajoules).
</p>

<dl><dt>Zeptojoule</dt>
<dd>The zeptojoule (zJ) is equal to one sextillionth (10<sup>−21</sup>) of one joule. 160 zeptojoules is about one <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">electronvolt</a>.</dd>
<dt>Picojoule</dt>
<dd>The Picojoule (pJ) is equal to one trillionth (10<sup>−12</sup>) of one joule.</dd>
<dt>Nanojoule</dt>
<dd>The nanojoule (nJ) is equal to one billionth (10<sup>−9</sup>) of one joule. 160 nanojoules is about the <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> of a flying mosquito.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Microjoule</dt>
<dd>The microjoule (μJ) is equal to one millionth (10<sup>−6</sup>) of one joule. The <a href="/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider" title="Large Hadron Collider">Large Hadron Collider</a> (LHC) produces collisions of the microjoule order (7 TeV) per particle.</dd>
<dt>Millijoule</dt>
<dd>The millijoule (mJ) is equal to one thousandth (10<sup>−3</sup>) of a joule.</dd>
<dt>Kilojoule</dt>
<dd>The kilojoule (kJ) is equal to one thousand (10<sup>3</sup>) joules. Nutritional food labels in most countries express energy in kilojoules (kJ).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cal_vs_kJ_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cal_vs_kJ-10">[10]</a></sup><div class="paragraphbreak" style="margin-top:0.5em"></div> One square metre of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> receives about 1.4 kilojoules of <a href="/wiki/Sunlight#Solar_constant" title="Sunlight">solar radiation</a> every second in full daylight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TSI_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-TSI-11">[11]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Megajoule</dt>
<dd>The megajoule (MJ) is equal to one million (10<sup>6</sup>) joules, or approximately the kinetic energy of a one megagram (tonne) vehicle moving at 161 km/h.<div class="paragraphbreak" style="margin-top:0.5em"></div> The energy required to heat 10 liters of liquid water at constant pressure from 0 °C (32 °F) to 100 °C (212 °F) is approximately 4.2 MJ.<div class="paragraphbreak" style="margin-top:0.5em"></div> One <a href="/wiki/Kilowatt_hour" title="Kilowatt hour">kilowatt hour</a> of electricity is 3.6 megajoules.</dd>
<dt>Gigajoule</dt>
<dd>The gigajoule (GJ) is equal to one billion (10<sup>9</sup>) joules. 6 GJ is about the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_energy" title="Chemical energy">chemical energy</a> of combusting 1 barrel (159 l) of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> 2 GJ is about the <a href="/wiki/Planck_energy" title="Planck energy">Planck energy</a> unit.</dd>
<dt>Terajoule</dt>
<dd>The terajoule (TJ) is equal to one trillion (10<sup>12</sup>) joules; or about 0.278 GWh (which is often used in energy tables). About 63 TJ of energy was released by <a href="/wiki/Little_Boy" title="Little Boy">the atomic bomb that exploded over Hiroshima</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hironaga_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-hironaga-13">[13]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>, with a mass of approximately 450 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megagram" title="Megagram">megagrams</a> and orbital velocity of 7.7 km/s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iss_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-iss-14">[14]</a></sup> has a <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> of roughly 13 TJ. In 2017 <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irma" title="Hurricane Irma">Hurricane Irma</a> was estimated to have a peak wind energy of 112 TJ.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Petajoule</dt>
<dd>The petajoule (PJ) is equal to one quadrillion (10<sup>15</sup>) joules. 210 PJ is about 50 megatons of TNT. This is the amount of energy released by the <a href="/wiki/Tsar_Bomba" title="Tsar Bomba">Tsar Bomba</a>, the largest man-made explosion ever.</dd>
<dt>Exajoule</dt>
<dd>The exajoule (EJ) is equal to one quintillion (10<sup>18</sup>) joules. The <a href="/wiki/2011_T%C5%8Dhoku_earthquake_and_tsunami" title="2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami">2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami</a> in Japan had 1.41 EJ of energy according to its rating of 9.0 on the <a href="/wiki/Moment_magnitude_scale" title="Moment magnitude scale">moment magnitude scale</a>. Yearly <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_the_United_States" title="Energy in the United States">U.S. energy consumption</a> amounts to roughly 94 EJ.</dd>
<dt>Zettajoule</dt>
<dd>The zettajoule (ZJ) is equal to one sextillion (10<sup>21</sup>) joules. The human <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_energy_resources_and_consumption" title="World energy resources and consumption">annual global energy consumption</a> is approximately 0.5 ZJ.</dd>
<dt>Yottajoule</dt>
<dd>The yottajoule (YJ) is equal to one septillion (10<sup>24</sup>) joules. This is approximately the amount of energy required to heat <a href="/wiki/Water#On_Earth" title="Water">all the water on Earth</a> by 1 °C. The thermal output of the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> is approximately 400 YJ per second.</dd></dl>
