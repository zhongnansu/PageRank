<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Chemisorption</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Chemisorption</b> is a kind of <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a> which involves a chemical reaction between the surface and the adsorbate.  New chemical bonds are generated at the adsorbant surface.  Examples include macroscopic phenomena that can be very obvious, like <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a>, and subtler effects associated with <a href="/wiki/Heterogeneous_catalysis" title="Heterogeneous catalysis">heterogeneous catalysis</a>. The strong interaction between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adsorbate" title="Adsorbate">adsorbate</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Substrate_(chemistry)" title="Substrate (chemistry)">substrate</a> <a href="/wiki/Interface_(matter)" title="Interface (matter)">surface</a> creates new types of electronic <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">bonds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oura_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-oura-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In contrast with chemisorption is <a href="/wiki/Physisorption" title="Physisorption">physisorption</a>, which leaves the chemical species of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adsorbate" title="Adsorbate">adsorbate</a> and surface intact. It is conventionally accepted that the energetic threshold separating the <a href="/wiki/Binding_energy" title="Binding energy">binding energy</a> of "physisorption" from that of "chemisorption" is about 0.5 eV per adsorbed <a href="/wiki/Chemical_species" title="Chemical species">species</a>.
</p><p>Due to specificity, the nature of chemisorption can greatly differ, depending on the chemical identity and the surface structure.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemisorption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An important example of chemisorption is in <a href="/wiki/Heterogeneous_catalysis" title="Heterogeneous catalysis">heterogeneous catalysis</a> which involves molecules reacting with each other via the formation of chemisorbed intermediates.  After the chemisorbed species combine (by forming bonds with each other) the product desorbs from the surface.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hydrogenation_on_catalyst.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1017" data-file-width="451" height="496" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Hydrogenation_on_catalyst.png/220px-Hydrogenation_on_catalyst.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Hydrogenation_on_catalyst.png/330px-Hydrogenation_on_catalyst.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Hydrogenation_on_catalyst.png/440px-Hydrogenation_on_catalyst.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hydrogenation_on_catalyst.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">Hydrogenation</a> of an <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkene</a> on a solid catalyst entails chemisorption of the molecules of hydrogen and alkene, which form bonds to the surface atoms.</div></div></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Self-assembled_monolayers">Self-assembled monolayers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemisorption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Self-assembled monolayers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Self-Assembled_monolayer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Self-Assembled monolayer (page does not exist)">Self-Assembled monolayers</a> (SAMs) are formed by chemisorbing reactive reagents with metal surfaces.  A famous example involves <a href="/wiki/Thiol" title="Thiol">thiols</a> (RS-H) adsorbing onto the surface of <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>.  This process forms strong Au-SR bonds and releases H<sub>2</sub>.  The densely packed SR groups protect the surface.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas-surface_chemisorption">Gas-surface chemisorption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemisorption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Gas-surface chemisorption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adsorption_kinetics">Adsorption kinetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemisorption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Adsorption kinetics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As an instance of adsorption, chemisorption follows the adsorption process.  The first stage is for the adsorbate particle to come into contact with the surface.  The particle needs to be trapped onto the surface by not possessing enough energy to leave the gas-surface <a href="/wiki/Potential_well" title="Potential well">potential well</a>.  If it elastically collides with the surface, then it would return to the bulk gas.  If it loses enough <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a> through an <a href="/wiki/Inelastic_collision" title="Inelastic collision">inelastic collision</a>, then it "sticks" onto the surface, forming a precursor state bonded to the surface by weak forces, similar to physisorption.  The particle diffuses on the surface until it finds a deep chemisorption potential well.  Then it reacts with the surface or simply desorbs after enough energy and time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rettner_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-rettner-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The reaction with the surface is dependent on the chemical species involved.  Applying the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gibbs_energy" title="Gibbs energy">Gibbs energy</a> equation for reactions:
</p>

<p>General <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a> states that for spontaneous reactions at constant temperature and pressure, the change in free energy should be negative.  Since a free particle is restrained to a surface, and unless the surface atom is highly mobile, entropy is lowered.  This means that the <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy" title="Enthalpy">enthalpy</a> term must be negative, implying an <a href="/wiki/Exothermic_reaction" title="Exothermic reaction">exothermic reaction</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gasser_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-gasser-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Figure 1 is a graph of physisorption and chemisorption energy curves of <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>.  Physisorption is given as a <a href="/wiki/Lennard-Jones_potential" title="Lennard-Jones potential">Lennard-Jones potential</a> and chemisorption is given as a <a href="/wiki/Morse_potential" title="Morse potential">Morse potential</a>.  There exists a point of crossover between the physisorption and chemisorption, meaning a point of transfer.  It can occur above or below the zero-energy line (with a difference in the Morse potential, a), representing an <a href="/wiki/Activation_energy" title="Activation energy">activation energy</a> requirement or lack of.  Most simple gases on clean metal surfaces lack the activation energy requirement.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modeling">Modeling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemisorption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Modeling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

</p><p>There exist several models to describe surface reactions: the <a href="/wiki/Langmuir_adsorption_model" title="Langmuir adsorption model">Langmuir-Hinschelwood mechanism</a> in which both reacting species are adsorbed, and the <a href="/wiki/Reactions_on_surfaces" title="Reactions on surfaces">Eley-Rideal mechanism</a> in which one is adsorbed and the other reacts with it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gasser_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-gasser-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Real systems have many irregularities, making theoretical calculations more difficult:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clark_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-clark-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Solid surfaces are not necessarily at equilibrium.</li>
<li>They may be perturbed and irregular, defects and such.</li>
<li>Distribution of adsorption energies and odd adsorption sites.</li>
<li>Bonds formed between the adsorbates.</li></ul>
<p>Compared to physisorption where adsorbates are simply sitting on the surface, the adsorbates can change the surface, along with its structure.  The structure can go through relaxation, where the first few layers change interplanar distances without changing the surface structure, or reconstruction where the surface structure is changed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clark_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-clark-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>For example, oxygen can form very strong bonds (~4 eV) with metals, such as Cu(110).  This comes with the breaking apart of surface bonds in forming surface-adsorbate bonds.  A large restructuring occurs by missing row as seen in Figure 2.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dissociation_chemisorption">Dissociation chemisorption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chemisorption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dissociation chemisorption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A particular brand of gas-surface chemisorption is the <a href="/wiki/Dissociation_(chemistry)" title="Dissociation (chemistry)">dissociation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Diatomic_molecule" title="Diatomic molecule">diatomic</a> gas molecules, such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>.  One model used to describe the process is precursor-mediation.  The absorbed molecule is adsorbed onto a surface into a precursor state.  The molecule then diffuses across the surface to the chemisorption sites.  They break the molecular bond in favor of new bonds to the surface.  The energy to overcome the activation potential of dissociation usually comes from the translational energy and vibrational energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rettner_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-rettner-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>An example is the hydrogen and <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> system, one that has been studied many times over.  It has a large activation energy of .35 – .85 eV.  The vibrational excitation of the hydrogen molecule promotes dissociation on low index surfaces of copper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rettner_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-rettner-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
