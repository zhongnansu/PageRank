<p><b>Terraforming</b> or <b>terraformation</b> (literally, "Earth-shaping") of a <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">moon</a>, or other body is the <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">hypothetical</a> process of deliberately modifying its <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>, surface <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> to be similar to the environment of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> to make it <a href="/wiki/Planetary_habitability" title="Planetary habitability">habitable</a> by Earth-like life.
</p><p>The concept of terraforming developed from both <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> and actual <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a>. The term was coined by <a href="/wiki/Jack_Williamson" title="Jack Williamson">Jack Williamson</a> in a science-fiction short story ("<a href="/wiki/Collision_Orbit" title="Collision Orbit">Collision Orbit</a>") published during 1942 in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astounding_Science_Fiction" title="Astounding Science Fiction">Astounding Science Fiction</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> but the concept may <a href="/wiki/Terraforming_in_popular_culture" title="Terraforming in popular culture">pre-date this work</a>.
</p><p>Even if the environment of a planet could be altered deliberately, the feasibility of creating an unconstrained planetary environment that mimics Earth on another planet has yet to be verified. <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> is usually considered to be the most likely candidate for terraforming. Much study has been done concerning the possibility of heating the planet and altering its atmosphere, and <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> has even hosted debates on the subject. Several potential methods of <a href="/wiki/Terraforming_of_Mars" title="Terraforming of Mars">altering the climate of Mars</a> may fall within humanity's technological capabilities, but at present the economic resources required to do so are far beyond that which any government or society is willing to allocate to it. The long timescales and practicality of terraforming are the subject of debate. Other unanswered questions relate to the <a href="/wiki/Ethics_of_terraforming" title="Ethics of terraforming">ethics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Logistics" title="Logistics">logistics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Methodology" title="Methodology">methodology</a> of altering the environment of an extraterrestrial world.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_scholarly_study">History of scholarly study</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terraforming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of scholarly study">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The renowned astronomer <a href="/wiki/Carl_Sagan" title="Carl Sagan">Carl Sagan</a> proposed the <a href="/wiki/Planetary_engineering" title="Planetary engineering">planetary engineering</a> of Venus in an article published in the journal <i><a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a></i> in 1961.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Planet_Venus_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Planet_Venus-2">[2]</a></sup> Sagan imagined seeding the atmosphere of <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> with <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a>, which would convert water, nitrogen and carbon dioxide into <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a>. As this process removed <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> from the atmosphere, the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a> would be reduced until surface temperatures dropped to "comfortable" levels. The resulting carbon, Sagan supposed, would be incinerated by the high surface temperatures of Venus, and thus be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestering" title="Carbon sequestering">sequestered</a> in the form of "graphite or some involatile form of carbon" on the planet's surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sagan276_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sagan276-3">[3]</a></sup> However, later discoveries about the conditions on <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> made this particular approach impossible. One problem is that the clouds of Venus are composed of a highly concentrated <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> solution. Even if atmospheric algae could thrive in the hostile environment of Venus's upper atmosphere, an even more insurmountable problem is that its atmosphere is simply far too thick—the high atmospheric pressure would result in an "atmosphere of nearly pure molecular oxygen" and cause the planet's surface to be thickly covered in fine graphite powder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sagan276_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sagan276-3">[3]</a></sup> This volatile combination could not be sustained through time. Any carbon that was fixed in organic form would be liberated as carbon dioxide again through combustion, "short-circuiting" the terraforming process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sagan276_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sagan276-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sagan also visualized making <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> habitable for human life in "Planetary Engineering on Mars" (1973), an article published in the journal <i><a href="/wiki/Icarus_(journal)" title="Icarus (journal)">Icarus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Three years later, NASA addressed the issue of planetary engineering officially in a study, but used the term "planetary ecosynthesis" instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The study concluded that it was possible for Mars to support life and be made into a <a href="/wiki/Planetary_habitability" title="Planetary habitability">habitable planet</a>. The first conference session on terraforming, then referred to as "Planetary Modeling", was organized that same year.
</p><p>In March 1979, NASA engineer and author <a href="/wiki/James_Oberg" title="James Oberg">James Oberg</a> organized the First Terraforming Colloquium, a special session at the Lunar and Planetary Science Conference in Houston. Oberg popularized the terraforming concepts discussed at the colloquium to the general public in his book <i>New Earths</i> (1981).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Not until 1982 was the word <i>terraforming</i> used in the title of a published journal article. Planetologist Christopher McKay wrote "Terraforming Mars", a paper for the <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_of_the_British_Interplanetary_Society" title="Journal of the British Interplanetary Society">Journal of the British Interplanetary Society</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The paper discussed the prospects of a self-regulating Martian biosphere, and McKay's use of the word has since become the preferred term.
In 1984, <a href="/wiki/James_Lovelock" title="James Lovelock">James Lovelock</a> and Michael Allaby published <i>The Greening of Mars</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Lovelock's book was one of the first to describe a novel method of warming Mars, where <a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon" title="Chlorofluorocarbon">chlorofluorocarbons</a> (CFCs) are added to the atmosphere.
</p><p>Motivated by Lovelock's book, biophysicist <a href="/wiki/Robert_Haynes" title="Robert Haynes">Robert Haynes</a> worked behind the scenes<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> to promote terraforming, and contributed the <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">neologism</a> <i><b>Ecopoiesis</b></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> forming the word from the Greek <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">οἶκος</span>, <i>oikos</i>, "house",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">ποίησις</span>, <i>poiesis</i>, "production".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Ecopoiesis refers to the <i>origin of an ecosystem</i>.  In the context of space exploration, Haynes describes ecopoiesis as the "fabrication of a sustainable ecosystem on a currently lifeless, sterile planet".  Fogg defines ecopoiesis as a type of <a href="/wiki/Planetary_engineering" title="Planetary engineering">planetary engineering</a> and is one of the first stages of terraformation. This primary stage of ecosystem creation is usually restricted to the initial seeding of microbial life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPE_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPE-12">[12]</a></sup> As conditions approach that of Earth, plant life could be brought in, and this will accelerate the production of oxygen, theoretically making the planet eventually able to support animal life.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aspects_and_definitions">Aspects and definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terraforming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Aspects and definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Beginning in 1985, <a href="/wiki/Martyn_J._Fogg" title="Martyn J. Fogg">Martyn J. Fogg</a> began publishing several articles on terraforming. He also served as editor for a full issue on terraforming for the <i>Journal of the British Interplanetary Society</i> in 1992. In his book <i>Terraforming: Engineering Planetary Environments</i> (1995), Fogg proposed the following definitions for different aspects related to terraforming:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPE_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-EPE-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Planetary_engineering" title="Planetary engineering">Planetary engineering</a>: the application of technology for the purpose of influencing the global properties of a planet.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Climate_engineering" title="Climate engineering">Geoengineering</a>: planetary engineering applied specifically to Earth. It includes only those macroengineering concepts that deal with the alteration of some global parameter, such as the greenhouse effect, atmospheric composition, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation">insolation</a> or impact flux.</li>
<li>Terraforming: a process of planetary engineering, specifically directed at enhancing the capacity of an extraterrestrial planetary environment to support life as we know it. The ultimate achievement in terraforming would be to create an open planetary <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> emulating all the functions of the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a> of Earth, one that would be fully habitable for human beings.</li></ul>
<p>Fogg also devised definitions for candidate planets of varying degrees of human compatibility:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Habitable Planet (HP): A world with an environment sufficiently similar to Earth as to allow comfortable and free human habitation.</li>
<li>Biocompatible Planet (BP): A planet possessing the necessary physical parameters for life to flourish on its surface. If initially lifeless, then such a world could host a <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a> of considerable complexity without the need for terraforming.</li>
<li>Easily Terraformable Planet (ETP): A planet that might be rendered biocompatible, or possibly habitable, and maintained so by modest planetary engineering techniques and with the limited resources of a starship or robot precursor mission.</li></ul>
<p>Fogg suggests that <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> was a biologically compatible planet in its youth, but is not now in any of these three categories, because it can only be terraformed with greater difficulty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitability_requirements">Habitability requirements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terraforming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Habitability requirements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Planetary_habitability" title="Planetary habitability">Planetary habitability</a></div>
<p>An absolute requirement for life is an energy source, but the notion of planetary habitability implies that many other geophysical, geochemical, and astrophysical criteria must be met before the surface of an astronomical body is able to support life. Of particular interest is the set of factors that has sustained complex, multicellular animals in addition to simpler organisms on Earth. Research and theory in this regard is a component of <a href="/wiki/Planetary_science" title="Planetary science">planetary science</a> and the emerging discipline of <a href="/wiki/Astrobiology" title="Astrobiology">astrobiology</a>.
</p><p>In its astrobiology roadmap, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> has defined the principal habitability criteria as "extended regions of liquid water, conditions favorable for the assembly of complex <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_molecule" title="Organic molecule">organic molecules</a>, and energy sources to sustain <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA1_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA1-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preliminary_stages">Preliminary stages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terraforming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Preliminary stages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Once conditions become more suitable for <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">introduced species</a>, the importation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microbial" title="Microbial">microbial</a> life could begin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPE_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-EPE-12">[12]</a></sup>  As conditions approach that of Earth, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plants" title="Plants">plant life</a> could also be brought in.  This would accelerate the production of oxygen, which theoretically would make the planet eventually able to support <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animal</a> life.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prospective_targets">Prospective targets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terraforming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Prospective targets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TerraformedMars.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="8658" data-file-width="8658" height="210" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/TerraformedMars.jpg/210px-TerraformedMars.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/TerraformedMars.jpg/315px-TerraformedMars.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/TerraformedMars.jpg/420px-TerraformedMars.jpg 2x" width="210"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TerraformedMars.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Artist's conception of a terraformed Mars</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mars">Mars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terraforming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Terraforming_of_Mars" title="Terraforming of Mars">Terraforming of Mars</a></div>
<p>In many respects, <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> is the most Earth-like planet in the Solar System.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> It is thought that Mars once had a more Earth-like environment early in its history, with a thicker atmosphere and abundant water that was lost over the course of hundreds of millions of years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>The exact mechanism of this loss is still unclear, though three mechanisms in particular seem likely: First, whenever surface water is present, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>) reacts with rocks to form <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonates</a>, thus drawing atmosphere off and binding it to the planetary surface. On Earth, this process is counteracted when <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a> works to cause volcanic eruptions that vent carbon dioxide back to the atmosphere. On Mars, the lack of such tectonic activity worked to prevent the recycling of gases locked up in sediments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forgetmars81_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-forgetmars81-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Second, the lack of a <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a> around Mars may have allowed the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> to gradually erode the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forgetmars81_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-forgetmars81-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">Convection</a> within the core of Mars, which is made mostly of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> originally generated a <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">magnetic field</a>.  However the <a href="/wiki/Dynamo_theory" title="Dynamo theory">dynamo</a> ceased to function long ago,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and the magnetic field of Mars has largely disappeared, probably due to "... loss of core heat, solidification of most of the core, and/or changes in the mantle convection regime."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Results from the NASA <a href="/wiki/MAVEN" title="MAVEN">MAVEN</a> mission show that the atmosphere is removed primarily due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronal_Mass_Ejection" title="Coronal Mass Ejection">Coronal Mass Ejection</a> events, where outbursts of high-velocity protons from the sun impact the atmosphere. Mars does still retain a limited <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">magnetosphere</a> that covers approximately 40% of its surface. Rather than uniformly covering and protecting the atmosphere from solar wind, however, the magnetic field takes the form of a collection of smaller, umbrella-shaped fields, mainly clustered together around the planet's southern hemisphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SOLWIND_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-SOLWIND-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Finally, between approximately 4.1 and 3.8 billion years ago, <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroid</a> impacts during the <a href="/wiki/Late_Heavy_Bombardment" title="Late Heavy Bombardment">Late Heavy Bombardment</a> caused significant changes to the surface environment of objects in the Solar System. The low <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a> of Mars suggests that these impacts could have ejected much of the Martian atmosphere into deep space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-forgetmars80_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-forgetmars80-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Terraforming Mars would entail two major interlaced changes: building the atmosphere and heating it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> A thicker atmosphere of greenhouse gases such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> would trap incoming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a>. Because the raised temperature would add greenhouse gases to the atmosphere, the two processes would augment each other.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Carbon dioxide alone would not suffice to sustain a temperature above the freezing point of water, so a mixture of specialized greenhouse molecules might be manufactured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TerraformedVenus.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="8432" data-file-width="8432" height="210" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/TerraformedVenus.jpg/210px-TerraformedVenus.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/TerraformedVenus.jpg/315px-TerraformedVenus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/TerraformedVenus.jpg/420px-TerraformedVenus.jpg 2x" width="210"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TerraformedVenus.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Artist's conception of a terraformed <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a></div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Venus">Venus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terraforming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Venus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Terraforming_of_Venus" title="Terraforming of Venus">Terraforming of Venus</a></div>
<p>Terraforming <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> requires two major changes; removing most of the planet's dense 9 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">MPa</a> carbon dioxide atmosphere and reducing the planet's 450 °C (723.15 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a>) surface temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fogg1987_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-fogg1987-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Landis2011_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Landis2011-28">[28]</a></sup> These goals are closely interrelated, because Venus's extreme temperature is thought to be due to the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a> caused by its dense atmosphere. Sequestering the atmospheric carbon would likely solve the temperature problem as well.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TerraformedMoonFromEarth.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="10000" data-file-width="10000" height="210" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/TerraformedMoonFromEarth.jpg/210px-TerraformedMoonFromEarth.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/TerraformedMoonFromEarth.jpg/315px-TerraformedMoonFromEarth.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/TerraformedMoonFromEarth.jpg/420px-TerraformedMoonFromEarth.jpg 2x" width="210"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TerraformedMoonFromEarth.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Artist's conception of what the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> might look like terraformed</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Moon">The Moon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terraforming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The Moon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although the gravity on Earth's <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">moon</a> is too low to hold an atmosphere for geological spans of time, if given an atmosphere, it would retain the atmosphere for spans of time that are long compared to human lifespans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landis1990_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-landis1990-29">[29]</a></sup> Landis<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landis1990_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-landis1990-29">[29]</a></sup> and others<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-benford2014_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-benford2014-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> have thus proposed that it could be feasible to terraform the moon, although not all agree with that proposal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Landis estimates that a 1 PSI atmosphere of pure oxygen on the moon would require on the order of two hundred trillion tons of oxygen, and suggests it could be produced by reducing the oxygen from an amount of lunar rock equivalent to a cube about fifty kilometers on an edge.  Alternatively, he suggests that the water content of "fifty to a hundred comets" the size of Halley's comet would do the job, "assuming that the water doesn't splash away when the comets hit the moon."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landis1990_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-landis1990-29">[29]</a></sup> Likewise, Benford calculates that terraforming the moon would require "about 100 comets the size of Halley's."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-benford2014_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-benford2014-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_bodies_in_the_Solar_System">Other bodies in the Solar System</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terraforming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other bodies in the Solar System">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colonization_of_Mercury" title="Colonization of Mercury">Colonization of Mercury</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colonization_of_Ceres" title="Colonization of Ceres">Colonization of Ceres</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colonization_of_Callisto" title="Colonization of Callisto">Colonization of Callisto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colonization_of_Europa" title="Colonization of Europa">Colonization of Europa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colonization_of_Titan" title="Colonization of Titan">Colonization of Titan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colonization_of_the_outer_Solar_System" title="Colonization of the outer Solar System">Colonization of the outer Solar System</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Colonization_of_trans-Neptunian_objects" title="Colonization of trans-Neptunian objects">Colonization of trans-Neptunian objects</a></div>
<p>Other possible candidates for terraforming (possibly only partial or paraterraforming) include <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Callisto_(moon)" title="Callisto (moon)">Callisto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ganymede_(moon)" title="Ganymede (moon)">Ganymede</a>, <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">Europa</a>, and even <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">Mercury</a>, Saturn's moon <a href="/wiki/Enceladus" title="Enceladus">Enceladus</a>, and the dwarf planet <a href="/wiki/Ceres_(dwarf_planet)" title="Ceres (dwarf planet)">Ceres</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_possibilities">Other possibilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terraforming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other possibilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_Terraforming">Biological Terraforming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terraforming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Biological Terraforming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_contamination" title="Interplanetary contamination">Interplanetary contamination</a></div>
<p>Many proposals for planetary engineering involve the use of genetically engineered bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>As <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_biology" title="Synthetic biology">synthetic biology</a> matures over the coming decades it may become possible to build designer organisms from scratch that directly manufacture desired products efficiently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Lisa Nip, Ph.D. candidate at the <a href="/wiki/MIT_Media_Lab" title="MIT Media Lab">MIT Media Lab</a>'s Molecular Machines group, said that by synthetic biology, scientists could genetically engineer humans, plants and bacteria to create Earth-like conditions on another planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Gary King, microbiologist at <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_State_University" title="Louisiana State University">Louisiana State University</a> studying the most extreme organisms on Earth, notes that "synthetic biology has given us a remarkable toolkit that can be used to manufacture new kinds of organisms specially suited for the systems we want to plan for" and outlines the prospects for terraforming, saying "we'll want to investigate our chosen microbes, find the genes that code for the survival and terraforming properties that we want (like radiation and drought resistance), and then use that knowledge to genetically engineer specifically Martian-designed microbes". He sees the project's biggest bottleneck in the ability to genetically tweak and tailor the right microbes, estimating that this hurdle could take "a decade or more" to be solved. He also notes that the it would be best to develop "not a single kind microbe but a suite of several that work together".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/DARPA" title="DARPA">DARPA</a> is researching using photosynthesizing plants, bacteria, and algae grown directly on the Mars surface that could warm up and thicken its atmosphere. In 2015 the agency and some of its research partners have created a software called <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=DTA_GView&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="DTA GView (page does not exist)">DTA GView</a> − a '<a href="/wiki/Google_Maps" title="Google Maps">Google Maps</a> of <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genomes</a>', in which genomes of several organisms can be pulled up on the program to immediately show a list of known genes and where they are located in the genome. According to Alicia Jackson, deputy director of <a href="/wiki/Biological_Technologies_Office_(DARPA)" title="Biological Technologies Office (DARPA)">DARPA's Biological Technologies Office</a> by this they have developed a "technological toolkit to transform not just hostile places here on Earth, but to go into space not just to visit, but to stay".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Paraterraforming">Paraterraforming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terraforming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Paraterraforming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Also known as the "worldhouse" concept, paraterraforming involves the construction of a habitable enclosure on a planet which encompasses most of the planet's usable area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The enclosure would consist of a transparent roof held one or more kilometers above the surface, pressurized with a breathable atmosphere, and anchored with tension towers and cables at regular intervals. The worldhouse concept is similar to the concept of a domed habitat, but one which covers all (or most) of the planet.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adapting_humans">Adapting humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terraforming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Adapting humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It has also been suggested that instead of or in addition to terraforming a hostile environment humans might adapt to these places by the use of <a href="/wiki/Genetic_engineering" title="Genetic engineering">genetic engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">biotechnology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyborg" title="Cyborg">cybernetic enhancements</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-motherboard1_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-motherboard1-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Issues">Issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terraforming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethical_issues">Ethical issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terraforming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Ethical issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ethics_of_terraforming" title="Ethics of terraforming">Ethics of terraforming</a></div>
<p>There is a philosophical debate within <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> as to whether terraforming other worlds is an <a href="/wiki/Ethics" title="Ethics">ethical</a> endeavor. From the  point of view of a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cosmocentric_ethic&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cosmocentric ethic (page does not exist)">cosmocentric ethic</a>, this involves balancing the need for the preservation of human life against the intrinsic value of existing planetary ecologies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>On the pro-terraforming side of the argument, there are those like <a href="/wiki/Robert_Zubrin" title="Robert Zubrin">Robert Zubrin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martyn_J._Fogg" title="Martyn J. Fogg">Martyn J. Fogg</a>, Richard L. S. Taylor and the late <a href="/wiki/Carl_Sagan" title="Carl Sagan">Carl Sagan</a> who believe that it is humanity's moral obligation to make other worlds suitable for <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a>, as a continuation of the history of life transforming the environments around it on Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zoob1_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-zoob1-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> They also point out that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Future_of_the_Earth" title="Future of the Earth">Earth would eventually be destroyed if nature takes its course</a>, so that humanity faces a very long-term choice between terraforming other worlds or allowing all terrestrial life to become <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinct</a>. Terraforming totally barren planets, it is asserted, is not morally wrong as it does not affect any other life.
</p><p>The opposing argument posits that terraforming would be an unethical interference in <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">nature</a>, and that given humanity's past treatment of Earth, other planets may be better off without human interference. Still others strike a middle ground, such as <a href="/wiki/Christopher_McKay" title="Christopher McKay">Christopher McKay</a>, who argues that terraforming is ethically sound only once we have completely assured that an alien planet does not harbor life of its own; but that if it does, we should not try to reshape it to our own use, but we should engineer its environment to artificially nurture the <a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life" title="Extraterrestrial life">alien life</a> and help it thrive and co-evolve, or even co-exist with humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ontomars_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-ontomars-52">[52]</a></sup> Even this would be seen as a type of terraforming to the strictest of ecocentrists, who would say that all life has the right, in its home biosphere, to evolve without outside interference.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_issues">Economic issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terraforming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Economic issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The initial cost of such projects as planetary terraforming would be gargantuan, and the infrastructure of such an enterprise would have to be built from scratch. Such <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a> is not yet developed, let alone financially feasible at the moment. John Hickman has pointed out that almost none of the current schemes for terraforming incorporate <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economic strategies</a>, and most of their models and expectations seem highly optimistic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_issues">Political issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terraforming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Political issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty" title="Outer Space Treaty">Outer Space Treaty</a></div>
<p>National pride, rivalries between nations, and the politics of <a href="/wiki/Public_relations" title="Public relations">public relations</a> have in the past been the primary motivations for shaping space projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> It is reasonable to assume that these factors would also be present in planetary terraforming efforts.
</p>
