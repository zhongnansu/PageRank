<p>The <b>United States Department of Energy</b> (<b>DOE</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_the_United_States" title="Cabinet of the United States">cabinet</a>-level department of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">United States Government</a> concerned with the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>' policies regarding energy and safety in handling nuclear material. Its responsibilities include the nation's <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear weapons</a> program, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactor</a> production for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Energy_conservation" title="Energy conservation">energy conservation</a>, energy-related research, <a href="/wiki/High-level_radioactive_waste_management" title="High-level radioactive waste management">radioactive waste disposal</a>, and domestic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_production" title="Energy production">energy production</a>. It also directs research in <a href="/wiki/Genomics" title="Genomics">genomics</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Human_Genome_Project" title="Human Genome Project">Human Genome Project</a> originated in a DOE initiative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> DOE sponsors more research in the physical sciences than any other U.S. federal agency, the majority of which is conducted through its system of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy_National_Laboratories" title="United States Department of Energy National Laboratories">National Laboratories</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
The agency is administered by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Energy" title="United States Secretary of Energy">United States Secretary of Energy</a>, and its <a href="/wiki/Headquarters" title="Headquarters">headquarters</a> are located in <a href="/wiki/Southwest,_Washington,_D.C." title="Southwest, Washington, D.C.">Southwest</a> <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independence_Avenue_(Washington_D.C.)" title="Independence Avenue (Washington D.C.)">Independence Avenue</a> in the <a href="/wiki/James_V._Forrestal_Building" title="James V. Forrestal Building">James V. Forrestal Building</a>, named for <a href="/wiki/James_Forrestal" title="James Forrestal">James Forrestal</a>, as well as in <a href="/wiki/Germantown,_Maryland" title="Germantown, Maryland">Germantown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>.
</p><p>Former Governor of Texas <a href="/wiki/Rick_Perry" title="Rick Perry">Rick Perry</a> is the current Secretary of Energy. He was confirmed by a 62 to 37 vote in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a> on March 2, 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_and_consolidation">Formation and consolidation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation and consolidation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1942, during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the United States started the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">Manhattan Project</a>, a project to develop the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_bomb" title="Atomic bomb">atomic bomb</a>, under the eye of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Army_Corps_of_Engineers" title="U.S. Army Corps of Engineers">U.S. Army Corps of Engineers</a>. After the war in 1946, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Atomic_Energy_Commission" title="United States Atomic Energy Commission">Atomic Energy Commission</a> (AEC) was created to control the future of the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Among other nuclear projects, the AEC produced fabricated uranium fuel cores at locations such as <a href="/wiki/Fernald_Feed_Materials_Production_Center" title="Fernald Feed Materials Production Center">Fernald Feed Materials Production Center</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cincinnati,_Ohio" title="Cincinnati, Ohio">Cincinnati, Ohio</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCLO950_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCLO950-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1974, the AEC gave way to the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Regulatory_Commission" title="Nuclear Regulatory Commission">Nuclear Regulatory Commission</a>, which was tasked with regulating the nuclear power industry, and the <a href="/wiki/Energy_Research_and_Development_Administration" title="Energy Research and Development Administration">Energy Research and Development Administration</a>, which was tasked to manage the nuclear weapon, naval reactor, and energy development programs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">1973 oil crisis</a> called attention to the need to consolidate energy policy. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
On August 4, 1977, <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">President Jimmy Carter</a> signed into law <i>The Department of Energy Organization Act of 1977</i> (<a class="external text" href="http://legislink.org/us/pl-95-91" rel="nofollow">Pub.L. 95–91</a>, 91 <a href="/wiki/United_States_Statutes_at_Large" title="United States Statutes at Large">Stat.</a> <a class="external text" href="http://legislink.org/us/stat-91-565" rel="nofollow">565</a>, enacted August 4, 1977), which created the Department of Energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Relyea2003_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Relyea2003-14">[14]</a></sup> The new agency, which began operations on October 1, 1977, consolidated the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Energy_Administration" title="Federal Energy Administration">Federal Energy Administration</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Energy_Research_and_Development_Administration" title="Energy Research and Development Administration">Energy Research and Development Administration</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Power_Commission" title="Federal Power Commission">Federal Power Commission</a>, and programs of various other agencies. Former Secretary of Defense <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_Schlesinger" title="James Schlesinger">James Schlesinger</a>, who served under Presidents Nixon and Ford during the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>, was appointed as the first secretary.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weapon_plans_stolen">Weapon plans stolen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Weapon plans stolen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wen_Ho_Lee" title="Wen Ho Lee">Wen Ho Lee</a></div>
<p>In December 1999, the FBI was investigating how China obtained plans for a specific nuclear device. <a href="/wiki/Wen_Ho_Lee" title="Wen Ho Lee">Wen Ho Lee</a> was accused of stealing nuclear secrets from <a href="/wiki/Los_Alamos_National_Laboratory" title="Los Alamos National Laboratory">Los Alamos National Laboratory</a> for the <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">People's Republic of China</a>. Federal officials, including then-Energy Secretary <a href="/wiki/Bill_Richardson" title="Bill Richardson">Bill Richardson</a>, publicly named Lee as a suspect before he was charged with a crime. The U. S. Congress held hearings to investigate the Department of Energy's mishandling of his case. Republican senators thought that an independent agency should be in charge of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons" title="Nuclear weapons">nuclear weapons</a> and security issues, not the Department of Energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slate_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-slate-15">[15]</a></sup> All but one of the 59 charges against Lee were eventually dropped because the investigation finally proved that the plans the Chinese obtained could not have come from Lee. Lee filed suit and won a $1.6 million settlement against the federal government and news agencies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnn_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-cnn-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Loan_guarantee_program_of_2005">Loan guarantee program of 2005</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Loan guarantee program of 2005">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Loan_guarantee" title="Loan guarantee">Loan guarantee</a></div>
<p>In 2001, <a href="/wiki/American_Solar_Challenge" title="American Solar Challenge">American Solar Challenge</a> was sponsored by the United States Department of Energy and the <a href="/wiki/National_Renewable_Energy_Laboratory" title="National Renewable Energy Laboratory">National Renewable Energy Laboratory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> After the 2005 race, the U.S. Department of Energy discontinued its sponsorship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Title XVII of <a href="/wiki/Energy_Policy_Act_of_2005" title="Energy Policy Act of 2005">Energy Policy Act of 2005</a> authorizes the DOE to issue loan guarantees to eligible projects that "avoid, reduce, or sequester air pollutants or anthropogenic emissions of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>" and "employ new or significantly improved technologies as compared to technologies in service in the United States at the time the guarantee is issued".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In loan guarantees, a conditional commitment requires to meet an equity commitment, as well as other conditions, before the loan guarantee is completed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>The United States Department of Energy, the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Threat_Initiative" title="Nuclear Threat Initiative">Nuclear Threat Initiative</a> (NTI), the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Nuclear_Materials_Management" title="Institute of Nuclear Materials Management">Institute of Nuclear Materials Management</a> (INMM), and the <a href="/wiki/International_Atomic_Energy_Agency" title="International Atomic Energy Agency">International Atomic Energy Agency</a> (IAEA) partnered to develop and launch the <a href="/wiki/World_Institute_for_Nuclear_Security" title="World Institute for Nuclear Security">World Institute for Nuclear Security</a> (WINS) in September 2008. WINS is an international non-governmental organization designed to provide a forum to share best practices in strengthening the security and safety of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_material" title="Nuclear material">nuclear</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> materials and facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The department is under the control and supervision of a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Energy" title="United States Secretary of Energy">United States Secretary of Energy</a>, a political appointee of the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a>. The Energy Secretary is assisted in managing the department by a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Deputy_Secretary_of_Energy" title="United States Deputy Secretary of Energy">United States Deputy Secretary of Energy</a>, also appointed by the president, who assumes the duties of the secretary in his absence. The department also has three under secretaries, each appointed by the president, who oversee the major areas of the department's work. The president also appoints seven officials with the rank of Assistant Secretary of Energy who have line management responsibility for major organizational elements of the Department. The Energy Secretary assigns their functions and duties.
</p><p><br/>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Symbolism_in_the_seal">Symbolism in the seal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Symbolism in the seal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Excerpt from the <a href="/wiki/Code_of_Federal_Regulations" title="Code of Federal Regulations">Code of Federal Regulations</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Title_10_of_the_Code_of_Federal_Regulations" title="Title 10 of the Code of Federal Regulations">Title 10: Energy</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>The official seal of the Department of energy "includes a green shield bisected by a gold-colored lightning bolt, on which is emblazoned a gold-colored symbolic <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_derrick" title="Oil derrick">oil derrick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Windmill" title="Windmill">windmill</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dynamo" title="Dynamo">dynamo</a>. It is crested by the white head of an <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagle</a>, atop a white rope. Both appear on a blue field surrounded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concentric" title="Concentric">concentric</a> circles in which the name of the agency, in gold, appears on a green background."
</p><p>"The <a href="/wiki/Eagle" title="Eagle">eagle</a> represents the care in planning and the purposefulness of efforts required to respond to the Nation's increasing demands for <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_derrick" title="Oil derrick">oil derrick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Windmill" title="Windmill">windmill</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dynamo" title="Dynamo">dynamo</a> serve as representative technologies whose enhanced development can help meet these demands. The rope represents the cohesiveness in the development of the technologies and their link to our future capabilities. The <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a> bolt represents the power of the natural forces from which energy is derived and the Nation's challenge in harnessing the forces."
</p><p>"The color scheme is derived from nature, symbolizing both the source of energy and the support of man's existence. The blue field represents <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, green represents <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> resources and the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">earth</a> itself, and gold represents the creation of energy in the release of natural forces. By invoking this <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbolism</a>, the color scheme represents the Nation's commitment to meet its energy needs in a manner consistent with the <a href="/wiki/Conservation_(ethic)" title="Conservation (ethic)">preservation</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">natural environment</a>."
</p>

<p>The DOE/<a href="/wiki/National_Nuclear_Security_Administration" title="National Nuclear Security Administration">NNSA</a> has federal responsibility for the design, testing and production of all nuclear weapons. NNSA in turn uses contractors to carry out its responsibilities at the following government owned sites:
</p>
<ul><li>Design of the nuclear components of the weapon: <a href="/wiki/Los_Alamos_National_Laboratory" title="Los Alamos National Laboratory">Los Alamos National Laboratory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Livermore_National_Laboratory" title="Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory">Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory</a></li>

<li>Manufacturing of key components: <a href="/wiki/Los_Alamos_National_Laboratory" title="Los Alamos National Laboratory">Los Alamos National Laboratory</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City_Plant" title="Kansas City Plant">Kansas City Plant</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Y-12_National_Security_Complex" title="Y-12 National Security Complex">Y-12 National Security Complex</a></li>
<li>Testing: <a href="/wiki/Nevada_Test_Site" title="Nevada Test Site">Nevada Test Site</a></li>
<li>Final weapon and warhead assembling and dismantling: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pantex" title="Pantex">Pantex</a></li></ul>

<p>President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> unveiled on May 7, 2009, a $26.4 billion budget request for DOE for fiscal year (FY) 2010, including $2.3 billion for the DOE <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Energy_Efficiency_and_Renewable_Energy" title="Office of Energy Efficiency and Renewable Energy">Office of Energy Efficiency and Renewable Energy</a> (EERE). The budget aims to substantially expand the use of <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> sources while improving energy transmission infrastructure. It also makes significant investments<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="vague (December 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> in <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_electric_vehicle" title="Hybrid electric vehicle">hybrids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_hybrid" title="Plug-in hybrid">plug-in hybrids</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Smart_grid" title="Smart grid">smart grid</a> technologies, and in scientific research and innovation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-energy1_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-energy1-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>As part of the $789 billion economic stimulus package in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Recovery_and_Reinvestment_Act" title="American Recovery and Reinvestment Act">American Recovery and Reinvestment Act</a> of 2009, Congress provided Energy with an additional $38.3 billion for fiscal years 2009 and 2010, adding about 75 percent to Energy's annual budgets. Most of the stimulus spending was in the form of grants and contracts.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p>In March 2018, Energy Secretary Rick Perry testified to a Senate panel about the Trump administration’s DOE budget request for fiscal year 2019. The budget request prioritizes nuclear security while making large cuts to energy efficiency and renewable energy programs. The proposal is a $500 million in crease in funds over fiscal year 2017. It "promotes innovations like a new Office of Cybersecurity, Energy Security, and Emergency Response (CESER) and gains for the Office of Fossil Energy. Investments would be made to strengthen the National Nuclear Security Administration and modernize the nuclear force, as well as in weapons activities and advanced computing." However, the budget for the Office of Energy Efficiency and Renewable Energy would be lowered to $696 million under the plan, down from $1.3 billion in fiscal year 2017. Overall, the department’s energy and related programs would be cut by $1.9 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_Savings_Performance_Contract">Energy Savings Performance Contract</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Energy Savings Performance Contract">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Energy_Savings_Performance_Contract" title="Energy Savings Performance Contract">Energy Savings Performance Contract</a></div>
<p>Energy Savings Performance Contracts (ESPCs) are contracts under which a contractor designs, constructs, and obtains the necessary financing for an energy savings project, and the federal agency makes payments over time to the contractor from the savings in the agency's utility bills. The contractor guarantees the energy improvements will generate savings, and after the contract ends, all continuing cost savings accrue to the federal agency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_Innovation_Hubs">Energy Innovation Hubs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_Department_of_Energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Energy Innovation Hubs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Energy Innovation Hubs are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multi-disciplinary" title="Multi-disciplinary">multi-disciplinary</a> meant to advance highly promising areas of energy science and technology from their early stages of research to the point that the risk level will be low enough for industry to commercialize the technologies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-energy1_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-energy1-28">[28]</a></sup> The Consortium for Advanced Simulation of Light Water Reactors (CASL) was the first DOE Energy Innovation Hub established in July 2010, for the purpose of providing advanced modeling and simulation (M&amp;S) solutions for commercial nuclear reactors.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The 2009 DOE budget includes $280 million to fund eight Energy Innovation Hubs, each of which is focused on a particular energy challenge. Two of the eight hubs are included in the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Energy_Efficiency_and_Renewable_Energy" title="Office of Energy Efficiency and Renewable Energy">EERE</a> budget and will focus on integrating smart materials, designs, and systems into buildings to better conserve energy and on designing and discovering new concepts and materials needed to convert solar energy into electricity. Another two hubs, included in the DOE Office of Science budget, were created to tackle the challenges of devising advanced methods of energy storage and creating fuels directly from sunlight without the use of plants or microbes. Yet another hub was made to develop "smart" materials to  allow the electrical grid to adapt and respond to changing conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-energy1_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-energy1-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2012, The DOE awarded $120 million to the <a href="/wiki/Ames_Laboratory" title="Ames Laboratory">Ames Laboratory</a> to start a new EIH, the Critical Materials Institute, which will focus on improving the supply of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rare_earth_element" title="Rare earth element">rare earth elements</a>, which is  controlled by China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NetworkWorld_2013_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-NetworkWorld_2013-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>

The initial budget of about $400 million was a part of the <a href="/wiki/American_Recovery_and_Reinvestment_Act_of_2009" title="American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009">economic stimulus bill</a> of February 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
