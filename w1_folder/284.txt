<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rasyslami.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="324" data-file-width="432" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Rasyslami.jpg/300px-Rasyslami.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/19/Rasyslami.jpg 1.5x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rasyslami.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Three-dimensional representations of several fatty acids. <a href="/wiki/Saturation_(chemistry)" title="Saturation (chemistry)">Saturated</a> fatty acids have perfectly straight chain structure. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unsaturated_compound" title="Unsaturated compound">Unsaturated</a> ones are typically bent, unless they have a <a href="#Unsaturated_fatty_acids">trans</a> configuration.</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemistry</a>, a <b>fatty acid</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxylic acid</a> with a long <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aliphatic" title="Aliphatic">aliphatic</a> chain, which is either <a href="/wiki/Saturation_(chemistry)" title="Saturation (chemistry)">saturated</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unsaturated_compound" title="Unsaturated compound">unsaturated</a>. Most naturally occurring fatty acids have an <a href="/wiki/Branched_chain_fatty_acids" title="Branched chain fatty acids">unbranched chain</a> of an even number of carbon atoms, from 4 to 28.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iupac_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-iupac-1">[1]</a></sup> Fatty acids are usually not found per se in organisms, but instead as three main classes of <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">esters</a>: <a href="/wiki/Triglyceride" title="Triglyceride">triglycerides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phospholipid" title="Phospholipid">phospholipids</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cholesterol_ester" title="Cholesterol ester">cholesterol esters</a>. In any of these forms, fatty acids are both important dietary sources of fuel for animals and they are important structural components for <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatty_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The concept of fatty acid (<i>acide gras</i>) was introduced by <a href="/wiki/Michel_Eug%C3%A8ne_Chevreul" title="Michel Eugène Chevreul">Michel Eugène Chevreul</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> though he used initially some variant terms, <i>graisse acide</i> and <i>acide huileux</i> ("acid fat" and "oily acid").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_fatty_acids">Types of fatty acids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatty_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types of fatty acids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Isomers_of_oleic_acid.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="955" data-file-width="1183" height="242" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Isomers_of_oleic_acid.png/300px-Isomers_of_oleic_acid.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Isomers_of_oleic_acid.png/450px-Isomers_of_oleic_acid.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Isomers_of_oleic_acid.png/600px-Isomers_of_oleic_acid.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Isomers_of_oleic_acid.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Comparison of the <a href="/wiki/Cis%E2%80%93trans_isomerism" title="Cis–trans isomerism"><i>trans</i> isomer</a> <a href="/wiki/Elaidic_acid" title="Elaidic acid">Elaidic acid</a> (top) and the  <i>cis</i> isomer <a href="/wiki/Oleic_acid" title="Oleic acid">oleic acid</a> (bottom).</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Length_of_fatty_acids">Length of fatty acids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatty_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Length of fatty acids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fatty acids differ by length, often categorized as short to very long. 
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Short-chain_fatty_acid" title="Short-chain fatty acid">Short-chain fatty acids</a> (SCFA) are fatty acids with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aliphatic" title="Aliphatic">aliphatic</a> tails of five or fewer <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbons</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Butyric_acid" title="Butyric acid">butyric acid</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Medium-chain_triglyceride" title="Medium-chain triglyceride">Medium-chain fatty acids</a> (MCFA) are fatty acids with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aliphatic" title="Aliphatic">aliphatic</a> tails of 6 to 12<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-e-medicine_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-e-medicine-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbons</a>, which can form <a href="/wiki/Medium-chain_triglyceride" title="Medium-chain triglyceride">medium-chain triglycerides</a>.</li>
<li>Long-chain fatty acids (LCFA) are fatty acids with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aliphatic" title="Aliphatic">aliphatic</a> tails of 13 to 21 <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lipidworld_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-lipidworld-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Very_long_chain_fatty_acid" title="Very long chain fatty acid">Very long chain fatty acids</a> (VLCFA) are fatty acids with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aliphatic" title="Aliphatic">aliphatic</a> tails of 22 or more <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbons</a>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Saturated_fatty_acids">Saturated fatty acids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatty_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Saturated fatty acids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Saturated_fat" title="Saturated fat">Saturated fat</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For a more comprehensive list, see <a href="/wiki/List_of_saturated_fatty_acids" title="List of saturated fatty acids">List of saturated fatty acids</a>.</div>
<p>Saturated fatty acids have no C=C double bonds. They have the same formula CH<sub>3</sub>(CH<sub>2</sub>)<sub>n</sub>COOH, with variations in "n". An important saturated fatty acid is <a href="/wiki/Stearic_acid" title="Stearic acid">stearic acid</a> (n = 16), which when neutralized with lye is the most common form of <a href="/wiki/Soap" title="Soap">soap</a>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unsaturated_fatty_acids">Unsaturated fatty acids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatty_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Unsaturated fatty acids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Unsaturated_fat" title="Unsaturated fat">Unsaturated fat</a></div>
<p>Unsaturated fatty acids have one or more C=C <a href="/wiki/Double_bond" title="Double bond">double bonds</a>. The C=C double bonds can give either <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cis-trans_isomerism" title="Cis-trans isomerism"><i>cis</i> or <i>trans</i></a>  isomers.  
</p>
<dl><dt><i>cis</i> </dt>
<dd>A <i>cis</i> configuration means that the two hydrogen atoms adjacent to the double bond stick out on the same side of the chain. The rigidity of the double bond freezes its conformation and, in the case of the <i>cis</i> isomer, causes the chain to bend and restricts the conformational freedom of the fatty acid. The more double bonds the chain has in the <i>cis</i> configuration, the less flexibility it has. When a chain has many <i>cis</i> bonds, it becomes quite curved in its most accessible conformations. For example, <a href="/wiki/Oleic_acid" title="Oleic acid">oleic acid</a>, with one double bond, has a "kink" in it, whereas <a href="/wiki/Linoleic_acid" title="Linoleic acid">linoleic acid</a>, with two double bonds, has a more pronounced bend. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha-linolenic_acid" title="Alpha-linolenic acid">α-Linolenic acid</a>, with three double bonds, favors a hooked shape. The effect of this is that, in restricted environments, such as when fatty acids are part of a phospholipid in a lipid bilayer, or triglycerides in lipid droplets, cis bonds limit the ability of fatty acids to be closely packed, and therefore can affect the melting temperature of the membrane or of the fat.</dd>
<dt><i>trans</i> </dt>
<dd>A <i>trans</i> configuration, by contrast, means that the adjacent two hydrogen atoms lie on <i>opposite</i> sides of the chain. As a result, they do not cause the chain to bend much, and their shape is similar to straight saturated fatty acids.</dd></dl>
<p>In most naturally occurring unsaturated fatty acids, each double bond has three <i>n</i> carbon atoms after it, for some <b>n</b>, and all are cis bonds. Most fatty acids in the <i>trans</i> configuration (<a href="/wiki/Trans_fat" title="Trans fat">trans fats</a>) are not found in nature and are the result of human processing (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenation</a>).
</p><p>The differences in geometry between the various types of unsaturated fatty acids, as well as between saturated and unsaturated fatty acids, play an important role in biological processes, and in the construction of biological structures (such as cell membranes).

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature">Nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatty_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Numbering_of_the_carbon_atoms_in_a_fatty_acid">Numbering of the carbon atoms in a fatty acid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatty_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Numbering of the carbon atoms in a fatty acid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:442px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fatty_acid_numbering.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="169" data-file-width="1236" height="60" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Fatty_acid_numbering.png/440px-Fatty_acid_numbering.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Fatty_acid_numbering.png/660px-Fatty_acid_numbering.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Fatty_acid_numbering.png/880px-Fatty_acid_numbering.png 2x" width="440"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fatty_acid_numbering.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Numbering of carbon atoms</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Essential_fatty_acid#Nomenclature_and_terminology" title="Essential fatty acid">The position of the carbon atoms</a> in a fatty acid can be indicated from the COOH- (or carboxy) end, or from the –CH<sub>3</sub> (or methyl) end. If indicated from the -COOH end, then the C-1, C-2, C-3, ….(etc.) notation is used (blue numerals in the diagram on the right, where C-1 is the –COOH carbon). If the position is counted from the other, –CH<sub>3</sub>, end then the position is indicated by the ω-<i>n</i> notation (numerals in red, where ω-1 refers to the methyl carbon).
</p><p>The positions of the double bonds in a fatty acid chain can, therefore, be indicated in two ways, using the C-<i>n</i> or the ω-<i>n</i> notation. Thus, in an 18 carbon fatty acid, a double bond between C-12 (or ω-7) and C-13 (or ω-6) is reported either as Δ<sup>12</sup> if counted from the –COOH end (indicating only the “beginning” of the double bond), or as ω-6 (or omega-6) if counting from the –CH<sub>3</sub> end. The “Δ” is the Greek letter “delta”, which translates into “D” ( for <b>D</b>ouble bond) in the Roman alphabet. Omega (ω) is the last letter in the Greek alphabet, and is therefore used to indicate the “last” carbon atom in the fatty acid chain. Since the ω-<i>n</i> notation is used almost exclusively to indicate the positions of the double bonds close to the –CH<sub>3</sub> end in <a href="/wiki/Essential_fatty_acid" title="Essential fatty acid">essential fatty acids</a>, there is no necessity for an equivalent “Δ”-like notation - the use of the “ω-<i>n</i>” notation always refers to the position of a double bond.
</p><p>Fatty acids with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Odd_number" title="Odd number">odd number</a> of carbon atoms are called <a href="/wiki/Odd-chain_fatty_acid" title="Odd-chain fatty acid">odd-chain fatty acids</a>, whereas the rest are even-chain fatty acids. The difference is <a href="/wiki/Gluconeogenesis#Precursors" title="Gluconeogenesis">relevant to gluconeogenesis</a>.
</p>

</td>
<td><b><i>n</i>−<i>x</i></b> (<b><i>n</i> minus <i>x</i></b>; also <b>ω−<i>x</i></b> or <b>omega-<i>x</i></b>) <b>nomenclature</b> both provides names for individual compounds and classifies them by their likely biosynthetic properties in animals. A double bond is located on the <i>x</i><sup>th</sup> carbon–carbon bond, counting from the terminal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methyl" title="Methyl">methyl</a> carbon (designated as <i>n</i> or ω) toward the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonyl" title="Carbonyl">carbonyl</a> carbon. For example, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha-linolenic_acid" title="Alpha-linolenic acid">α-Linolenic acid</a> is classified as a <a href="/wiki/Omega-3_fatty_acid" title="Omega-3 fatty acid"><i>n</i>−3</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Omega-3" title="Omega-3">omega-3</a> fatty acid, and so it is likely to share a biosynthetic pathway with other compounds of this type. The ω−<i>x</i>, omega-<i>x</i>, or "omega" notation is common in popular nutritional literature, but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAC_nomenclature" title="IUPAC nomenclature">IUPAC</a> has deprecated it in favor of <i>n</i>−<i>x</i> notation in technical documents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nomenclature-1979_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-nomenclature-1979-13">[13]</a></sup> The most commonly researched fatty acid biosynthetic pathways are <a href="/wiki/Omega-3_fatty_acid" title="Omega-3 fatty acid"><i>n</i>−3</a> and <a href="/wiki/Omega-6_fatty_acid" title="Omega-6 fatty acid"><i>n</i>−6</a>.
</td></tr>
<tr>
<th>Lipid numbers
</th>
<td>18:3<br/><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma-linolenic_acid" title="Gamma-linolenic acid">18:3ω6</a><br/><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha-linolenic_acid" title="Alpha-linolenic acid">18:3, <i>cis</i>,<i>cis</i>,<i>cis</i>-Δ<sup>9</sup>,Δ<sup>12</sup>,Δ<sup>15</sup></a><br/><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha-linolenic_acid" title="Alpha-linolenic acid">18:3(9,12,15)</a>
</td>
<td><b>Lipid numbers</b> take the form <i>C</i>:<i>D</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-c:d_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-c:d-9">[9]</a></sup> where <i>C</i> is the number of carbon atoms in the fatty acid and <i>D</i> is the number of double bonds in the fatty acid (if more than one, the double bonds are assumed to be interrupted by <a href="/wiki/Methylene_bridge" title="Methylene bridge"><span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> units</a>, <i>i.e.</i>, at intervals of 3 carbon atoms along the chain). This notation can be ambiguous, as some different fatty acids can have the same numbers. Consequently, when ambiguity exists this notation is usually paired with either a Δ<sup><i>x</i></sup> or <i>n</i>−<i>x</i> term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nomenclature-1979_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-nomenclature-1979-13">[13]</a></sup> IUPAC nomenclature of lipids recommendations use the first mentioned notation with a list of double bond positions in parentheses appended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUPAClipid_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-IUPAClipid-11">[11]</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Free_fatty_acids"><span id="Free_fatty_acids"></span> Free fatty acids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatty_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Free fatty acids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Circulatory_system" title="Circulatory system">circulating</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Blood_plasma" title="Blood plasma">plasma</a> (plasma fatty acids) are not in their <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">ester</a>, fatty acids are known as non-esterified fatty acids (NEFAs) or free fatty acids (FFAs). FFAs are always bound to a <a href="/wiki/Transport_protein" title="Transport protein">transport protein</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Albumin" title="Albumin">albumin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dorlands_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dorlands-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatty_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial">Industrial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatty_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Industrial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fatty acids are usually produced industrially by the <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Triglyceride" title="Triglyceride">triglycerides</a>, with the removal of <a href="/wiki/Glycerol" title="Glycerol">glycerol</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Oleochemical" title="Oleochemical">oleochemicals</a>).  <a href="/wiki/Phospholipid" title="Phospholipid">Phospholipids</a> represent another source. Some fatty acids are produced synthetically by <a href="/wiki/Carbonylation" title="Carbonylation">hydrocarboxylation</a> of alkenes.<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Template:Says_whom%3F&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Template:Says whom? (page does not exist)">Template:Says whom?</a>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="By_animals">By animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatty_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: By animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fatty_acid_synthesis" title="Fatty acid synthesis">Fatty acid synthesis</a></div>
<p>In animals, fatty acids are formed from carbohydrates predominantly in the <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adipose_tissue" title="Adipose tissue">adipose tissue</a>, and the  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mammary_glands" title="Mammary glands">mammary glands</a> during lactation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stryer_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-stryer-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Carbohydrates are converted into <a href="/wiki/Pyruvic_acid" title="Pyruvic acid">pyruvate</a> by <a href="/wiki/Glycolysis" title="Glycolysis">glycolysis</a> as the first important step in the conversion of carbohydrates into fatty acids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stryer_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-stryer-16">[16]</a></sup> Pyruvate is then decarboxylated to form <a href="/wiki/Acetyl-CoA" title="Acetyl-CoA">acetyl-CoA</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrion" title="Mitochondrion">mitochondrion</a>. However, this acetyl CoA  needs to be transported into <a href="/wiki/Cytosol" title="Cytosol">cytosol</a> where the synthesis of fatty acids occurs. This cannot occur directly. To obtain cytosolic acetyl-CoA, <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid" title="Citric acid">citrate</a> (produced by the condensation of acetyl-CoA with <a href="/wiki/Oxaloacetic_acid" title="Oxaloacetic acid">oxaloacetate</a>) is removed from the <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid_cycle" title="Citric acid cycle">citric acid cycle</a> and carried across the inner mitochondrial membrane into the cytosol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stryer_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-stryer-16">[16]</a></sup> There it is cleaved by <a href="/wiki/ATP_citrate_lyase" title="ATP citrate lyase">ATP citrate lyase</a> into acetyl-CoA and oxaloacetate. The oxaloacetate is returned to the mitochondrion as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malate" title="Malate">malate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ferre_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ferre-17">[17]</a></sup> The cytosolic acetyl-CoA is carboxylated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acetyl_CoA_carboxylase" title="Acetyl CoA carboxylase">acetyl CoA carboxylase</a> into <a href="/wiki/Malonyl-CoA" title="Malonyl-CoA">malonyl-CoA</a>, the first committed step  in the  synthesis of fatty acids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ferre_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-ferre-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voet_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Voet-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Malonyl-CoA is then involved in a repeating series of reactions that lengthens the growing fatty acid chain by two carbons at a time. Almost all natural fatty acids, therefore, have even numbers of carbon atoms. When synthesis is  complete the free fatty acids are nearly always combined with glycerol (three fatty acids to one glycerol molecule) to form <a href="/wiki/Triglyceride" title="Triglyceride">triglycerides</a>, the main storage form of fatty acids, and thus of energy in animals. However, fatty acids are also important components of the <a href="/wiki/Phospholipid" title="Phospholipid">phospholipids</a> that form the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phospholipid_bilayers" title="Phospholipid bilayers">phospholipid bilayers</a> out of which all the membranes of the cell are constructed (the <a href="/wiki/Cell_wall" title="Cell wall">cell wall</a>, and the membranes that enclose all the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organelles" title="Organelles">organelles</a> within the cells, such as the <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">nucleus</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitochondria" title="Mitochondria">mitochondria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Endoplasmic_reticulum" title="Endoplasmic reticulum">endoplasmic reticulum</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Golgi_apparatus" title="Golgi apparatus">Golgi apparatus</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stryer_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-stryer-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>The "uncombined fatty acids" or "free fatty acids" found in the circulation of animals come from the breakdown (or <a href="/wiki/Lipolysis" title="Lipolysis">lipolysis</a>) of stored <a href="/wiki/Triglyceride" title="Triglyceride">triglycerides</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stryer_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-stryer-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>  Because they are insoluble in water, these fatty acids are transported bound to plasma <a href="/wiki/Albumin" title="Albumin">albumin</a>. The levels of "free fatty acids" in the blood are limited by the availability of albumin binding sites. They can be taken up from the blood by all cells that have mitochondria (with the exception of the cells of the <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a>). Fatty acids can only be broken down in mitochondria, by means of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta-oxidation" title="Beta-oxidation">beta-oxidation</a> followed by further combustion in the <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid_cycle" title="Citric acid cycle">citric acid cycle</a> to CO<sub>2</sub> and water. Cells in the central nervous system, which, although they possess mitochondria, cannot take free fatty acids up from the blood, as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood-brain_barrier" title="Blood-brain barrier">blood-brain barrier</a> is impervious to most free fatty acids,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> excluding <a href="/wiki/Short-chain_fatty_acid" title="Short-chain fatty acid">short-chain fatty acids</a> and  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medium-chain_fatty_acid" title="Medium-chain fatty acid">medium-chain fatty acids</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SCFA_MCT-mediated_BBB_passage_-_2005_review_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-SCFA_MCT-mediated_BBB_passage_-_2005_review-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SCFA_MCT-mediated_BBB_passage_-_2014_review_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-SCFA_MCT-mediated_BBB_passage_-_2014_review-21">[21]</a></sup> These cells have to manufacture their own fatty acids from carbohydrates, as described above, in order to produce and maintain the phospholipids of their cell membranes, and those of their organelles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stryer_16-5"><a href="#cite_note-stryer-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions_of_fatty_acids">Reactions of fatty acids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatty_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Reactions of fatty acids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fatty acids exhibit reactions like other carboxylic acids, i.e. they undergo <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Esterification" title="Esterification">esterification</a> and acid-base reactions.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acidity">Acidity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatty_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Acidity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fatty acids do not show a great variation in their acidities, as indicated by their respective <a href="/wiki/Acid_dissociation_constant" title="Acid dissociation constant">p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub></a>.  <a href="/wiki/Nonanoic_acid" title="Nonanoic acid">Nonanoic acid</a>, for example, has a p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> of 4.96, being only slightly weaker than acetic acid (4.76).  As the chain length increases, the solubility of the fatty acids in water decreases, so that the longer-chain fatty acids have minimal effect on the <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> of an aqueous solution.  Even those fatty acids that are insoluble in water will dissolve in warm <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>, and can be <a href="/wiki/Titration" title="Titration">titrated</a> with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> solution using <a href="/wiki/Phenolphthalein" title="Phenolphthalein">phenolphthalein</a> as an indicator.  This analysis is used to determine the free fatty acid content of fats; i.e., the proportion of the triglycerides that have been <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolyzed</a>.
</p><p>Neutralization of fatty acids, i.e. <a href="/wiki/Saponification" title="Saponification">saponification</a>, is a widely practiced route to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metallic_soap" title="Metallic soap">metallic soaps</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrogenation_and_hardening">Hydrogenation and hardening</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatty_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Hydrogenation and hardening">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">Hydrogenation</a> of unsaturated fatty acids is widely practiced. Typical conditions involve 2.0–3.0 MPa of H<sub>2</sub> pressure, 150 °C, and nickel supported on silica as a catalyst.  This treatment affords saturated fatty acids.  The extent of hydrogenation is indicated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iodine_number" title="Iodine number">iodine number</a>.  Hydrogenated fatty acids are less prone toward <a href="/wiki/Rancidification" title="Rancidification">rancidification</a>.  Since the saturated fatty acids are <a href="/wiki/Melting_point" title="Melting point">higher melting</a> than the unsaturated precursors, the process is called hardening. Related technology is used to convert vegetable oils into <a href="/wiki/Margarine" title="Margarine">margarine</a>. The hydrogenation of triglycerides (vs fatty acids) is advantageous because the carboxylic acids degrade the nickel catalysts, affording nickel soaps. During partial hydrogenation, unsaturated fatty acids can be isomerized from <i>cis</i> to <i>trans</i> configuration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>More forcing hydrogenation, i.e. using higher pressures of H<sub>2</sub> and higher temperatures, converts fatty acids into <a href="/wiki/Fatty_alcohol" title="Fatty alcohol">fatty alcohols</a>.  Fatty alcohols are, however, more easily produced from fatty acid <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">esters</a>.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Varrentrapp_reaction" title="Varrentrapp reaction">Varrentrapp reaction</a> certain unsaturated fatty acids are cleaved in molten alkali, a reaction at one time of relevance to structure elucidation.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Auto-oxidation_and_rancidity">Auto-oxidation and rancidity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatty_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Auto-oxidation and rancidity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rancidification" title="Rancidification">Rancidification</a></div>
<p>Unsaturated fatty acids undergo a chemical change known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auto-oxidation" title="Auto-oxidation">auto-oxidation</a>. The process requires oxygen (air) and is accelerated by the presence of trace metals. Vegetable oils resist this process to a small degree because they contain antioxidants, such as <a href="/wiki/Tocopherol" title="Tocopherol">tocopherol</a>.  Fats and oils often are treated with <a href="/wiki/Chelation" title="Chelation">chelating agents</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid" title="Citric acid">citric acid</a> to remove the metal catalysts.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ozonolysis">Ozonolysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatty_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Ozonolysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Unsaturated fatty acids are susceptible to degradation by ozone.  This reaction is practiced in the production of <a href="/wiki/Azelaic_acid" title="Azelaic acid">azelaic acid</a> ((CH<sub>2</sub>)<sub>7</sub>(CO<sub>2</sub>H)<sub>2</sub>) from <a href="/wiki/Oleic_acid" title="Oleic acid">oleic acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Analysis">Analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatty_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In chemical analysis, fatty acids are separated by gas chromatography of methyl esters; additionally, a separation of unsaturated isomers is possible by argentation thin-layer chromatography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Circulation">Circulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatty_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Circulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Digestion_and_intake">Digestion and intake</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatty_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Digestion and intake">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Digestion#Fat_digestion" title="Digestion">Digestion § Fat digestion</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Short-chain_fatty_acid" title="Short-chain fatty acid">Short-</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medium-chain_fatty_acids" title="Medium-chain fatty acids">medium-chain fatty acids</a> are absorbed directly into the blood via intestine capillaries and travel through the <a href="/wiki/Portal_vein" title="Portal vein">portal vein</a> just as other absorbed nutrients do. However, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long-chain_fatty_acids" title="Long-chain fatty acids">long-chain fatty acids</a> are not directly released into the intestinal capillaries. Instead they are absorbed into the fatty walls of the intestine <a href="/wiki/Intestinal_villus" title="Intestinal villus">villi</a> and reassembled again into <a href="/wiki/Triglyceride" title="Triglyceride">triglycerides</a>. The triglycerides are coated with <a href="/wiki/Cholesterol" title="Cholesterol">cholesterol</a> and protein (protein coat) into a compound called a <a href="/wiki/Chylomicron" title="Chylomicron">chylomicron</a>.
</p><p>From within the cell, the chylomicron is released into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lymphatic" title="Lymphatic">lymphatic</a> capillary called a <a href="/wiki/Lacteal" title="Lacteal">lacteal</a>, which merges into larger lymphatic vessels. It is transported via the lymphatic system and the <a href="/wiki/Thoracic_duct" title="Thoracic duct">thoracic duct</a> up to a location near the heart (where the arteries and veins are larger). The thoracic duct empties the chylomicrons into the bloodstream via the left <a href="/wiki/Subclavian_vein" title="Subclavian vein">subclavian vein</a>. At this point the chylomicrons can transport the triglycerides to tissues where they are stored or metabolized for energy.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metabolism">Metabolism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatty_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Metabolism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fatty_acid_metabolism" title="Fatty acid metabolism">Fatty acid metabolism</a></div>
<p>When metabolized, fatty acids yield large quantities of <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a>. Many cell types can use either <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> or fatty acids for this purpose. Fatty acids (provided either by ingestion or by drawing on triglycerides stored in fatty tissues) are distributed to cells to serve as a fuel for muscular contraction and general metabolism. They are broken down to CO<sub>2</sub> and water by the intra-cellular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitochondria" title="Mitochondria">mitochondria</a>, releasing large amounts of energy, captured in the form of <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a> through <a href="/wiki/Beta_oxidation" title="Beta oxidation">beta oxidation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid_cycle" title="Citric acid cycle">citric acid cycle</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Essential_fatty_acids">Essential fatty acids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatty_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Essential fatty acids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Essential_fatty_acid" title="Essential fatty acid">Essential fatty acid</a></div>
<p>Fatty acids that are required for good health but cannot be made in sufficient quantity from other substrates, and therefore must be obtained from food, are called essential fatty acids. There are two series of essential fatty acids: one has a double bond <a href="/wiki/Omega-3_fatty_acid" title="Omega-3 fatty acid">three carbon atoms</a> away from the methyl end; the other has a double bond <a href="/wiki/Omega-6_fatty_acid" title="Omega-6 fatty acid">six carbon atoms</a> away  from the methyl end. Humans lack the ability to introduce double bonds in fatty acids beyond carbons 9 and 10, as counted from the carboxylic acid side.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Two essential fatty acids are <a href="/wiki/Linoleic_acid" title="Linoleic acid">linoleic acid</a> (LA) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha-linolenic_acid" title="Alpha-linolenic acid">alpha-linolenic acid</a> (ALA). These fatty acids are widely distributed in plant oils. The human body has a limited ability to convert ALA into the longer-chain <a href="/wiki/Omega-3_fatty_acid" title="Omega-3 fatty acid">omega-3 fatty acids</a> — <a href="/wiki/Eicosapentaenoic_acid" title="Eicosapentaenoic acid">eicosapentaenoic acid</a> (EPA) and <a href="/wiki/Docosahexaenoic_acid" title="Docosahexaenoic acid">docosahexaenoic acid</a> (DHA), which can also be obtained from fish. Omega-3 and <a href="/wiki/Omega-6_fatty_acid" title="Omega-6 fatty acid">omega-6</a> fatty acids are <a href="/wiki/Biosynthesis" title="Biosynthesis">biosynthetic</a> precursors to <a href="/wiki/Cannabinoid#Endocannabinoids" title="Cannabinoid">endocannabinoids</a> with <a href="/wiki/Nociception" title="Nociception">antinociceptive</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anxiolytic" title="Anxiolytic">anxiolytic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nervous_system" title="Nervous system">neurogenic</a> properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatty_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_fatty_acids" title="Blood fatty acids">Blood fatty acids</a></div>
<p>Blood fatty acids are in different forms in different stages in the blood circulation. They are taken in through the intestine in <a href="/wiki/Chylomicron" title="Chylomicron">chylomicrons</a>, but also exist in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Very_low_density_lipoprotein" title="Very low density lipoprotein">very low density lipoproteins</a> (VLDL) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_density_lipoprotein" title="Low density lipoprotein">low density lipoproteins</a> (LDL) after processing in the liver. In addition, when released from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adipocytes" title="Adipocytes">adipocytes</a>, fatty acids exist in the blood as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_fatty_acids" title="Free fatty acids">free fatty acids</a>.
</p><p>It is proposed that the blend of fatty acids exuded by mammalian skin, together with <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactic acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pyruvic_acid" title="Pyruvic acid">pyruvic acid</a>, is distinctive and enables animals with a keen sense of smell to differentiate individuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_uses">Industrial uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fatty_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Industrial uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fatty acids are mainly used in the production of <a href="/wiki/Soap" title="Soap">soap</a>, both for cosmetic purposes and, in the case of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metallic_soap" title="Metallic soap">metallic soaps</a>, as lubricants.  Fatty acids are also converted, via their methyl esters, to <a href="/wiki/Fatty_alcohol" title="Fatty alcohol">fatty alcohols</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fatty_amine" title="Fatty amine">fatty amines</a>, which are precursors to surfactants, detergents, and lubricants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-26">[26]</a></sup> Other applications include their use as  <a href="/wiki/Emulsion#Emulsifiers" title="Emulsion">emulsifiers</a>, texturizing agents, wetting agents, <a href="/wiki/Defoamer" title="Defoamer">anti-foam agents</a>, or stabilizing agents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-buildingblocks_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-buildingblocks-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Esters of fatty acids with simpler alcohols (such as methyl-, ethyl-, n-propyl-, isopropyl- and butyl esters) are used as emollients in cosmetics and other personal care products and as synthetic lubricants. Esters of fatty acids with more complex alcohols, such as <a href="/wiki/Sorbitol" title="Sorbitol">sorbitol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">ethylene glycol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diethylene_glycol" title="Diethylene glycol">diethylene glycol</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene_glycol" title="Polyethylene glycol">polyethylene glycol</a> are consumed in food, or used for personal care and water treatment, or used as synthetic lubricants or fluids for metal working.
</p>
