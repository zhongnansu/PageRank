<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Water-gas shift reaction</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>water-gas shift reaction</b> (WGSR) describes the reaction of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> (the mixture of carbon monoxide and hydrogen (not water) is known as <a href="/wiki/Water_gas" title="Water gas">water gas</a>):
</p>
<dl><dd>CO  +  H<sub>2</sub>O   ⇌  CO<sub>2</sub>  +  H<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>The water gas shift reaction was discovered by Italian physicist <a href="/wiki/Felice_Fontana" title="Felice Fontana">Felice Fontana</a> in 1780. It was not until much later that the industrial value of this reaction was realized. Before the early 20th century, hydrogen was obtained by reacting steam under high pressure with iron to produce iron, iron oxide and hydrogen.  With the development of industrial processes that required hydrogen, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haber%E2%80%93Bosch" title="Haber–Bosch">Haber–Bosch</a> ammonia synthesis, a less expensive and more efficient method of hydrogen production was needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lamm_Catalysts_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lamm_Catalysts-1">[1]</a></sup>  As a resolution to this problem, the WGSR was combined with the <a href="/wiki/Coal_gasification" title="Coal gasification">gasification of coal</a> to produce a pure hydrogen product.  As the idea of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_economy" title="Hydrogen economy">hydrogen economy</a> gains popularity, the focus on hydrogen as a replacement fuel source for hydrocarbons is increasing.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water-gas_shift_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The WGSR is an important industrial reaction that is used in the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>, and hydrogen. It is also often used in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/Steam_reforming" title="Steam reforming">steam reforming</a> of methane and other hydrocarbons. In the <a href="/wiki/Fischer%E2%80%93Tropsch_process" title="Fischer–Tropsch process">Fischer–Tropsch process</a>, the WGSR is one of the most important reactions used to balance the H<sub>2</sub>/CO ratio. It provides a source of hydrogen at the expense of carbon monoxide, which is important for the production of high purity hydrogen for use in ammonia synthesis.
</p><p>The water-gas shift reaction may be an undesired side reaction in processes involving water and carbon monoxide, e.g. the rhodium-based <a href="/wiki/Monsanto_process" title="Monsanto process">Monsanto process</a>. The iridium-based <a href="/wiki/Cativa_process" title="Cativa process">Cativa process</a> uses less water, which suppresses this reaction.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fuel_cells">Fuel cells</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water-gas_shift_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fuel cells">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The WGSR can aid in the efficiency of <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">fuel cells</a> by increasing hydrogen production. The WGSR is considered a critical component in the reduction of carbon monoxide concentrations in cells that are susceptible to carbon monoxide poisoning such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proton_exchange_membrane_fuel_cell" title="Proton exchange membrane fuel cell">proton exchange membrane (PEM) fuel cell</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lamm_Catalysts_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lamm_Catalysts-1">[1]</a></sup>   The benefits of this application are two-fold: not only would the water gas shift reaction effectively reduce the concentration of carbon monoxide, but it would also increase the efficiency of the fuel cells by increasing hydrogen production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lamm_Catalysts_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lamm_Catalysts-1">[1]</a></sup>  Unfortunately, current commercial catalysts that are used in industrial water gas shift processes are not compatible with fuel cell applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Callaghan_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Callaghan-2">[2]</a></sup> With the high demand for clean fuel and the critical role of the water gas shift reaction in hydrogen fuel cells, the development of water gas shift catalysts for the application in fuel cell technology is an area of current research interest.
</p><p>Catalysts for fuel cell application would need to operate at low temperatures. Since the WGSR is slow at lower temperatures where equilibrium favors hydrogen production, WGS reactors require large amounts of catalysts, which increases their cost and size beyond practical application.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lamm_Catalysts_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lamm_Catalysts-1">[1]</a></sup> The commercial LTS catalyst used in large scale industrial plants is also pyrophoric in its inactive state and therefore presents safety concerns for consumer applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Callaghan_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Callaghan-2">[2]</a></sup> Developing a catalyst that can overcome these limitations is relevant to implementation of a hydrogen economy.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction_conditions">Reaction conditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water-gas_shift_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reaction conditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The equilibrium of this reaction shows a significant temperature dependence and the equilibrium constant decreases with an increase in temperature, that is, higher carbon monoxide conversion is observed at lower temperatures.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperature_dependence">Temperature dependence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water-gas_shift_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Temperature dependence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WGS_Keq.fw.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="372" data-file-width="567" height="262" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/WGS_Keq.fw.png/400px-WGS_Keq.fw.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/ac/WGS_Keq.fw.png 1.5x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WGS_Keq.fw.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A plot of the temperature dependence of <i>K</i><sub>eq</sub>, the value of which approaches 1 at <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003110000000000000♠</span>1100 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Callaghan_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Callaghan-2">[2]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The water gas shift reaction is a moderately <a href="/wiki/Exothermic_reaction" title="Exothermic reaction">exothermic</a> reversible reaction. Therefore, with increasing temperature the reaction rate increases but the conversion of reactants to products becomes less favorable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Due to its exothermic nature, high carbon monoxide conversion is <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_versus_kinetic_reaction_control" title="Thermodynamic versus kinetic reaction control">thermodynamically</a> favored at low temperatures. Despite the thermodynamic favorability at low temperatures, the reaction is <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_versus_kinetic_reaction_control" title="Thermodynamic versus kinetic reaction control">kinetically</a> favored at high temperatures. 
The water-gas shift reaction is <a href="/wiki/Chemical_equilibrium#Effect_of_temperature" title="Chemical equilibrium">sensitive to temperature</a>, with a tendency to shift towards reactants as temperature increases due to <a href="/wiki/Le_Chatelier%27s_principle" title="Le Chatelier's principle">Le Chatelier's principle</a>. </p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Practical_concerns">Practical concerns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water-gas_shift_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Practical concerns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In order to take advantage of both the <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chemical_kinetics" title="Chemical kinetics">kinetics</a> of the reaction, the industrial scale water gas shift reaction is conducted in multiple <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_process" title="Adiabatic process">adiabatic stages</a> consisting of  a high temperature shift (HTS) followed by a low temperature shift (LTS) with intersystem cooling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-4">[4]</a></sup>  The initial HTS takes advantage of the high reaction rates, but is thermodynamically limited, which results in incomplete conversion of carbon monoxide and a 2-4% carbon monoxide exit composition. To shift the equilibrium toward hydrogen production, a subsequent low temperature shift reactor is employed to produce a carbon monoxide exit composition of less than 1%. The transition from the HTS to the LTS reactors necessitates intersystem cooling. Due to the different reaction conditions, different catalysts must be employed at each stage to ensure optimal activity.  The commercial HTS catalyst is the iron oxide–chromium oxide catalyst and the LTS catalyst is a copper-based catalyst. The order proceeds from high to low temperature due to the susceptibility of the copper catalyst to poisoning by sulfur that may remain after the steam reformation process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Callaghan_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Callaghan-2">[2]</a></sup>  This necessitates the removal of the sulfur compounds prior to the LTS reactor by a guard bed in order to protect the copper catalyst. Conversely, the iron used in the HTS reaction is generally more robust and resistant toward poisoning by sulfur compounds. While both the HTS and LTS catalysts are commercially available, their specific composition varies based on vendor. An important limitation for the HTS is the H<sub>2</sub>O/CO ratio where low ratios may lead to side reactions such as the formation of metallic iron, methanation, carbon deposition, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fischer%E2%80%93Tropsch_reaction" title="Fischer–Tropsch reaction">Fischer–Tropsch reaction</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Low_temperature_shift">Low temperature shift</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water-gas_shift_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Low temperature shift">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The typical composition of a commercial LTS catalyst has been reported as 32-33% CuO, 34-53% ZnO, 15-33% Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Callaghan_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Callaghan-2">[2]</a></sup> The active catalytic species is CuO. The function of ZnO  is to provide structural support as well as prevent the poisoning of copper by sulfur. The Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> prevents dispersion and pellet shrinkage.  The LTS shift reactor operates at a range of 200–240 °C. The upper temperature limit is due to the susceptibility of copper to thermal sintering. These lower temperatures also reduce the occurrence of side reactions that are observed in the case of the HTS.  Noble metals such as platinum, supported on ceria, have also been used for LTS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="High_temperature_shift_catalysts">High temperature shift catalysts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water-gas_shift_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: High temperature shift catalysts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The typical composition of commercial HTS catalyst has been reported as 74.2% Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, 10.0% Cr<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, 0.2% MgO (remaining percentage attributed to volatile components).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newsome_1980_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Newsome_1980-6">[6]</a></sup>  The chromium acts to stabilize the iron oxide and prevents <a href="/wiki/Sintering" title="Sintering">sintering</a>. The operation of HTS catalysts occurs within the temperature range of 310 <sup>o</sup>C to 450 <sup>o</sup>C. The temperature increases along the length of the reactor due to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermic</a> nature of the reaction. As such, the inlet temperature is maintained at 350 <sup>o</sup>C to prevent the exit temperature from exceeding 550 <sup>o</sup>C. Industrial reactors operate at a range from atmospheric pressure to 8375 kPa (82.7 atm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newsome_1980_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Newsome_1980-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism">Mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water-gas_shift_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While the WGSR has been extensively studied for over a hundred years, the mechanism remains under debate. A universal rate expression and mechanistic understanding have proven elusive, reflecting the many reaction variables, vagarities of the catalyst, and the proprietary nature of commercial processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Two main mechanisms have been proposed: an associative ‘Langmuir–Hinshelwood’ mechanism, and a regenerative ‘redox’ mechanism. While the regenerative mechanism is generally implemented to describe the WGS at higher temperatures, at low temperature both the redox and associative mechanisms are suitable explanations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Giuseppe_dissertation_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Giuseppe_dissertation-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:552px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WGS_mechanism.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="849" data-file-width="1434" height="326" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/WGS_mechanism.png/550px-WGS_mechanism.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/WGS_mechanism.png/825px-WGS_mechanism.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/WGS_mechanism.png/1100px-WGS_mechanism.png 2x" width="550"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WGS_mechanism.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Associative and Redox Mechanism of the Water Gas Shift<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Associative_mechanism">Associative mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water-gas_shift_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Associative mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1920 Armstrong and Hilditch first proposed the associative mechanism. In this mechanism CO and H<sub>2</sub>O are adsorbed onto the surface of the metal catalyst followed by the formation of an intermediate and the desorption of H<sub>2</sub> and CO<sub>2</sub>. In the initial step, H<sub>2</sub>O dissociates into a metal adsorbed OH and H. The hydroxide then reacts with CO to form a carboxyl or formate intermediate which subsequently decomposes into CO<sub>2</sub> and the metal adsorbed H, which ultimately yields H<sub>2</sub>. While this mechanism may be valid under LTS conditions, the redox mechanism which does not involve any long lived surface intermediates is a more suitable explanation of the WGS mechanism at higher temperatures.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Redox_mechanism">Redox mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water-gas_shift_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Redox mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The regenerative ‘redox’ mechanism is the most commonly accepted mechanism for the WGSR. It involves a regenerative change in the oxidation state of the catalytic metal. In this mechanism, H<sub>2</sub>O is activated first by the abstraction of H from water followed by dissociation or disproportionation of the resulting OH to afford atomic O. The CO is then oxidized by the atomic O forming CO<sub>2</sub> which returns the catalytic surface back to its pre-reaction state. Alternatively, CO may be directly oxidized by the OH to form a carboxyl intermediate, followed by the dissociation or disproportionation of the carboxyl. Finally H is recombined to H<sub>2</sub> and CO<sub>2</sub> and H<sub>2</sub> are desorbed from the metal. The principal difference in these mechanisms is the formation of CO<sub>2</sub>. The redox mechanism generates CO<sub>2</sub> by reaction with adsorbed oxygen, while the associative mechanism forms CO<sub>2</sub> via the dissociation of an intermediate. The mechanism of <a href="/wiki/Decarboxylation" title="Decarboxylation">decarboxylation</a> is debated; it may involve <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%92-hydride_elimination" title="Β-hydride elimination">β-hydride elimination</a>, or it may require the action of an external base.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Homogeneous_models">Homogeneous models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water-gas_shift_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Homogeneous models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Metal_carbonyl" title="Metal carbonyl">Metal carbonyls</a> catalyze the WGSR in solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The mechanism entails nucleophilic attack of water or hydroxide on a M-CO center, generating a <a href="/wiki/Metallacarboxylic_acid" title="Metallacarboxylic acid">metallacarboxylic acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lamm_Catalysts_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Lamm_Catalysts-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reverse_water-gas_shift">Reverse water-gas shift</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water-gas_shift_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Reverse water-gas shift">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:In-Situ_Resource_Utilization_Testbed.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="387" data-file-width="324" height="299" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/In-Situ_Resource_Utilization_Testbed.gif/250px-In-Situ_Resource_Utilization_Testbed.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d1/In-Situ_Resource_Utilization_Testbed.gif 1.5x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:In-Situ_Resource_Utilization_Testbed.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>ISRU RWGS testbed (NASA KSC)</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:422px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RWGS_reactor_flowchart.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="488" data-file-width="1078" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/RWGS_reactor_flowchart.png/420px-RWGS_reactor_flowchart.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/RWGS_reactor_flowchart.png/630px-RWGS_reactor_flowchart.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/RWGS_reactor_flowchart.png/840px-RWGS_reactor_flowchart.png 2x" width="420"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RWGS_reactor_flowchart.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Reverse water-gas shift flow cycle</div></div></div>
<p>Depending on the reaction conditions, the equilibrium for the water gas shift can be pushed in either the forward or reverse direction. The reversibility of the WGSR is important in the production of ammonia, methanol, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fischer%E2%80%93Tropsch" title="Fischer–Tropsch">Fischer–Tropsch</a> synthesis where the ratio of H<sub>2</sub>/CO is critical. The RWGS reaction is also gaining interest in the context of the human missions to Mars primarily for its potential to produce water and oxygen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RWGS_process_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-RWGS_process-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The Mars atmosphere is about 95% CO<sub>2</sub> which can be utilized by the RWGS reaction given a source of hydrogen. Coupling the RWGS with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_electrolysis" title="Water electrolysis">water electrolysis</a> process will yield methane and oxygen. Post electrolysis, the hydrogen produced can be recycled back into the RWGS reactor for the continued conversion of CO<sub>2</sub>. Because this reaction is only mildly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endothermic" title="Endothermic">endothermic</a>, the thermal power needed to drive this reaction can potentially be produced by a <a href="/wiki/Sabatier_reaction" title="Sabatier reaction">Sabatier reactor</a>.
</p>
