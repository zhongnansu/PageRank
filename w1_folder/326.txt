<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Solvent</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Solvent_(disambiguation)" title="Solvent (disambiguation)">Solvent (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Acetic_acid.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="986" data-file-width="598" height="280" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Acetic_acid.jpg/170px-Acetic_acid.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Acetic_acid.jpg/255px-Acetic_acid.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Acetic_acid.jpg/340px-Acetic_acid.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Acetic_acid.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A bottle of <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a>, a liquid solvent</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>solvent</b> (from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a> <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/solvo#Latin" title="wikt:solvo">solvō</a></i>, "loosen, untie, solve") is a substance that dissolves a solute (a chemically distinct liquid, solid or gas), resulting in a <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solution</a>. A solvent is usually a liquid but can also be a solid, a gas, or a <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_fluid" title="Supercritical fluid">supercritical fluid</a>. The quantity of solute that can dissolve in a specific volume of solvent varies with <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>. Common uses for <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic</a> solvents are in <a href="/wiki/Dry_cleaning" title="Dry cleaning">dry cleaning</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Tetrachloroethylene" title="Tetrachloroethylene">tetrachloroethylene</a>), as <a href="/wiki/Paint_thinner" title="Paint thinner">paint thinners</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Toluene" title="Toluene">toluene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turpentine" title="Turpentine">turpentine</a>), as nail polish removers and glue solvents (<a href="/wiki/Acetone" title="Acetone">acetone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methyl_acetate" title="Methyl acetate">methyl acetate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethyl_acetate" title="Ethyl acetate">ethyl acetate</a>), in spot removers (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Hexane" title="Hexane">hexane</a>, petrol ether), in detergents (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D-limonene" title="D-limonene">citrus terpenes</a>) and in <a href="/wiki/Perfume" title="Perfume">perfumes</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>). Water is a solvent for <a href="/wiki/Chemical_polarity#Polarity_of_molecules" title="Chemical polarity">polar molecules</a> and the most common solvent used by living things; all the ions and proteins in a cell are dissolved in water within a cell. Solvents find various applications in chemical, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical" title="Pharmaceutical">pharmaceutical</a>, oil, and gas industries, including in <a href="/wiki/Chemical_synthesis" title="Chemical synthesis">chemical syntheses</a> and purification processes.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solutions_and_solvation">Solutions and solvation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solvent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Solutions and solvation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When one substance is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dissolution_(chemistry)" title="Dissolution (chemistry)">dissolved</a> into another, a <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solution</a> is formed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> This is opposed to the situation when the compounds are <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">insoluble</a> like sand in water. In a solution, all of the ingredients are uniformly distributed at a molecular level and no residue remains. A solvent-solute mixture consists of a single <a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">phase</a> with all solute molecules occurring as <i>solvates</i> (solvent-solute <a href="/wiki/Coordination_complex" title="Coordination complex">complexes</a>), as opposed to separate continuous phases as in suspensions, emulsions and other types of non-solution mixtures. The ability of one compound to be dissolved in another is known as <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">solubility</a>; if this occurs in all proportions, it is called <a href="/wiki/Miscibility" title="Miscibility">miscible</a>.
</p><p>In addition to mixing, the substances in a solution interact with each other at the molecular level. When something is dissolved, molecules of the solvent arrange around <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecules" title="Molecules">molecules</a> of the solute. <a href="/wiki/Heat_transfer" title="Heat transfer">Heat transfer</a> is involved and <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a> is increased making the solution more <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamically</a> stable than the solute and solvent separately. This arrangement is mediated by the respective chemical properties of the solvent and solute, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bonding" title="Hydrogen bonding">hydrogen bonding</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bond_dipole_moment" title="Bond dipole moment">dipole moment</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polarizability" title="Polarizability">polarizability</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Solvation does not cause a chemical reaction or chemical configuration changes in the solute. However, solvation resembles a <a href="/wiki/Coordination_complex" title="Coordination complex">coordination complex</a> formation reaction, often with considerable energetics (heat of solvation and entropy of solvation) and is thus far from a neutral process.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solvent_classifications">Solvent classifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solvent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Solvent classifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Solvents can be broadly classified into two categories: <i>polar</i> and <i>non-polar</i>. A special case is <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>, whose solutions are known as <a href="/wiki/Amalgam_(chemistry)" title="Amalgam (chemistry)">amalgams</a>; also, other <a href="/wiki/Liquid_metal" title="Liquid metal">metal solutions</a> exist which are liquid at room temperature. Generally, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dielectric_constant" title="Dielectric constant">dielectric constant</a> of the solvent provides a rough measure of a solvent's polarity. The strong polarity of water is indicated by its high dielectric constant of 88 (at 0 °C).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIST_water_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIST_water-3">[3]</a></sup> Solvents with a dielectric constant of less than 15 are generally considered to be nonpolar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p177_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-p177-4">[4]</a></sup> The dielectric constant measures the solvent's tendency to partly cancel the field strength of the electric field of a <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">charged particle</a> immersed in it. This reduction is then compared to the <a href="/wiki/Field_strength" title="Field strength">field strength</a> of the charged particle in a vacuum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p177_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-p177-4">[4]</a></sup> Heuristically, the dielectric constant of a solvent can be thought of as its ability to reduce the solute's effective <a href="/wiki/Chemical_polarity" title="Chemical polarity">internal charge</a>. Generally, the dielectric constant of a solvent is an acceptable predictor of the solvent's ability to dissolve common <a href="/wiki/Ionic_compound" title="Ionic compound">ionic compounds</a>, such as salts.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_polarity_scales">Other polarity scales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solvent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other polarity scales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dielectric constants are not the only measure of polarity. Because solvents are used by chemists to carry out chemical reactions or observe chemical and biological phenomena, more specific measures of polarity are required. Most of these measures are sensitive to chemical structure.
</p><p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Grunwald%E2%80%93Winstein_equation" title="Grunwald–Winstein equation">Grunwald–Winstein</a> m<b>Y</b> scale</i> measures polarity in terms of solvent influence on buildup of positive charge of a solute during a chemical reaction.
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Edward_Kosower" title="Edward Kosower">Kosower</a>'s <b>Z</b> scale</i> measures polarity in terms of the influence of the solvent on <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">UV</a>-absorption maxima of a salt, usually <a href="/wiki/Pyridinium" title="Pyridinium">pyridinium</a> <a href="/wiki/Iodide" title="Iodide">iodide</a> or the pyridinium <a href="/wiki/Zwitterion" title="Zwitterion">zwitterion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Donor number and donor acceptor scale</i> measures polarity in terms of how a solvent interacts with specific substances, like a strong <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lewis_acid" title="Lewis acid">Lewis acid</a> or a strong Lewis base.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hildebrand_parameter" title="Hildebrand parameter">Hildebrand parameter</a></i> is the square root of <b>cohesive energy density</b>. It can be used with nonpolar compounds, but cannot accommodate complex chemistry.
</p><p>Reichardt's dye, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solvatochromic" title="Solvatochromic">solvatochromic</a> dye that changes color in response to polarity, gives a scale of <i>E<sub>T</sub></i>(30) values. <i>E<sub>T</sub></i> is the transition energy between the ground state and the lowest excited state in kcal/mol, and (30) identifies the dye. Another, roughly correlated scale (<i>E<sub>T</sub></i>(33)) can be defined with <a href="/wiki/Nile_red" title="Nile red">Nile red</a>.
</p><p>The polarity, dipole moment, polarizability and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bonding" title="Hydrogen bonding">hydrogen bonding</a> of a solvent determines what type of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">compounds</a> it is able to dissolve and with what other solvents or liquid compounds it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miscible" title="Miscible">miscible</a>. Generally, polar solvents dissolve polar compounds best and non-polar solvents dissolve non-polar compounds best: "like dissolves like". Strongly polar compounds like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sugars" title="Sugars">sugars</a> (e.g. sucrose) or ionic compounds, like <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_chemistry" title="Inorganic chemistry">inorganic</a> <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a> (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Table_salt" title="Table salt">table salt</a>) dissolve only in very polar solvents like water, while strongly non-polar compounds like <a href="/wiki/Oil" title="Oil">oils</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wax" title="Wax">waxes</a> dissolve only in very non-polar organic solvents like <a href="/wiki/Hexane" title="Hexane">hexane</a>. Similarly, water and <a href="/wiki/Hexane" title="Hexane">hexane</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Vinegar" title="Vinegar">vinegar</a> and vegetable oil) are not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miscible" title="Miscible">miscible</a> with each other and will quickly separate into two layers even after being shaken well.
</p><p>Polarity can be separated to different contributions. For example, the <b>Kamlet-Taft parameters</b> are dipolarity/polarizability (<i>π*</i>), hydrogen-bonding acidity (<i>α</i>) and hydrogen-bonding basicity (<i>β</i>). These can be calculated from the wavelength shifts of 3–6 different solvatochromic dyes in the solvent, usually including <a href="/wiki/Reichardt%27s_dye" title="Reichardt's dye">Reichardt's dye</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitroaniline" title="Nitroaniline">nitroaniline</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Diethylnitroaniline&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Diethylnitroaniline (page does not exist)">diethylnitroaniline</a>. Another option, Hansen's parameters, separate the cohesive energy density into dispersion, polar and hydrogen bonding contributions.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polar_protic_and_polar_aprotic">Polar protic and polar aprotic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solvent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Polar protic and polar aprotic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Solvents with a dielectric constant (more accurately, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relative_static_permittivity" title="Relative static permittivity">relative static permittivity</a>) greater than 15 (i.e. polar or polarizable) can be further divided into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protic" title="Protic">protic</a> and aprotic. Protic solvents solvate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">anions</a> (negatively charged solutes) strongly via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bonding" title="Hydrogen bonding">hydrogen bonding</a>. Water is a protic solvent. Aprotic solvents such as <a href="/wiki/Acetone" title="Acetone">acetone</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dichloromethane" title="Dichloromethane">dichloromethane</a> tend to have large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_dipole_moment" title="Molecular dipole moment">dipole moments</a> (separation of partial positive and partial negative charges within the same molecule) and solvate positively charged species via their negative dipole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reactions</a> the use of polar protic solvents favors the <a href="/wiki/SN1_reaction" title="SN1 reaction">S<sub>N</sub>1</a> <a href="/wiki/Reaction_mechanism" title="Reaction mechanism">reaction mechanism</a>, while polar aprotic solvents favor the <a href="/wiki/SN2_reaction" title="SN2 reaction">S<sub>N</sub>2</a> reaction mechanism. These polar solvents are capable of forming hydrogen bonds with water to dissolve in water whereas non polar solvents are not capable of strong hydrogen bonds.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hansen_solubility_parameter_values">Hansen solubility parameter values</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solvent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Hansen solubility parameter values">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Hansen solubility parameter values<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansen_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-hansen-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansen2_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-hansen2-15">[15]</a></sup> are based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Van_der_Waals_forces" title="Van der Waals forces">dispersion bonds</a> (δD), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_bonds" title="Polar bonds">polar bonds</a> (δP) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bonds" title="Hydrogen bonds">hydrogen bonds</a> (δH). These contain information about the inter-molecular interactions with other solvents and also with polymers, pigments, nanoparticles, etc. This allows for rational formulations knowing, for example, that there is a good HSP match between a solvent and a polymer. Rational substitutions can also be made for "good" solvents (effective at dissolving the solute) that are "bad" (expensive or hazardous to health or the environment). The following table shows that the intuitions from "non-polar", "polar aprotic" and "polar protic" are put numerically – the "polar" molecules have higher levels of δP and the protic solvents have higher levels of δH. Because numerical values are used, comparisons can be made rationally by comparing numbers. For example, acetonitrile is much more polar than acetone but exhibits slightly less hydrogen bonding.
</p>

<p>If, for environmental or other reasons, a solvent or solvent blend is required to replace another of equivalent solvency, the substitution can be made on the basis of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hansen_solubility_parameters" title="Hansen solubility parameters">Hansen solubility parameters</a> of each. The values for mixtures are taken as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weighted_average" title="Weighted average">weighted averages</a> of the values for the neat solvents. This can be calculated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trial-and-error" title="Trial-and-error">trial-and-error</a>, a spreadsheet of values, or HSP software.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansen_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-hansen-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hansen2_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-hansen2-15">[15]</a></sup> A 1:1 mixture of <a href="/wiki/Toluene" title="Toluene">toluene</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1,4_dioxane" title="1,4 dioxane">1,4 dioxane</a> has δD, δP and δH values of 17.8, 1.6 and 5.5, comparable to those of <a href="/wiki/Chloroform" title="Chloroform">chloroform</a> at 17.8, 3.1 and 5.7 respectively. Because of the health hazards associated with toluene itself, other mixtures of solvents may be found using a full <a href="/wiki/Hansen_solubility_parameter" title="Hansen solubility parameter">HSP</a> dataset.
</p>

<p>The boiling point is an important property because it determines the speed of evaporation. Small amounts of low-boiling-point solvents like <a href="/wiki/Diethyl_ether" title="Diethyl ether">diethyl ether</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dichloromethane" title="Dichloromethane">dichloromethane</a>, or acetone will evaporate in seconds at room temperature, while high-boiling-point solvents like water or <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfoxide" title="Dimethyl sulfoxide">dimethyl sulfoxide</a> need higher temperatures, an air flow, or the application of <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a> for fast evaporation.
</p>
<ul><li>Low boilers: boiling point below 100 °C (boiling point of water)</li>
<li>Medium boilers: between 100 °C and 150 °C</li>
<li>High boilers: above 150 °C</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Density">Density</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solvent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Density">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most organic solvents have a lower <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> than water, which means they are lighter than and will form a layer on top of water. Important exceptions are most of the <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogenated</a> solvents like <a href="/wiki/Dichloromethane" title="Dichloromethane">dichloromethane</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chloroform" title="Chloroform">chloroform</a> will sink to the bottom of a container, leaving water as the top layer. This is crucial to remember when <a href="/wiki/Partition_coefficient" title="Partition coefficient">partitioning</a> compounds between solvents and water in a <a href="/wiki/Separatory_funnel" title="Separatory funnel">separatory funnel</a> during chemical syntheses.
</p><p>Often, <a href="/wiki/Specific_gravity" title="Specific gravity">specific gravity</a> is cited in place of density. Specific gravity is defined as the density of the solvent divided by the density of water at the same temperature. As such, specific gravity is a unitless value. It readily communicates whether a water-insoluble solvent will float (SG &lt; 1.0) or sink (SG &gt; 1.0) when mixed with water.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solvent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fire">Fire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solvent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Fire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most organic solvents are flammable or highly flammable, depending on their volatility. Exceptions are some chlorinated solvents like <a href="/wiki/Dichloromethane" title="Dichloromethane">dichloromethane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chloroform" title="Chloroform">chloroform</a>. Mixtures of solvent vapors and air can <a href="/wiki/Explosion" title="Explosion">explode</a>. Solvent vapors are heavier than air; they will sink to the bottom and can travel large distances nearly undiluted. Solvent vapors can also be found in supposedly empty drums and cans, posing a <a href="/wiki/Flash_fire" title="Flash fire">flash fire</a> hazard; hence empty containers of volatile solvents should be stored open and upside down.
</p><p>Both <a href="/wiki/Diethyl_ether" title="Diethyl ether">diethyl ether</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_disulfide" title="Carbon disulfide">carbon disulfide</a> have exceptionally low <a href="/wiki/Autoignition_temperature" title="Autoignition temperature">autoignition temperatures</a> which increase greatly the fire risk associated with these solvents. The autoignition temperature of carbon disulfide is below 100 °C (212 °F), so objects such as <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a> pipes, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_bulb" title="Light bulb">light bulbs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hotplate" title="Hotplate">hotplates</a>, and recently-extinguished <a href="/wiki/Bunsen_burner" title="Bunsen burner">bunsen burners</a> are able to ignite its vapours.
</p><p>In addition some solvents, such as methanol, can burn with a very hot flame which can be nearly invisible under some lighting conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> This can delay or prevent the timely recognition of a dangerous fire, until flames spread to other materials.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Explosive_peroxide_formation">Explosive peroxide formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solvent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Explosive peroxide formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ether" title="Ether">Ethers</a> like <a href="/wiki/Diethyl_ether" title="Diethyl ether">diethyl ether</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tetrahydrofuran" title="Tetrahydrofuran">tetrahydrofuran</a> (THF) can form highly explosive <a href="/wiki/Organic_peroxide" title="Organic peroxide">organic peroxides</a> upon exposure to oxygen and light. THF is normally more likely to form such peroxides than diethyl ether. One of the most susceptible solvents is <a href="/wiki/Diisopropyl_ether" title="Diisopropyl ether">diisopropyl ether</a>, but all ethers are considered to be potential peroxide sources.
</p><p>The heteroatom (<a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>) stabilizes the formation of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_radical" title="Free radical">free radical</a> which is formed by the abstraction of a <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> atom by another free radical.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The carbon-centred free radical thus formed is able to react with an oxygen molecule to form a peroxide compound. The process of peroxide formation is greatly accelerated by exposure to even low levels of light, but can proceed slowly even in dark conditions. 
</p><p>Unless a <a href="/wiki/Desiccant" title="Desiccant">desiccant</a> is used which can destroy the peroxides, they will concentrate during <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distillation</a>, due to their higher <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">boiling point</a>. When sufficient peroxides have formed, they can form a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystalline" title="Crystalline">crystalline</a>, shock-sensitive solid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitate" title="Precipitate">precipitate</a> at the mouth of a container or bottle. Minor mechanical disturbances, such as scraping the inside of a vessel or the dislodging of a deposit, merely twisting the cap may provide sufficient energy for the peroxide to explode or <a href="/wiki/Detonation" title="Detonation">detonate</a>. Peroxide formation is not a significant problem when fresh solvents are used up quickly; they are more of a problem in laboratories which may take years to finish a single bottle. Low-volume users should acquire only small amounts of peroxide-prone solvents, and dispose of old solvents on a regular periodic schedule.
</p><p>To avoid explosive peroxide formation, ethers should be stored in an aritight container, away from light, because both light and air can encourage peroxide formation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>A number of tests can be used to detect the presence of a peroxide in an ether; one is to use a combination of <a href="/wiki/Iron(II)_sulfate" title="Iron(II) sulfate">iron(II) sulfate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Potassium_thiocyanate" title="Potassium thiocyanate">potassium thiocyanate</a>. The peroxide is able to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidize" title="Oxidize">oxidize</a> the Fe<sup>2+</sup> ion to an Fe<sup>3+</sup> ion, which then forms a deep-red <a href="/wiki/Coordination_complex" title="Coordination complex">coordination complex</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Thiocyanate" title="Thiocyanate">thiocyanate</a>.
</p><p>Peroxides may be removed by washing with acidic iron(II) sulfate, filtering through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alumina" title="Alumina">alumina</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distilling</a> from <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a>/<a href="/wiki/Benzophenone" title="Benzophenone">benzophenone</a>. Aluminum does not destroy the peroxides but merely traps them, and must be disposed of properly. The advantage of using sodium/benzophenone is that <a href="/wiki/Moisture" title="Moisture">moisture</a> and oxygen are removed as well.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_effects">Health effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solvent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Health effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>General health hazards associated with solvent exposure include toxicity to the nervous system, reproductive damage, liver and kidney damage, respiratory impairment, cancer, and <a href="/wiki/Dermatitis" title="Dermatitis">dermatitis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acute_exposure">Acute exposure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solvent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Acute exposure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many solvents can lead to a sudden loss of consciousness if <a href="/wiki/Inhalation" title="Inhalation">inhaled</a> in large amounts. Solvents like <a href="/wiki/Diethyl_ether" title="Diethyl ether">diethyl ether</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chloroform" title="Chloroform">chloroform</a> have been used in medicine as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anesthetics" title="Anesthetics">anesthetics</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sedatives" title="Sedatives">sedatives</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypnotics" title="Hypnotics">hypnotics</a> for a long time. <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">Ethanol</a> (grain alcohol) is a widely used and abused <a href="/wiki/Psychoactive_drug" title="Psychoactive drug">psychoactive drug</a>. Diethyl ether, chloroform, and many other solvents e.g. from <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> or glues are abused recreationally in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volatile_substance_abuse" title="Volatile substance abuse">glue sniffing</a>, often with harmful long term health effects like <a href="/wiki/Neurotoxicity" title="Neurotoxicity">neurotoxicity</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a>. 
Fraudulent substitution of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1,5-pentanediol" title="1,5-pentanediol">1,5-pentanediol</a> for the psychoactive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1,4-butanediol" title="1,4-butanediol">1,4-butanediol</a> by a subcontractor caused the <a href="/wiki/Bindeez" title="Bindeez">Bindeez</a> product recall<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>.
If ingested, the so called toxic alcohols (other than ethanol) such as <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Propanol" title="Propanol">propanol</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">ethylene glycol</a> metabolize into toxic aldehydes and acids, which cause potentially fatal <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_acidosis" title="Metabolic acidosis">metabolic acidosis</a>.  
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>  The commonly available alcohol solvent methanol can cause permanent blindness or death if ingested. The solvent <a href="/wiki/2-Butoxyethanol" title="2-Butoxyethanol">2-butoxyethanol</a>, used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fracking_fluid" title="Fracking fluid">fracking fluids</a>, can cause <a href="/wiki/Hypotension" title="Hypotension">hypotension</a> and metabolic acidosis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chronic_exposure">Chronic exposure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solvent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Chronic exposure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chronic_solvent-induced_encephalopathy" title="Chronic solvent-induced encephalopathy">Chronic solvent-induced encephalopathy</a></div>
<p>Some solvents including <a href="/wiki/Chloroform" title="Chloroform">chloroform</a> and <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a> a common ingredient in <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> are known to be <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogenic</a>, while many others are considered by the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> to be likely carcinogens. Solvents can damage internal organs like the <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidneys</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Nervous_system" title="Nervous system">nervous system</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/Human_brain" title="Human brain">brain</a>. The cumulative effects of long-term or repeated exposure to solvents are called <a href="/wiki/Chronic_solvent-induced_encephalopathy" title="Chronic solvent-induced encephalopathy">chronic solvent-induced encephalopathy</a> (CSE).
</p><p>Chronic exposure to organic solvents in the work environment can produce a range of adverse neuropsychiatric effects. For example, occupational exposure to organic solvents has been associated with higher numbers of painters suffering from <a href="/wiki/Alcoholism" title="Alcoholism">alcoholism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid1606027_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid1606027-24">[24]</a></sup> Ethanol has a <a href="/wiki/Synergy" title="Synergy">synergistic</a> effect when taken in combination with many solvents; for instance, a combination of <a href="/wiki/Toluene" title="Toluene">toluene</a>/<a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a> and ethanol causes greater <a href="/wiki/Nausea" title="Nausea">nausea</a>/<a href="/wiki/Vomiting" title="Vomiting">vomiting</a> than either substance alone.
</p><p>Many solvents are known or suspected to be cataractogenic, greatly increasing the risk of developing <a href="/wiki/Cataract" title="Cataract">cataracts</a> in the lens of the eye.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Solvent exposure has also been associated with neurotoxic damage causing <a href="/wiki/Hearing_loss" title="Hearing loss">hearing loss</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Color_vision" title="Color vision">color vision</a> losses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_contamination">Environmental contamination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Solvent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Environmental contamination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A major pathway to induce health effects arises from spills or leaks of solvents that reach the underlying soil. Since solvents readily migrate substantial distances, the creation of widespread <a href="/wiki/Soil_contamination" title="Soil contamination">soil contamination</a> is not uncommon; this is particularly a health risk if <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifers</a> are affected. There may be about 5000 sites worldwide that have major subsurface solvent contamination.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
