<p><b>Veins</b> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_vessels" title="Blood vessels">blood vessels</a> that carry <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> toward the <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a>. Most veins carry deoxygenated blood from the tissues back to the heart; exceptions are the <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_vein" title="Pulmonary vein">pulmonary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Umbilical_vein" title="Umbilical vein">umbilical veins</a>, both of which carry oxygenated blood to the heart. In contrast to veins, <a href="/wiki/Artery" title="Artery">arteries</a> carry blood away from the heart.
</p><p>Veins are less muscular than arteries and are often closer to the skin. There are valves in most veins to prevent <a href="/wiki/Regurgitation_(circulation)" title="Regurgitation (circulation)">backflow</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Veins are present throughout the body as tubes that carry blood back to the heart. Veins are classified in a number of ways, including superficial vs. deep, pulmonary vs. systemic, and large vs. small. 
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Superficial_vein" title="Superficial vein">Superficial veins</a> are those closer to the surface of the body, and have no corresponding arteries.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Deep_vein" title="Deep vein">Deep veins</a> are deeper in the body and have corresponding arteries.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Perforator_vein" title="Perforator vein">Perforator veins</a> drain from the superficial to the deep veins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dorlands_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dorlands-1">[1]</a></sup> These are usually referred to in the lower limbs and feet.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communicating_veins" title="Communicating veins">Communicating veins</a> are veins that directly connect superficial veins to deep veins.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_vein" title="Pulmonary vein">Pulmonary veins</a> are a set of veins that deliver oxygenated blood from the <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">lungs</a> to the heart.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systemic_vein" title="Systemic vein">Systemic veins</a> drain the tissues of the body and deliver deoxygenated blood to the heart.</li></ul>
<p><span id="Venous_valves"></span>Most veins are equipped with valves to prevent blood flowing in the reverse direction.
</p><p>Veins are translucent, so the color a vein appears from an organism's exterior is determined in large part by the color of <a href="/wiki/Venous_blood" title="Venous blood">venous blood</a>, which is usually dark red as a result of its low oxygen content. Veins appear blue because the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subcutis" title="Subcutis">subcutaneous fat</a> absorbs low-frequency light, permitting only the highly energetic blue wavelengths to penetrate through to the dark vein and reflect back to the viewer. The colour of a vein can be affected by the characteristics of a person's skin, how much oxygen is being carried in the blood, and how big and deep the vessels are.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> When a vein is drained of blood and removed from an organism, it appears grey-white.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Venous_system">Venous system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Venous system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_veins_of_the_human_body" title="List of veins of the human body">List of veins of the human body</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Major_Veins.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1214" data-file-width="995" height="268" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Major_Veins.png/220px-Major_Veins.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Major_Veins.png/330px-Major_Veins.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Major_Veins.png/440px-Major_Veins.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Major_Veins.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Branches of <a href="/wiki/Inferior_vena_cava" title="Inferior vena cava">inferior vena cava</a></div></div></div>
<p>The largest veins in the human body are the <a href="/wiki/Venae_cavae" title="Venae cavae">venae cavae</a>. These are two large veins which enter the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right_atrium" title="Right atrium">right atrium</a> of the heart from above and below. The <a href="/wiki/Superior_vena_cava" title="Superior vena cava">superior vena cava</a> carries blood from the arms and head to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right_atrium" title="Right atrium">right atrium</a> of the heart, while the <a href="/wiki/Inferior_vena_cava" title="Inferior vena cava">inferior vena cava</a> carries blood from the legs and <a href="/wiki/Abdomen" title="Abdomen">abdomen</a> to the heart. The inferior vena cava is <a href="/wiki/Retroperitoneal_space" title="Retroperitoneal space">retroperitoneal</a> and runs to the right and roughly parallel to the <a href="/wiki/Abdominal_aorta" title="Abdominal aorta">abdominal aorta</a> along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_vertebral_column" title="Human vertebral column">spine</a>. Large veins feed into these two veins, and smaller veins into these. Together this forms the venous system.
</p><p>Whilst the main veins hold a relatively constant position, the position of veins person to person can display quite a lot of variation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_vein" title="Pulmonary vein">pulmonary veins</a> carry relatively oxygenated blood from the lungs to the heart. The <a href="/wiki/Superior_vena_cava" title="Superior vena cava">superior</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inferior_vena_cava" title="Inferior vena cava">inferior venae cavae</a> carry relatively deoxygenated blood from the upper and lower systemic circulations, respectively.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Portal_venous_system" title="Portal venous system">portal venous system</a> is a series of veins or venules that directly connect two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capillary_bed" title="Capillary bed">capillary beds</a>. Examples of such systems include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hepatic_portal_vein" title="Hepatic portal vein">hepatic portal vein</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hypophyseal_portal_system" title="Hypophyseal portal system">hypophyseal portal system</a>.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peripheral_vein" title="Peripheral vein">peripheral veins</a> carry blood from the limbs and <a href="/wiki/Hand" title="Hand">hands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Foot" title="Foot">feet</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Microanatomy">Microanatomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Microanatomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Histology" title="Histology">Microscopically</a>, veins have a thick outer layer made of <a href="/wiki/Connective_tissue" title="Connective tissue">connective tissue</a>, called the <a href="/wiki/Tunica_externa" title="Tunica externa">tunica externa</a> or <i>tunica adventitia</i>. There is a middle layer of bands of <a href="/wiki/Smooth_muscle" title="Smooth muscle">smooth muscle</a> called <a href="/wiki/Tunica_media" title="Tunica media">tunica media</a>, which are, in general, much thinner than those of arteries, as veins do not function primarily in a contractile manner and are not subject to the high pressures of <a href="/wiki/Systole" title="Systole">systole</a>, as arteries are. The interior is lined with <a href="/wiki/Endothelium" title="Endothelium">endothelial cells</a> called <a href="/wiki/Tunica_intima" title="Tunica intima">tunica intima</a>. The precise location of veins varies much more from person to person than that of <a href="/wiki/Artery" title="Artery">arteries</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Veins serve to return blood from organs to the heart. Veins are also called "capacitance vessels" because most of the blood volume (60%) is contained within veins.  In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systemic_circulation" title="Systemic circulation">systemic circulation</a> oxygenated blood is pumped by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left_ventricle" title="Left ventricle">left ventricle</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Artery" title="Artery">arteries</a> to the muscles and organs of the body, where its nutrients and gases are exchanged at <a href="/wiki/Capillary" title="Capillary">capillaries</a>.  After taking up cellular waste and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> in capillaries, blood is channeled through vessels that converge with one another to form venules, which continue to converge and form the larger veins.  The de-<a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygenated</a> blood is taken by veins to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right_atrium" title="Right atrium">right atrium</a> of the heart, which transfers the blood to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right_ventricle" title="Right ventricle">right ventricle</a>, where it is then pumped through the pulmonary arteries to the <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">lungs</a>.  In <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_circulation" title="Pulmonary circulation">pulmonary circulation</a> the <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_vein" title="Pulmonary vein">pulmonary veins</a> return oxygenated blood from the lungs to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left_atrium" title="Left atrium">left atrium</a>, which empties into the left ventricle, completing the cycle of blood circulation.
</p><p>The return of blood to the heart is assisted by the action of the <a href="/wiki/Skeletal-muscle_pump" title="Skeletal-muscle pump">muscle pump</a>, and by the thoracic pump action of breathing during respiration.  Standing or sitting for a prolonged period of time can cause low venous return from venous pooling (vascular) shock. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fainting" title="Fainting">Fainting</a> can occur but usually baroreceptors within the aortic sinuses initiate a <a href="/wiki/Baroreflex" title="Baroreflex">baroreflex</a> such that angiotensin II and norepinephrine stimulate vasoconstriction and heart rate increases to return blood flow. <a href="/wiki/Neurogenic_shock" title="Neurogenic shock">Neurogenic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shock_(circulatory)#Hypovolaemic" title="Shock (circulatory)">hypovolaemic shock</a> can also cause fainting.  In these cases, the smooth muscles surrounding the veins become slack and the veins fill with the majority of the blood in the body, keeping blood away from the brain and causing unconsciousness. Jet pilots wear pressurized suits to help maintain their venous return and blood pressure.
</p><p>The arteries are perceived as carrying oxygenated blood to the tissues, while veins carry deoxygenated blood back to the heart. This is true of the systemic circulation, by far the larger of the two circuits of blood in the body, which transports oxygen from the heart to the tissues of the body.  However, in pulmonary circulation, the arteries carry deoxygenated blood from the heart to the lungs, and veins return blood from the lungs to the heart.  The difference between veins and arteries is their direction of flow (out of the heart by arteries, returning to the heart for veins), not their oxygen content.  In addition, deoxygenated blood that is carried from the tissues back to the heart for reoxygenation in the systemic circulation still carries some oxygen, though it is considerably less than that carried by the systemic arteries or pulmonary veins.
</p><p>Although most veins take blood back to the heart, there is an exception. Portal veins carry blood between capillary beds. For example, the hepatic portal vein takes blood from the capillary beds in the digestive tract and transports it to the capillary beds in the liver. The blood is then drained in the gastrointestinal tract and spleen, where it is taken up by the hepatic veins, and blood is taken back into the heart. Since this is an important function in mammals, damage to the hepatic portal vein can be dangerous. Blood clotting in the hepatic portal vein can cause portal hypertension, which results in a decrease of blood fluid to the liver.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cardiac_veins">Cardiac veins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cardiac veins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Blood_vessel" title="Blood vessel">vessels</a> that remove the deoxygenated <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a> muscle are known as cardiac veins. These include the <a href="/wiki/Great_cardiac_vein" title="Great cardiac vein">great cardiac vein</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_cardiac_vein" title="Middle cardiac vein">middle cardiac vein</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Small_cardiac_vein" title="Small cardiac vein">small cardiac vein</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Smallest_cardiac_veins" title="Smallest cardiac veins">smallest cardiac veins</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Anterior_cardiac_veins" title="Anterior cardiac veins">anterior cardiac veins</a>. Coronary veins carry blood with a poor level of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myocardium" title="Myocardium">myocardium</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right_atrium" title="Right atrium">right atrium</a>. Most of the blood of the coronary veins returns through the <a href="/wiki/Coronary_sinus" title="Coronary sinus">coronary sinus</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Anatomy" title="Anatomy">anatomy</a> of the veins of the heart is very variable, but generally it is formed by the following veins: heart veins that go into the coronary sinus: the <a href="/wiki/Great_cardiac_vein" title="Great cardiac vein">great cardiac vein</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_cardiac_vein" title="Middle cardiac vein">middle cardiac vein</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Small_cardiac_vein" title="Small cardiac vein">small cardiac vein</a>, the posterior vein of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left_ventricle" title="Left ventricle">left ventricle</a>, and the vein of Marshall. Heart veins that go directly to the right atrium: the anterior cardiac veins, the <a href="/wiki/Smallest_cardiac_veins" title="Smallest cardiac veins">smallest cardiac veins</a> (Thebesian veins).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_significance">Clinical significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Clinical significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diseases">Diseases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Diseases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Venous_insufficiency">Venous insufficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Venous insufficiency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chronic_venous_insufficiency" title="Chronic venous insufficiency">Chronic venous insufficiency</a></div>
<p>Venous insufficiency is the most common disorder of the venous system, and is usually manifested as <a href="/wiki/Telangiectasia" title="Telangiectasia">spider veins</a> or <a href="/wiki/Varicose_veins" title="Varicose veins">varicose veins</a>. Several varieties of treatments are used, depending on the patient's particular type and pattern of veins and on the physician's preferences.  Treatment can include Endovenous Thermal Ablation using radiofrequency or laser energy, <a href="/wiki/Vein_stripping" title="Vein stripping">vein stripping</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ambulatory_phlebectomy" title="Ambulatory phlebectomy">ambulatory phlebectomy</a>, foam <a href="/wiki/Sclerotherapy" title="Sclerotherapy">sclerotherapy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">lasers</a>, or compression.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postphlebitic_syndrome" title="Postphlebitic syndrome">Postphlebitic syndrome</a> is venous insufficiency that develops following <a href="/wiki/Deep_vein_thrombosis" title="Deep vein thrombosis">deep vein thrombosis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid16822286_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid16822286-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Deep_vein_thrombosis">Deep vein thrombosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Deep vein thrombosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Deep_vein_thrombosis" title="Deep vein thrombosis">Deep vein thrombosis</a></div>
<p>Deep vein thrombosis is a condition in which a <a href="/wiki/Thrombus" title="Thrombus">blood clot</a> forms in a deep vein. This is usually the veins of the legs, although it can also occur in the veins of the arms. Immobility, active cancer, obesity, traumatic damage and congenital disorders that make clots more likely are all risk factors for deep vein thrombosis. It can cause the affected limb to swell, and cause pain and an overlying skin rash. In the worst case, a deep vein thrombosis can extend, or a part of a clot can break off and land in the lungs, called <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_embolism" title="Pulmonary embolism">pulmonary embolism</a>.
</p><p>The decision to treat deep vein thrombosis depends on its size, a person's symptoms, and their risk factors. It generally involves <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anticoagulation" title="Anticoagulation">anticoagulation</a> to prevents clots or to reduce the size of the clot.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Portal_hypertension">Portal hypertension</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Portal hypertension">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The portal veins are found within the abdomen and carry blood through to the liver. <a href="/wiki/Portal_hypertension" title="Portal hypertension">Portal hypertension</a> is associated with cirrhosis or disease of the liver, or other conditions such as an obstructing clot (<a href="/wiki/Budd%E2%80%93Chiari_syndrome" title="Budd–Chiari syndrome">Budd Chiari syndrome</a>) or compression from tumours or tuberculosis lesions. When the pressure increases in the portal veins, a collateral circulation develops, causing visible veins such as <a href="/wiki/Esophageal_varices" title="Esophageal varices">oesophageal varices</a>.
</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Thrombophlebitis" title="Thrombophlebitis">Thrombophlebitis</a> is an inflammatory condition of the veins related to <a href="/wiki/Thrombus" title="Thrombus">blood clots</a>.
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Venous_valve_00013.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="416" data-file-width="560" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Venous_valve_00013.gif/220px-Venous_valve_00013.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Venous_valve_00013.gif/330px-Venous_valve_00013.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Venous_valve_00013.gif/440px-Venous_valve_00013.gif 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Venous_valve_00013.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Video of venous valve in action</div></div></div><p><a href="/wiki/Ultrasound" title="Ultrasound">Ultrasound</a>, particularly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duplex_ultrasonography" title="Duplex ultrasonography">duplex ultrasound</a>, is a common way that veins can be seen.
</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Veins_of_clinical_significance">Veins of clinical significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Veins of clinical significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Batson Venous plexus, or simply Batson's Plexus, runs through the inner vertebral column connecting the thoracic and pelvic veins.  These veins get their notoriety from the fact that they are valveless, which is believed to be the reason for metastasis of certain cancers. 
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Great_saphenous_vein" title="Great saphenous vein">great saphenous vein</a> is the most important superficial vein of the lower limb. First described by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persia" title="Persia">Persian</a> physician <a href="/wiki/Avicenna" title="Avicenna">Avicenna</a>, this vein derives its name from the word <i>safina</i>, meaning "hidden". This vein is "hidden" in its own <a href="/wiki/Fascial_compartment" title="Fascial compartment">fascial compartment</a> in the thigh and exits the fascia only near the knee. Incompetence of this vein is an important cause of <a href="/wiki/Varicose_veins" title="Varicose veins">varicose veins</a> of lower limbs.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thebesian_vein" title="Thebesian vein">Thebesian veins</a> within the myocardium of the heart are valveless veins that drain directly into the chambers of the heart. The coronary veins all empty into the <a href="/wiki/Coronary_sinus" title="Coronary sinus">coronary sinus</a> which empties into the right atrium.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Dural_venous_sinuses" title="Dural venous sinuses">dural venous sinuses</a> within the dura mater surrounding the brain receive blood from the brain and also are a point of entry of cerebrospinal fluid from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arachnoid_villi" title="Arachnoid villi">arachnoid villi</a> absorption. Blood eventually enters the internal jugular vein.
</p>

<p><i>Phlebology</i> is the medical specialty devoted to the diagnosis and treatment of venous disorders.  A medical specialist in phlebology is termed a <a href="/wiki/Phlebologist" title="Phlebologist">phlebologist</a>. A related image is called a <a href="/wiki/Impedance_phlebography" title="Impedance phlebography">phlebograph</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/American_Medical_Association" title="American Medical Association">American Medical Association</a> added phlebology to their list of self-designated practice specialties in 2005.  In 2007 the American Board of Phlebology (ABPh), subsequently known as the American Board of Venous &amp; Lymphatic Medicine (ABVLM), was established to improve the standards of phlebologists and the quality of their patient care by establishing a certification examination, as well as requiring maintenance of certification. Although As of  2017<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Vein&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> not a Member Board of the American Board of Medical Specialties (ABMS), the American Board of Venous &amp; Lymphatic Medicine uses a certification exam based on ABMS standards.
</p><p>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=American_College_of_Phlebology&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="American College of Phlebology (page does not exist)">American College of Phlebology</a> (ACP), one of the largest medical societies in the world for physicians and allied health professionals working in the field of phlebology, has 2000 members.  The ACP encourages education and training to improve the standards of medical practitioners and the quality of patient care.
</p><p>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=American_Venous_Forum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="American Venous Forum (page does not exist)">American Venous Forum</a> (AVF) is a medical society for physicians and allied health professionals dedicated to improving the care of patients with venous and lymphatic disease.  The majority of its members manage the entire spectrum of venous and lymphatic diseases – from varicose veins to congenital abnormalities to deep vein thrombosis to chronic venous diseases.  Founded in 1987, the AVF encourages research, clinical innovation, hands-on education, data collection and patient outreach.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vein&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Charta_ex_qva_figvram_parare_convenit,_illi_qvae_nervorvm_seriem_exprimit_appendendam,_1543..JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="530" height="319" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Charta_ex_qva_figvram_parare_convenit%2C_illi_qvae_nervorvm_seriem_exprimit_appendendam%2C_1543..JPG/220px-Charta_ex_qva_figvram_parare_convenit%2C_illi_qvae_nervorvm_seriem_exprimit_appendendam%2C_1543..JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Charta_ex_qva_figvram_parare_convenit%2C_illi_qvae_nervorvm_seriem_exprimit_appendendam%2C_1543..JPG/330px-Charta_ex_qva_figvram_parare_convenit%2C_illi_qvae_nervorvm_seriem_exprimit_appendendam%2C_1543..JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Charta_ex_qva_figvram_parare_convenit%2C_illi_qvae_nervorvm_seriem_exprimit_appendendam%2C_1543..JPG/440px-Charta_ex_qva_figvram_parare_convenit%2C_illi_qvae_nervorvm_seriem_exprimit_appendendam%2C_1543..JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Charta_ex_qva_figvram_parare_convenit,_illi_qvae_nervorvm_seriem_exprimit_appendendam,_1543..JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Human anatomical chart of blood vessels, with heart, lungs, liver and kidneys included. Other organs are numbered and arranged around it. Before cutting out the figures on this page, Vesalius suggests that readers glue the page onto parchment and gives instructions on how to assemble the pieces and paste the multilayered figure onto a base "muscle man" illustration. "Epitome", fol.14a. HMD Collection, WZ 240 V575dhZ 1543.</div></div></div>
<p>The earliest known writings on the circulatory system are found in the <a href="/wiki/Ebers_Papyrus" title="Ebers Papyrus">Ebers Papyrus</a> (16th century BCE), an <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_medicine" title="Ancient Egyptian medicine">ancient Egyptian medical papyrus</a> containing over 700 prescriptions and remedies, both physical and spiritual. In the <a href="/wiki/Papyrus" title="Papyrus">papyrus</a>, it acknowledges the connection of the heart to the arteries. The Egyptians thought air came in through the mouth and into the lungs and heart. From the heart, the air travelled to every member through the arteries. Although this concept of the circulatory system is only partially correct, it represents one of the earliest accounts of scientific thought.
</p><p>In the 6th century BCE, the knowledge of circulation of vital fluids through the body was known to the <a href="/wiki/Ayurveda" title="Ayurveda">Ayurvedic</a> physician <a href="/wiki/Sushruta" title="Sushruta">Sushruta</a> in <a href="/wiki/History_of_India" title="History of India">ancient India</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dwivedi&amp;Dwivedi07_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dwivedi&amp;Dwivedi07-6">[6]</a></sup> He also seems to have possessed knowledge of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arteries" title="Arteries">arteries</a>, described as 'channels' by Dwivedi &amp; Dwivedi (2007).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dwivedi&amp;Dwivedi07_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dwivedi&amp;Dwivedi07-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Heart_valve" title="Heart valve">valves of the heart</a> were discovered by a physician of the <a href="/wiki/Hippocrates" title="Hippocrates">Hippocratean</a> school around the 4th century BCE. However their function was not properly understood then. Because blood pools in the veins after death, arteries look empty. Ancient anatomists assumed they were filled with air and that they were for transport of air.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_Medicine" title="Ancient Greek Medicine">Greek physician</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herophilus" title="Herophilus">Herophilus</a>, distinguished veins from arteries but thought that the <a href="/wiki/Pulse" title="Pulse">pulse</a> was a property of arteries themselves. Greek anatomist <a href="/wiki/Erasistratus" title="Erasistratus">Erasistratus</a> observed that arteries that were cut during life bleed. He ascribed the fact to the phenomenon that air escaping from an artery is replaced with blood that entered by very small vessels between veins and arteries. Thus he apparently postulated capillaries but with reversed flow of blood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2nd century AD <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_Medicine" title="Ancient Greek Medicine">Greek</a> physician <a href="/wiki/Galen" title="Galen">Galen</a> knew that blood vessels carried blood and identified venous (dark red) and arterial (brighter and thinner) blood, each with distinct and separate functions. Growth and energy were derived from venous blood created in the liver from chyle, while arterial blood gave vitality by containing pneuma (air) and originated in the heart. Blood flowed from both creating organs to all parts of the body where it was consumed and there was no return of blood to the heart or liver. The heart did not pump blood around, the heart's motion sucked blood in during diastole and the blood moved by the pulsation of the arteries themselves.
</p><p>Galen believed that the arterial blood was created by venous blood passing from the left ventricle to the right by passing through 'pores' in the interventricular septum, air passed from the lungs via the pulmonary artery to the left side of the heart. As the arterial blood was created 'sooty' vapors were created and passed to the lungs also via the pulmonary artery to be exhaled.
</p><p>In 1025, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Canon_of_Medicine" title="The Canon of Medicine">The Canon of Medicine</a></i> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Iranian_Medicine" title="Ancient Iranian Medicine">Persian physician</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avicenna" title="Avicenna">Avicenna</a>, "erroneously accepted the Greek notion regarding the existence of a hole in the ventricular septum by which the blood traveled between the ventricles." While also refining Galen's erroneous theory of the pulse, Avicenna provided the first correct explanation of pulsation: "Every beat of the pulse comprises two movements and two pauses. Thus, expansion : pause : contraction : pause. [...] The pulse is a movement in the heart and arteries ... which takes the form of alternate expansion and contraction."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hajar_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hajar-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1242, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medicine_in_medieval_Islam" title="Medicine in medieval Islam">Arabian physician</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ibn_al-Nafis" title="Ibn al-Nafis">Ibn al-Nafis</a>, became the first person to accurately describe the process of <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_circulation" title="Pulmonary circulation">pulmonary circulation</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> for which he has been described as the <i>Arab Father of Circulation</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Ibn al-Nafis stated in his <i>Commentary on Anatomy in Avicenna's Canon</i>:
</p>
<blockquote><p>"...the blood from the right chamber of the heart must arrive at the left chamber but there is no direct pathway between them. The thick septum of the heart is not perforated and does not have visible pores as some people thought or invisible pores as Galen thought. The blood from the right chamber must flow through the vena arteriosa (<a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_artery" title="Pulmonary artery">pulmonary artery</a>) to the lungs, spread through its substances, be mingled there with air, pass through the arteria venosa (<a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_vein" title="Pulmonary vein">pulmonary vein</a>) to reach the left chamber of the heart and there form the vital spirit..."</p></blockquote>
<p>In addition, Ibn al-Nafis had an insight into what would become a larger theory of the <a href="/wiki/Capillary" title="Capillary">capillary</a> circulation. He stated that "there must be small communications or pores (<i>manafidh</i> in Arabic) between the pulmonary artery and vein," a prediction that preceded the discovery of the capillary system by more than 400 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Ibn al-Nafis' theory, however, was confined to blood transit in the lungs and did not extend to the entire body.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Michael_Servetus" title="Michael Servetus">Michael Servetus</a> was the first European to describe the function of pulmonary circulation, although his achievement was not widely recognized at the time, for a few reasons. He firstly described it in the "Manuscript of Paris"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> (near 1546), but this work was never published. And later he published  this description, but in a theological treatise, <i>Christianismi Restitutio</i>, not in a book on medicine. Only three copies of the book survived but these remained hidden for decades, the rest were burned  shortly after its publication in 1553 because of persecution of Servetus by religious authorities.
</p><p>Better known discovery of pulmonary circulation was by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vesalius" title="Vesalius">Vesalius</a>'s successor at <a href="/wiki/University_of_Padua" title="University of Padua">Padua</a>, <a href="/wiki/Realdo_Colombo" title="Realdo Colombo">Realdo Colombo</a>, in 1559.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:William_Harvey_(1578-1657)_Venenbild.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="901" data-file-width="1293" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/William_Harvey_%281578-1657%29_Venenbild.jpg/220px-William_Harvey_%281578-1657%29_Venenbild.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/William_Harvey_%281578-1657%29_Venenbild.jpg/330px-William_Harvey_%281578-1657%29_Venenbild.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/William_Harvey_%281578-1657%29_Venenbild.jpg/440px-William_Harvey_%281578-1657%29_Venenbild.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:William_Harvey_(1578-1657)_Venenbild.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Image of veins from <a href="/wiki/William_Harvey" title="William Harvey">William Harvey</a>'s <i>Exercitatio Anatomica de Motu Cordis et Sanguinis in Animalibus</i></div></div></div>
<p>Finally, <a href="/wiki/William_Harvey" title="William Harvey">William Harvey</a>, a pupil of <a href="/wiki/Hieronymus_Fabricius" title="Hieronymus Fabricius">Hieronymus Fabricius</a> (who had earlier described the valves of the veins without recognizing their function), performed a sequence of experiments, and published <i>Exercitatio Anatomica de Motu Cordis et Sanguinis in Animalibus</i> in 1628, which "demonstrated that there had to be a direct connection between the venous and arterial systems throughout the body, and not just the lungs. Most importantly, he argued that the beat of the heart produced a continuous circulation of blood through minute connections at the extremities of the body. This is a conceptual leap that was quite different from Ibn al-Nafis' refinement of the anatomy and bloodflow in the heart and lungs."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> This work, with its essentially correct exposition, slowly convinced the medical world. However, Harvey was not able to identify the capillary system connecting arteries and veins; these were later discovered by <a href="/wiki/Marcello_Malpighi" title="Marcello Malpighi">Marcello Malpighi</a> in 1661.
</p><p>In 1956, <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Cournand" title="André Frédéric Cournand">André Frédéric Cournand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Werner_Forssmann" title="Werner Forssmann">Werner Forssmann</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dickinson_W._Richards" title="Dickinson W. Richards">Dickinson W. Richards</a> were awarded the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Nobel_laureates_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="List of Nobel laureates in Physiology or Medicine">Nobel Prize</a> in Medicine "for their discoveries concerning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_catheterization" title="Heart catheterization">heart catheterization</a> and pathological changes in the circulatory system."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
In his Nobel lecture, Forssmann credits Harvey as birthing cardiology with the publication of his book in 1628.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1970s, <a href="/wiki/Diana_McSherry" title="Diana McSherry">Diana McSherry</a> developed computer-based systems to create images of the circulatory system and heart without the need for surgery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wayne_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wayne-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
