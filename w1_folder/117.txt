<p>The <b>tonne</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ʌ/: 'u' in 'cut'">ʌ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span></span>/</a></span><small class="nowrap"> (<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap"><a href="/wiki/File:En-us-tonne.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="20" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" width="11"/></a> </span><a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/06/En-us-tonne.ogg" title="En-us-tonne.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</small></span>) (Non-SI unit, symbol: <b>t</b>), commonly referred to as the <b>metric ton</b> in the United States, is a <a href="/wiki/Non-SI_units_mentioned_in_the_SI" title="Non-SI units mentioned in the SI">non-SI</a> <a href="/wiki/Metric_system#Variants" title="Metric system">metric unit</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> equal to 1,000 <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kilograms</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bippy_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-bippy-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FR1998_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FR1998-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> or one <b>megagram</b> (Mg); it is equivalent to approximately <span class="nowrap">2,204.6</span> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_avoirdupois_pound" title="International avoirdupois pound">pounds</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NGS_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-NGS-5">[5]</a></sup> <span class="nowrap">1.102 <a href="/wiki/Short_ton" title="Short ton">short tons</a> (US)</span> or 0.984 <a href="/wiki/Long_ton" title="Long ton">long tons</a> (UK). Although not part of the SI, the tonne is <a href="/wiki/Non-SI_units_mentioned_in_the_SI" title="Non-SI units mentioned in the SI">accepted</a> for use with SI units and prefixes by the <a href="/wiki/International_Committee_for_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Committee for Weights and Measures">International Committee for Weights and Measures</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The metric tonne is derived from the weight of pure water; there are 1,000 litres in one metric tonne.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Symbol_and_abbreviations">Symbol and abbreviations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tonne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Symbol and abbreviations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">SI</a> symbol for the tonne is "t", adopted at the same time as the unit in 1879.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bippy_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-bippy-2">[2]</a></sup> Its use is also official for the metric ton in the United States, having been adopted by the United States <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">National Institute of Standards and Technology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fedreg_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-fedreg-7">[7]</a></sup> It is a symbol, not an abbreviation, and should not be followed by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Period_(punctuation)" title="Period (punctuation)">period</a>. Informal and non-approved symbols or abbreviations include "T", "mT", "MT", and "mt".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Some of these are SI symbols for other units: "T" is the SI symbol for the <a href="/wiki/Tesla_(unit)" title="Tesla (unit)">tesla</a> and "Mt" is the SI symbol for megatonne (equivalent to one teragram); if describing <a href="/wiki/TNT_equivalent" title="TNT equivalent">TNT equivalent</a> units of energy, this is equivalent to 4.184 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petajoules" title="Petajoules">petajoules</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_and_spelling">Origin and spelling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tonne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin and spelling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In French and all English-speaking countries that are predominantly metric, <i>tonne</i> is the correct spelling. It is usually pronounced the same as ton <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ʌ/: 'u' in 'cut'">ʌ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>, but when it is important to clarify that the metric term is meant, rather than short ton, the final "e" can also be pronounced, i.e. "tonny" <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ʌ/: 'u' in 'cut'">ʌ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span></span>/</a></span></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>  In Australia, it is also pronounced <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">ɒ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Before <a href="/wiki/Metrication_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Metrication in the United Kingdom">metrication in the UK</a> the unit used for most purposes was the Imperial ton of 2,240 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pound_avoirdupois" title="Pound avoirdupois">pounds avoirdupois</a> or 20 <a href="/wiki/Hundredweight" title="Hundredweight">hundredweight</a> (usually referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Long_ton" title="Long ton">long ton</a> in the US), equivalent to 1,016 kg, differing by just 1.6% from the tonne. The UK <a href="/wiki/Weights_and_Measures_Acts_(UK)#20th_century" title="Weights and Measures Acts (UK)">Weights and Measures Act 1985</a> explicitly excluded from use for trade certain imperial units, including the ton, unless the item being sold or the weighing equipment being used was weighed or certified prior to 1 December 1980, and even then only if the buyer was made aware that the weight of the item was measured in imperial units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A complete citation is needed (October 2015)">full citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States <i>metric ton</i> is the name for this unit used and recommended by NIST;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fedreg_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-fedreg-7">[7]</a></sup> an unqualified mention of a <i>ton</i> almost invariably refers to a <a href="/wiki/Short_ton" title="Short ton">short ton</a> of 2,000 pounds (907 kg), and <i>tonne</i> is rarely used in speech or writing.
</p><p><i>Ton</i> and <i>tonne</i> are both derived from a <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic</a> word in general use in the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> area since the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> (cf. <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> and <a href="/wiki/Old_Frisian" title="Old Frisian">Old Frisian</a> <i>tunne</i>, <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">Old High German</a> and <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">Medieval Latin</a> <i>tunna</i>, <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> <i>tonne</i>) to designate a large cask, or <i>tun</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> A full tun, standing about a metre high, could easily weigh a tonne. An English <a href="/wiki/Tun_(unit)" title="Tun (unit)">tun</a> (an old wine cask volume measurement equivalent to 954 litres) of wine weighs roughly a tonne, 954 kg if full of water, a little less for wine.
</p><p>The spelling <i>tonne</i> pre-dates the introduction of the SI in 1960; it has been used with this meaning in France since 1842,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> when there were no metric prefixes for multiples of 10<sup>6</sup> and above, and is now used as the standard spelling for the metric mass measurement in most <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>-speaking countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In the United States, the unit was originally referred to using the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> words <i>millier</i> or <i>tonneau</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> but these terms are now obsolete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FR1998_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FR1998-3">[3]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_unit" title="Imperial unit">Imperial</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_customary_units" title="United States customary units">US customary</a> units comparable to the tonne are both spelled <i>ton</i> in English, though they differ in mass.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conversions">Conversions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tonne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Conversions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One tonne is equivalent to:
</p>
<ul><li>Metric/SI: 1 megagram (Mg) (by definition). Equal to <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7006100000000000000♠</span>1<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span> grams (g)</span> or <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003100000000000000♠</span>1000 kilograms (kg)</span>.
<ul><li>Megagram, Mg, is the official SI unit. Mg is distinct from mg, milligram.</li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pound_(mass)" title="Pound (mass)">Pounds</a> (lb): Exactly <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1000</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">0.453 592 37</span></span> lb (by definition of the pound),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbs447_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-nbs447-21">[21]</a></sup> or approximately <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003100000000006859♠</span>2<span style="margin-left:.25em;">204</span>.622<span style="margin-left:.25em;">622</span> lb</span> (10 <abbr title="significant figures">s.f.</abbr>).</li>
<li>US/<a href="/wiki/Short_ton" title="Short ton">Short tons</a> (ST): Exactly <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">0.907 184 74</span></span> short tons, or approximately <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7000110231131100000♠</span>1.102<span style="margin-left:.25em;">311</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">311</span></span> ST (10 <abbr title="significant figures">s.f.</abbr>).
<ul><li>One short ton is exactly <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002907184740000000♠</span>0.907<span style="margin-left:.25em;">184</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">74</span> t</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NISTUnits_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-NISTUnits-22">[22]</a></sup></li></ul></li>
<li>Imperial/<a href="/wiki/Long_ton" title="Long ton">Long tons</a> (LT): Exactly <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">1</span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">1.016 046 9088</span></span> long tons, or approximately <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6999984206527600000♠</span>0.984<span style="margin-left:.25em;">206</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">5276</span></span> LT (10 <abbr title="significant figures">s.f.</abbr>).
<ul><li>One long ton is exactly <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7003101604690880000♠</span>1.016<span style="margin-left:.25em;">046</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">9088</span> t</span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NISTUnits_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-NISTUnits-22">[22]</a></sup></li></ul></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Derived_units">Derived units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tonne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Derived units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For multiples of the tonne, it is more usual to speak of thousands or millions of tonnes. Kilotonne, megatonne, and gigatonne are more usually used for the energy of nuclear explosions and other events in <a href="/wiki/TNT_equivalent" title="TNT equivalent">equivalent mass of TNT</a>, often loosely as approximate figures.  When used in this context, there is little need to distinguish between metric and other tons, and the unit is spelt either as <i>ton</i> or <i>tonne</i> with the relevant prefix attached.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_usage">Alternative usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tonne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Alternative usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metric_ton_unit" title="Metric ton unit">metric ton unit</a></b> (mtu) can mean 10 kilograms (22 lb) within metal (e.g. tungsten, manganese) trading, particularly within the US. It traditionally referred to a metric ton of ore containing 1% (i.e. 10 kg) of metal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
The following excerpt from a mining geology textbook describes its usage in the particular case of tungsten:
</p>
<blockquote style="font-size:100%">
<p>"Tungsten concentrates are usually traded in metric tonne units (originally designating one tonne of ore containing 1% of WO<sub>3</sub>, today used to measure WO<sub>3</sub> quantities in 10 kg units. One metric tonne unit (mtu) of tungsten (VI) contains 7.93 kilograms of tungsten." (Walter L Pohl, <i>Economic Geology: Principles and Practices</i>, English edition, 2011, p 183.)
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Note that tungsten is also known as wolfram and has the atomic symbol W.
</p><p>In the case of <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>, the acronym <i>MTU</i> is sometimes considered to be <i>metric ton of uranium</i>, meaning  1,000 kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>A gigatonne of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_equivalent" title="Carbon dioxide equivalent">carbon dioxide equivalent</a> (GtCO2eq) is a unit used by the UN climate change panel, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPCC" title="IPCC">IPCC</a>, to measure the effect of a technology or process on <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_mass_as_proxy_for_energy">Use of mass as proxy for energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tonne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Use of mass as proxy for energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/TNT_equivalent" title="TNT equivalent">TNT equivalent</a></div>
<p>The <i>tonne of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trinitrotoluene" title="Trinitrotoluene">trinitrotoluene</a> (TNT)</i> is used as a proxy for energy, usually of explosions (TNT is a common <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_explosive" title="High explosive">high explosive</a>). Prefixes are used: kiloton(ne), megaton(ne), gigaton(ne), especially for expressing <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon_yield" title="Nuclear weapon yield">nuclear weapon yield</a>, based on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuel_value" title="Fuel value">specific combustion energy</a> of TNT of about 4.2 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megajoule" title="Megajoule">MJ</a>/<a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kg</a> (or one <a href="/wiki/Calorie" title="Calorie">thermochemical calorie</a> per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milligram" title="Milligram">milligram</a>).  Hence, 1 t TNT = 4.2 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigajoule" title="Gigajoule">GJ</a>, 1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kiloton" title="Kiloton">kt</a> TNT = 4.2 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terajoule" title="Terajoule">TJ</a>, 1 Mt TNT = 4.2 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petajoule" title="Petajoule">PJ</a>.
</p><p>The SI unit of energy is the <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">joule</a>. Assuming that a TNT explosion releases 1,000 small (thermochemical) <a href="/wiki/Calorie" title="Calorie">calories</a> per gram (4.2 <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">kJ</a>/g), one tonne of TNT is equivalent to 4.2 <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">gigajoules</a>.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_industry" title="Petroleum industry">petroleum industry</a> the <a href="/wiki/Tonne_of_oil_equivalent" title="Tonne of oil equivalent">tonne of oil equivalent</a> (toe) is a <a href="/wiki/Units_of_energy" title="Units of energy">unit of energy</a>: the amount of energy released by burning one tonne of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a>, approximately 42 GJ.  There are several slightly different definitions.  This is ten times as much as a tonne of TNT because atmospheric oxygen is used.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unit_of_force">Unit of force</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tonne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Unit of force">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like the <a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">gram</a> and the kilogram, the tonne gave rise to a (now obsolete) force unit of the same name, the tonne-force, equivalent to about 9.8 kilonewtons: a unit also often called simply "tonne" or "metric ton" without identifying it as a unit of force.  In contrast to the tonne as a mass unit, the tonne-force or metric ton-force is not acceptable for use with SI, partly because it is not an exact multiple of the SI unit of force, the <a href="/wiki/Newton_(unit)" title="Newton (unit)">newton</a>.
</p>
