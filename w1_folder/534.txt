<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Plastic crystal</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>plastic crystal</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystal</a> composed of weakly interacting molecules that possess some orientational or conformational degree of freedom. The name plastic crystal refers to the mechanical softness of such phases: they resemble waxes and are easily deformed. If the internal degree of freedom is molecular rotation, the name <b>rotor phase</b> or <b>rotatory phase</b> is also used. Typical examples are the <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">modifications</a> Methane I and Ethane I. 
In addition to the conventional molecular plastic crystals, there are also emerging ionic plastic crystals, particularly organic ionic plastic crystals (OIPCs) and protic organic ionic plastic crystals (POIPCs).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1,2,4-Triazolium_perfluorobutanesulfonate_as_an_archetypal_pure_protic_organic_ionic_plastic_crystal_electrolyte_for_all-solid-state_fuel_cells_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-1,2,4-Triazolium_perfluorobutanesulfonate_as_an_archetypal_pure_protic_organic_ionic_plastic_crystal_electrolyte_for_all-solid-state_fuel_cells-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Imidazolium_methanesulfonate_as_a_high_temperature_proton_conductor_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Imidazolium_methanesulfonate_as_a_high_temperature_proton_conductor-2">[2]</a></sup> POIPCs are solid protic organic salts formed by proton transfer from a Brønsted acid to a Brønsted base and in essence are protic ionic liquids in the molten state, have found to be promising solid-state proton conductors for high temperature <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proton_exchange_membrane_fuel_cell" title="Proton exchange membrane fuel cell">proton exchange membrane fuel cells</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1,2,4-Triazolium_perfluorobutanesulfonate_as_an_archetypal_pure_protic_organic_ionic_plastic_crystal_electrolyte_for_all-solid-state_fuel_cells_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-1,2,4-Triazolium_perfluorobutanesulfonate_as_an_archetypal_pure_protic_organic_ionic_plastic_crystal_electrolyte_for_all-solid-state_fuel_cells-1">[1]</a></sup> Examples include 1,2,4-triazolium perfluorobutanesulfonate<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1,2,4-Triazolium_perfluorobutanesulfonate_as_an_archetypal_pure_protic_organic_ionic_plastic_crystal_electrolyte_for_all-solid-state_fuel_cells_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-1,2,4-Triazolium_perfluorobutanesulfonate_as_an_archetypal_pure_protic_organic_ionic_plastic_crystal_electrolyte_for_all-solid-state_fuel_cells-1">[1]</a></sup> and imidazolium methanesulfonate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Imidazolium_methanesulfonate_as_a_high_temperature_proton_conductor_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Imidazolium_methanesulfonate_as_a_high_temperature_proton_conductor-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>If the internal degree of freedom freezes in a disordered way, an <a href="/wiki/Orientational_glass" title="Orientational glass">orientational glass</a> is obtained.
</p><p>The orientational degree of freedom may be an almost free rotation, or it may be a jump diffusion between a restricted number of possible orientations, as was shown for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrabromide" title="Carbon tetrabromide">carbon tetrabromide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The X-ray <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">diffraction</a> patterns of plastic crystals are characterized by strong diffuse intensity in addition to the sharp Bragg peaks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1,2,4-Triazolium_perfluorobutanesulfonate_as_an_archetypal_pure_protic_organic_ionic_plastic_crystal_electrolyte_for_all-solid-state_fuel_cells_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-1,2,4-Triazolium_perfluorobutanesulfonate_as_an_archetypal_pure_protic_organic_ionic_plastic_crystal_electrolyte_for_all-solid-state_fuel_cells-1">[1]</a></sup> In a powder pattern this intensity appears to resemble an amorphous background as one would expect for a liquid,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1,2,4-Triazolium_perfluorobutanesulfonate_as_an_archetypal_pure_protic_organic_ionic_plastic_crystal_electrolyte_for_all-solid-state_fuel_cells_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-1,2,4-Triazolium_perfluorobutanesulfonate_as_an_archetypal_pure_protic_organic_ionic_plastic_crystal_electrolyte_for_all-solid-state_fuel_cells-1">[1]</a></sup> but for a single crystal the diffuse contribution reveals itself to be highly structured. The Bragg peaks can be used to determine an average structure but due to the large amount of disorder this is not very insightful. It is the structure of the diffuse scattering that reflects the details of the constrained disorder in the system. Recent advances in two-dimensional detection at synchrotron beam lines facilitate the study of such patterns.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plastic_crystals_versus_liquid_crystals">Plastic crystals versus liquid crystals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plastic_crystal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plastic crystals versus liquid crystals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like <a href="/wiki/Liquid_crystal" title="Liquid crystal">liquid crystals</a>, plastic crystals can be considered a transitional stage between real solids and real liquids and can be considered <i>soft matter</i>. Another common denominator is the simultaneous presence of order and disorder. Both types of phases are usually observed between the true solid and liquid phases on the temperature scale:
</p>
<dl><dd>true crystal -&gt; plastic crystal -&gt; true liquid</dd>
<dd>true crystal -&gt; liquid crystal -&gt; true liquid</dd></dl>
<p>The difference between liquid and plastic crystals is easily observed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-ray_diffraction" title="X-ray diffraction">X-ray diffraction</a>. Plastic crystals possess strong long range order and therefore show sharp Bragg reflections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1,2,4-Triazolium_perfluorobutanesulfonate_as_an_archetypal_pure_protic_organic_ionic_plastic_crystal_electrolyte_for_all-solid-state_fuel_cells_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-1,2,4-Triazolium_perfluorobutanesulfonate_as_an_archetypal_pure_protic_organic_ionic_plastic_crystal_electrolyte_for_all-solid-state_fuel_cells-1">[1]</a></sup> Liquid crystals show no or very broad Bragg peaks because the order is not long range. The molecules that give rise to liquid crystalline behavior often have a strongly elongated or disc like shape. Plastic crystals consist usually of almost spherical objects. In this respect one could see them as opposites.
</p>
