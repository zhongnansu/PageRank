<p>A <b>hot spring</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Spring_(hydrology)" title="Spring (hydrology)">spring</a> produced by the emergence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geothermal_(geology)" title="Geothermal (geology)">geothermally heated</a> <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> that rises from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_crust" title="Earth's crust">Earth's crust</a>. While some of these springs contain water that is a safe temperature for bathing, others are so hot that immersion can result in injury or death.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_spring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Natural_iron_hot_spring.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="480" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Natural_iron_hot_spring.jpg/170px-Natural_iron_hot_spring.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Natural_iron_hot_spring.jpg/255px-Natural_iron_hot_spring.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Natural_iron_hot_spring.jpg/340px-Natural_iron_hot_spring.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Natural_iron_hot_spring.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"Blood Pond" hot spring in <a href="/wiki/Beppu" title="Beppu">Beppu</a>, Japan</div></div></div>
<p>There is no universally accepted definition of a hot spring. For example, one can find the phrase <i>hot spring</i> defined as 
</p>
<ul><li>any <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geothermal_(geology)" title="Geothermal (geology)">geothermal</a> spring<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>a spring with water temperatures above its surroundings<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li>a natural spring with water temperature above <a href="/wiki/Human_body_temperature" title="Human body temperature">human body temperature</a> – which is normally between 36.5 and 37.5 °C (97.7 and 99.5 °F)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>a natural spring with warm water above body temperature<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GM_Guelma_Hammam_Challala01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="346" data-file-width="500" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/GM_Guelma_Hammam_Challala01.jpg/220px-GM_Guelma_Hammam_Challala01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/GM_Guelma_Hammam_Challala01.jpg/330px-GM_Guelma_Hammam_Challala01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/GM_Guelma_Hammam_Challala01.jpg/440px-GM_Guelma_Hammam_Challala01.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GM_Guelma_Hammam_Challala01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Hammam_Maskhoutine" title="Hammam Maskhoutine">Hammam Maskhoutine</a> in <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a></div></div></div>
<ul><li>a thermal spring with water warmer than 36.7 °C (98 °F)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>a natural spring of water greater than 21.1 °C (70 °F) (synonymous with thermal spring)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>a natural discharge of <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> with elevated temperatures<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>a type of thermal spring in which hot water is brought to the surface. The water temperature of a hot spring is usually 6.5 °C (12 °F) or more above mean air temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Note that by this definition, "thermal spring" is not synonymous with the term "hot spring"</li>
<li>a spring whose hot water is brought to the surface (synonymous with a thermal spring). The water temperature of the spring is usually 8.3 °C (15 °F) or more above the mean air temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li>a spring with water above the core human body temperature – 36.7 °C (98 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pubs.nrc-cnrc.gc.ca_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-pubs.nrc-cnrc.gc.ca-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>a spring with water above average ambient ground temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>a spring with water temperatures above 50 °C (122 °F)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HotSpring_8344.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/HotSpring_8344.jpg/220px-HotSpring_8344.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/HotSpring_8344.jpg/330px-HotSpring_8344.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/HotSpring_8344.jpg/440px-HotSpring_8344.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HotSpring_8344.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hot spring in <a href="/wiki/Lassen_Volcanic_National_Park" title="Lassen Volcanic National Park">Lassen Volcanic National Park</a></div></div></div>
<p>The related term "<b>warm spring</b>" is defined as a spring with water temperature less than a hot spring by many sources, although Pentecost et al. (2003) suggest that the phrase "warm spring" is not useful and should be avoided.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pubs.nrc-cnrc.gc.ca_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-pubs.nrc-cnrc.gc.ca-14">[14]</a></sup> The US <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">NOAA</a> Geophysical Data Center defines a "warm spring" as a spring with water between 20 and 50 °C (68 and 122 °F)
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources_of_heat">Sources of heat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_spring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sources of heat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">Water</a> issuing from a hot spring is heated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geothermal_(geology)" title="Geothermal (geology)">geothermally</a>, that is, with <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> produced from the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a>. In general, the temperature of rocks within the earth increases with depth. The rate of temperature increase with depth is known as the geothermal <a href="/wiki/Gradient" title="Gradient">gradient</a>. If water percolates deeply enough into the crust, it will be heated as it comes into contact with hot rocks. The water from hot springs in non-<a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic</a> areas is heated in this manner.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Crepuscular_rays_and_Dead_trees_at_Mammoth_Hot_Springs.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1283" data-file-width="2000" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Crepuscular_rays_and_Dead_trees_at_Mammoth_Hot_Springs.jpg/220px-Crepuscular_rays_and_Dead_trees_at_Mammoth_Hot_Springs.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Crepuscular_rays_and_Dead_trees_at_Mammoth_Hot_Springs.jpg/330px-Crepuscular_rays_and_Dead_trees_at_Mammoth_Hot_Springs.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Crepuscular_rays_and_Dead_trees_at_Mammoth_Hot_Springs.jpg/440px-Crepuscular_rays_and_Dead_trees_at_Mammoth_Hot_Springs.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Crepuscular_rays_and_Dead_trees_at_Mammoth_Hot_Springs.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">Steam</a> <a href="/wiki/Crepuscular_rays" title="Crepuscular rays">Crepuscular rays</a> at <a href="/wiki/Mammoth_Hot_Springs" title="Mammoth Hot Springs">Mammoth Hot Springs</a></div></div></div>
<p>In active volcanic zones such as <a href="/wiki/Yellowstone_National_Park" title="Yellowstone National Park">Yellowstone National Park</a>, water may be heated by coming into contact with <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a> (molten rock). The high temperature gradient near magma may cause water to be heated enough that it boils or becomes <a href="/wiki/Superheating" title="Superheating">superheated</a>. If the water becomes so hot that it builds <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a> pressure and erupts in a jet above the surface of the Earth, it is called a <a href="/wiki/Geyser" title="Geyser">geyser</a>. If the water only reaches the surface in the form of <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a>, it is called a <a href="/wiki/Fumarole" title="Fumarole">fumarole</a>. If the water is mixed with mud and <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a>, it is called a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mud_pot" title="Mud pot">mud pot</a>.
</p><p>Note that hot springs in volcanic areas are often at or near the <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">boiling point</a>. People have been seriously scalded and even killed by accidentally or intentionally entering these springs.
</p><p>Warm springs are sometimes the result of hot and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spring_(hydrosphere)" title="Spring (hydrosphere)">cold springs</a> mixing. They may occur within a volcanic area or outside of one. One example of a non-volcanic warm spring is <a href="/wiki/Warm_Springs,_Georgia" title="Warm Springs, Georgia">Warm Springs, Georgia</a> (frequented for its <a href="/wiki/Physical_therapy" title="Physical therapy">therapeutic</a> effects by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paraplegic" title="Paraplegic">paraplegic</a> <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">U.S. President</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franklin_Delano_Roosevelt" title="Franklin Delano Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a>, who built the <a href="/wiki/Little_White_House" title="Little White House">Little White House</a> there).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flow_rates">Flow rates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_spring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Flow rates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Islande_source_Deildartunguhver.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="1024" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Islande_source_Deildartunguhver.jpg/170px-Islande_source_Deildartunguhver.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Islande_source_Deildartunguhver.jpg/255px-Islande_source_Deildartunguhver.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Islande_source_Deildartunguhver.jpg/340px-Islande_source_Deildartunguhver.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Islande_source_Deildartunguhver.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Deildartunguhver" title="Deildartunguhver">Deildartunguhver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>: the highest flow hot spring in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a></div></div></div>
<p>Hot springs range in flow rate from the tiniest "seeps" to veritable rivers of hot water. Sometimes there is enough pressure that the water shoots upward in a <a href="/wiki/Geyser" title="Geyser">geyser</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Fountain" title="Fountain">fountain</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="High_flow_hot_springs">High flow hot springs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_spring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: High flow hot springs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are many claims in the literature about the flow rates of hot springs. There are many more high flow non-thermal springs than geothermal springs. For example, there are 33 recognized "magnitude one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spring_(hydrosphere)" title="Spring (hydrosphere)">springs</a>" (having a flow in excess of 2,800 L/s (99 cu ft/s) in <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> alone. <a href="/wiki/Silver_Springs,_Florida" title="Silver Springs, Florida">Silver Springs, Florida</a> has a flow of more than 21,000 L/s (740 cu ft/s). Springs with high flow rates include:
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Excelsior_Geyser" title="Excelsior Geyser">Excelsior Geyser</a> Crater in <a href="/wiki/Yellowstone_National_Park" title="Yellowstone National Park">Yellowstone National Park</a> yields about 4,000 U.S. gal/min (0.25 m<sup>3</sup>/s).</li>
<li>Evans Plunge in <a href="/wiki/Hot_Springs,_South_Dakota" title="Hot Springs, South Dakota">Hot Springs, South Dakota</a> has a flow rate of 5,000 U.S. gal/min (0.32 m<sup>3</sup>/s) of 87 °F (31 °C) spring water. The Plunge, built in 1890, is the world's largest natural warm water indoor swimming pool.</li>
<li>The combined flow of the 47 hot springs in <a href="/wiki/Hot_Springs,_Arkansas" title="Hot Springs, Arkansas">Hot Springs, Arkansas</a> is 35 L/s (1.2 cu ft/s).</li>
<li>The hot spring of <a href="/wiki/Saturnia" title="Saturnia">Saturnia</a>, Italy with around 500 liters a second<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>
<li>The combined flow of the hot springs complex in <a href="/wiki/Truth_or_Consequences,_New_Mexico" title="Truth or Consequences, New Mexico">Truth or Consequences, New Mexico</a> is estimated at 99 liters/second.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lava_Hot_Springs" title="Lava Hot Springs">Lava Hot Springs</a> in <a href="/wiki/Idaho" title="Idaho">Idaho</a> has a flow of 130 liters/second.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glenwood_Springs" title="Glenwood Springs">Glenwood Springs</a> in <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a> has a flow of 143 liters/second.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Springs" title="Elizabeth Springs">Elizabeth Springs</a> in western <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>, Australia might have had a flow of 158 liters/second in the late 19th century, but now has a flow of about 5 liters/second.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Deildartunguhver" title="Deildartunguhver">Deildartunguhver</a> in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> has a flow of 180 liters/second.</li>
<li>The hot springs of <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Caldas_Novas" title="Caldas Novas">Caldas Novas</a> ("New Hot Springs" in <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>) are tapped by 86 wells, from which 333 liters/second are pumped for 14 hours per day. This corresponds to a peak average flow rate of 3.89 liters/second per well.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>The 2,850 hot springs of <a href="/wiki/Beppu" title="Beppu">Beppu</a> in Japan are the highest flow hot spring complex in Japan. Together the Beppu hot springs produce about 1,592 liters/second, or corresponding to an average hot spring flow of 0.56 liters/second.</li>
<li>The 303 hot springs of <a href="/wiki/Kokonoe,_%C5%8Cita" title="Kokonoe, Ōita">Kokonoe</a> in Japan produce 1,028 liters/second, which gives the average hot spring a flow of 3.39 liters/second.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/%C5%8Cita_Prefecture" title="Ōita Prefecture">Ōita Prefecture</a> has 4,762 hot springs, with a total flow of 4,437 liters/second, so the average hot spring flow is 0.93 liters/second.</li>
<li>The highest flow rate hot spring in Japan is the <a href="/wiki/Tamagawa_Hot_Spring" title="Tamagawa Hot Spring">Tamagawa Hot Spring</a> in <a href="/wiki/Akita_Prefecture" title="Akita Prefecture">Akita Prefecture</a>, which has a flow rate of 150 liters/second. The Tamagawa Hot Spring feeds a 3 m (9.8 ft) wide stream with a temperature of 98 °C (208 °F).</li>
<li>There are at least three hot springs in the <a href="/wiki/Nage" title="Nage">Nage</a> region 8 km (5.0 mi) south west of <a href="/wiki/Bajawa" title="Bajawa">Bajawa</a> in <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> that collectively produce more than 453.6 liters/second.</li>
<li>There are another three large hot springs (Mengeruda, Wae Bana and Piga) 18 km (11 mi) north east of Bajawa, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> that together produce more than 450 liters/second of hot water.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Dalhousie_Springs" title="Dalhousie Springs">Dalhousie Springs</a> complex in Australia had a peak total flow of more than 23,000 liters/second in 1915, giving the average spring in the complex an output of more than 325 liters/second. This has been reduced now to a peak total flow of 17,370 liters/second so the average spring has a peak output of about 250 liters/second.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>In Yukon’s Boreal Forest, 25 minutes north-west of Whitehorse in northern Canada, <a href="/wiki/Takhini_Hot_Springs" title="Takhini Hot Springs">Takhini Hot Springs</a> flows out of the Earth’s interior at  385 L/min (85 imp gal/min; 102 US gal/min) and 47 °C (118 °F) year-round.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Therapeutic_uses">Therapeutic uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_spring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Therapeutic uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Onsen_in_Nachikatsuura,_Japan.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="878" data-file-width="1234" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Onsen_in_Nachikatsuura%2C_Japan.jpg/220px-Onsen_in_Nachikatsuura%2C_Japan.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Onsen_in_Nachikatsuura%2C_Japan.jpg/330px-Onsen_in_Nachikatsuura%2C_Japan.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Onsen_in_Nachikatsuura%2C_Japan.jpg/440px-Onsen_in_Nachikatsuura%2C_Japan.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Onsen_in_Nachikatsuura,_Japan.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Japanese open air hot spring (<a href="/wiki/Onsen" title="Onsen">onsen</a>) in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nachikatsuura,_Wakayama" title="Nachikatsuura, Wakayama">Nachikatsuura, Wakayama</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bain_romain_de_Khenchela.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Bain_romain_de_Khenchela.jpg/220px-Bain_romain_de_Khenchela.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Bain_romain_de_Khenchela.jpg/330px-Bain_romain_de_Khenchela.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Bain_romain_de_Khenchela.jpg/440px-Bain_romain_de_Khenchela.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bain_romain_de_Khenchela.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Hammam_Essalihine" title="Hammam Essalihine">Hammam Essalihine</a>, Roman hot spring in <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a></div></div></div>
<p>Because heated water can hold more <a href="/wiki/Solvation" title="Solvation">dissolved</a> <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solids</a> than cold water, warm and especially hot springs often have very high <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> content, containing everything from simple <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> to <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a>, and even <a href="/wiki/Radium" title="Radium">radium</a>. Because of both the <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a> and the claimed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical" title="Medical">medical</a> value some of these springs have, they are often popular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tourist" title="Tourist">tourist</a> destinations, and locations for <a href="/wiki/Physical_therapy" title="Physical therapy">rehabilitation</a> <a href="/wiki/Clinic" title="Clinic">clinics</a> for those with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disabilities" title="Disabilities">disabilities</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biota">Biota</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_spring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Biota">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Thermophile" title="Thermophile">Thermophile</a></div>
<p>A thermophile is an organism — a type of <a href="/wiki/Extremophile" title="Extremophile">extremophile</a> — that <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/thrives" title="wikt:thrives">thrives</a> at high temperatures, between 45 and 80 °C (113 and 176 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Thermophiles are found in hot springs, as well as <a href="/wiki/Deep_sea" title="Deep sea">deep sea</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent" title="Hydrothermal vent">hydrothermal vents</a> and decaying plant matter such as peat bogs and compost.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Algal_mats_on_hot_pool,_Orakei_Korako_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Algal_mats_on_hot_pool%2C_Orakei_Korako_1.jpg/220px-Algal_mats_on_hot_pool%2C_Orakei_Korako_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Algal_mats_on_hot_pool%2C_Orakei_Korako_1.jpg/330px-Algal_mats_on_hot_pool%2C_Orakei_Korako_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Algal_mats_on_hot_pool%2C_Orakei_Korako_1.jpg/440px-Algal_mats_on_hot_pool%2C_Orakei_Korako_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Algal_mats_on_hot_pool,_Orakei_Korako_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Algal_mat" title="Algal mat">Algal mats</a> growing in the <i>Map of Africa</i> hot pool, <a href="/wiki/Orakei_Korako" title="Orakei Korako">Orakei Korako</a>, New Zealand</div></div></div>
<p>Some hot springs biota are infectious to humans. For example:
</p>
<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Naegleria_fowleri" title="Naegleria fowleri">Naegleria fowleri</a></i>, an <a href="/wiki/Amoeba" title="Amoeba">amoeba</a>, live in warm waters and soils worldwide and may cause <a href="/wiki/Meningitis" title="Meningitis">meningitis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Several deaths have been attributed to this amoeba, which enters the brain through the nasal passages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Acanthamoeba" title="Acanthamoeba">Acanthamoeba</a> also can spread through hot springs, according to the US <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control" title="Centers for Disease Control">Centers for Disease Control</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Legionella" title="Legionella">Legionella</a> <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> have been spread through hot springs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></li>
<li>Viruses have been collected from very extreme environments, for example, a hot spring with a temperature of 87 to 93 °C (189 to 199 °F) and an incredibly acidic <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> of 1.5 in <a href="/wiki/Pozzuoli" title="Pozzuoli">Pozzuoli</a>, Italy. These viruses were observed to infect cells in the laboratory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></li></ul>

<p>There are hot springs in many countries and on all continents of the world. Countries that are renowned for their hot springs include China, <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Onsen" title="Onsen">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, and <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, but there are hot springs in many other places as well:
</p>
<ul><li>Widely renowned since a chemistry professor's report in 1918 classified them as one of the world's most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrolytic" title="Electrolytic">electrolytic</a> mineral waters,  the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Termas_de_Rio_Hondo" title="Termas de Rio Hondo">Rio Hondo Hot Springs</a> in northern <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a> have become among the most visited on earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Cacheuta_Spa" title="Cacheuta Spa">Cacheuta Spa</a> is another famous hot springs in Argentina.</li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chaudes-Aigues-Lavoir.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3072" data-file-width="2304" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Chaudes-Aigues-Lavoir.JPG/170px-Chaudes-Aigues-Lavoir.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Chaudes-Aigues-Lavoir.JPG/255px-Chaudes-Aigues-Lavoir.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Chaudes-Aigues-Lavoir.JPG/340px-Chaudes-Aigues-Lavoir.JPG 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chaudes-Aigues-Lavoir.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chaudes-Aigues-Lavoir</div></div></div>
<ul><li>The springs in Europe with the highest temperatures are located in France, in a small village named <a href="/wiki/Chaudes-Aigues" title="Chaudes-Aigues">Chaudes-Aigues</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Located at the heart of the French volcanic region <a href="/wiki/Auvergne" title="Auvergne">Auvergne</a>, the thirty natural hot springs of Chaudes-Aigues have temperatures ranging from 45° C (113° F) to more than 80° C (176° F). The hottest one, the "Source du Par", has a temperature of 82° C (179.6° F). The hot waters running under the village have provided heat for the houses and for the church since the 14th Century. Chaudes-Aigues (Cantal, France) is a <a href="/wiki/Spa_town" title="Spa town">spa town</a> known since the Roman Empire for the treatment of rheumatism.</li>
<li>One of the highly potential geothermal energy reservoirs in India is the Tattapani thermal springs of Madhya Pradesh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etiquette">Etiquette</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hot_spring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Etiquette">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The customs and practices observed differ depending on the hot spring. It is common practice that bathers should wash before entering the water so as not to contaminate the water (with/without soap).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In many countries, like Japan, it is required to enter the hot spring with no clothes on, including swimwear. Typically in these circumstances, there are different facilities or times for men and women. In some countries, if it is a public hot spring, swimwear is required.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
