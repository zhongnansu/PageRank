<p>In <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a> and related fields, a <b>stratum</b> (plural: <b>strata</b>) is a layer of <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rock</a> or <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Igneous_rock" title="Igneous rock">igneous rock</a> that were formed at the Earth's surface,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> with internally consistent characteristics that distinguish it from other layers. The "stratum" is the fundamental unit in a stratigraphic column and forms the basis of the study of <a href="/wiki/Stratigraphy" title="Stratigraphy">stratigraphy</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stratum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SEUtahStrat.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1794" data-file-width="3161" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/SEUtahStrat.JPG/250px-SEUtahStrat.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/SEUtahStrat.JPG/375px-SEUtahStrat.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/SEUtahStrat.JPG/500px-SEUtahStrat.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SEUtahStrat.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">Permian</a> through <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> strata in the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Plateau" title="Colorado Plateau">Colorado Plateau</a> area of southeastern <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a> demonstrates the principles of <a href="/wiki/Stratigraphy" title="Stratigraphy">stratigraphy</a>.  These strata make up much of the famous prominent rock formations in widely spaced protected areas such as <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Reef_National_Park" title="Capitol Reef National Park">Capitol Reef National Park</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canyonlands_National_Park" title="Canyonlands National Park">Canyonlands National Park</a>.  From top to bottom: Rounded tan domes of the <a href="/wiki/Navajo_Sandstone" title="Navajo Sandstone">Navajo Sandstone</a>, layered red <a href="/wiki/Kayenta_Formation" title="Kayenta Formation">Kayenta Formation</a>, cliff-forming, vertically jointed, red <a href="/wiki/Wingate_Sandstone" title="Wingate Sandstone">Wingate Sandstone</a>, slope-forming, purplish <a href="/wiki/Chinle_Formation" title="Chinle Formation">Chinle Formation</a>, layered, lighter-red <a href="/wiki/Moenkopi_Formation" title="Moenkopi Formation">Moenkopi Formation</a>, and white, layered <a href="/wiki/Cutler_Formation" title="Cutler Formation">Cutler Formation</a> sandstone.  Picture from <a href="/wiki/Glen_Canyon_National_Recreation_Area" title="Glen Canyon National Recreation Area">Glen Canyon National Recreation Area</a>, Utah.</div></div></div>
<p>Each layer is generally one of a number of parallel layers that lie one upon another, laid down by natural processes. They may extend over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1_E11_m%C2%B2" title="1 E11 m²">hundreds of thousands of square kilometers</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s surface. Strata are typically seen as bands of different colored or differently structured material exposed in <a href="/wiki/Cliff" title="Cliff">cliffs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Road" title="Road">road</a> cuts, <a href="/wiki/Quarry" title="Quarry">quarries</a>, and <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a> banks. Individual bands may vary in <a href="/wiki/Thickness_(geology)" title="Thickness (geology)">thickness</a> from a few <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millimeter" title="Millimeter">millimeters</a> to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilometer" title="Kilometer">kilometer</a> or more. Each band represents a specific mode of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deposition_(sediment)" title="Deposition (sediment)">deposition</a>: river <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silt</a>, beach <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a>, coal <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamp</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sand_dune" title="Sand dune">sand dune</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava</a> bed, etc.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming">Naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stratum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Geologist" title="Geologist">Geologists</a> study rock strata and categorize them by the material of beds. Each distinct layer is typically assigned to the name of sheet, usually based on a town, river, mountain, or region where the formation is exposed and available for study. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Burgess_Shale" title="Burgess Shale">Burgess Shale</a> is a thick exposure of dark, occasionally <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossiliferous</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shale" title="Shale">shale</a> exposed high in the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Rockies" title="Canadian Rockies">Canadian Rockies</a> near <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Burgess_Pass&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Burgess Pass (page does not exist)">Burgess Pass</a>. Slight distinctions in material in a formation may be described as "members" (or sometimes "beds"). Formations are collected into "groups" while groups may be collected into "supergroups".
</p>
