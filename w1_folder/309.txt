<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Alcohol</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about the class of chemical compounds. For <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> found in <a href="/wiki/Alcoholic_drink" title="Alcoholic drink">alcoholic drinks</a>, see <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_(drug)" title="Alcohol (drug)">Alcohol (drug)</a>. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Alcohol_(disambiguation)" title="Alcohol (disambiguation)">Alcohol (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alcohol.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="1556" height="116" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Alcohol.png/180px-Alcohol.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Alcohol.png/270px-Alcohol.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Alcohol.png/360px-Alcohol.png 2x" width="180"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alcohol.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Ball-and-stick_model" title="Ball-and-stick model">Ball-and-stick model</a> of an alcohol molecule (R<sub>3</sub>COH). The red and grey balls represent the hydroxyl group (-OH). The three "R's" stand for carbon substituents or hydrogen atoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alcohol_general.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="68" data-file-width="115" height="106" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Alcohol_general.svg/180px-Alcohol_general.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Alcohol_general.svg/270px-Alcohol_general.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Alcohol_general.svg/360px-Alcohol_general.svg.png 2x" width="180"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alcohol_general.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The bond angle between an hydroxyl group (-OH) and a chain of carbon atoms (R)</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, an <b>alcohol</b> is any <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compound</a> in which the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl" title="Hydroxyl">hydroxyl</a> <a href="/wiki/Functional_group" title="Functional group">functional group</a> (–<a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">H</a>) is bound to a <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The term alcohol originally referred to the primary alcohol <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> (ethyl alcohol), which is <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_(drug)" title="Alcohol (drug)">used as a drug</a> and is the main alcohol present in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcoholic beverages</a>. An important class of alcohols, of which <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a> and ethanol are the simplest members, includes all compounds the general formula for which is C<sub>n</sub>H<sub>2n+1</sub>OH.  <i>It is these simple monoalcohols that are the subject of this article.</i>
</p><p>The suffix <i>-ol</i> appears in the <a href="/wiki/IUPAC_nomenclature_of_organic_chemistry" title="IUPAC nomenclature of organic chemistry">IUPAC chemical name</a> of all substances where the hydroxyl group is the functional group with the highest priority. When a higher priority group is present in the compound, the prefix <i>hydroxy-</i> is used in its IUPAC name. The suffix <i>-ol</i> in non-IUPAC names (such as <a href="/wiki/Paracetamol" title="Paracetamol">paracetamol</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cholesterol" title="Cholesterol">cholesterol</a>) also typically indicates that the substance is an alcohol. However, many substances that contain hydroxyl functional groups (particularly sugars, such as <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a>) have names which include neither the suffix <i>-ol</i>, nor the prefix <i>hydroxy-</i>. 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcohol_distillation" title="Alcohol distillation">Alcohol distillation</a> was known to Islamic chemists as early as the eighth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab" title="Arab">Arab</a> chemist, <a href="/wiki/Al-Kindi" title="Al-Kindi">al-Kindi</a>, unambiguously described the distillation of wine in a treatise titled as "The Book of the chemistry of Perfume and Distillations".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Persian_people" title="Persian people">Persian</a> <a href="/wiki/Medicine_in_the_medieval_Islamic_world" title="Medicine in the medieval Islamic world">physician</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alchemy_and_chemistry_in_medieval_Islam" title="Alchemy and chemistry in medieval Islam">alchemist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polymath" title="Polymath">polymath</a> and <a href="/wiki/Islamic_philosophy" title="Islamic philosophy">philosopher</a> <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_ibn_Zakariya_al-Razi" title="Muhammad ibn Zakariya al-Razi">Rhazes</a> (854 CE – 925 CE)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> is credited with the discovery of ethanol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature">Nomenclature</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h3>
<p>The word "alcohol" is from the Arabic <i><a href="/wiki/Kohl_(cosmetics)" title="Kohl (cosmetics)">kohl</a></i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar">الكحل</span>‎, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Arabic" title="Romanization of Arabic">translit.</a> </small><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language transliteration">al-kuḥl</i>), a powder used as an eyeliner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-" title="Al-">Al-</a> is the Arabic <a href="/wiki/Article_(grammar)" title="Article (grammar)">definite article</a>, equivalent to <i>the</i> in English. <i>Alcohol</i> was originally used for the very fine powder produced by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sublimation_(chemistry)" title="Sublimation (chemistry)">sublimation</a> of the natural mineral <a href="/wiki/Stibnite" title="Stibnite">stibnite</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Antimony_trisulfide" title="Antimony trisulfide">antimony trisulfide</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">Sb<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>S<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>. It was considered to be the essence or "spirit" of this mineral. It was used as an <a href="/wiki/Antiseptic" title="Antiseptic">antiseptic</a>, eyeliner, and <a href="/wiki/Kohl_(cosmetics)" title="Kohl (cosmetics)">cosmetic</a>. The meaning of alcohol was extended to distilled substances in general, and then narrowed to ethanol, when "spirits" was a synonym for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distilled_beverage" title="Distilled beverage">hard liquor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bartholomew_Traheron" title="Bartholomew Traheron">Bartholomew Traheron</a>, in his 1543 translation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_of_Vigo" title="John of Vigo">John of Vigo</a>, introduces the word as a term used by "barbarous" (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moorish" title="Moorish">Moorish</a>) authors for "fine powder." Vigo wrote: "the barbarous auctours use alcohol, or (as I fynde it sometymes wryten) alcofoll, for moost fine poudre."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-oed-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The 1657 <i>Lexicon Chymicum</i>, by William Johnson glosses the word as "antimonium sive stibium."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> By extension, the word came to refer to any fluid obtained by distillation, including "alcohol of wine," the distilled essence of wine. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Libavius" title="Libavius">Libavius</a> in <i>Alchymia</i> (1594) refers to "vini alcohol vel vinum alcalisatum". Johnson (1657) glosses <i>alcohol vini</i> as "quando omnis superfluitas vini a vino separatur, ita ut accensum ardeat donec totum consumatur, nihilque fæcum aut phlegmatis in fundo remaneat." The word's meaning became restricted to "spirit of wine" (the chemical known today as <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>) in the 18th century and was extended to the class of substances so-called as "alcohols" in modern chemistry after 1850.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oed_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-oed-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The term <i>ethanol</i> was invented 1892, combining the word <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">ethane</a> with the "-ol" ending of "alcohol".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Systematic_names">Systematic names</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Chemical_nomenclature" title="Chemical nomenclature">IUPAC nomenclature</a> is used in scientific publications and where precise identification of the substance is important, especially in cases where the relative complexity of the molecule does not make such a systematic name unwieldy. In naming simple alcohols, the name of the alkane chain loses the terminal <i>e</i> and adds the suffix <i>-ol</i>, <i>e.g.</i>, as in "ethanol" from the alkane chain name "ethane".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reusch-alcohols_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-reusch-alcohols-16">[16]</a></sup> When necessary, the position of the hydroxyl group is indicated by a number between the alkane name and the <i>-ol</i>: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propan-1-ol" title="Propan-1-ol">propan-1-ol</a> for <span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>CH<span <p>In cases where the OH functional group is bonded to an sp<sup>2</sup> carbon on an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aromatic_compound" title="Aromatic compound">aromatic ring</a> the molecule is known as a <a href="/wiki/Phenols" title="Phenols">phenol</a>, and is named using the IUPAC rules for naming phenols.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_names">Common names</span></h3>
<p>In other less formal contexts, an alcohol is often called with the name of the corresponding alkyl group followed by the word "alcohol", e.g., <a href="/wiki/Methyl_group" title="Methyl group">methyl</a> alcohol, <a href="/wiki/Ethyl_group" title="Ethyl group">ethyl</a> alcohol. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propyl" title="Propyl">Propyl</a> alcohol may be <a href="/wiki/Propanol" title="Propanol"><i>n</i>-propyl alcohol</a> or <a href="/wiki/Isopropyl_alcohol" title="Isopropyl alcohol">isopropyl alcohol</a>, depending on whether the hydroxyl group is bonded to the end or middle carbon on the straight <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a> chain. As described under systematic naming, if another group on the molecule takes priority, the alcohol moiety is often indicated using the "hydroxy-" prefix.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NamingRules_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-NamingRules-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p><span id="secondary"></span><span id="tertiary"></span>
Alcohols are then classified into primary, secondary (<i>sec-</i>, <i>s-</i>), and tertiary (<i>tert-</i>, <i>t-</i>), based upon the number of carbon atoms connected to the carbon atom that bears the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl" title="Hydroxyl">hydroxyl</a> <a href="/wiki/Functional_group" title="Functional group">functional group</a>. (The respective numeric shorthands 1°, 2°, and 3° are also sometimes used in informal settings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>) The primary alcohols have general formulas RCH<sub>2</sub>OH. The simplest primary alcohol is methanol (CH<sub>3</sub>OH), for which R=H, and the next is ethanol, for which R=CH<sub>3</sub>, the <a href="/wiki/Methyl_group" title="Methyl group">methyl group</a>. Secondary alcohols are those of the form RR'CHOH, the simplest of which is 2-propanol (R=R'=CH<sub>3</sub>). For the tertiary alcohols the general form is RR'R"COH. The simplest example is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tert-butanol" title="Tert-butanol">tert-butanol</a> (2-methylpropan-2-ol), for which each of R, R', and R" is CH<sub>3</sub>. In these shorthands, R, R', and R" represent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Substituents" title="Substituents">substituents</a>, alkyl or other attached, generally organic groups.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alcohol_by_Country.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="820" data-file-width="1800" height="91" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Alcohol_by_Country.png/200px-Alcohol_by_Country.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Alcohol_by_Country.png/300px-Alcohol_by_Country.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Alcohol_by_Country.png/400px-Alcohol_by_Country.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alcohol_by_Country.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Total recorded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcohol_consumption_by_country" title="Alcohol consumption by country">alcohol per capita consumption</a> (15+), in litres of pure ethanol<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Alcohols have a long history of myriad uses. For simple mono-alcohols, which is the focus on this article, the following are most important industrial alcohols:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann1_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann1-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>methanol, mainly for the production of <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">formaldehyde</a> and as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuel_additive" title="Fuel additive">fuel additive</a></li>
<li>ethanol, mainly for alcoholic beverages, fuel additive, solvent</li>
<li>1-propanol, 1-butanol, and isobutyl alcohol for use as a solvent and precursor to solvents</li>
<li>C6–C11 alcohols used for <a href="/wiki/Plasticizer" title="Plasticizer">plasticizers</a>, e.g. in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyvinylchloride" title="Polyvinylchloride">polyvinylchloride</a></li>
<li>fatty alcohol (C12–C18), precursors to <a href="/wiki/Detergent" title="Detergent">detergents</a></li></ul>
<p>Methanol is the most common industrial alcohol, with about 12 million tons/y produced in 1980. The combined capacity of the other alcohols is about the same, distributed roughly equally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann1_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann1-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicity">Toxicity</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a></div>
<p>With respect to acute toxicity, simple alcohols have low acute toxicities.  Doses of several milliliters are tolerated.  For <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pentanol" title="Pentanol">pentanols</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hexanol" title="Hexanol">hexanols</a>, <a href="/wiki/Octanol" title="Octanol">octanols</a> and longer alcohols, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LD50" title="LD50">LD50</a> range from 2–5 g/kg (rats, oral).  Methanol and ethanol are less acutely toxic.  All alcohols are mild skin irritants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann1_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann1-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>The metabolism of methanol (and ethylene glycol) is affected by the presence of ethanol, which has a higher affinity for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liver_alcohol_dehydrogenase" title="Liver alcohol dehydrogenase">liver alcohol dehydrogenase</a>.  In this way methanol will be excreted intact in urine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schep_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schep-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span></h2>
<p>In general, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl_group" title="Hydroxyl group">hydroxyl group</a> makes alcohols <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_molecule" title="Polar molecule">polar</a>. Those groups can form <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bond" title="Hydrogen bond">hydrogen bonds</a> to one another and to most other compounds.  Owing to the presence of the polar OH alcohols are more water-soluble than simple hydrocarbons.  Methanol, ethanol, and propanol are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miscible" title="Miscible">miscible</a> in water. <a href="/wiki/N-Butanol" title="N-Butanol">Butanol</a>, with a four-carbon chain, is moderately soluble.
</p><p>Because of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bonding" title="Hydrogen bonding">hydrogen bonding</a>, alcohols tend to have higher boiling points than comparable <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ether" title="Ether">ethers</a>. The boiling point of the alcohol ethanol is 78.29 °C, compared to 69 °C for the hydrocarbon <a href="/wiki/Hexane" title="Hexane">hexane</a>, and 34.6 °C for <a href="/wiki/Diethyl_ether" title="Diethyl ether">diethyl ether</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence_in_nature">Occurrence in nature</span></h2>
<p>Simple alcohols are found widely in nature.  Ethanol is most prominent because it is the product of fermentation, a major energy-producing pathway.  The other simple alcohols are formed in only trace amounts.  More complex alcohols are pervasive, as manifested in sugars, some amino acids, and fatty acids.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ziegler_and_oxo_processes">Ziegler and oxo processes</span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Ziegler_process" title="Ziegler process">Ziegler process</a>, linear alcohols are produced from ethylene and <a href="/wiki/Triethylaluminium" title="Triethylaluminium">triethylaluminium</a> followed by oxidation and hydrolysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann1_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann1-22">[22]</a></sup>  An idealized synthesis of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1-octanol" title="1-octanol">1-octanol</a> is shown:
</p>
<dl><dd>Al(C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>5</sub>)<sub>3</sub>  +  9 C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>4</sub>   →  Al(C<sub>8</sub>H<sub>17</sub>)<sub>3</sub></dd>
<dd>Al(C<sub>8</sub>H<sub>17</sub>)<sub>3</sub> +  3 O  +  3 H<sub>2</sub>O  →  3 HOC<sub>8</sub>H<sub>17</sub>  +  Al(OH)<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>The process generates a range of alcohols that are separated by <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distillation</a>.
</p><p>Many higher alcohols are produced by <a href="/wiki/Hydroformylation" title="Hydroformylation">hydroformylation</a> of alkenes followed by hydrogenation. When applied to a terminal alkene, as is common, one typically obtains a linear alcohol:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann1_22-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann1-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>RCH=CH<sub>2</sub>  +  H<sub>2</sub>  +  CO   →  RCH<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>CHO</dd>
<dd>RCH<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>CHO +  3 H<sub>2</sub>  →  RCH<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>OH</dd></dl>
<p>Such processes give <a href="/wiki/Fatty_alcohol" title="Fatty alcohol">fatty alcohols</a>, which are useful for detergents.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydration_reactions">Hydration reactions</span></h3>
<p>Some low molecular weight alcohols of industrial importance are produced by the addition of water to alkenes. Ethanol, isopropanol, 2-butanol, and tert-butanol are produced by this general method. Two implementations are employed, the direct and indirect methods. The direct method avoids the formation of stable intermediates, typically using acid catalysts. In the indirect method, the alkene is converted to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulfate_ester" title="Sulfate ester">sulfate ester</a>, which is subsequently hydrolyzed. The direct <a href="/wiki/Hydration_reaction" title="Hydration reaction">hydration</a> using <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">ethylene</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ethanol#Ethylene_hydration" title="Ethanol">ethylene hydration</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ECT4_820_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-ECT4_820-26">[26]</a></sup> or other alkenes from <a href="/wiki/Cracking_(chemistry)" title="Cracking (chemistry)">cracking</a> of fractions of distilled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a>.
</p><p>Hydration is also used industrially to produce the diol <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_glycol" title="Ethylene glycol">ethylene glycol</a> from <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_oxide" title="Ethylene oxide">ethylene oxide</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_routes">Biological routes</span></h3>
<p>Ethanol is obtained by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">fermentation</a> using <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> produced from sugar from the <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a>, in the presence of yeast and temperature of less than 37 °C to produce ethanol. For instance, such a process might proceed by the conversion of <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a> by the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Invertase" title="Invertase">invertase</a> into <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fructose" title="Fructose">fructose</a>, then the conversion of <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> by the enzyme complex <a href="/wiki/Zymase" title="Zymase">zymase</a> into <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> (and carbon dioxide).
</p><p>Several species of the benign bacteria in the intestine use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(food)" title="Fermentation (food)">fermentation</a> as a form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anaerobic_metabolism" title="Anaerobic metabolism">anaerobic metabolism</a>. This <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolic</a> reaction produces <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> as a waste product. Thus, human bodies contain some quantity of alcohol endogenously produced by these bacteria. In rare cases, this can be sufficient to cause "<a href="/wiki/Auto-brewery_syndrome" title="Auto-brewery syndrome">auto-brewery syndrome</a>" in which intoxicating quantities of alcohol are produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Like ethanol, <a href="/wiki/N-Butanol" title="N-Butanol">butanol</a> can be produced by fermentation processes. Saccharomyces yeast are known to produce these higher alcohols at temperatures above 75 °F (24 °C). The bacterium <i><a href="/wiki/Clostridium_acetobutylicum" title="Clostridium acetobutylicum">Clostridium acetobutylicum</a></i> can feed on <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a> to produce butanol on an industrial scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zverlov_et_al_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zverlov_et_al-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Substitution">Substitution</span></h4>
<p>Primary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkyl_halide" title="Alkyl halide">alkyl halides</a> react with aqueous <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">NaOH</a> or <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">KOH</a> mainly to primary alcohols in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nucleophilic_aliphatic_substitution" title="Nucleophilic aliphatic substitution">nucleophilic aliphatic substitution</a>. (Secondary and especially tertiary alkyl halides will give the elimination (alkene) product instead). <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grignard_reagent" title="Grignard reagent">Grignard reagents</a> react with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbonyl" title="Carbonyl">carbonyl</a> groups to secondary and tertiary alcohols. Related reactions are the <a href="/wiki/Barbier_reaction" title="Barbier reaction">Barbier reaction</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nozaki-Hiyama_reaction" title="Nozaki-Hiyama reaction">Nozaki-Hiyama reaction</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Reduction">Reduction</span></h4>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aldehydes" title="Aldehydes">Aldehydes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">ketones</a> are <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduced</a> with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_borohydride" title="Sodium borohydride">sodium borohydride</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lithium_aluminium_hydride" title="Lithium aluminium hydride">lithium aluminium hydride</a> (after an acidic workup). Another reduction by aluminiumisopropylates is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verley_reduction" title="Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verley reduction">Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verley reduction</a>. <a href="/wiki/Noyori_asymmetric_hydrogenation" title="Noyori asymmetric hydrogenation">Noyori asymmetric hydrogenation</a> is the asymmetric reduction of β-keto-esters.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrolysis">Hydrolysis</span></h4>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkenes" title="Alkenes">Alkenes</a> engage in an acid catalysed <a href="/wiki/Hydration_reaction" title="Hydration reaction">hydration reaction</a> using concentrated sulfuric acid as a catalyst that gives usually secondary or tertiary alcohols. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroboration-oxidation" title="Hydroboration-oxidation">hydroboration-oxidation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxymercuration-reduction" title="Oxymercuration-reduction">oxymercuration-reduction</a> of alkenes are more reliable in organic synthesis. Alkenes react with NBS and water in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halohydrin_formation_reaction" title="Halohydrin formation reaction">halohydrin formation reaction</a>. <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">Amines</a> can be converted to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diazonium_salt" title="Diazonium salt">diazonium salts</a>, which are then hydrolyzed.
</p><p>The formation of a secondary alcohol via reduction and hydration is shown:
</p>
<dl><dd><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Secondary_alcohol_formation-diagram.svg" title="Preparation of a secondary alcohol"><img alt="Preparation of a secondary alcohol" data-file-height="96" data-file-width="566" height="59" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Secondary_alcohol_formation-diagram.svg/350px-Secondary_alcohol_formation-diagram.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Secondary_alcohol_formation-diagram.svg/525px-Secondary_alcohol_formation-diagram.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Secondary_alcohol_formation-diagram.svg/700px-Secondary_alcohol_formation-diagram.svg.png 2x" width="350"/></a></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deprotonation">Deprotonation</span></h3>
<p>With a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PKa" title="PKa">pK<sub>a</sub></a> of around 16–19, they are, in general, slightly weaker <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a> than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" title="Water (molecule)">water</a>.  With strong bases such as <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydride" title="Sodium hydride">sodium hydride</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> they form <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a>  called <b><a href="/wiki/Alkoxide" title="Alkoxide">alkoxides</a></b>, with the general formula <a href="/wiki/Alkyl" title="Alkyl">R</a>O<sup>−</sup> <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">M</a><sup>+</sup>.
</p>
<dl><dd>2 R-OH + 2 NaH  → 2 R-O<sup>−</sup>Na<sup>+</sup> + 2 H<sub>2</sub></dd>
<dd>2 R-OH + 2 Na   →   2 R-O<sup>−</sup>Na<sup>+</sup> + H<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>The acidity of alcohols is strongly affected by <a href="/wiki/Solvation" title="Solvation">solvation</a>.  In the gas phase, alcohols are more acidic than is water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nucleophilic_substitution">Nucleophilic substitution</span></h3>
<p>The OH group is not a good <a href="/wiki/Leaving_group" title="Leaving group">leaving group</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nucleophilic_substitution" title="Nucleophilic substitution">nucleophilic substitution</a> reactions, so neutral alcohols do not react in such reactions. However, if the oxygen is first protonated to give R−OH<sub>2</sub><sup>+</sup>, the leaving group (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" title="Water (molecule)">water</a>) is much more stable, and the nucleophilic substitution can take place. For instance, tertiary alcohols react with <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> to produce tertiary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkyl_halide" title="Alkyl halide">alkyl halides</a>, where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl_group" title="Hydroxyl group">hydroxyl group</a> is replaced by a <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> atom by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unimolecular_nucleophilic_substitution" title="Unimolecular nucleophilic substitution">unimolecular nucleophilic substitution</a>. If primary or secondary alcohols are to be reacted with <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>, an activator such as <a href="/wiki/Zinc_chloride" title="Zinc chloride">zinc chloride</a> is needed. In alternative fashion, the conversion may be performed directly using <a href="/wiki/Thionyl_chloride" title="Thionyl chloride">thionyl chloride</a>.<sup>[1]</sup>
</p><p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alcohol_reaction_examples.gif" title="Some simple conversions of alcohols to alkyl chlorides"><img alt="Some simple conversions of alcohols to alkyl chlorides" data-file-height="209" data-file-width="1419" height="103" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Alcohol_reaction_examples.gif/700px-Alcohol_reaction_examples.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Alcohol_reaction_examples.gif/1050px-Alcohol_reaction_examples.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Alcohol_reaction_examples.gif/1400px-Alcohol_reaction_examples.gif 2x" width="700"/></a>
</p><p>Alcohols may, likewise, be converted to alkyl bromides using <a href="/wiki/Hydrobromic_acid" title="Hydrobromic acid">hydrobromic acid</a> or <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus_tribromide" title="Phosphorus tribromide">phosphorus tribromide</a>, for example:
</p>
<dl><dd>3 R-OH + PBr<sub>3</sub> → 3 RBr + H<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barton-McCombie_deoxygenation" title="Barton-McCombie deoxygenation">Barton-McCombie deoxygenation</a> an alcohol is deoxygenated to an <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">alkane</a> with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organotin" title="Organotin">tributyltin hydride</a> or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organoborane" title="Organoborane">trimethylborane</a>-water complex in a <a href="/wiki/Radical_substitution" title="Radical substitution">radical substitution</a> reaction.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dehydration">Dehydration</span></h3>
<p>Meanwhile, the oxygen atom has <a href="/wiki/Lone_pair" title="Lone pair">lone pairs</a> of nonbonded electrons that render it weakly <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">basic</a> in the presence of strong acids such as <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>. For example, with methanol:
</p><p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Methanol_acid_base.svg" title="Acidity &amp; basicity of methanol"><img alt="Acidity &amp; basicity of methanol" data-file-height="95" data-file-width="512" height="93" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Methanol_acid_base.svg/500px-Methanol_acid_base.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Methanol_acid_base.svg/750px-Methanol_acid_base.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Methanol_acid_base.svg/1000px-Methanol_acid_base.svg.png 2x" width="500"/></a>
</p><p>Upon treatment with strong acids, alcohols undergo the E1 <a href="/wiki/Elimination_reaction" title="Elimination reaction">elimination reaction</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkenes</a>. The reaction, in general, obeys <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zaitsev%27s_Rule" title="Zaitsev's Rule">Zaitsev's Rule</a>, which states that the most stable (usually the most substituted) alkene is formed. Tertiary alcohols eliminate easily at just above room temperature, but primary alcohols require a higher temperature.
</p><p>This is a diagram of acid catalysed dehydration of ethanol to produce <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">ethylene</a>:
</p><p><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DehydrationOfAlcoholWithH-.png"><img alt="DehydrationOfAlcoholWithH-.png" data-file-height="225" data-file-width="1500" height="83" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/DehydrationOfAlcoholWithH-.png/550px-DehydrationOfAlcoholWithH-.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/DehydrationOfAlcoholWithH-.png/825px-DehydrationOfAlcoholWithH-.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/DehydrationOfAlcoholWithH-.png/1100px-DehydrationOfAlcoholWithH-.png 2x" width="550"/></a>
</p><p>A more controlled elimination reaction is the  with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_disulfide" title="Carbon disulfide">carbon disulfide</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iodomethane" title="Iodomethane">iodomethane</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Esterification">Esterification</span></h3>
<p>Alcohol and <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxylic acids</a> react in the so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fischer_esterification" title="Fischer esterification">Fischer esterification</a>.  The reaction usually requires a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalyst" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a>, such as concentrated sulfuric acid:
</p>
<dl><dd>R-OH + R'-CO<sub>2</sub>H → R'-CO<sub>2</sub>R + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Other types of ester are prepared in a similar manner –  for example, <a href="/wiki/Tosyl" title="Tosyl">tosyl</a> (tosylate) esters are made by reaction of the alcohol with p-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toluenesulfonyl" title="Toluenesulfonyl">toluenesulfonyl</a> chloride in pyridine.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxidation">Oxidation</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_oxidation" title="Alcohol oxidation">Alcohol oxidation</a></div>
<p>Primary alcohols (R-CH<sub>2</sub>OH) can be oxidized either to <a href="/wiki/Aldehyde" title="Aldehyde">aldehydes</a> (R-CHO) or to <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxylic acids</a> (R-CO<sub>2</sub>H).  The oxidation of secondary alcohols (R<sup>1</sup>R<sup>2</sup>CH-OH) normally terminates at the <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">ketone</a> (R<sup>1</sup>R<sup>2</sup>C=O) stage. Tertiary alcohols (R<sup>1</sup>R<sup>2</sup>R<sup>3</sup>C-OH) are resistant to oxidation.
</p><p>The direct <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_of_primary_alcohols_to_carboxylic_acids" title="Oxidation of primary alcohols to carboxylic acids">oxidation of primary alcohols to carboxylic acids</a> normally proceeds via the corresponding aldehyde, which is transformed via an <i>aldehyde hydrate</i> (R-CH(OH)<sub>2</sub>) by reaction with water before it can be further oxidized to the carboxylic acid.
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alcohol_to_aldehyde_to_acid.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="230" data-file-width="1474" height="78" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Alcohol_to_aldehyde_to_acid.png/500px-Alcohol_to_aldehyde_to_acid.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Alcohol_to_aldehyde_to_acid.png/750px-Alcohol_to_aldehyde_to_acid.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Alcohol_to_aldehyde_to_acid.png/1000px-Alcohol_to_aldehyde_to_acid.png 2x" width="500"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alcohol_to_aldehyde_to_acid.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mechanism of <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_of_primary_alcohols_to_carboxylic_acids" title="Oxidation of primary alcohols to carboxylic acids">oxidation of primary alcohols to carboxylic acids</a> via aldehydes and aldehyde hydrates</div></div></div></div>
<p>Reagents useful for the transformation of primary alcohols to aldehydes are normally also suitable for the <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_of_secondary_alcohols_to_ketones" title="Oxidation of secondary alcohols to ketones">oxidation of secondary alcohols to ketones</a>. These include <a href="/wiki/Collins_reagent" title="Collins reagent">Collins reagent</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dess-Martin_periodinane" title="Dess-Martin periodinane">Dess-Martin periodinane</a>. The direct oxidation of primary alcohols to carboxylic acids can be carried out using <a href="/wiki/Potassium_permanganate" title="Potassium permanganate">potassium permanganate</a> or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jones_reagent" title="Jones reagent">Jones reagent</a>.
</p>
