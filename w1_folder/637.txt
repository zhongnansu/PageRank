<p>An <b>interglacial period</b> (or alternatively <b>interglacial</b>, <b>interglaciation</b>) is a geological interval of warmer global average temperature lasting thousands of years that separates consecutive <a href="/wiki/Glacial_period" title="Glacial period">glacial periods</a> within an <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a>. The current <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> interglacial began at the end of the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a>, about 11,700 years ago.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interglacials_during_the_Pleistocene">Interglacials during the Pleistocene</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interglacial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Interglacials during the Pleistocene">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the 2.5 million year span of the <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a>, numerous glacials, or significant advances of continental ice sheets in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, have occurred at intervals of approximately 40,000 to 100,000 years. These long glacial periods were separated by more temperate and shorter interglacials. 
</p><p>During interglacials, such as the present one, the climate warms and the <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> recedes polewards following the ice sheets. Forests return to areas that once supported tundra vegetation. Interglacials are identified on land or in shallow <a href="/wiki/Inland_sea_(geology)" title="Inland sea (geology)">epicontinental seas</a> by their paleontology. Floral and faunal remains of species pointing to temperate climate and indicating a specific age are used to identify particular interglacials. Commonly used are mammalian and molluscan species, pollen and plant macro-remains (seeds and fruits). However, many other fossil remains may be helpful: insects,   ostracods, foraminifera, diatoms, etc. Recently, ice cores and ocean sediment cores provide more quantitative and accurately dated evidence for temperatures and total ice volumes.
</p><p>The interglacials and glacials coincide with cyclic changes in the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_orbit" title="Earth's orbit">Earth's orbit</a>. Three orbital variations contribute to interglacials. The first is a change in the Earth's orbit around the sun, or <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">eccentricity</a>. The second is a shift in the tilt of the Earth's axis, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obliquity" title="Obliquity">obliquity</a>. The third is <a href="/wiki/Axial_precession" title="Axial precession">precession</a>, or wobbling motion of Earth's axis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
Warm <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">summers</a> in the Southern hemisphere occur when that hemisphere is tilted toward the sun and the Earth is nearest the sun in its elliptical orbit. Cool summers occur when the Earth is farthest from the sun during that season.  These effects are more pronounced when the eccentricity of the orbit is large. When the obliquity is large, seasonal changes are more extreme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Interglacials are a useful tool for geological mapping and also for anthropologists, as they can be used as a dating method for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hominid" title="Hominid">hominid</a> fossils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Brief periods of milder climate that occurred during the last glacial are called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstadial" title="Interstadial">interstadials</a>. Most (not all) interstadials are shorter than interglacials. Interstadial climate may have been relatively warm but this is not necessarily so. Because the colder periods (stadials) have often been very dry, wetter (so not necessarily warmer) periods have been registered in the sedimentary record as interstadials as well. 
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_isotope_ratio_cycle" title="Oxygen isotope ratio cycle">oxygen isotope ratio</a> obtained from seabed sediment <a href="/wiki/Core_sample" title="Core sample">core samples</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_proxy" title="Climate proxy">proxy</a> for average global temperature, is an important source of information about changes in the climate of the earth.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interglacial_optimum">Interglacial optimum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Interglacial&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Interglacial optimum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An interglacial optimum, or <i>climatic optimum of an interglacial</i>, is the period within an interglacial that experienced the most 'favourable' <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> that occurred during that interglacial, often during the middle part. The climatic optimum of an interglacial follows, and is followed by, phases that are within the same interglacial and that experienced a less favourable climate (but nevertheless a 'better' climate than during the preceding/succeeding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glacial" title="Glacial">glacials</a>).
During an interglacial optimum, sea levels rise to their highest values, but not necessarily exactly at the same time as the climatic optimum.
</p><p>In the present interglacial, the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Holocene_climatic_optimum" title="Holocene climatic optimum">climatic optimum</a> occurred during the <a href="/wiki/Subboreal" title="Subboreal">Subboreal</a> (5 to 2.5 <a href="/wiki/Year#SI_prefix_multipliers" title="Year">ka</a> <a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">BP</a>, which corresponds to 3000 BC–500 BC) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlanticum" title="Atlanticum">Atlanticum</a> (9 to 5 ka, which corresponds to roughly 7000 BC–3000 BC). Our current climatic phase following this climatic optimum is still within the same interglacial (the Holocene).  This warm period was followed by a gradual decline until about 2,000 years ago, with <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Warm_Period" title="Medieval Warm Period">another warm period</a> until the <a href="/wiki/Little_Ice_Age" title="Little Ice Age">Little Ice Age</a> (1250–1850).
</p><p>The preceding interglacial optimum occurred during the Late <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eemian_Stage" title="Eemian Stage">Eemian Stage</a>, 131–114 ka. During the Eemian the climatic optimum took place during <a href="/wiki/Pollen_zone" title="Pollen zone">pollen zone</a> E4 in the type area (city of <a href="/wiki/Amersfoort" title="Amersfoort">Amersfoort</a>, <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>). Here this zone is characterized by the expansion of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quercus" title="Quercus">Quercus</a></i> (oak), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corylus" title="Corylus">Corylus</a></i> (hazel), <i><a href="/wiki/Taxus" title="Taxus">Taxus</a></i> (yew), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ulmus" title="Ulmus">Ulmus</a></i> (elm), <i><a href="/wiki/Fraxinus" title="Fraxinus">Fraxinus</a></i> (ash), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carpinus" title="Carpinus">Carpinus</a></i> (hornbeam), and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Picea" title="Picea">Picea</a></i> (spruce). During the Eemian Stage sea level was about 8 meters higher than today and the water temperature of the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> was about 2 °C higher than at present.
</p>
