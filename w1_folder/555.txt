<p>The <b>halogens</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'h' in 'hi'">h</span><span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">æ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="/dʒ/: 'j' in 'jam'">dʒ</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span></span>, <wbr/><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'h' in 'hi'">h</span><span title="/eɪ/: 'a' in 'face'">eɪ</span></span>-, <wbr/>-<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span></span>-, <wbr/>-<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="/dʒ/: 'j' in 'jam'">dʒ</span><span title="/ɛ/: 'e' in 'dress'">ɛ</span><span title="'n' in 'nigh'">n</span></span>/</a></span></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>) are a <a href="/wiki/Group_(periodic_table)" title="Group (periodic table)">group</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic table</a> consisting of five chemically related <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">elements</a>: <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a> (F), <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> (Cl), <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">bromine</a> (Br), <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a> (I), and <a href="/wiki/Astatine" title="Astatine">astatine</a> (At). The artificially created element 117 (<a href="/wiki/Tennessine" title="Tennessine">tennessine</a>, Ts) may also be a halogen. In the modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAC" title="IUPAC">IUPAC</a> nomenclature, this group is known as <b>group 17</b>. The symbol <b>X</b> is often used generically to refer to any halogen.
</p><p>The name "halogen" means "salt-producing". When halogens react with metals they produce a wide range of salts, including <a href="/wiki/Calcium_fluoride" title="Calcium fluoride">calcium fluoride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a> (common table salt), <a href="/wiki/Silver_bromide" title="Silver bromide">silver bromide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Potassium_iodide" title="Potassium iodide">potassium iodide</a>.
</p><p>The group of halogens is the only <a href="/wiki/Group_(periodic_table)" title="Group (periodic table)">periodic table group</a> that contains elements in three of the main <a href="/wiki/State_of_matter" title="State of matter">states of matter</a> at <a href="/wiki/Standard_conditions_for_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard conditions for temperature and pressure">standard temperature and pressure</a>. All of the halogens form acids when bonded to hydrogen. Most halogens are typically produced from <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a> or <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a>. The middle halogens, that is chlorine, bromine and iodine, are often used as <a href="/wiki/Disinfectant" title="Disinfectant">disinfectants</a>. Organobromides are the most important class of <a href="/wiki/Flame_retardant" title="Flame retardant">flame retardants</a>. Elemental halogens are dangerous and can be lethally toxic.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The fluorine mineral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluorospar" title="Fluorospar">fluorospar</a> was known as early as 1529. Early chemists realized that fluorine compounds contain an undiscovered element, but were unable to isolate it. In 1860, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Gore_(chemist)" title="George Gore (chemist)">George Gore</a>, an English chemist, ran a current of electricity through <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">hydrofluoric acid</a> and probably produced fluorine, but he was unable to prove his results at the time. In 1886, <a href="/wiki/Henri_Moissan" title="Henri Moissan">Henri Moissan</a>, a chemist in Paris, performed <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> on <a href="/wiki/Potassium_bifluoride" title="Potassium bifluoride">potassium bifluoride</a> dissolved in anhydrous <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">hydrogen fluoride</a>, and successfully isolated fluorine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature's_Building_Blocks_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nature's_Building_Blocks-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">Hydrochloric acid</a> was known to <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">alchemists</a> and early chemists. However, elemental chlorine was not produced until 1774, when <a href="/wiki/Carl_Wilhelm_Scheele" title="Carl Wilhelm Scheele">Carl Wilhelm Scheele</a> heated hydrochloric acid with <a href="/wiki/Manganese_dioxide" title="Manganese dioxide">manganese dioxide</a>. Scheele called the element "dephlogisticated muriatic acid", which is how chlorine was known for 33 years. In 1807, <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Davy" title="Humphry Davy">Humphry Davy</a> investigated chlorine and discovered that it is an actual element. Chlorine combined with hydrochloric acid,as well as sulfuric acid in certain instances created chlorine gas a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poisonous_gas" title="Poisonous gas">poisonous gas</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. It displaced oxygen in contaminated areas and replaced common oxygenated air with the toxic chlorine gas.  In which the gas would burn human tissue externally and internally,especially the lungs making breathing difficult or impossible depending on level of contamination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature's_Building_Blocks_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nature's_Building_Blocks-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Bromine was discovered in the 1820s by <a href="/wiki/Antoine_J%C3%A9r%C3%B4me_Balard" title="Antoine Jérôme Balard">Antoine Jérôme Balard</a>. Balard discovered bromine by passing chlorine gas through a sample of <a href="/wiki/Brine" title="Brine">brine</a>. He originally proposed the name <i>muride</i> for the new element, but the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_Academy" title="French Academy">French Academy</a> changed the element's name to bromine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature's_Building_Blocks_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nature's_Building_Blocks-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Iodine was discovered by <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Courtois" title="Bernard Courtois">Bernard Courtois</a>, who was using <a href="/wiki/Seaweed" title="Seaweed">seaweed</a> ash as part of a process for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saltpeter" title="Saltpeter">saltpeter</a> manufacture. Courtois typically boiled the seaweed ash with water to generate <a href="/wiki/Potassium_chloride" title="Potassium chloride">potassium chloride</a>. However, in 1811, Courtois added sulfuric acid to his process, and found that his process produced purple fumes that condensed into black crystals. Suspecting that these crystals were a new element, Courtois sent samples to other chemists for investigation. Iodine was proven to be a new element by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Gay-Lussac" title="Joseph Gay-Lussac">Joseph Gay-Lussac</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature's_Building_Blocks_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Nature's_Building_Blocks-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1931, <a href="/wiki/Fred_Allison" title="Fred Allison">Fred Allison</a> claimed to have discovered element 85 with a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Magneto-optical_machine&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Magneto-optical machine (page does not exist)">magneto-optical machine</a>, and named the element Alabamine, but was mistaken. In 1937, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rajendralal_De&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rajendralal De (page does not exist)">Rajendralal De</a> claimed to have discovered element 85 in minerals, and called the element dakine, but he was also mistaken. An attempt at discovering element 85 in 1939 by <a href="/wiki/Horia_Hulubei" title="Horia Hulubei">Horia Hulubei</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yvette_Cauchois" title="Yvette Cauchois">Yvette Cauchois</a> via <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopy</a> was also unsuccessful, as was an attempt in the same year by <a href="/wiki/Walter_Minder" title="Walter Minder">Walter Minder</a>, who discovered an iodine-like element resulting from <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a> of <a href="/wiki/Polonium" title="Polonium">polonium</a>. Element 85, now named astatine, was produced successfully in 1940 by <a href="/wiki/Dale_R._Corson" title="Dale R. Corson">Dale R. Corson</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K.R._Mackenzie" title="K.R. Mackenzie">K.R. Mackenzie</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emilio_G._Segr%C3%A8" title="Emilio G. Segrè">Emilio G. Segrè</a>, who bombarded <a href="/wiki/Bismuth" title="Bismuth">bismuth</a> with <a href="/wiki/Alpha_particle" title="Alpha particle">alpha particles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature's_Building_Blocks_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Nature's_Building_Blocks-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1811, the German chemist <a href="/wiki/Johann_Schweigger" title="Johann Schweigger">Johann Schweigger</a> proposed that the name "halogen" – meaning "salt producer", from αλς [als] "salt" and γενειν [genein] "to beget" – replace the name "chlorine", which had been proposed by the English chemist <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Davy" title="Humphry Davy">Humphry Davy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>  Davy's name for the element prevailed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> However, in 1826, the <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">chemist</a> Baron <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6ns_Jacob_Berzelius" title="Jöns Jacob Berzelius">Jöns Jacob Berzelius</a> proposed the term "halogen" for the elements fluorine, chlorine, and iodine, which produce a <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">sea-salt</a>-like substance when they form a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">compound</a> with an alkaline metal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fluorine's name comes from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> word <i>fluere</i>, meaning "to flow", because it was derived from the mineral <a href="/wiki/Fluorite" title="Fluorite">fluorospar</a>, which was used as a <a href="/wiki/Flux_(metallurgy)" title="Flux (metallurgy)">flux</a> in metal working. Chlorine's name comes from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> word <i>chloros</i>, meaning "greenish-yellow". Bromine's name comes from the Greek word <i>bromos</i>, meaning "stench". Iodine's name comes from the Greek word <i>iodes</i>, meaning "violet". Astatine's name comes from the Greek word <i>astatos</i>, meaning "unstable".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature's_Building_Blocks_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-Nature's_Building_Blocks-4">[4]</a></sup> Tennessine is named after the US state of <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical">Chemical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Chemical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The halogens show trends in chemical bond energy moving from top to bottom of the periodic table column with fluorine deviating slightly.  (It follows trend in having the highest bond energy in compounds with other atoms, but it has very weak bonds within the diatomic F<sub>2</sub> molecule.) This means, as you go down the periodic table, the reactivity of the element will decrease because of the increasing size of the atoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>

<p>Halogens are highly <a href="/wiki/Reactivity_(chemistry)" title="Reactivity (chemistry)">reactive</a>, and as such can be harmful or lethal to <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">biological organisms</a> in sufficient quantities. This high reactivity is due to the high <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">electronegativity</a> of the atoms due to their high <a href="/wiki/Effective_nuclear_charge" title="Effective nuclear charge">effective nuclear charge</a>. Because the halogens have seven valence electrons in their outermost energy level, they can gain an electron by reacting with atoms of other elements to satisfy the <a href="/wiki/Octet_rule" title="Octet rule">octet rule</a>. <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">Fluorine</a> is one of the most reactive elements, attacking otherwise-inert materials such as glass, and forming compounds with the usually inert <a href="/wiki/Noble_gas" title="Noble gas">noble gases</a>. It is a <a href="/wiki/Corrosive_substance" title="Corrosive substance">corrosive</a> and highly toxic gas. The reactivity of fluorine is such that, if used or stored in laboratory glassware, it can react with glass in the presence of small amounts of water to form <a href="/wiki/Silicon_tetrafluoride" title="Silicon tetrafluoride">silicon tetrafluoride</a> (SiF<sub>4</sub>). Thus, fluorine must be handled with substances such as <a href="/wiki/Polytetrafluoroethylene" title="Polytetrafluoroethylene">Teflon</a> (which is itself an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organofluorine" title="Organofluorine">organofluorine</a> compound), extremely dry glass, or metals such as copper or steel, which form a protective layer of fluoride on their surface.
</p><p>The high reactivity of fluorine allows paradoxically some of the strongest bonds possible, especially to carbon. For example, Teflon is fluorine bonded with carbon and is extremely resistant to thermal and chemical attack and has a high melting point.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Molecules">Molecules</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Molecules">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Diatomic_halogen_molecules">Diatomic halogen molecules</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Diatomic halogen molecules">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>

Due to relatively weak intermolecular forces, chlorine and fluorine form part of the group known as "elemental gases".

<p>The elements become less reactive and have higher melting points as the atomic number increases. The higher melting points are caused by stronger <a href="/wiki/London_dispersion_force" title="London dispersion force">London dispersion forces</a> resulting from more electrons.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Compounds">Compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrogen_halides">Hydrogen halides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hydrogen halides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>All of the halogens have been observed to react with hydrogen to form <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_halide" title="Hydrogen halide">hydrogen halides</a>. For fluorine, chlorine, and bromine, this reaction is in the form of:
</p>
<dl><dd>H<sub>2</sub> + X<sub>2</sub> → 2HX</dd></dl>
<p>However, hydrogen iodide and hydrogen astatide can split back into their constituent elements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-assorted_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-assorted-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>The hydrogen-halogen reactions get gradually less reactive toward the heavier halogens. A fluorine-hydrogen reaction is explosive even when it is dark and cold. A chlorine-hydrogen reaction is also explosive, but only in the presence of light and heat. A bromine-hydrogen reaction is even less explosive; it is explosive only when exposed to flames. Iodine and astatine only partially react with hydrogen, forming <a href="/wiki/Chemical_equilibrium" title="Chemical equilibrium">equilibria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-assorted_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-assorted-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>All halogens form binary compounds with hydrogen known as the hydrogen halides: <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">hydrogen fluoride</a> (HF), <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride" title="Hydrogen chloride">hydrogen chloride</a> (HCl), <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bromide" title="Hydrogen bromide">hydrogen bromide</a> (HBr), <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_iodide" title="Hydrogen iodide">hydrogen iodide</a> (HI), and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_astatide" title="Hydrogen astatide">hydrogen astatide</a> (HAt). All of these compounds form acids when mixed with water. Hydrogen fluoride is the only hydrogen halide that forms <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bond" title="Hydrogen bond">hydrogen bonds</a>. Hydrochloric acid, hydrobromic acid, hydroiodic acid, and hydroastatic acid are all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strong_acid" title="Strong acid">strong acids</a>, but hydrofluoric acid is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weak_acid" title="Weak acid">weak acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>All of the hydrogen halides are <a href="/wiki/Irritation" title="Irritation">irritants</a>. Hydrogen fluoride and hydrogen chloride are highly <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acidic</a>. Hydrogen fluoride is used as an <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industrial</a> chemical, and is highly toxic, causing <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_edema" title="Pulmonary edema">pulmonary edema</a> and damaging cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Hydrogen chloride is also a dangerous chemical. Breathing in gas with more than fifty parts per million of hydrogen chloride can cause death in humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Hydrogen bromide is even more toxic and irritating than hydrogen chloride. Breathing in gas with more than thirty parts per million of hydrogen bromide can be lethal to humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Hydrogen iodide, like other hydrogen halides, is toxic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Metal_halides">Metal halides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Metal halides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Metal_halides" title="Metal halides">Metal halides</a></div>
<p>All the halogens are known to react with sodium to form <a href="/wiki/Sodium_fluoride" title="Sodium fluoride">sodium fluoride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_bromide" title="Sodium bromide">sodium bromide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_iodide" title="Sodium iodide">sodium iodide</a>, and sodium astatide. Heated sodium's reaction with halogens produces bright-orange flames. Sodium's reaction with chlorine is in the form of:
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="texhtml">2Na + Cl<sub>2</sub> → 2NaCl</span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-assorted_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-assorted-11">[11]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>Iron reacts with fluorine, chlorine, and bromine to form Iron(III) halides. These reactions are in the form of:
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="texhtml">2Fe + 3X<sub>2</sub> → 2FeX<sub>3</sub></span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-assorted_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-assorted-11">[11]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>However, when iron reacts with iodine, it forms only <a href="/wiki/Iron(II)_iodide" title="Iron(II) iodide">iron(II) iodide</a>.
</p><p>Iron wool can react rapidly with fluorine to form the white compound <a href="/wiki/Iron(III)_fluoride" title="Iron(III) fluoride">iron(III) fluoride</a> even in cold temperatures. When chlorine comes into contact with heated iron, they react to form the black <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron_(III)_chloride" title="Iron (III) chloride">iron (III) chloride</a>. However, if the reaction conditions are moist, this reaction will instead result in a reddish-brown product. Iron can also react with bromine to form <a href="/wiki/Iron(III)_bromide" title="Iron(III) bromide">iron(III) bromide</a>. This compound is reddish-brown in dry conditions. Iron's reaction with bromine is less reactive than its reaction with fluorine or chlorine. Hot iron can also react with iodine, but it forms iron(II) iodide. This compound may be gray, but the reaction is always contaminated with excess iodine, so it is not known for sure. Iron's reaction with iodine is less vigorous than its reaction with the lighter halogens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-assorted_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-assorted-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Interhalogen_compounds">Interhalogen compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Interhalogen compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Interhalogen" title="Interhalogen">Interhalogen</a></div>
<p>Interhalogen compounds are in the form of XY<sub>n</sub> where X and Y are halogens and n is one, three, five, or seven. Interhalogen compounds contain at most two different halogens. Large interhalogens, such as <span class="texhtml">ClF<sub>3</sub></span> can be produced by a reaction of a pure halogen with a smaller interhalogen such as <span class="texhtml">ClF</span>. All interhalogens except <a href="/wiki/Iodine_heptafluoride" title="Iodine heptafluoride"><span class="texhtml">IF<sub>7</sub></span></a> can be produced by directly combining pure halogens in various conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chemistry_of_Interhalogens_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chemistry_of_Interhalogens-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Interhalogens are typically more reactive than all diatomic halogen molecules except F<sub>2</sub> because interhalogen bonds are weaker. However, the chemical properties of interhalogens are still roughly the same as those of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diatomic" title="Diatomic">diatomic</a> halogens. Many interhalogens consist of one or more atoms of fluorine bonding to a heavier halogen. Chlorine can bond with up to 3 fluorine atoms, bromine can bond with up to five fluorine atoms, and iodine can bond with up to seven fluorine atoms. Most interhalogen compounds are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Covalent" title="Covalent">covalent</a> gases. However, there are some interhalogens that are liquids, such as BrF<sub>3</sub>, and many iodine-containing interhalogens are solids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chemistry_of_Interhalogens_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chemistry_of_Interhalogens-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Organohalogen_compounds">Organohalogen compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Organohalogen compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>

<p>Many synthetic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_compounds" title="Organic compounds">organic compounds</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastic</a> <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymers</a>, and a few natural ones, contain halogen atoms; these are known as <i>halogenated</i> compounds or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_halide" title="Organic halide">organic halides</a>. Chlorine is by far the most abundant of the halogens in seawater, and the only one needed in relatively large amounts (as chloride ions) by humans. For example, chloride ions play a key role in <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a> function by mediating the action of the inhibitory transmitter <a href="/wiki/Gamma-Aminobutyric_acid" title="Gamma-Aminobutyric acid">GABA</a> and are also used by the body to produce stomach acid. Iodine is needed in trace amounts for the production of <a href="/wiki/Thyroid" title="Thyroid">thyroid</a> hormones such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thyroxine" title="Thyroxine">thyroxine</a>. Organohalogens are also synthesized through the <a href="/wiki/Nucleophilic_abstraction" title="Nucleophilic abstraction">nucleophilic abstraction</a> reaction.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Polyhalogenated_compounds">Polyhalogenated compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Polyhalogenated compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p><a href="/wiki/Polyhalogenated_compound" title="Polyhalogenated compound">Polyhalogenated compounds</a> are industrially created compounds substituted with multiple halogens.  Many of them are very toxic and bioaccumulate in humans, and have a very wide application range.  They include <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_biphenyl" title="Polychlorinated biphenyl">PCBs</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PBDE" title="PBDE">PBDEs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Perfluorinated_compound" title="Perfluorinated compound">perfluorinated compounds</a> (PFCs), as well as numerous other compounds.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions_with_water">Reactions with water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Reactions with water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Fluorine reacts vigorously with water to produce <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> (O<sub>2</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">hydrogen fluoride</a> (HF):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="texhtml">2 F<sub>2</sub>(g) + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O(l) → O<sub>2</sub>(g) + 4 HF(aq)</span></dd></dl>
<p>Chlorine has maximum solubility of ca. 7.1 g Cl<sub>2</sub> per kg of water at ambient temperature (21 °C).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>  Dissolved chlorine reacts to form <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> (HCl) and <a href="/wiki/Hypochlorous_acid" title="Hypochlorous acid">hypochlorous acid</a>, a solution that can be used as a <a href="/wiki/Disinfectant" title="Disinfectant">disinfectant</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bleach" title="Bleach">bleach</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="texhtml">Cl<sub>2</sub>(g) + H<sub>2</sub>O(l) → HCl(aq) + HClO(aq)</span></dd></dl>
<p>Bromine has a solubility of 3.41 g per 100 g of water,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> but it slowly reacts to form <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bromide" title="Hydrogen bromide">hydrogen bromide</a> (HBr) and <a href="/wiki/Hypobromous_acid" title="Hypobromous acid">hypobromous acid</a> (HBrO):
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="texhtml">Br<sub>2</sub>(g) + H<sub>2</sub>O(l) → HBr(aq) + HBrO(aq)</span></dd></dl>
<p>Iodine, however, is minimally soluble in water (0.03 g/100 g water at 20 °C) and does not react with it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>  However, iodine will form an aqueous solution in the presence of iodide ion, such as by addition of <a href="/wiki/Potassium_iodide" title="Potassium iodide">potassium iodide</a> (KI), because the <a href="/wiki/Triiodide" title="Triiodide">triiodide</a> ion is formed.
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Fluorine has one stable and naturally occurring <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_fluorine" title="Isotopes of fluorine">isotope</a>, fluorine-19. However, there are trace amounts in nature of the radioactive isotope fluorine-23, which occurs via <a href="/wiki/Cluster_decay" title="Cluster decay">cluster decay</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protactinium-231" title="Protactinium-231">protactinium-231</a>. A total of eighteen isotopes of fluorine have been discovered, with atomic masses ranging from 14 to 31. Chlorine has two stable and naturally occurring <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_chlorine" title="Isotopes of chlorine">isotopes</a>, chlorine-35 and chlorine-37. However, there are trace amounts in nature of the isotope <a href="/wiki/Chlorine-36" title="Chlorine-36">chlorine-36</a>, which occurs via <a href="/wiki/Spallation" title="Spallation">spallation</a> of argon-36. A total of 24 isotopes of chlorine have been discovered, with atomic masses ranging from 28 to 51.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature's_Building_Blocks_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-Nature's_Building_Blocks-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are two stable and naturally occurring <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_bromine" title="Isotopes of bromine">isotopes of bromine</a>, bromine-79 and bromine-81. A total of 32 isotopes of bromine have been discovered, with atomic masses ranging 67 to 98. There is one stable and naturally occurring <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_iodine" title="Isotopes of iodine">isotope of iodine</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iodine-127" title="Iodine-127">iodine-127</a>. However, there are trace amounts in nature of the radioactive isotope <a href="/wiki/Iodine-129" title="Iodine-129">iodine-129</a>, which occurs via spallation and from the radioactive decay of uranium in ores. Several other radioactive isotopes of iodine have also been created naturally via the decay of uranium. A total of 38 isotopes of iodine have been discovered, with atomic masses ranging from 108 to 145.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature's_Building_Blocks_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-Nature's_Building_Blocks-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are no stable <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_astatine" title="Isotopes of astatine">isotopes of astatine</a>. However, there are three naturally occurring radioactive isotopes of astatine produced via radioactive decay of <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neptunium" title="Neptunium">neptunium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a>. These isotopes are astatine-215, astatine-217, and astatine-219. A total of 31 isotopes of astatine have been discovered, with atomic masses ranging from 193 to 223.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature's_Building_Blocks_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-Nature's_Building_Blocks-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Approximately six million metric tons of the fluorine mineral <a href="/wiki/Fluorite" title="Fluorite">fluorite</a> are produced each year. Four hundred-thousand metric tons of hydrofluoric acid are made each year. Fluorine gas is made from hydrofluoric acid produced as a by-product in <a href="/wiki/Phosphoric_acid" title="Phosphoric acid">phosphoric acid</a> manufacture. Approximately 15,000 metric tons of fluorine gas are made per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature's_Building_Blocks_4-9"><a href="#cite_note-Nature's_Building_Blocks-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The mineral <a href="/wiki/Halite" title="Halite">halite</a> is the mineral that is most commonly mined for chlorine, but the minerals <a href="/wiki/Carnallite" title="Carnallite">carnallite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sylvite" title="Sylvite">sylvite</a> are also mined for chlorine. Forty million metric tons of chlorine are produced each year by the <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Brine" title="Brine">brine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature's_Building_Blocks_4-10"><a href="#cite_note-Nature's_Building_Blocks-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Approximately 450,000 metric tons of bromine are produced each year. Fifty percent of all bromine produced is produced in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, 35% in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, and most of the remainder in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. Historically, bromine was produced by adding <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> and bleaching powder to natural brine. However, in modern times, bromine is produced by electrolysis, a method invented by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbert_Dow" title="Herbert Dow">Herbert Dow</a>. It is also possible to produce bromine by passing chlorine through seawater and then passing air through the seawater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature's_Building_Blocks_4-11"><a href="#cite_note-Nature's_Building_Blocks-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2003, 22,000 metric tons of iodine were produced. Chile produces 40% of all iodine produced, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> produces 30%, and smaller amounts are produced in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and the United States. Until the 1950s, iodine was extracted from <a href="/wiki/Kelp" title="Kelp">kelp</a>. However, in modern times, iodine is produced in other ways. One way that iodine is produced is by mixing <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> with <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrate</a> ores, which contain some <a href="/wiki/Iodate" title="Iodate">iodates</a>. Iodine is also extracted from <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> fields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature's_Building_Blocks_4-12"><a href="#cite_note-Nature's_Building_Blocks-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Even though astatine is naturally occurring, it is usually produced by bombarding bismuth with alpha particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature's_Building_Blocks_4-13"><a href="#cite_note-Nature's_Building_Blocks-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

Chlorine is a gas, bromine is a liquid, and iodine is a solid. <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">Fluorine</a> could not be included in the image due to its high <a href="/wiki/Reactivity_(chemistry)" title="Reactivity (chemistry)">reactivity</a>, and astatine due to its radioactivity.</div></div></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Both chlorine and bromine are used as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disinfectants" title="Disinfectants">disinfectants</a> for drinking water, swimming pools, fresh wounds, spas, dishes, and surfaces. They kill <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> and other potentially harmful <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a> through a process known as <a href="/wiki/Sterilization_(microbiology)" title="Sterilization (microbiology)">sterilization</a>. Their reactivity is also put to use in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bleaching_agent" title="Bleaching agent">bleaching</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hypochlorite" title="Sodium hypochlorite">Sodium hypochlorite</a>, which is produced from chlorine, is the active ingredient of most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cloth" title="Cloth">fabric</a> bleaches, and chlorine-derived bleaches are used in the production of some <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a> products. Chlorine also reacts with sodium to create <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a>, which is table salt.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Halogen_lamp" title="Halogen lamp">Halogen lamps</a> are a type of incandescent lamp using a <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a> filament in bulbs that have a small amounts of a halogen, such as iodine or bromine added. This enables the production of lamps that are much smaller than non-halogen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incandescent_lightbulb" title="Incandescent lightbulb">incandescent lightbulbs</a> at the same <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">wattage</a>. The gas reduces the thinning of the filament and blackening of the inside of the bulb resulting in a bulb that has a much greater life. Halogen lamps glow at a higher temperature (2800 to 3400 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">kelvins</a>) with a whiter color than other incandescent bulbs. However, this requires bulbs to be manufactured from <a href="/wiki/Fused_quartz" title="Fused quartz">fused quartz</a> rather than silica glass to reduce breakage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edison_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Edison-30">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Drug_discovery" title="Drug discovery">drug discovery</a>, the incorporation of halogen atoms into a lead drug candidate results in analogues that are usually more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lipophilic" title="Lipophilic">lipophilic</a> and less water-soluble.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[28]</a></sup> As a consequence, halogen atoms are used to improve penetration through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lipid_membrane" title="Lipid membrane">lipid membranes</a> and tissues. It follows that there is a tendency for some halogenated drugs to accumulate in <a href="/wiki/Adipose_tissue" title="Adipose tissue">adipose tissue</a>.
</p><p>The chemical reactivity of halogen atoms depends on both their point of attachment to the lead and the nature of the halogen. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aromatic" title="Aromatic">Aromatic</a> halogen groups are far less reactive than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aliphatic" title="Aliphatic">aliphatic</a> halogen groups, which can exhibit considerable chemical reactivity. For aliphatic carbon-halogen bonds, the C-F bond is the strongest and usually less chemically reactive than aliphatic C-H bonds. The other aliphatic-halogen bonds are weaker, their reactivity increasing down the periodic table. They are usually more chemically reactive than aliphatic C-H bonds. As a consequence, the most common halogen substitutions are the less reactive aromatic fluorine and chlorine groups.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_role">Biological role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Biological role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fluoride anions are found in ivory, bones, teeth, blood, eggs, urine, and hair of organisms. Fluoride anions in very small amounts may be essential for humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-who.int_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-who.int-32">[29]</a></sup> There are 0.5 milligrams of fluorine per liter of human blood. Human bones contain 0.2 to 1.2% fluorine. Human tissue contains approximately 50 parts per billion of fluorine. A typical 70-kilogram human contains 3 to 6 grams of fluorine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature's_Building_Blocks_4-14"><a href="#cite_note-Nature's_Building_Blocks-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Chloride anions are essential to a large number of species, humans included. The concentration of chlorine in the <a href="/wiki/Dry_weight" title="Dry weight">dry weight</a> of cereals is 10 to 20 parts per million, while in <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">potatoes</a> the concentration of chloride is 0.5%. Plant growth is adversely affected by chloride levels in the <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> falling below 2 parts per million. Human blood contains an average of 0.3% chlorine. Human bone typically contains 900 parts per million of chlorine. Human tissue contains approximately 0.2 to 0.5% chlorine. There is a total of 95 grams of chlorine in a typical 70-kilogram human.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature's_Building_Blocks_4-15"><a href="#cite_note-Nature's_Building_Blocks-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some bromine in the form of the bromide anion is present in all organisms. A biological role for bromine in humans has not been proven, but some organisms contain <a href="/wiki/Organobromine_compound" title="Organobromine compound">organobromine compounds</a>. Humans typically consume 1 to 20 milligrams of bromine per day. There are typically 5 parts per million of bromine in human blood, 7 parts per million of bromine in human bones, and 7 parts per million of bromine in human tissue. A typical 70-kilogram human contains 260 milligrams of bromine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature's_Building_Blocks_4-16"><a href="#cite_note-Nature's_Building_Blocks-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Humans typically consume less than 100 micrograms of iodine per day. Iodine deficiency can cause <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_disability" title="Intellectual disability">intellectual disability</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organoiodine_chemistry" title="Organoiodine chemistry">Organoiodine compounds</a> occur in humans in some of the <a href="/wiki/Gland" title="Gland">glands</a>, especially the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thyroid_gland" title="Thyroid gland">thyroid gland</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomach</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epidermis_(skin)" title="Epidermis (skin)">epidermis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune system</a>. Foods containing iodine include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cod_(food)" title="Cod (food)">cod</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oyster" title="Oyster">oysters</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shrimp_(food)" title="Shrimp (food)">shrimp</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herring_(food)" title="Herring (food)">herring</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lobster" title="Lobster">lobsters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sunflower_seed" title="Sunflower seed">sunflower seeds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seaweed" title="Seaweed">seaweed</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Edible_mushroom" title="Edible mushroom">mushrooms</a>. However, iodine is not known to have a biological role in plants. There are typically 0.06 milligrams per liter of iodine in human blood, 300 parts per billion of iodine in human bones, and 50 to 700 parts per billion of iodine in human tissue. There are 10 to 20 milligrams of iodine in a typical 70-kilogram human.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature's_Building_Blocks_4-17"><a href="#cite_note-Nature's_Building_Blocks-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Astatine" title="Astatine">Astatine</a> has no biological role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature's_Building_Blocks_4-18"><a href="#cite_note-Nature's_Building_Blocks-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicity">Toxicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Toxicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The halogens tend to decrease in toxicity towards the heavier halogens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Elements_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Elements-33">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fluorine gas is extremely toxic; breathing in fluorine at a concentration of 25 parts per million is potentially lethal.  Hydrofluoric acid is also toxic, being able to penetrate skin and cause highly painful <a href="/wiki/Chemical_burn" title="Chemical burn">burns</a>. In addition, fluoride anions are toxic, but not as toxic as pure fluorine. Fluoride can be lethal in amounts of 5 to 10 grams. Prolonged consumption of fluoride above concentrations of 1.5 mg/L is associated with a risk of <a href="/wiki/Dental_fluorosis" title="Dental fluorosis">dental fluorosis</a>, an aesthetic condition of the teeth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[31]</a></sup> At concentrations above 4 mg/L, there is an increased risk of developing <a href="/wiki/Skeletal_fluorosis" title="Skeletal fluorosis">skeletal fluorosis</a>, a condition in which bone fractures become more common due to the hardening of bones. Current recommended levels in <a href="/wiki/Water_fluoridation" title="Water fluoridation">water fluoridation</a>, a way to prevent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dental_caries" title="Dental caries">dental caries</a>, range from 0.7 to 1.2 mg/L to avoid the detrimental effects of fluoride while at the same time reaping the benefits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[32]</a></sup> People with levels between normal levels and those required for skeletal fluorosis tend to have symptoms similar to <a href="/wiki/Arthritis" title="Arthritis">arthritis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature's_Building_Blocks_4-19"><a href="#cite_note-Nature's_Building_Blocks-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Chlorine gas is highly toxic. Breathing in chlorine at a concentration of 3 parts per million can rapidly cause a toxic reaction. Breathing in chlorine at a concentration of 50 parts per million is highly dangerous. Breathing in chlorine at a concentration of 500 parts per million for a few minutes is lethal. Breathing in chlorine gas is highly painful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Elements_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Elements-33">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Pure bromine is somewhat toxic, but less toxic than fluorine and chlorine. One hundred milligrams of bromine is lethal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature's_Building_Blocks_4-20"><a href="#cite_note-Nature's_Building_Blocks-4">[4]</a></sup> Bromide anions are also toxic, but less so than bromine. Bromide has a lethal dose of 30 grams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature's_Building_Blocks_4-21"><a href="#cite_note-Nature's_Building_Blocks-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Iodine is somewhat toxic, being able to irritate the lungs and eyes, with a safety limit of 1 milligram per cubic meter. When taken orally, 3 grams of iodine can be lethal. Iodide anions are mostly nontoxic, but these can also be deadly if ingested in large amounts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature's_Building_Blocks_4-22"><a href="#cite_note-Nature's_Building_Blocks-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Astatine is very <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> and thus highly dangerous, but it has not been produced in macroscopic quantities and hence it is most unlikely that its toxicity will be of much relevance to the average individual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature's_Building_Blocks_4-23"><a href="#cite_note-Nature's_Building_Blocks-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Superhalogen">Superhalogen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Superhalogen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Superatom" title="Superatom">Superatom</a></div>
<p>Certain aluminium clusters have superatom properties. These aluminium clusters are generated as anions (<span class="texhtml">Al<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"><i>n</i></sub></span></span> with <i>n</i> = 1, 2, 3, ... ) in helium gas and reacted with a gas containing iodine. When analyzed by mass spectrometry one main reaction product turns out to be <span class="texhtml"><span class="chemf nowrap">Al<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">13</sub></span>I<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bergeron/2004_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-bergeron/2004-36">[33]</a></sup> These clusters of 13 aluminium atoms with an extra electron added do not appear to react with oxygen when it is introduced in the same gas stream. Assuming each atom liberates its 3 valence electrons, this means 40 electrons are present, which is one of the magic numbers for sodium and implies that these numbers are a reflection of the noble gases.
</p><p>Calculations show that the additional electron is located in the aluminium cluster at the location directly opposite from the iodine atom. The cluster must therefore have a higher electron affinity for the electron than iodine and therefore the aluminium cluster is called a superhalogen (i.e., the vertical electron detachment energies of the moieties that make up the negative ions are larger than those of any halogen atom).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[34]</a></sup> The cluster component in the <span class="texhtml"><span class="chemf nowrap">Al<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">13</sub></span>I<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span></span> ion is similar to an iodide ion or a bromide ion. The related <span class="texhtml"><span class="chemf nowrap">Al<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">13</sub></span>I<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span></span> cluster is expected to behave chemically like the <a href="/wiki/Triiodide" title="Triiodide">triiodide</a> ion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ball_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-ball-38">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bergeron/2005_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-bergeron/2005-39">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
