<p><b>Discourse</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>discursus</i>, "running to and from") denotes written and spoken <a href="/wiki/Communication" title="Communication">communications</a>:
</p>
<ul><li>In <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">semantics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Discourse_analysis" title="Discourse analysis">discourse analysis</a>: Discourse is a conceptual generalization of <a href="/wiki/Conversation" title="Conversation">conversation</a> within each modality and context of communication.</li>
<li>The totality of codified language (vocabulary) used in a given field of intellectual enquiry and of social practice, such as legal discourse, medical discourse, religious discourse, et cetera.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>In the work of <a href="/wiki/Michel_Foucault" title="Michel Foucault">Michel Foucault</a>, and that of the social theoreticians he inspired: <i>discourse</i> describes "an entity of sequences, of signs, in that they are enouncements (<i>énoncés</i>)", statements in conversation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foucault1969_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Foucault1969-2">[2]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>As discourse, an "enouncement" (statement) is not a unit of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semiotic" title="Semiotic">semiotic</a> signs, but an abstract construct that allows the semiotic signs to assign meaning, and so communicate specific, repeatable communications to, between, and among objects, subjects, and statements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foucault1969_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Foucault1969-2">[2]</a></sup> Therefore, a discourse is composed of semiotic sequences (relations among signs that communicate meaning) between and among objects, subjects, and statements.
</p><p>The term "discursive formation" (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">formation discursive</i>) conceptually describes the regular communications (written and spoken) that produce such discourses, such as informal conversations. As a philosopher, Michel Foucault applied the discursive formation in the analyses of large bodies of knowledge, such as <a href="/wiki/Political_economy" title="Political economy">political economy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_history" title="Natural history">natural history</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foucault1970_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Foucault1970-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the first sense-usage (semantics and discourse analysis), the term <i>discourse</i> is studied in <a href="/wiki/Corpus_linguistics" title="Corpus linguistics">corpus linguistics</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Study_of_language" title="Study of language">study of language</a> expressed in <i><a href="/wiki/Text_corpus" title="Text corpus">corpora</a></i> (samples) of "real world" text. In the second sense (the codified language of a field of enquiry) and in the third sense (a statement, <i>un énoncé</i>), the analysis of a <i>discourse</i> examines and determines the connections among <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a> and <a href="/wiki/Structure_and_agency" title="Structure and agency">structure and agency</a>.
</p><p>Moreover, because a discourse is a body of text meant to communicate specific data, information, and knowledge, there exist internal relations in the content of a given discourse; likewise, there exist external relations among discourses. As such, a discourse does not exist <i>per se</i> (in itself), but is related to other discourses, by way of inter-discursivity; therefore, in the course of <a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">intellectual enquiry</a>, the discourse among researchers features the questions and answers of <i>What is ...?</i> and <i>What is not. ...</i>, conducted according to the meanings (denotation and connotation) of the concepts (statements) used in the given field of enquiry, such as <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethnography" title="Ethnography">ethnography</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>; <a href="/wiki/Cultural_studies" title="Cultural studies">cultural studies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Literary_theory" title="Literary theory">literary theory</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_science" title="Philosophy of science">philosophy of science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminism</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_humanities">The humanities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discourse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The humanities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Humanities" title="Humanities">humanities</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social sciences</a>, the term <i>discourse</i> describes a formal way of thinking that can be expressed through language; the discourse is a social boundary that defines what statements can be said about a topic.
</p><p>Discourse can affect the person's perspective; it is impossible to avoid discourse for any subject. For example, two notably distinct discourses can be used about various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guerrilla" title="Guerrilla">guerrilla</a> movements describing them either as "<a href="/wiki/Resistance_movement" title="Resistance movement">freedom fighters</a>" or "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terrorists" title="Terrorists">terrorists</a>". In other words, the chosen discourse provides the vocabulary, expressions and perhaps also the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stylistics_(linguistics)" title="Stylistics (linguistics)">style</a> needed to communicate.
</p><p>Discourses are embedded in different rhetorical genres and metagenres that constrain and enable them. That is language talking about language, for instance the <a href="/wiki/American_Psychiatric_Association" title="American Psychiatric Association">American Psychiatric Association</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DSMIV" title="DSMIV">DSMIV</a> manual tells which terms have to be used in talking about mental health, thereby mediating meanings and dictating practices of the professionals of psychology and psychiatry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Discourse is closely linked to different theories of <a href="/wiki/Power_(social_and_political)" title="Power (social and political)">power</a> and <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a>, at least as long as defining discourses is seen to mean defining reality itself. This conception of discourse is largely derived from the work of French philosopher Michel Foucault.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formal_semantics">Formal semantics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discourse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formal semantics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Discourse_representation_theory" title="Discourse representation theory">discourse representation theory</a> describes the <a href="/wiki/Formal_semantics_(linguistics)" title="Formal semantics (linguistics)">formal semantics</a> of a sentence using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Predicate_logic" title="Predicate logic">predicate logic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modernism">Modernism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discourse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modernism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Modern theorists were focused on achieving progress and believed in the existence of natural and social laws which could be used universally to develop knowledge and thus a better understanding of society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Larrain,_1994_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Larrain,_1994-7">[7]</a></sup> Modernist theorists were preoccupied with obtaining the truth and reality and sought to develop theories which contained certainty and predictability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Best_&amp;_Kellner,_1991_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Best_&amp;_Kellner,_1991-8">[8]</a></sup> Modernist theorists therefore viewed discourse as being relative to talking or way of talking and understood discourse to be functional.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strega,_2005_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Strega,_2005-9">[9]</a></sup> Discourse and language transformations are ascribed to progress or the need to develop new or more "accurate" words to describe new discoveries, understandings, or areas of interest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strega,_2005_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Strega,_2005-9">[9]</a></sup> In modern times, language and discourse are dissociated from power and ideology and instead conceptualized as "natural" products of common sense usage or progress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strega,_2005_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Strega,_2005-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Modernism" title="Modernism">Modernism</a> further gave rise to the liberal discourses of rights, equality, freedom, and justice; however, this rhetoric masked substantive inequality and failed to account for differences, according to Regnier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structuralism">Structuralism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discourse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Structuralism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Structuralism" title="Structuralism">Structuralist</a> theorists, such as <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_de_Saussure" title="Ferdinand de Saussure">Ferdinand de Saussure</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Lacan" title="Jacques Lacan">Jacques Lacan</a>, argue that all human actions and social formations are related to <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a> and can be understood as systems of related elements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howarth,_2000_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Howarth,_2000-11">[11]</a></sup> This means that the "…individual elements of a system only have significance when considered in relation to the structure as a whole, and that structures are to be understood as self-contained, self-regulated, and self-transforming entities." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In other words, it is the structure itself that determines the significance, meaning and function of the individual elements of a system. Structuralism has made an important contribution to our understanding of language and social systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Course_in_General_Linguistics" title="Course in General Linguistics">Saussure's theory of language</a> highlights the decisive role of meaning and signification in structuring human life more generally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Howarth,_2000_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Howarth,_2000-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Postmodernism">Postmodernism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Discourse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Postmodernism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following the perceived limitations of the modern era, emerged <a href="/wiki/Postmodernism" title="Postmodernism">postmodern</a> theory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Larrain,_1994_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Larrain,_1994-7">[7]</a></sup> Postmodern theorists rejected modernist claims that there was one theoretical approach that explained all aspects of society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Best_&amp;_Kellner,_1991_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Best_&amp;_Kellner,_1991-8">[8]</a></sup> Rather, postmodernist theorists were interested in examining the variety of experience of individuals and groups and emphasized differences over similarities and common experiences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strega,_2005_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Strega,_2005-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>In contrast to modern theory, postmodern theory is more fluid and allows for individual differences as it rejected the notion of social laws. Postmodern theorists shifted away from truth seeking and instead sought answers for how truths are produced and sustained. Postmodernists contended that truth and knowledge is plural, contextual, and historically produced through discourses. Postmodern researchers therefore embarked on analyzing discourses such as texts, language, policies and practices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strega,_2005_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-Strega,_2005-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>French social theorist <a href="/wiki/Michel_Foucault" title="Michel Foucault">Michel Foucault</a> developed a notion of discourse in his early work, especially the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaeology_of_knowledge" title="Archaeology of knowledge">Archaeology of knowledge</a></i> (1972). In <i>Discursive Struggles Within Social Welfare: Restaging Teen Motherhood</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Iara Lessa summarizes Foucault's definition of discourse as "systems of thoughts composed of ideas, attitudes, courses of action, beliefs and practices that systematically construct the subjects and the worlds of which they speak." Foucault traces the role of discourses in wider social processes of legitimating and power, emphasizing the construction of current truths, how they are maintained and what power relations they carry with them. Foucault later theorized that discourse is a medium through which power relations produce speaking subjects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strega,_2005_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-Strega,_2005-9">[9]</a></sup> Foucault (1977, 1980) argued that power and knowledge are inter-related and therefore every human relationship is a struggle and negotiation of power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Foucault further stated that power is always present and can both produce and constrain the truth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strega,_2005_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-Strega,_2005-9">[9]</a></sup> Discourse according to Foucault (1977, 1980, 2003) is related to power as it operates by rules of exclusion. Discourse therefore is controlled by objects, what can be spoken of; ritual, where and how one may speak; and the privileged, who may speak.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Coining the phrases <a href="/wiki/Power-knowledge" title="Power-knowledge">power-knowledge</a> Foucault (1980) stated knowledge was both the creator of power and creation of power.  An object becomes a "node within a network."  In his work, <i>The Archaeology of Knowledge,</i> Foucault uses the example of a book to illustrate a node within a network.  A book is not made up of individual words on a page, each of which has meaning, but rather "is caught up in a system of references to other books, other texts, other sentences."  The meaning of that book is connected to a larger, overarching web of knowledge and ideas to which it relates.
</p><p>One of the key discourses that Foucault identified as part of his critique of power-knowledge was that of <a href="/wiki/Neoliberalism" title="Neoliberalism">neoliberalism</a>, which he related very closely to his conceptualization of <a href="/wiki/Governmentality" title="Governmentality">governmentality</a> in his lectures on biopolitics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> This trajectory of Foucault's thinking has been taken up widely within <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_Geography" title="Human Geography">Human Geography</a>.
</p>
