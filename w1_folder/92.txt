<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Hypermobility (travel)</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Hypermobile</b> travelers are "highly mobile individuals" who take "frequent trips, often over great distances." They "account for a large share of the overall kilometres travelled, especially by air."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gossling_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-gossling-1">[1]</a></sup> These people contribute significantly to the overall amount of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airmiles" title="Airmiles">airmiles</a> flown within a given society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  Although concerns over hypermobility apply to several modes of transport, the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_aviation" title="Environmental impact of aviation">environmental impact of aviation</a> and especially its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emissions" title="Greenhouse gas emissions">greenhouse gas emissions</a> have brought particular focus on flying.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Among the reasons for this focus is that these emissions, because they are made at high altitude, have a climate impact that is commonly estimated to be 2.7 higher than the same emissions if made at ground-level.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the amount of time people have spent in motion has remained constant since 1950, the shift from feet and bicycles to cars and planes has increased the speed of travel fivefold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  This results in the twin effects of wider and shallower regions of social activity around each person (further exacerbated by electronic communication which can be seen as a form of virtual mobility), and a degradation of the social and physical environment brought about by the high speed traffic (as theorised by urban designer <a href="/wiki/Donald_Appleyard" title="Donald Appleyard">Donald Appleyard</a>).
</p><p>The changes are brought about locally due to the use of cars and motorways, and internationally by aeroplanes.  Some of the social threats of hypermobility include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polarisation_between_rich_and_poor" title="Polarisation between rich and poor">polarisation between rich and poor</a></li>
<li>reduced health and fitness</li></ul>
<p>The addictive properties of hypermobile travel have been noted by researchers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Higham_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Higham-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Widespread <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> use is seen as a contributory factor towards hypermobility due to the increased ease which it enables travel to be desired and organized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> To the extent that the Internet perversely stimulates travel, it represents a lost opportunity to reduce overall emissions because online communication is a straightforward <a href="/wiki/Substitute_good" title="Substitute good">substitute</a> for physical travel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monbiot_2012_09_28_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-monbiot_2012_09_28-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>The term <b>hypermobility</b> arose around 1980 concerning the flow of capital,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and since the early 1990s has also referred to excessive travel. [<i>See:</i> Hepworth and Ducatel (1992);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Whitelegg (1993);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Lowe (1994);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> van der Stoep (1995);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Shields (1996);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Cox (1997);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Adams (1999);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-adams-22">[22]</a></sup> Khisty and Zeitler (2001);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Stefan_G%C3%B6ssling" title="Stefan Gössling">Gössling</a> et al. (2009);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gossling_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-gossling-1">[1]</a></sup> Mander &amp; Randles (2009);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and (Higham 2014).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Higham_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Higham-8">[8]</a></sup>] The term is widely credited as having been coined by Adams (1999), but apart from the title of the work it says nothing explicit about it except that "[t]he term hypermobility is used in this essay to suggest that it may be possible to have too much of a good thing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gossling_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-gossling-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-adams-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
