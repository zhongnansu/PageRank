<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Forestry</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Forestry</b> is the science and craft of creating, managing, using, conserving, and repairing <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a>, <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodlands</a>, and associated resources to meet desired goals, needs, and values for human and environment benefits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dictionaryofforestry_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-dictionaryofforestry-1">[1]</a></sup> Forestry is practiced in <a href="/wiki/Plantation" title="Plantation">plantations</a> and natural <a href="/wiki/Stand_level_modelling" title="Stand level modelling">stands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The science of forestry has elements that belong to the biological, physical, social, political and managerial sciences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Modern forestry generally embraces a broad range of concerns, in what is known as multiple-use management, including the provision of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timber" title="Timber">timber</a>, fuel wood, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wildlife_habitat" title="Wildlife habitat">wildlife habitat</a>, natural <a href="/wiki/Water_resources" title="Water resources">water quality management</a>, <a href="/wiki/Recreation" title="Recreation">recreation</a>, landscape and community protection, employment, aesthetically appealing <a href="/wiki/Landscape" title="Landscape">landscapes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> management, <a href="/wiki/Watershed_management" title="Watershed management">watershed management</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erosion_control" title="Erosion control">erosion control</a>, and preserving forests as '<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_sink" title="Carbon dioxide sink">sinks</a>' for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmospheric</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>.  A practitioner of forestry is known as a <a href="/wiki/Forester" title="Forester">forester</a>. Other common terms are: a <a href="/wiki/Verderer" title="Verderer">verderer</a>, or a silviculturalist. <a href="/wiki/Silviculture" title="Silviculture">Silviculture</a> is narrower than forestry, being concerned only with forest plants, but is often used synonymously with forestry.
</p><p>Forest ecosystems have come to be seen as the most important component of the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and forestry has emerged as a vital <a href="/wiki/Applied_science" title="Applied science">applied science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Craft" title="Craft">craft</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a>.
</p><p>Forestry is an important economic segment in various industrial countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> For example, in Germany, forests cover nearly a third of the land area,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bwi2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-bwi2-6">[6]</a></sup> wood is the most important <a href="/wiki/Renewable_resource" title="Renewable resource">renewable resource</a>, and forestry supports more than a million jobs and about €181 billion of value to the German economy each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forestry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forestry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Preindustrial_age&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Preindustrial age (page does not exist)">preindustrial age</a> has been dubbed by <a href="/wiki/Werner_Sombart" title="Werner Sombart">Werner Sombart</a> and others as the 'wooden age', as timber and firewood were the basic resources for energy, construction and housing. The development of modern forestry is closely connected with the rise of <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a>, economy as a science and varying notions of land use and property.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Roman <a href="/wiki/Latifundium" title="Latifundium">Latifundiae</a>, large agricultural estates, were quite successful in maintaining the large supply of wood that was necessary for the Roman Empire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yale_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-yale-9">[9]</a></sup> Large <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestations</a> came with respectively after the decline of the Romans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yale_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-yale-9">[9]</a></sup> However  already in the 5th century, <a href="/wiki/Monk" title="Monk">monks</a> in the then Byzantine <a href="/wiki/Romagna" title="Romagna">Romagna</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adriatic" title="Adriatic">Adriatic</a> coast, were able to establish <a href="/wiki/Stone_pine" title="Stone pine">stone pine</a> plantations to provide <a href="/wiki/Firewood" title="Firewood">fuelwood</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pine_nut" title="Pine nut">food</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pines_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pines-10">[10]</a></sup> This was the beginning of the massive forest mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante Alighieri</a> in his 1308 poem <a href="/wiki/Divine_Comedy" title="Divine Comedy">Divine Comedy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pines_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pines-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Similar sustainable formal forestry practices were developed by the <a href="/wiki/Visigoths" title="Visigoths">Visigoths</a> in the 7th century when, faced with the ever-increasing shortage of wood, they instituted a code concerned with the preservation of <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pine</a> forests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pines_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Pines-10">[10]</a></sup> The use and management of many forest resources has a long history in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> as well, dating back to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_Dynasty" title="Han Dynasty">Han Dynasty</a> and taking place under the landowning <a href="/wiki/Gentry" title="Gentry">gentry</a>. A similar approach was used in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. It was also later written about by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" title="Ming Dynasty">Ming Dynasty</a> Chinese scholar <a href="/wiki/Xu_Guangqi" title="Xu Guangqi">Xu Guangqi</a> (1562–1633).
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, land usage rights in medieval and early modern times allowed different users to access forests and pastures. <a href="/wiki/Plant_litter" title="Plant litter">Plant litter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Resin_extraction" title="Resin extraction">resin extraction</a> were important, as <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(resin)" title="Pitch (resin)">pitch (resin)</a> was essential for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caulking" title="Caulking">caulking</a> of ships, falking and hunting rights, firewood and building, timber gathering in <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wood_pastures&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wood pastures (page does not exist)">wood pastures</a>, and for grazing animals in forests. The notion of "<a href="/wiki/Commons" title="Commons">commons</a>" (German "Allmende") refers to the underlying traditional legal term of <a href="/wiki/Common_land" title="Common land">common land</a>. The idea of enclosed private property came about during modern times. However, most hunting rights were retained by members of the nobility which preserved the right of the nobility to access and use common land for recreation, like <a href="/wiki/Fox_hunting" title="Fox hunting">fox hunting</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_modern_forestry_development">Early modern forestry development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forestry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early modern forestry development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TJ_harvesteri.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/TJ_harvesteri.jpg/220px-TJ_harvesteri.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/TJ_harvesteri.jpg/330px-TJ_harvesteri.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/TJ_harvesteri.jpg/440px-TJ_harvesteri.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TJ_harvesteri.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Logging" title="Logging">Timber harvesting</a> in <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gewinnung_von_Schnittreisig.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3190" data-file-width="6000" height="117" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Gewinnung_von_Schnittreisig.jpg/220px-Gewinnung_von_Schnittreisig.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Gewinnung_von_Schnittreisig.jpg/330px-Gewinnung_von_Schnittreisig.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Gewinnung_von_Schnittreisig.jpg/440px-Gewinnung_von_Schnittreisig.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gewinnung_von_Schnittreisig.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Exploitation of brushwood at the <a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goldner_Steinr%C3%BCck" title="de:Goldner Steinrück">Golden Steinrueck</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vogelsberg_Mountains" title="Vogelsberg Mountains">Vogelsberg</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hans_Carl_von_Carlowitz.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="730" height="238" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Hans_Carl_von_Carlowitz.jpg/170px-Hans_Carl_von_Carlowitz.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Hans_Carl_von_Carlowitz.jpg/255px-Hans_Carl_von_Carlowitz.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Hans_Carl_von_Carlowitz.jpg/340px-Hans_Carl_von_Carlowitz.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hans_Carl_von_Carlowitz.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Hans_Carl_von_Carlowitz" title="Hans Carl von Carlowitz">Hans Carl von Carlowitz</a>, German miner</div></div></div>
<p>Systematic management of forests for a sustainable yield of timber began in Portugal in the 13th century when <a href="/wiki/Afonso_III_of_Portugal" title="Afonso III of Portugal">Afonso III of Portugal</a> planted the Pinhal do Rei near <a href="/wiki/Leiria" title="Leiria">Leiria</a> to prevent coastal erosion and soil degradation, and as a sustainable source for timber used in naval construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Livermore2004_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Livermore2004-11">[11]</a></sup> His successor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denis,_King_of_Portugal" title="Denis, King of Portugal">Dom Dinis</a> continued the practice and the forest exists still today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Forest management also flourished in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_forest_in_Central_Europe" title="History of the forest in Central Europe">German</a> states in the 14th century, e.g. in <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg" title="Nuremberg">Nuremberg</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and in 16th-century <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Typically, a forest was divided into specific sections and mapped; the harvest of timber was planned with an eye to regeneration. As <a href="/wiki/Timber_rafting" title="Timber rafting">timber rafting</a> allowed for connecting large continental forests, as in south western Germany, via Main, Neckar, Danube and Rhine with the coastal cities and states, early modern forestry and remote trading were closely connected. Large firs in the black forest were called „Holländer“, as they were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timber_trade" title="Timber trade">traded</a> to the Dutch ship yards. Large timber rafts on the Rhine were 200 to 400m in length, 40m in width and consisted of several thousand logs. The crew consisted of 400 to 500 men, including shelter, bakeries, ovens and livestock stables.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Timber rafting infrastructure allowed for large interconnected networks all over continental Europe and is still of importance in Finland.
</p><p>Starting with the sixteenth century, enhanced world <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maritime_trade" title="Maritime trade">maritime trade</a>, a boom in housing construction in Europe and the success and further <a href="/wiki/Berggeschrey" title="Berggeschrey">Berggeschrey</a> (rushes) of the mining industry increased timber consumption sharply. The notion of 'Nachhaltigkeit', <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> in forestry, is closely connected to the work of <a href="/wiki/Hans_Carl_von_Carlowitz" title="Hans Carl von Carlowitz">Hans Carl von Carlowitz</a> (1645–1714), a mining administrator in <a href="/wiki/Saxony" title="Saxony">Saxony</a>. His book <i>Sylvicultura oeconomica, oder haußwirthliche Nachricht und Naturmäßige Anweisung zur wilden Baum-Zucht</i> (1713) was the first comprehensive treatise about sustainable yield forestry. In the UK, and, to an extent, in continental Europe, the <a href="/wiki/Enclosure" title="Enclosure">enclosure</a> movement and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clearances" title="Clearances">clearances</a> favored  strictly enclosed private property.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rad_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rad-16">[16]</a></sup> The Agrarian reformers, early economic writers and scientists tried to get rid of the traditional commons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rad1_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-rad1-17">[17]</a></sup> At the time, an alleged <a href="/wiki/Tragedy_of_the_commons" title="Tragedy of the commons">tragedy of the commons</a> together with fears of a <a href="/wiki/Holznot" title="Holznot">Holznot</a>, an imminent wood shortage played a watershed role in the controversies about cooperative land use patterns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ws_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-ws-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>The practice of establishing tree plantations in the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> was promoted by <a href="/wiki/John_Evelyn" title="John Evelyn">John Evelyn</a>, though it had already acquired some popularity. <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">Louis XIV</a>'s minister <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Colbert" title="Jean-Baptiste Colbert">Jean-Baptiste Colbert</a>'s oak <a href="/wiki/Forest_of_Tron%C3%A7ais" title="Forest of Tronçais">Forest of Tronçais</a>, planted for the future use of the <a href="/wiki/French_Navy" title="French Navy">French Navy</a>, matured as expected in the mid-19th century: "Colbert had thought of everything except the steamship," <a href="/wiki/Fernand_Braudel" title="Fernand Braudel">Fernand Braudel</a> observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In parallel, schools of forestry were established beginning in the late 18th century in <a href="/wiki/Hesse" title="Hesse">Hesse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and elsewhere in Europe.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forest_conservation_and_early_globalization">Forest conservation and early globalization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forestry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Forest conservation and early globalization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forest_conservation" title="Forest conservation">forest conservation</a></div>
<p>During the late 19th and early 20th centuries, forest preservation programs were established in British India, the United States, and Europe. Many foresters were either from continental Europe (like <a href="/wiki/Dietrich_Brandis" title="Dietrich Brandis">Sir Dietrich Brandis</a>), or educated there (like <a href="/wiki/Gifford_Pinchot" title="Gifford Pinchot">Gifford Pinchot</a>). Sir <a href="/wiki/Dietrich_Brandis" title="Dietrich Brandis">Dietrich Brandis</a> is considered the father of tropical forestry, European concepts and practices had to be adapted in tropical and semi arid climate zones. The development of <a href="/wiki/Plantation" title="Plantation">plantation</a> forestry was one of the (controversial) answers to the specific challenges in the tropical colonies. The enactment and evolution of <a href="/wiki/Forestry_law" title="Forestry law">forest laws</a> and binding regulations occurred in most Western nations in the 20th century in response to growing conservation concerns and the increasing technological capacity of <a href="/wiki/Logging" title="Logging">logging</a> companies. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_forestry&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tropical forestry (page does not exist)">Tropical forestry</a> is a separate branch of forestry which deals mainly with equatorial forests that yield woods such as <a href="/wiki/Teak" title="Teak">teak</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mahogany" title="Mahogany">mahogany</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanization">Mechanization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forestry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mechanization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Forestry mechanization was always in close connection to metal working and the development of mechanical tools to cut and transport timber to its destination. Rafting belongs to the earliest means of transport.  Steel saws came up in the 15th century. The 19th century widely increased the availability of steel for <a href="/wiki/Whipsaw" title="Whipsaw">whipsaws</a> and introduced <a href="/wiki/Forest_railway" title="Forest railway">Forest railways</a> and railways in general for transport and as forestry customer. Further human induced changes, however, came since World War II, respectively in line with the "1950s syndrome".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The first portable chainsaw was invented in <a href="/wiki/Chainsaw#History" title="Chainsaw">1918 in Canada</a>, but large impact of mechanization in forestry started after World War II. Forestry <a href="/wiki/Harvester_(forestry)" title="Harvester (forestry)">harvesters</a> are among the most recent developments. Although <a href="/wiki/HEBUST_forestry_unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="HEBUST forestry unmanned aerial vehicle">drones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aerial_Forest_Protection_Service_(Russia)" title="Aerial Forest Protection Service (Russia)">planes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Forest_inventory#Tools_used_in_inventory" title="Forest inventory">laser scanning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Landsat_5#Mission_history" title="Landsat 5">satellites</a> and robots also play a part in forestry.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forestry_in_the_21st_century">Forestry in the 21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forestry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Forestry in the 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Laser_guided_cutting_of_wood_inside_modern_woodmill.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1067" data-file-width="1600" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Laser_guided_cutting_of_wood_inside_modern_woodmill.jpg/220px-Laser_guided_cutting_of_wood_inside_modern_woodmill.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Laser_guided_cutting_of_wood_inside_modern_woodmill.jpg/330px-Laser_guided_cutting_of_wood_inside_modern_woodmill.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Laser_guided_cutting_of_wood_inside_modern_woodmill.jpg/440px-Laser_guided_cutting_of_wood_inside_modern_woodmill.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Laser_guided_cutting_of_wood_inside_modern_woodmill.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A modern <a href="/wiki/Sawmill" title="Sawmill">sawmill</a></div></div></div>
<p>Today a strong body of <a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">research</a> exists regarding the management of <a href="/wiki/Forest_ecology" title="Forest ecology">forest ecosystems</a> and the genetic improvement of <a href="/wiki/Tree_breeding" title="Tree breeding">tree species and varieties</a>. Forestry studies also include the development of better methods for the planting, protecting, <a href="/wiki/Thinning" title="Thinning">thinning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Controlled_burn" title="Controlled burn">controlled burning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Felling" title="Felling">felling</a>, extracting, and processing of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timber" title="Timber">timber</a>. One of the applications of modern forestry is <a href="/wiki/Reforestation" title="Reforestation">reforestation</a>, in which trees are planted and tended in a given area.
</p><p>Trees provide numerous environmental, social and economic benefits for people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In many regions the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forest_industry" title="Forest industry">forest industry</a> is of major ecological, economic, and social importance. Third-party <a href="/wiki/Certification" title="Certification">certification</a> systems that provide independent verification of sound forest <a href="/wiki/Stewardship" title="Stewardship">stewardship</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_forest_management" title="Sustainable forest management">sustainable forestry</a> have become commonplace in many areas since the 1990s. These certification systems developed as a response to criticism of some forestry practices, particularly deforestation in less-developed regions along with concerns over resource management in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developed_world" title="Developed world">developed world</a>. Some certification systems<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (June 2018)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> are criticized<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (June 2018)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> for primarily acting as marketing tools and for lacking in their claimed independence.
</p><p>In topographically severe forested terrain, proper forestry is important for the prevention or minimization of serious soil <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> or even <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">landslides</a>. In areas with a high potential for landslides, forests can <a href="/wiki/Erosion_control" title="Erosion control">stabilize soils</a> and prevent property damage or loss, human injury, or loss of life.
</p><p>Public perception of <a href="/wiki/Forest_management" title="Forest management">forest management</a> has become controversial, with growing public concern over perceived mismanagement of the forest and increasing demands that forest land be managed for uses other than for pure timber production, for example: indigenous rights, <a href="/wiki/Outdoor_recreation" title="Outdoor recreation">recreation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Watershed_management" title="Watershed management">watershed management</a>, and preservation of <a href="/wiki/Wilderness" title="Wilderness">wilderness</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waterways" title="Waterways">waterways</a> and <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">wildlife habitat</a>. Some of the advantages and disadvantages accruing to <a href="/wiki/Monoculture" title="Monoculture">monoculture</a> in farming also apply to monoculture in forestry. Sharp disagreements over the role of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forest_fires" title="Forest fires">forest fires</a>, <a href="/wiki/Logging" title="Logging">logging</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motorized_recreation" title="Motorized recreation">motorized recreation</a> and other issues drive debate while the public demand for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wood_products" title="Wood products">wood products</a> continues to increase.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Foresters">Foresters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forestry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Foresters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Forester" title="Forester">Forester</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sanpablotregua.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="492" data-file-width="656" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Sanpablotregua.JPG/220px-Sanpablotregua.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Sanpablotregua.JPG/330px-Sanpablotregua.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Sanpablotregua.JPG/440px-Sanpablotregua.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sanpablotregua.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Foresters of the <a href="/wiki/Austral_University_of_Chile" title="Austral University of Chile">Austral University of Chile</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valdivian_forest" title="Valdivian forest">Valdivian forests</a> of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=San_Pablo_de_Tregua&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="San Pablo de Tregua (page does not exist)">San Pablo de Tregua</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a></div></div></div>
<p>Foresters work for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timber_industry" title="Timber industry">timber industry</a>, government agencies, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_group" title="Conservation group">conservation groups</a>, local authorities, <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban</a> parks boards, citizens' associations, and private <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landowner" title="Landowner">landowners</a>. The forestry profession includes a wide diversity of jobs, with educational requirements ranging from college bachelor's degrees to PhDs for highly specialized work. Industrial foresters plan forest regeneration starting with careful harvesting. Urban foresters manage trees in urban green spaces. Foresters work in <a href="/wiki/Plant_nursery" title="Plant nursery">tree nurseries</a> growing <a href="/wiki/Seedling" title="Seedling">seedlings</a> for woodland creation or regeneration projects. Foresters improve tree <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a>. Forest engineers develop new building systems.
Professional foresters <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timber_cruise" title="Timber cruise">measure</a> and <a href="/wiki/Growth_and_yield_modelling" title="Growth and yield modelling">model</a> the growth of forests with tools like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GIS" title="GIS">geographic information systems</a>. Foresters may combat <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insect</a> infestation, disease, forest and <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grassland</a> <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">wildfire</a>, but increasingly allow these natural aspects of forest <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a> to run their course when the likelihood of <a href="/wiki/Epidemic" title="Epidemic">epidemics</a> or risk of life or property are low. Increasingly, foresters participate in wildlife <a href="/wiki/Conservation_movement" title="Conservation movement">conservation</a> planning and <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">watershed</a> protection. Foresters have been mainly concerned with timber management, especially reforestation, maintaining forests at prime conditions, and fire control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-galegroup1_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-galegroup1-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forestry_plans">Forestry plans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forestry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Forestry plans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Foresters develop and implement forest management plans relying on mapped resource <a href="/wiki/Forest_inventory" title="Forest inventory">inventories</a> showing an area's <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topographical</a> features as well as its distribution of trees (by species) and other plant cover. Plans also include landowner objectives, roads, <a href="/wiki/Culvert" title="Culvert">culverts</a>, proximity to human habitation, water features and hydrological conditions, and soils information. Forest management plans typically include recommended <a href="/wiki/Silviculture" title="Silviculture">silvicultural</a> treatments and a timetable for their implementation. Application of digital maps in Geographic Informations systems (GIS) that extracts and integrates different information about forest terrains, soil type and tree covers, etc. using, e.g. laser scanning, enhances forest management plans in modern systems.
</p><p>Forest management plans include recommendations to achieve the landowner's objectives and desired future condition for the property subject to ecological, financial, logistical (e.g. access to resources), and other constraints.  On some properties, plans focus on producing quality wood products for processing or sale. Hence, tree species, quantity, and form, all central to the value of harvested products quality and quantity, tend to be important components of silvicultural plans.
</p><p>Good management plans include consideration of future conditions of the stand after any recommended harvests treatments, including future treatments (particularly in intermediate stand treatments), and plans for natural or artificial regeneration after final harvests.
</p><p>The objectives of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landowner" title="Landowner">landowners</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leasehold_estate" title="Leasehold estate">leaseholders</a> influence plans for harvest and subsequent site treatment. In Britain, plans featuring "good forestry practice" must always consider the needs of other stakeholders such as nearby communities or rural residents living within or adjacent to woodland areas. Foresters consider tree felling and environmental legislation when developing plans. Plans instruct the sustainable harvesting and replacement of trees. They indicate whether road building or other forest engineering operations are required.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">Agriculture</a> and forest leaders are also trying to understand how the <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> change legislation will affect what they do. The information gathered will provide the data that will determine the role of agriculture and forestry in a new climate change regulatory system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-galegroup1_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-galegroup1-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forestry_as_a_science">Forestry as a science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forestry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Forestry as a science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Over the past centuries, <a href="/wiki/Silviculture" title="Silviculture">forestry</a> was regarded as a separate science. With the rise of <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_science" title="Environmental science">environmental science</a>, there has been a reordering in the applied sciences. In line with this view, forestry is a primary land-use science comparable with <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Under these headings, the fundamentals behind the management of natural forests comes by way of natural ecology. Forests or tree plantations, those whose primary purpose is the extraction of forest products, are planned and managed utilizing a mix of ecological and agroecological principles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetic_diversity_in_forestry">Genetic diversity in forestry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forestry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Genetic diversity in forestry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Provenance" title="Provenance">provenance</a> of <a href="/wiki/Forest_reproductive_material" title="Forest reproductive material">forest reproductive material</a> used to plant forests has great influence on how the trees develop, hence why it is important to use forest reproductive material of good quality and of high <a href="/wiki/Genetic_diversity" title="Genetic diversity">genetic diversity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vries15_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-vries15-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>The term, <dfn>genetic diversity</dfn> describe differences in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DNA_sequence" title="DNA sequence">DNA sequence</a> between individuals as distinct from variation caused by environmental influences. The unique genetic composition of an individual (its <a href="/wiki/Genotype" title="Genotype">genotype</a>) will determine its performance (its <a href="/wiki/Phenotype" title="Phenotype">phenotype</a>) at a particular site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-konnert15_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-konnert15-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Genetic diversity is needed to maintain the vitality of forests and to provide <a href="/wiki/Ecological_resilience" title="Ecological resilience">resilience</a> to <a href="/wiki/Pest_(organism)" title="Pest (organism)">pests</a> and <a href="/wiki/Disease" title="Disease">diseases</a>. Genetic diversity also ensures that forest trees can survive, adapt and evolve under changing environmental conditions. Furthermore, genetic diversity is the foundation of biological diversity at species and <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> levels. <a href="/wiki/Forest_genetic_resources" title="Forest genetic resources">Forest genetic resources</a> are therefore important to consider in forest management.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vries15_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-vries15-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>Genetic diversity in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forests" title="Forests">forests</a> is threatened by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forest_fires" title="Forest fires">forest fires</a>, pests and diseases, <a href="/wiki/Habitat_fragmentation" title="Habitat fragmentation">habitat fragmentation</a>, poor silvicultural practices and inappropriate use of forest reproductive material. Furthermore, the marginal populations of many tree species are facing new threats due to climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vries15_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-vries15-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>Most countries in Europe have recommendations or guidelines for selecting species and provenances that can be used in a given site or zone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-konnert15_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-konnert15-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forestry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_forestry_education">History of forestry education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forestry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: History of forestry education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_historic_schools_of_forestry" title="List of historic schools of forestry">List of historic schools of forestry</a></div>
<p>The first dedicated forestry school was established by <a href="/wiki/Georg_Ludwig_Hartig" title="Georg Ludwig Hartig">Georg Ludwig Hartig</a> at <a href="/wiki/Hungen" title="Hungen">Hungen</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Wetterau" title="Wetterau">Wetterau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hesse" title="Hesse">Hesse</a>, in 1787, though forestry had been taught earlier in central Europe, including at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Giessen" title="University of Giessen">University of Giessen</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Landgraviate_of_Hesse-Darmstadt" title="Landgraviate of Hesse-Darmstadt">Hesse-Darmstadt</a>.
</p><p>In Spain, the first forestry school was the Forest Engineering School of Madrid (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Escuela_T%C3%A9cnica_Superior_de_Ingenieros_de_Montes" title="Escuela Técnica Superior de Ingenieros de Montes">Escuela Técnica Superior de Ingenieros de Montes</a>), founded in 1844.
</p><p>The first in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Biltmore_Forest_School" title="Biltmore Forest School">Biltmore Forest School</a> was established near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asheville" title="Asheville">Asheville</a>, North Carolina, by <a href="/wiki/Carl_A._Schenck" title="Carl A. Schenck">Carl A. Schenck</a> on September 1, 1898, on the grounds of <a href="/wiki/George_Washington_Vanderbilt_II" title="George Washington Vanderbilt II">George W. Vanderbilt's</a> <a href="/wiki/Biltmore_Estate" title="Biltmore Estate">Biltmore Estate</a>. Another early school was the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_New_York_State_College_of_Forestry" title="History of the New York State College of Forestry">New York State College of Forestry</a>, established at <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a> just a few weeks later, in September 1898. Early 19th century North American foresters went to Germany to study forestry. Some early German foresters also emigrated to North America.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> the first forestry school was established in Brazil, in <a href="/wiki/Vi%C3%A7osa,_Minas_Gerais" title="Viçosa, Minas Gerais">Viçosa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minas_Gerais" title="Minas Gerais">Minas Gerais</a>, in 1962, and moved the next year to become a faculty at the <a href="/wiki/Federal_University_of_Paran%C3%A1" title="Federal University of Paraná">Federal University of Paraná</a>, in Curitiba.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forestry_education_today">Forestry education today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forestry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Forestry education today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_forestry_universities_and_colleges" title="List of forestry universities and colleges">List of forestry universities and colleges</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_forestry_technical_schools" title="List of forestry technical schools">List of forestry technical schools</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Burn9582.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="262" data-file-width="350" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Burn9582.JPG/220px-Burn9582.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Burn9582.JPG/330px-Burn9582.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/ff/Burn9582.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Burn9582.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prescribed_burning" title="Prescribed burning">Prescribed burning</a> is used by foresters to reduce fuel loads</div></div></div>
<p>Today, forestry education typically includes training in general <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">botany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soil_science" title="Soil science">soil science</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">climatology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Forest_management" title="Forest management">forest management</a>. Education in the basics of <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political science</a> is often considered an advantage. Professional skills in conflict resolution and communication are also important in training programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>In India, forestry education is imparted in the <a href="/wiki/List_of_agricultural_universities_and_colleges#India" title="List of agricultural universities and colleges">agricultural universities</a> and in Forest Research Institutes (deemed universities). Four year degree programmes are conducted in these universities at the undergraduate level. Masters and Doctorate degrees are also available in these universities.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Higher_education" title="Higher education">postsecondary</a> forestry education leading to a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor's degree">Bachelor's degree</a> or <a href="/wiki/Master%27s_degree" title="Master's degree">Master's degree</a> is accredited by the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_American_Foresters" title="Society of American Foresters">Society of American Foresters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> the Canadian Institute of Forestry awards silver rings to graduates from accredited university BSc programs,  as well as college and technical programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>In many European countries, training in forestry is made in accordance with requirements of the <a href="/wiki/Bologna_Process" title="Bologna Process">Bologna Process</a> and the <a href="/wiki/European_Higher_Education_Area" title="European Higher Education Area">European Higher Education Area</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Forest_Research_Organizations" title="International Union of Forest Research Organizations">International Union of Forest Research Organizations</a> is the only international organization that coordinates forest science efforts worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
