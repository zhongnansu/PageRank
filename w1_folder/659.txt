<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Limnic eruption</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lake_nyos.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="750" data-file-width="1116" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Lake_nyos.jpg/220px-Lake_nyos.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Lake_nyos.jpg/330px-Lake_nyos.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Lake_nyos.jpg/440px-Lake_nyos.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lake_nyos.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Lake_Nyos" title="Lake Nyos">Lake Nyos</a> shortly after a limnic eruption.</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>limnic eruption</b>, also termed a <b>lake overturn</b>, is a rare type of <a href="/wiki/Natural_disaster" title="Natural disaster">natural disaster</a> in which dissolved <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) suddenly erupts from deep lake waters, forming a gas cloud capable of suffocating wildlife, livestock, and humans. A limnic eruption may also cause <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunamis</a> as the rising CO<sub>2</sub> displaces water. Scientists believe <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic activity</a>, and other explosive events can serve as triggers for limnic eruptions. Lakes in which such activity occurs are referred to as <b>limnically active lakes</b> or <b>exploding lakes</b>. Some features of limnically active lakes include:
</p>
<ul><li>CO<sub>2</sub>-saturated incoming water</li>
<li>A cool lake bottom indicating an absence of direct volcanic interaction with lake waters</li>
<li>An upper and lower thermal layer with differing CO<sub>2</sub> saturations</li>
<li>Proximity to areas with volcanic activity</li></ul>
<p>Investigations of the <a href="/wiki/Lake_Monoun" title="Lake Monoun">Lake Monoun</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Nyos_disaster" title="Lake Nyos disaster">Lake Nyos</a> casualties led scientists to classify limnic eruptions as a distinct type of disaster event, even though they can be indirectly linked to volcanic eruptions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_vulcan_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGS_vulcan-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_occurrences">Historical occurrences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limnic_eruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historical occurrences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cow_killed_by_Lake_Nyos_gasses.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="382" data-file-width="254" height="331" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Cow_killed_by_Lake_Nyos_gasses.jpg/220px-Cow_killed_by_Lake_Nyos_gasses.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c2/Cow_killed_by_Lake_Nyos_gasses.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cow_killed_by_Lake_Nyos_gasses.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cattle killed by the 1986 limnic eruption at <a href="/wiki/Lake_Nyos" title="Lake Nyos">Lake Nyos</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Due to the largely invisible nature of the underlying cause (CO<sub>2</sub> gas) behind limnic eruptions, it is difficult to determine to what extent eruptions have occurred in the past. In recent history, this phenomenon has been observed twice. The first limnic eruption occurred in <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a> at Lake Monoun in 1984, causing <a href="/wiki/Asphyxia" title="Asphyxia">asphyxiation</a> and death of 37 people living nearby.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A second, deadlier eruption <a href="/wiki/Lake_Nyos_disaster" title="Lake Nyos disaster">happened at neighbouring Lake Nyos</a> in 1986, this time releasing over 80 million m<sup>3</sup> of CO<sub>2,</sub> killing around 1,700 people and 3,500 livestock, again by asphyxiation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>A third lake, <a href="/wiki/Lake_Kivu" title="Lake Kivu">Lake Kivu</a>, rests on the border between the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rwanda" title="Rwanda">Rwanda</a>, and contains massive amounts of dissolved CO<sub>2</sub>. Sediment samples from the lake taken by Professor Robert Hecky (<a href="/wiki/University_of_Michigan" title="University of Michigan">University of Michigan</a>) showed an event caused living creatures in the lake to go extinct around every 1000 years, and caused nearby vegetation to be swept back into the lake. Limnic eruptions can be detected and quantified on a CO<sub>2</sub> concentration scale by taking air samples of the affected region.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Messel_pit" title="Messel pit">Messel pit</a> <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> deposits of <a href="/wiki/Messel" title="Messel">Messel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, show evidence of a limnic eruption there in the early <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a>. Among the victims are perfectly preserved <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turtle" title="Turtle">turtles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crocodile" title="Crocodile">crocodiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anteater" title="Anteater">anteaters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Insectivore" title="Insectivore">insectivores</a>, early <a href="/wiki/Primate" title="Primate">primates</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Palaeotheriidae" title="Palaeotheriidae">paleotheres</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limnic_eruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Limnic_eruption&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <p>For a lake to undergo a limnic eruption, the water must be nearly <a href="/wiki/Saturation_(chemistry)" title="Saturation (chemistry)">saturated</a> with gas. CO<sub>2</sub> was the primary component in the two observed cases (Lake Nyos and Lake Monoun). In Lake Kivu, scientists<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (August 2016)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> are concerned about the concentrations of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> gas as well. CO<sub>2</sub> may originate from <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_gas" title="Volcanic gas">volcanic gas</a> emitted from under the lake or from decomposition of organic material. Before a lake is saturated, it behaves like an unopened <a href="/wiki/Carbonation" title="Carbonation">carbonated beverage</a> (e.g., a <a href="/wiki/Soft_drink" title="Soft drink">soft drink</a>): the CO<sub>2</sub> is <a href="/wiki/Solvation" title="Solvation">dissolved</a> in the water.  In both the lake and the soft drink, CO<sub>2</sub> dissolves much more readily at higher <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> (<a href="/wiki/Henry%27s_law" title="Henry's law">Henry's law</a>). This is why bubbles in a can of soda form only after the can is opened; when the pressure is released, the CO<sub>2</sub> comes out of <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solution</a>. In the case of lakes, the bottom is at a much higher pressure; the deeper it is, the higher the pressure is at the bottom. Therefore huge amounts of CO<sub>2</sub> can be dissolved in large, deep lakes. CO<sub>2</sub> also dissolves more readily in cooler water, such as that found at a lake bottom. A small rise in water <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> can lead to the release of a large amount of CO<sub>2</sub>.
</p><p>Once a lake is saturated with CO<sub>2</sub>, it is very unstable, but a trigger is needed to set off an eruption. In the case of the 1986 Lake Nyos eruption, <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">landslides</a> were the suspected triggers, but a volcanic eruption, an <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquake</a>, or even <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain storms</a> are potential triggers. Another possible cause of a limnic eruption is gradual gas saturation at specific depths which can trigger spontaneous gas development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> For any of these cases, the trigger pushes some of the gas saturated water higher in the lake, where pressure is insufficient to keep CO<sub>2</sub> in solution. As bubbles start forming the water is lifted even higher in the lake (<a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a>), where yet more CO<sub>2</sub> comes out of solution. This process forms a column of gas, at which point the water at the bottom of this column is pulled up by <a href="/wiki/Suction" title="Suction">suction</a>, and it, too, loses CO<sub>2</sub> in a runaway process.  This eruption discharges CO<sub>2</sub> into the air and can even displace enough water to form a tsunami.
</p><p>Limnic eruptions are exceptionally rare for several reasons. First, a CO<sub>2</sub> source must exist (regions with volcanic activity are most at risk). Second, the vast majority of lakes are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holomictic" title="Holomictic">holomictic</a> (i.e., their layers mix regularly), preventing a buildup of dissolved gases. Only <a href="/wiki/Meromictic_lake" title="Meromictic lake">meromictic lakes</a> do not mix and remain <a href="/wiki/Stratification_(water)" title="Stratification (water)">stratified</a>, allowing CO<sub>2</sub> to remain dissolved. It is estimated only one meromictic lake exists for every 1,000 holomictic lakes. Finally, a lake must be deep enough to have sufficient pressure to dissolve large amounts of CO<sub>2</sub>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consequences">Consequences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limnic_eruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Consequences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Once an eruption occurs, a large CO<sub>2</sub> cloud forms above the lake and expands to the surrounding region. Because CO<sub>2</sub> is <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">denser</a> than air, it has a tendency to sink to the ground, simultaneously displacing breathable air, resulting in <a href="/wiki/Asphyxia" title="Asphyxia">asphyxia</a>. CO<sub>2</sub> can make human bodily fluids highly acidic and potentially cause <a href="/wiki/Hypercapnia" title="Hypercapnia">CO<sub>2</sub> poisoning</a>. As victims gasp for air, they actually accelerate asphyxia by inhaling CO<sub>2</sub> gas.
</p><p>At Lake Nyos, the gas cloud descended into a nearby village where it settled, killing nearly everyone; casualties as far as 25 km (16 mi) were reported<sup><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">citation needed</a></sup>. A change in skin color on some bodies led scientists to hypothesize the gas cloud may have contained dissolved acid such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride" title="Hydrogen chloride">hydrogen chloride</a>, though this hypothesis is disputed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PMC_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-PMC-5">[5]</a></sup> Many victims were found with blisters on their skin, thought to have been caused by <a href="/wiki/Pressure_ulcer" title="Pressure ulcer">pressure ulcers</a>, which were likely caused by low blood oxygen levels in those asphyxiated by carbon dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-6">[6]</a></sup> Thousands of cattle and wild animals were also asphyxiated, but no official count reported. Notably, nearby vegetation was largely unaffected, except any growing immediately adjacent to the lake. There, vegetation was damaged or destroyed by a 5 m (16 ft) high tsunami caused by the violent eruption.
</p><p>Carbon dioxide released from a lake is at a low enough temperature to cause frostbite<sup><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">citation needed</a></sup>, consistent with blisters found on the skin of survivors of the <a href="/wiki/Lake_Monoun" title="Lake Monoun">Lake Monoun</a> eruption<sup><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">citation needed</a></sup>. Survivors reported a smell of rotten eggs and feeling warm before passing out<sup><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">citation needed</a></sup>; this could be explained by sensory <a href="/wiki/Hallucination" title="Hallucination">hallucination</a> associated with high blood concentrations of carbon dioxide<sup><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">citation needed</a></sup>. Studies of jet pilots and carbon dioxide exposure report similar effects as reported by limnic eruption survivors: the smell of rotten eggs or gunpowder, and a feeling of warmth<sup><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">citation needed</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Degassing">Degassing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limnic_eruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Degassing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Efforts are under way to develop a solution for removing the gas from these lakes and to prevent a build-up which could lead to another catastrophe. A team led by French scientist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Michel_Halbwachs&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Michel Halbwachs (page does not exist)">Michel Halbwachs</a> began experimenting at <a href="/wiki/Lake_Monoun" title="Lake Monoun">Lake Monoun</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Nyos" title="Lake Nyos">Lake Nyos</a> in 1990 using <a href="/wiki/Siphon" title="Siphon">siphons</a> to degas the waters of these lakes in a controlled manner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The team positions a pipe vertically in the lake with its upper end above the water surface. Water saturated with CO<sub>2</sub> enters the bottom of the pipe and rises to the top. The lower pressure at the surface allows the gas to come out of solution. Only a small amount of water must be mechanically pumped initially through the pipe to start the flow. As saturated water rises, the CO<sub>2</sub> comes out of solution and forms bubbles. The natural buoyancy of the bubbles draws the water up the pipe at high velocity resulting in a fountain at the surface. The degassifying water acts like a pump, drawing more water into the bottom of the pipe, and creating a self-sustaining flow. This is the same process which leads to a natural eruption, but in this case it is controlled by the size of the pipe.
</p><p>Each pipe has a limited pumping capacity and several would be required for both <a href="/wiki/Lake_Monoun" title="Lake Monoun">Lake Monoun</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Nyos" title="Lake Nyos">Lake Nyos</a> to degas a significant fraction of the deep lake water and render the lakes safe.  The deep lake waters are slightly acidic due to the dissolved CO<sub>2</sub> which causes corrosion to the pipes and electronics, necessitating ongoing maintenance. There is some concern CO<sub>2</sub> from the pipes could settle on the surface of the lake forming a thin layer of unbreathable air and thus potentially causing problems for wildlife.
</p><p>In January 2001, a single pipe was installed by the <a href="/wiki/French_Cameroons" title="French Cameroons">French-Cameroun</a> team on <a href="/wiki/Lake_Nyos" title="Lake Nyos">Lake Nyos</a>, and two more pipes were installed in 2011 with funding support from the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Development_Programme" title="United Nations Development Programme">United Nations Development Programme</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hsw_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-hsw-9">[9]</a></sup> A pipe was installed at <a href="/wiki/Lake_Monoun" title="Lake Monoun">Lake Monoun</a> in 2003 and two more were added in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hsw_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-hsw-9">[9]</a></sup>  These three pipes are thought to be sufficient to prevent an increase in CO<sub>2</sub> levels, removing approximately the same amount of gas that naturally enters at the lake bed<sup><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">citation needed</a></sup>. In January 2003, an 18-month project was approved to fully degas <a href="/wiki/Lake_Monoun" title="Lake Monoun">Lake Monoun</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and the lake has since been rendered safe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nature_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Lake_Kivu.27s_potential_danger"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Lake_Kivu's_potential_danger">Lake Kivu's potential danger</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limnic_eruption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lake Kivu's potential danger">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LakeKivu_satellite.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="464" data-file-width="296" height="345" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/LakeKivu_satellite.jpg/220px-LakeKivu_satellite.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/19/LakeKivu_satellite.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LakeKivu_satellite.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Satellite image of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Kivu" title="Lake Kivu">Lake Kivu</a>.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lake_Kivu" title="Lake Kivu">Lake Kivu</a> is not only 2,000 times larger than <a href="/wiki/Lake_Nyos" title="Lake Nyos">Lake Nyos</a>, but is also located in a far more densely populated area, with over two million people living along its shores. Lake Kivu has not reached a high level of CO<sub>2</sub> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/saturation" title="wikt:saturation">saturation</a>
yet; if the water were to become heavily saturated, a limnic eruption would pose a great risk to human and animal life. Two significant changes in Lake Kivu's physical state have brought attention to a possible limnic eruption: the high rates of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> dissociation and a rising surface temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">[11]</a></sup> Research investigating historical and present-day temperatures show Lake Kivu's surface temperature is increasing by about 0.12 °C per decade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">[11]</a></sup> Lake Kivu is in close proximity to potential triggers: <a href="/wiki/Mount_Nyiragongo" title="Mount Nyiragongo">Mount Nyiragongo</a> (an active volcano which erupted in January 2002), an active earthquake zone, and other active volcanoes<sup><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">citation needed</a></sup>.
</p><p>While the lake could be degassed in a manner similar to Lake Monoun and Lake Nyos, due to the size of Lake Kivu and the volume of gas it contains, such an operation would be expensive, running into the millions of dollars.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A scheme initiated in 2010 to use methane trapped in the lake as a fuel source to generate electricity in <a href="/wiki/Rwanda" title="Rwanda">Rwanda</a> has led to a degree of CO<sub>2</sub> degassing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>  During the procedure for extracting the flammable methane gas used to fuel power stations on the shore, some CO<sub>2</sub> is removed in a process known as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Catalyst_scrubbing&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Catalyst scrubbing (page does not exist)">catalyst scrubbing</a>. It is unclear whether enough gas will be removed to eliminate the danger of a limnic eruption at Lake Kivu.
</p>
