<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">System of measurement</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>system of measurement</b> is a collection of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Units_of_measurement" title="Units of measurement">units of measurement</a> and rules relating them to each other. Systems of measurement have historically been important, regulated and defined for the purposes of <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Commerce" title="Commerce">commerce</a>. Systems of measurement in modern use include the <a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">metric system</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_system" title="Imperial system">imperial system</a>, and <a href="/wiki/United_States_customary_units" title="United States customary units">United States customary units</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=System_of_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_measurement" title="History of measurement">History of measurement</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a> gave rise to the <a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">metric system</a>, and this has spread around the world, replacing most customary units of measure. In most systems, <a href="/wiki/Length" title="Length">length</a> (distance), <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">time</a> are <i>base quantities</i>.
</p><p>Later science developments showed that either <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">electric charge</a> or <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric current</a> could be added to extend the set of base quantities by which many other <a href="/wiki/Metrology" title="Metrology">metrological units</a> could be easily defined. (However, electrical units are not necessary for such a set. <a href="/wiki/Gaussian_units" title="Gaussian units">Gaussian units</a>, for example, have only length, mass, and time as base quantities, and the ampere is defined in terms of other units.) Other quantities, such as <a href="/wiki/Power_(physics)" title="Power (physics)">power</a> and <a href="/wiki/Speed" title="Speed">speed</a>, are derived from the base set: for example, speed is distance per unit time. Historically a wide range of units was used for the same type of quantity: in different contexts, length was measured in <a href="/wiki/Inch" title="Inch">inches</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foot_(length)" title="Foot (length)">feet</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yards" title="Yards">yards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fathom" title="Fathom">fathoms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rod_(unit)" title="Rod (unit)">rods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chain_(unit)" title="Chain (unit)">chains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Furlong" title="Furlong">furlongs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mile" title="Mile">miles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nautical_mile" title="Nautical mile">nautical miles</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stadia_(length)" title="Stadia (length)">stadia</a>, <a href="/wiki/League_(unit)" title="League (unit)">leagues</a>, with conversion factors which were not powers of ten. Such arrangements were satisfactory in their own contexts.
</p><p>The preference for a more universal and consistent system (based on more rational base units) only gradually spread with the growth of science. Changing a measurement system has substantial financial and cultural costs which must be offset against the advantages to be obtained from using a more rational system. However pressure built up, including from scientists and engineers for conversion to a more rational, and also internationally consistent, basis of measurement.
</p><p>In antiquity, <i>systems of measurement</i> were defined locally: the different units might be defined independently according to the length of a king's thumb or the size of his foot, the length of stride, the length of arm, or maybe the weight of water in a keg of specific size, perhaps itself defined in <i>hands</i> and <i>knuckles</i>. The unifying characteristic is that there was some definition based on some standard. Eventually <i><a href="/wiki/Cubit" title="Cubit">cubits</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Yard" title="Yard">strides</a></i> gave way to "customary units" to meet the needs of merchants and scientists.
</p><p>In the metric system and other recent systems, a single basic unit is used for each base quantity. Often secondary units (multiples and submultiples) are derived from the basic units by multiplying by powers of ten, i.e. by simply moving the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decimal_point" title="Decimal point">decimal point</a>. Thus the basic metric unit of length is the <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metre</a>; a distance of 1.234 m is 1,234 millimetres, or 0.001234 kilometres.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_practice">Current practice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=System_of_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Current practice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Metrication" title="Metrication">Metrication</a></div>
<p>Metrication is complete or nearly complete in almost all countries. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_customary_units" title="US customary units">US customary units</a> are heavily used in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and to some degree in <a href="/wiki/Liberia" title="Liberia">Liberia</a>. Traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burmese_units_of_measurement" title="Burmese units of measurement">Burmese units of measurement</a> are used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burma" title="Burma">Burma</a>. U.S. units are used in limited contexts in Canada due to the large volume of trade; there is also considerable use of Imperial weights and measures, despite <i>de jure</i> Canadian conversion to metric.
</p><p>A number of other jurisdictions have laws mandating or permitting other systems of measurement in some or all contexts, such as the United Kingdom – whose <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Road_signage_legislation_(UK)" title="Road signage legislation (UK)">road signage legislation</a>, for instance, only allows distance signs displaying <a href="/wiki/Imperial_units" title="Imperial units">imperial units</a> (miles or yards)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TSRGD_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-TSRGD-1">[1]</a></sup> – or Hong Kong.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HK_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-HK-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States, metric units are used almost universally in science, widely in the military, and partially in industry, but customary units predominate in household use. At retail stores, the liter is a commonly used unit for volume, especially on bottles of beverages, and milligrams, rather than grains, are used for medications. Some other standard non-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI" title="SI">SI</a> units are still in international use, such as <a href="/wiki/Nautical_mile" title="Nautical mile">nautical miles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">knots</a> in aviation and shipping.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Metric_system">Metric system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=System_of_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Metric system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">Metric system</a> and <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MetricImperialUSCustomaryUnits.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1066" data-file-width="800" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/MetricImperialUSCustomaryUnits.jpg/170px-MetricImperialUSCustomaryUnits.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/MetricImperialUSCustomaryUnits.jpg/255px-MetricImperialUSCustomaryUnits.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/MetricImperialUSCustomaryUnits.jpg/340px-MetricImperialUSCustomaryUnits.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MetricImperialUSCustomaryUnits.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A baby bottle that measures in three measurement systems—metric, imperial (UK), and US customary.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">Metric systems</a> of units have evolved since the adoption of the first well-defined system in France in 1795. During this evolution the use of these systems has spread throughout the world, first to non-English-speaking countries, and then to English speaking countries.
</p><p>Multiples and submultiples of metric units are related by powers of ten and their names are formed with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_prefix" title="SI prefix">prefixes</a>. This relationship is compatible with the decimal system of numbers and it contributes greatly to the convenience of metric units.
</p><p>In the early metric system there were two base units, the <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metre</a> for length and the <a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">gram</a> for mass. The other units of length and mass, and all units of area, volume, and derived units such as density were derived from these two base units.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mesures_usuelles" title="Mesures usuelles">Mesures usuelles</a> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> for <i>customary measurements</i>) were a system of measurement introduced as a compromise between the metric system and traditional measurements. It was used in France from 1812 to 1839.
</p><p>A number of variations on the metric system have been in use. These include <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_metric_system" title="Gravitational metric system">gravitational systems</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Centimetre%E2%80%93gram%E2%80%93second_system_of_units" title="Centimetre–gram–second system of units">centimetre–gram–second systems</a> (cgs) useful in science, the <a href="/wiki/Metre%E2%80%93tonne%E2%80%93second_system_of_units" title="Metre–tonne–second system of units">metre–tonne–second system</a> (mts) once used in the USSR and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metre%E2%80%93kilogram%E2%80%93second_system_of_units" title="Metre–kilogram–second system of units">metre–kilogram–second system</a> (mks).
</p><p>The current international standard metric system is the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a> (<i>Système international d'unités</i> or SI) It is an mks system based on the <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kilogram</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">second</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">kelvin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ampere" title="Ampere">ampere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Candela" title="Candela">candela</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mole</a>.
</p><p>The SI includes two classes of units which are defined and agreed internationally. The first of these classes includes the seven <a href="/wiki/SI_base_unit" title="SI base unit">SI base units</a> for length, mass, time, temperature, electric current, luminous intensity and amount of substance. The second class consists of the <a href="/wiki/SI_derived_unit" title="SI derived unit">SI derived units</a>. These derived units are defined in terms of the seven base units. All other quantities (e.g. work, force, power) are expressed in terms of SI derived units.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Imperial_and_US_customary_units">Imperial and US customary units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=System_of_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Imperial and US customary units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Imperial_and_US_customary_measurement_systems" title="Imperial and US customary measurement systems">Imperial and US customary measurement systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Imperial_units" title="Imperial units">Imperial units</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_customary_units" title="US customary units">US customary units</a></div>
<p>Both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imperial_unit" title="Imperial unit">imperial units</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_customary_units" title="United States customary units">US customary units</a> derive from earlier <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_unit" title="English unit">English units</a>. Imperial units were mostly used in the former <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">British Commonwealth</a>, but in all these countries they have been largely supplanted by the metric system. They are still used for some applications in the United Kingdom but have been mostly replaced by the metric system in <a href="/wiki/Commerce" title="Commerce">commercial</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific" title="Scientific">scientific</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industrial</a> applications.
US customary units, however, are still the main system of measurement in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. While some steps towards <a href="/wiki/Metrication" title="Metrication">metrication</a> have been made (mainly in the late 1960s and early 1970s), the customary units have a strong hold due to the vast industrial infrastructure and commercial development.
</p><p>While imperial and US customary systems are closely related, there are a number of <a href="/wiki/Comparison_of_the_imperial_and_US_customary_measurement_systems" title="Comparison of the imperial and US customary measurement systems">differences between them</a>. Units of length and area (the <a href="/wiki/Inch" title="Inch">inch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Foot_(unit)" title="Foot (unit)">foot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yard" title="Yard">yard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mile" title="Mile">mile</a> etc.) are identical except for surveying purposes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (July 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avoirdupois" title="Avoirdupois">Avoirdupois</a> units of mass and weight differ for units larger than a <a href="/wiki/Pound_(mass)" title="Pound (mass)">pound</a> (lb). The imperial system uses a stone of 14 lb, a long <a href="/wiki/Hundredweight" title="Hundredweight">hundredweight</a> of 112 lb and a long <a href="/wiki/Ton" title="Ton">ton</a> of 2240 lb. The stone is not used in the US and the hundredweights and tons are short: 100 lb and 2000 lb respectively.
</p><p>Where these systems most notably differ is in their units of volume. A US <a href="/wiki/Fluid_ounce" title="Fluid ounce">fluid ounce</a> (fl oz), about 29.6 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millilitre" title="Millilitre">millilitres</a> (ml), is slightly larger than the imperial fluid ounce (about 28.4 ml). However, as there are 16 US fl oz to a US <a href="/wiki/Pint" title="Pint">pint</a> and 20 imp fl oz per imperial pint, the imperial pint is about 20% larger. The same is true of <a href="/wiki/Quart" title="Quart">quarts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gallon" title="Gallon">gallons</a>, etc. Six US gallons are a little less than five imperial gallons.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avoirdupois" title="Avoirdupois">Avoirdupois</a> system served as the general system of mass and weight. In addition to this there are the <a href="/wiki/Troy_weight" title="Troy weight">Troy</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Apothecaries%27_system" title="Apothecaries' system">Apothecaries' systems</a>. Troy weight was customarily used for <a href="/wiki/Precious_metal" title="Precious metal">precious metals</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_powder" title="Black powder">black powder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gemstone" title="Gemstone">gemstones</a>. The troy ounce is the only unit of the system in current use; it is used for precious metals. Although the troy ounce is larger than its Avoirdupois equivalent, the pound is smaller. The obsolete troy pound was divided into 12 ounces, rather than the 16 ounces per pound of the Avoirdupois system. The Apothecaries' system was traditionally used in <a href="/wiki/Pharmacology" title="Pharmacology">pharmacology</a>, but has now been replaced by the metric system; it shared the same pound and ounce as the troy system but with different further subdivisions.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_units">Natural units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=System_of_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Natural units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Natural_units" title="Natural units">Natural units</a> are <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physical</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Units_of_measurement" title="Units of measurement">units of measurement</a> defined in terms of universal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_constants" title="Physical constants">physical constants</a> in such a manner that selected physical constants take on the numerical value of one when expressed in terms of those units.  Natural units are so named because their definition relies on only properties of <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">nature</a> and not on any human construct. Various systems of natural units are possible.
</p><p>Some other examples are as follows:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Geometrized_unit_system" title="Geometrized unit system">Geometric unit systems</a> are useful in <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" title="Theory of relativity">relativistic physics</a>. In these systems the base physical units are chosen so that the <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">speed of light</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_constant" title="Gravitational constant">gravitational constant</a> are set equal to unity.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Planck_units" title="Planck units">Planck units</a> are a form of geometric units obtained by also setting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boltzmann%27s_constant" title="Boltzmann's constant">Boltzmann's constant</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Coulomb%27s_law" title="Coulomb's law">Coulomb force constant</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reduced_Planck_constant" title="Reduced Planck constant">reduced Planck constant</a> to unity. They are based only on properties of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_space" title="Free space">free space</a> rather than any object or particle.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Natural_units#Stoney_units" title="Natural units">Stoney units</a> are similar to Planck units but set the <a href="/wiki/Elementary_charge" title="Elementary charge">elementary charge</a> to unity and allow Planck's constant to float (i.e. it is a number that has to be determined by experiment).</li>
<li><a href='/wiki/Natural_units#"Schrödinger"_units' title="Natural units">"Schrödinger" units</a> are also similar to Planck units and also set the elementary charge to unity, but allow the speed of light to float.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Atomic_units" title="Atomic units">Atomic units</a> are a system of units used in <a href="/wiki/Atomic_physics" title="Atomic physics">atomic physics</a>, particularly for describing the properties of <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a>. The atomic units have been chosen such that several the constants relating to the electron are all equal to one. They are similar to "Schrödinger" units but set the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_mass" title="Electron mass">electron mass</a> to unity and allow the gravitational constant to float. The unit of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> in this system is the total energy of the <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bohr_atom" title="Bohr atom">Bohr atom</a> and called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hartree_energy" title="Hartree energy">Hartree energy</a>. The unit of length is the <a href="/wiki/Bohr_radius" title="Bohr radius">Bohr radius</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Natural_units#Electronic_system_of_units" title="Natural units">Electronic units</a> are similar to Stoney units but set the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_mass" title="Electron mass">electron mass</a> to unity and allow the gravitational constant to float. They are also similar to atomic units but set the speed of light to unity and allow Planck's constant to float.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Natural_units#Quantum_electrodynamical_system_of_units" title="Natural units">Quantum electrodynamical units</a> are similar to the electronic system of units except that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proton_mass" title="Proton mass">proton mass</a> is normalised rather than the electron mass.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-standard_units">Non-standard units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=System_of_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Non-standard units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/List_of_unusual_units_of_measurement" title="List of unusual units of measurement">Non-standard measurement units</a>, sometimes found in books, newspapers etc., include:

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Area">Area</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=System_of_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Area">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/American_football#Field_and_players" title="American football">American football field</a>, which has a playing area 100 <a href="/wiki/Yard" title="Yard">yards</a> (91.4 m) long by 160 feet (48.8 m) wide. This is often used by the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> public media for the sizes of large buildings or parks. It is used both as a unit of length (100 yd or 91.4 m, the length of the playing field excluding goal areas) and as a unit of area (57,600 sq ft or 5,350 m<sup>2</sup>), about 1.32 <a href="/wiki/Acre" title="Acre">acres</a> (0.53 <a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">ha</a>).</li>
<li>British media also frequently uses the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Association_football_pitch" title="Association football pitch">football pitch</a> for equivalent purposes, although <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soccer" title="Soccer">soccer</a> pitches are not of a fixed size, but instead can vary within defined limits (100–130 yd or 91.4–118.9 m long, and 50–100 yd or 45.7–91.4 m wide, giving an area of 5,000 to 13,000 sq yd or 4,181 to 10,870 m<sup>2</sup>). However the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Champions_League" title="UEFA Champions League">UEFA Champions League</a> field must be exactly 105 by 68 m (114.83 by 74.37 yd) giving an area of 7,140 m<sup>2</sup> (0.714 ha) or 8,539 <a href="/wiki/Square_yard" title="Square yard">sq yd</a> (1.764 <a href="/wiki/Acre" title="Acre">acres</a>). Example: <a class="external text" href="http://www.belfasttelegraph.co.uk/news/local-national/article2698634.ece" rel="nofollow">HSS vessels are aluminium catamarans <b>about the size of a football pitch</b>... - Belfast Telegraph 23 June 2007</a></li>
<li>Larger areas are sometimes expressed as a multiple of the areas of certain American states, or subdivisions of the UK etc.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy">Energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=System_of_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>A ton of <a href="/wiki/TNT_equivalent" title="TNT equivalent">TNT equivalent</a>, and its multiples the kiloton, the megaton, and the gigaton. Often used in stating the power of very energetic events such as <a href="/wiki/Explosion" title="Explosion">explosions</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic" title="Volcanic">volcanic</a> events and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earthquakes" title="Earthquakes">earthquakes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroid</a> impacts. A <a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">gram</a> of TNT as a unit of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> has been defined as 1000 thermochemical calories (1,000 <a href="/wiki/Calorie" title="Calorie">cal</a> or 4,184 <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">J</a>).</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Little_Boy" title="Little Boy">atom bomb dropped on Hiroshima</a>. Its force is often used in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_media" title="Public media">public media</a> and popular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Books" title="Books">books</a> as a unit of energy. (Its yield was roughly 13 kilotons, or 60 TJ.)</li>
<li>One stick of <a href="/wiki/Dynamite" title="Dynamite">dynamite</a></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Units_of_currency">Units of currency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=System_of_measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Units of currency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A unit of measurement that applies to <a href="/wiki/Money" title="Money">money</a> is called a <a href="/wiki/Unit_of_account" title="Unit of account">unit of account</a> in economics and unit of measure in accounting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This is normally a <a href="/wiki/Currency" title="Currency">currency</a> issued by a <a href="/wiki/Country" title="Country">country</a> or a fraction thereof; for instance, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US dollar</a> and US cent (​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>100</sub></span> of a dollar), or the <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euro</a> and euro cent.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/ISO_4217" title="ISO 4217">ISO 4217</a> is the international standard describing three letter codes (also known as the currency code) to define the names of currencies established by the International Organization for Standardization (ISO).
</p>
