<p><b>Acrylic acid</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAC" title="IUPAC">IUPAC</a>: <b>propenoic acid</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compound</a> with the formula CH<sub>2</sub>=CHCOOH.  It is the simplest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unsaturated_compound" title="Unsaturated compound">unsaturated</a> <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxylic acid</a>, consisting of a <a href="/wiki/Vinyl_group" title="Vinyl group">vinyl group</a> connected directly to a <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxylic acid</a> terminus. This colorless liquid has a characteristic acrid or tart smell. It is miscible with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" title="Water (molecule)">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohols</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ether" title="Ether">ethers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chloroform" title="Chloroform">chloroform</a>. More than a million tons are produced annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acrylic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Acrylic acid is produced from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propylene" title="Propylene">propylene</a>, which is a byproduct of <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">ethylene</a> and gasoline production:
</p>
<dl><dd>CH<sub>2</sub>=CHCH<sub>3</sub> + ​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>3</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> O<sub>2</sub> → CH<sub>2</sub>=CHCO<sub>2</sub>H + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Because propane is a significantly cheaper raw material than propylene, considerable research efforts are being undertaken to develop a process based on the one-step selective oxidation of propane to acrylic acid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Carboxylation" title="Carboxylation">Carboxylating</a> ethylene to acrylic acid under <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_carbon_dioxide" title="Supercritical carbon dioxide">supercritical carbon dioxide</a> condition is thermodynamically possible when an efficient catalyst is developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SCY_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-SCY-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Because acrylic acid and its esters have long been valued commercially, many other methods have been developed, but most have been abandoned for economic or environmental reasons. An early method was the hydrocarboxylation of acetylene ("<a href="/wiki/Walter_Reppe" title="Walter Reppe">Reppe chemistry</a>"):
</p>
<dl><dd>HCCH + CO + H<sub>2</sub>O → CH<sub>2</sub>=CHCO<sub>2</sub>H</dd></dl>
<p>This method requires nickel carbonyl and high pressures of carbon monoxide. It<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> was once manufactured by the hydrolysis of acrylonitrile, which is derived from <a href="/wiki/Propene" title="Propene">propene</a> by ammoxidation, but was abandoned<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> because the method<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (November 2017)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> cogenerates ammonium derivatives.  Other now abandoned precursors to acrylic acid include <a href="/wiki/Ethenone" title="Ethenone">ethenone</a> and ethylene cyanohydrin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Dow_Chemical_Company" title="Dow Chemical Company">Dow Chemical Company</a> and its partner OPX Biotechnologies are investigating using fermented <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a> to produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3-hydroxypropionic_acid" title="3-hydroxypropionic acid">3-hydroxypropionic acid</a> (3HP), an acrylic-acid precursor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The goal is to reduce greenhouse-gas emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions_and_uses">Reactions and uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acrylic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reactions and uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Acrylic acid undergoes the typical <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid#Reactions" title="Carboxylic acid">reactions of a carboxylic acid</a>.  When reacted with an <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a>, it forms the corresponding <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">ester</a>. The esters and <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a> of acrylic acid are collectively known as <a href="/wiki/Acrylate" title="Acrylate">acrylates</a> (or propenoates). The most common alkyl esters of acrylic acid are methyl, butyl, ethyl, and 2-ethylhexyl acrylate.
</p><p>Acrylic acid and its esters readily combine with themselves (to form <a href="/wiki/Polyacrylic_acid" title="Polyacrylic acid">polyacrylic acid</a>) or other <a href="/wiki/Monomer" title="Monomer">monomers</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Acrylamide" title="Acrylamide">acrylamides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acrylonitrile" title="Acrylonitrile">acrylonitrile</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vinyl_group" title="Vinyl group">vinyl</a> compounds, <a href="/wiki/Styrene" title="Styrene">styrene</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Butadiene" title="Butadiene">butadiene</a>) by reacting at their double bond, forming <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homopolymer" title="Homopolymer">homopolymers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Copolymer" title="Copolymer">copolymers</a>, which are used in the manufacture of various <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coating" title="Coating">coatings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adhesive" title="Adhesive">adhesives</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elastomer" title="Elastomer">elastomers</a>, as well as floor polishes and paints.
</p><p>Acrylic acid is a compound, which is used of many industries like the <a href="/wiki/Diaper" title="Diaper">diaper</a> industry, the <a href="/wiki/Water_treatment" title="Water treatment">water treatment</a> industry or the textiles industry. On a worldwide scale the consumption rate of acrylic acid is projected to reach more than an estimated 8,000 kilo tons, by 2020. This increase is expected to occur as a result of using this product in new applications, including <a href="/wiki/Personal_care" title="Personal care">personal care</a> products, <a href="/wiki/Detergent" title="Detergent">detergents</a> and products that are used for adult <a href="/wiki/Urinary_incontinence" title="Urinary incontinence">incontinence</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Substituents">Substituents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acrylic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Substituents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a substituent acrylic acid can be found as an <a href="/wiki/Acyl_group" title="Acyl group">acyl group</a> or a carboxyalkyl group, depending on the removal of the group from the molecule.
More specifically, these are:
</p>
<ol><li>The <a href="/wiki/Acryloyl_group" title="Acryloyl group">acryloyl group</a>, with the removal of the −OH from carbon-1.</li>
<li>The <b>2-carboxyethenyl group</b>, with the removal of a −H from carbon-3. This substituent group is found in <a href="/wiki/Chlorophyll" title="Chlorophyll">chlorophyll</a>.</li></ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acrylic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Acrylic acid is severely irritating and corrosive to the skin and the respiratory tract. Eye contact can result in severe and irreversible injury. Low exposure will cause minimal or no health effects, while high exposure could result in <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_edema" title="Pulmonary edema">pulmonary edema</a>.  The <a href="/wiki/Median_lethal_dose" title="Median lethal dose">LD<sub style="font-size:-1">50</sub></a> is 340 mg/kg (rat, oral).
</p><p>Acrylic acid is a constituent of <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_smoke" title="Tobacco smoke">tobacco smoke</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TalhoutSchulz2011_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-TalhoutSchulz2011-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
