<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Anaerobic oxidation of methane</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Anaerobic oxidation of methane</b> (<b>AOM</b>) is a microbial process occurring in anoxic marine and freshwater <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sediments" title="Sediments">sediments</a>. During AOM <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> is oxidized with different terminal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_acceptors" title="Electron acceptors">electron acceptors</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrite" title="Nitrite">nitrite</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metals" title="Metals">metals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coupled_to_sulfate_reduction">Coupled to sulfate reduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaerobic_oxidation_of_methane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Coupled to sulfate reduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AOM_Overview.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="997" data-file-width="512" height="487" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/AOM_Overview.svg/250px-AOM_Overview.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/AOM_Overview.svg/375px-AOM_Overview.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/AOM_Overview.svg/500px-AOM_Overview.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AOM_Overview.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Three mechanisms of Anaerobic Oxidation of Methane (AOM). The first method (top) is mediated by a consortium of anaerobic methanotrophic (ANME) archaea from the clades 1,2a,2b &amp; 2c and sulfate reducing bacteria (SRB). The oxidation of methane occurs in the ANME where electrons are passed directly to the SRB, which performs sulfate reduction <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid26375009_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid26375009-2">[2]</a></sup> .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wegener2015_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wegener2015-3">[3]</a></sup> The second method (middle) links methane oxidation with nitrate reduction, mediated by consortia of ANME archaea and Anammox bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid23892779_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid23892779-4">[4]</a></sup> The third mechanism (bottom) also links methane oxidation with nitrate reduction but is mediated by ANME archaea and NC10 bacteria. Unlike the first two mechanisms, both the ANME archaea and NC10 bacteria compete for methane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The overall reaction is:
</p>
<dl><dd>CH<sub>4</sub> + SO<sub>4</sub><sup>2−</sup> → HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup> + HS<sup>−</sup> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Sulfate-driven AOM is mediated by a syntrophic consortium of methanotrophic <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulfate-reducing_bacteria" title="Sulfate-reducing bacteria">sulfate-reducing bacteria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> They often form small aggregates or sometimes voluminous mats. The archaeal partner is abbreviated ANME, which stands for "anaerobic <a href="/wiki/Methanotroph" title="Methanotroph">methanotroph</a>". ANME's are very closely related to methanogenic archaea and recent investigations suggest that AOM is an enzymatic reversal of <a href="/wiki/Methanogenesis" title="Methanogenesis">methanogenesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid20520712_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid20520712-7">[7]</a></sup> It is still poorly understood how the syntrophic partners interact and which intermediates are exchanged between the archaeal and bacterial cell. The research on AOM is hindered by the fact that the responsible organisms have not been isolated. This is because these organisms show very slow growth rates with a minimum doubling time of a few months.
Countless isolation efforts have not been able to isolate one of the anaerobic methanotrophs, a possible explanation can be that the ANME archaea and the SRB have an obligate syntrophic interaction and can therefore not be isolated individually.
</p><p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benthic" title="Benthic">benthic</a> marine areas with strong methane releases from fossil reservoirs (e.g. at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cold_seeps" title="Cold seeps">cold seeps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mud_volcano" title="Mud volcano">mud volcanoes</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_hydrate" title="Gas hydrate">gas hydrate</a> deposits) AOM can be so high that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemosynthetic" title="Chemosynthetic">chemosynthetic</a> organisms like filamentous sulfur bacteria (see <a href="/wiki/Beggiatoa" title="Beggiatoa">Beggiatoa</a>) or animals (clams, tube worms) with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symbiont" title="Symbiont">symbiont</a> sulfide-oxidizing bacteria can thrive on the large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a> that are produced during AOM. The production of <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> from AOM can result in the precipitation of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> or so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Authigenic" title="Authigenic">authigenic</a> carbonates. These authigenic carbonates are known to be the most <sup>13</sup>C depleted carbonates on Earth, with δ<sup>13</sup>C values as low as -125 per mil <a href="/wiki/%CE%9413C#Reference_standard" title="Δ13C">PDB</a> reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coupled_to_nitrate_and_nitrite_reduction">Coupled to nitrate and nitrite reduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaerobic_oxidation_of_methane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Coupled to nitrate and nitrite reduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The overall reactions are:
</p>
<dl><dd>CH<sub>4</sub> + 4NO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup> → CO<sub>2</sub> + 4NO<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> + 2H<sub>2</sub>O</dd>
<dd>3CH<sub>4</sub> + 8NO<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> + 8H<sup>+</sup> → 3CO<sub>2</sub> + 4N<sub>2</sub> + 10H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Recently, ANME-2d is shown to be responsible nitrate-driven AOM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid23892779_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid23892779-4">[4]</a></sup> The ANME-2d, named <i>Methanoperedens nitroreducens</i>, is able to perform nitrate-driven AOM without a partner organism via reverse methanogenesis with nitrate as the terminal electron acceptor, using genes for nitrate reduction that have been laterally transferred from a bacterial donor. This was also the first complete reverse methanogenesis pathway including the <i>mcr</i> and <i>mer</i> genes.
</p><p>In 2010, omics analysis showed that nitrite reduction can be coupled to methane oxidation by a single bacterial species, NC10, without the need for an archaeal partner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid20336137_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid20336137-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_relevance">Environmental relevance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anaerobic_oxidation_of_methane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Environmental relevance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>AOM is considered to be a very important process reducing the emission of the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> methane from the ocean into the atmosphere. It is estimated that almost 80% of all the methane that arises from marine sediments is oxidized anaerobically by this process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reeburgh2007_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reeburgh2007-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
