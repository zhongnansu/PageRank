<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Plantation</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pinus_taeda_plantation.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2336" data-file-width="3504" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Pinus_taeda_plantation.jpg/220px-Pinus_taeda_plantation.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Pinus_taeda_plantation.jpg/330px-Pinus_taeda_plantation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Pinus_taeda_plantation.jpg/440px-Pinus_taeda_plantation.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pinus_taeda_plantation.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pine</a> plantation in the United States. Tree plantations are usually easily distinguished from natural forests by the trees being planted in straight lines.</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>plantation</b> is the large-scale estate meant for farming that specializes in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cash_crops" title="Cash crops">cash crops</a>. The crops that are grown include <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>, 
<a href="/wiki/Coffee" title="Coffee">coffee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tea" title="Tea">tea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cocoa_bean" title="Cocoa bean">cocoa</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sugar_cane" title="Sugar cane">sugar cane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sisal" title="Sisal">sisal</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_seeds" title="Oil seeds">oil seeds</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_palm" title="Oil palm">oil palms</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rubber_trees" title="Rubber trees">rubber trees</a>, and fruits. <a href="/wiki/Protectionism" title="Protectionism">Protectionist</a> policies and natural <a href="/wiki/Comparative_advantage" title="Comparative advantage">comparative advantage</a> have sometimes contributed to determining where plantations were located.
</p><p><span id="Plantation_house"></span>
A <b>plantation house</b> is the main house of a plantation, often a substantial <a href="/wiki/Farmhouse" title="Farmhouse">farmhouse</a>, which often serves as a symbol for the plantation as a whole.  <a href="/wiki/Plantation_complexes_in_the_Southern_United_States#Plantation_house" title="Plantation complexes in the Southern United States">Plantation houses in the Southern United States</a> and in other areas are known as quite grand and expensive architectural works today, partially due to <a href="/wiki/Survivorship_bias" title="Survivorship bias">survivorship bias</a> and the rise of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_cotton" title="King cotton">king cotton</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Antebellum_South" title="Antebellum South">Antebellum South</a>, though most were more utilitarian, working farmhouses.
</p><p>Among the earliest examples of plantations were the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latifundia" title="Latifundia">latifundia</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, which produced large quantities of wine and olive oil for export. Plantation agriculture grew rapidly with the increase in international trade and the development of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Globalisation" title="Globalisation">worldwide economy</a> that followed the expansion of <a href="/wiki/Colonialism" title="Colonialism">European colonial empires</a>. Like every economic activity, it has changed over time. Earlier forms of plantation agriculture were associated with large disparities of wealth and income, foreign ownership and political influence, and exploitative social systems such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indentured_labor" title="Indentured labor">indentured labor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">slavery</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forest_plantations">Forest plantations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plantation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Forest plantations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Silvtrees.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="190" data-file-width="301" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Silvtrees.jpg/220px-Silvtrees.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f8/Silvtrees.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Silvtrees.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A plantation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coast_Douglas-fir" title="Coast Douglas-fir">Douglas-fir</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a></div></div></div>
<p>Industrial plantations are established to produce a high volume of wood in a short period of time. Plantations are grown by state forestry authorities (for example, the <a href="/wiki/Forestry_Commission" title="Forestry Commission">Forestry Commission</a> in Britain) and/or the paper and wood industries and other private landowners (such as <a href="/wiki/Weyerhaeuser" title="Weyerhaeuser">Weyerhaeuser</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rayonier" title="Rayonier">Rayonier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Pacific_Industries" title="Sierra Pacific Industries">Sierra Pacific Industries</a> in the United States, <a href="/wiki/Asia_Pulp_%26_Paper" title="Asia Pulp &amp; Paper">Asia Pulp &amp; Paper</a> in Indonesia). <a href="/wiki/Christmas_tree" title="Christmas tree">Christmas trees</a> are often grown on plantations as well. In southern and southeastern Asia, <a href="/wiki/Teak" title="Teak">teak</a> plantations have recently replaced the natural forest.
</p><p>Industrial plantations are actively managed for the commercial production of forest products. Industrial plantations are usually large-scale. Individual blocks are usually even-aged and often consist of just one or two species. These species can be exotic or indigenous. The plants used for the plantation are often genetically altered for desired traits such as growth and resistance to pests and diseases in general and specific traits, for example in the case of timber species, volumic wood production and stem straightness. <a href="/wiki/Forest_genetic_resources" title="Forest genetic resources">Forest genetic resources</a> are the basis for genetic alteration. Selected individuals grown in <a href="/wiki/Seed_orchard" title="Seed orchard">seed orchards</a> are a good source for seeds to develop adequate planting material.
</p><p>Wood production on a tree plantation is generally higher than that of natural forests. While forests managed for wood production commonly yield between 1 and 3 cubic meters per hectare per year, plantations of fast-growing species commonly yield between 20 and 30 cubic meters or more per hectare annually; a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_Fir" title="Grand Fir">Grand Fir</a> plantation at Craigvinean in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> has a growth rate of 34 cubic meters per hectare per year (Aldhous &amp; Low 1974), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monterey_Pine" title="Monterey Pine">Monterey Pine</a> plantations in southern <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> can yield up to 40 cubic meters per hectare per year (Everard &amp; Fourt 1974). In 2000, while plantations accounted for 5% of global forest, it is estimated that they supplied about 35% of the world's roundwood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth_cycle">Growth cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plantation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Growth cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>In the first year, the ground is prepared usually by the combination of burning, herbicide spraying, and/or <a href="/wiki/Tillage" title="Tillage">cultivation</a> and then saplings are planted by human crew or by machine. The saplings are usually obtained in bulk from industrial nurseries, which may specialize in selective breeding in order to produce fast growing disease- and pest-resistant strains.</li>
<li>In the first few years until the canopy closes, the saplings are looked after, and may be dusted or sprayed with fertilizers or pesticides until established.</li>
<li>After the canopy closes, with the tree crowns touching each other, the plantation is becoming dense and crowded, and tree growth is slowing due to competition. This stage is termed 'pole stage'. When competition becomes too intense (for <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pine</a> trees, when the <a href="/wiki/Live_crown" title="Live crown">live crown</a> is less than a third of the tree's total height), it is time to thin out the section. There are several methods for <a href="/wiki/Thinning" title="Thinning">thinning</a>, but where topography permits, the most popular is 'row-thinning', where every third or fourth or fifth row of trees is removed, usually with a <a href="/wiki/Harvester_(forestry)" title="Harvester (forestry)">harvester</a>. Many trees are <a href="/wiki/Thinning" title="Thinning">removed</a>, leaving regular clear lanes through the section so that the remaining trees have room to expand again. The removed trees are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delimbing" title="Delimbing">delimbed</a>, forwarded to the forest road, loaded onto trucks, and sent to a mill. A typical pole stage plantation tree is 7–30 cm in <a href="/wiki/Diameter_at_breast_height" title="Diameter at breast height">diameter at breast height</a> (dbh). Such trees are sometimes not suitable for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timber" title="Timber">timber</a>, but are used as pulp for paper and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particleboard" title="Particleboard">particleboard</a>, and as chips for <a href="/wiki/Oriented_strand_board" title="Oriented strand board">oriented strand board</a>.</li>
<li>As the trees grow and become dense and crowded again, the thinning process is repeated. Depending on growth rate and species, trees at this age may be large enough for timber milling; if not, they are again used as pulp and chips.</li>
<li>Around year 10-60 the plantation is now mature and (in economic terms) is falling off the back side of its growth curve. That is to say, it is passing the point of maximum wood growth per hectare per year, and so is ready for the final harvest. All remaining trees are felled, delimbed, and taken to be processed.</li>
<li>The ground is cleared, and the cycle can be restarted.</li></ul>
<p>Some plantation trees, such as pines and eucalyptus, can be at high risk of fire damage because their leaf oils and resins are flammable to the point of a tree being explosive under some conditions<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Conversely, an afflicted plantation can in some cases be cleared of pest species cheaply through the use of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prescribed_burn" title="Prescribed burn">prescribed burn</a>, which kills all lesser plants but does not significantly harm the mature trees.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_forest_loss">Natural forest loss</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plantation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Natural forest loss">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many forestry experts claim that the establishment of plantations will reduce or eliminate the need to exploit natural forest for wood production. In principle this is true because due to the high productivity of plantations less land is needed. Many point to the example of New Zealand, where 19% of the forest area provides 99% of the supply of industrial round wood. It has been estimated that the world's demand for fiber could be met by just 5% of the world forest (Sedjo &amp; Botkin 1997). However, in practice, plantations are replacing natural forest, for example in Indonesia. According to the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">FAO</a>, about 7% of the natural closed forest being lost in the tropics is land being converted to plantations. The remaining 93% of the loss is land being converted to agriculture and other uses. Worldwide, an estimated 15% of plantations in tropical countries are established on closed canopy natural forest.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a>, there are proposals encouraging the use of plantations to reduce <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> levels (though this idea is being challenged by some groups on the grounds that the sequestered CO<sub>2</sub> is eventually released after harvest).
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms_of_plantations">Criticisms of plantations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plantation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Criticisms of plantations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In contrast to a naturally regenerated forest, plantations are typically grown as even-aged <a href="/wiki/Monoculture" title="Monoculture">monocultures</a>, primarily for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timber" title="Timber">timber</a> production.
</p>
<ul><li>Plantations are usually near- or total monocultures. That is, the same species of tree is planted across a given area, whereas a natural forest would contain a far more diverse range of tree species.</li>
<li>Plantations may include tree species that would not naturally occur in the area. They may include unconventional types such as hybrids, and <a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_tree" title="Genetically modified tree">genetically modified trees</a> may be used sometime in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Since the primary interest in plantations is to produce <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wood_pulp" title="Wood pulp">pulp</a>, the types of trees found in plantations are those that are best-suited to industrial applications. For example, <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spruce" title="Spruce">spruce</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus" title="Eucalyptus">eucalyptus</a> are widely planted far beyond their natural range because of their fast growth rate, tolerance of rich or degraded agricultural land and potential to produce large volumes of raw material for industrial use.</li>
<li>Plantations are always young forests in ecological terms. Typically, trees grown in plantations are harvested after 10 to 60 years, rarely up to 120 years. This means that the forests produced by plantations do not contain the type of growth, soil or wildlife typical of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_growth" title="Old growth">old-growth natural forest</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecosystems" title="Ecosystems">ecosystems</a>. Most conspicuous is the absence of decaying dead wood, a crucial component of natural forest ecosystems.</li></ul>
<p>In the 1970s, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> began to establish high-yield, intensively managed, short rotation plantations. These types of plantations are sometimes called fast-wood plantations or fiber farms and often managed on a short-rotation basis, as little as 5 to 15 years. They are becoming more widespread in South America, Asia and other areas. The environmental and social impacts of this type of plantation has caused them to become controversial. In <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, for example, large multi-national pulp companies have harvested large areas of natural forest without regard for regeneration. From 1980 to 2000, about 50% of the 1.4 million hectares of pulpwood plantations in Indonesia have been established on what was formerly natural forest land.
</p><p>The replacement of natural forest with tree plantations has also caused social problems. In some countries, again, notably Indonesia, conversions of natural forest are made with little regard for rights of the local people. Plantations established purely for the production of fiber provide a much narrower range of services than the original natural forest for the local people. <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> has sought to limit this damage by limiting the amount of land owned by one entity and, as a result, smaller plantations are owned by local farmers who then sell the wood to larger companies. Some large environmental organizations are critical of these high-yield plantations and are running an anti-plantation campaign, notably the <a href="/wiki/Rainforest_Action_Network" title="Rainforest Action Network">Rainforest Action Network</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a>.
==
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Farm_and_home">Farm and home</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plantation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Farm and home">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Farm or home plantations are typically established for the production of timber and fire wood for home use and sometimes for sale. Management may be less intensive than with Industrial plantations. In time, this type of plantation can become difficult to distinguish from naturally regenerated forest.
</p><p>Teak and bamboo plantations in India have given good results and an alternative crop solution to farmers of central India, where conventional farming was popular. But due to rising input costs of farming many farmers have done teak and bamboo plantations which require very little water (only during first two years). Teak and bamboo have legal protection from theft. Bamboo, once planted, gives output for 50 years till flowering occurs. Teak requires 20 years to grow to full maturity and fetch returns.
</p><p>These may be established for watershed or soil protection. They are established for erosion control, landslide stabilization and windbreaks. Such plantations are established to foster native species and promote forest regeneration on degraded lands as a tool of <a href="/wiki/Environmental_restoration" title="Environmental restoration">environmental restoration</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_impact">Ecological impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plantation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ecological impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Probably the single most important factor a plantation has on the local environment is the site where the plantation is established. If natural forest is cleared for a planted forest then a reduction in <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> and loss of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitat_(ecology)" title="Habitat (ecology)">habitat</a> will likely result. In some cases, their establishment may involve draining <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a> to replace mixed <a href="/wiki/Hardwood" title="Hardwood">hardwoods</a> that formerly predominated with pine species.
If a plantation is established on abandoned agricultural land, or highly degraded land, it can result in an increase in both habitat and biodiversity. A planted forest can be profitably established on lands that will not support agriculture or suffer from lack of natural regeneration.
</p><p>The tree species used in a plantation is also an important factor. Where non-native varieties or species are grown, few of the native fauna are adapted to exploit these and further <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_loss" title="Biodiversity loss">biodiversity loss</a> occurs. However, even non-native tree species may serve as <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_corridor" title="Wildlife corridor">corridors</a> for wildlife and act as a buffer for native forest, reducing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edge_effect" title="Edge effect">edge effect</a>.
</p><p>Once a plantation is established, how it is managed becomes the important environmental factor. The single most important factor of management is the rotation period. Plantations harvested on longer rotation periods (30 years or more) can provide similar benefits to a naturally regenerated forest managed for wood production, on a similar rotation. This is especially true if native species are used. In the case of exotic species, the habitat can be improved significantly if the impact is mitigated by measures such as leaving blocks of native species in the plantation, or retaining corridors of natural forest. In Brazil, similar measures are required by government regulation
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sugar">Sugar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plantation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sugar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sugar_plantations_in_the_Caribbean" title="Sugar plantations in the Caribbean">Sugar plantations in the Caribbean</a></div>
<p>Sugar plantations were highly valued in the Caribbean by the British and French colonists in the 17th and 18th centuries and the use of sugar in Europe rose during this period. Sugarcane is still an important crop in Cuba. Sugar plantations also arose in countries such as Barbados and Cuba because of the natural endowments that they had. These natural endowments included soil that was conducive to growing sugar and a high marginal product of labor realized through the increasing number of slaves.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fishing">Fishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plantation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Fishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colony_of_Newfoundland" title="Colony of Newfoundland">Newfoundland</a> was colonized by <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> in 1610, the original colonists were called "Planters" and their fishing rooms were known as "fishing plantations". These terms were used well into the 20th century.
</p><p>The following three plantations are maintained by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Government of Newfoundland and Labrador">Government of Newfoundland and Labrador</a> as provincial heritage sites:
</p>
<ul><li>Sea-Forest Plantation was a 17th-century fishing plantation established at Cuper's Cove (present-day <a href="/wiki/Cupids" title="Cupids">Cupids</a>) under a <a href="/wiki/Royal_charter" title="Royal charter">royal charter</a> issued by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" title="James I of England">King James I</a>.</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mockbeggar_Plantation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mockbeggar Plantation (page does not exist)">Mockbeggar Plantation</a> is an 18th-century fishing plantation at <a href="/wiki/Bonavista,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Bonavista, Newfoundland and Labrador">Bonavista</a>.</li>
<li>Pool Plantation a 17th-century fishing plantation maintained by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_David_Kirke" title="Sir David Kirke">Sir David Kirke</a> and his heirs at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferryland,_Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Ferryland, Newfoundland and Labrador">Ferryland</a>. The plantation was destroyed by <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> invaders in 1696.</li></ul>
<p>Other fishing plantations:
</p>
<ul><li>Bristol's Hope Plantation, a 17th-century fishing plantation established at Harbour Grace, created by the Bristol Society of Merchant-Adventurers.</li>
<li>Benger Plantation, an 18th-century fishing plantation maintained by James Benger and his heirs at Ferryland. It was built on the site of Georgia plantation.</li>
<li>Piggeon's Plantation, an 18th-century fishing plantation maintained by Ellias Piggeon at Ferryland.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Slavery">Slavery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plantation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Slavery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Plantation_economy" title="Plantation economy">Plantation economy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">Slavery</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CottonpickHoustonWhere17.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1070" data-file-width="1676" height="140" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/CottonpickHoustonWhere17.png/220px-CottonpickHoustonWhere17.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/CottonpickHoustonWhere17.png/330px-CottonpickHoustonWhere17.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/CottonpickHoustonWhere17.png/440px-CottonpickHoustonWhere17.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CottonpickHoustonWhere17.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1913 photo: African-Americans picking cotton on a plantation in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">South</a></div></div></div>
<p>African <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slave_labour" title="Slave labour">slave labour</a> was used extensively to work on early plantations (such as tobacco, rice, cotton, and sugar plantations) in the American colonies and the United States, throughout the Caribbean, the Americas, and in European-occupied areas of Africa. Several notable historians and economists such as <a href="/wiki/Eric_Williams" title="Eric Williams">Eric Williams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Rodney" title="Walter Rodney">Walter Rodney</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a> contend that the global <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capitalist" title="Capitalist">capitalist</a> economy was largely founded upon the creation and produce of thousands of slave <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labor_camps" title="Labor camps">labor camps</a> based in <a href="/wiki/Colonialism" title="Colonialism">colonial</a> plantations, exploiting tens of millions of purchased Africans.
</p><p>In modern times, the low wages typically paid to plantation workers are the basis of plantation profitability in some areas.
</p><p>In more recent times, overt slavery has been replaced by <i>para-slavery</i> or <i>slavery-in-kind</i>, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sharecropping_system" title="Sharecropping system">sharecropping system</a>. At its most extreme, workers are in "<a href="/wiki/Debt_bondage" title="Debt bondage">debt bondage</a>": they must work to pay off a debt at such punitive interest rates that it may never be paid off. Others work unreasonably long hours and are paid subsistence wages that (in practice) may only be spent in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Truck_system" title="Truck system">company store</a>.
</p><p>In Brazil, a sugarcane plantation was termed an <i><a href="/wiki/Engenho" title="Engenho">engenho</a></i> ("engine"), and the 17th-century English usage for organized colonial production was "factory." Such colonial social and economic structures are discussed at <a href="/wiki/Plantation_economy" title="Plantation economy">Plantation economy</a>.
</p><p>Sugar workers on plantations in <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> and elsewhere in the Caribbean lived in <a href="/wiki/Company_town" title="Company town">company towns</a> known as <i><a href="/wiki/Batey_(sugar_workers%27_town)" title="Batey (sugar workers' town)">bateyes</a></i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Antebellum_American_South">Antebellum American South</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plantation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Antebellum American South">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Plantations_in_the_American_South" title="Plantations in the American South">Plantations in the American South</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plantation_complexes_in_the_Southeastern_United_States" title="Plantation complexes in the Southeastern United States">Plantation complexes in the Southeastern United States</a></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">American South</a>, <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_United_States_(1789%E2%80%931849)" title="History of the United States (1789–1849)">antebellum</a> plantations were centered on a "<a href="/wiki/List_of_plantations_in_the_United_States" title="List of plantations in the United States">plantation house</a>," the residence of the owner, where important business was conducted. Slavery and plantations had different characteristics in different regions of the South. As the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_South" title="Upper South">Upper South</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a> colonies developed first, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historians" title="Historians">historians</a> of the antebellum South defined planters as those who held 20 or more slaves. Major planters held many more, especially in the Deep South as it developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peter_Kolchin_1993_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Peter_Kolchin_1993-3">[3]</a></sup> The majority of slaveholders held 10 or fewer slaves, often just a few to labor domestically. By the late 18th century, most planters in the Upper South had switched from exclusive tobacco cultivation to mixed-crop production, both because tobacco had exhausted the soil and because of changing markets. The shift away from tobacco meant they had slaves in excess of the number needed for labor, and they began to sell them in the internal slave trade.
</p><p>There was a variety of domestic architecture on plantations. The largest and wealthiest planter families, for instance, those with estates fronting on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_River_(Virginia)" title="James River (Virginia)">James River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, constructed mansions in brick and Georgian style, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Shirley_Plantation" title="Shirley Plantation">Shirley Plantation</a>. Common or smaller planters in the late 18th and 19th century had more modest wood frame buildings, such as <a href="/wiki/Piney_Grove_at_Southall%27s_Plantation" title="Piney Grove at Southall's Plantation">Southall Plantation</a> in <a href="/wiki/Charles_City_County,_Virginia" title="Charles City County, Virginia">Charles City County</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Schriever_LA_Jackson_Plantation_House_1940.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="380" data-file-width="524" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Schriever_LA_Jackson_Plantation_House_1940.jpg/220px-Schriever_LA_Jackson_Plantation_House_1940.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Schriever_LA_Jackson_Plantation_House_1940.jpg/330px-Schriever_LA_Jackson_Plantation_House_1940.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Schriever_LA_Jackson_Plantation_House_1940.jpg/440px-Schriever_LA_Jackson_Plantation_House_1940.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Schriever_LA_Jackson_Plantation_House_1940.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Old Jackson Plantation house, <a href="/wiki/Schriever,_Louisiana" title="Schriever, Louisiana">Schriever</a>, <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, June 1940</div></div></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina_Lowcountry" title="South Carolina Lowcountry">Lowcountry</a> of <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, by contrast, even before the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>, planters holding large rice plantations typically owned hundreds of slaves. In Charleston and Savannah, the elite also held numerous slaves to work as household servants. The 19th-century development of the <a href="/wiki/Deep_South" title="Deep South">Deep South</a> for cotton cultivation depended on large plantations with much more acreage than was typical of the Upper South; and for labor, planters held hundreds of slaves.
</p><p>Until December 1865 <a href="/wiki/Slavery_in_the_United_States" title="Slavery in the United States">slavery</a> was legal in parts of the United States. Most slaves were employed in agriculture, and <i>planter</i> was a term commonly used to describe a farmer with many slaves.
</p><p>The term planter has no universally-accepted definition, but academic historians have defined it to identify the elite class, "a landowning farmer of substantial means."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peter_Kolchin_1993_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Peter_Kolchin_1993-3">[3]</a></sup>  In the "<a href="/wiki/Black_Belt_(U.S._region)" title="Black Belt (U.S. region)">Black Belt</a>" counties of <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>, the terms "planter" and "farmer" were often synonymous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oakes_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oakes-4">[4]</a></sup> Robert Fogel and Stanley Engerman define large planters as owning over 50 slaves, and medium planters as owning between 16 and 50 slaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fogel_and_Engerman_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fogel_and_Engerman-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>In his study of Black Belt counties in Alabama, Jonathan Wiener defines planters by ownership of real property, rather than of slaves. A planter, for Wiener, owned at least $10,000 worth of real estate in 1850 and $32,000 worth in 1860, equivalent to about the top 8 percent of landowners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiener_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wiener-6">[6]</a></sup> In his study of southwest Georgia, Lee Formwalt also defines planters in size of land holdings rather than slaves. Formwalt's planters are in the top 4.5 percent of land owners, translating into real estate worth $6,000 or more in 1850, $24,000 or more in 1860, and $11,000 or more in 1870.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Formwalt_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Formwalt-7">[7]</a></sup> In his study of <a href="/wiki/Harrison_County,_Texas" title="Harrison County, Texas">Harrison County, Texas</a>, Randolph B. Campbell classifies large planters as owners of 20 slaves, and small planters as owners of between ten and 19 slaves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Campbell_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell-8">[8]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Chicot_County,_Arkansas" title="Chicot County, Arkansas">Chicot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phillips_County,_Arkansas" title="Phillips County, Arkansas">Phillips</a> counties, Arkansas, Carl H. Moneyhon defines large planters as owners of twenty or more slaves, and six hundred or more acres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moneyhon_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moneyhon-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
