<p>A <b>grape</b> is a fruit, botanically a  <a href="/wiki/Berry_(botany)" title="Berry (botany)">berry</a>, of the <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a> woody <a href="/wiki/Vine" title="Vine">vines</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">flowering plant</a> <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Vitis" title="Vitis">Vitis</a></i>.
</p><p>Grapes can be eaten fresh as <a href="/wiki/Table_grape" title="Table grape">table grapes</a> or they can be used for making wine, jam, juice, jelly, grape seed extract, raisins, vinegar, and grape seed oil. Grapes are a non-<a href="/wiki/Climacteric_(botany)" title="Climacteric (botany)">climacteric</a> type of fruit, generally occurring in clusters.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>The cultivation of the domesticated grape began 6,000–8,000 years ago in the <a href="/wiki/Near_East" title="Near East">Near East</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-This_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-This-1">[1]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yeast_(wine)" title="Yeast (wine)">Yeast</a>, one of the earliest domesticated <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a>, occurs naturally on the skins of grapes, leading to the discovery of alcoholic drinks such as wine. The earliest archeological evidence for a dominant position of wine-making in human culture dates from 8,000 years ago in <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">Georgia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Areni-1_winery" title="Areni-1 winery">oldest known winery</a> was found in <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a>, dating to around 4000 BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> By the 9th century AD the city of <a href="/wiki/Shiraz" title="Shiraz">Shiraz</a> was known to produce some of the finest wines in the Middle East. Thus it has been proposed that <a href="/wiki/Syrah" title="Syrah">Syrah</a> red wine is named after <a href="/wiki/Shiraz" title="Shiraz">Shiraz</a>, a city in Persia where the grape was used to make <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shirazi_wine" title="Shirazi wine">Shirazi wine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hugh_J_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hugh_J-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egyptian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hieroglyphics" title="Hieroglyphics">hieroglyphics</a> record the cultivation of purple grapes, and history attests to the ancient <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greeks_(wine)" title="Greeks (wine)">Greeks</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phoenicians_(wine)" title="Phoenicians (wine)">Phoenicians</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome_(wine)" title="Ancient Rome (wine)">Romans</a> growing purple grapes for both eating and wine production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The growing of grapes would later spread to other regions in Europe, as well as North Africa, and eventually in North America.
</p><p>In North America, native grapes belonging to various species of the genus <i>Vitis</i> proliferate in the wild across the continent, and were a part of the diet of many <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Native Americans</a>, but were considered by European colonists to be unsuitable for wine. <i>Vitis vinifera</i> cultivars were imported for that purpose.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span></h2>
<p>Grapes are a type of <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a> that grow in clusters of 15 to 300, and can be crimson, black, dark blue, yellow, green, orange, and pink. "White" grapes are actually green in color, and are evolutionarily derived from the purple grape. <a href="/wiki/Mutation" title="Mutation">Mutations</a> in two regulatory genes of white grapes turn off production of <a href="/wiki/Anthocyanin" title="Anthocyanin">anthocyanins</a>, which are responsible for the color of purple grapes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Anthocyanins and other <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigment</a> chemicals of the larger family of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyphenols_in_grape" title="Polyphenols in grape">polyphenols</a> in purple grapes are responsible for the varying shades of purple in red wines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Grapes are typically an ellipsoid shape resembling a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prolate_spheroid" title="Prolate spheroid">prolate spheroid</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grapevines">Grapevines</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Vitis" title="Vitis">Vitis</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ConcordGrapes.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="705" data-file-width="659" height="182" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/ConcordGrapes.jpg/170px-ConcordGrapes.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/ConcordGrapes.jpg/255px-ConcordGrapes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/ConcordGrapes.jpg/340px-ConcordGrapes.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ConcordGrapes.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Concord is a variety of North American <i>labrusca</i> grape</div></div></div>
<p>Most grapes come from <a href="/wiki/List_of_grape_varieties" title="List of grape varieties">cultivars</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Vitis_vinifera" title="Vitis vinifera">Vitis vinifera</a></i>, the European grapevine native to the Mediterranean and Central Asia. Minor amounts of fruit and wine come from American and Asian species such as:
</p>
<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Vitis_amurensis" title="Vitis amurensis">Vitis amurensis</a></i>, the most important Asian species</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Vitis_labrusca" title="Vitis labrusca">Vitis labrusca</a></i>, the North American table and grape juice grapevines (including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concord_(grape)" title="Concord (grape)">Concord</a> <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivar</a>), sometimes used for wine, are native to the Eastern United States and Canada.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Vitis_mustangensis" title="Vitis mustangensis">Vitis mustangensis</a></i>, (the mustang grape) found in Mississippi, Alabama, Louisiana, Texas, and Oklahoma</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Vitis_riparia" title="Vitis riparia">Vitis riparia</a></i>, a wild vine of North America, is sometimes used for winemaking and for jam. It is native to the entire Eastern U.S. and north to <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Vitis_rotundifolia" title="Vitis rotundifolia">Vitis rotundifolia</a></i> (the muscadines) used for jams and wine, are native to the Southeastern United States from <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_production">Distribution and production</span></h2>
<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:702px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Top_grapes_countries_producers_in_the_world.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="625" data-file-width="1425" height="307" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Top_grapes_countries_producers_in_the_world.png/700px-Top_grapes_countries_producers_in_the_world.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Top_grapes_countries_producers_in_the_world.png/1050px-Top_grapes_countries_producers_in_the_world.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Top_grapes_countries_producers_in_the_world.png/1400px-Top_grapes_countries_producers_in_the_world.png 2x" width="700"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Top_grapes_countries_producers_in_the_world.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Top 20 grape producing countries in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></div></div></div></div>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">Food and Agriculture Organization</a> (FAO), 75,866 square kilometers of the world are dedicated to grapes. Approximately 71% of world grape production is used for wine, 27% as fresh fruit, and 2% as <a href="/wiki/Dried_fruit" title="Dried fruit">dried fruit</a>. A portion of grape production goes to producing grape juice to be reconstituted for fruits canned "with no added sugar" and "100% natural". The area dedicated to vineyards is increasing by about 2% per year.
</p><p>There are no reliable statistics that break down grape production by variety. It is believed that the most widely planted variety is <a href="/wiki/Sultana_(grape)" title="Sultana (grape)">Sultana</a>, also known as Thompson Seedless, with at least 3,600 km<sup>2</sup> (880,000 acres) dedicated to it. The second most common variety is <a href="/wiki/Air%C3%A9n" title="Airén">Airén</a>. Other popular varieties include <a href="/wiki/Cabernet_Sauvignon" title="Cabernet Sauvignon">Cabernet Sauvignon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sauvignon_blanc" title="Sauvignon blanc">Sauvignon blanc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cabernet_Franc" title="Cabernet Franc">Cabernet Franc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Merlot" title="Merlot">Merlot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grenache" title="Grenache">Grenache</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tempranillo" title="Tempranillo">Tempranillo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Riesling" title="Riesling">Riesling</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chardonnay" title="Chardonnay">Chardonnay</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Table_and_wine_grapes">Table and wine grapes</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wine_grapes03.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1060" height="257" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Wine_grapes03.jpg/170px-Wine_grapes03.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Wine_grapes03.jpg/255px-Wine_grapes03.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Wine_grapes03.jpg/340px-Wine_grapes03.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wine_grapes03.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Wine grapes on the vine</div></div></div>
<p>Commercially cultivated grapes can usually be classified as either <a href="/wiki/Table_grape" title="Table grape">table</a> or wine grapes, based on their intended method of consumption: eaten raw (table grapes) or used to make <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a> (wine grapes). While almost all of them belong to the same species, <i><a href="/wiki/Vitis_vinifera" title="Vitis vinifera">Vitis vinifera</a></i>, table and wine grapes have significant differences, brought about through <a href="/wiki/Selective_breeding" title="Selective breeding">selective breeding</a>. Table grape cultivars tend to have large, seedless fruit (see below) with relatively thin skin. Wine grapes are smaller, usually seeded, and have relatively thick skins (a desirable characteristic in winemaking, since much of the aroma in wine comes from the skin). Wine grapes also tend to be very sweet: they are harvested at the time when their juice is approximately 24% sugar by weight. By comparison, commercially produced "100% grape juice", made from table grapes, is usually around 15% sugar by weight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Seedless_grapes">Seedless grapes</span></h2>
<p>Seedless cultivars now make up the overwhelming majority of table grape plantings. Because grapevines are <a href="/wiki/Vegetative_reproduction" title="Vegetative reproduction">vegetatively propagated</a> by cuttings, the lack of seeds does not present a problem for reproduction. It is an issue for breeders, who must either use a seeded variety as the female parent or rescue embryos early in development using <a href="/wiki/Plant_tissue_culture" title="Plant tissue culture">tissue culture</a> techniques.
</p><p>There are several sources of the seedlessness trait, and essentially all commercial cultivators get it from one of three sources: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thompson_Seedless" title="Thompson Seedless">Thompson Seedless</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Russian_Seedless&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Russian Seedless (page does not exist)">Russian Seedless</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Black_Monukka&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Black Monukka (page does not exist)">Black Monukka</a>, all being cultivars of <i><a href="/wiki/Vitis_vinifera" title="Vitis vinifera">Vitis vinifera</a></i>. There are currently more than a dozen varieties of seedless grapes. Several, such as Einset Seedless, Benjamin Gunnels's Prime seedless grapes, Reliance, and Venus, have been specifically cultivated for hardiness and quality in the relatively cold climates of northeastern United States and southern <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>An offset to the improved eating quality of seedlessness is the loss of potential health benefits provided by the enriched <a href="/wiki/Phytochemical" title="Phytochemical">phytochemical</a> content of grape seeds (see <a href="#Health_claims">Health claims</a>, below).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Raisins.2C_currants_and_sultanas"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Raisins,_currants_and_sultanas">Raisins, currants and sultanas</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Raisin" title="Raisin">Raisin</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Raisins_01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2025" data-file-width="3223" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Raisins_01.jpg/220px-Raisins_01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Raisins_01.jpg/330px-Raisins_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Raisins_01.jpg/440px-Raisins_01.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Raisins_01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Raisin" title="Raisin">Raisins</a></div></div></div>
<p>In most of Europe and North America, dried grapes are referred to as "raisins" or the local equivalent. In the UK, three different varieties are recognized, forcing the EU to use the term "dried vine fruit" in official documents.
</p><p>A <i><a href="/wiki/Raisin" title="Raisin">raisin</a></i> is any dried grape. While <i>raisin</i> is a French <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">loanword</a>, the word in French refers to the fresh fruit; <i>grappe</i> (from which the English <i>grape</i> is derived) refers to the bunch (as in <i>une grappe de raisins</i>).
</p><p>A <i><a href="/wiki/Zante_currant" title="Zante currant">currant</a></i> is a dried <a href="/wiki/Zakynthos" title="Zakynthos">Zante</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_Corinth" title="Black Corinth">Black Corinth</a> grape, the name being a corruption of the French <i>raisin de Corinthe</i> (<a href="/wiki/Corinth" title="Corinth">Corinth</a> grape). <i>Currant</i> has also come to refer to the <a href="/wiki/Blackcurrant" title="Blackcurrant">blackcurrant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Redcurrant" title="Redcurrant">redcurrant</a>, two berries unrelated to grapes.
</p><p>A <i>sultana</i> was originally a raisin made from <a href="/wiki/Sultana_(grape)" title="Sultana (grape)">Sultana</a> grapes of Turkish origin (known as Thompson Seedless in the United States), but the word is now applied to raisins made from either white grapes or red grapes that are bleached to resemble the traditional sultana.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Juice">Juice</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Grape_Juice.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="1334" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Grape_Juice.jpg/170px-Grape_Juice.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Grape_Juice.jpg/255px-Grape_Juice.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Grape_Juice.jpg/340px-Grape_Juice.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Grape_Juice.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Grape juice</div></div></div>
<p>Grape juice is obtained from crushing and blending grapes into a liquid. The juice is often sold in stores or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(wine)" title="Fermentation (wine)">fermented</a> and made into <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brandy" title="Brandy">brandy</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Vinegar" title="Vinegar">vinegar</a>.  Grape juice that has been pasteurized, removing any naturally occurring yeast, will not ferment if kept sterile, and thus contains no alcohol.  In the <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a> industry, grape juice that contains 7–23% of pulp, skins, stems and seeds is often referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Must" title="Must">must</a>". In North America, the most common grape juice is purple and made from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concord_(grape)" title="Concord (grape)">Concord grapes</a>, while white grape juice is commonly made from <a href="/wiki/Niagara_(grape)" title="Niagara (grape)">Niagara grapes</a>, both of which are varieties of native American grapes, a different species from European wine grapes. In California, Sultana (known there as Thompson Seedless) grapes are sometimes diverted from the raisin or table market to produce white juice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_claims">Health claims</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Health_effects_of_wine" title="Health effects of wine">Health effects of wine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grape_therapy" title="Grape therapy">Grape therapy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Grape_and_raisin_toxicity_in_dogs" title="Grape and raisin toxicity in dogs">Grape and raisin toxicity in dogs</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="French_paradox">French paradox</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/French_paradox" title="French paradox">French paradox</a></div>
<p>Comparing diets among Western countries, researchers have discovered that although the French tend to eat higher levels of animal fat, the incidence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_disease" title="Heart disease">heart disease</a> remains low in France. This phenomenon has been termed the French paradox, and is thought to occur from protective benefits of regularly consuming red wine. Apart from potential benefits of alcohol itself, including reduced <a href="/wiki/Platelet" title="Platelet">platelet</a> aggregation and <a href="/wiki/Vasodilation" title="Vasodilation">vasodilation</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyphenols_in_grape" title="Polyphenols in grape">polyphenols</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Resveratrol" title="Resveratrol">resveratrol</a>) mainly in the grape skin provide other suspected health benefits, such as:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Alteration of molecular mechanisms in blood vessels, reducing susceptibility to vascular damage</li>
<li>Decreased activity of <a href="/wiki/Angiotensin" title="Angiotensin">angiotensin</a>, a systemic hormone causing blood vessel constriction that would elevate blood pressure</li>
<li>Increased production of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vasodilator" title="Vasodilator">vasodilator</a> hormone, <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a> (<a href="/wiki/Endothelium-derived_relaxing_factor" title="Endothelium-derived relaxing factor">endothelium-derived relaxing factor</a>)</li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Armenian_dolma.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="600" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Armenian_dolma.jpeg/200px-Armenian_dolma.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Armenian_dolma.jpeg/300px-Armenian_dolma.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Armenian_dolma.jpeg/400px-Armenian_dolma.jpeg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Armenian_dolma.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Using grape leaves in cuisine (<a href="/wiki/Dolma" title="Dolma">Dolma</a>)</div></div></div>
<p>Although adoption of wine consumption is not recommended by some health authorities,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> a significant volume of research indicates moderate consumption, such as one glass of red wine a day for women and two for men, may confer health benefits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Emerging evidence is that wine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyphenols_in_grape" title="Polyphenols in grape">polyphenols</a> such as resveratrol<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> provide physiological benefit, whereas alcohol itself may have protective effects on the cardiovascular system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> More may be seen in the article <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long-term_effects_of_alcohol" title="Long-term effects of alcohol">Long-term effects of alcohol</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Resveratrol">Resveratrol</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Resveratrol" title="Resveratrol">Resveratrol</a></div>
<p>Resveratrol is found in widely varying amounts among grape varieties, primarily in their skins and seeds, which, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muscadine" title="Muscadine">muscadine</a> grapes, have about one hundred times higher concentration than pulp.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guhqhr_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-guhqhr-27">[27]</a></sup> Fresh grape skin contains about 50 to 100 micrograms of resveratrol per gram.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anthocyanins_and_other_phenolics">Anthocyanins and other phenolics</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wine_grape_diagram_en.svg"><img alt="Anatomical-style diagram of three grapes on their stalks. Two of the grapes are shown in cross-section with all their internal parts labeled." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="585" data-file-width="678" height="259" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Wine_grape_diagram_en.svg/300px-Wine_grape_diagram_en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Wine_grape_diagram_en.svg/450px-Wine_grape_diagram_en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Wine_grape_diagram_en.svg/600px-Wine_grape_diagram_en.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wine_grape_diagram_en.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Grape cross-section</div></div></div>
<p>Anthocyanins tend to be the main <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyphenols_in_grape" title="Polyphenols in grape">polyphenolics</a> in purple grapes whereas <a href="/wiki/Flavan-3-ol" title="Flavan-3-ol">flavan-3-ols</a> (i.e. catechins) are the more abundant phenolic in white varieties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hozczp_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-hozczp-29">[29]</a></sup> Total phenolic content, a laboratory index of antioxidant strength, is higher in purple varieties due almost entirely to anthocyanin density in purple grape skin compared to absence of anthocyanins in white grape skin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hozczp_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-hozczp-29">[29]</a></sup> It is these anthocyanins that are attracting the efforts of scientists to define their properties for human health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jafcsymp_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-jafcsymp-30">[30]</a></sup> Phenolic content of grape skin varies with <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivar</a>, soil composition, climate, geographic origin, and cultivation practices or exposure to diseases, such as fungal infections.
</p><p>Red <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a> may offer health benefits more so than white because potentially beneficial compounds are present in grape skin, and only red wine is fermented with skins. The amount of fermentation time a wine spends in contact with grape skins is an important determinant of its resveratrol content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Ordinary non-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muscadine" title="Muscadine">muscadine</a> <a href="/wiki/Red_wine" title="Red wine">red wine</a> contains between 0.2 and 5.8 mg/L,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> depending on the grape variety, because it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(food)" title="Fermentation (food)">fermented</a> with the skins, allowing the wine to absorb the resveratrol. By contrast, a <a href="/wiki/White_wine" title="White wine">white wine</a> contains lower phenolic contents because it is fermented after removal of skins.
</p><p>Wines produced from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muscadine" title="Muscadine">muscadine</a> grapes may contain more than 40 mg/L, an exceptional phenolic content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guhqhr_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-guhqhr-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> In muscadine skins, <a href="/wiki/Ellagic_acid" title="Ellagic acid">ellagic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myricetin" title="Myricetin">myricetin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quercetin" title="Quercetin">quercetin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kaempferol" title="Kaempferol">kaempferol</a>, and trans-resveratrol are major phenolics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Contrary to previous results, ellagic acid and not resveratrol is the major phenolic in muscadine grapes.
</p><p>The flavonols <a href="/wiki/Syringetin" title="Syringetin">syringetin</a>, syringetin 3-O-galactoside, <a href="/wiki/Laricitrin" title="Laricitrin">laricitrin</a> and laricitrin 3-O-galactoside are also found in purple grape but absent in white grape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mattivi_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mattivi-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seed_constituents">Seed constituents</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Grape_seed_extract" title="Grape seed extract">Grape seed extract</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grape_seed_oil" title="Grape seed oil">Grape seed oil</a></div>
<p>Biochemical and preliminary <a href="/wiki/Clinical_trial" title="Clinical trial">clinical</a> studies have demonstrated potential biological properties of grape seed oligomeric <a href="/wiki/Procyanidin" title="Procyanidin">procyanidins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> For example, laboratory tests indicated a potential anticancer effect from grape seed extract.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/American_Cancer_Society" title="American Cancer Society">American Cancer Society</a>, "there is very little reliable scientific evidence available at this time that drinking red wine, eating grapes, or following the grape diet can prevent or treat cancer in people".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acs_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-acs-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Grape_seed_oil" title="Grape seed oil">Grape seed oil</a> from crushed seeds is used in <a href="/wiki/Cosmeceutical" title="Cosmeceutical">cosmeceuticals</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skincare" title="Skincare">skincare</a> products for perceived health benefits. Grape seed oil contains <a href="/wiki/Tocopherol" title="Tocopherol">tocopherols</a> (<a href="/wiki/Vitamin_E" title="Vitamin E">vitamin E</a>) and high contents of <a href="/wiki/Phytosterol" title="Phytosterol">phytosterols</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polyunsaturated_fatty_acid" title="Polyunsaturated fatty acid">polyunsaturated fatty acids</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Linoleic_acid" title="Linoleic acid">linoleic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oleic_acid" title="Oleic acid">oleic acid</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha-linolenic_acid" title="Alpha-linolenic acid">alpha-linolenic acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grape_and_raisin_toxicity_in_dogs">Grape and raisin toxicity in dogs</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Grape_and_raisin_toxicity_in_dogs" title="Grape and raisin toxicity in dogs">Grape and raisin toxicity in dogs</a></div>
<p>The consumption of grapes and raisins presents a potential health threat to dogs. Their toxicity to dogs can cause the animal to develop <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acute_renal_failure" title="Acute renal failure">acute renal failure</a> (the sudden development of <a href="/wiki/Kidney_failure" title="Kidney failure">kidney failure</a>) with <a href="/wiki/Anuria" title="Anuria">anuria</a> (a lack of urine production) and may be fatal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grape_therapy">Grape therapy</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Grape_therapy" title="Grape therapy">Grape therapy</a></div>
<p>Grape therapy, also known as ampelotherapy (from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" title="Ancient Greek language">Ancient Greek</a> <i> </i>ἄμπελος<i> (<a href="/wiki/Ampelos" title="Ampelos">ampelos</a>)</i>, meaning '<a href="/wiki/Vine" title="Vine">vine</a>'), is a form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naturopathic" title="Naturopathic">naturopathic</a> medicine or <a href="/wiki/Alternative_medicine" title="Alternative medicine">alternative medicine</a> that involves heavy consumption of grapes, including seeds, and parts of the vine, including leaves. Although there is some limited evidence of positive benefits from the consumption of grapes for health purposes, extreme claims, such as its ability to cure cancer, have been widely derided as "<a href="/wiki/Quackery" title="Quackery">quackery</a>".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources_(medicine)" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources (medicine)"><span title="Material near this tag needs references to reliable medical sources. (April 2015)">medical citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_significance">Religious significance</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Wine#Religious_significance" title="Wine">Wine § Religious significance</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SSACRAM_101.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1607" data-file-width="1434" height="247" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/SSACRAM_101.JPG/220px-SSACRAM_101.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/SSACRAM_101.JPG/330px-SSACRAM_101.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/SSACRAM_101.JPG/440px-SSACRAM_101.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SSACRAM_101.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Grapes embroidered on a 17th-century <a href="/wiki/Chasuble" title="Chasuble">chasuble</a>, Antwerp</div></div></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a>, grapes are first mentioned when <a href="/wiki/Noah" title="Noah">Noah</a> grows them on his farm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Instructions concerning wine are given in the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Proverbs" title="Book of Proverbs">book of Proverbs</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Isaiah" title="Book of Isaiah">book of Isaiah</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Deuteronomy tells of the use of <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a> during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> feasts. Grapes were also significant to both the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Greeks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a>, and their god of <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dionysus" title="Dionysus">Dionysus</a>, was linked to grapes and <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a>, being frequently portrayed with grape leaves on his head.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Grapes are especially significant for <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christians</a>, who since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Church" title="Early Church">Early Church</a> have used wine in their celebration of the <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">Eucharist</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Views on the significance of the wine vary between <a href="/wiki/Christian_denomination" title="Christian denomination">denominations</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Christian_art" title="Christian art">Christian art</a>, grapes often represent the <a href="/wiki/Blood_of_Christ" title="Blood of Christ">blood of Christ</a>, such as the grape leaves in <a href="/wiki/John_the_Baptist_(Caravaggio)#John_the_Baptist,_Toledo" title="John the Baptist (Caravaggio)">Caravaggio's <i>John the Baptist</i></a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_religion">Use in religion</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Christian_views_on_alcohol" title="Christian views on alcohol">Christian views on alcohol</a></div>
<p>Christians have traditionally used wine during worship services as a means of remembering the blood of Jesus Christ which was <a href="/wiki/Atonement_in_Christianity" title="Atonement in Christianity">shed for the remission of sins</a>. Christians who oppose the partaking of alcoholic beverages sometimes use grape juice <a class="external text" href="https://www.lds.org/new-era/2008/11/to-the-point/why-do-we-use-water-instead-of-wine-for-the-sacrament?lang=eng" rel="nofollow">or water</a> as the "cup" or "wine" in the <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">Lord's Supper</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UMC_–_Grape_Juice_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-UMC_–_Grape_Juice-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a> continues to use wine in the celebration of the Eucharist because it is part of the tradition passed down through the ages starting with Jesus Christ at the Last Supper, where Catholics believe the consecrated bread and wine <i>literally</i> become the body and blood of Jesus Christ, a dogma known as <a href="/wiki/Transubstantiation" title="Transubstantiation">transubstantiation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Wine is used (not grape juice) both due to its strong Scriptural roots, and also to follow the tradition set by the early Christian Church.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The Code of Canon Law of the Catholic Church (1983), Canon 924 says that the wine used must be natural, made from grapes of the vine, and not corrupt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> In some circumstances, a priest may obtain special permission to use grape juice for the consecration; however, this is extremely rare and typically requires sufficient impetus to warrant such a dispensation, such as personal health of the priest.
</p><p>Although alcohol is permitted in <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a>, grape juice is sometimes used as an alternative for <a href="/wiki/Kiddush" title="Kiddush">kiddush</a> on <a href="/wiki/Shabbat" title="Shabbat">Shabbat</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish_holiday" title="Jewish holiday">Jewish holidays</a>, and has the same blessing as wine. Many authorities maintain that grape juice must be capable of turning into wine naturally in order to be used for kiddush. Common practice, however, is to use any <a href="/wiki/Kashrut" title="Kashrut">kosher</a> grape juice for kiddush.
</p>
