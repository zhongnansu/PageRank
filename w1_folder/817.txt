<p>The <b>demographics of the European Union</b> show a highly populated, culturally diverse <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">union</a> of 28 <a href="/wiki/Member_state_of_the_European_Union" title="Member state of the European Union">member states</a>.
As of 1 January 2018, the population of the EU is about 512.6 million people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population_by_country">Population by country</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demographics_of_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Population by country">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The most populous member state is <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, with an estimated 82.8 million people, and the least populous member state is Malta with 0.48 million. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_birth_rate" title="List of countries by birth rate">Birth rates in the EU</a> are low with the average woman having 1.6 children.  The highest birth-rates are found in <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a> with 16.876 births per thousand people per year and France with 13.013 births per thousand people per year.  Germany has the lowest birth rate in Europe with 8.221 births per thousand people per year.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Migration">Migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demographics_of_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The movement of people within the Union i.e. internal migration, remains limited; it has traditionally followed two patterns:
</p>
<ul><li>Younger workers from less economically developed regions and countries of the EU tend to move to more prosperous regions in their country or to EU countries with good economic prospects (i.e. UK, Ireland, Germany, Netherlands, France, Italy, Portugal, Spain, Poland<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>)</li>
<li>Retirees from wealthier places with colder weather (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Benelux" title="Benelux">Benelux</a>, Britain and Germany) tend to move to the sun belt in southern Europe - i.e. Spain, Portugal, <a href="/wiki/Southern_France" title="Southern France">Southern France</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_peninsula" title="Italian peninsula">Italian peninsula</a> and Greece.</li></ul>
<p>Citizens from the European Union make up a growing proportion of immigrants in Spain. They mainly come from countries like the UK and Germany, but the British migration case is of particular interest due to its magnitude. The British authorities estimate that the real population of British citizens living in Spain is much higher than Spanish official figures suggest, establishing them at about 1,000,000 and about 800,000 being permanent residents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Immigration_and_emigration">Immigration and emigration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demographics_of_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Immigration and emigration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Immigration_to_Europe" title="Immigration to Europe">Immigration to Europe</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emigration_from_Europe" title="Emigration from Europe">Emigration from Europe</a></div>
<p>At present, more people immigrate into the European Union than <a href="/wiki/Emigration" title="Emigration">emigrate</a> from it.  Immigration is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Controversial" title="Controversial">controversial</a> issue in many member states, including Belgium, Sweden, Germany, Italy, the Netherlands, Spain, France, and the UK.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In 2010, 47.3 million people living in the EU, or 9.4% of the total population, had been born outside their resident country.  Of these, 31.4 million (6.3%) had been born outside the EU; 16.0 million (3.2%) had been born in another member state.  The largest absolute numbers of people born outside the EU were in Germany (6.4 million), France (5.1 million), the United Kingdom (4.7 million), Spain (4.1 million), Italy (3.2 million), and the Netherlands (1.4 million).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Spain in particular receives most of the immigrants coming illegally to Europe from Africa, probably due to its large coastal area and its proximity to and land borders with Morocco at Ceuta and Melilla; African immigrants try to enter the country by boat from Morocco or Senegal or by jumping the border fences.  For example, during just the first weekend of September 2006, more than 1,300 illegal immigrants arrived on beaches in the Canary Islands<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and estimates are that between 50,000 and 70,000 people enter the European Union illegally through Spanish borders or beaches. <a href="/wiki/Separation_barrier" title="Separation barrier">Border fences</a> have been built at both the <a href="/wiki/Ceuta_border_fence" title="Ceuta border fence">Ceuta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Melilla_border_fence" title="Melilla border fence">Melilla</a> borders in an attempt to stop illegal entrance to the country. Illegal immigration is an issue in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_politics" title="Spanish politics">Spanish politics</a>, and also a big human rights problem, since many people die during the journey. Spain has been Europe's largest absorber of migrants for the past six years, with its immigrant population increasing fourfold as 2.8 million people have arrived, mostly from Latin America. Spectacular growth in Spain's immigrant population came as the country's economy created more than half of all the new jobs in the European Union between 2001 and 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>The net migration rate for the EU in 2008 was 3.1 per 1,000 inhabitants;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epp.eurostat.ec.europa.eu_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-epp.eurostat.ec.europa.eu-20">[20]</a></sup> this figure is for migration into and out of the European Union, and therefore excludes any internal movements between member states.  Annual net migration has varied from 1.5 to 2.0 million people since 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epp.eurostat.ec.europa.eu_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-epp.eurostat.ec.europa.eu-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demographics_of_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_the_European_Union" title="Religion in the European Union">Religion in the European Union</a></div>
<p>The EU has significant religious diversity, mirroring its diverse history and culture. The largest religious group professes 
<a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> and accounts for 72% of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">EU</a> population,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> predominantly Roman Catholicism, Protestantism and Eastern Orthodoxy. Several EU nations do not have a Christian majority and for example in <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a> the majority have no religious affiliation.
</p><p>Today, theism is losing prevalence in Europe in favour of atheism and agnosticism, and religion is losing prevalence in favour of secularism. European countries have experienced a decline in church attendance as well as a decline in the number of people professing a religious belief. The 2005 Eurobarometer Poll found that, on average, 52% of the citizens of EU Member States state that they believe there is a God, 27% believe there is some sort of spirit or life force and 18% don't believe there is any sort of spirit, God or life force. 3% declined to answer.
</p><p>A decrease in church membership and church attendance in Europe (especially in <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>) has been noted and church attendance (percentage wise of the total population by country) in northern and western Europe is typically in the single digits.
</p><p>European indigenous (or native) religions are still alive in small and diverse minorities, especially in <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baltic_states" title="Baltic states">Baltic states</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>.
</p><p>The recent influx of immigrants to the affluent EU nations has brought in various religions of their native homelands, including <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sikhism" title="Sikhism">Sikhism</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith" title="Bahá'í Faith">Bahá'í Faith</a>. <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a> has had a long history in Europe and has coexisted with the other religions for centuries, despite periods of persecution or genocide by European rulers. <a href="/wiki/Islam_in_Europe" title="Islam in Europe">Islam</a> too has had a long history in Europe, with Spain and Portugal at one time having a Muslim majority.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hourani_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hourani-23">[23]</a></sup> Large Muslim populations also exist in the Balkans and parts of Eastern Europe, due to a legacy of centuries of Ottoman rule.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demographics_of_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_the_European_Union" title="Languages of the European Union">Languages of the European Union</a></div>
<p>The first official language of each of the 28 Member Countries has the status of an official language of the European Union. In total there are 24, with <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">Bulgarian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">Romanian</a> gaining official language status on 1 January 2007, when the last two countries joined the European Union, and <a href="/wiki/Croatian_language" title="Croatian language">Croatian</a> becoming official in 2013.
</p><p>English is the most spoken language in the EU, being spoken by around 51% of its population. This high proportion is because 38% of EU citizens speak it as a language other than their mother tongue (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Second_language" title="Second language">second</a> or <a href="/wiki/Foreign_language" title="Foreign language">foreign language</a>).  German is the most spoken <a href="/wiki/First_language" title="First language">first language</a>, spoken by more than 18% of the population.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographic_future">Demographic future</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demographics_of_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Demographic future">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ageing_of_Europe" title="Ageing of Europe">Ageing of Europe</a></div>
<p>The EU faces challenges in its demographic future. Most concerns center around several related issues: an ageing population, growing life expectancy and immigrant flow.
</p><p>After hitting a historical low of 1.47 children born per female, the total fertility rate of the EU started to increase again, to reach a level of 1.60 in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eurostat_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eurostat-24">[24]</a></sup> The positive trend was observed in all member states with the exception of Luxembourg, Malta and Portugal. The largest increases over this period were observed in Bulgaria (from 1.23 children per woman in 2003 to 1.57 in 2009), Slovenia (from 1.20 to 1.53), the Czech Republic (from 1.18 to 1.49) and Lithuania (from 1.26 to 1.55).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eurostat_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Eurostat-24">[24]</a></sup> In 2009, the Member States with the highest fertility rates were Ireland (2.06), France (2.00), Sweden (1.94), and the United Kingdom (1.90), all approaching the replacement level of 2.1 children born per female.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eurostat_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Eurostat-24">[24]</a></sup> The lowest rates were observed in Latvia (1.31), Hungary and Portugal (both 1.32) and Germany (1.36). The increasing fertility rate has also been accompanied by an upward trend in the natural increase of the population which is due to the moderate increase of the crude birth rate that reached 10.9 births per 1000 inhabitants in 2008, an increase of 0.3 compared with 2007. The increase was observed in all member countries except Germany. The EU crude death rate remained stable at 9.7 per 1000 inhabitants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epp.eurostat.ec.europa.eu_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-epp.eurostat.ec.europa.eu-20">[20]</a></sup> The relatively low fertility rate means retirement age workers are not entirely replaced by younger workers joining the workforce. The EU faces a potential future dominated by an ever-increasing population of retired citizens, without enough younger workers to fund (via taxes) retirement programs or other state welfare agendas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>A low fertility rate, without supplement from immigration, also suggests a declining overall EU population,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> which further suggests economic contraction or even a possible economic crisis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Some media have noted the 'baby crisis' in the EU,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> some governments have noted the problem,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and the UN and other multinational authorities have warned of a possible crisis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> At this point however such a decrease in the population of the EU is not observed as the overall natural growth remains positive and the EU continues to attract large numbers of immigrants. In 2010, a breakdown of the population by citizenship showed that there were 20.1 million foreign citizens living in the EU representing 4% of the population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eurostat_24-3"><a href="#cite_note-Eurostat-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Over the last 50 years, life expectancy at birth in the EU27 has increased by around 10 years for both women and men, to reach 82.4 years for women and 76.4 years for men in 2008. The life expectancy at birth rose in all Member States, with the largest increases for both women and men recorded in Estonia and Slovenia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eurostat_24-4"><a href="#cite_note-Eurostat-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethnic_composition">Ethnic composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demographics_of_the_European_Union&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ethnic composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_ethnic_groups" title="European ethnic groups">European ethnic groups</a></div>
<p>There is no precise or universally accepted definition of the terms "ethnic group" or "nationality". In the context of European ethnography in particular, the terms <i>ethnic group</i>, <i>people</i> (without nation state), <i>nationality</i>, <i>national minority</i>, <i>ethnic minority</i>, <i>linguistic community</i>, <i>linguistic group</i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linguistic_minority" title="Linguistic minority">linguistic minority</a></i> are used as mostly synonymous, although preference may vary in usage with respect to the situation specific to the individual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Countries_of_Europe" title="Countries of Europe">countries of Europe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>

<p>On current trends European populations will become more ethnically diverse, with the possibility that today's majority ethnic groups will no longer comprise a numerical majority in some countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2011, almost a quarter of new EU citizens were <a href="/wiki/Moroccans" title="Moroccans">Moroccans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkish_people" title="Turkish people">Turks</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecuadorian_people" title="Ecuadorian people">Ecuadorian</a> or <a href="/wiki/Indian_people" title="Indian people">Indians</a>. The new citizens in the EU27 in 2011 came mainly from Africa (26% of the total number of citizenships acquired), Asia (23%), non-EU27 Europe (19%), North and South America (17%) or another EU27 Member State (11%). In 2011, the largest groups that acquired citizenship of an EU27 Member State were citizens of Morocco (64 300 persons, of which 55% acquired citizenship of France or Spain), Turkey (48 900, 58% acquired German citizenship), Ecuador (33 700, 95% acquired Spanish citizenship) and India (31 700, 83% acquired British citizenship).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2012, 34.3 million foreign citizens lived in the 27 European Union member states, accounting for 6.8% of the European Union population,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup> of whom 20.5 million were third country nationals (i.e. nationals of non-EU countries). The number of foreign-born (which includes those who have naturalised or are dual nationals) was 48.9 million or 9.7 per cent of the total population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>A total of 8.0 million citizens from European countries outside of the EU-27 were residing in the EU at the beginning of 2012; among these more than half were citizens of Turkey, Albania or Ukraine. The next biggest group was from Africa (24.5%), followed by Asia (22.0%), the Americas (14.2%) and Oceania (0.8%). Romanians (living in another EU Member State) and Turkish citizens made up the biggest groups of non-nationals living in the EU-27 in 2012. There were 4.4 million Romanian citizens living outside of Romania within the EU-27 and 2.3 million Turkish citizens living in the EU-27; each of these two groups of people accounted for 7.0% of all foreigners living in the EU-27 in 2012. The third largest group was Moroccans (1.9 million people, or 5.6% of all foreigners).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>Approximately  20 million non-Europeans live in the EU, 4% of the overall population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
