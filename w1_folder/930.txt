<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Eddy covariance</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eddy_Covariance_IRGA_Sonic.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2272" data-file-width="1704" height="267" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Eddy_Covariance_IRGA_Sonic.jpg/200px-Eddy_Covariance_IRGA_Sonic.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Eddy_Covariance_IRGA_Sonic.jpg/300px-Eddy_Covariance_IRGA_Sonic.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Eddy_Covariance_IRGA_Sonic.jpg/400px-Eddy_Covariance_IRGA_Sonic.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eddy_Covariance_IRGA_Sonic.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eddy covariance system consisting of an ultrasonic anemometer and infrared gas analyser (IRGA).</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>eddy covariance</b> (also known as <b>eddy correlation</b> and <b>eddy flux</b>) technique is a key atmospheric measurement technique to measure and calculate vertical turbulent fluxes within <a href="/wiki/Planetary_boundary_layer" title="Planetary boundary layer">atmospheric boundary layers</a>. The method analyzes high-frequency <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scalar_(physics)" title="Scalar (physics)">scalar</a> atmospheric data series, and yields values of <a href="/wiki/Flux" title="Flux">fluxes</a> of these properties. It is a <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistical</a> method used in <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a> and other applications (micrometeorology, oceanography, hydrology, agricultural sciences, industrial and regulatory applications, etc.) to determine exchange rates of trace gases over natural ecosystems and agricultural fields, and to quantify gas emissions rates from other land and water areas. It is frequently used to estimate <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heat_flux" title="Heat flux">heat</a>, water vapour, carbon dioxide and methane fluxes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The technique is also used extensively for verification and tuning of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_climate_model" title="Global climate model">global climate models</a>, mesoscale and weather models, complex biogeochemical and ecological models, and remote sensing estimates from satellites and aircraft. The technique is mathematically complex, and requires significant care in setting up and processing data. To date, there is no uniform terminology or a single methodology for the Eddy Covariance technique, but much effort is being made by flux measurement networks (e.g., <a href="/wiki/FluxNet" title="FluxNet">FluxNet</a>, <a class="external text" href="http://ameriflux.lbl.gov/" rel="nofollow">Ameriflux</a>, <a class="external text" href="http://www.icos-infrastructure.eu/" rel="nofollow">ICOS</a>, <a class="external text" href="http://www.carboeurope.org/" rel="nofollow">CarboEurope</a>, <a class="external text" href="http://fluxnet.ccrp.ec.gc.ca/e_about.htm" rel="nofollow">Fluxnet Canada</a>, <a class="external text" href="http://www.ozflux.org.au/" rel="nofollow">OzFlux</a>, <a class="external text" href="http://www.neoninc.org/" rel="nofollow">NEON</a>, and <a class="external text" href="http://www.ileaps.org/" rel="nofollow">iLEAPS</a>) to unify the various approaches.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eddycorrelationsystem.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="601" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Eddycorrelationsystem.jpg/200px-Eddycorrelationsystem.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Eddycorrelationsystem.jpg/300px-Eddycorrelationsystem.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Eddycorrelationsystem.jpg/400px-Eddycorrelationsystem.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eddycorrelationsystem.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An eddy correlation instrument measuring oxygen fluxes in benthic environments.</div></div></div>
<p>The technique has additionally proven applicable under water to the <a href="/wiki/Benthic_zone" title="Benthic zone">benthic zone</a> for measuring oxygen fluxes between seafloor and overlying water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In these environments, the technique is generally known as the eddy correlation technique, or just eddy correlation. Oxygen fluxes are extracted from raw measurements largely following the same principles as used in the atmosphere, and they are typically used as a proxy for carbon exchange, which is important for local and global carbon budgets. For most benthic ecosystems, eddy correlation is the most accurate technique for measuring <i>in-situ</i> fluxes. The technique's development and its applications under water remains a fruitful area of research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General_principles">General principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eddy_covariance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: General principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Representation of the air flow in the atmospheric boundary layer</b>
</p><p>Air flow can be imagined as a horizontal flow of numerous rotating eddies, that is, turbulent vortices of various sizes, with each eddy having horizontal and vertical components. The situation looks chaotic, but vertical movement of the components can be measured from the tower.
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Py%C3%B6rrekovarianssi-tekniikan_kaaviokuva.jpg"><img alt="Pyörrekovarianssi-tekniikan kaaviokuva.jpg" data-file-height="292" data-file-width="600" height="292" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/65/Py%C3%B6rrekovarianssi-tekniikan_kaaviokuva.jpg" width="600"/></a></div></div><center><sup>[4]</sup></center><center><sup>[4]</sup></center>
<p><b>Physical meaning of the eddy covariance method</b>
</p><p>At one physical point on the tower, at Time1, Eddy1 moves parcel of air c1 down at the speed w1. Then, at Time2, Eddy2 moves parcel c2 up at the speed w2. Each parcel has gas concentration, pressure, temperature, and humidity. If these factors, along with the speed are known, we can determine the flux. For example, if one knew how many molecules of water went down with eddies at Time 1, and how many molecules went up with eddies at Time2, at the same point, one could calculate the vertical flux of water at this point over this time. 
So, vertical flux can be presented as a covariance of the vertical wind velocity and the concentration of the entity of interest.
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EddyCovariance_diagram_2.jpg"><img alt="EddyCovariance diagram 2.jpg" data-file-height="205" data-file-width="600" height="205" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5f/EddyCovariance_diagram_2.jpg" width="600"/></a></div></div><center><sup>[4]</sup></center><center><sup>[4]</sup></center>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mathematical_foundation">Mathematical foundation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eddy_covariance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mathematical foundation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In mathematical terms, "eddy flux" is computed as a <a href="/wiki/Covariance" title="Covariance">covariance</a> between instantaneous deviation in vertical wind speed (w') from the mean value (w-overbar) and instantaneous deviation in gas concentration, mixing ratio (s'), from its mean value (s-overbar), multiplied by mean air density (ρa). Several mathematical operations and assumptions, including Reynolds decomposition, are involved in getting from physically complete equations of the turbulent flow to practical equations for computing "eddy flux", as shown below.
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EddyCovariance_equations_part_1.jpg"><img alt="EddyCovariance equations part 1.jpg" data-file-height="385" data-file-width="600" height="385" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/5/59/EddyCovariance_equations_part_1.jpg" width="600"/></a></div></div>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EddyCovariance_equations_part_2.jpg"><img alt="EddyCovariance equations part 2.jpg" data-file-height="336" data-file-width="600" height="336" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/8/81/EddyCovariance_equations_part_2.jpg" width="600"/></a></div></div><center><sup>[4]</sup></center><center><sup>[4]</sup></center>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_assumptions">Major assumptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eddy_covariance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Major assumptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>Measurements at a point can represent an upwind area</li>
<li>Measurements are done inside the boundary layer of interest</li>
<li>Fetch/<a href="/wiki/Flux_footprint" title="Flux footprint">flux footprint</a> is adequate – fluxes are measured only at area of interest</li>
<li>Flux is fully turbulent – most of the net vertical transfer is done by eddies</li>
<li>Terrain is horizontal and uniformed: average of fluctuations is zero; density fluctuations negligible; flow convergence &amp; divergence negligible</li>
<li>Instruments can detect very small changes at high frequency, ranging from minimum of 5 Hz and to 40 Hz for tower-based measurements</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Eddy_covariance_processing_software">Eddy covariance  processing software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eddy_covariance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Eddy covariance processing software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Currently (2011) there are many software programs <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
to process eddy covariance data and derive quantities such as heat, momentum, and gas fluxes. The programs range significantly in complexity, flexibility, number of allowed instruments and variables, help system and user support. Some programs are <a href="/wiki/Open-source_software" title="Open-source software">open-source software</a>, while others are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Closed-source" title="Closed-source">closed-source</a> or <a href="/wiki/Proprietary_software" title="Proprietary software">proprietary</a>.
</p><p>Examples include free fully supported and <a class="external text" href="https://www.licor.com/env/help/eddypro/topics_eddypro/EddyPro_Home.html" rel="nofollow">documented</a> open-source software such as <a class="external text" href="http://www.licor.com/env/products/eddy_covariance/software.html" rel="nofollow">EddyPro</a>; free unsupported open-source programs such as <a class="external text" href="http://gaia.agraria.unitus.it/eco2s" rel="nofollow">ECO<sub>2</sub>S</a> and <a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20080821152535/http://www.met.wau.nl/" rel="nofollow">ECpack</a>; free closed-source packages such as <a class="external text" href="https://www.geos.ed.ac.uk/homes/jbm/micromet/EdiRe/" rel="nofollow">EdiRe</a>, <a class="external text" href="https://epub.uni-bayreuth.de/342/" rel="nofollow">TK3</a>, <a class="external text" href="http://www.climatexchange.nl/projects/alteddy/index.htm" rel="nofollow">Alteddy</a>, and <a class="external text" href="https://www.bgc-jena.mpg.de/www/uploads/Publications/TechnicalReports/tech_report10.pdf" rel="nofollow">EddySoft</a>. Eddy covariance processing algorithms can also be implemented in commercial software such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matlab" title="Matlab">Matlab</a> or the free <a href="/wiki/Open-source_software" title="Open-source software">open-source software</a> <a href="/wiki/R_(programming_language)" title="R (programming language)">R</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Summary">Summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eddy_covariance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In summation, the 3D wind and another variable (usually gas concentration, temperature or momentum) are decomposed into <a href="/wiki/Average" title="Average">mean</a> and fluctuating components.  The covariance is calculated between the fluctuating component of the vertical wind and the fluctuating component of gas concentration. The measured flux is proportional to the covariance.
</p><p>The area from which the detected eddies originate is described probabilistically and called a <a href="/wiki/Flux_footprint" title="Flux footprint">flux footprint</a>. The flux footprint area is dynamic in size and shape, changing with wind direction, thermal stability and measurements height, and has a gradual border.
</p><p>The effect of sensor separation, finite sampling length, sonic path averaging, as well as other instrumental limitations, affect frequency response of the measurement system and may need a co-spectral correction, especially noticeable with closed-path instruments and at low heights below 1 to 1.5 m.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eddy_accumulation">Eddy accumulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eddy_covariance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Eddy accumulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="True_eddy_accumulation">True eddy accumulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eddy_covariance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: True eddy accumulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The true eddy accumulation technique can be used to measure fluxes of trace gases for which there are no fast enough analysers available, thus where the eddy covariance technique is unsuitable. The basic idea is that upwards moving air parcels (updrafts) and downwards moving air parcels (downdrafts) are sampled proportionally to their velocity into separate reservoirs. A slow response gas analyser can then be used to quantify the average gas concentrations in both updraft and downdraft reservoirs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Relaxed_eddy_accumulation">Relaxed eddy accumulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eddy_covariance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Relaxed eddy accumulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The main difference between the true and the relaxed eddy accumulation technique is that the latter samples air with a constant flow rate that is not proportional to the vertical wind speed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
