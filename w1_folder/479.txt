<p><b>Orbiting Carbon Observatory 2</b> (<b>OCO-2</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Environmental_science" title="Environmental science">environmental science</a> satellite which launched on 2 July 2014. A <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> mission, it is a replacement for the <a href="/wiki/Orbiting_Carbon_Observatory" title="Orbiting Carbon Observatory">Orbiting Carbon Observatory</a> which was lost in a launch failure in 2009. It is the second successful high-precision (better than 0.3%) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space-based_Measurements_of_Carbon_Dioxide" title="Space-based Measurements of Carbon Dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub> observing satellite</a>, after <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_Gases_Observing_Satellite" title="Greenhouse Gases Observing Satellite">GOSAT</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission_overview">Mission overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbiting_Carbon_Observatory_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mission overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The OCO-2 satellite was built by <a href="/wiki/Orbital_Sciences_Corporation" title="Orbital Sciences Corporation">Orbital Sciences Corporation</a>, based around the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LEOStar-2" title="LEOStar-2">LEOStar-2</a> bus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The spacecraft is being used to study <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> concentrations and distributions in the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>OCO-2 was ordered after the original OCO spacecraft failed to achieve orbit. During the first satellite's launch atop a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taurus_(rocket)" title="Taurus (rocket)">Taurus-XL</a> in February 2009, the payload fairing failed to separate from around the spacecraft and the rocket did not have sufficient power to enter orbit with its additional mass. Although a Taurus launch was initially contracted for the reflight, the launch contract was cancelled after the same malfunction occurred on the launch of the <a href="/wiki/Glory_(satellite)" title="Glory (satellite)">Glory</a> satellite two years later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Launch_of_Orbiting_Carbon_Observatory-2_(201407020003HQ).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2200" data-file-width="3000" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Launch_of_Orbiting_Carbon_Observatory-2_%28201407020003HQ%29.jpg/220px-Launch_of_Orbiting_Carbon_Observatory-2_%28201407020003HQ%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Launch_of_Orbiting_Carbon_Observatory-2_%28201407020003HQ%29.jpg/330px-Launch_of_Orbiting_Carbon_Observatory-2_%28201407020003HQ%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Launch_of_Orbiting_Carbon_Observatory-2_%28201407020003HQ%29.jpg/440px-Launch_of_Orbiting_Carbon_Observatory-2_%28201407020003HQ%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Launch_of_Orbiting_Carbon_Observatory-2_(201407020003HQ).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Launch of OCO-2 on a Delta II rocket</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/United_Launch_Alliance" title="United Launch Alliance">United Launch Alliance</a> launched OCO-2 using a <a href="/wiki/Delta_II" title="Delta II">Delta II</a> rocket at the beginning of a 30-second <a href="/wiki/Launch_window" title="Launch window">launch window</a> at 09:56 UTC (2:56 PDT) on 2 July 2014. Flying in the 7320-10C configuration, the rocket launched from <a href="/wiki/Vandenberg_AFB_Space_Launch_Complex_2" title="Vandenberg AFB Space Launch Complex 2">Space Launch Complex 2W</a> at <a href="/wiki/Vandenberg_Air_Force_Base" title="Vandenberg Air Force Base">Vandenberg Air Force Base</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The initial launch attempt on 1 July at 09:56:44 UTC was scrubbed at 46 seconds on the countdown clock due to a faulty valve on the water suppression system, used to flow water on the launch pad to dampen the acoustic energy during launch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>OCO-2 joined the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A-train_satellite_constellation" title="A-train satellite constellation">A-train satellite constellation</a>, becoming the sixth satellite in the group. Members of the A-train fly very close together in <a href="/wiki/Sun-synchronous_orbit" title="Sun-synchronous orbit">sun-synchronous orbit</a>, to make nearly simultaneous measurements of Earth. A particularly short launch window of 30 seconds was necessary to achieve a proper position in the train.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> As of 19 September 2016 it was in an orbit with a <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">perigee</a> of 701.1 km (435.6 mi), an <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">apogee</a> of 703.8 km (437.3 mi) and a 98.2 degree <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">inclination</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heavens-above_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-heavens-above-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The mission is expected to cost <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$467.7 million</span>, including design, development, launch and operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oco2-press_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-oco2-press-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Rather than directly measuring concentrations of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere, OCO-2 records how much of the sunlight reflected off the Earth is absorbed by CO<span style="display:inline-block; margin-bottom:-0.3em; vertical-align:-0.4em; line-height:1.2em; font-size:70%; text-align:left;"><br/>2</span> molecules in an air column.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> OCO-2 makes measurements in three different <a href="/wiki/Spectral_bands" title="Spectral bands">spectral bands</a> over four to eight different footprints of approximately 1.29 km × 2.25 km (0.80 mi × 1.40 mi) each.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esrh-oco_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-esrh-oco-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOsterman20155_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOsterman20155-12">[12]</a></sup>  About 24 <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_sounding" title="Atmospheric sounding">soundings</a> are collected per second while in sunlight and over 10% of these are sufficiently cloud free for further analysis.  One spectral band is used for column measurements of oxygen (A-band 0.765 microns), and two are used for column measurements of carbon dioxide (weak band 1.61 microns, strong band 2.06 microns).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOsterman20157_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOsterman20157-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the retrieval algorithm measurements from the three bands are combined to yield column-averaged dry-air mole fractions of carbon dioxide.  Because these are dry-air mole fractions, these measurements do not change with water content or surface pressure.  Because the molecular oxygen content of the atmosphere (i.e. excluding the oxygen in water vapour) is well known to be 20.95%, oxygen is used as a measure of the total dry air column.  To ensure these measurements are traceable to the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a>, OCO-2 measurements are carefully compared with measurements by the <a href="/wiki/Total_Carbon_Column_Observing_Network" title="Total Carbon Column Observing Network">Total Carbon Column Observing Network</a> (TCCON).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOsterman20157_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOsterman20157-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Data_products">Data products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbiting_Carbon_Observatory_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Data products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mission data are provided to the public by the NASA Goddard Earth Science Data and Information Services Center (GES DISC). The Level 1B data product is the least processed and contains records for all collected soundings (about 74,000 soundings per orbit). The Level 2 product contains estimates of the column-averaged dry-air mole fractions of carbon dioxide, among other parameters such as surface <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosol</a> content. The Level 3 product consists of global maps of carbon dioxide concentrations developed by OCO-2 scientists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOsterman20151-2_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOsterman20151-2-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
