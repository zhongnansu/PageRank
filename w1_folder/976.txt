<p>The <b>Sierra Club</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Environmental_organization" title="Environmental organization">environmental organization</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. It was founded on May 28, 1892, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Francisco,_California" title="San Francisco, California">San Francisco, California</a>, by the Scottish-American preservationist <a href="/wiki/John_Muir" title="John Muir">John Muir</a>, who became its first president. The Sierra Club primarily operates in the United States; an affiliated organization, <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club_Canada" title="Sierra Club Canada">Sierra Club Canada</a>, operates in Canada and deals exclusively with Canadian issues.
</p><p>Traditionally associated with the <a href="/wiki/Progressivism_in_the_United_States" title="Progressivism in the United States">progressive movement</a>, the club was one of the first large-scale environmental preservation organizations in the world, and currently engages in lobbying politicians to promote environmentalist policies. Recent focuses of the club include promoting <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_energy" title="Sustainable energy">sustainable energy</a>, mitigating <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Beyond_Coal" title="Beyond Coal">opposing coal</a>. The club is known for its political endorsements, which are often sought after by candidates in local elections; it generally supports <a href="/wiki/Modern_liberalism_in_the_United_States" title="Modern liberalism in the United States">liberal</a> and progressive candidates in elections.
</p><p>The Sierra Club is organized on both a national and local level. The club is divided into large chapters representing large geographic areas, some of which have tens of thousands of members. These chapters are divided into regional groups, and special interest sections, committees, and task forces. While much activity is coordinated at a local level, the Club is a unified organization; decisions made at the national level take precedent.
</p><p>In addition to political advocacy, the Sierra Club organizes outdoor recreation activities, and has historically been a notable organization for <a href="/wiki/Mountaineering" title="Mountaineering">mountaineering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rock_climbing" title="Rock climbing">rock climbing</a> in the United States. Members of the Sierra Club pioneered the <a href="/wiki/Yosemite_Decimal_System" title="Yosemite Decimal System">Yosemite Decimal System</a> of climbing, and were responsible for a substantial amount of the early development of climbing. Much of this activity occurred in the group's namesake <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a>. The Sierra Club does not set standards for or regulate alpinism, but it organizes wilderness courses, hikes, and occasional alpine expeditions for members. In <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, the club, through its outdoor recreation groups, is usually considered the state's analogue to other state mountaineering clubs such as <a href="/wiki/Mazamas" title="Mazamas">Mazamas</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Mountain_Club" title="Colorado Mountain Club">Colorado Mountain Club</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Sierra Club's stated mission is "To explore, enjoy, and protect the wild places of the earth; To practice and promote the responsible use of the earth's ecosystems and resources; To educate and enlist humanity to protect and restore the quality of the natural and human environment; and to use all lawful means to carry out these objectives."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mission_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-mission-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Sierra Club is governed by a 15-member <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Board_of_Directors" title="Board of Directors">Board of Directors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Each year, five directors are elected to three-year terms, and all club members are eligible to vote. A president is elected annually by the Board from among its members. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Executive_Director" title="Executive Director">Executive Director</a> runs the day-to-day operations of the group. <a href="/wiki/Michael_Brune" title="Michael Brune">Michael Brune</a>, formerly of <a href="/wiki/Rainforest_Action_Network" title="Rainforest Action Network">Rainforest Action Network</a>, has served as the organization's executive director since 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Brune succeeded <a href="/wiki/Carl_Pope_(environmentalist)" title="Carl Pope (environmentalist)">Carl Pope</a>. Pope stepped down amid discontent that the group had strayed from its core principles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pope_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-pope-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sierra Club members belong to statewide chapters and local groups. National and local special-interest sections, committees, and task forces address particular issues. The national Sierra Club sets the organization's policy agenda and overarching rules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mission_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-mission-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The club is known for engaging in two main activities: promoting and guiding outdoor recreational activities, which is done throughout the United States but primarily in California (especially Southern California), and political activism to promote environmental causes. Richard M. Skinner of the <a href="/wiki/Brookings_Institution" title="Brookings Institution">Brookings Institution</a> describes the Sierra Club as one of the United States' "leading environmental organizations". The Sierra Club makes endorsements of individual candidates for elected office, which has substantial weight given the club's reputation and large membership.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Skinner2007_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Skinner2007-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding">Founding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Founding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Journalist <a href="/wiki/Robert_Underwood_Johnson" title="Robert Underwood Johnson">Robert Underwood Johnson</a> had worked with John Muir on the successful campaign to create a large <a href="/wiki/Yosemite_National_Park" title="Yosemite National Park">Yosemite National Park</a> surrounding the much smaller state park which had been created in 1864.  This campaign succeeded in 1890. As early as 1889, Johnson had encouraged Muir to form an "association" to help protect the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a>, and preliminary meetings were held to plan the group. Others involved in the early planning included artist <a href="/wiki/William_Keith_(artist)" title="William Keith (artist)">William Keith</a>, <a href="/wiki/Willis_Linn_Jepson" title="Willis Linn Jepson">Willis Linn Jepson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Willard_Drake_Johnson" title="Willard Drake Johnson">Willard Drake Johnson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_LeConte" title="Joseph LeConte">Joseph LeConte</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Starr_Jordan" title="David Starr Jordan">David Starr Jordan</a>.
</p><p>In May 1892, a group of professors from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_California_at_Berkeley" title="University of California at Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a> helped Muir and attorney <a href="/wiki/Warren_Olney" title="Warren Olney">Warren Olney</a> launch the new organization modeled after the eastern <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Mountain_Club" title="Appalachian Mountain Club">Appalachian Mountain Club</a>. The Sierra Club's charter members elected Muir president, an office he held until his death in 1914.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-history-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Club's first goals included establishing <a href="/wiki/Glacier_National_Park_(U.S.)" title="Glacier National Park (U.S.)">Glacier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mount_Rainier_National_Park" title="Mount Rainier National Park">Mount Rainier</a> national parks, convincing the California legislature to give <a href="/wiki/Yosemite_Valley" title="Yosemite Valley">Yosemite Valley</a> to the U.S. federal government, and preserving California's coastal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coast_Redwood" title="Coast Redwood">redwoods</a>.
</p><p>Muir escorted President <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a> through Yosemite in 1903, and two years later the California legislature ceded Yosemite Valley and <a href="/wiki/Mariposa_Grove" title="Mariposa Grove">Mariposa Grove</a> to the federal government. The Sierra Club won its first lobbying victory with the creation of the country's second national park, after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yellowstone" title="Yellowstone">Yellowstone</a> in 1872.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legacy_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-legacy-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hetch_Hetchy_controversy">Hetch Hetchy controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hetch Hetchy controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Theodore-Roosevelt-and-John-Muir_1906.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="464" data-file-width="618" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Theodore-Roosevelt-and-John-Muir_1906.jpg/220px-Theodore-Roosevelt-and-John-Muir_1906.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Theodore-Roosevelt-and-John-Muir_1906.jpg/330px-Theodore-Roosevelt-and-John-Muir_1906.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Theodore-Roosevelt-and-John-Muir_1906.jpg/440px-Theodore-Roosevelt-and-John-Muir_1906.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Theodore-Roosevelt-and-John-Muir_1906.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Muir" title="John Muir">John Muir</a> in <a href="/wiki/Yosemite_National_Park" title="Yosemite National Park">Yosemite National Park</a>, c. 1906</div></div></div>
<p>In the first decade of the 1900's, the Sierra Club became embroiled in the  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hetch_Hetchy_Reservoir" title="Hetch Hetchy Reservoir">Hetch Hetchy Reservoir</a> controversy that divided preservationists from "resource management" conservationists. In the late 19th century, the city of San Francisco was rapidly outgrowing its limited water supply, which depended on intermittent local springs and streams. In 1890, San Francisco mayor <a href="/wiki/James_D._Phelan" title="James D. Phelan">James D. Phelan</a> proposed to build a dam and aqueduct on the Tuolumne River, one of the largest southern Sierra rivers, as a way to increase and stabilize the city's water supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-controversy_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-controversy-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Gifford_Pinchot" title="Gifford Pinchot">Gifford Pinchot</a>, a progressive supporter of public utilities and head of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Forest_Service" title="US Forest Service">US Forest Service</a>, which then had jurisdiction over the national parks, supported the creation of the Hetch Hetchy dam. Muir appealed to his friend U.S. President Roosevelt, who would not commit himself against the dam, given its popularity with the people of San Francisco (a <a href="/wiki/Referendum" title="Referendum">referendum</a> in 1908 confirmed a seven-to-one majority in favor of the dam and municipal water). Muir and attorney <a href="/wiki/William_Edward_Colby" title="William Edward Colby">William Edward Colby</a> began a national campaign against the dam, attracting the support of many eastern conservationists. With the 1912 election of U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">Woodrow Wilson</a>, who carried San Francisco, supporters of the dam had a friend in the White House.
</p><p>The bill to dam Hetch Hetchy passed Congress in 1913, and so the Sierra Club lost its first major battle. In retaliation, the Club supported creation of the <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">National Park Service</a> in 1916, to remove the parks from Forest Service oversight. <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Mather" title="Stephen Mather">Stephen Mather</a>, a Club member from Chicago and an opponent of the Hetch Hetchy dam, became the first National Park Service director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legacy_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-legacy-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="1920.27s.2C_.2730s_and_.2740s"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1920's,_'30s_and_'40s">1920's, '30s and '40s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1920's, '30s and '40s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the 1920's and 1930's, the Sierra Club functioned as a social and recreational society, conducting outings, maintaining trails and building huts and lodges in the Sierras. Preservation campaigns included a several-year effort to enlarge <a href="/wiki/Sequoia_National_Park" title="Sequoia National Park">Sequoia National Park</a> (achieved in 1926) and over three decades of work to protect and then preserve <a href="/wiki/Kings_Canyon_National_Park" title="Kings Canyon National Park">Kings Canyon National Park</a> (established in 1940). Historian Stephen Fox notes, "In the 1930's most of the three thousand members were middle-aged Republicans."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legacy_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-legacy-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/New_Deal" title="New Deal">New Deal</a> brought many conservationists to the Democratic Party, and many Democrats entered the ranks of conservationists. Leading the generation of <a href="/wiki/Young_Turks_(U.S._politics)" title="Young Turks (U.S. politics)">Young Turks</a> who revitalized the Sierra Club after World War II were attorneys <a href="/wiki/Richard_M._Leonard" title="Richard M. Leonard">Richard Leonard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bestor_Robinson" title="Bestor Robinson">Bestor Robinson</a>, nature photographer <a href="/wiki/Ansel_Adams" title="Ansel Adams">Ansel Adams</a>, and <a href="/wiki/David_Brower" title="David Brower">David Brower</a>.
</p><p>Adams sponsored Brower for membership in the Club, and he was appointed to the editorial board of the <i>Sierra Club Bulletin.</i> After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> Brower returned to his job with the University of California Press, and began editing the <i>Sierra Club Bulletin</i> in 1946.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legacy_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-legacy-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_reach">National reach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: National reach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1950, the Sierra Club had some 7,000 members, mostly on the West Coast. That year the Atlantic chapter became the first formed outside California. An active volunteer board of directors ran the organization, assisted by a small clerical staff. Brower was appointed the first executive director in 1952, and the Club began to catch up with major conservation organizations such as the <a href="/wiki/National_Audubon_Society" title="National Audubon Society">National Audubon Society</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_Wildlife_Federation" title="National Wildlife Federation">National Wildlife Federation</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Wilderness_Society_(United_States)" title="The Wilderness Society (United States)">The Wilderness Society</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Izaak_Walton_League" title="Izaak Walton League">Izaak Walton League</a>, which had long had professional staff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legacy_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-legacy-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Sierra Club secured its national reputation in the battle against the <a href="/wiki/Echo_Park_Dam" title="Echo Park Dam">Echo Park Dam</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur_National_Monument" title="Dinosaur National Monument">Dinosaur National Monument</a> in <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a>, which had been announced by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Reclamation" title="Bureau of Reclamation">Bureau of Reclamation</a> in 1950. Brower led the fight, marshaling support from other conservation groups. Brower's background in publishing proved decisive; with the help of publisher Alfred Knopf, <i>This Is Dinosaur</i> was rushed into press. Invoking the specter of Hetch Hetchy, conservationists effectively lobbied Congress, which deleted the Echo Park dam from the <a href="/wiki/Colorado_River" title="Colorado River">Colorado River</a> project as approved in 1955. Recognition of the Sierra Club's role in the Echo Park dam victory boosted membership from 10,000 in 1956 to 15,000 in 1960.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legacy_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-legacy-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Sierra Club was now truly a national conservation organization, and preservationists took the offensive with wilderness proposals. The Club's Biennial Wilderness Conferences, launched in 1949 in concert with The Wilderness Society, became an important force in the campaign that secured passage of the <a href="/wiki/Wilderness_Act" title="Wilderness Act">Wilderness Act</a> in 1964.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legacy_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-legacy-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Book_series">Book series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Book series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1960, Brower launched the Exhibit Format book series with <i>This Is the American Earth</i>, and in 1962 <i>In Wildness Is the Preservation of the World</i>, with color photographs by <a href="/wiki/Eliot_Porter" title="Eliot Porter">Eliot Porter</a>. These coffee-table books, published by their <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club_Books" title="Sierra Club Books">Sierra Club Books</a> division, introduced the Sierra Club to a wider audience. Fifty thousand copies were sold in the first four years, and by 1960 sales exceeded $10 million. Soon Brower was publishing two new titles a year in the Exhibit Format series, but not all did as well as <i>In Wildness.</i> Although the books were successful in introducing the public to wilderness preservation and the Sierra Club, they lost money for the organization, some $60,000 a year after 1964. Financial management became a matter of contention between Brower and his board of directors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legacy_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-legacy-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grand_Canyon_campaign">Grand Canyon campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Grand Canyon campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Sierra Club's most publicized crusade of the 1960s was the effort to stop the Bureau of Reclamation from building two dams that would flood portions of the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Canyon" title="Grand Canyon">Grand Canyon</a>. Opposing the <a href="/wiki/Bridge_Canyon_Dam" title="Bridge Canyon Dam">Bridge Canyon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marble_Canyon_Dam" title="Marble Canyon Dam">Marble Canyon</a> dam projects, full-page ads the Club placed in the <i>New York Times</i> and the <i>Washington Post</i> in 1966 exclaimed, "This time it's the Grand Canyon they want to flood," and asked, "Should we also flood the Sistine Chapel so tourists can get nearer the ceiling?" The ads generated a storm of protest to the Congress, prompting the <a href="/wiki/Internal_Revenue_Service" title="Internal Revenue Service">Internal Revenue Service</a> to announce it was suspending the Sierra Club's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/501(c)#501(c)(3)" title="501(c)">501(c)(3)</a> status pending an investigation. The board had taken the precaution of setting up the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club_Foundation" title="Sierra Club Foundation">Sierra Club Foundation</a> as a (c)(3) organization in 1960 for endowments and contributions for educational and other non-lobbying activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-history-8">[8]</a></sup> Even so, contributions to the Club dropped off, aggravating its annual operating deficits. Membership, however, climbed sharply in response to the investigation into the legitimacy of the society's tax status by the IRS from 30,000 in 1965 to 57,000 in 1967 and 75,000 in 1969.
</p><p>The victory over the dam projects and challenges from the IRS did not come without costs. To make up for the power that would have been produced by the dams, the Sierra Club actually advocated for coal power plants. The result of the campaign and its trade-off was, in the words of historian Andrew Needham, that "the Grand Canyon became protected, sacred space," while "the Navajo Reservation"—which housed some of the main power plants picking up the slack – "became increasingly industrial."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="End_of_the_Brower_era">End of the Brower era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: End of the Brower era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite the Club's success in blocking plans for the Grand Canyon dams and weathering the transition from 501(c)(3) to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/501(c)#501(c)(4)" title="501(c)">501(c)(4)</a> status, tension grew over finances between Brower and the board of directors. The Club's annual deficits rose from $100,000 in 1967 and 1968 to some $200,000 in 1969. Another conflict occurred over the Club's policy toward the nuclear power plant to be constructed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Gas_and_Electric" title="Pacific Gas and Electric">Pacific Gas and Electric</a> (PG&amp;E) at <a href="/wiki/Diablo_Canyon_Power_Plant" title="Diablo Canyon Power Plant">Diablo Canyon</a> near <a href="/wiki/San_Luis_Obispo,_California" title="San Luis Obispo, California">San Luis Obispo, California</a>. Although the Club had played the leading role blocking PG&amp;E's nuclear power plant proposed for <a href="/wiki/Bodega_Bay,_California" title="Bodega Bay, California">Bodega Bay, California</a> in the early 1960's, that case had been built around the local environmental impact and earthquake danger from the nearby <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=San_Andrea%27s_fault&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="San Andrea's fault (page does not exist)">San Andrea's fault</a>, not from opposition to nuclear power itself. In exchange for moving the new proposed site from the environmentally sensitive Nipomo Dunes to Diablo Canyon, the board of directors voted to support PG&amp;E's plan for the power plant. A membership referendum in 1967 upheld the board's decision. But Brower concluded that nuclear power at any location was a mistake, and he voiced his opposition to the plant, contrary to the Club's official policy. As pro- and anti-Brower factions polarized, the annual election of new directors reflected the conflict. Brower's supporters won a majority in 1968, but in the April 1969 election the anti-Brower candidates won all five open positions. Ansel Adams and president Richard Leonard, two of his closest friends on the board, led the opposition to Brower, charging him with financial recklessness and insubordination and calling for his ouster as executive director. The board voted ten to five to accept Brower's resignation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-history-8">[8]</a></sup> Eventually reconciled with the Club, Brower was elected to the board of directors for a term from 1983 to 1988, and again from 1995 to 2000.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="McCloskey_years">McCloskey years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: McCloskey years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Michael McCloskey, hired by Brower in 1961 as the Club's first northwest field representative, became the Club's second executive director in 1969. An administrator attentive to detail, McCloskey had set up the Club's conservation department in 1965 and guided the campaigns to save the Grand Canyon and establish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Redwoods_National_Park" title="Redwoods National Park">Redwoods National Park</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Cascades_National_Park" title="North Cascades National Park">North Cascades National Park</a>. During the 1970s, McCloskey led the Club's legislative activity—preserving <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaskan</a> lands and eastern wilderness areas, and supporting the new environmental agenda: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toxic_Substances_Control_Act" title="Toxic Substances Control Act">Toxic Substances Control Act</a> of 1976, the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)" title="Clean Air Act (United States)">Clean Air Act</a> amendments, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surface_Mining_Control_and_Reclamation_Act" title="Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act">Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act</a> of 1977, passed during the administration of President <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">Jimmy Carter</a>. The Sierra Club made its first presidential endorsement in 1984 in support of <a href="/wiki/Walter_Mondale" title="Walter Mondale">Walter Mondale</a>'s unsuccessful campaign to unseat <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a>. McCloskey resigned as executive director in 1985 after 16-1/2 years (the same length of time Brower had led the organization), and assumed the title of chairman, becoming the Club's senior strategist, devoting his time to conservation policy rather than budget planning and administration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> After a two-year interlude with Douglas Wheeler, whose Republican credentials were disconcerting to liberal members, the Club hired Michael Fischer, the former head of the <a href="/wiki/California_Coastal_Commission" title="California Coastal Commission">California Coastal Commission</a>, who served as executive director from 1987 to 1992. <a href="/wiki/Carl_Pope_(environmentalist)" title="Carl Pope (environmentalist)">Carl Pope</a>, formerly the Club's legislative director, was named executive director in 1992.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lobbying_within_the_club">Lobbying within the club</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Lobbying within the club">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 1990s, club members Jim Bensman, Roger Clarke, David Dilworth, Chad Hanson and David Orr along with about 2,000 members formed the John Muir Sierrans (JMS), an internal caucus, to promote changes to club positions. They favored a zero-cut forest policy on public lands and, a few years later, decommissioning Glen Canyon Dam. JMS was successful in changing club positions on both counts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In September 2005, the Sierra Club held its first Sierra Summit in San Francisco. Approximately 1,000 volunteers from around the country, selected by their chapters and groups, were delegates; some nondelegate members also attended. There were seminars and exhibit presentations about current environmental issues and about techniques for more effective activism. Prominent guest speakers included <a href="/wiki/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">Al Gore</a>; <a href="/wiki/Bill_Maher" title="Bill Maher">Bill Maher</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy,_Jr." title="Robert F. Kennedy, Jr.">Robert F. Kennedy, Jr.</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Arianna_Huffington" title="Arianna Huffington">Arianna Huffington</a>.
</p><p>In 2008, several Sierra Club officers quit in protest after the Sierra Club agreed to promote products by <a href="/wiki/Clorox" title="Clorox">Clorox</a>, which had been named one of a "dangerous dozen" chemical companies by the <a href="/wiki/Public_Interest_Research_Group" title="Public Interest Research Group">Public Interest Research Group</a> in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> According to Carl Pope, the Sierra Club chairman, the deal brought the Club 1.3 million US dollars over the four-year term of the contract.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-articles.latimes.com_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-articles.latimes.com-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Also in 2008, the Sierra Club endorsed <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senator</a> <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> for <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a>, citing "his strong record of support for clean air, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wetlands" title="Wetlands">wetlands</a> protection, and clean energy."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Between 2007 and 2010, the Sierra Club accepted over 25 million US dollars in donations from the gas industry, mostly from <a href="/wiki/Aubrey_McClendon" title="Aubrey McClendon">Aubrey McClendon</a>, CEO of <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Energy" title="Chesapeake Energy">Chesapeake Energy</a>, one of the biggest gas drilling companies in the US and a firm heavily involved in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fracking" title="Fracking">fracking</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 2011, Sierra Club chairman <a href="/wiki/Carl_Pope_(environmentalist)" title="Carl Pope (environmentalist)">Carl Pope</a> stepped down amid discontent about the Clorox deal and other issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-articles.latimes.com_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-articles.latimes.com-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>In January 2013, executive director <a href="/wiki/Michael_Brune" title="Michael Brune">Michael Brune</a> announced <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> that the Sierra Club would officially participate in the first civil disobedience action in its 120-year history as part of the ongoing protest calling on the Obama administration to reject the <a href="/wiki/Keystone_Pipeline" title="Keystone Pipeline">Keystone XL</a> tar sands pipeline, stating, "we are watching a global crisis unfold before our eyes, and to stand aside and let it happen – even though we know how to stop it – would be unconscionable." On 13 February 2013, Michael Brune was arrested along with forty-eight people, including civil rights leader <a href="/wiki/Julian_Bond" title="Julian Bond">Julian Bond</a> and NASA climate scientist <a href="/wiki/James_Hansen" title="James Hansen">James Hansen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2015, the Sierra Club appointed its first black president of the board of directors, <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Mair" title="Aaron Mair">Aaron Mair</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Politico-Green_groups_set_sights_on_diversity_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Politico-Green_groups_set_sights_on_diversity-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Sierra Club endorsed <a href="/wiki/Hillary_Clinton" title="Hillary Clinton">Hillary Clinton</a> in the 2016 U.S. presidential election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Outdoor_programs">Outdoor programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Outdoor programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mountaineering">Mountaineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Mountaineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1901, <a href="/wiki/William_Edward_Colby" title="William Edward Colby">William Colby</a> organized the first Sierra Club <a href="/wiki/Excursion" title="Excursion">excursion</a> to <a href="/wiki/Yosemite_Valley" title="Yosemite Valley">Yosemite Valley</a>. The annual <a href="/wiki/High_Trips" title="High Trips">High Trips</a> were led by <a href="/wiki/Mountaineering" title="Mountaineering">mountaineers</a>  such as <a href="/wiki/Francis_P._Farquhar" title="Francis P. Farquhar">Francis P. Farquhar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Nisbet_LeConte" title="Joseph Nisbet LeConte">Joseph Nisbet LeConte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norman_Clyde" title="Norman Clyde">Norman Clyde</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walter_A._Starr,_Jr." title="Walter A. Starr, Jr.">Walter A. Starr, Jr.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jules_Eichorn" title="Jules Eichorn">Jules Eichorn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glen_Dawson_(mountaineer)" title="Glen Dawson (mountaineer)">Glen Dawson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ansel_Adams" title="Ansel Adams">Ansel Adams</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_R._Brower" title="David R. Brower">David R. Brower</a>. A number of <a href="/wiki/First_ascent" title="First ascent">first ascents</a>  in the Sierra Nevada were made on Sierra Club outings. Sierra Club members were also early enthusiasts of rock climbing. In 1911, the first chapter was formed, Angeles, and it began conducting local excursions in the mountains surrounding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles</a> and throughout the West.
</p><p>Many major innovations in mountaineering have come from Sierra Club members. <a href="/wiki/Norman_Clyde" title="Norman Clyde">Norman Clyde</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jules_Eichorn" title="Jules Eichorn">Jules Eichorn</a>, considered among the most famous members of the club, completed many first ascents of peaks in the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Glen_Dawson_(mountaineer)" title="Glen Dawson (mountaineer)">Glen Dawson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Royal_Robbins" title="Royal Robbins">Royal Robbins</a>, often on trips sponsored and organized by the Rock Climbing Section of the Angeles Chapter, opened up much of the <a href="/wiki/Tahquitz_Peak" title="Tahquitz Peak">Tahquitz Peak</a> area to climbing, which at the time was said to contain some of the hardest routes in the world. The <a href="/wiki/Yosemite_Decimal_System" title="Yosemite Decimal System">Yosemite Decimal System</a>, the primary method of rating a route's difficulty used in the United States, was developed into its modern form at Tahquitz.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Steve_Roper" title="Steve Roper">Steve Roper</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Fifty_Classic_Climbs_of_North_America" title="Fifty Classic Climbs of North America">Fifty Classic Climbs of North America</a></i>, sponsored and published by the Sierra Club, is still considered one of the definitive rock climbing guidebooks in the United States.
</p><p>Due to an increasing focus on political activity and concerns about financial liability, mountaineering activity in the Sierra Club has subsided since the 1980s. Some chapters, mostly in California, continue to maintain large mountaineering programs.
</p><p>The club currently occasionally awards the <a href="/wiki/Francis_P._Farquhar_Mountaineering_Award" title="Francis P. Farquhar Mountaineering Award">Francis P. Farquhar Mountaineering Award</a> to outstanding member mountaineers.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hiking_and_Outings">Hiking and Outings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Hiking and Outings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, a number of Sierra Club leaders joined the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/10th_Mountain_Division_(United_States)" title="10th Mountain Division (United States)">10th Mountain Division</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-http://sierraclub.org/outings_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-http://sierraclub.org/outings-23">[23]</a></sup> Among them was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_R._Brower" title="David R. Brower">David R. Brower</a>, who managed the High Trip program from 1947 to 1954, while serving as a major in the Army Reserve.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>In many areas of the country, Sierra Club also organizes hiking tours. Sierra Club's website has a "hiking near me" function. Section "<a class="external text" href="http://sierraclub.org/near-you" rel="nofollow">Sierra Club Near You</a>" shows all the upcoming trips in nearby area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>The historic High Trips, sometimes large expeditions with more than a hundred participants and crew, have given way to smaller and more numerous excursions held across the United States and abroad. These outings form a major part of Sierra Club culture, and in some chapters, constitute the majority of member activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-http://sierraclub.org/outings_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-http://sierraclub.org/outings-23">[23]</a></sup> Other chapters, however, may sponsor very few outdoor or recreational activities, being focused solely on political advocacy. Generally, chapters in California are much more active with regard to outdoor activities.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sierra_Club_awards"><span id="Sierra_Club_awards"></span> Sierra Club awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Sierra Club awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ladybug,_Ready_for_Takeoff.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1649" data-file-width="1530" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Ladybug%2C_Ready_for_Takeoff.jpg/170px-Ladybug%2C_Ready_for_Takeoff.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Ladybug%2C_Ready_for_Takeoff.jpg/255px-Ladybug%2C_Ready_for_Takeoff.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Ladybug%2C_Ready_for_Takeoff.jpg/340px-Ladybug%2C_Ready_for_Takeoff.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ladybug,_Ready_for_Takeoff.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Ladybug, Ready for Takeoff</i> – Grand Prize Winner in the Sierra Club's April 2010 Trails Photo Contest</div></div></div>
<p>The Sierra Club presents a number of annual awards. These awards include the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club_John_Muir_Award" title="Sierra Club John Muir Award">Sierra Club John Muir Award</a>, which is the organization's top award.
The John Muir Award is given to an individual with "a distinguished record of achievement in national or international conservation causes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other awards given by the Sierra Club include the <a href="/wiki/Ansel_Adams_Award_for_Conservation_Photography" title="Ansel Adams Award for Conservation Photography">Ansel Adams Award for Conservation Photography</a>, which honors excellence in conservation photography; and the <a href="/wiki/Francis_P._Farquhar_Mountaineering_Award" title="Francis P. Farquhar Mountaineering Award">Francis P. Farquhar Mountaineering Award</a>, which recognizes contributions to mountaineering.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Policy_positions">Policy positions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Policy positions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_management">Land management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Land management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Land management, access, conservation are traditionally considered the core advocacy areas of the Sierra Club. Uniquely for a progressive organization, the Sierra Club has strong grassroots organization in rural areas, with much activity focused on ensuring equitable and environmentally-friendly use of public lands. This is particularly accentuated by the fact that the club attracts many people who primarily join the club for recreation and use of public land for hiking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Culhane2013_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Culhane2013-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some Sierra Club members have urged the Club to be more forceful in advocating for the protection of <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Forest" title="United States National Forest">National Forests</a> and other federally owned public lands. For example, in 2002 the Club was criticized for joining with the <a href="/wiki/The_Wilderness_Society_(United_States)" title="The Wilderness Society (United States)">Wilderness Society</a> in agreeing to a compromise that would allow logging in the <a href="/wiki/Black_Hills" title="Black Hills">Black Hills</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Dakota" title="South Dakota">South Dakota</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition_to_coal">Opposition to coal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Opposition to coal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Beyond_Coal" title="Beyond Coal">Beyond Coal</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil-fuel_phase-out" title="Fossil-fuel phase-out">fossil-fuel phase-out</a></div>
<p>A goal of the Sierra Club is to replace coal with other energy sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Through its "<a href="/wiki/Beyond_Coal" title="Beyond Coal">Beyond Coal</a>" campaign, the Sierra Club has set a goal to close half of all coal plants in the U.S. by 2017. American business magnate and former New York City mayor <a href="/wiki/Michael_Bloomberg" title="Michael Bloomberg">Michael Bloomberg</a> donated $50 million to the Sierra Club's anti-coal work in 2011, and announced another $30 million gift to Sierra's Beyond Coal campaign in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The Beyond Coal campaign says 187 coal plants have been closed since 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Other funders of the Sierra Club's anti-coal campaign include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_and_Flora_Hewlett_Foundation" title="William and Flora Hewlett Foundation">William and Flora Hewlett Foundation</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_D._and_Catherine_T._MacArthur_Foundation" title="John D. and Catherine T. MacArthur Foundation">John D. and Catherine T. MacArthur Foundation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The CEO of <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Energy" title="Chesapeake Energy">Chesapeake Energy</a>, a natural gas company, donated $26 million to the Beyond Coal campaign between 2007 and 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beyond_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-beyond-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Sierra Club is also opposed to nuclear energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition_to_dams">Opposition to dams</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Opposition to dams">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Sierra Club opposes dams it considers inappropriate, including some government-built dams in national parks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In the early 20th century, the organization fought against the damming and flooding of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hetch_Hetchy_Valley" title="Hetch Hetchy Valley">Hetch Hetchy Valley</a> in <a href="/wiki/Yosemite_National_Park" title="Yosemite National Park">Yosemite National Park</a>. Despite this lobbying, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congress</a> authorized the construction of <a href="/wiki/Hetch_Hetchy" title="Hetch Hetchy">O'Shaughnessy Dam</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Tuolumne_River" title="Tuolumne River">Tuolumne River</a>. The Sierra Club continues to support removal of the dam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Sierra Club advocates the decommissioning of <a href="/wiki/Glen_Canyon_Dam" title="Glen Canyon Dam">Glen Canyon Dam</a> and the draining of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Powell" title="Lake Powell">Lake Powell</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The Club also supports removal, breaching or decommissioning of many other dams, including four dams on the lower <a href="/wiki/Snake_River" title="Snake River">Snake River</a> in eastern <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alliance_with_organized_labor">Alliance with organized labor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Alliance with organized labor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Sierra Club is a member of the BlueGreen Alliance, a coalition of environmental groups and labor unions.  The BlueGreen Alliance was formed in 2006 and grew out of a less-formal collaboration between the Sierra Club and the <a href="/wiki/United_Steelworkers" title="United Steelworkers">United Steelworkers</a>. In 2012, the <a href="/wiki/Laborers%27_International_Union_of_North_America" title="Laborers' International Union of North America">Laborers' International Union of North America</a> left the coalition due to the Sierra Club and other environmental groups' opposition to the <a href="/wiki/Keystone_Pipeline" title="Keystone Pipeline">Keystone Pipeline</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Population_and_immigration">Population and immigration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Population and immigration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Immigration was historically among the most divisive issues within the club. In 1996, after years of debate, the Sierra Club adopted a neutral position on immigration levels. As the club has shifted to the left over the years, this position was amended in 2013 to support "an equitable path to citizenship for undocumented immigrants".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colorlines_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-colorlines-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="History_2">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Although the position of the Sierra Club has generally been favorable towards immigration, some critics of the Sierra Club have charged that the efforts of some club members to restrain immigration, are a continuation of aspects of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_population_control" title="Human population control">human population control</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eugenics_movement" title="Eugenics movement">eugenics movement</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> In 1969, the Sierra Club published <a href="/wiki/Paul_R._Ehrlich" title="Paul R. Ehrlich">Paul R. Ehrlich</a>'s book, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Population_Bomb" title="The Population Bomb">The Population Bomb</a></i>, in which he said that population growth was responsible for environmental decline and advocated coercive measures to reduce it. Some observers have argued that the book had a "racial dimension" in the tradition of the <a href="/wiki/Eugenics" title="Eugenics">Eugenics</a> movement, and that it "reiterated many of <a href="/wiki/Henry_Fairfield_Osborn" title="Henry Fairfield Osborn">Osborn's</a> jeremiads."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monitor_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-monitor-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stern_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-stern-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the 1980s, some Sierra Club members, including Paul Ehrlich's wife <a href="/wiki/Anne_H._Ehrlich" title="Anne H. Ehrlich">Anne</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-monitor_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-monitor-46">[46]</a></sup> wanted to take the Club into the contentious field of <a href="/wiki/Immigration_to_the_United_States" title="Immigration to the United States">immigration to the United States</a>. The Club's position was that <a href="/wiki/Human_overpopulation" title="Human overpopulation">overpopulation</a> was a significant factor in the degradation of the environment. Accordingly, the Club supported stabilizing and reducing U.S. and world population. Some members argued that, as a practical matter, U.S. population could not be stabilized, let alone reduced, at the then-current levels of immigration. They urged the Club to support <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Immigration_reduction" title="Immigration reduction">immigration reduction</a>. The Club had previously addressed the issue of "mass immigration",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> and in 1988, the organization's Population Committee and Conservation Coordinating Committee stated that immigration to the U.S. should be limited, so as to achieve population stabilization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other Sierra Club members thought that the immigration issue was too far from the Club's core environmentalist mission, and were also concerned that involvement would impair the organization's political ability to pursue its other objectives. In 1998, 60.1% of Sierra Club voting members voted that the organization should remain neutral on America's immigration policies, while 39.2% supported a measure calling for stricter curbs on immigration to the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1996, after the Sierra Club board adopted a neutral position on immigration policy, some members who were advocates of immigration reduction organized themselves as "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=SUSPS&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="SUSPS (page does not exist)">SUSPS</a>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> – a name originally derived from "Sierrans for U.S. Population Stabilization", which now stands for "Support U.S. Population Stabilization."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> SUSPS advocates a return to the Sierra Club's "traditional" (1970–1996) immigration policy stance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> SUSPS has called for fully closing the borders of the United States, and for returning to immigration levels established by the <a href="/wiki/Immigration_Act_of_1924" title="Immigration Act of 1924">Immigration Act of 1924</a>, which includes strict ethnic quotas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>The controversy resurfaced when a group of three immigration reduction proponents ran in the 2004 Sierra Club Board of Directors election, hoping to move the Club's position away from a neutral stance on immigration, and to restore the stance previously held.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Groups outside of the Club became involved, such as the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Poverty_Law_Center" title="Southern Poverty Law Center">Southern Poverty Law Center</a> and <a href="/wiki/MoveOn" title="MoveOn">MoveOn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Of the three candidates, two (Frank Morris and David Pimentel), were on the board of the anti-immigration group Diversity Alliance for a Sustainable America<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Intelligence_Report_2004,_p._57_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Intelligence_Report_2004,_p._57-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> and two (<a href="/wiki/Richard_Lamm" title="Richard Lamm">Richard Lamm</a> and Frank Morris) were on the board of directors or the board of advisors of the <a href="/wiki/Federation_for_American_Immigration_Reform" title="Federation for American Immigration Reform">Federation for American Immigration Reform</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Intelligence_Report_2004,_p._57_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-Intelligence_Report_2004,_p._57-59">[59]</a></sup> both had also held leadership positions within the <a href="/wiki/NAACP" title="NAACP">NAACP</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Their candidacies were denounced by a fourth candidate, <a href="/wiki/Morris_Dees" title="Morris Dees">Morris Dees</a> of the SPLC, as a "hostile takeover" attempt by "radical anti-immigrant activists."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> The immigration reduction proponents won 7% of all votes cast in the election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> In 2005, members voted 102,455 to 19,898 against a proposed change to "recognize the need to adopt lower limits on migration to the United States."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p><p>With the increased number of progressive activists joining the club in recent years, the Sierra Club has dramatically shifted its stance on immigration further towards the affirmative. Today, the Sierra Club supports a path to citizenship for undocumented immigrants, and works with immigrant groups to promote environmental justice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-colorlines_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-colorlines-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy_over_housing_stance">Controversy over housing stance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Controversy over housing stance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Sierra Club has come under criticism for opposing high-density housing development projects in California, which are intended to reduce housing shortage and reduce greenhouse gas emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-67">[67]</a></sup> Ethan Elkind, director of the climate program at the Center for Law, Energy and the Environment (CLEE) at UC Berkeley Law, said that the Sierra Club's opposition to <a href="/wiki/California_Senate_Bill_827" title="California Senate Bill 827">California Senate Bill 827</a> - which would require cities to allow denser and taller housing near public transport centrals, and ease the parking requirements that cities can impose on housing developments - was "surprising".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-67">[67]</a></sup> He wrote, "is Sierra Club an organization of wealthy homeowners who want to keep newcomers out of their upscale, transit-rich areas? Or are they actually committed to fighting climate change by providing enough housing for Californians in low-carbon, infill areas? Because their opposition to SB 827 unfortunately indicates more of the former than the latter."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_67-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Affiliates_and_subsidiaries">Affiliates and subsidiaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Affiliates and subsidiaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club_Foundation" title="Sierra Club Foundation">Sierra Club Foundation</a> was founded in 1960 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_R._Brower" title="David R. Brower">David R. Brower</a>. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/501(c)#501(c)(3)" title="501(c)">501(c)(3)</a> organization, it was founded after the <a href="/wiki/Internal_Revenue_Service" title="Internal Revenue Service">Internal Revenue Service</a> revoked the Sierra Club's tax-exempt status due to the group's political activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Sierra Club added its first Canadian chapter in 1963 and in 1989 opened a national office in Ottawa. Canadian affiliates of the Sierra Club operate under the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club_Canada" title="Sierra Club Canada">Sierra Club Canada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1971, volunteer lawyers who had worked with the Sierra Club established the Sierra Club Legal Defense Fund. This was a separate organization that used the "Sierra Club" name under license from the Club; it changed its name to <a href="/wiki/Earthjustice" title="Earthjustice">Earthjustice</a> in 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Student_Coalition" title="Sierra Student Coalition">Sierra Student Coalition</a> (SSC) is the student-run arm of the Sierra Club. Founded by <a href="/wiki/Adam_Werbach" title="Adam Werbach">Adam Werbach</a> in 1991, it has 30,000 members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Sierra Club Voter Education Fund is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/527_group" title="527 group">527 group</a> that became active in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._presidential_election,_2004" title="U.S. presidential election, 2004">2004 Presidential election</a> by airing television advertisements about the major party candidates' positions on environmental issues. Through the Environmental Voter Education Campaign (EVEC), the Club sought to mobilize volunteers for phone banking, door-to-door canvassing and postcard writing to emphasize these issues in the campaign.
</p><p>The organization maintains a publishing imprint, <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club_Books" title="Sierra Club Books">Sierra Club Books</a>, publishing books on environmental issues, wilderness photographic essays, nature guides, and other related subjects. They publish the Sierra Club Calendars, perennial bestsellers, featuring photographs by well-known nature photographers such as <a href="/wiki/Galen_Rowell" title="Galen Rowell">Galen Rowell</a>. They also publish the <a href="/wiki/John_Muir" title="John Muir">John Muir</a> library, which includes many of their founder's titles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Wilderness_Travel_Course" title="Wilderness Travel Course">Wilderness Travel Course</a> is a basic mountaineering class that is administered by the Sierra Club.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Budget_and_funding">Budget and funding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Budget and funding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Sierra Club's annual budget was $88 million in 2011 and $100 million in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> In 2013, the group's budget was $97.8 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guidestar_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-guidestar-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2008, <a href="/wiki/Clorox" title="Clorox">Clorox</a> donated $1.3 million to the Sierra Club in exchange for the right to display the Sierra Club's logo on a line of cleaning products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pope_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-pope-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2012, it was reported that the Sierra Club had secretly accepted over $26 million in gifts from the natural gas industry, mostly from <a href="/wiki/Aubrey_McClendon" title="Aubrey McClendon">Aubrey McClendon</a>, CEO of <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Energy" title="Chesapeake Energy">Chesapeake Energy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> The Sierra Club used the Chesapeake Energy money for its Beyond Coal campaign to block new coal-fired power plants and close old ones. Michael Brune reported that he learned of the gifts after he succeeded Carl Pope as executive director of the Sierra Club in 2010. Brune reported that he ended the financial agreement with natural gas industry interests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beyond_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-beyond-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2014, the Energy and Environment Legal Institute filed a referral with the <a href="/wiki/Internal_Revenue_Service" title="Internal Revenue Service">Internal Revenue Service</a> pointing out that Sierra Club and Sierra Club Foundation were not paying income taxes from sales of solar panels for their partners across the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Sierra Club has an affiliated super PAC. It spent $1,000,575 on the 2014 elections, all of it opposing Republican candidates for office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> The Sierra Club is a partner of <a href="/wiki/America_Votes" title="America Votes">America Votes</a>, an organization that coordinates and promotes progressive issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p><p>Donors to the Sierra Club have included <a href="/wiki/David_Gelbaum" title="David Gelbaum">David Gelbaum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Bloomberg" title="Michael Bloomberg">Michael Bloomberg</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_and_Flora_Hewlett_Foundation" title="William and Flora Hewlett Foundation">William and Flora Hewlett Foundation</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_D._and_Catherine_T._MacArthur_Foundation" title="John D. and Catherine T. MacArthur Foundation">John D. and Catherine T. MacArthur Foundation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> The Sierra Club has also received funding from the <a href="/wiki/Democracy_Alliance" title="Democracy Alliance">Democracy Alliance</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tides_(organization)#The_Advocacy_Fund" title="Tides (organization)">Tides Foundation Advocacy Fund</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2015, a PR front group, known as the Environmental Policy Alliance, claimed that the Sierra Club and other U.S. environmental groups received funding from groups with ties to Russia's state-owned oil company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p>
