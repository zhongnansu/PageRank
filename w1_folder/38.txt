<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Cement</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Cement_(disambiguation)" title="Cement (disambiguation)">Cement (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">Concrete</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mortar_(masonry)" title="Mortar (masonry)">mortar (masonry)</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:USMC-110806-M-IX060-148.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5616" data-file-width="3744" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/USMC-110806-M-IX060-148.jpg/220px-USMC-110806-M-IX060-148.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/USMC-110806-M-IX060-148.jpg/330px-USMC-110806-M-IX060-148.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/USMC-110806-M-IX060-148.jpg/440px-USMC-110806-M-IX060-148.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:USMC-110806-M-IX060-148.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cement is often supplied as a powder, which is mixed with other materials and water.</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>cement</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Binder_(material)" title="Binder (material)">binder</a>, a substance used for construction that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solidification" title="Solidification">sets</a>, hardens, and adheres to other <a href="/wiki/Material" title="Material">materials</a> to bind them together. Cement is seldom used on its own, but rather to bind sand and gravel (<a href="/wiki/Construction_aggregate" title="Construction aggregate">aggregate</a>) together. Cement mixed with fine aggregate produces <a href="/wiki/Mortar_(masonry)" title="Mortar (masonry)">mortar</a> for masonry, or with <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gravel" title="Gravel">gravel</a>, produces <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a>.
</p><p>Cements used in construction are usually <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inorganic" title="Inorganic">inorganic</a>, often <a href="/wiki/Lime_(material)" title="Lime (material)">lime</a> or <a href="/wiki/Calcium_silicate" title="Calcium silicate">calcium silicate</a> based, and can be characterized as either <b>hydraulic</b> or <b>non-hydraulic</b>, depending on the ability of the cement to set in the presence of water (see <a href="/wiki/Lime_mortar#Hydraulic_and_non-hydraulic_lime" title="Lime mortar">hydraulic and non-hydraulic lime plaster</a>).
</p><p><b>Non-hydraulic cement</b> does not set in wet conditions or under water. Rather, it sets as it dries and reacts with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> in the air. It is resistant to attack by chemicals after setting.
</p><p><b>Hydraulic cements</b> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Portland_cement" title="Portland cement">Portland cement</a>) set and become <a href="/wiki/Adhesive" title="Adhesive">adhesive</a> due to a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reaction</a> between the dry ingredients and water. The chemical reaction results in mineral <a href="/wiki/Hydrate" title="Hydrate">hydrates</a> that are not very water-soluble and so are quite durable in water and safe from chemical attack. This allows setting in wet conditions or under water and further protects the hardened material from chemical attack. The chemical process for hydraulic cement found by ancient Romans used <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_ash" title="Volcanic ash">volcanic ash</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pozzolana" title="Pozzolana">pozzolana</a>) with added lime (calcium oxide).
</p><p>The word "cement" can be traced back to the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> term <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opus_caementicium" title="Opus caementicium">opus caementicium</a></i>, used to describe <a href="/wiki/Masonry" title="Masonry">masonry</a> resembling modern <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a> that was made from crushed rock with <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">burnt lime</a> as binder. The volcanic ash and pulverized <a href="/wiki/Brick" title="Brick">brick</a> supplements that were added to the burnt lime, to obtain a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hydraulic_binder&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hydraulic binder (page does not exist)">hydraulic binder</a>, were later referred to as <i>cementum</i>, <i>cimentum</i>, <i>cäment</i>, and <i>cement</i>. In modern times, organic polymers are sometimes used as cements in concrete.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Non-hydraulic cement</b>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slaked_lime" title="Slaked lime">slaked lime</a> (<a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">calcium oxide</a> mixed with water), hardens by <a href="/wiki/Carbonation" title="Carbonation">carbonation</a> in the presence of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, which is naturally present in the air. First <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">calcium oxide</a> (lime) is produced from <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> (limestone or chalk) by <a href="/wiki/Calcination" title="Calcination">calcination</a> at temperatures above 825 °C (1,517 °F) for about 10 hours at <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>CaCO<sub>3</sub> → CaO + CO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>The calcium oxide is then <i>spent</i> (slaked) mixing it with water to make slaked lime (calcium hydroxide):
</p>
<dl><dd>CaO + H<sub>2</sub>O → Ca(OH)<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Once the excess water is completely evaporated (this process is technically called <i>setting</i>), the carbonation starts:
</p>
<dl><dd>Ca(OH)<sub>2</sub> + CO<sub>2</sub> → CaCO<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>This reaction takes time, because the partial pressure of carbon dioxide in the air is low. The carbonation reaction requires that the dry cement be exposed to air, so the slaked lime is a non-hydraulic cement and cannot be used under water. This process is called the <i>lime cycle</i>.
</p><p>Conversely, <b>hydraulic cement</b> hardens by hydration when water is added. Hydraulic cements (such as <a href="/wiki/Portland_cement" title="Portland cement">Portland cement</a>) are made of a mixture of silicates and oxides, the four main components being:
</p>
<dl><dd><a href="/wiki/Belite" title="Belite">Belite</a> (2CaO·SiO<sub>2</sub>);</dd>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Alite" title="Alite">Alite</a> (3CaO·SiO<sub>2</sub>);</dd>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Tricalcium_aluminate" title="Tricalcium aluminate">Tricalcium aluminate</a> (3CaO·Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) (historically, and still occasionally, called 'celite');</dd>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Brownmillerite" title="Brownmillerite">Brownmillerite</a> (4CaO·Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>·Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>).</dd></dl>
<p>The silicates are responsible for the cement's mechanical properties—the tricalcium aluminate and brownmillerite are essential for formation of the liquid phase during the kiln sintering (firing). The chemistry of these reactions is not completely clear and is still the object of research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Perhaps the earliest known occurrence of cement is from twelve million years ago. A deposit of cement was formed after an occurrence of oil shale located adjacent to a bed of limestone burned due to natural causes. These ancient deposits were investigated in the 1960s and 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MAST_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-MAST-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternatives_to_cement_used_in_antiquity">Alternatives to cement used in antiquity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Alternatives to cement used in antiquity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cement, chemically speaking, is a product that includes lime as the primary curing ingredient, but is far from the first material used for cement<i>ation</i>. The Babylonians and Assyrians used <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bitumen" title="Bitumen">bitumen</a> to bind together burnt brick or alabaster slabs. In Egypt stone blocks were cemented together with a <a href="/wiki/Mortar_(masonry)" title="Mortar (masonry)">mortar</a> made of <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a> and roughly burnt <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a> (CaSO<sub>4</sub>·2H<sub>2</sub>O), which often contained calcium carbonate (CaCO<sub>3</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blezard_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blezard-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Macedonians_and_Romans">Macedonians and Romans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Macedonians and Romans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lime (calcium oxide) was used on Crete and by the ancient Greeks. There is evidence that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minoans" title="Minoans">Minoans</a> of Crete used crushed potshards as an artificial pozzolan for hydraulic cement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blezard_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blezard-3">[3]</a></sup> Nobody knows who first discovered that a combination of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slaked_lime" title="Slaked lime">hydrated non-hydraulic lime</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Pozzolan" title="Pozzolan">pozzolan</a> produces a hydraulic mixture (see also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pozzolanic_reaction" title="Pozzolanic reaction">Pozzolanic reaction</a>)—but such concrete was used by the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Macedonians" title="Ancient Macedonians">Ancient Macedonians</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and three centuries later on a large scale by <a href="/wiki/Roman_engineering" title="Roman engineering">Roman engineers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>There is... a kind of powder which from natural causes produces astonishing results. It is found in the neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/Baiae" title="Baiae">Baiae</a> and in the country belonging to the towns round about Mt. Vesuvius. This substance when mixed with lime and rubble not only lends strength to buildings of other kinds, but even when piers of it are constructed in the sea, they set hard under water.</p><div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>Marcus Vitruvius Pollio, Liber II, <i>De Architectura</i>, Chapter VI "Pozzolana" Sec. 1</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>The Greeks used volcanic tuff from the island of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thera" title="Thera">Thera</a> as their pozzolan and the Romans used crushed volcanic ash (activated <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_silicate" title="Aluminium silicate">aluminium silicates</a>) with lime. This mixture could set under water, increasing its resistance.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="resistance to what? (October 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The material was called <i>pozzolana</i> from the town of <a href="/wiki/Pozzuoli" title="Pozzuoli">Pozzuoli</a>, west of Naples where volcanic ash was extracted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ridi2010_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ridi2010-7">[7]</a></sup> In the absence of pozzolanic ash, the Romans used powdered brick or pottery as a substitute and they may have used crushed tiles for this purpose before discovering natural sources near Rome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blezard_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Blezard-3">[3]</a></sup> The huge <a href="/wiki/Dome" title="Dome">dome</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Pantheon,_Rome" title="Pantheon, Rome">Pantheon</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> and the massive <a href="/wiki/Baths_of_Caracalla" title="Baths of Caracalla">Baths of Caracalla</a> are examples of ancient structures made from these concretes, many of which still stand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The vast system of <a href="/wiki/Roman_aqueduct" title="Roman aqueduct">Roman aqueducts</a> also made extensive use of hydraulic cement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Roman concrete was rarely used on the outside of buildings. The normal technique was to use brick facing material as the formwork for an infill of mortar mixed with an aggregate of broken pieces of stone, brick, <a href="/wiki/Sherd" title="Sherd">potsherds</a>, recycled chunks of concrete, or other building rubble.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cowan_1975_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cowan_1975-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Any preservation of this knowledge in literature from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> is unknown, but medieval <a href="/wiki/Masonry" title="Masonry">masons</a> and some military engineers actively used hydraulic cement in structures such as <a href="/wiki/Canal" title="Canal">canals</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fortress" title="Fortress">fortresses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harbor" title="Harbor">harbors</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Shipyard" title="Shipyard">shipbuilding facilities</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sismondo_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sismondo-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mukerji_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mukerji-12">[12]</a></sup> A mixture of lime mortar and aggregate with brick or stone facing material was used in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Roman_Empire" title="Eastern Roman Empire">Eastern Roman Empire</a> as well as in the West into the <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic period</a>. The German <a href="/wiki/Rhineland" title="Rhineland">Rhineland</a> continued to use hydraulic mortar throughout the Middle Ages, having local pozzolana deposits called <a href="/wiki/Trass" title="Trass">trass</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cowan_1975_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cowan_1975-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="16th_century">16th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 16th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tabby_(cement)" title="Tabby (cement)">Tabby</a> is a building material made from oyster-shell lime, sand, and whole oyster shells to form a concrete. The Spanish introduced it to the Americas in the sixteenth century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.com_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.com-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="18th_century">18th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 18th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The technical knowledge for making hydraulic cement was formalized by French and British engineers in the 18th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sismondo_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sismondo-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/John_Smeaton" title="John Smeaton">John Smeaton</a> made an important contribution to the development of cements while planning the construction of the third <a href="/wiki/Eddystone_Lighthouse" title="Eddystone Lighthouse">Eddystone Lighthouse</a> (1755–59) in the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a> now known as <a href="/wiki/Smeaton%27s_Tower" title="Smeaton's Tower">Smeaton's Tower</a>. He needed a hydraulic mortar that would set and develop some strength in the twelve-hour period between successive high tides. He performed experiments with combinations of different limestones and additives including trass and <a href="/wiki/Pozzolana" title="Pozzolana">pozzolanas</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blezard_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Blezard-3">[3]</a></sup> and did exhaustive market research on the available hydraulic limes, visiting their production sites, and noted that the "hydraulicity" of the lime was directly related to the clay content of the <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> used to make it. Smeaton was a <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineer" title="Civil engineer">civil engineer</a> by profession, and took the idea no further.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_coastal_plain" title="Atlantic coastal plain">South Atlantic seaboard</a> of the United States, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tabby_(cement)" title="Tabby (cement)">tabby</a> relying on the oyster-shell <a href="/wiki/Midden" title="Midden">middens</a> of earlier Native American populations was used in house construction from the 1730s to the 1860s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.com_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.com-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a> particularly, good quality building stone became ever more expensive during a period of rapid growth, and it became a common practice to construct prestige buildings from the new industrial bricks, and to finish them with a <a href="/wiki/Stucco" title="Stucco">stucco</a> to imitate stone. Hydraulic limes were favored for this, but the need for a fast set time encouraged the development of new cements. Most famous was Parker's "<a href="/wiki/Roman_cement" title="Roman cement">Roman cement</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> This was developed by <a href="/wiki/James_Parker_(cement_maker)" title="James Parker (cement maker)">James Parker</a> in the 1780s, and finally patented in 1796. It was, in fact, nothing like material used by the Romans, but was a "natural cement" made by burning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Septarian_concretion" title="Septarian concretion">septaria</a> – nodules that are found in certain clay deposits, and that contain both <a href="/wiki/Clay_minerals" title="Clay minerals">clay minerals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>. The burnt <a href="/wiki/Nodule_(geology)" title="Nodule (geology)">nodules</a> were ground to a fine powder. This product, made into a mortar with sand, set in 5–15 minutes. The success of "Roman cement" led other manufacturers to develop rival products by burning artificial <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_lime" title="Hydraulic lime">hydraulic lime</a> cements of <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a>.
Roman cement quickly became popular but was largely replaced by Portland cement in the 1850s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blezard_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Blezard-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Apparently unaware of Smeaton's work, the same principle was identified by Frenchman <a href="/wiki/Louis_Vicat" title="Louis Vicat">Louis Vicat</a> in the first decade of the nineteenth century. Vicat went on to devise a method of combining chalk and clay into an intimate mixture, and, burning this, produced an "artificial cement" in 1817<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> considered the "principal forerunner"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blezard_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-Blezard-3">[3]</a></sup> of Portland cement and "...Edgar Dobbs of Southwark patented a cement of this kind in 1811."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blezard_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-Blezard-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>In Russia, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Egor_Cheliev&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Egor Cheliev (page does not exist)">Egor Cheliev</a> created a new binder by mixing lime and clay. His results were published in 1822 in his book <i>A Treatise on the Art to Prepare a Good Mortar</i> published in St. Petersburg. A few years later in 1825, he published another book, which described various methods of making cement and concrete, and the benefits of cement in the construction of buildings and embankments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:William_Aspdin_Radford_cyclopedia_Volume_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="644" data-file-width="464" height="236" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/William_Aspdin_Radford_cyclopedia_Volume_1.jpg/170px-William_Aspdin_Radford_cyclopedia_Volume_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/William_Aspdin_Radford_cyclopedia_Volume_1.jpg/255px-William_Aspdin_Radford_cyclopedia_Volume_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/William_Aspdin_Radford_cyclopedia_Volume_1.jpg/340px-William_Aspdin_Radford_cyclopedia_Volume_1.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:William_Aspdin_Radford_cyclopedia_Volume_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/William_Aspdin" title="William Aspdin">William Aspdin</a> is considered the inventor of "modern" <a href="/wiki/Portland_cement" title="Portland cement">Portland cement</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-William_Aspdin_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-William_Aspdin-18">[18]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Portland_cement" title="Portland cement">Portland cement</a>, the most common type of cement in general use around the world as a basic ingredient of <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mortar_(masonry)" title="Mortar (masonry)">mortar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stucco" title="Stucco">stucco</a>, and non-speciality <a href="/wiki/Grout" title="Grout">grout</a>, was developed in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> in the mid 19th century, and usually originates from <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>. <a href="/wiki/James_Frost_(cement_maker)" title="James Frost (cement maker)">James Frost</a> produced what he called "British cement" in a similar manner around the same time, but did not obtain a patent until 1822.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In 1824, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Aspdin" title="Joseph Aspdin">Joseph Aspdin</a> patented a similar material, which he called <i>Portland cement</i>, because the render made from it was in color similar to the prestigious <a href="/wiki/Portland_stone" title="Portland stone">Portland stone</a> quarried on the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Portland" title="Isle of Portland">Isle of Portland</a>, Dorset, England. However, Aspdins' cement was nothing like modern Portland cement but was a first step in its development, called a <i>proto-Portland cement</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blezard_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-Blezard-3">[3]</a></sup> Joseph Aspdins' son <a href="/wiki/William_Aspdin" title="William Aspdin">William Aspdin</a> had left his fathers company and in his cement manufacturing apparently accidentally produced <a href="/wiki/Calcium_silicate" title="Calcium silicate">calcium silicates</a> in the 1840s, a middle step in the development of Portland cement. William Aspdin's innovation was counterintuitive for manufacturers of "artificial cements", because they required more lime in the mix (a problem for his father), a much higher kiln temperature (and therefore more fuel), and the resulting <a href="/wiki/Clinker_(cement)" title="Clinker (cement)">clinker</a> was very hard and rapidly wore down the <a href="/wiki/Millstone" title="Millstone">millstones</a>, which were the only available grinding technology of the time. Manufacturing costs were therefore considerably higher, but the product set reasonably slowly and developed strength quickly, thus opening up a market for use in concrete. The use of concrete in construction grew rapidly from 1850 onward, and was soon the dominant use for cements. Thus Portland cement began its predominant role. <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Charles_Johnson" title="Isaac Charles Johnson">Isaac Charles Johnson</a> further refined the production of <i>meso-Portland cement</i> (middle stage of development) and claimed he was the real father of Portland cement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Setting time and "early strength" are important characteristics of cements. Hydraulic limes, "natural" cements, and "artificial" cements all rely on their <a href="/wiki/Belite" title="Belite">belite</a> content for <a href="/wiki/Physical_strength" title="Physical strength">strength</a> development. Belite develops strength slowly. Because they were burned at temperatures below 1,250 °C (2,280 °F), they contained no <a href="/wiki/Alite" title="Alite">alite</a>, which is responsible for early strength in modern cements. The first cement to consistently contain alite was made by William Aspdin in the early 1840s: This was what we call today "modern" Portland cement. Because of the air of mystery with which William Aspdin surrounded his product, others (<i>e.g.,</i> Vicat and Johnson) have claimed precedence in this invention, but recent analysis<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> of both his concrete and raw cement have shown that William Aspdin's product made at <a href="/wiki/Northfleet" title="Northfleet">Northfleet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a> was a true alite-based cement. However, Aspdin's methods were "rule-of-thumb": Vicat is responsible for establishing the chemical basis of these cements, and Johnson established the importance of <a href="/wiki/Sintering" title="Sintering">sintering</a> the mix in the kiln.
</p><p>In the US the first large-scale use of cement was <a href="/wiki/Rosendale_cement" title="Rosendale cement">Rosendale cement</a>, a natural cement mined from a massive deposit of a large <a href="/wiki/Dolostone" title="Dolostone">dolostone rock</a> deposit discovered in the early 19th century near <a href="/wiki/Rosendale,_New_York" title="Rosendale, New York">Rosendale, New York</a>. Rosendale cement was extremely popular for the foundation of buildings (<i>e.g.</i>, <a href="/wiki/Statue_of_Liberty" title="Statue of Liberty">Statue of Liberty</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Capitol" title="United States Capitol">Capitol Building</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Bridge" title="Brooklyn Bridge">Brooklyn Bridge</a>) and lining water pipes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Natural_Cement_Comes_Back_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Natural_Cement_Comes_Back-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sorel_cement" title="Sorel cement">Sorel cement</a> was patented in 1867 by Frenchman <a href="/wiki/Stanislas_Sorel" title="Stanislas Sorel">Stanislas Sorel</a>. It was stronger than Portland cement but its poor water resistance and corrosive qualities limited its use in building construction. The next development in the manufacture of Portland cement was the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Rotary_kiln" title="Rotary kiln">rotary kiln</a>, which produced a stronger, more homogeneous mixture and facilitated a continuous manufacturing process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blezard_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-Blezard-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Factory_of_National_Cement_Share_Company.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3744" data-file-width="5616" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Factory_of_National_Cement_Share_Company.jpg/220px-Factory_of_National_Cement_Share_Company.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Factory_of_National_Cement_Share_Company.jpg/330px-Factory_of_National_Cement_Share_Company.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Factory_of_National_Cement_Share_Company.jpg/440px-Factory_of_National_Cement_Share_Company.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Factory_of_National_Cement_Share_Company.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The National Cement Share Company of <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>'s new plant in <a href="/wiki/Dire_Dawa" title="Dire Dawa">Dire Dawa</a>.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Calcium_aluminate_cements" title="Calcium aluminate cements">Calcium aluminate cements</a> were patented in 1908 in France by Jules Bied for better resistance to sulfates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the US, after World War One, the long <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Curing_time&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Curing time (page does not exist)">curing time</a> of at least a month for <a href="/wiki/Rosendale_cement" title="Rosendale cement">Rosendale cement</a> made it unpopular for constructing highways and bridges, and many states and construction firms turned to Portland cement. Because of the switch to Portland cement, by the end of the 1920s only one of the 15 Rosendale cement companies had survived. But in the early 1930s, builders discovered that, while Portland cement set faster, it was not as durable, especially for highways—to the point that some states stopped building highways and roads with cement. Bertrain H. Wait, an engineer whose company had helped construct the New York City's <a href="/wiki/Catskill_Aqueduct" title="Catskill Aqueduct">Catskill Aqueduct</a>, was impressed with the durability of Rosendale cement, and came up with a blend of both Rosendale and synthetic cements that had the good attributes of both. It was highly durable and had a much faster setting time. Wait convinced the New York Commissioner of Highways to construct an experimental section of highway near <a href="/wiki/New_Paltz,_New_York" title="New Paltz, New York">New Paltz, New York</a>, using one sack of Rosendale to six sacks of synthetic cement. It was a success, and for decades the Rosendale-synthetic cement blend was used in highway and bridge construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Natural_Cement_Comes_Back_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Natural_Cement_Comes_Back-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_cements">Modern cements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Modern cements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Modern hydraulic development began with the start of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> (around 1800), driven by three main needs:
</p>
<ul><li>Hydraulic <a href="/wiki/Cement_render" title="Cement render">cement render</a> (<a href="/wiki/Stucco" title="Stucco">stucco</a>) for finishing brick buildings in wet climates</li>
<li>Hydraulic mortars for masonry construction of harbor works, etc., in contact with sea water</li>
<li>Development of strong concretes</li></ul>
<p>Modern cements are often <a href="/wiki/Portland_cement" title="Portland cement">Portland cement</a> or Portland cement blends, but industry also uses other cements.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Portland_cement">Portland cement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Portland cement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Portland_cement" title="Portland cement">Portland cement</a></div>
<p>Portland cement is by far the most common type of cement in general use around the world. This cement is made by heating <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> (calcium carbonate) with other materials (such as <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a>) to 1450 °C in a <a href="/wiki/Kiln" title="Kiln">kiln</a>, in a process known as <a href="/wiki/Calcination" title="Calcination">calcination</a> that liberates a molecule of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> from the calcium carbonate to form <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">calcium oxide</a>, or quicklime—which then chemically combines with the other materials in the mix to form calcium silicates and other cementitious compounds. The resulting hard substance, called 'clinker', is then ground with a small amount of <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a> into a powder to make <i>ordinary Portland cement</i>, the most commonly used type of cement (often referred to as OPC).
Portland cement is a basic ingredient of <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mortar_(masonry)" title="Mortar (masonry)">mortar</a>, and most non-specialty <a href="/wiki/Grout" title="Grout">grout</a>. The most common use for Portland cement is to make concrete. Concrete is a composite material made of <a href="/wiki/Construction_aggregate" title="Construction aggregate">aggregate</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gravel" title="Gravel">gravel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a>), cement, and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>. As a construction material, concrete can be cast in almost any shape, and once it hardens, can be a structural (load bearing) element. Portland cement may be grey or white.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Portland_cement_blends">Portland cement blends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Portland cement blends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Portland cement blends are often available as inter-ground mixtures from cement producers, but similar formulations are often also mixed from the ground components at the concrete mixing plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Portland blast-furnace slag cement, or Blast furnace cement</b> (ASTM C595 and EN 197-1 nomenclature respectively), contains up to 95% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ground_granulated_blast_furnace_slag" title="Ground granulated blast furnace slag">ground granulated blast furnace slag</a>, with the rest Portland clinker and a little gypsum. All compositions produce high ultimate strength, but as slag content is increased, early strength is reduced, while sulfate resistance increases and heat evolution diminishes. Used as an economic alternative to Portland sulfate-resisting and low-heat cements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Portland-fly ash cement</b> contains up to 40% <a href="/wiki/Fly_ash" title="Fly ash">fly ash</a> under ASTM standards (ASTM C595), or 35% under EN standards (EN 197-1). The fly ash is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pozzolanic" title="Pozzolanic">pozzolanic</a>, so that ultimate strength is maintained. Because fly ash addition allows a lower concrete water content, early strength can also be maintained. Where good quality cheap fly ash is available, this can be an economic alternative to ordinary Portland cement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Portland pozzolan cement</b> includes fly ash cement, since fly ash is a <a href="/wiki/Pozzolan" title="Pozzolan">pozzolan</a>, but also includes cements made from other natural or artificial pozzolans. In countries where <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_ash" title="Volcanic ash">volcanic ashes</a> are available (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>), these cements are often the most common form in use. The maximum replacement ratios are generally defined as for Portland-fly ash cement.
</p><p><b>Portland silica fume cement</b>. Addition of <a href="/wiki/Silica_fume" title="Silica fume">silica fume</a> can yield exceptionally high strengths, and cements containing 5–20% silica fume are occasionally produced, with 10% being the maximum allowed addition under EN 197-1. However, silica fume is more usually added to Portland cement at the concrete mixer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Masonry cements</b> are used for preparing bricklaying <a href="/wiki/Mortar_(masonry)" title="Mortar (masonry)">mortars</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stuccos" title="Stuccos">stuccos</a>, and must not be used in concrete. They are usually complex proprietary formulations containing Portland clinker and a number of other ingredients that may include limestone, hydrated lime, air entrainers, retarders, waterproofers and coloring agents. They are formulated to yield workable mortars that allow rapid and consistent masonry work. Subtle variations of Masonry cement in the US are Plastic Cements and Stucco Cements. These are designed to produce a controlled bond with masonry blocks.
</p><p><b>Expansive cements</b> contain, in addition to Portland clinker, expansive clinkers (usually sulfoaluminate clinkers), and are designed to offset the effects of drying shrinkage normally encountered in hydraulic cements. This cement can make concrete for floor slabs (up to 60 m square) without contraction joints.
</p><p><b>White blended cements</b> may be made using white clinker (containing little or no iron) and white supplementary materials such as high-purity <a href="/wiki/Metakaolin" title="Metakaolin">metakaolin</a>. <b>Colored cements</b> serve decorative purposes. Some standards allow the addition of pigments to produce <i>colored Portland cement</i>. Other standards (e.g., ASTM) don't allow pigments in Portland cement, and colored cements are sold as <i>blended hydraulic cements</i>.
</p><p><i>Very finely ground cements</i> are cement mixed with sand or with slag or other pozzolan type minerals that are extremely finely ground together. Such cements can have the same physical characteristics as normal cement but with 50% less cement, particularly due to their increased surface area for the chemical reaction. Even with intensive grinding they can use up to 50% less energy to fabricate than ordinary Portland cements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Justnes_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Justnes-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_cements">Other cements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Other cements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Pozzolan-lime cements</i> are mixtures of ground <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pozzolanic_ash" title="Pozzolanic ash">pozzolan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_lime" title="Agricultural lime">lime</a>. These are the cements the Romans used, and are present in surviving Roman structures like the <a href="/wiki/Pantheon,_Rome" title="Pantheon, Rome">Pantheon</a> in Rome. They develop strength slowly, but their ultimate strength can be very high. The hydration products that produce strength are essentially the same as those in Portland cement.
</p><p><i>Slag-lime cements</i>—<a href="/wiki/Ground_granulated_blast-furnace_slag" title="Ground granulated blast-furnace slag">ground granulated blast-furnace slag</a> is not hydraulic on its own, but is "activated" by addition of alkalis, most economically using lime. They are similar to pozzolan lime cements in their properties. Only granulated slag (i.e., water-quenched, glassy slag) is effective as a cement component.
</p><p><i>Supersulfated cements</i> contain about 80% ground granulated blast furnace slag, 15% <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a> or <a href="/wiki/Anhydrite" title="Anhydrite">anhydrite</a> and a little Portland clinker or lime as an activator. They produce strength by formation of <a href="/wiki/Ettringite" title="Ettringite">ettringite</a>, with strength growth similar to a slow Portland cement. They exhibit good resistance to aggressive agents, including sulfate.
<a href="/wiki/Calcium_aluminate_cements" title="Calcium aluminate cements">Calcium aluminate cements</a> are hydraulic cements made primarily from <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bauxite" title="Bauxite">bauxite</a>. The active ingredients are monocalcium aluminate CaAl<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> (CaO · Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> or CA in <a href="/wiki/Cement_chemist_notation" title="Cement chemist notation">Cement chemist notation</a>, CCN) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mayenite" title="Mayenite">mayenite</a> Ca<sub>12</sub>Al<sub>14</sub>O<sub>33</sub> (12 CaO · 7 Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, or C<sub>12</sub>A<sub>7</sub> in CCN). Strength forms by hydration to calcium aluminate hydrates. They are well-adapted for use in refractory (high-temperature resistant) concretes, e.g., for furnace linings.
</p><p><i>Calcium sulfoaluminate cements</i> are made from clinkers that include <a href="/wiki/Ye%27elimite" title="Ye'elimite">ye'elimite</a> (Ca<sub>4</sub>(AlO<sub>2</sub>)<sub>6</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> or C<sub>4</sub>A<sub>3</sub><span style="text-decoration:overline;">S</span> in <a href="/wiki/Cement_chemist_notation" title="Cement chemist notation">Cement chemist's notation</a>) as a primary phase. They are used in expansive cements, in ultra-high early strength cements, and in "low-energy" cements. Hydration produces ettringite, and specialized physical properties (such as expansion or rapid reaction) are obtained by adjustment of the availability of calcium and sulfate ions. Their use as a low-energy alternative to Portland cement has been pioneered in China, where several million tonnes per year are produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Energy requirements are lower because of the lower kiln temperatures required for reaction, and the lower amount of limestone (which must be endothermically decarbonated) in the mix. In addition, the lower limestone content and lower fuel consumption leads to a CO<sub>2</sub> emission around half that associated with Portland clinker. However, SO<sub>2</sub> emissions are usually significantly higher.
</p><p><i>"Natural" cements</i> corresponding to certain cements of the pre-Portland era, are produced by burning <a href="/wiki/Argillaceous_minerals" title="Argillaceous minerals">argillaceous limestones</a> at moderate temperatures. The level of clay components in the limestone (around 30–35%) is such that large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Belite" title="Belite">belite</a> (the low-early strength, high-late strength mineral in Portland cement) are formed without the formation of excessive amounts of free lime. As with any natural material, such cements have highly variable properties.
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Geopolymer" title="Geopolymer">Geopolymer</a> cements</i> are made from mixtures of water-soluble alkali metal silicates, and aluminosilicate mineral powders such as <a href="/wiki/Fly_ash" title="Fly ash">fly ash</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metakaolin" title="Metakaolin">metakaolin</a>.
</p><p><b>Polymer cements</b> are made from organic chemicals that polymerise. Producers often use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermoset" title="Thermoset">thermoset</a> materials. While they are often significantly more expensive, they can give a water proof material that has useful tensile strength.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Setting_and_curing">Setting and curing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Setting and curing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cement starts to set when mixed with water, which causes a series of hydration chemical reactions. The constituents slowly hydrate and the mineral hydrates solidify. The interlocking of the hydrates gives cement its strength. Contrary to a common belief, hydraulic cement does not set by drying out—proper curing requires maintaining the appropriate moisture content during the curing process. If hydraulic cements dry out during curing, the resulting product can be significantly weakened. Nonetheless, a minimum temperature of 5 °C is recommended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_issues">Safety issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Safety issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bags of cement routinely have health and safety warnings printed on them because not only is cement highly <a href="/wiki/Alkali" title="Alkali">alkaline</a>, but the setting process is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermic</a>. As a result, wet cement is strongly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Causticity" title="Causticity">caustic</a> (pH = 13.5) and can easily cause severe <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skin_burn" title="Skin burn">skin burns</a> if not promptly washed off with water. Similarly, dry cement powder in contact with <a href="/wiki/Mucous_membrane" title="Mucous membrane">mucous membranes</a> can cause severe eye or respiratory irritation. Some trace elements, such as chromium, from impurities naturally present in the raw materials used to produce cement may cause <a href="/wiki/Allergic_contact_dermatitis" title="Allergic contact dermatitis">allergic dermatitis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hse.gov.uk_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-hse.gov.uk-35">[35]</a></sup> Reducing agents such as ferrous sulfate (FeSO<sub>4</sub>) are often added to cement to convert the carcinogenic hexavalent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chromate" title="Chromate">chromate</a> (CrO<sub>4</sub><sup>2−</sup>) into trivalent chromium (Cr<sup>3+</sup>), a less toxic chemical species. Cement users need also to wear appropriate gloves and protective clothing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cement_industry_in_the_world">Cement industry in the world</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Cement industry in the world">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cement_Production_2010.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="650" data-file-width="750" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Cement_Production_2010.png/250px-Cement_Production_2010.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Cement_Production_2010.png/375px-Cement_Production_2010.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Cement_Production_2010.png/500px-Cement_Production_2010.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cement_Production_2010.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Global Cement Production in 2010</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cement_Capacity_2010.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="650" data-file-width="750" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Cement_Capacity_2010.png/250px-Cement_Capacity_2010.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Cement_Capacity_2010.png/375px-Cement_Capacity_2010.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Cement_Capacity_2010.png/500px-Cement_Capacity_2010.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cement_Capacity_2010.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Global Cement Capacity in 2010</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_cement_production" title="List of countries by cement production">List of countries by cement production</a></div>
<p>In 2010, the world production of hydraulic cement was <a href="/wiki/Orders_of_magnitude_(mass)#1012_to_1017_kg" title="Orders of magnitude (mass)">3,300 million tonnes (3.2<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>9</sup> long tons; 3.6<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>9</sup> short tons)</a>. The top three producers were <a href="/wiki/Cement_industry_in_China" title="Cement industry in China">China</a> with 1,800, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> with 220, and <a href="/wiki/Cement_industry_in_the_United_States" title="Cement industry in the United States">USA</a> with 63.5 million tonnes for a total of over half the world total by the world's three most populated states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>For the world capacity to produce cement in 2010, the situation was similar with the top three states (China, India, and USA) accounting for just under half the world total capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>Over 2011 and 2012, global consumption continued to climb, rising to 3585 Mt in 2011 and 3736 Mt in 2012, while annual <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">growth rates</a> eased to 8.3% and 4.2%, respectively.
</p><p>China, representing an increasing share of world cement consumption, remains the main engine of global growth. By 2012, Chinese demand was recorded at 2160 Mt, representing 58% of world consumption. Annual growth rates, which reached 16% in 2010, appear to have softened, slowing to 5–6% over 2011 and 2012, as China’s economy targets a more sustainable growth rate.
</p><p>Outside of China, worldwide consumption climbed by 4.4% to 1462 Mt in 2010, 5% to 1535 Mt in 2011, and finally 2.7% to 1576 Mt in 2012.
</p><p>Iran is now the 3rd largest cement producer in the world and has increased its output by over 10% from 2008 to 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Due to climbing energy costs in Pakistan and other major cement-producing countries, Iran is in a unique position as a trading partner, utilizing its own surplus petroleum to power clinker plants. Now a top producer in the Middle-East, Iran is further increasing its dominant position in local markets and abroad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>The performance in North America and Europe over the 2010–12 period contrasted strikingly with that of China, as the global financial crisis evolved into a sovereign debt crisis for many economies in this region and recession. Cement consumption levels for this region fell by 1.9% in 2010 to 445 Mt, recovered by 4.9% in 2011, then dipped again by 1.1% in 2012.
</p><p>The performance in the rest of the world, which includes many emerging economies in Asia, Africa and Latin America and representing some 1020 Mt cement demand in 2010, was positive and more than offset the declines in North America and Europe. Annual consumption growth was recorded at 7.4% in 2010, moderating to 5.1% and 4.3% in 2011 and 2012, respectively.
</p><p>As at year-end 2012, the global cement industry consisted of 5673 cement production facilities, including both integrated and grinding, of which 3900 were located in China and 1773 in the rest of the world.
</p><p>Total cement capacity worldwide was recorded at 5245 Mt in 2012, with 2950 Mt located in China and 2295 Mt in the rest of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cement_industry_in_China" title="Cement industry in China">Cement industry in China</a></div>
<p>"For the past 18 years, China consistently has produced more cement than any other country in the world. [...] (However,) China's cement export peaked in 1994 with 11 million tonnes shipped out and has been in steady decline ever since. Only 5.18 million tonnes were exported out of China in 2002. Offered at $34 a ton, Chinese cement is pricing itself out of the market as Thailand is asking as little as $20 for the same quality."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2006, it was estimated that China manufactured 1.235 billion tonnes of cement, which was 44% of the world total cement production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NEAA070619b_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-NEAA070619b-45">[45]</a></sup> "Demand for cement in China is expected to advance 5.4% annually and exceed 1 billion tonnes in 2008, driven by slowing but healthy growth in construction expenditures. Cement consumed in China will amount to 44% of global demand, and China will remain the world's largest national consumer of cement by a large margin."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2010, 3.3 billion tonnes of cement was consumed globally. Of this, China accounted for 1.8 billion tonnes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impacts"><span id="Environmental_and_social_impacts"></span>Environmental impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Environmental impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cement manufacture causes environmental impacts at all stages of the process. These include emissions of airborne pollution in the form of dust, gases, noise and vibration when operating machinery and during blasting in <a href="/wiki/Quarry" title="Quarry">quarries</a>, and damage to countryside from quarrying. Equipment to reduce dust emissions during quarrying and manufacture of cement is widely used, and equipment to trap and separate exhaust gases are coming into increased use. Environmental protection also includes the re-integration of quarries into the countryside after they have been closed down by returning them to nature or re-cultivating them.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CO2_emissions">CO<sub>2</sub> emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: CO2 emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Carbon concentration in cement spans from ≈5% in cement structures to ≈8% in the case of roads in cement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Cement manufacturing releases <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> in the atmosphere both directly when <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> is heated, producing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lime_(mineral)" title="Lime (mineral)">lime</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> and also indirectly through the use of energy if its production involves the emission of CO<sub>2</sub>. The cement industry produces about 10% of global man-made CO<sub>2</sub> emissions, of which 60% is from the chemical process, and 40% from burning fuel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>Nearly 900 kg of CO<sub>2</sub> are emitted for every 1000 kg of Portland cement produced. 
In the European Union, the specific energy consumption for the production of cement clinker has been reduced by approximately 30% since the 1970s. This reduction in primary energy requirements is equivalent to approximately 11 million tonnes of coal per year with corresponding benefits in reduction of CO<sub>2</sub> emissions. This accounts for approximately 5% of anthropogenic CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>The majority of carbon dioxide emissions in the manufacture of Portland cement (approximately 60%) are produced from the chemical decomposition of limestone to lime, an ingredient in Portland cement clinker. These emissions may be reduced by lowering the clinker content of cement.
</p><p>To reduce the transport of heavier raw materials and to minimize the associated costs, it is more economical to build cement plants closer to the limestone quarries rather than to the consumer centers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>In certain applications, <a href="/wiki/Lime_mortar" title="Lime mortar">lime mortar</a> reabsorbs some of the CO<sub>2</sub> as was released in its manufacture, and has a lower energy requirement in production than mainstream cement.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Newly developed cement types from Novacem<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Eco-cement" title="Eco-cement">Eco-cement</a> can absorb <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> from ambient air during hardening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Use of the <a href="/wiki/Kalina_cycle" title="Kalina cycle">Kalina cycle</a> during production can also increase energy efficiency.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heavy_metal_emissions_in_the_air">Heavy metal emissions in the air</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Heavy metal emissions in the air">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In some circumstances, mainly depending on the origin and the composition of the raw materials used, the high-temperature calcination process of limestone and clay minerals can release in the atmosphere gases and dust rich in volatile <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_(chemistry)" title="Heavy metal (chemistry)">heavy metals</a>, a.o, <a href="/wiki/Thallium#Thallium_pollution" title="Thallium">thallium</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> are the most toxic. Heavy metals (Tl, Cd, Hg, ...) and also <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a> are often found as trace elements in common metal <a href="/wiki/Sulfide" title="Sulfide">sulfides</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pyrite" title="Pyrite">pyrite (FeS<sub>2</sub>)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sphalerite" title="Sphalerite">zinc blende (ZnS)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galena" title="Galena">galena (PbS)</a>, ...) present as secondary minerals in most of the raw materials. Environmental regulations exist in many countries to limit these emissions. As of 2011 in the United States, cement kilns are "legally allowed to pump more toxins into the air than are hazardous-waste incinerators."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heavy_metals_present_in_the_clinker">Heavy metals present in the clinker</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Heavy metals present in the clinker">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The presence of heavy metals in the clinker arises both from the natural raw materials and from the use of recycled by-products or alternative fuels. The high pH prevailing in the cement porewater (12.5 &lt; pH &lt; 13.5) limits the mobility of many heavy metals by decreasing their solubility and increasing their sorption onto the cement mineral phases. <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">Nickel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> are commonly found in cement in non-negligible concentrations. <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">Chromium</a> may also directly arise as natural impurity from the raw materials or as secondary contamination from the abrasion of hard chromium steel alloys used in the ball mills when the clinker is ground. As <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chromate" title="Chromate">chromate</a> (CrO<sub>4</sub><sup>2−</sup>) is toxic and may cause severe skin allergies at trace concentration, it is sometimes reduced into trivalent Cr(III) by addition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferrous_sulfate" title="Ferrous sulfate">ferrous sulfate</a> (FeSO<sub>4</sub>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_alternative_fuels_and_by-products_materials">Use of alternative fuels and by-products materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Use of alternative fuels and by-products materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A cement plant consumes 3 to 6 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigajoule" title="Gigajoule">GJ</a> of fuel per tonne of clinker produced, depending on the raw materials and the process used. Most cement kilns today use coal and petroleum coke as primary fuels, and to a lesser extent natural gas and fuel oil. Selected waste and by-products with recoverable calorific value can be used as fuels in a cement kiln (referred to as <a href="/wiki/Co-processing" title="Co-processing">co-processing</a>), replacing a portion of conventional fossil fuels, like coal, if they meet strict specifications. Selected waste and by-products containing useful minerals such as calcium, silica, alumina, and iron can be used as raw materials in the kiln, replacing raw materials such as clay, <a href="/wiki/Shale" title="Shale">shale</a>, and limestone. Because some materials have both useful mineral content and recoverable calorific value, the distinction between alternative fuels and raw materials is not always clear. For example, sewage sludge has a low but significant calorific value, and burns to give ash containing minerals useful in the clinker matrix.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>Normal cement kiln operation provides combustion conditions that are more than adequate for the destruction of even the most difficult to destroy organic substances. This is primarily due to the high temperatures of the kiln gases (2000 °C in the combustion gas from the main burners and 1100 °C in the gas from the burners in the precalciner). The gas residence time at high temperature in the rotary kiln is of the order of 5–10 seconds and in the precalciner more than 3 seconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-groundwork.org.za_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-groundwork.org.za-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>Due to <a href="/wiki/Bovine_spongiform_encephalopathy" title="Bovine spongiform encephalopathy">bovine spongiform encephalopathy</a> (BSE) in the European beef industry, the use of animal-derived products to feed cattle is now severely restricted. Large quantities of waste animal meat and bone meal (MBM), also known as animal flour, must be safely disposed or transformed. The production of cement kilns, together with the incineration, is to date one of the two main ways to treat this solid effluent of the food industry.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_cement">Green cement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Green cement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Green cement is a cementitious material that meets or exceeds the functional performance capabilities of ordinary Portland cement by incorporating and optimizing recycled materials, thereby reducing consumption of natural raw materials, water, and energy, resulting in a more sustainable construction material.
</p><p>New manufacturing processes for producing green cement are being researched with the goal to reduce, or even eliminate, the production and release of damaging pollutants and greenhouse gasses, particularly CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>Growing environmental concerns and the increasing cost of fuels of fossil origin have resulted in many countries in a sharp reduction of the resources needed to produce cement and effluents (dust and exhaust gases).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-groundwork.org.za_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-groundwork.org.za-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>Peter Trimble, a design student at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Edinburgh" title="University of Edinburgh">University of Edinburgh</a>, has proposed 'DUPE' based on <i><a href="/wiki/Sporosarcina_pasteurii" title="Sporosarcina pasteurii">Sporosarcina pasteurii</a></i>, a bacterium with binding qualities which, when mixed with <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urine" title="Urine">urine</a>, produces a concrete said to be 70% as strong as conventional materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p>
