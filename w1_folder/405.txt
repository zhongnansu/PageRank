<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Carbon fixation</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">Carbon cycle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">Carbon sequestration</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:20100422_235222_Cyanobacteria.jpg"><img alt="Filamentous cyanobacterium" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2222" data-file-width="2222" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/20100422_235222_Cyanobacteria.jpg/220px-20100422_235222_Cyanobacteria.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/20100422_235222_Cyanobacteria.jpg/330px-20100422_235222_Cyanobacteria.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/20100422_235222_Cyanobacteria.jpg/440px-20100422_235222_Cyanobacteria.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:20100422_235222_Cyanobacteria.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">Cyanobacteria</a> such as these carry out photosynthesis.  Their emergence foreshadowed the evolution of many photosynthetic plants, which oxygenated Earth's atmosphere.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Carbon fixation</b> or <b>сarbon assimilation</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">conversion</a> process of inorganic carbon (<a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>) to <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a> by living <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a>. The most prominent example is <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>, although <a href="/wiki/Chemosynthesis" title="Chemosynthesis">chemosynthesis</a> is another form of carbon fixation that can take place in the absence of sunlight. Organisms that grow by fixing carbon are called <a href="/wiki/Autotroph" title="Autotroph">autotrophs</a>.  Autotrophs include <a href="/wiki/Phototroph" title="Phototroph">photoautotrophs</a>, which synthesize organic compounds using the energy of sunlight, and <a href="/wiki/Lithotroph" title="Lithotroph">lithoautotrophs</a>, which synthesize organic compounds using the energy of inorganic oxidation.  <a href="/wiki/Heterotroph" title="Heterotroph">Heterotrophs</a> are organisms that grow using the carbon fixed by autotrophs. The organic compounds are used by heterotrophs to produce energy and to build body structures.  "Fixed carbon", "reduced carbon", and "organic carbon" are equivalent terms for various organic compounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Geider_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Geider-1">[1]</a></sup>23
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Net_vs_gross_CO2_fixation">Net vs gross CO<sub>2</sub> fixation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_fixation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Net vs gross CO2 fixation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:602px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CO2FixnData.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="188" data-file-width="653" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/CO2FixnData.png/600px-CO2FixnData.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c1/CO2FixnData.png 1.5x" width="600"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CO2FixnData.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Graphic showing net annual amounts of CO<sub>2</sub> fixation by land and sea-based organisms.</div></div></div></div>
<p>It is estimated that approximately 258 billion tons of carbon dioxide are converted by photosynthesis annually.  The majority of the fixation occurs in marine environments, especially areas of high nutrients.  The gross amount of carbon dioxide fixed is much larger since approximately 40% is consumed by respiration following photosynthesis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Geider_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Geider-1">[1]</a></sup>  Given the scale of this process, it is understandable that <a href="/wiki/RuBisCO" title="RuBisCO">RuBisCO</a> is the most abundant protein on Earth.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview_of_pathways">Overview of pathways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_fixation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview of pathways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Six autotrophic carbon fixation pathways are known as of 2011. The Calvin cycle fixes carbon in the <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplasts</a> of plants and algae, and in the <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">cyanobacteria</a>. It also fixes carbon in the anoxygenic photosynthetic in one type of <a href="/wiki/Proteobacteria" title="Proteobacteria">proteobacteria</a> called <a href="/wiki/Purple_bacteria" title="Purple bacteria">purple bacteria</a>, and in some non-phototrophic proteobacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Swan11_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Swan11-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxygenic_photosynthesis">Oxygenic photosynthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_fixation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Oxygenic photosynthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>, energy from sunlight drives the carbon fixation <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_pathway" title="Metabolic pathway">pathway</a>. <i>Oxygenic</i> photosynthesis is used by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primary_producer" title="Primary producer">primary producers</a>—plants, <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">cyanobacteria</a>. They contain the pigment <a href="/wiki/Chlorophyll" title="Chlorophyll">chlorophyll</a>, and use the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calvin_cycle" title="Calvin cycle">Calvin cycle</a> to fix carbon autotrophically. The process works like this:
</p>
<dl><dd>2H<sub>2</sub>O → 4e<sup>−</sup> + 4H<sup>+</sup> + O<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<dl><dd>CO<sub>2</sub> + 4e<sup>−</sup> + 4H<sup>+</sup> → CH<sub>2</sub>O + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>In the first step, water is dissociated into <a href="/wiki/Electron_transfer" title="Electron transfer">electrons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydronium" title="Hydronium">protons</a>, and free <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>. This allows the use of water, one of the most abundant substances on Earth, as an electron donor—as a source of reducing power. The release of free oxygen is a side-effect of enormous consequence. The first step uses the energy of sunlight to oxidize water to O<sub>2</sub>, and, ultimately, to produce <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a>
</p>
<dl><dd>ADP + P<sub>i</sub> ⇌ ATP + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>and the reductant, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NADPH" title="NADPH">NADPH</a>
</p>
<dl><dd>NADP<sup>+</sup> + 2e<sup>−</sup> + 2H<sup>+</sup> ⇌ NADPH + H<sup>+</sup></dd></dl>
<p>In the second step, called the Calvin cycle, the actual fixation of carbon dioxide is carried out.  This process consumes ATP and NADPH. The Calvin cycle in plants accounts for the preponderance of carbon fixation on land. In <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> and cyanobacteria, it accounts for the preponderance of carbon fixation in the oceans. The Calvin cycle converts carbon dioxide into sugar, as <a href="/wiki/Triose" title="Triose">triose</a> phosphate (TP), which is <a href="/wiki/Glyceraldehyde_3-phosphate" title="Glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate">glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate</a> (GAP) together with <a href="/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone_phosphate" title="Dihydroxyacetone phosphate">dihydroxyacetone phosphate</a> (DHAP):
</p>
<dl><dd>3 CO<sub>2</sub>  +  12 e<sup>−</sup>  +  12 H<sup>+</sup>  +  P<sub>i</sub>   →   TP   +   4 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>An alternative perspective accounts for NADPH  (source of e<sup>−</sup>) and ATP:
</p>
<dl><dd>3 CO<sub>2</sub>  +  6 NADPH  +  6 H<sup>+</sup>  +  9 ATP  +  5 H<sub>2</sub>O   →   TP  +  6 NADP<sup>+</sup>  +  9 ADP  +  8 P<sub>i</sub></dd></dl>
<p>The formula for inorganic phosphate (P<sub>i</sub>) is HOPO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup> + 2H<sup>+</sup>. Formulas for triose and TP are C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>-CH<sub>2</sub>OH and C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>-CH<sub>2</sub>OPO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup> + 2H<sup>+</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolutionary_considerations">Evolutionary considerations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_fixation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Evolutionary considerations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Somewhere between 3.5 and 2.3 billion years ago, the ancestors of <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">cyanobacteria</a> evolved oxygenic photosynthesis, enabling the use of the abundant yet relatively oxidized molecule H<sub>2</sub>O as an electron donor to the electron transport chain of light-catalyzed proton-pumping responsible for efficient ATP synthesis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brasier06_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brasier06-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tomitani06_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tomitani06-4">[4]</a></sup>  When this evolutionary breakthrough occurred, autotrophy (growth using inorganic carbon as the sole carbon source) is believed to have already been developed.  However, the proliferation of cyanobacteria, due to their novel ability to exploit water as a source of electrons, radically altered the global environment by oxygenating the atmosphere and by achieving large fluxes of CO<sub>2</sub> consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kopp05_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kopp05-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_concentrating_mechanisms">Carbon concentrating mechanisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_fixation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Carbon concentrating mechanisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many photosynthetic organisms have not acquired inorganic carbon concentrating mechanisms (CCM), which increase the concentration of carbon dioxide available to the initial carboxylase of the Calvin cycle, the enzyme <a href="/wiki/RuBisCO" title="RuBisCO">RuBisCO</a>. The benefits of CCM include increased tolerance to low external concentrations of inorganic carbon, and reduced losses to <a href="/wiki/Photorespiration" title="Photorespiration">photorespiration</a>. CCM can make plants more tolerant of heat and water stress.
</p><p>Carbon concentrating mechanisms use the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_anhydrase" title="Carbonic anhydrase">carbonic anhydrase</a> (CA), which catalyze both the dehydration of <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> to carbon dioxide and the hydration of carbon dioxide to bicarbonate
</p>
<dl><dd>HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup> + H<sup>+</sup> ⇌ CO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Lipid membranes are much less permeable to bicarbonate than to carbon dioxide. To capture inorganic carbon more effectively, some plants have adapted the <a href="/wiki/Anaplerotic_reactions" title="Anaplerotic reactions">anaplerotic reactions</a>
</p>
<dl><dd>HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup> + H<sup>+</sup> + PEP → OAA + P<sub>i</sub></dd></dl>
<p>catalyzed by <a href="/wiki/Phosphoenolpyruvate_carboxylase" title="Phosphoenolpyruvate carboxylase">PEP carboxylase</a> (PEPC), to carboxylate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphoenolpyruvate" title="Phosphoenolpyruvate">phosphoenolpyruvate</a> (PEP) to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxaloacetate" title="Oxaloacetate">oxaloacetate</a> (OAA) which is a C<sub>4</sub> <a href="/wiki/Dicarboxylic_acid" title="Dicarboxylic acid">dicarboxylic acid</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="CAM_plants">CAM plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_fixation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: CAM plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CAM_photosynthesis" title="CAM photosynthesis">CAM plants</a> that use Crassulacean acid metabolism as an adaptation for arid conditions. CO<sub>2</sub> enters through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stomata" title="Stomata">stomata</a> during the night and is converted into the 4-carbon compound, <a href="/wiki/Malic_acid" title="Malic acid">malic acid</a>, which releases CO<sub>2</sub> for use in the Calvin cycle during the day, when the stomata are closed. The dung jade plant (<i><a href="/wiki/Crassula" title="Crassula">Crassula ovata</a></i>) and <a href="/wiki/Cactus" title="Cactus">cacti</a> are typical of CAM plants. Sixteen thousand species of plants use CAM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dodd02_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dodd02-6">[6]</a></sup> These plants have a carbon isotope signature of −20 to −10 ‰.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O'Leary88_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-O'Leary88-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="C4_plants">C<sub>4</sub> plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_fixation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: C4 plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/C4_carbon_fixation" title="C4 carbon fixation">C<sub>4</sub> plants</a> preface the Calvin cycle with reactions that incorporate CO<sub>2</sub> into one of the 4-carbon compounds, malic acid or aspartic acid. C<sub>4</sub> plants have a distinctive internal leaf anatomy. Tropical grasses, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sugar_cane" title="Sugar cane">sugar cane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">maize</a> are C<sub>4</sub> plants, but there are many broadleaf plants that are C<sub>4</sub>. Overall, 7600 species of terrestrial plants use C<sub>4</sub> carbon fixation, representing around 3% of all species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sage99_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sage99-8">[8]</a></sup> These plants have a carbon isotope signature of −16 to −10 ‰.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O'Leary88_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-O'Leary88-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="C3_plants">C<sub>3</sub> plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_fixation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: C3 plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The large majority of plants are <a href="/wiki/C3_carbon_fixation" title="C3 carbon fixation">C<sub>3</sub> plants</a>. They are so-called to distinguish them from the CAM and C<sub>4</sub> plants, and because the carboxylation products of the Calvin cycle are 3-carbon compounds. They lack C<sub>4</sub> dicarboxylic acid cycles, and therefore have higher carbon dioxide compensation points than CAM or C<sub>4</sub> plants. C<sub>3</sub> plants have a <a href="/wiki/%CE%9413C" title="Δ13C">carbon isotope signature</a> of −24 to −33‰.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O'Leary88_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-O'Leary88-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bacteria">Bacteria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_fixation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Bacteria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Some bacteria use <a href="/wiki/Carboxysome" title="Carboxysome">carboxysomes</a> as carbon concentrating mechanism.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_autotrophic_pathways">Other autotrophic pathways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_fixation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other autotrophic pathways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Of the five other autotrophic pathways, two are known only in <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, two only in <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a>, and one in both bacteria and archaea.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reductive_citric_acid_cycle">Reductive citric acid cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_fixation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Reductive citric acid cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Reverse_Krebs_cycle" title="Reverse Krebs cycle">reductive citric acid cycle</a> is the oxidative <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid_cycle" title="Citric acid cycle">citric acid cycle</a> run in reverse. It has been found in <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_organism" title="Anaerobic organism">anaerobic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microaerophile" title="Microaerophile">microaerobic</a> bacteria. It was proposed in 1966 by Evans, Buchanan and Arnon who were working with the anoxygenic photosynthetic <a href="/wiki/Green_sulfur_bacteria" title="Green sulfur bacteria">green sulfur bacterium</a> that they called <i>Chlorobium thiosulfatophilum</i>. The reductive citric acid cycle is sometimes called the Arnon-Buchanan cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evans66_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Evans66-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reductive_acetyl_CoA_pathway">Reductive acetyl CoA pathway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_fixation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Reductive acetyl CoA pathway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Wood%E2%80%93Ljungdahl_pathway" title="Wood–Ljungdahl pathway">reductive acetyl CoA pathway</a> operated in strictly anaerobic bacteria (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acetogens" title="Acetogens">acetogens</a>) and archaea (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methanogens" title="Methanogens">methanogens</a>). The pathway was proposed in 1965 by Ljungdahl and Wood. They were working with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gram-positive" title="Gram-positive">gram-positive</a> <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a> producing bacterium <i>Clostridium thermoaceticum</i>, which is now named <i><a href="/wiki/Moorella_thermoacetica" title="Moorella thermoacetica">Moorella thermoacetica</a></i>. Hydrogenotrophic methanogenesis, which is only found in certain archaea and accounts for 80% of global methanogenesis, is also based on the reductive acetyl CoA pathway. The pathway is often referred to as the Wood–Ljungdahl pathway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ljungdahl65_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ljungdahl65-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ljungdahl09_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ljungdahl09-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="3-Hydroxypropionate_and_two_related_cycles">3-Hydroxypropionate and two related cycles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_fixation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 3-Hydroxypropionate and two related cycles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3-Hydroxypropionate_pathway" title="3-Hydroxypropionate pathway">3-hydroxypropionate cycle</a> is utilized <i>only</i> by <a href="/wiki/Chloroflexi_(phylum)" title="Chloroflexi (phylum)">green nonsulfur bacteria</a>. It was proposed in 2002 for the anoxygenic photosynthetic <i><a href="/wiki/Chloroflexus_aurantiacus" title="Chloroflexus aurantiacus">Chloroflexus aurantiacus</a></i>. None of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enzymes" title="Enzymes">enzymes</a> that participate in the 3-hydroxypropionate cycle are especially oxygen sensitive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herter02_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Herter02-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zarzycki09_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zarzycki09-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>A variant of the 3-hydroxypropionate pathway was  found to operate in aerobic extreme thermoacidophile archaeon <a href="/wiki/Metallosphaera" title="Metallosphaera"><i>Metallosphaera sedula</i></a>.  This pathway, called the 3-hydroxypropionate/4-hydroxybutyrate cycle .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berg07_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Berg07-14">[14]</a></sup>  And yet another variant of the 3-hydroxypropionate pathway is the dicarboxylate/4-hydroxybutyrate cycle.  It was discovered in anaerobic archaea.
It was proposed in 2008 for the hyperthermophile archeon <a href="/wiki/Ignicoccus" title="Ignicoccus"><i>Ignicoccus hospitalis</i></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huber08_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Huber08-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemosynthesis">Chemosynthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_fixation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Chemosynthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Chemosynthesis" title="Chemosynthesis">Chemosynthesis</a> is carbon fixation driven by the oxidation of inorganic substances (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> gas or <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>).  Sulfur- and hydrogen-oxidizing bacteria often use the Calvin cycle or the reductive citric acid cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-autotrophic_pathways">Non-autotrophic pathways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_fixation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Non-autotrophic pathways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although almost all heterotrophs cannot synthesize complete organic molecules from carbon dioxide, some carbon dioxide is incorporated in their metabolism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Notably <a href="/wiki/Pyruvate_carboxylase" title="Pyruvate carboxylase">pyruvate carboxylase</a> consumes carbon dioxide (as bicarbonate ions) as part of <a href="/wiki/Gluconeogenesis" title="Gluconeogenesis">gluconeogenesis</a>, and carbon dioxide is consumed in various <a href="/wiki/Anaplerotic_reactions" title="Anaplerotic reactions">anaplerotic reactions</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_isotope_discrimination">Carbon isotope discrimination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_fixation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Carbon isotope discrimination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carboxylase" title="Carboxylase">carboxylases</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/RuBisCO" title="RuBisCO">RuBisCO</a>, preferentially bind the lighter carbon stable isotope <a href="/wiki/Carbon-12" title="Carbon-12">carbon-12</a> over the heavier <a href="/wiki/Carbon-13" title="Carbon-13">carbon-13</a>. This is known as carbon isotope discrimination and results in carbon-12 to carbon-13 ratios in the plant that are higher than in the free air. Measurement of this ratio is important in the evaluation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_use_efficiency" title="Water use efficiency">water use efficiency</a> in plants, and also in assessing the possible or likely sources of carbon in global carbon cycle studies.
</p>
