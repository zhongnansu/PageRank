<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Group 14 hydride</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Group 14 hydrides</b> are <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compounds</a> composed of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> atoms and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_group" title="Carbon group">carbon group</a> atoms (the elements of group 14 are <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germanium" title="Germanium">germanium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tetrahydrides">Tetrahydrides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Group_14_hydride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Tetrahydrides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The tetrahydride series has the chemical formula XH<sub>4</sub>, with X representing any of the carbon family. Methane is commonly the result of the decomposition of organic matter and is a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a>. The other hydrides are generally unstable, poisonous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal_hydride" title="Metal hydride">metal hydrides</a>.
</p><p>They take on a pyramidal structure, and as such are not polar molecules like the other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P-block" title="P-block">p-block</a> hydrides.
</p><p>Unlike other light hydrides such as <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">hydrogen fluoride</a>, methane does not exhibit any anomalous effects attributed to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bonding" title="Hydrogen bonding">hydrogen bonding</a>, and so its properties conform well to the prevailing trend of heavier group 14 hydrides.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hexahydrides">Hexahydrides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Group_14_hydride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hexahydrides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Higher_group_14_hydrides">Higher group 14 hydrides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Group_14_hydride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Higher group 14 hydrides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All straight-chain saturated group 14 hydrides follow the formula X<sub><i>n</i></sub>H<sub>2<i>n</i>+2</sub>, the same formula for the <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">alkanes</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cubane-3D-vdW.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1109" data-file-width="1100" height="101" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Cubane-3D-vdW.png/100px-Cubane-3D-vdW.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Cubane-3D-vdW.png/150px-Cubane-3D-vdW.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Cubane-3D-vdW.png/200px-Cubane-3D-vdW.png 2x" width="100"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cubane-3D-vdW.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Cubane" title="Cubane">Cubane</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Platonic_hydrocarbon" title="Platonic hydrocarbon">Platonic hydrocarbon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prismanes" title="Prismanes">prismane</a></div></div></div>
<p>Many other group 14 hydrides are known. Carbon forms a huge variety of <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a> (with <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Butane" title="Butane">butane</a> following methane and ethane among the alkanes, but also including <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkenes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alkyne" title="Alkyne">alkynes</a>, cyclic and branched compounds, as well as aromatic hydrocarbons such as <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toluene" title="Toluene">toluene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Limonene" title="Limonene">limonene</a>), whose study forms the core of <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood301_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood301-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Alongside hydrogen, carbon can form compounds with the chemically similar <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogens</a>, forming <a href="/wiki/Haloalkane" title="Haloalkane">haloalkanes</a>. The simplest of this series, the <a href="/wiki/Halomethane" title="Halomethane">halomethanes</a>, contain compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Dichloromethane" title="Dichloromethane">dichloromethane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iodoform" title="Iodoform">iodoform</a>. Other such important chemicals include <a href="/wiki/Vinyl_chloride" title="Vinyl chloride">vinyl chloride</a>, which is used in the production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PVC" title="PVC">PVC</a>.
</p><p>The other group 14 elements have a lower tendency to catenate. <a href="/wiki/Silanes" title="Silanes">Silanes</a> Si<sub><i>n</i></sub>H<sub>2<i>n</i>+2</sub> are known for <i>n</i> = 1–8 (thermal stability decreasing as <i>n</i> increases), as are cyclosilanes Si<sub>5</sub>H<sub>10</sub> and Si<sub>6</sub>H<sub>12</sub>. They are very reactive, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrophoric" title="Pyrophoric">pyrophoric</a> colourless gases or volatile liquids. Their volatility is intermediate between the <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">alkanes</a> and the germanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood337_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood337-2">[2]</a></sup> Unsaturated silanes, the <a href="/wiki/Silenes" title="Silenes">silenes</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Silynes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Silynes (page does not exist)">silynes</a>, have been characterized spectroscopically.  The first members of each respectively are <a href="/wiki/Disilene" title="Disilene">disilene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Disilyne" title="Disilyne">disilyne</a>, the silicon analogues of <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">ethylene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Acetylene" title="Acetylene">acetylene</a>). The first five germanes Ge<sub><i>n</i></sub>H<sub>2<i>n</i>+2</sub> are known and are fairly similar to the silanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood374_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood374-3">[3]</a></sup> Stannane, a strong reducing agent slowly decomposes at room temperature to tin and hydrogen gas, and is decomposed by concentrated aqueous acids or alkalis; distannane, Sn<sub>2</sub>H<sub>6</sub> is still more unstable, and longer stannanes are unknown. Plumbane is very poorly characterised and is only known in trace amounts: even at low temperatures, synthesis methods that yield the other MH<sub>4</sub> compounds fail to give PbH<sub>4</sub>. No other plumbanes are known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenwood375_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood375-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Compounds containing hydrogen and multiple group 14 elements are known, one of the most famous of these being <a href="/wiki/Tetraethyllead" title="Tetraethyllead">tetraethyllead</a>.
</p>
