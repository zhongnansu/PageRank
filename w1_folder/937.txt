<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Methanol economy</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>methanol economy</b> is a suggested future <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economy</a> in which <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_ether" title="Dimethyl ether">dimethyl ether</a> replace <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a> as a means of energy storage, ground transportation fuel, and <a href="/wiki/Raw_material" title="Raw material">raw material</a> for synthetic hydrocarbons and their products. It offers an alternative to the proposed <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_economy" title="Hydrogen economy">hydrogen economy</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethanol_economy" title="Ethanol economy">ethanol economy</a>.
</p><p>In the 1990s, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobel_prize" title="Nobel prize">Nobel prize</a> winner <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_A._Olah" title="George A. Olah">George A. Olah</a> advocated a methanol economy;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> in 2006, he and two co-authors, <a href="/wiki/G._K._Surya_Prakash" title="G. K. Surya Prakash">G. K. Surya Prakash</a> and Alain Goeppert, published a summary of the state of fossil fuel and alternative energy sources, including their availability and limitations, before suggesting a methanol economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Methanol can be produced from a wide variety of sources including still-abundant fossil fuels (<a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oil_shale" title="Oil shale">oil shale</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tar_sands" title="Tar sands">tar sands</a>, etc.) as well as agricultural products and municipal waste, <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a> and varied <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>. It can also be made from chemical recycling of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanol_economy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fuel_cell_NASA_p48600ac.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1418" data-file-width="1115" height="280" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Fuel_cell_NASA_p48600ac.jpg/220px-Fuel_cell_NASA_p48600ac.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Fuel_cell_NASA_p48600ac.jpg/330px-Fuel_cell_NASA_p48600ac.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Fuel_cell_NASA_p48600ac.jpg/440px-Fuel_cell_NASA_p48600ac.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fuel_cell_NASA_p48600ac.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Direct-methanol fuel cell</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fuel">Fuel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanol_economy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fuel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Methanol_fuel" title="Methanol fuel">Methanol fuel</a></div>
<p>Methanol is a fuel for heat engines and fuel cells. Due to its high <a href="/wiki/Octane_rating" title="Octane rating">octane rating</a> it can be used directly as a fuel in <a href="/wiki/Flexible-fuel_vehicle" title="Flexible-fuel vehicle">flex-fuel cars</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_electric_vehicle" title="Hybrid electric vehicle">hybrid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_hybrid" title="Plug-in hybrid">plug-in hybrid</a> vehicles) using existing internal combustion engines (ICE). Methanol can also be burned in some other kinds of engine or to provide heat as other liquid fuels are used. <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">Fuel cell</a>s, can use methanol either directly in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct_Methanol_Fuel_Cell" title="Direct Methanol Fuel Cell">Direct Methanol Fuel Cells</a> (DMFC) or indirectly (after conversion into <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> by reforming).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feedstock">Feedstock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanol_economy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Feedstock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Methanol is already used today on a large scale to produce a variety of chemicals and products. Global methanol demand as a chemical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feedstock" title="Feedstock">feedstock</a> reached around 42 million metric tonnes per year as of 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Through the methanol-to-gasoline (MTG) process, it can be transformed into gasoline. Using the methanol-to-olefin (MTO) process, methanol can also be converted to <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">ethylene</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Propylene" title="Propylene">propylene</a>, the two chemicals produced in largest amounts by the <a href="/wiki/Petrochemical_industry" title="Petrochemical industry">petrochemical industry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> These are important building blocks for the production of essential polymers (LDPE, HDPE, PP) and like other chemical intermediates are currently produced mainly from petroleum feedstock. Their production from methanol could therefore reduce our dependency on petroleum. It would also make it possible to continue producing these chemicals when fossil fuels reserves are depleted.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanol_economy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Today most <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a> is produced from <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> through <a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">syngas</a>. <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a> is currently the world's largest methanol exporter, with exports mainly to the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The natural gas that serves as feedstock for the production of methanol comes from the same sources as other uses. Unconventional gas resources such as <a href="/wiki/Coalbed_methane" title="Coalbed methane">coalbed methane</a>, tight sand gas and eventually the very large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methane_hydrate" title="Methane hydrate">methane hydrate</a> resources present under the continental shelves of the seas and Siberian and Canadian tundra could also be used to provide the necessary gas. 
</p><p>The conventional route to methanol from <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> passes through syngas generation by <a href="/wiki/Steam_reforming" title="Steam reforming">steam reforming</a> combined (or not) with partial oxidation. New and more efficient ways to convert methane into methanol are also being developed. These include:
</p>
<ul><li>Methane oxidation with homogeneous catalysts in <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> media</li>
<li>Methane bromination followed by hydrolysis of the obtained <a href="/wiki/Bromomethane" title="Bromomethane">bromomethane</a></li>
<li>Direct oxidation of methane with oxygen</li>
<li>Microbial or photochemical conversion of methane</li>
<li>Partial methane oxidation with trapping of the partially oxidized product and subsequent extraction on copper and iron exchanged <a href="/wiki/Zeolite" title="Zeolite">Zeolite</a> (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha-Oxygen" title="Alpha-Oxygen">Alpha-Oxygen</a>)</li></ul>
<p>All these synthetic routes emit the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> CO<sub>2</sub>. To mitigate this, methanol can be made through ways minimizing the emission of CO<sub>2</sub>. One solution is to produce it from syngas obtained by biomass gasification. For this purpose any biomass can be used including <a href="/wiki/Wood_economy" title="Wood economy">wood</a>, wood wastes, grass, agricultural crops and their by-products, animal waste, aquatic plants and municipal waste. There is no need to use food crops as in the case of ethanol from corn, sugar cane and wheat.
</p>
<dl><dd>Biomass → Syngas (CO, CO<sub>2</sub>, H<sub>2</sub>) → CH<sub>3</sub>OH</dd></dl>
<p>Methanol can be synthesized from carbon and hydrogen from any source, including still available <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>.  CO<sub>2</sub> emitted from fossil fuel burning power plants and other industries and eventually even the CO<sub>2</sub> contained in the air, can be a source of carbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>  It can also be made from chemical recycling of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, which <a href="/wiki/Carbon_Recycling_International" title="Carbon Recycling International">Carbon Recycling International</a> has demonstrated with its first commercial scale plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Initially the major source will be the CO<sub>2</sub> rich flue gases of fossil-fuel-burning power plants or exhaust from cement and other factories. In the longer range however, considering diminishing fossil fuel resources and the effect of their utilization on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">earth's atmosphere</a>, even the low concentration of atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> itself could be captured and recycled via methanol, thus supplementing nature’s own photosynthetic cycle. Efficient new absorbents to capture atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> are being developed, mimicking plants' ability. Chemical recycling of CO<sub>2</sub> to new fuels and materials could thus become feasible, making them renewable on the human timescale.
</p><p>Methanol can also be produced from CO<sub>2</sub> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalytic_hydrogenation" title="Catalytic hydrogenation">catalytic hydrogenation</a> of CO<sub>2</sub> with H<sub>2</sub> where the hydrogen has been obtained from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_electrolysis" title="Water electrolysis">water electrolysis</a>.  This is the process used by <a href="/wiki/Carbon_Recycling_International" title="Carbon Recycling International">Carbon Recycling International</a> of <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>. Methanol may also be produced through CO<sub>2</sub> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrochemical_reduction" title="Electrochemical reduction">electrochemical reduction</a>, if electrical power is available. The energy needed for these reactions in order to be carbon neutral would come from renewable energy sources such as wind, hydroelectricity and solar as well as  nuclear power. In effect, all of them allow free energy to be stored in easily transportable methanol, which is made immediately from hydrogen and carbon dioxide, rather than attempting to store energy in free hydrogen.
</p>
<dl><dd>CO<sub>2</sub> + 3H<sub>2</sub> → CH<sub>3</sub>OH + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>or with  electric energy
</p>
<dl><dd>CO<sub>2</sub> +5H<sub>2</sub>O + 6 e<sup>−1</sup> → CH<sub>3</sub>OH + 6 HO<sup>−1</sup></dd>
<dd>6 HO<sup>−1</sup>  → 3H<sub>2</sub>O + 3/2 O<sub>2</sub> + 6 e<sup>−1</sup></dd>
<dd>Total:</dd>
<dd>CO<sub>2</sub> +2H<sub>2</sub>O + electric energy → CH<sub>3</sub>OH + 3/2 O<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>The necessary CO<sub>2</sub> would be captured from fossil fuel burning power plants and other industrial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flue_gases" title="Flue gases">flue gases</a> including cement factories. With diminishing fossil fuel resources and therefore CO<sub>2</sub> emissions, the CO<sub>2</sub> content in the air could also be used. Considering the low concentration of CO<sub>2</sub> in air (0.04%) improved and economically viable technologies to absorb CO<sub>2</sub> will have to be developed. For this reason, extraction of CO<sub>2</sub> from water could be more feasible due to its higher concentrations in dissolved form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> This would allow the chemical recycling of CO<sub>2</sub>, thus mimicking nature’s photosynthesis.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages">Advantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanol_economy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Advantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the process of <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>, green plants use the energy of sunlight to split water into free oxygen (which is released) and free hydrogen. Rather than attempt to store the hydrogen, plants immediately capture carbon dioxide from the air to allow the hydrogen to reduce it to storable fuels such as hydrocarbons (plant oils and <a href="/wiki/Terpene" title="Terpene">terpenes</a>) and polyalcohols (<a href="/wiki/Glycerol" title="Glycerol">glycerol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starches</a>). In the methanol economy, any process which similarly produces free hydrogen, proposes to immediately use it "captively" to reduce carbon dioxide into methanol, which, like plant products from photosynthesis, has great advantages in storage and transport over free hydrogen itself.
</p><p>Methanol is a liquid under normal conditions, allowing it to be stored, transported and dispensed easily, much like <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel fuel</a>. It can also be readily transformed by dehydration into <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_ether" title="Dimethyl ether">dimethyl ether</a>, a diesel fuel substitute with a <a href="/wiki/Cetane_number" title="Cetane number">cetane number</a> of 55.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_with_hydrogen">Comparison with hydrogen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanol_economy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Comparison with hydrogen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Methanol economy advantages compared to a hydrogen economy:
</p>
<ul><li>Efficient energy storage by volume, as compared with <a href="/wiki/Compressed_hydrogen" title="Compressed hydrogen">compressed hydrogen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> When hydrogen pressure-confinement vessel is taken into account, an advantage in energy storage by weight can also be realized. The volumetric <a href="/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">energy density</a> of methanol is considerably higher than liquid hydrogen, in part because of the low density of liquid hydrogen of 71 grams/litre. Hence there is actually more hydrogen in a litre of methanol (99 grams/litre) than in a litre of liquid hydrogen, and methanol needs no cryogenic container maintained at a temperature of -253 °C .</li>
<li>A liquid <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_infrastructure" title="Hydrogen infrastructure">hydrogen infrastructure</a> would be prohibitively expensive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zubrin-117_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-zubrin-117-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-romm-94_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-romm-94-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-luft-329_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-luft-329-14">[14]</a></sup> Methanol can use existing gasoline infrastructure with only limited modifications.</li>
<li>Can be blended with gasoline (for example in <a href="/wiki/M85_fuel" title="M85 fuel">M85</a>, a mixture containing 85% methanol and 15% gasoline).</li>
<li>User friendly. Hydrogen is volatile, and its confinements uses high pressure or cryogenic systems.</li>
<li>Less losses : Hydrogen leaks more easily than methanol. Heat will evaporate liquid hydrogen, giving expected losses up to 0.3% per day in storage tanks. (see <a class="external text" href="http://www.chart-ferox.com/getattachment/37f20eca-f9ee-4eaa-88c7-0f373e93f8ca/VT_FULL_EN.aspx" rel="nofollow">Chart Ferox storage tanks Liquid oxygen</a>).</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_with_ethanol">Comparison with ethanol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanol_economy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Comparison with ethanol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Can be made from any organic material using proven technology going through syngas. There is no need to use food crops and compete with food production. The amount of methanol that can be generated from biomass is much greater than ethanol.</li>
<li>Can compete with and complement ethanol in a diversified energy marketplace. Methanol obtained from fossil fuels has a lower price than ethanol.</li>
<li>Can be blended in gasoline like ethanol. In 2007, China blended more than 1 billion US gallons (3,800,000 m<sup>3</sup>) of methanol into fuel and will introduce methanol fuel standard by mid-2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/M85_fuel" title="M85 fuel">M85</a>, a mixture of 85% methanol and 15% gasoline can be used much like <a href="/wiki/E85" title="E85">E85</a> sold in some gas stations today.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disadvantages">Disadvantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Methanol_economy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Disadvantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>High energy costs currently associated with generating and transporting hydrogen offsite.</li>
<li>Depending on the feedstock the generation in itself may be not clean.</li>
<li>Presently generated from <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> still dependent on fossil fuels (although any combustible hydrocarbon can be used).</li>
<li>Energy density (by weight or volume) one half of that of gasoline and 24% less than ethanol<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li>Handling
<ul><li>If no inhibitors are used, methanol is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corrosive" title="Corrosive">corrosive</a> to some common metals including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum" title="Aluminum">aluminum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a>. Parts of the engine fuel-intake systems are made from aluminum. Similar to ethanol, compatible material for fuel tanks, gasket and engine intake have to be used.</li>
<li>As with <a href="/wiki/Pipeline_transport#Ethanol" title="Pipeline transport">similarly corrosive and hydrophilic ethanol</a>, existing <a href="/wiki/Pipeline_transport" title="Pipeline transport">pipelines</a> designed for petroleum products cannot handle methanol. Thus methanol requires shipment at higher energy cost in trucks and trains, until new pipeline infrastructure can be built, or existing pipelines are retrofitted for methanol transport.</li>
<li>Methanol, as an alcohol, increases the permeability of some plastics to fuel vapors (e.g. high-density polyethylene).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>  This property of methanol has the possibility of increasing emissions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compounds" title="Volatile organic compounds">volatile organic compounds</a> (VOCs) from fuel, which contributes to increased tropospheric <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> and possibly human exposure.</li></ul></li>
<li>Low volatility in cold weather: pure methanol-fueled engines can be difficult to start, and they run inefficiently until warmed up. This is why a mixture containing 85% methanol and 15% gasoline called M85 is generally used in ICEs. The gasoline allows the engine to start even at lower temperatures.</li>
<li>With the exception of low level exposure, methanol is toxic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Methanol is lethal when ingested in larger amounts (30 to 100 mL).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> But so are most motor fuels, including gasoline (120 to 300 mL) and diesel fuel. Gasoline also contains small amounts of many compounds known to be carcinogenic (e.g. benzene). Methanol is not a carcinogen, nor does it contain carcinogens. However, methanol may be metabolized in the body to formaldehyde, which is both toxic and carcinogenic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>  Methanol occurs naturally in small quantities in the human body and in edible fruits.</li>
<li>Methanol is a liquid: this creates a greater fire risk compared to hydrogen in open spaces as Methanol leaks do not dissipate. Methanol burns invisibly unlike gasoline. Compared to gasoline, however, methanol is much safer. It is more difficult to ignite and releases less heat when it burns. Methanol fires can be extinguished with plain water, whereas gasoline floats on water and continues to burn. The EPA has estimated that switching fuels from gasoline to methanol would reduce the incidence of fuel related fires by 90%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li>Methanol is water-soluble: accidentally released, it may undergo relatively rapid groundwater transport, causing <a href="/wiki/Groundwater_pollution" title="Groundwater pollution">groundwater pollution</a>, although this risk has not been thoroughly studied. An accidental release of methanol in the environment would, however, cause much less damage than a comparable gasoline or crude <a href="/wiki/Oil_spill" title="Oil spill">oil spill</a>. Unlike these fuels, methanol is biodegradable and totally soluble in water, and would be rapidly diluted to a concentration low enough for <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganism</a> to start <a href="/wiki/Biodegradation" title="Biodegradation">biodegradation</a>. This effect is already exploited in water treatment plants, where methanol is already used for <a href="/wiki/Denitrification" title="Denitrification">denitrification</a> and as a nutrient for bacteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></li></ul>
