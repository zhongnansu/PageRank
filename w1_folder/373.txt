<p><b>Milankovitch cycles</b> describe the collective effects of changes in the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s movements on its <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> over thousands of years. The term is named for <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbian</a> <a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">geophysicist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomer</a> <a href="/wiki/Milutin_Milankovi%C4%87" title="Milutin Milanković">Milutin Milanković</a>. In the 1920s, he hypothesized that variations in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eccentricity_(orbit)" title="Eccentricity (orbit)">eccentricity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">axial tilt</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Precession" title="Precession">precession</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_orbit" title="Earth's orbit">Earth's orbit</a> resulted in cyclical variation in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a> reaching the Earth, and that this <a href="/wiki/Orbital_forcing" title="Orbital forcing">orbital forcing</a> strongly influenced climatic patterns on Earth.
</p><p>Similar astronomical hypotheses had been advanced in the 19th century by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_Adhemar" title="Joseph Adhemar">Joseph Adhemar</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Croll" title="James Croll">James Croll</a> and others, but verification was difficult because there was no reliably dated evidence, and because it was unclear which periods were important.
</p><p>Now, materials on Earth that have been unchanged for millennia (obtained via ice, rock, and deep ocean cores) are being studied to indicate the history of <a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">Earth's climate</a>. Though they are consistent with the Milankovitch hypothesis, there are still several <a href="#Problems">observations</a> that the hypothesis does not explain.
</p>

<h2><span id="Earth.27s_movements"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth's_movements">Earth's movements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Milankovitch_cycles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Earth's movements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s rotation around its axis, and revolution around the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, evolve over time due to <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">gravitational interactions</a> with other bodies in the solar system. The variations are complex, but a few cycles are dominant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Earth's orbit varies between nearly circular and mildly <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">elliptical</a> (its eccentricity varies). When the orbit is more elongated, there is more variation in the distance between the Earth and the Sun, and in the amount of solar radiation, at different times in the year. In addition, the rotational tilt of the Earth (its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obliquity" title="Obliquity">obliquity</a>) changes slightly. A greater tilt makes the seasons more extreme. Finally, the direction in the <a href="/wiki/Fixed_stars" title="Fixed stars">fixed stars</a> pointed to by the Earth's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_Axis" title="Earth's Axis">axis</a> changes (<a href="/wiki/Axial_precession" title="Axial precession">axial precession</a>), while the Earth's elliptical orbit around the Sun rotates (<a href="/wiki/Apsidal_precession" title="Apsidal precession">apsidal precession</a>). The combined effect is that proximity to the Sun occurs during different <a href="/wiki/Season#Astronomical" title="Season">astronomical seasons</a>.
</p><p>Milankovitch studied changes in these movements of the Earth, which alter the amount and location of solar radiation reaching the Earth. This is known as <i>solar forcing</i> (an example of <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a>). Milankovitch emphasized the changes experienced at 65° north due to the great amount of land at that latitude. Land masses change temperature more quickly than oceans, because of the mixing of surface and deep water and the fact that soil has a lower <a href="/wiki/Volumetric_heat_capacity" title="Volumetric heat capacity">volumetric heat capacity</a> than water.
</p>
<h3><span id="Orbital_shape_.28eccentricity.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbital_shape_(eccentricity)">Orbital shape (eccentricity)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Milankovitch_cycles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Orbital shape (eccentricity)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">Orbital eccentricity</a></div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303569">@media all and (max-width:720px){.mw-parser-output .tmulti>.thumbinner{width:100%!important;max-width:none!important}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .tsingle{float:none!important;max-width:none!important;width:100%!important;text-align:center}}</style><div class="thumb tmulti tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:378px;max-width:378px"><div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:202px;max-width:202px"><div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eccentricity_zero.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="582" data-file-width="661" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Eccentricity_zero.svg/200px-Eccentricity_zero.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Eccentricity_zero.svg/300px-Eccentricity_zero.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Eccentricity_zero.svg/400px-Eccentricity_zero.svg.png 2x" width="200"/></a></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">Circular orbit, no eccentricity</div></div><div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:172px;max-width:172px"><div class="thumbimage"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eccentricity_half.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="717" data-file-width="695" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Eccentricity_half.svg/170px-Eccentricity_half.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Eccentricity_half.svg/255px-Eccentricity_half.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Eccentricity_half.svg/340px-Eccentricity_half.svg.png 2x" width="170"/></a></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">Orbit with 0.5 eccentricity; Earth's orbit is never this eccentric</div></div><div style="clear:left"></div></div></div>
<p>The Earth's orbit approximates an <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">ellipse</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eccentricity_(orbit)" title="Eccentricity (orbit)">Eccentricity</a> measures the departure of this ellipse from circularity. The shape of the Earth's orbit varies between nearly circular (with the lowest eccentricity of 0.000055) and mildly elliptical (highest eccentricity of 0.0679)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Its geometric or logarithmic mean is 0.0019. The major component of these variations occurs with a period of 413,000 years (eccentricity variation of ±0.012). Other components have 95,000-year and 125,000-year cycles (with a beat period of 400,000 years). They loosely combine into a 100,000-year cycle (variation of −0.03 to +0.02). The present eccentricity is 0.017 and decreasing.
</p><p>Eccentricity varies primarily due to the gravitational pull of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a>. However, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-major_axis" title="Semi-major axis">semi-major axis</a> of the orbital ellipse remains unchanged; according to <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">perturbation theory</a>, which computes the evolution of the orbit, the semi-major axis is <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_invariant" title="Adiabatic invariant">invariant</a>. The orbital period (the length of a <a href="/wiki/Sidereal_year" title="Sidereal year">sidereal year</a>) is also invariant, because according to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kepler%27s_Laws_of_Planetary_Motion#Third_law" title="Kepler's Laws of Planetary Motion">Kepler's third law</a>, it is determined by the semi-major axis.
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_on_temperature">Effect on temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Milankovitch_cycles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Effect on temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The semi-major axis is a constant. Therefore, when Earth's orbit becomes more eccentric, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-minor_axis" title="Semi-minor axis">semi-minor axis</a> shortens. This increases the magnitude of seasonal changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The relative increase in solar irradiation at closest approach to the Sun (<a href="/wiki/Perihelion_and_aphelion" title="Perihelion and aphelion">perihelion</a>) compared to the irradiation at the furthest distance (<a href="/wiki/Perihelion_and_aphelion" title="Perihelion and aphelion">aphelion</a>) is slightly larger than four times the eccentricity. For Earth's current orbital eccentricity, incoming solar radiation varies by about 6.8%, while the distance from the Sun currently varies by only 3.4% (5.1 million <a href="/wiki/Kilometre" title="Kilometre">km</a>). Perihelion presently occurs around January 3, while aphelion is around July 4. When the orbit is at its most eccentric, the amount of solar radiation at perihelion will be about 23% more than at aphelion. However, the Earth's eccentricity is always so small that the variation in solar irradiation is a minor factor in <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">seasonal</a> climate variation, compared to axial tilt and even compared to the relative ease of heating the larger land masses of the northern hemisphere.
</p>

<p>The seasons are quadrants of the Earth's orbit, marked by the two solstices and the two equinoxes. <a href="/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws_of_planetary_motion#Second_law" title="Kepler's laws of planetary motion">Kepler's second law</a> states that a body in orbit traces equal areas over equal times; its orbital velocity is highest around perihelion and lowest around aphelion. The Earth spends less time near perihelion and more time near aphelion. This means that the lengths of the seasons vary.
</p><p>Perihelion currently occurs around January 3, so the Earth's greater velocity shortens winter and autumn in the northern hemisphere. Summer in the northern hemisphere is 4.66 days longer than winter, and spring is 2.9 days longer than autumn.
</p><p>Greater eccentricity increases the variation in the Earth's orbital velocity. However, currently, the Earth's orbit is becoming less eccentric (more nearly circular). This will make the seasons more similar in length.
</p>

<h3><span id="Axial_tilt_.28obliquity.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Axial_tilt_(obliquity)">Axial tilt (obliquity)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Milankovitch_cycles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Axial tilt (obliquity)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">Axial tilt</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Earth_obliquity_range.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="523" data-file-width="545" height="211" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Earth_obliquity_range.svg/220px-Earth_obliquity_range.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Earth_obliquity_range.svg/330px-Earth_obliquity_range.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Earth_obliquity_range.svg/440px-Earth_obliquity_range.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Earth_obliquity_range.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>22.1–24.5° range of Earth's obliquity</div></div></div>
<p>The angle of the Earth's axial tilt with respect to the orbital plane (the obliquity of the <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">ecliptic</a>) varies between 22.1° and 24.5°, over a cycle of about 41,000 years. The current tilt is 23.44°, roughly halfway between its extreme values. The tilt last reached its maximum in 8,700 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Before_Common_Era" title="Before Common Era">BCE</a>. It is now in the decreasing phase of its cycle, and will reach its minimum around the year 11,800 <a href="/wiki/Common_Era" title="Common Era">CE</a>.
</p><p>Increased tilt increases the amplitude of the seasonal cycle in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation">insolation</a>, providing more solar radiation in each hemisphere's summer and less in winter. However, these effects are not uniform everywhere on the Earth's surface. Increased tilt increases the total annual solar radiation at higher latitudes, and decreases the total closer to the equator.
</p><p>The current trend of decreasing tilt, by itself, will promote milder seasons (warmer winters and colder summers), as well as an overall cooling trend. Because most of the planet's snow and ice lies at high latitude, decreasing tilt may encourage the onset of an <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a> for two reasons: There is less overall summer insolation, and also less insolation at higher latitudes, which melts less of the previous winter's snow and ice.
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Axial_precession">Axial precession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Milankovitch_cycles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Axial precession">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Axial_precession" title="Axial precession">Axial precession</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Earth_precession.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="764" data-file-width="670" height="251" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Earth_precession.svg/220px-Earth_precession.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Earth_precession.svg/330px-Earth_precession.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Earth_precession.svg/440px-Earth_precession.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Earth_precession.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Precessional movement</div></div></div>
<p>Axial precession is the trend in the direction of the Earth's axis of rotation relative to the fixed stars, with a period of 25,771.5 years. This motion means that eventually Polaris will no longer be the north <a href="/wiki/Pole_star" title="Pole star">pole star</a>. It is caused by the tidal forces exerted by the Sun and the Moon on the solid Earth; both contribute roughly equally to this effect.
</p><p>Currently, perihelion occurs during the southern hemisphere's summer. This means that solar radiation due to (1) axial tilt inclining the southern hemisphere toward the Sun and (2) the Earth's proximity to the Sun, both reach maximum during the summer and both reach minimum during the winter. Their effects on heating are additive, which means that seasonal variation in irradiation of the southern hemisphere is more extreme. In the northern hemisphere, these two factors reach maximum at opposite times of the year: The north is tilted toward the Sun when the Earth is furthest from the Sun. The two effects work in opposite directions, resulting in less extreme variations in insolation.
</p><p>In about 13,000 years, the north pole will be tilted toward the Sun when the Earth is at perihelion. Axial tilt and orbital eccentricity will both contribute their maximum increase in solar radiation during the northern hemisphere's summer. Axial precession will promote more extreme variation in irradiation of the northern hemisphere and less extreme variation in the south.
</p><p>When the Earth's axis is aligned such that aphelion and perihelion occur near the equinoxes, axial tilt will not be aligned with or against eccentricity.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Apsidal_precession">Apsidal precession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Milankovitch_cycles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Apsidal precession">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Apsidal_precession" title="Apsidal precession">Apsidal precession</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:347px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Precessing_Kepler_orbit_280frames_e0.6_smaller.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="345" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/89/Precessing_Kepler_orbit_280frames_e0.6_smaller.gif" width="345"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Precessing_Kepler_orbit_280frames_e0.6_smaller.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Planets orbiting the Sun follow elliptical (oval) orbits that rotate gradually over time (apsidal precession). The eccentricity of this ellipse, as well as the rate of precession, is exaggerated for visualization. Most orbits in the Solar System have a much smaller eccentricity, making them nearly circular.</div></div></div>
<p>In addition, the orbital ellipse itself precesses in space, in an irregular fashion, completing a full cycle every 112,000 years relative to the fixed stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heuvel_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heuvel-6">[6]</a></sup> Apsidal precession occurs in the plane of the ecliptic and alters the orientation of the Earth's orbit relative to the ecliptic. This happens primarily as a result of interactions with Jupiter and Saturn. Smaller contributions are also made by the sun's oblateness and by the effects of <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a> that are well known for Mercury.
</p><p>Apsidal precession combines with the 25,771.5-year cycle of axial precession (see <a href="#Axial_precession">above</a>) to vary the position in the year that the Earth reaches perihelion.  Apsidal precession shortens this period to 23,000 years on average (varying between 20,800 and 29,000 years).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heuvel_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Heuvel-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:452px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Precession_and_seasons.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="639" data-file-width="787" height="365" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Precession_and_seasons.svg/450px-Precession_and_seasons.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Precession_and_seasons.svg/675px-Precession_and_seasons.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Precession_and_seasons.svg/900px-Precession_and_seasons.svg.png 2x" width="450"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Precession_and_seasons.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Effects of precession on the seasons (using the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a> terms).</div></div></div>
<p>As the orientation of Earth's orbit changes, each season will gradually start earlier in the year. Precession means the Earth's nonuniform motion (see <a href="#Orbital_shape_and_temperature">above</a>) will affect different seasons. Winter, for instance, will be in a different section of the orbit. When the Earth's apsides are aligned with the equinoxes, the length of spring and summer combined will equal that of autumn and winter. When they are aligned with the solstices, the difference in the length of these seasons will be greatest.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Orbital_inclination">Orbital inclination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Milankovitch_cycles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Orbital inclination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">Orbital inclination</a></div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">inclination</a> of Earth's orbit drifts up and down relative to its present orbit. This three-dimensional movement is known as "precession of the ecliptic" or "planetary precession". Earth's current inclination relative to the <a href="/wiki/Invariable_plane" title="Invariable plane">invariable plane</a> (the plane that represents the <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">angular momentum</a> of the Solar System, approximately the orbital plane of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>) is 1.57°.
</p><p>Milankovitch did not study apsidal precession. It was discovered more recently and measured, relative to Earth's orbit, to have a period of about 70,000 years. However, when measured independently of Earth's orbit, but relative to the invariable plane, precession has a period of about 100,000 years. This period is very similar to the 100,000-year eccentricity period. Both periods closely match the 100,000-year pattern of glacial events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid11607741_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid11607741-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Problems">Problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Milankovitch_cycles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cyclic_deposits.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1704" data-file-width="2272" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Cyclic_deposits.jpg/220px-Cyclic_deposits.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Cyclic_deposits.jpg/330px-Cyclic_deposits.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Cyclic_deposits.jpg/440px-Cyclic_deposits.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cyclic_deposits.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The nature of sediments can vary in a cyclic fashion, and these cycles can be displayed in the sedimentary record. Here, cycles can be observed in the colouration and resistance of different strata.</div></div></div>
<p>Artifacts taken from the Earth have been studied to infer the cycles of past climate. A study of the chronology of Antarctic ice cores using oxygen-nitrogen ratios in air bubbles trapped in the ice, which appear to respond directly to the local insolation, concluded that the climatic response documented in the ice cores was driven by northern hemisphere insolation as proposed by the Milankovitch hypothesis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Analysis of deep-ocean cores, analysis of lake depths,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and a seminal paper by <a href="/wiki/James_Hays" title="James Hays">Hays</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Imbrie" title="John Imbrie">Imbrie</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Shackleton" title="Nicholas Shackleton">Shackleton</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hays1976_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hays1976-11">[11]</a></sup> provide additional validation through physical artifacts. Climate records contained in a 1,700 ft (520 m) core of rock drilled in Arizona show a pattern synchronized with Earth's eccentricity, and cores drilled in New England match it, going back 215 million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>These studies fit so well with the orbital periods that they supported Milankovitch's hypothesis that variations in the Earth's orbit influence climate. However, the fit was not perfect, and problems remained reconciling hypothesis with observation.
</p>
<h3><span id="100.2C000-year_problem"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="100,000-year_problem">100,000-year problem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Milankovitch_cycles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 100,000-year problem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/100,000-year_problem" title="100,000-year problem">100,000-year problem</a></div>
<p>Of all the orbital cycles, Milankovitch believed that obliquity had the greatest effect on climate, and that it did so by varying the summer insolation in northern high latitudes. Therefore, he deduced a 41,000-year period for ice ages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Milankovitch_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Milankovitch-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> However, subsequent research<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hays1976_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hays1976-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> has shown that <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a> cycles of the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">Quaternary glaciation</a> over the last million years have been at a 100,000-year period, which matches the eccentricity cycle.
</p><p>Various explanations for this discrepancy have been proposed, including <a href="/wiki/Frequency_modulation" title="Frequency modulation">frequency modulation</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> or various feedbacks (from <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Carbon dioxide in Earth's atmosphere">carbon dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic rays</a>, or from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_sheet_dynamics" title="Ice sheet dynamics">ice sheet dynamics</a>).
Some models can reproduce the 100,000-year cycles as a result of non-linear interactions between small changes in the Earth's orbit and internal oscillations of the climate system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ghil1994_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ghil1994-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gildor2000_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gildor2000-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Jung-Eun Lee of <a href="/wiki/Brown_University" title="Brown University">Brown University</a> proposes that precession changes the amount of energy that Earth absorbs, because the southern hemisphere's greater ability to grow sea ice reflects more energy away from Earth.  Moreover, Lee says, "Precession only matters when eccentricity is large. That's why we see a stronger 100,000-year pace than a 21,000-year pace."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some have argued that the length of the climate record is insufficient to establish a statistically significant relationship between climate and eccentricity variations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wunsch2004_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wunsch2004-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transition_problem">Transition problem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Milankovitch_cycles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Transition problem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Five_Myr_Climate_Change.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="372" data-file-width="1024" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Five_Myr_Climate_Change.svg/400px-Five_Myr_Climate_Change.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Five_Myr_Climate_Change.svg/600px-Five_Myr_Climate_Change.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Five_Myr_Climate_Change.svg/800px-Five_Myr_Climate_Change.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Five_Myr_Climate_Change.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Variations of cycle times, curves determined from ocean sediments</div></div></div>
<p>In fact, from 1–3 million years ago, climate cycles did match the 41,000-year cycle in obliquity. After 1 million years ago, this switched to the 100,000-year cycle matching eccentricity. The <i>transition problem</i> refers to the need to explain what changed 1 million years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unsplit_peak_problem">Unsplit peak problem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Milankovitch_cycles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Unsplit peak problem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Even the well-dated climate records of the last million years do not exactly match the shape of the eccentricity curve. Eccentricity has component cycles of 95,000 and 125,000 years. However, some researchers say the records do not show these peaks, but only show a single cycle of 100,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stage_5_problem">Stage 5 problem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Milankovitch_cycles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Stage 5 problem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Deep-sea core samples show that the interglacial interval known as <a href="/wiki/Marine_isotope_stage#Stages" title="Marine isotope stage">marine isotope stage 5</a> began 130,000 years ago. This is 10,000 years before the solar forcing that the Milankovitch hypothesis predicts.  (This is also known as the <i>causality problem</i>, because the effect precedes the putative cause.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_exceeds_cause">Effect exceeds cause</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Milankovitch_cycles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Effect exceeds cause">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_feedback" title="Climate change feedback">Climate change feedback</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vostok_420ky_4curves_insolation.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1092" data-file-width="1600" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Vostok_420ky_4curves_insolation.jpg/250px-Vostok_420ky_4curves_insolation.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Vostok_420ky_4curves_insolation.jpg/375px-Vostok_420ky_4curves_insolation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Vostok_420ky_4curves_insolation.jpg/500px-Vostok_420ky_4curves_insolation.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vostok_420ky_4curves_insolation.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>420,000 years of <a href="/wiki/Ice_core" title="Ice core">ice core</a> data from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vostok,_Antarctica" title="Vostok, Antarctica">Vostok, Antarctica</a> research station, with more recent times on the left</div></div></div>
<p>Artifacts show that the variation in Earth's climate is much more extreme than the variation in the intensity of solar radiation calculated as the Earth's orbit evolves. If orbital forcing causes <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, science needs to explain why the observed effect is amplified compared to the theoretical effect.
</p><p>Some climate systems exhibit amplification (<a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedback</a>) and damping responses (<a href="/wiki/Negative_feedback" title="Negative feedback">negative feedback</a>). An example of amplification would be if, with the land masses around 65° north covered in year-round ice, solar energy were reflected away. Amplification would mean that an ice age induces changes that impede orbital forcing from ending the ice age.
</p><p>The Earth's current <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">orbital inclination</a> is 1.57° (see <a href="#Orbital_inclination">above</a>). Earth presently moves through the invariable plane around January 9 and July 9. At these times, there is an increase in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteor" title="Meteor">meteors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Noctilucent_cloud" title="Noctilucent cloud">noctilucent clouds</a>. If this is because there is a disk of dust and debris in the invariable plane, then when the Earth's orbital inclination is near 0° and it is orbiting through this dust, materials could be accreted into the atmosphere. This process could explain the narrowness of the 100,000-year climate cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Muller1997_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Muller1997-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Muller2_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Muller2-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Present_and_future_conditions">Present and future conditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Milankovitch_cycles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Present and future conditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:InsolationSummerSolstice65N.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="245" data-file-width="1000" height="123" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/InsolationSummerSolstice65N.png/500px-InsolationSummerSolstice65N.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/InsolationSummerSolstice65N.png/750px-InsolationSummerSolstice65N.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/90/InsolationSummerSolstice65N.png 2x" width="500"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:InsolationSummerSolstice65N.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Past and future of daily average insolation at top of the atmosphere on the day of the summer solstice, at 65 N latitude. The green curve is with eccentricity <i>e</i> hypothetically set to 0. The red curve uses the actual (predicted) value of <i>e</i>. Blue dot is current conditions, at 2 ky A.D.</div></div></div>
<p>Since orbital variations are predictable,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Varadi2003_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Varadi2003-28">[28]</a></sup> any model that relates orbital variations to climate can be run forward to predict future climate, with two caveats: the mechanism by which <a href="/wiki/Orbital_forcing" title="Orbital forcing">orbital forcing</a> influences climate is not definitive; and non-orbital effects can be important (for example, <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">Human impact on the environment</a> principally increases in <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> result in a warmer climate<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>).
</p><p>An often-cited 1980 orbital model by <a href="/wiki/John_Imbrie" title="John Imbrie">Imbrie</a> predicted "the long-term cooling trend that began some 6,000 years ago will continue for the next 23,000 years."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Imbriel1980_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Imbriel1980-32">[32]</a></sup> More recent work suggests that orbital variations should gradually increase 65° N summer insolation over the next 25,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Earth's orbit will become less eccentric for about the next 100,000 years, so changes in this insolation will be dominated by changes in obliquity, and should not decline enough to permit a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Next_glacial_period" title="Next glacial period">new glacial period</a> in the next 50,000 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berger2002_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Berger2002-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_beyond_Earth">Effects beyond Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Milankovitch_cycles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Effects beyond Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Other bodies in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> undergo orbital fluctuations like the Milankovitch cycles. Any geological effects would not be as pronounced as climate change on the Earth, but might cause the movement of elements in the solid state:
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mars">Mars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Milankovitch_cycles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Mars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> has no moon large enough to stabilize its obliquity, which has varied from 10 to 70 degrees. This would explain recent observations of its surface compared to evidence of different conditions in its past, such as the extent of its <a href="/wiki/Martian_polar_ice_caps" title="Martian polar ice caps">polar caps</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Outer_planets">Outer planets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Milankovitch_cycles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Outer planets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Saturn's moon <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a> has a cycle of approximately 60,000 years that could change the location of the <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> lakes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Neptune's moon <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">Triton</a> has a variation similar to Titan's, which could cause its solid <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> deposits to migrate over long time scales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exoplanets">Exoplanets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Milankovitch_cycles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Exoplanets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Scientists using computer models to study extreme axial tilts have concluded that high obliquity would cause climate extremes that would threaten Earth-like life. They noted that high obliquity would not likely sterilize a planet completely, but would make it harder for warm-blooded, land-based life to thrive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Although the obliquity they studied is more extreme than Earth ever experiences, there are scenarios 1.5 to 4.5 billion years from now, as the Moon's stabilizing effect lessens, where obliquity could leave its current range and the poles could eventually point almost directly at the Sun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
