<p><b>Olfaction</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemoreception" title="Chemoreception">chemoreception</a> that forms the <a href="/wiki/Sense" title="Sense">sense</a> of smell. Olfaction has many purposes, such as the detection of hazards, <a href="/wiki/Pheromone" title="Pheromone">pheromones</a>, and food. It integrates with other senses to form the sense of <a href="/wiki/Flavor" title="Flavor">flavor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> Olfaction occurs when <a href="/wiki/Aroma_compound" title="Aroma compound">odorants</a> bind to specific sites on <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_receptor" title="Olfactory receptor">olfactory receptors</a> located in the <a href="/wiki/Nasal_cavity" title="Nasal cavity">nasal cavity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Glomerulus_(olfaction)" title="Glomerulus (olfaction)">Glomeruli</a> aggregate signals from these receptors and transmit them to the <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_bulb" title="Olfactory bulb">olfactory bulb</a>, where the sensory input will start to interact with parts of the brain responsible for smell identification, <a href="/wiki/Memory" title="Memory">memory</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Emotion" title="Emotion">emotion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Often, land organisms will have separate olfaction systems for smell and taste (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Orthonasal_smell&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Orthonasal smell (page does not exist)">orthonasal smell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Retronasal_smell" title="Retronasal smell">retronasal smell</a>), but water-dwelling organisms usually have only one system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_olfactory_system">Main olfactory system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olfaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Main olfactory system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrates</a>, smells are sensed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olfactory_sensory_neuron" title="Olfactory sensory neuron">olfactory sensory neurons</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_epithelium" title="Olfactory epithelium">olfactory epithelium</a>. The olfactory epithelium is made up of at least six morphologically and biochemically different cell types.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The proportion of olfactory <a href="/wiki/Epithelium" title="Epithelium">epithelium</a> compared to respiratory epithelium (not innervated, or supplied with nerves) gives an indication of the animal's olfactory sensitivity. Humans have about 10 cm<sup>2</sup> (1.6 sq in) of olfactory epithelium, whereas some dogs have 170 cm<sup>2</sup> (26 sq in). A dog's olfactory epithelium is also considerably more densely innervated, with a hundred times more receptors per square centimeter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Molecules of odorants passing through the <a href="/wiki/Superior_nasal_concha" title="Superior nasal concha">superior nasal concha</a> of the nasal passages dissolve in the <a href="/wiki/Mucus" title="Mucus">mucus</a> that lines the superior portion of the cavity and are detected by <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_receptor" title="Olfactory receptor">olfactory receptors</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dendrites" title="Dendrites">dendrites</a> of the olfactory sensory neurons. This may occur by diffusion or by the binding of the odorant to <a href="/wiki/Odorant-binding_protein" title="Odorant-binding protein">odorant-binding proteins</a>. The mucus overlying the epithelium contains <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mucopolysaccharides" title="Mucopolysaccharides">mucopolysaccharides</a>, salts, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enzymes" title="Enzymes">enzymes</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibodies" title="Antibodies">antibodies</a> (these are highly important, as the olfactory neurons provide a direct passage for infection to pass to the <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a>). This mucus acts as a solvent for odor molecules, flows constantly, and is replaced approximately every ten minutes.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, smells are sensed by olfactory sensory neurons in the chemosensory <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sensilla" title="Sensilla">sensilla</a>, which are present in insect antenna, palps, and tarsa, but also on other parts of the insect body. Odorants penetrate into the cuticle pores of chemosensory sensilla and get in contact with insect odorant-binding proteins (OBPs) or <a href="/wiki/Chemosensory_protein" title="Chemosensory protein">Chemosensory proteins</a> (CSPs), before activating the sensory neurons.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Receptor_neuron">Receptor neuron</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olfaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Receptor neuron">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The binding of the <a href="/wiki/Ligand" title="Ligand">ligand</a> (odor molecule or odorant) to the receptor leads to an <a href="/wiki/Action_potential" title="Action potential">action potential</a> in the receptor neuron, via a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_messenger" title="Second messenger">second messenger</a> pathway, depending on the organism. In mammals, the odorants stimulate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adenylate_cyclase" title="Adenylate cyclase">adenylate cyclase</a> to synthesize <a href="/wiki/Cyclic_adenosine_monophosphate" title="Cyclic adenosine monophosphate">cAMP</a> via a <a href="/wiki/G_protein" title="G protein">G protein</a> called G<sub>olf</sub>. cAMP, which is the second messenger here, opens a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclic_nucleotide-gated_ion_channel" title="Cyclic nucleotide-gated ion channel">cyclic nucleotide-gated ion channel</a> (CNG), producing an influx of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">cations</a> (largely <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">Ca</a><sup>2+</sup>  with some <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">Na</a><sup>+</sup>) into the cell, slightly depolarising it. The Ca<sup>2+</sup> in turn opens a Ca<sup>2+</sup>-activated <a href="/wiki/Chloride_channel" title="Chloride channel">chloride channel</a>, leading to efflux of <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">Cl</a><sup>−</sup>, further depolarizing the cell and triggering an action potential. Ca<sup>2+</sup> is then extruded through a <a href="/wiki/Sodium-calcium_exchanger" title="Sodium-calcium exchanger">sodium-calcium exchanger</a>. A calcium-<a href="/wiki/Calmodulin" title="Calmodulin">calmodulin</a> complex also acts to inhibit the binding of cAMP to the cAMP-dependent channel, thus contributing to olfactory adaptation.
</p><p>This mechanism of transduction is somewhat unusual, in that cAMP works by directly binding to the <a href="/wiki/Ion_channel" title="Ion channel">ion channel</a> rather than through activation of <a href="/wiki/Protein_kinase_A" title="Protein kinase A">protein kinase A</a>. It is similar to the transduction mechanism for <a href="/wiki/Photoreceptor_cell" title="Photoreceptor cell">photoreceptors</a>, in which the second messenger <a href="/wiki/Cyclic_guanosine_monophosphate" title="Cyclic guanosine monophosphate">cGMP</a> works by directly binding to ion channels, suggesting that maybe one of these receptors was evolutionarily adapted into the other. There are also considerable similarities in the immediate processing of stimuli by <a href="/wiki/Lateral_inhibition" title="Lateral inhibition">lateral inhibition</a>.
</p><p>Averaged activity of the receptor neurons can be measured in several ways. In vertebrates, responses to an odor can be measured by an <a href="/wiki/Electro-olfactography" title="Electro-olfactography">electro-olfactogram</a> or through calcium imaging of receptor neuron terminals in the olfactory bulb. In insects, one can perform <a href="/wiki/Electroantennography" title="Electroantennography">electroantennography</a> or calcium imaging within the olfactory bulb.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Olfactory_bulb_projections">Olfactory bulb projections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olfaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Olfactory bulb projections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

In general, glomeruli receive input from ORs of one specific type and connect to the principal neurons of the OB, mitral and tufted cells (MT cells)."><img alt="Early Olfactory System" data-file-height="1469" data-file-width="1017" height="433" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Early_Olfactory_System.svg/300px-Early_Olfactory_System.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Early_Olfactory_System.svg/450px-Early_Olfactory_System.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Early_Olfactory_System.svg/600px-Early_Olfactory_System.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a></div>
<p>Olfactory sensory neurons project <a href="/wiki/Axon" title="Axon">axons</a> to the brain within the <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_nerve" title="Olfactory nerve">olfactory nerve</a>, (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cranial_nerve" title="Cranial nerve">cranial nerve</a><span class="nowrap"> </span>I). These nerve fibers, lacking <a href="/wiki/Myelin" title="Myelin">myelin</a> sheaths, pass to the <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_bulb" title="Olfactory bulb">olfactory bulb</a> of the brain through perforations in the <a href="/wiki/Cribriform_plate" title="Cribriform plate">cribriform plate</a>, which in turn projects olfactory information to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olfactory_cortex" title="Olfactory cortex">olfactory cortex</a> and other areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The axons from the <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_receptor" title="Olfactory receptor">olfactory receptors</a> converge in the outer layer of the olfactory bulb within small (≈50 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micrometer_(unit)" title="Micrometer (unit)">micrometers</a> in diameter) structures called <a href="/wiki/Glomerulus_(olfaction)" title="Glomerulus (olfaction)">glomeruli</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitral_cell" title="Mitral cell">Mitral cells</a>, located in the inner layer of the olfactory bulb, form synapses with the axons of the sensory neurons within glomeruli and send the information about the <a href="/wiki/Odor" title="Odor">odor</a> to other parts of the olfactory system, where multiple signals may be processed to form a synthesized olfactory perception. A large degree of convergence occurs, with 25,000 axons synapsing on 25 or so mitral cells, and with each of these mitral cells projecting to multiple glomeruli. Mitral cells also project to <a href="/wiki/Periglomerular_cell" title="Periglomerular cell">periglomerular cells</a> and <a href="/wiki/Granule_cell" title="Granule cell">granular cells</a> that inhibit the mitral cells surrounding it (<a href="/wiki/Lateral_inhibition" title="Lateral inhibition">lateral inhibition</a>). Granular cells also mediate inhibition and excitation of mitral cells through pathways from centrifugal fibers and the anterior olfactory nuclei. Neuromodulators like <a href="/wiki/Acetylcholine" title="Acetylcholine">acetylcholine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serotonin" title="Serotonin">serotonin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norepinephrine" title="Norepinephrine">norepinephrine</a> all send axons to the olfactory bulb and have been implicated in gain modulation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> pattern separation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Memory" title="Memory">memory</a> functions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> respectively.
</p><p>The mitral cells leave the olfactory bulb in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lateral_olfactory_tract" title="Lateral olfactory tract">lateral olfactory tract</a>, which synapses on five major regions of the cerebrum: the <a href="/wiki/Anterior_olfactory_nucleus" title="Anterior olfactory nucleus">anterior olfactory nucleus</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_tubercle" title="Olfactory tubercle">olfactory tubercle</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Amygdala" title="Amygdala">amygdala</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Piriform_cortex" title="Piriform cortex">piriform cortex</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Entorhinal_cortex" title="Entorhinal cortex">entorhinal cortex</a>. The anterior olfactory nucleus projects, via the <a href="/wiki/Anterior_commissure" title="Anterior commissure">anterior commissure</a>, to the contralateral olfactory bulb, inhibiting it. The piriform cortex has two major divisions with anatomically distinct organizations and functions. The anterior piriform cortex (APC) appears to be better at determining the chemical structure of the odorant molecules, and the posterior piriform cortex (PPC) has a strong role in categorizing odors and assessing similarities between odors (e.g. minty, woody, and citrus are odors that can, despite being highly variant chemicals, be distinguished via the PPC in a concentration-independent manner).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The piriform cortex projects to the <a href="/wiki/Medial_dorsal_nucleus" title="Medial dorsal nucleus">medial dorsal nucleus</a> of the thalamus, which then projects to the orbitofrontal cortex. The orbitofrontal cortex mediates conscious perception of the odor (citation needed). The three-layered piriform cortex projects to a number of thalamic and hypothalamic nuclei, the <a href="/wiki/Hippocampus" title="Hippocampus">hippocampus</a> and amygdala and the orbitofrontal cortex, but its function is largely unknown. The entorhinal cortex projects to the amygdala and is involved in emotional and autonomic responses to odor. It also projects to the hippocampus and is involved in motivation and memory. Odor information is stored in <a href="/wiki/Long-term_memory" title="Long-term memory">long-term memory</a> and has strong connections to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emotional_memory" title="Emotional memory">emotional memory</a>. This is possibly due to the olfactory system's close anatomical ties to the <a href="/wiki/Limbic_system" title="Limbic system">limbic system</a> and hippocampus, areas of the brain that have long been known to be involved in emotion and place memory, respectively.
</p><p>Since any one receptor is responsive to various odorants, and there is a great deal of convergence at the level of the olfactory bulb, it may seem strange that human beings are able to distinguish so many different odors. It seems that a highly complex form of processing must be occurring; however, as it can be shown that, while many neurons in the olfactory bulb (and even the pyriform cortex and amygdala) are responsive to many different odors, half the neurons in the orbitofrontal cortex are responsive to only one odor, and the rest to only a few. It has been shown through microelectrode studies that each individual odor gives a particular spatial map of excitation in the olfactory bulb. It is possible that the brain is able to distinguish specific odors through spatial encoding, but temporal coding must also be taken into account. Over time, the spatial maps change, even for one particular odor, and the brain must be able to process these details as well.
</p><p>Inputs from the two <a href="/wiki/Nostril" title="Nostril">nostrils</a> have separate inputs to the brain, with the result that, when each nostril takes up a different odorant, a person may experience perceptual rivalry in the olfactory sense akin to that of <a href="/wiki/Binocular_rivalry" title="Binocular rivalry">binocular rivalry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, smells are sensed by sensilla located on the antenna and maxillary palp and first processed by the <a href="/wiki/Antennal_lobe" title="Antennal lobe">antennal lobe</a> (analogous to the <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_bulb" title="Olfactory bulb">olfactory bulb</a>), and next by the mushroom bodies and lateral horn.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accessory_olfactory_system">Accessory olfactory system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olfaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Accessory olfactory system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many animals, including most mammals and reptiles, but not humans, have two distinct and segregated olfactory systems: a main olfactory system, which detects volatile stimuli, and an accessory olfactory system, which detects fluid-phase stimuli. Behavioral evidence suggests that these fluid-phase stimuli often function as <a href="/wiki/Pheromone" title="Pheromone">pheromones</a>, although pheromones can also be detected by the main olfactory system. In the accessory olfactory system, stimuli are detected by the <a href="/wiki/Vomeronasal_organ" title="Vomeronasal organ">vomeronasal organ</a>, located in the vomer, between the <a href="/wiki/Nose" title="Nose">nose</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mouth" title="Mouth">mouth</a>. Snakes use it to smell prey, sticking their tongue out and touching it to the organ. Some mammals make a facial expression called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flehmen" title="Flehmen">flehmen</a> to direct stimuli to this organ.
</p><p>The sensory receptors of the accessory olfactory system are located in the vomeronasal organ. As in the main olfactory system, the axons of these sensory neurons project from the vomeronasal organ to the <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_bulb#Accessory_olfactory_bulb" title="Olfactory bulb">accessory olfactory bulb</a>, which in the mouse is located on the dorsal-posterior portion of the main <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_bulb" title="Olfactory bulb">olfactory bulb</a>. Unlike in the main olfactory system, the axons that leave the accessory olfactory bulb do not project to the brain's cortex but rather to targets in the <a href="/wiki/Amygdala" title="Amygdala">amygdala</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bed_nucleus_of_the_stria_terminalis" title="Bed nucleus of the stria terminalis">bed nucleus of the stria terminalis</a>, and from there to the <a href="/wiki/Hypothalamus" title="Hypothalamus">hypothalamus</a>, where they may influence aggression and mating behavior.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_incest_avoidance">Human incest avoidance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olfaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Human incest avoidance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Body_odor" title="Body odor">Body odor</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Major_histocompatibility_complex" title="Major histocompatibility complex">MHC</a> genes (known as <a href="/wiki/Human_leukocyte_antigen" title="Human leukocyte antigen">HLA</a> in humans) are a group of genes present in many animals and important for the <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune system</a>; in general, offspring from parents with differing MHC genes have a stronger immune system. Fish, mice, and female humans are able to smell some aspect of the MHC genes of potential sex partners and prefer partners with MHC genes different from their own.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Humans can detect individuals that are blood-related kin (mothers/fathers and children but not husbands and wives) from olfaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Mothers can identify by body odor their biological children but not their stepchildren. Pre-adolescent children can olfactorily detect their full siblings but not half-siblings or step siblings, and this might explain <a href="/wiki/Inbreeding_avoidance" title="Inbreeding avoidance">incest avoidance</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Westermarck_effect" title="Westermarck effect">Westermarck effect</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Functional imaging shows that this olfactory kinship detection process involves the frontal-temporal junction, the <a href="/wiki/Insular_cortex" title="Insular cortex">insula</a>, and the dorsomedial <a href="/wiki/Prefrontal_cortex" title="Prefrontal cortex">prefrontal cortex</a>, but not the primary or secondary olfactory cortices, or the related <a href="/wiki/Piriform_cortex" title="Piriform cortex">piriform cortex</a> or <a href="/wiki/Orbitofrontal_cortex" title="Orbitofrontal cortex">orbitofrontal cortex</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Olfactory_coding_and_perception">Olfactory coding and perception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olfaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Olfactory coding and perception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The process by which olfactory information is coded in the brain to allow for proper perception is still being researched, and is not completely understood. When an odorant is detected by receptors, they in a sense break the odorant down, and then the brain puts the odorant back together for identification and perception.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The odorant binds to receptors that recognize only a specific functional group, or feature, of the odorant, which is why the chemical nature of the odorant is important.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leon_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Leon-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>After binding the odorant, the receptor is activated and will send a signal to the glomeruli.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leon_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Leon-19">[19]</a></sup> Each glomerulus receives signals from multiple receptors that detect similar odorant features. Because several receptor types are activated due to the different chemical features of the odorant, several glomeruli are activated as well. All of the signals from the glomeruli are then sent to the brain, where the combination of glomeruli activation encodes the different chemical features of the odorant. The brain then essentially puts the pieces of the activation pattern back together in order to identify and perceive the odorant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leon_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-Leon-19">[19]</a></sup> This distributed code allows the brain to detect specific odors in mixtures of many background odors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is a general idea that the layout of brain structures corresponds to physical features of stimuli (called topographic coding), and similar analogies have been made in olfaction with concepts such as a layout corresponding to chemical features (called chemotopy) or perceptual features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-understanding_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-understanding-21">[21]</a></sup> While chemotopy remains a highly controversial concept,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> evidence exists for perceptual information implemented in the spatial dimensions of olfactory networks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-understanding_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-understanding-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although conventional wisdom and lay literature, based on impressionistic findings in the 1920s, have long presented human olfaction as capable of distinguishing between roughly 10,000 unique odors, recent research has suggested that the average individual is capable of distinguishing over one trillion unique odors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Researchers in the most recent study, which tested the psychophysical responses to combinations of over 128 unique odor molecules with combinations composed of up to 30 different component molecules, noted that this estimate is "conservative" and that some subjects of their research might be capable of deciphering between a thousand trillion odorants, adding that their worst performer could probably still distinguish between 80<span class="nowrap"> </span>million scents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Authors of the study concluded, "This is far more than previous estimates of distinguishable olfactory stimuli. It demonstrates that the human olfactory system, with its hundreds of different olfactory receptors, far out performs the other senses in the number of physically different stimuli it can discriminate."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> However, it was also noted by the authors that the ability to distinguish between smells is not analogous to being able to consistently identify them, and that subjects were not typically capable of identifying individual odor stimulants from within the odors the researchers had prepared from multiple odor molecules. In November 2014 the study was strongly criticized by Caltech scientist Markus Meister, who wrote that the study's "extravagant claims are based on errors of mathematical logic".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The logic of his paper has in turn been criticized by the authors of the original paper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetics">Genetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olfaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Genetics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Different people smell different odors, and most of these differences are caused by genetic differences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Although <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_receptor" title="Olfactory receptor">odorant receptor</a> genes make up one of the largest gene families in the human genome, only a handful of genes have been linked conclusively to particular smells. For instance, the odorant receptor <a href="/wiki/OR5A1" title="OR5A1">OR5A1</a> and its genetic variants (alleles) are responsible for our ability (or failure) to smell β-<a href="/wiki/Ionone" title="Ionone">ionone</a>, a key aroma in foods and beverages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-=McRae2013_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-=McRae2013-30">[30]</a></sup> Similarly, the odorant receptor <a href="/wiki/OR2J3" title="OR2J3">OR2J3</a> is associated with the ability to detect the "grassy" odor, cis-3-hexen-1-ol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McRae2012_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-McRae2012-31">[31]</a></sup> The preference (or dislike) of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cilantro" title="Cilantro">cilantro</a> (coriander) has been linked to the olfactory receptor <a href="/wiki/OR6A2" title="OR6A2">OR6A2</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interactions_with_other_senses">Interactions with other senses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olfaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Interactions with other senses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Olfaction_and_flavor">Olfaction and flavor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olfaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Olfaction and flavor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Flavor perception is an aggregation of <a href="/wiki/Hearing" title="Hearing">auditory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taste" title="Taste">taste</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haptic_perception" title="Haptic perception">haptic</a>, and smell sensory information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Retronasal_smell" title="Retronasal smell">Retronasal smell</a> plays the biggest role in the sensation of flavor. During the process of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mastication" title="Mastication">mastication</a>, the tongue manipulates food to release odorants. These odorants enter the nasal cavity during exhalation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-33">[33]</a></sup> The olfaction of food has the sensation of being in the mouth because of co-activation of the motor cortex and olfactory epithelium during mastication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Olfaction, <a href="/wiki/Taste" title="Taste">taste</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Trigeminal_nerve" title="Trigeminal nerve">trigeminal</a> receptors (also called <a href="/wiki/Chemesthesis" title="Chemesthesis">chemesthesis</a>) together contribute to <a href="/wiki/Flavor" title="Flavor">flavor</a>. The human <a href="/wiki/Tongue" title="Tongue">tongue</a> can distinguish only among five distinct qualities of taste, while the nose can distinguish among hundreds of substances, even in minute quantities. It is during <a href="/wiki/Exhalation" title="Exhalation">exhalation</a> that the olfaction contribution to flavor occurs, in contrast to that of proper smell, which occurs during the <a href="/wiki/Inhalation" title="Inhalation">inhalation</a> phase of breathing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-33">[33]</a></sup> The olfactory system is the only human sense that bypasses the thalamus and connects directly to the forebrain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Olfaction_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Olfaction-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Audition">Audition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olfaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Audition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Olfaction and <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sound</a> information has been shown to converge in the olfactory tubercles of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rodents" title="Rodents">rodents</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> This neural convergence is proposed to give rise to a perception termed <a href="/wiki/Smound" title="Smound">smound</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scientific_American_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scientific_American-36">[36]</a></sup> Whereas a <a href="/wiki/Flavor" title="Flavor">flavor</a> results from interactions between smell and taste, a smound may result from interactions between smell and sound.
</p>

<p>The following are disorders of olfaction:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>

<li><a href="/wiki/Hyposmia" title="Hyposmia">Hyposmia</a> – decreased ability to smell</li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Parosmia" title="Parosmia">Parosmia</a> – things smell worse than they should<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Phantosmia" title="Phantosmia">Phantosmia</a> – "hallucinated smell", often unpleasant in nature</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quantification_in_industry">Quantification in industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olfaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Quantification in industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nasal_Ranger.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Nasal_Ranger.JPG/220px-Nasal_Ranger.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Nasal_Ranger.JPG/330px-Nasal_Ranger.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Nasal_Ranger.JPG/440px-Nasal_Ranger.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nasal_Ranger.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nasal_Ranger&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nasal Ranger (page does not exist)">Nasal Ranger</a>, an olfactometer, in use</div></div></div>
<p>Scientists have devised methods for quantifying the intensity of odors, in particular for the purpose of analyzing unpleasant or objectionable odors released by an industrial source into a community. Since the 1800s industrial countries have encountered incidents where proximity of an industrial source or landfill produced adverse reactions among nearby residents regarding airborne odor. The basic theory of odor analysis is to measure what extent of dilution with "pure" air is required before the sample in question is rendered indistinguishable from the "pure" or reference standard. Since each person perceives odor differently, an "odor panel" composed of several different people is assembled, each sniffing the same sample of diluted specimen air. A field <a href="/wiki/Olfactometer" title="Olfactometer">olfactometer</a> can be utilized to determine the magnitude of an odor.
</p><p>Many air management districts in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">US</a> have numerical standards of acceptability for the intensity of odor that is allowed to cross into a residential property. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Bay_Area_Air_Quality_Management_District" title="Bay Area Air Quality Management District">Bay Area Air Quality Management District</a> has applied its standard in regulating numerous industries, landfills, and sewage treatment plants. Example applications this district has engaged are the <a href="/wiki/San_Mateo,_California" title="San Mateo, California">San Mateo, California</a>, wastewater treatment plant; the <a href="/wiki/Shoreline_Amphitheatre" title="Shoreline Amphitheatre">Shoreline Amphitheatre</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mountain_View,_California" title="Mountain View, California">Mountain View, California</a>; and the <a href="/wiki/IT_Corporation" title="IT Corporation">IT Corporation</a> waste ponds, <a href="/wiki/Martinez,_California" title="Martinez, California">Martinez, California</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_plants_and_animals">In plants and animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olfaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: In plants and animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The tendrils of plants are especially sensitive to airborne <a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound" title="Volatile organic compound">volatile organic compounds</a>. Parasites such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dodder" title="Dodder">dodder</a> make use of this in locating their preferred hosts and locking on to them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The emission of volatile compounds is detected when foliage is browsed by animals. Threatened plants are then able to take defensive chemical measures, such as moving <a href="/wiki/Tannin" title="Tannin">tannin</a> compounds to their foliage. (See <a href="/wiki/Plant_perception_(physiology)#Senses_in_plants" title="Plant perception (physiology)">Plant perception</a>).
</p><p>The importance and sensitivity of smell varies among different organisms; most <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> have a good sense of smell, whereas most <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> do not, except the <a href="/wiki/Procellariiformes" title="Procellariiformes">tubenoses</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Petrel" title="Petrel">petrels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Albatross" title="Albatross">albatrosses</a>), certain species of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vultures" title="Vultures">vultures</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Kiwi" title="Kiwi">kiwis</a>. Among mammals, it is well developed in the <a href="/wiki/Carnivore" title="Carnivore">carnivores</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ungulate" title="Ungulate">ungulates</a>, which must always be aware of each other, and in those that smell for their food, such as <a href="/wiki/Mole_(animal)" title="Mole (animal)">moles</a>. Having a strong sense of smell is referred to as <i><b>macrosmatic</b></i>.
</p><p>Figures suggesting greater or lesser sensitivity in various species reflect experimental findings from the reactions of animals exposed to aromas in known extreme dilutions. These are, therefore, based on perceptions by these animals, rather than mere nasal function. That is, the brain's smell-recognizing centers must react to the stimulus detected for the animal to be said to show a response to the smell in question. It is estimated that dogs in general have an olfactory sense approximately ten thousand to a hundred thousand times more acute than a human's.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> This does not mean they are overwhelmed by smells our noses can detect; rather, it means they can discern a molecular presence when it is in much greater dilution in the carrier, air.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scenthound" title="Scenthound">Scenthounds</a> as a group can smell one- to ten-million times more acutely than a human, and <a href="/wiki/Bloodhound" title="Bloodhound">bloodhounds</a>, which have the keenest sense of smell of any dogs,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> have noses ten- to one-hundred-million times more sensitive than a human's. They were bred for the specific purpose of tracking humans, and can detect a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scent_trail" title="Scent trail">scent trail</a> a few days old. The second-most-sensitive nose is possessed by the <a href="/wiki/Basset_Hound" title="Basset Hound">Basset Hound</a>, which was bred to track and hunt rabbits and other small animals.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bear" title="Bear">Bears</a>, such as the Silvertip Grizzly found in parts of North America, have a sense of smell seven times stronger than that of the bloodhound, essential for locating food underground. Using their elongated claws, bears dig deep trenches in search of burrowing animals and nests as well as roots, bulbs, and insects. Bears can detect the scent of food from up to eighteen miles away; because of their immense size, they often scavenge new kills, driving away the predators (including packs of wolves and human hunters) in the process.
</p><p>The sense of smell is less developed in the <a href="/wiki/Catarrhini" title="Catarrhini">catarrhine</a> <a href="/wiki/Primate" title="Primate">primates</a>, and nonexistent in <a href="/wiki/Cetacea" title="Cetacea">cetaceans</a>, which compensate with a well-developed sense of <a href="/wiki/Taste" title="Taste">taste</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In some <a href="/wiki/Strepsirrhini" title="Strepsirrhini">strepsirrhines</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Red-bellied_lemur" title="Red-bellied lemur">red-bellied lemur</a>, scent glands occur atop the head. In many species, olfaction is highly tuned to <a href="/wiki/Pheromone" title="Pheromone">pheromones</a>; a male <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silkworm" title="Silkworm">silkworm</a> moth, for example, can sense a single molecule of <a href="/wiki/Bombykol" title="Bombykol">bombykol</a>.
</p><p>Fish, too, have a well-developed sense of smell, even though they inhabit an aquatic environment. Salmon utilize their sense of smell to identify and return to their home stream waters. Catfish use their sense of smell to identify other individual catfish and to maintain a social hierarchy. Many fishes use the sense of smell to identify mating partners or to alert to the presence of food.
</p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Insect_olfaction" title="Insect olfaction">insect olfaction</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inbreeding_avoidance">Inbreeding avoidance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olfaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Inbreeding avoidance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since <a href="/wiki/Inbreeding_depression" title="Inbreeding depression">inbreeding</a> is detrimental, it tends to be avoided. In the house mouse, the <a href="/wiki/Major_urinary_proteins" title="Major urinary proteins">major urinary protein</a> (MUP) gene cluster provides a highly polymorphic scent signal of genetic identity that appears to underlie <a href="/wiki/Kin_recognition" title="Kin recognition">kin recognition</a> and inbreeding avoidance. Thus, there are fewer matings between mice sharing MUP haplotypes than would be expected if there were random mating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17997307_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17997307-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Olfaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Lady_and_the_unicorn_Smell.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="558" data-file-width="480" height="256" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/The_Lady_and_the_unicorn_Smell.jpg/220px-The_Lady_and_the_unicorn_Smell.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/The_Lady_and_the_unicorn_Smell.jpg/330px-The_Lady_and_the_unicorn_Smell.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/The_Lady_and_the_unicorn_Smell.jpg/440px-The_Lady_and_the_unicorn_Smell.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Lady_and_the_unicorn_Smell.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/The_Lady_and_the_Unicorn" title="The Lady and the Unicorn">The Lady and the Unicorn</a></i>, a Flemish tapestry depicting the sense of smell, 1484–1500. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_national_du_Moyen_%C3%82ge" title="Musée national du Moyen Âge">Musée national du Moyen Âge</a></i>, Paris.</div></div></div>
<p>Early scientific study of olfaction includes the extensive doctoral dissertation of <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_Gamble" title="Eleanor Gamble">Eleanor Gamble</a>, published in 1898, which compared olfactory to other <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_modality" title="Stimulus modality">stimulus modalities</a>, and implied that smell had a lower intensity discrimination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> As the Epicurean and atomistic Roman philosopher <a href="/wiki/Lucretius" title="Lucretius">Lucretius</a> (1st<span class="nowrap"> </span>century BCE) speculated, different odors are attributed to different shapes and sizes of "atoms" (odor molecules in the modern understanding) that stimulate the olfactory organ <a class="external autonumber" href="https://www.academia.edu/321004/Lucretius_the_Biochemistry_of_Olfaction_and_Scientific_Discovery" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>. A modern demonstration of that theory was the cloning of olfactory receptor proteins by <a href="/wiki/Linda_B._Buck" title="Linda B. Buck">Linda B. Buck</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Axel" title="Richard Axel">Richard Axel</a> (who were awarded the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prize</a> in 2004), and subsequent pairing of odor molecules to specific receptor proteins. Each odor receptor molecule recognizes only a particular molecular feature or class of odor molecules. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mammals" title="Mammals">Mammals</a> have about a thousand <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a> that code for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olfactory_receptors" title="Olfactory receptors">odor reception</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Of the genes that code for odor receptors, only a portion are functional. Humans have far fewer active odor receptor genes than other primates and other mammals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> In mammals, each <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_receptor_neuron" title="Olfactory receptor neuron">olfactory receptor neuron</a> expresses only one functional odor receptor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Odor receptor nerve cells function like a key–lock system: if the airborne molecules of a certain chemical can fit into the lock, the nerve cell will respond. There are, at present, a number of competing theories regarding the mechanism of odor coding and perception. According to the <a href="/wiki/Shape_theory_of_olfaction" title="Shape theory of olfaction">shape theory</a>, each receptor detects a feature of the odor <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecule</a>. The weak-shape theory, known as the <a href="/wiki/Odotope_theory" title="Odotope theory">odotope theory</a>, suggests that different receptors detect only small pieces of molecules, and these minimal inputs are combined to form a larger olfactory perception (similar to the way visual perception is built up of smaller, information-poor sensations, combined and refined to create a detailed overall perception)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. According to a new study, researchers have found that a functional relationship exists between molecular volume of odorants and the olfactory neural response.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10.1101/013516_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-10.1101/013516-46">[46]</a></sup> An alternative theory, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vibration_theory" title="Vibration theory">vibration theory</a> proposed by <a href="/wiki/Luca_Turin" title="Luca Turin">Luca Turin</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> posits that odor receptors detect the frequencies of vibrations of odor molecules in the infrared range by <a href="/wiki/Quantum_tunnelling" title="Quantum tunnelling">quantum tunnelling</a>. However, the behavioral predictions of this theory have been called into question.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> There is no theory yet that explains olfactory perception completely.
</p>
