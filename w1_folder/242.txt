<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Phase diagram</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><dl><dd><i>For the use of this term in mathematics and physics, see <a href="/wiki/Phase_space" title="Phase space">phase space</a>.</i></dd></dl>
<p>A <b>phase diagram</b> in <a href="/wiki/Physical_chemistry" title="Physical chemistry">physical chemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mineralogy" title="Mineralogy">mineralogy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">materials science</a> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Chart" title="Chart">chart</a> used to show conditions (pressure, temperature, volume, etc.) at which thermodynamically distinct <a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">phases</a> occur and coexist at <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium" title="Thermodynamic equilibrium">equilibrium</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phase_diagram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Common components of a phase diagram are <i>lines of equilibrium</i> or <i>phase boundaries</i>, which refer to lines that mark conditions under which multiple phases can coexist at equilibrium.  Phase transitions occur along lines of equilibrium.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Triple_point" title="Triple point">Triple points</a> are points on phase diagrams where lines of equilibrium intersect.  Triple points mark conditions at which three different phases can coexist.  For example, the water phase diagram has a triple point corresponding to the single temperature and pressure at which solid, liquid, and gaseous water can coexist in a stable equilibrium (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002273160000000000♠</span>273.16 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a></span> and a partial vapor pressure of <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002611657000000000♠</span>611.657 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">Pa</a></span>).
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Solidus_(chemistry)" title="Solidus (chemistry)">solidus</a> is the temperature below which the substance is stable in the solid state. The <a href="/wiki/Liquidus" title="Liquidus">liquidus</a> is the temperature above which the substance is stable in a liquid state. There may be a gap between the solidus and liquidus; within the gap, the substance consists of a mixture of crystals and liquid (like a "<a href="/wiki/Slurry" title="Slurry">slurry</a>").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Working_fluids" title="Working fluids">Working fluids</a> are often categorized by on the basis of the shape of their phase diagram.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phase_diagram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2-dimensional_diagrams">2-dimensional diagrams</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phase_diagram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2-dimensional diagrams">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Phase-diag2.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="443" data-file-width="530" height="259" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Phase-diag2.svg/310px-Phase-diag2.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Phase-diag2.svg/465px-Phase-diag2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Phase-diag2.svg/620px-Phase-diag2.svg.png 2x" width="310"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Phase-diag2.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A typical phase diagram. The solid green line applies to most substances; the dotted green line gives <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)#Density_of_water_and_ice" title="Water (molecule)">the anomalous behavior of water</a>.  The green lines mark the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freezing_point" title="Freezing point">freezing point</a> and the blue line the <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">boiling point</a>, showing how they vary with pressure.</div></div></div><p>The simplest phase diagrams are pressure–temperature diagrams of a single simple substance, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" title="Water (molecule)">water</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system" title="Cartesian coordinate system">axes</a> correspond to the <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> and <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>. The phase diagram shows, in pressure–temperature space, the lines of equilibrium or phase boundaries between the three phases of <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a>.
</p><p>The curves on the phase diagram show the points where the free energy (and other derived properties) becomes non-analytic: their derivatives with respect to the coordinates (temperature and pressure in this example) change discontinuously (abruptly). For example, the heat capacity of a container filled with ice will change abruptly as the container is heated past the melting point.  The open spaces, where the <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_free_energy" title="Thermodynamic free energy">free energy</a> is <a href="/wiki/Analytic_function" title="Analytic function">analytic</a>, correspond to single phase regions. Single phase regions are separated by lines of non-analytical behavior, where <a href="/wiki/Phase_transition" title="Phase transition">phase transitions</a> occur, which are called <b>phase boundaries</b>.
</p><p>In the diagram on the right, the phase boundary between liquid and gas does not continue indefinitely. Instead, it terminates at a point on the phase diagram called the <a href="/wiki/Critical_point_(thermodynamics)" title="Critical point (thermodynamics)">critical point</a>. This reflects the fact that, at extremely high temperatures and pressures, the liquid and gaseous phases become indistinguishable,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> in what is known as a <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_fluid" title="Supercritical fluid">supercritical fluid</a>. In water, the critical point occurs at around <i>T</i><sub>c</sub> = 647.096 K (373.946 °C), <i>p</i><sub>c</sub> = 22.064 MPa (217.75 atm) and <i>ρ</i><sub>c</sub> = 356 kg/m³.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The existence of the liquid–gas critical point reveals a slight ambiguity in labelling the single phase regions. When going from the liquid to the gaseous phase, one usually crosses the phase boundary, but it is possible to choose a path that never crosses the boundary by going to the right of the critical point. Thus, the liquid and gaseous phases can blend continuously into each other. The solid–liquid phase boundary can only end in a critical point if the solid and liquid phases have the same <a href="/wiki/Symmetry_group" title="Symmetry group">symmetry group</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>For most substances, the solid–liquid phase boundary (or fusion curve) in the phase diagram has a positive <a href="/wiki/Slope" title="Slope">slope</a> so that the melting point increases with pressure. This is true whenever the solid phase is <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">denser</a> than the liquid phase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whitten_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whitten-4">[4]</a></sup> The greater the pressure on a given substance, the closer together the molecules of the substance are brought to each other, which increases the effect of the substance's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intermolecular_forces" title="Intermolecular forces">intermolecular forces</a>. Thus, the substance requires a higher temperature for its molecules to have enough energy to break out of the fixed pattern of the solid phase and enter the liquid phase. A similar concept applies to liquid–gas phase changes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dorin_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dorin-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Water is an exception which has a solid-liquid boundary with negative slope so that the melting point decreases with pressure. This occurs because ice (solid water) is less dense than liquid water, as shown by the fact that ice floats on water. At a molecular level, ice is less dense because it has a more extensive network of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bond" title="Hydrogen bond">hydrogen bonding</a> which requires a greater separation of water molecules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whitten_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Whitten-4">[4]</a></sup> Other exceptions are <a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">antimony</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bismuth" title="Bismuth">bismuth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The value of the slope dP/dT is given by the <a href="/wiki/Clausius%E2%80%93Clapeyron_relation" title="Clausius–Clapeyron relation">Clapeyron equation</a> for fusion (melting)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_thermodynamic_properties">Other thermodynamic properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phase_diagram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other thermodynamic properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In addition to temperature and pressure, other thermodynamic properties may be graphed in phase diagrams.  Examples of such thermodynamic properties include <a href="/wiki/Specific_volume" title="Specific volume">specific volume</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Specific_enthalpy" title="Specific enthalpy">specific enthalpy</a>, or specific <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a>.  For example, single-component graphs of temperature vs. specific entropy (<i>T</i> vs. <i>s</i>) for water/<a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a> or for a <a href="/wiki/Refrigerant" title="Refrigerant">refrigerant</a> are commonly used to illustrate <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_cycle" title="Thermodynamic cycle">thermodynamic cycles</a> such as a <a href="/wiki/Carnot_cycle" title="Carnot cycle">Carnot cycle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rankine_cycle" title="Rankine cycle">Rankine cycle</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Vapor-compression_refrigeration" title="Vapor-compression refrigeration">vapor-compression refrigeration</a> cycle.
</p><p>In a <a href="/wiki/Two-dimensional_graph" title="Two-dimensional graph">two-dimensional graph</a>, two of the thermodynamic quantities may be shown on the horizontal and vertical axes. Additional thermodynamic quantities may each be illustrated in increments as a series of lines - curved, straight, or a combination of curved and straight. Each of these <b>iso-</b>lines represents the thermodynamic quantity at a certain constant value.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="3-dimensional_diagrams">3-dimensional diagrams</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phase_diagram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 3-dimensional diagrams">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PVT_3D_diagram.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="972" data-file-width="866" height="224" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/PVT_3D_diagram.png/200px-PVT_3D_diagram.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/PVT_3D_diagram.png/300px-PVT_3D_diagram.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/PVT_3D_diagram.png/400px-PVT_3D_diagram.png 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PVT_3D_diagram.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>p</i>–<i>v</i>–<i>T</i> 3D diagram for fixed amount of pure material</div></div></div>
<p>It is possible to envision three-dimensional (3D) graphs showing three thermodynamic quantities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> For example, for a single component, a 3D Cartesian coordinate type graph can show temperature (<i>T</i>) on one axis, pressure (<i>p</i>) on a second axis, and specific volume (<i>v</i>) on a third.  Such a 3D graph is sometimes called a <i>p</i>–<i>v</i>–<i>T</i> diagram. The equilibrium conditions are shown as curves on a curved surface in 3D with areas for solid, liquid, and vapor phases and areas where solid and liquid, solid and vapor, or liquid and vapor coexist in equilibrium. A line on the surface called a <b>triple line</b> is where solid, liquid and vapor can all coexist in equilibrium. The critical point remains a point on the surface even on a 3D phase diagram.
</p><p>For water, the 3D <i>p</i>–<i>v</i>–<i>T</i> diagram is seen here:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>An <a href="/wiki/Orthographic_projection" title="Orthographic projection">orthographic projection</a> of the 3D <i>p</i>–<i>v</i>–<i>T</i> graph showing pressure and temperature as the vertical and horizontal axes collapses the 3D plot into the standard 2D pressure–temperature diagram. When this is done, the solid–vapor, solid–liquid, and liquid–vapor surfaces collapse into three corresponding curved lines meeting at the triple point, which is the collapsed orthographic projection of the triple line.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Binary_mixtures">Binary mixtures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phase_diagram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Binary mixtures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Other much more complex types of phase diagrams can be constructed, particularly when more than one pure component is present. In that case, <a href="/wiki/Concentration" title="Concentration">concentration</a> becomes an important variable.  Phase diagrams with more than two dimensions can be constructed that show the effect of more than two variables on the phase of a substance. Phase diagrams can use other variables in addition to or in place of temperature, pressure and composition, for example the strength of an applied electrical or magnetic field, and they can also involve substances that take on more than just three states of matter.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brosen_ironcarbon.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2063" data-file-width="1938" height="266" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Brosen_ironcarbon.svg/250px-Brosen_ironcarbon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Brosen_ironcarbon.svg/375px-Brosen_ironcarbon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Brosen_ironcarbon.svg/500px-Brosen_ironcarbon.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brosen_ironcarbon.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The iron–iron carbide (Fe–Fe<sub>3</sub>C) phase diagram. The percentage of carbon present and the temperature define the phase of the iron carbon alloy and therefore its physical characteristics and mechanical properties. The percentage of carbon determines the type of the ferrous alloy: iron, steel or cast iron</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eutektikum_new.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="230" data-file-width="369" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Eutektikum_new.svg/250px-Eutektikum_new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Eutektikum_new.svg/375px-Eutektikum_new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Eutektikum_new.svg/500px-Eutektikum_new.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eutektikum_new.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A phase diagram for a binary system displaying a eutectic point.</div></div></div>
<p>One type of phase diagram plots temperature against the relative concentrations of two substances in a <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/binary" title="wiktionary:binary">binary</a> <a href="/wiki/Mixture" title="Mixture">mixture</a> called a <i>binary phase diagram</i>, as shown at right. Such a mixture can be either a <a href="/wiki/Solid_solution" title="Solid solution">solid solution</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eutectic_point" title="Eutectic point">eutectic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peritectic" title="Peritectic">peritectic</a>, among others. These two types of mixtures result in very different graphs. Another type of binary phase diagram is a <i>boiling-point diagram</i> for a mixture of two components, i. e. <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compounds</a>. For two particular <a href="/wiki/Volatility_(chemistry)" title="Volatility (chemistry)">volatile</a> components at a certain pressure such as <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a>, a boiling-point diagram shows what <a href="/wiki/Vapor" title="Vapor">vapor</a> (gas) compositions are in <a href="/wiki/Vapor%E2%80%93liquid_equilibrium" title="Vapor–liquid equilibrium">equilibrium</a> with given liquid compositions depending on temperature.  In a typical binary boiling-point diagram, temperature is plotted on a vertical axis and mixture composition on a horizontal axis.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Binary_Boiling_Point_Diagram_new.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="378" data-file-width="391" height="242" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Binary_Boiling_Point_Diagram_new.svg/250px-Binary_Boiling_Point_Diagram_new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Binary_Boiling_Point_Diagram_new.svg/375px-Binary_Boiling_Point_Diagram_new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Binary_Boiling_Point_Diagram_new.svg/500px-Binary_Boiling_Point_Diagram_new.svg.png 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Binary_Boiling_Point_Diagram_new.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Boiling-point diagram</div></div></div>
<p>A simple example diagram with hypothetical components 1 and 2 in a non-<a href="/wiki/Azeotrope" title="Azeotrope">azeotropic</a> mixture is shown at right.  The fact that there are two separate curved lines joining the boiling points of the pure components means that the vapor composition is usually not the same as the liquid composition the vapor is in equilibrium with. See <a href="/wiki/Vapor%E2%80%93liquid_equilibrium#Boiling-point_diagrams" title="Vapor–liquid equilibrium">Vapor–liquid equilibrium</a> for more information.
</p><p>In addition to the above-mentioned types of phase diagrams, there are thousands of other possible combinations. Some of the major features of phase diagrams include congruent points, where a solid phase transforms directly into a liquid. There is also the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peritectoid" title="Peritectoid">peritectoid</a>, a point where two solid phases combine into one solid phase during cooling. The inverse of this, when one solid phase transforms into two solid phases during cooling, is called the eutectoid.
</p><p>A complex phase diagram of great technological importance is that of the <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>–<a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> system for less than 7% carbon (see <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>).
</p><p>The x-axis of such a diagram represents the concentration variable of the mixture. As the mixtures are typically far from dilute and their density as a function of temperature is usually unknown, the preferred concentration measure is <a href="/wiki/Mole_fraction" title="Mole fraction">mole fraction</a>. A volume-based measure like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molarity" title="Molarity">molarity</a> would be inadvisable.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crystals">Crystals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phase_diagram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Crystals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">Polymorphic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polyamorphism" title="Polyamorphism">polyamorphic</a> substances have multiple <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystal</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amorphous" title="Amorphous">amorphous</a> phases, which can be graphed in a similar fashion to solid, liquid, and gas phases.
</p>

The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_numeral" title="Roman numeral">Roman numerals</a> indicate various <a href="/wiki/Ice#Phases" title="Ice">ice phases</a>. A similar diagram may be found on the site Water structure and science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></div></div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mesophases">Mesophases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Phase_diagram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mesophases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some organic materials pass through intermediate states between solid and liquid; these states are called <a href="/wiki/Mesophase" title="Mesophase">mesophases</a>. Attention has been directed to mesophases because they enable <a href="/wiki/Display_device" title="Display device">display devices</a> and have become commercially important through the so-called <a href="/wiki/Liquid_crystal" title="Liquid crystal">liquid-crystal</a> technology. Phase diagrams are used to describe the occurrence of mesophases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
