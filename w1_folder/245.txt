<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Bar (unit)</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>bar</b> is a metric <a href="/wiki/Unit_of_measurement" title="Unit of measurement">unit</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a>, but is not approved as part of the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a> (SI). It is defined as  exactly equal to 100,000 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">Pa</a>, which is slightly less than the current average <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> on Earth at <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIPMSI_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-BIPMSI-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BSI_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-BSI-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The bar and the millibar were introduced by the Norwegian meteorologist <a href="/wiki/Vilhelm_Bjerknes" title="Vilhelm Bjerknes">Vilhelm Bjerknes</a>, who was a founder of the modern practice of <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">weather forecasting</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Bureau_of_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Bureau of Weights and Measures">International Bureau of Weights and Measures</a> (BIPM) <a href="/wiki/Non-SI_units_mentioned_in_the_SI" title="Non-SI units mentioned in the SI">lists</a> the bar as one of the "non-SI units [that authors] should have the freedom to use", but has declined to include it among the "Non-SI units accepted for use with the SI".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIPMSI_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-BIPMSI-1">[1]</a></sup> The bar has been legally recognised in countries of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> since 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BSI_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-BSI-2">[2]</a></sup> The US <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">National Institute of Standards and Technology</a> (NIST) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deprecated" title="Deprecated">deprecates</a> its use except for "limited use in <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>" and lists it as one of several units that "must not be introduced in fields where they are not presently used".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">International Astronomical Union</a> (IAU) also lists it under "Non-SI units and symbols whose continued use is deprecated".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Units derived from the bar include the <b>megabar</b> (symbol: <b>Mbar</b>), <b>kilobar</b> (symbol: <b>kbar</b>), <b>decibar</b> (symbol: <b>dbar</b>), <b>centibar</b> (symbol: <b>cbar</b>), and <b>millibar</b> (symbol: <b>mbar</b> or <b>mb</b>). The notation <b>bar(g)</b>, though deprecated by various bodies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BSI_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-BSI-2">[2]</a></sup> represents <a href="/wiki/Pressure_measurement#Absolute,_gauge_and_differential" title="Pressure measurement">gauge pressure</a>, i.e., pressure in bars above ambient or atmospheric pressure.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition_and_conversion">Definition and conversion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bar_(unit)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition and conversion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The bar is defined using the <a href="/wiki/SI_derived_unit" title="SI derived unit">SI derived unit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">pascal</a>: <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7005100000000000000♠</span>1 bar</span> ≡ 100,000 Pa ≡ 100,000 N/m<sup>2</sup>.
</p><p>Thus, <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7005100000000000000♠</span>1 bar</span> is equal to:
</p>
<ul><li>1,000,000 Ba (<a href="/wiki/Barye" title="Barye">barye</a>) (in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cgs_units" title="Cgs units">cgs units</a>);</li></ul>
<p>and 1 bar is approximately equal to:
</p>
<ul><li><span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6999987000000000000♠</span>0.987 <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">atm</a></span></li>
<li><span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7005100000180828655♠</span>14.5038 <a href="/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch" title="Pounds per square inch">psi</a></span> absolute</li>
<li><span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7001295300000000000♠</span>29.53 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inches_of_Mercury" title="Inches of Mercury">inHg</a></span></li>
<li><span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002750060000000000♠</span>750.06 <a href="/wiki/Millimeter_of_mercury" title="Millimeter of mercury">mmHg</a></span></li>
<li><span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002750060000000000♠</span>750.06 <a href="/wiki/Torr" title="Torr">Torr</a></span></li>
<li>1019.72 centimetres of water (cmH<sub>2</sub>O).</li></ul>
<p>Notes: 
</p>
<ul><li>1 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)#Usage" title="Bar (unit)">millibar</a> (<b>mbar</b>) = 1 one-thousandth bar, or <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6997100000000000000♠</span>1<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>−3</sup> <a href="/wiki/Bar" title="Bar">bar</a></span></li>
<li>1 millibar = 1 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hectopascal</a> (1 hPa = 100 Pa).</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bar_(unit)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <i>bar</i> has its origin in the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> word <span lang="el" title="Greek language text">βάρος</span> (<i lang="el-Latn" title="Greek language text">baros</i>), meaning <a href="/wiki/Weight" title="Weight">weight</a>. The unit's official symbol is <b>bar</b>; the earlier symbol <b>b</b> is now deprecated and conflicts with the use of <b>b</b> denoting the unit <a href="/wiki/Barn_(unit)" title="Barn (unit)">barn</a>, but it is still encountered, especially as <b>mb</b> (rather than the proper <b>mbar</b>) to denote the millibar. Between 1793 and 1795, the word bar was used  for a unit of weight in an early version of the metric system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bar_(unit)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">Atmospheric air pressure</a> is often given in millibars, where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_atmospheric_pressure" title="Standard atmospheric pressure">standard atmospheric pressure</a> at <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a> is defined as 1013.25 mbar, 101.325 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">kPa</a>, 1.01325 bar, which is about 14.7 <a href="/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch" title="Pounds per square inch">pounds per square inch</a>.  Despite the millibar not being an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI" title="SI">SI</a> unit, meteorologists and weather reporters worldwide have long measured air pressure in millibars as the values are convenient. After the advent of SI units, some meteorologists began using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hectopascals" title="Hectopascals">hectopascals</a> (symbol hPa) which are numerically equivalent to millibars; for the same reason, the hectopascal is now the standard unit used to express barometric pressures in aviation in most countries. For example, the weather office of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environment_Canada" title="Environment Canada">Environment Canada</a> uses kilopascals and hectopascals on their weather maps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In contrast, Americans are familiar with the use of the millibar in US reports of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">hurricanes</a> and other cyclonic storms.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In fresh water, there is an approximate numerical equivalence between the change in pressure in decibars and the change in depth from the water surface in <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metres</a>. Specifically, an increase of 1 decibar occurs for every 1.019716 m increase in depth. In sea water with respect to the gravity variation, the latitude and the geopotential anomaly the pressure can be converted into metres' depth according to an empirical formula (UNESCO Tech. Paper 44, p. 25).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>  As a result, decibars are commonly used in <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">oceanography</a>.
</p><p>Many engineers worldwide use the bar as a unit of pressure because, in much of their work, using pascals would involve using very large numbers. In measurement of <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_engineering" title="Vacuum engineering">vacuum engineering</a>, residual pressures are typically given in millibar, although <a href="/wiki/Torr" title="Torr">torr</a> or <a href="/wiki/Millimeter_of_mercury" title="Millimeter of mercury">millimeter of mercury</a> (mmHg) were historically common.
</p><p>Engineers that specialize in technical safety for offshore petrochemical facilities would be expected to exclusively refer to explosion loads in units of bar or bars. A bar is a convenient unit of measure for pressures generated by low frequency vapor cloud explosions that are commonly considered as part of accidental loading risk studies.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ReifendruckPruefen.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="854" data-file-width="1139" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/ReifendruckPruefen.jpg/220px-ReifendruckPruefen.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/ReifendruckPruefen.jpg/330px-ReifendruckPruefen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/ReifendruckPruefen.jpg/440px-ReifendruckPruefen.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ReifendruckPruefen.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A tire pressure gauge displaying bar (outside) and <a href="/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch" title="Pounds per square inch">pounds per square inch</a> (inside)</div></div></div>
<p>In the automotive field, <a href="/wiki/Turbocharger" title="Turbocharger">turbocharger</a> boost is often described in bars outside the USA. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tire_pressure" title="Tire pressure">Tire pressure</a> is often specified in bar.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> has characters for "mb" (㏔, U+33D4) and "bar" (㍴, U+3374), but they exist only for compatibility with legacy Asian encodings and are not intended to be used in new documents.
</p><p>The kilobar, equivalent to 100 MPa, is commonly used in geological systems, particularly in experimental <a href="/wiki/Petrology" title="Petrology">petrology</a>.
</p><p>"Bar(a)" and "bara" are sometimes used to indicate <a href="/wiki/Pressure_measurement#Absolute,_gauge_and_differential" title="Pressure measurement">absolute pressures</a> and "bar(g)" and "barg" for <a href="/wiki/Pressure_measurement#Absolute,_gauge_and_differential" title="Pressure measurement">gauge pressures</a>. This usage is deprecated and fuller descriptions such as "gauge pressure of 2 bar" or "2-bar gauge" are recommended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BSI_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-BSI-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
