<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Nucleic acid</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Friedrich_Miescher.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1078" data-file-width="788" height="301" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Friedrich_Miescher.jpg/220px-Friedrich_Miescher.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Friedrich_Miescher.jpg/330px-Friedrich_Miescher.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Friedrich_Miescher.jpg/440px-Friedrich_Miescher.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Friedrich_Miescher.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Swiss</a> <a href="/wiki/Scientist" title="Scientist">scientist</a> <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Miescher" title="Friedrich Miescher">Friedrich Miescher</a> discovered nucleic acids (<a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>) in 1869.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[notes 1]</a></sup> Later, he raised the idea that they could be involved in <a href="/wiki/Heredity" title="Heredity">heredity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[1]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p><b>Nucleic acids</b> are the <a href="/wiki/Biopolymer" title="Biopolymer">biopolymers</a>, or small <a href="/wiki/Biomolecule" title="Biomolecule">biomolecules</a>, essential to all known forms of <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a>. The term <i>nucleic acid</i> is the overall name for DNA and RNA. They are composed of <a href="/wiki/Nucleotide" title="Nucleotide">nucleotides</a>, which are the <a href="/wiki/Monomer" title="Monomer">monomers</a> made of three components: a <a href="/wiki/Pentose" title="Pentose">5-carbon sugar</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a> group and a <a href="/wiki/Nitrogenous_base" title="Nitrogenous base">nitrogenous base</a>. If the <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a> is a compound  <a href="/wiki/Ribose" title="Ribose">ribose</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a> is <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a> (ribonucleic acid); if the sugar is derived from ribose as <a href="/wiki/Deoxyribose" title="Deoxyribose">deoxyribose</a>, the polymer is <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> (deoxyribonucleic acid).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">Nucleic</a> acids are the most important of all biomolecules. They are found in abundance in all living things, where they function to create and encode and then store information in the nucleus of every living cell of every life-form <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organism</a> on Earth. In turn, they function to transmit and express that information inside and outside the cell nucleus—to the interior operations of the cell and ultimately to the next generation of each living organism. The encoded information is contained and conveyed via the <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid_sequence" title="Nucleic acid sequence">nucleic acid sequence</a>, which provides the 'ladder-step' ordering of nucleotides within the molecules of RNA and DNA.
</p><p>Strings of nucleotides are bonded to form helical backbones—typically, one for RNA, two for DNA—and assembled into chains of base-pairs selected from the five <a href="/wiki/Nucleobase" title="Nucleobase">primary, or canonical, nucleobases</a>, which are: adenine, cytosine, guanine, thymine, and uracil; note, thymine occurs only in DNA and uracil only in RNA. Using amino acids and the process known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protein_synthesis" title="Protein synthesis">protein synthesis</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[2]</a></sup> the specific sequencing in DNA of these <a href="/wiki/Base_pair" title="Base pair">nucleobase-pairs</a> enables storing and transmitting <a href="/wiki/Code#Genetic_code" title="Code">coded</a> instructions as <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a>. In RNA, base-pair sequencing provides for manufacturing new proteins that determine the frames and parts and most chemical processes of all life forms.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucleic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>Nuclein were discovered by <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Miescher" title="Friedrich Miescher">Friedrich Miescher</a> in 1869.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>In <b>1889</b> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Altmann" title="Richard Altmann">Richard Altmann</a> discovered that nuclein has acidic properties, and it became called nucleic acid</li>
<li>In <b>1938</b> <a href="/wiki/William_Astbury" title="William Astbury">Astbury</a> and Bell published the first X-ray diffraction pattern of DNA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>In <b>1953</b> <a href="/wiki/James_Watson" title="James Watson">Watson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francis_Crick" title="Francis Crick">Crick</a> determined the <a href="/wiki/Molecular_Structure_of_Nucleic_Acids:_A_Structure_for_Deoxyribose_Nucleic_Acid" title="Molecular Structure of Nucleic Acids: A Structure for Deoxyribose Nucleic Acid">structure of DNA</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Experimental studies of nucleic acids constitute a major part of modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_research" title="Biological research">biological</a> and <a href="/wiki/Medical_research" title="Medical research">medical research</a>, and form a foundation for <a href="/wiki/Genomics" title="Genomics">genome</a> and <a href="/wiki/Forensic_science" title="Forensic science">forensic science</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">biotechnology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_industry" title="Pharmaceutical industry">pharmaceutical industries</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IHGSC_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-IHGSC-7">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Venter_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Venter-8">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Budowle_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Budowle-9">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence_and_nomenclature">Occurrence and nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucleic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Occurrence and nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>nucleic acid</i> is the overall name for DNA and RNA, members of a family of <a href="/wiki/Biopolymer" title="Biopolymer">biopolymers</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> and is synonymous with <i><a href="/wiki/Polynucleotide" title="Polynucleotide">polynucleotide</a></i>. Nucleic acids were named for their initial discovery within the <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">nucleus</a>, and for the presence of phosphate groups (related to phosphoric acid).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> Although first discovered within the <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">nucleus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryotic</a> cells, nucleic acids are now known to be found in all life forms including within <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrion" title="Mitochondrion">mitochondria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplasts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">viruses</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Viroid" title="Viroid">viroids</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup> (note: there is debate as to <a href="/wiki/Life#Viruses" title="Life">whether viruses are living or non-living</a>). All living cells contain both DNA and RNA (except some cells such as mature red blood cells), while viruses contain either DNA or RNA, but usually not both.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brock,_Thomas_D.;_Madigan,_Michael_T._2009_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brock,_Thomas_D.;_Madigan,_Michael_T._2009-13">[12]</a></sup>
The basic component of biological nucleic acids is the <a href="/wiki/Nucleotide" title="Nucleotide">nucleotide</a>, each of which contains a pentose sugar (<a href="/wiki/Ribose" title="Ribose">ribose</a> or <a href="/wiki/Deoxyribose" title="Deoxyribose">deoxyribose</a>), a <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a> group, and a <a href="/wiki/Nucleobase" title="Nucleobase">nucleobase</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup>
Nucleic acids are also generated within the laboratory, through the use of <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> (DNA and RNA polymerases) and by <a href="/wiki/Solid-phase_synthesis" title="Solid-phase synthesis">solid-phase chemical synthesis</a>. The chemical methods also enable the generation of altered nucleic acids that are not found in nature,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> for example <a href="/wiki/Peptide_nucleic_acid" title="Peptide nucleic acid">peptide nucleic acids</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Molecular_composition_and_size">Molecular composition and size</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucleic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Molecular composition and size">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nucleic acids are generally very large molecules. Indeed, DNA molecules are probably the largest individual molecules known. Well-studied biological nucleic acid molecules range in size from 21 nucleotides (<a href="/wiki/Small_interfering_RNA" title="Small interfering RNA">small interfering RNA</a>) to large chromosomes (<a href="/wiki/Chromosome_1" title="Chromosome 1">human chromosome 1</a> is a single molecule that contains 247 million <a href="/wiki/Base_pair" title="Base pair">base pairs</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup>).
</p><p>In most cases, naturally occurring DNA molecules are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double_helix" title="Double helix">double-stranded</a> and RNA molecules are single-stranded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> There are numerous exceptions, however—some viruses have genomes made of <a href="/wiki/Reoviridae" title="Reoviridae">double-stranded RNA</a> and other viruses have <a href="/wiki/M13_bacteriophage" title="M13 bacteriophage">single-stranded DNA</a> genomes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> and, in some circumstances, nucleic acid structures with <a href="/wiki/Triple-stranded_DNA" title="Triple-stranded DNA">three</a> or <a href="/wiki/G-quadruplex" title="G-quadruplex">four</a> strands can form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Nucleic acids are linear <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymers</a> (chains) of nucleotides. Each nucleotide consists of three components: a <a href="/wiki/Purine" title="Purine">purine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pyrimidine" title="Pyrimidine">pyrimidine</a> <a href="/wiki/Nucleobase" title="Nucleobase">nucleobase</a> (sometimes termed <i>nitrogenous base</i> or simply <i>base</i>), a <a href="/wiki/Pentose" title="Pentose">pentose</a> <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a> group. The substructure consisting of a nucleobase plus sugar is termed a <a href="/wiki/Nucleoside" title="Nucleoside">nucleoside</a>. Nucleic acid types differ in the structure of the sugar in their nucleotides–DNA contains 2'-<a href="/wiki/Deoxyribose" title="Deoxyribose">deoxyribose</a> while RNA contains <a href="/wiki/Ribose" title="Ribose">ribose</a> (where the only difference is the presence of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl_group" title="Hydroxyl group">hydroxyl group</a>). Also, the nucleobases found in the two nucleic acid types are different: <a href="/wiki/Adenine" title="Adenine">adenine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cytosine" title="Cytosine">cytosine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Guanine" title="Guanine">guanine</a> are found in both RNA and DNA, while <a href="/wiki/Thymine" title="Thymine">thymine</a> occurs in DNA and <a href="/wiki/Uracil" title="Uracil">uracil</a> occurs in RNA.
</p><p>The sugars and phosphates in nucleic acids are connected to each other in an alternating chain (sugar-phosphate backbone) through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphodiester" title="Phosphodiester">phosphodiester</a> linkages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stryer_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stryer-21">[20]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid_nomenclature" title="Nucleic acid nomenclature">conventional nomenclature</a>, the carbons to which the phosphate groups attach are the 3'-end and the 5'-end carbons of the sugar. This gives nucleic acids <a href="/wiki/Directionality_(molecular_biology)" title="Directionality (molecular biology)">directionality</a>, and the ends of nucleic acid molecules are referred to as 5'-end and 3'-end. The nucleobases are joined to the sugars via an N-glycosidic linkage involving a nucleobase ring nitrogen (N-1 for pyrimidines and N-9 for purines) and the 1' carbon of the pentose sugar ring.
</p><p>Non-standard nucleosides are also found in both RNA and DNA and usually arise from modification of the standard nucleosides within the DNA molecule or the primary (initial) RNA transcript. <a href="/wiki/Transfer_RNA" title="Transfer RNA">Transfer RNA</a> (tRNA) molecules contain a particularly large number of modified nucleosides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Topology">Topology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucleic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Topology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Double-stranded nucleic acids are made up of complementary sequences, in which extensive <a href="/wiki/Base_pair" title="Base pair">Watson-Crick base pairing</a> results in a highly repeated and quite uniform <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid_double_helix" title="Nucleic acid double helix">double-helical three-dimensional structure</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup> In contrast, single-stranded RNA and DNA molecules are not constrained to a regular double helix, and can adopt <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid_tertiary_structure" title="Nucleic acid tertiary structure">highly complex three-dimensional structures</a> that are based on short stretches of intramolecular base-paired sequences including both Watson-Crick and noncanonical base pairs, and a wide range of complex tertiary interactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Nucleic acid molecules are usually unbranched, and may occur as linear and circular molecules. For example, bacterial chromosomes, <a href="/wiki/Plasmid" title="Plasmid">plasmids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mitochondrial_DNA" title="Mitochondrial DNA">mitochondrial DNA</a>, and chloroplast DNA are usually circular double-stranded DNA molecules, while chromosomes of the eukaryotic nucleus are usually linear double-stranded DNA molecules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brock,_Thomas_D.;_Madigan,_Michael_T._2009_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brock,_Thomas_D.;_Madigan,_Michael_T._2009-13">[12]</a></sup> Most RNA molecules are linear, single-stranded molecules, but both circular and branched molecules can result from <a href="/wiki/RNA_splicing" title="RNA splicing">RNA splicing</a> reactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alberts_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alberts-25">[24]</a></sup> The total amount of pyrimidine is equal to the total amount of purines. The diameter of the helix is  about 20A.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sequences">Sequences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucleic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sequences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid_sequence" title="Nucleic acid sequence">Nucleic acid sequence</a></div>
<p>One DNA or RNA molecule differs from another primarily in the <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid_sequence" title="Nucleic acid sequence">sequence of nucleotides</a>. Nucleotide sequences are of great importance in biology since they carry the ultimate instructions that encode all biological molecules, molecular assemblies, subcellular and cellular structures, organs, and organisms, and directly enable cognition, memory, and behavior (<i>see <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">Genetics</a></i>). Enormous efforts have gone into the development of experimental methods to determine the nucleotide sequence of biological DNA and RNA molecules,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup> and today hundreds of millions of nucleotides are <a href="/wiki/DNA_sequencing" title="DNA sequencing">sequenced</a> daily at genome centers and smaller laboratories worldwide. In addition to maintaining the GenBank nucleic acid sequence database, the <a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Biotechnology_Information" title="National Center for Biotechnology Information">National Center for Biotechnology Information</a> (NCBI, <a class="external free" href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov" rel="nofollow">https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov</a>) provides analysis and retrieval resources for the data in GenBank and other biological data made available through the NCBI web site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucleic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deoxyribonucleic_acid">Deoxyribonucleic acid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucleic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Deoxyribonucleic acid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a></div>
<p>Deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) is a nucleic acid containing the genetic instructions used in the development and functioning of all known living organisms. The DNA segments carrying this genetic information are called genes. Likewise, other DNA sequences have structural purposes, or are involved in regulating the use of this genetic information. Along with RNA and proteins, DNA is one of the three major macromolecules that are essential for all known forms of life.
DNA consists of two long polymers of simple units called nucleotides, with backbones made of sugars and phosphate groups joined by ester bonds. These two strands run in opposite directions to each other and are, therefore, anti-parallel. Attached to each sugar is one of four types of molecules called nucleobases (informally, bases). It is the sequence of these four nucleobases along the backbone that encodes information. This information is read using the genetic code, which specifies the sequence of the amino acids within proteins. The code is read by copying stretches of DNA into the related nucleic acid RNA in a process called transcription.
Within cells DNA is organized into long structures called chromosomes. During cell division these chromosomes are duplicated in the process of DNA replication, providing each cell its own complete set of chromosomes. Eukaryotic organisms (animals, plants, fungi, and protists) store most of their DNA inside the cell nucleus and some of their DNA in organelles, such as mitochondria or chloroplasts. In contrast, prokaryotes (bacteria and archaea) store their DNA only in the cytoplasm. Within the chromosomes, chromatin proteins such as histones compact and organize DNA. These compact structures guide the interactions between DNA and other proteins, helping control which parts of the DNA are transcribed.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ribonucleic_acid">Ribonucleic acid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucleic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ribonucleic acid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a></div>
<p>Ribonucleic acid (RNA) functions in converting genetic information from genes into the amino acid sequences of proteins. The three universal types of RNA include transfer RNA (tRNA), messenger RNA (mRNA), and ribosomal RNA (rRNA). <a href="/wiki/Messenger_RNA" title="Messenger RNA">Messenger RNA</a> acts to carry genetic sequence information between DNA and ribosomes, directing protein synthesis. <a href="/wiki/Ribosomal_RNA" title="Ribosomal RNA">Ribosomal RNA</a> is a major component of the ribosome, and catalyzes peptide bond formation. <a href="/wiki/Transfer_RNA" title="Transfer RNA">Transfer RNA</a> serves as the carrier molecule for amino acids to be used in protein synthesis, and is responsible for decoding the mRNA. In addition, many other <a href="/wiki/Non-coding_RNA" title="Non-coding RNA">classes of RNA</a> are now known.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Artificial_nucleic_acid">Artificial nucleic acid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nucleic_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Artificial nucleic acid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid_analogue" title="Nucleic acid analogue">Nucleic acid analogue</a></div>
<p>Artificial <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid_analogue" title="Nucleic acid analogue">nucleic acid analogues</a> have been designed and synthesized by chemists, and include <a href="/wiki/Peptide_nucleic_acid" title="Peptide nucleic acid">peptide nucleic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morpholino" title="Morpholino">morpholino</a>- and <a href="/wiki/Locked_nucleic_acid" title="Locked nucleic acid">locked nucleic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glycol_nucleic_acid" title="Glycol nucleic acid">glycol nucleic acid</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Threose_nucleic_acid" title="Threose nucleic acid">threose nucleic acid</a>. Each of these is distinguished from naturally occurring DNA or RNA by changes to the backbone of the molecules.
</p>
