<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Gas flare</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Flaring" redirects here. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Flare_(disambiguation)" title="Flare (disambiguation)">Flare (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Shell_haven_flare.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1280" data-file-width="960" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Shell_haven_flare.jpg/220px-Shell_haven_flare.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Shell_haven_flare.jpg/330px-Shell_haven_flare.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Shell_haven_flare.jpg/440px-Shell_haven_flare.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Shell_haven_flare.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Flare stack at the <a href="/wiki/Shell_Haven" title="Shell Haven">Shell Haven</a> refinery in England.</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>gas flare</b>, alternatively known as a <b>flare stack</b>, is a gas <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> device used in industrial plants such as <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">petroleum refineries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemical_plant" title="Chemical plant">chemical plants</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_gas_processing" title="Natural gas processing">natural gas processing</a> plants as well as at oil or gas production sites having <a href="/wiki/Oil_well" title="Oil well">oil wells</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_well" title="Gas well">gas wells</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drilling_rig" title="Drilling rig">offshore oil and gas rigs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfills</a>. 
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:North_Dakota_Flaring_of_Gas.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/North_Dakota_Flaring_of_Gas.JPG/220px-North_Dakota_Flaring_of_Gas.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/North_Dakota_Flaring_of_Gas.JPG/330px-North_Dakota_Flaring_of_Gas.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/North_Dakota_Flaring_of_Gas.JPG/440px-North_Dakota_Flaring_of_Gas.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:North_Dakota_Flaring_of_Gas.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>North Dakota Flaring of Gas</div></div></div>
<p>In industrial plants, flare stacks are primarily used for burning off <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flammability" title="Flammability">flammable</a> gas released by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pressure_relief_valve" title="Pressure relief valve">pressure relief valves</a> during unplanned over-pressuring of plant equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA-VOC_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-VOC-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coker_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coker-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mannan_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mannan-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beychok_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beychok-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> During plant or partial plant startups and shutdowns, flare stacks are also often used for the planned combustion of gases over relatively short periods.
</p><p>Gas flaring at many oil and gas production sites protects against the dangers of over-pressuring industrial plant equipment. When <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil">petroleum crude oil</a> is extracted and produced from onshore or offshore <a href="/wiki/Oil_well" title="Oil well">oil wells</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">raw natural gas</a> associated with the oil is brought to the surface as well. Especially in areas of the world lacking <a href="/wiki/Pipeline_transport" title="Pipeline transport">pipelines</a> and other gas transportation infrastructure, vast amounts of such <a href="/wiki/Associated_petroleum_gas" title="Associated petroleum gas">associated gas</a> are commonly flared as waste or unusable gas. The flaring of associated gas may occur at the top of a vertical flare stack (as in the adjacent photo) or it may occur in a ground-level flare in an earthen pit. Preferably, associated gas is reinjected into the reservoir, which saves it for future use while maintaining higher well pressure and crude oil producibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overall_flare_system_in_industrial_plants">Overall flare system in industrial plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas_flare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overall flare system in industrial plants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FlareStack_System.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="360" data-file-width="400" height="360" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/07/FlareStack_System.png" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FlareStack_System.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Schematic flow diagram of an overall vertical, elevated flare stack system in an industrial plant.</div></div></div>
<p>When industrial plant equipment items are over-pressured, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pressure_relief_valve" title="Pressure relief valve">pressure relief valve</a> is an essential safety device that automatically release gases and sometimes liquids. Those pressure relief valves are required by industrial design codes and standards as well as by law.
</p><p>The released gases and liquids are routed through large <a href="/wiki/Piping" title="Piping">piping</a> systems called <i>flare headers</i> to a vertical elevated flare. The released gases are <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">burned</a> as they exit the flare stacks. The size and brightness of the resulting flame depends upon the flammable material's flow rate in <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">joules</a> per hour (or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._customary_units" title="U.S. customary units">btu</a> per hour).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beychok_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Beychok-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Most industrial plant flares have a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vapor-liquid_separator" title="Vapor-liquid separator">vapor-liquid separator</a> (also known as a knockout drum) upstream of the flare to remove any large amounts of liquid that may accompany the relieved gases.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">Steam</a> is very often injected into the flame to reduce the formation of black smoke. When too much steam is added, a condition known as "over steaming" can occur resulting in reduced combustion efficiency and higher emissions. To keep the flare system functional, a small amount of gas is continuously burned, like a pilot light, so that the system is always ready for its primary purpose as an over-pressure safety system.
</p><p>The adjacent flow diagram depicts the typical components of an overall industrial flare stack system:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA-VOC_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-VOC-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coker_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Coker-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mannan_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mannan-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>A knockout drum to remove any oil or water from the relieved gases.</li>
<li>A water seal drum to prevent any flashback of the <a href="/wiki/Flame" title="Flame">flame</a> from the top of the flare stack.</li>
<li>An alternative gas recovery system for use during partial plant startups and shutdowns as well as other times when required. The recovered gas is routed into the fuel gas system of the overall industrial plant.</li>
<li>A steam injection system to provide an external <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a> force used for efficient mixing of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a> with the relieved gas, which promotes smokeless burning.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Pilot_light" title="Pilot light">pilot flame</a> (with its <a href="/wiki/Ignition_system" title="Ignition system">ignition system</a>) that burns all the time so that it is available to ignite relieved gases when needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>The flare stack, including a flashback prevention section at the upper part of the stack.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impacts_of_waste_flaring_associated_gas_from_oil_drilling_sites_and_other_facilities">Impacts of waste flaring associated gas from oil drilling sites and other facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas_flare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Impacts of waste flaring associated gas from oil drilling sites and other facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:187px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Niger_Delta_Gas-Flares.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="360" data-file-width="432" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Niger_Delta_Gas-Flares.jpg/185px-Niger_Delta_Gas-Flares.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Niger_Delta_Gas-Flares.jpg/278px-Niger_Delta_Gas-Flares.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Niger_Delta_Gas-Flares.jpg/370px-Niger_Delta_Gas-Flares.jpg 2x" width="185"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Niger_Delta_Gas-Flares.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Flaring of associated gas from an oil well site in Nigeria.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:187px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:First_gas_from_the_Oselvar_module_on_the_Ula_platform_on_April_14th,_2012.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1691" data-file-width="1243" height="252" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/First_gas_from_the_Oselvar_module_on_the_Ula_platform_on_April_14th%2C_2012.jpg/185px-First_gas_from_the_Oselvar_module_on_the_Ula_platform_on_April_14th%2C_2012.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/First_gas_from_the_Oselvar_module_on_the_Ula_platform_on_April_14th%2C_2012.jpg/278px-First_gas_from_the_Oselvar_module_on_the_Ula_platform_on_April_14th%2C_2012.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/First_gas_from_the_Oselvar_module_on_the_Ula_platform_on_April_14th%2C_2012.jpg/370px-First_gas_from_the_Oselvar_module_on_the_Ula_platform_on_April_14th%2C_2012.jpg 2x" width="185"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:First_gas_from_the_Oselvar_module_on_the_Ula_platform_on_April_14th,_2012.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Flaring gases from an oil platform in the North Sea.</div></div></div>
<p>Improperly operated flares may emit <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compounds" title="Volatile organic compounds">volatile organic compounds</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> compounds, which are known to exacerbate asthma and other respiratory  problems. Other emissions from improperly operated flares may include, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aromatic_hydrocarbons" title="Aromatic hydrocarbons">aromatic hydrocarbons</a> (<a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toluene" title="Toluene">toluene</a>,  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xylenes" title="Xylenes">xylenes</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benzapyrene" title="Benzapyrene">benzapyrene</a>, which are known to be carcinogenic.
</p><p>Flaring can affect wildlife by attracting birds and insects to the flame. Approximately 7,500 migrating songbirds were attracted to and killed by the flare at the liquefied natural gas terminal in Saint John, New Brunswick, Canada on September 13, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Similar incidents have occurred at flares on offshore oil and gas installations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Moths are known to be attracted to lights. A brochure published by the Secretariat of the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_Biological_Diversity" title="Convention on Biological Diversity">Convention on Biological Diversity</a> describing the Global Taxonomy Initiative describes a situation where "<i>a taxonomist working in a tropical forest noticed that a gas flare at an oil refinery was attracting and killing hundreds of these [hawk or sphinx] moths. Over the course of the months and years that the refinery was running a vast number of moths must have been killed, suggesting that plants could not be pollinated over a large area of forest</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of the end of 2011, 150 × 10<sup>9</sup> cubic meters (5.3 × 10<sup>12</sup> cubic feet) of associated gas are flared annually. That is equivalent to about 25 per cent of the annual natural gas consumption in the United States or about 30 per cent of the annual gas consumption in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GGFR_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-GGFR-11">[11]</a></sup> If it were to reach market, this quantity of gas (at a nominal value of $5.62 per 1000 cubic feet) would be worth $29.8 billion USD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PRICES_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-PRICES-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Also as of the end of 2011, 10 countries accounted for 72 per cent of the flaring, and twenty for 86 per cent. The top ten leading contributors to world gas flaring at the end of 2011, were (in declining order): Russia (27%), <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a> (11%), <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> (8%), <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> (7%), United States (5%), <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a> (4%), <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> (3%), <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a> (3%), <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a> (3%) and <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a> (3%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>That amount of flaring and burning of associated gas from oil drilling sites is a significant source of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) emissions. Coupled with fossil fuel combustion and cement production, flaring's carbon dioxide emissions in 2010 have tripled (1300 ± 110 GtCO2) compared to the last recording (years 1750-1970, 420 ± 35 GtCO had been emitted.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> 2400 × 10<sup>6</sup> tons of carbon dioxide are emitted annually in this way and it amounts to about 1.2 per cent of the worldwide emissions of carbon dioxide. That may seem to be insignificant, but in perspective it is more than half of the <i>Certified Emissions Reductions</i> (a type of carbon credits) that have been issued under the rules and mechanisms of the Kyoto Protocol as of June 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GGFR_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-GGFR-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Satellite data show that from 2005 to 2010, global gas flaring decreased by about 20%. The most significant reductions in terms of volume were made in Russia (down 40%) and Nigeria (down 29%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GGFR_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-GGFR-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_Barack_Obama" title="Presidency of Barack Obama">Obama administration</a> implemented rules curbing flaring, and subsequently the <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_Donald_Trump" title="Presidency of Donald Trump">Trump administration</a> attempted to delay implementation of the rules. In October 2017 a federal magistrate judge vacated the Department of Interior's move to delay implementation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_benefit">Environmental benefit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gas_flare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Environmental benefit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">Methane</a>'s estimated <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_potential" title="Global warming potential">global warming potential</a> is 104 times greater than that of CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atul2000_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Atul2000-18">[18]</a></sup>  Therefore, to the extent that gas flares convert methane to CO<sub>2</sub> before it is released into the atmosphere, they reduce the amount of global warming that would otherwise occur.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
