<p><b>Acid strength</b> refers to the tendency of an <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acid</a>, symbolised by the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formula</a> HA, to dissociate into a <a href="/wiki/Hydron_(chemistry)" title="Hydron (chemistry)">proton</a>, H<sup>+</sup>, and an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">anion</a>, A<sup>−</sup>. The <a href="/wiki/Dissociation_(chemistry)" title="Dissociation (chemistry)">dissociation</a> of a strong acid in solution is effectively complete except the most concentrated solutions.
</p>
<dl><dd>HA → H<sup>+</sup> + A<sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<p>Examples of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strong_acid" title="Strong acid">strong acids</a> are <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> (HCl), <a href="/wiki/Perchloric_acid" title="Perchloric acid">perchloric acid</a> (HClO<sub>4</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> (HNO<sub>3</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> (H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>).
</p><p>A weak acid is only partially dissociated, with both the undissociated acid and its dissociation products being present, in solution, in equilibrium with each other. 
</p>
<dl><dd>HA ⇌ H<sup>+</sup> + A<sup>−</sup>.</dd></dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">Acetic acid</a> (CH<sub>3</sub>COOH) is an example of a weak acid. The strength of a weak acid is quantified by its <a href="/wiki/Acid_dissociation_constant" title="Acid dissociation constant">acid dissociation constant</a>, pK<sub>a</sub> value.
</p><p>The strength of a weak <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic</a> acid may depend on substituent effects. The strength of an <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_chemistry" title="Inorganic chemistry">inorganic</a> acid is dependent on the <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a> for the atom to which the proton may be attached. Acid strength is solvent-dependent. For example, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride" title="Hydrogen chloride">hydrogen chloride</a> is a strong acid in aqueous solution, but is a weak acid when dissolved in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glacial_acetic_acid" title="Glacial acetic acid">glacial acetic acid</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measures_of_acid_strength">Measures of acid strength</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acid_strength&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Measures of acid strength">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The usual measure of the strength of an acid is its <a href="/wiki/Acid_dissociation_constant" title="Acid dissociation constant">acid dissociation constant</a> (<i>K</i><sub>a</sub>), which can be <a href="/wiki/Acid_dissociation_constant#Experimental_determination" title="Acid dissociation constant">determined experimentally</a> by <a href="/wiki/Titration" title="Titration">titration</a> methods. Stronger acids have a larger <i>K</i><sub>a</sub> and a smaller logarithmic constant (p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub><span class="nowrap"> </span>=<span class="nowrap"> </span>−log<span class="nowrap"> </span><i>K</i><sub>a</sub>) than weaker acids. The stronger an acid is, the more easily it loses a proton, H<sup>+</sup>. Two key factors that contribute to the ease of <a href="/wiki/Deprotonation" title="Deprotonation">deprotonation</a> are the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_polarity" title="Chemical polarity">polarity</a> of the H—A bond and the size of atom A, which determine the strength of the H—A bond. Acid strengths also depend on the stability of the conjugate base.
</p><p>While the p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> value measures the tendency of an acidic solute to transfer a proton to a standard solvent (most commonly water or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DMSO" title="DMSO">DMSO</a>), the tendency of an acidic solvent to transfer a proton to a reference solute (most commonly a weak <a href="/wiki/Aniline" title="Aniline">aniline</a> base) is measured by its <a href="/wiki/Hammett_acidity_function" title="Hammett acidity function">Hammett acidity function</a>, the <i>H</i><sub>0</sub> value.  Although these two concepts of acid strength often amount to the same general tendency of a substance to donate a proton, the p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> and <i>H</i><sub>0</sub> values are measures of distinct properties and may occasionally diverge.  For instance, hydrogen fluoride, whether dissolved in water (p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> = 3.2) or DMSO (p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> = 15), has p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> values indicating that it undergoes incomplete dissociation in these solvents, making it a weak acid.  However, as the rigorously dried, neat acidic medium, hydrogen fluoride has an <i>H</i><sub>0</sub> value of –15,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> making it a more strongly protonating medium than 100% sulfuric acid and thus, by definition, a <a href="/wiki/Superacid" title="Superacid">superacid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  (To prevent ambiguity, in the rest of this article, "strong acid" will, unless otherwise stated, refer to an acid that is strong as measured by its p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> value (p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> &lt; –1.74).  This usage is consistent with the common parlance of most practicing chemists.)
</p><p>When the acidic medium in question is a dilute aqueous solution, the <i>H</i><sub>0</sub> is approximately equal to the <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> value, which is a negative logarithm of the concentration of aqueous H<sup>+</sup> in solution. The pH of a simple solution of an acid in water is determined by both <i>K</i><sub>a</sub> and the acid concentration. For weak acid solutions, it depends on the <a href="/wiki/Dissociation_(chemistry)" title="Dissociation (chemistry)">degree of dissociation</a>, which may be determined by an equilibrium calculation. For concentrated solutions of acids, especially strong acids for which pH &lt; 0, the <i>H</i><sub>0</sub> value is a better measure of acidity than the pH.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Strong_acids">Strong acids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acid_strength&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Strong acids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Strong_acid.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="1102" height="120" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Strong_acid.png/220px-Strong_acid.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Strong_acid.png/330px-Strong_acid.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Strong_acid.png/440px-Strong_acid.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Strong_acid.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Image of a strong acid mostly dissociating. The small red circles represent H<sup>+</sup> ions.</div></div></div>
<p>A <i>strong acid</i> is an acid that dissociates according to the reaction 
</p>
<dl><dd>HA + S ⇌ SH<sup>+</sup> + A<sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<p>where S represents a solvent molecule, such as a molecule of water or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DMSO" title="DMSO">DMSO</a>, to such an extent that the concentration of the undissociated species HA is too low to be measured. For practical purposes a strong acid can be said to be completely dissociated. An example of a strong acid is <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>
</p>
<dl><dd>HCl → H<sup>+</sup> + Cl<sup>−</sup> (in aqueous solution)</dd></dl>
<p>Any acid with a pK<sub>a</sub> value which is less than about -2 is classed as a strong acid. This results from the very high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buffer_capacity" title="Buffer capacity">buffer capacity</a> of solutions with a <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH value</a> of 1 or less and is known as the <a href="/wiki/Leveling_effect" title="Leveling effect">leveling effect</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Experimental_determination">Experimental determination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acid_strength&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Experimental determination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Acid_dissociation_constant#Experimental_determination" title="Acid dissociation constant">Acid dissociation constant § Experimental determination</a></div>
<p>The experimental determination of a p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> value is commonly performed by means of a <a href="/wiki/Titration" title="Titration">titration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A typical procedure would be as follows. A quantity of strong acid is added to a solution containing the acid or a salt of the acid, to the point where the compound is fully protonated. The solution is then titrated with a strong base  
</p>
<dl><dd>HA + OH<sup>−</sup> → A<sup>−</sup> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>until only the deprotonated species, A<sup>−</sup>, remains in solution. At each point in the titration pH is measured using a <a href="/wiki/Glass_electrode" title="Glass electrode">glass electrode</a> and a <a href="/wiki/PH_meter" title="PH meter">pH meter</a>. The equilibrium constant is found by fitting calculated pH values to the observed values, using the method of <a href="/wiki/Least_squares" title="Least squares">least squares</a>.
</p>
<h3><span id="Conjugate_acid.2Fbase_pair"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Conjugate_acid/base_pair">Conjugate acid/base pair</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acid_strength&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conjugate acid/base pair">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It is sometimes stated that "the conjugate of a weak acid is a strong base". Such a statement is incorrect. For example, acetic acid is a weak acid which has a K<sub>a</sub> = 1.75 x 10<sup>−5</sup>. Its conjugate base is the <a href="/wiki/Acetate" title="Acetate">acetate</a> ion with K<sub>b</sub> = 10<sup>−14</sup>/K<sub>a</sub> = 5.7 x 10<sup>−10</sup> (from the relationship K<sub>a</sub> × K<sub>b</sub> = 10<sup>−14</sup>), which certainly does not correspond to a strong base. The conjugate of a weak acid is often a weak base and <i>vice versa</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acids_in_non-aqueous_solvents">Acids in non-aqueous solvents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acid_strength&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Acids in non-aqueous solvents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The strength of an acid varies from solvent to solvent. An acid which is strong in water may be weak in a less basic solvent, and an acid which is weak in water may be strong in a more basic solvent. According to <a href="/wiki/Br%C3%B8nsted%E2%80%93Lowry_acid%E2%80%93base_theory" title="Brønsted–Lowry acid–base theory">Brønsted–Lowry acid–base theory</a>, the solvent S can accept a proton.  
</p>
<dl><dd>HA + S ⇌ A<sup>−</sup> + HS<sup>+</sup>.</dd></dl>
<p>For example hydrochloric acid is a weak acid in solution in pure <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a>, HO<sub>2</sub>CCH<sub>3</sub>, which is more acidic than water.
</p>
<dl><dd>HO<sub>2</sub>CCH<sub>3</sub> + HCl ⇌ (HO)<sub>2</sub>CCH<sub>3</sub><sup>+</sup> + Cl<sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<p>The extent of ionization of the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_halide" title="Hydrogen halide">hydrohalic acids</a> decreases in the order HI &gt; HBr &gt; HCl. Acetic acid is said to be a <a href="/wiki/Leveling_effect#Leveling_and_differentiating_solvents" title="Leveling effect">differentiating solvent</a> for the three acids, while water is not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Housecroft_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Housecroft-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>(p. 217)</span></sup>
</p><p>An important example of a solvent which is more basic than water is <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfoxide" title="Dimethyl sulfoxide">dimethyl sulfoxide</a>, DMSO, (CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>SO. A compound which is a weak acid in water may become a strong acid in DMSO. <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">Acetic acid</a> is an example of such a substance. An extensive bibliography of p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> values in solution in DMSO and other solvents can be found at <a class="external text" href="http://tera.chem.ut.ee/~ivo/HA_UT/" rel="nofollow">Acidity–Basicity Data in Nonaqueous Solvents</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Superacid" title="Superacid">Superacids</a> are strong acids even in solvents of low dielectric constant. Examples of superacids are <a href="/wiki/Fluoroantimonic_acid" title="Fluoroantimonic acid">fluoroantimonic acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magic_acid" title="Magic acid">magic acid</a>. Some superacids can be crystallised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> They can also quantitatively stabilize <a href="/wiki/Carbocation" title="Carbocation">carbocations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Olah1968_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Olah1968-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
