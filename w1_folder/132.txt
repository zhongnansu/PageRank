<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Trace gas</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>trace gas</b> is a gas which makes up less than 1% by volume of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a>, and it includes all gases except <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> (78.1%) and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> (20.9%). The most abundant trace gas at 0.934% is <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>. <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">Water vapor</a> also occurs in the atmosphere with highly variable abundance.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_sources">Natural sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trace_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Natural sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several atmospheric trace gases such as <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> (in the lower atmosphere), <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxides</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"><i>x</i></sub></span></span> are <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a>, chemically reactive factors of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_quality" title="Air quality">air quality</a> at a regional level. Others such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> and <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">CH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> are important <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and are produced anthropogenically but mainly by <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a> and from natural geothermal sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_gas#Composition" title="Volcanic gas">volcanic activity</a> is an important source of trace gases as well.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change">Climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trace_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Earth's climate is sensitive to changes in trace gas concentrations and temperature of the upper <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> - lower <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a> region of the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> (UTLS). This zone extends from 6 to 25 km above <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a> where the interaction between radiation and trace gases, clouds and aerosols can lead to important climate feedback. Trace gases can be long lived or short lived. The first category includes several greenhouse gases (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">CH<sub>4</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">N<sub>2</sub>O</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon" title="Chlorofluorocarbon">CFCs</a>), whilst the latter encompasses species with a lifetime equal to or less than a few months (<a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">O<sub>3</sub></a>, nitrogen oxides, <a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound" title="Volatile organic compound">VOCs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">CO</a> etc).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mixing">Mixing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trace_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mixing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Trace gas exchanges between the upper troposphere and the lower stratosphere occur due to large air mass movements. Among these, one of the most important is the Asian monsoon circulation pattern. This way, pollutants (and among them, trace gases) from Asia, India and Indonesia reach the stratosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2010randel_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-2010randel-5">[5]</a></sup> Regarding the horizontal spread, the extratropical air in the stratosphere, is mixed towards the equator at the outer edge of the Asian monsoon anticyclone, thus affecting the concentration of trace gases in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2010konopka_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-2010konopka-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ozone_hole">Ozone hole</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Trace_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ozone hole">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Trace gases can react with each other, leading to imbalances that can deeply affect the life on Earth. For example, certain halogen species, such as odd-<a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> species (<a href="/wiki/Chlorine_monoxide" title="Chlorine monoxide">ClO</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">HCl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hypochlorous_acid" title="Hypochlorous acid">HOCl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chlorine_nitrate" title="Chlorine nitrate">ClONO<sub>2</sub></a>) or <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">bromine</a> species are involved in the destruction of <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>, through chemical reactions. On the other hand, nitrogen compounds can play a part both in the destruction and creation of ozone and a balanced can be reached.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2003grainger_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-2003grainger-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2000bureau_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-2000bureau-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1993mills_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-1993mills-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
