<p>In meteorology the <b>planetary boundary layer</b> (<b>PBL</b>), also known as the <b>atmospheric boundary layer</b> (<b>ABL</b>) or <i>peplosphere</i>, is the lowest part of the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> and its behavior is directly influenced by its contact with a <a href="/wiki/Planetary_surface" title="Planetary surface">planetary surface</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>. On Earth it usually responds to changes in surface <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a> in an hour or less. In this layer physical quantities such as <a href="/wiki/Flow_velocity" title="Flow velocity">flow velocity</a>, temperature, moisture, etc., display rapid fluctuations (<a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulence</a>) and vertical mixing is strong. Above the PBL is the "free atmosphere" where the wind is approximately <a href="/wiki/Geostrophic_wind" title="Geostrophic wind">geostrophic</a> (parallel to the isobars) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>, while within the PBL the wind is affected by surface <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">drag</a> and turns across the <a href="/wiki/Contour_line#Barometric_pressure" title="Contour line">isobars</a>. 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cause_of_surface_wind_gradient">Cause of surface wind gradient</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_boundary_layer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Cause of surface wind gradient">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Light_pollution_and_the_planetary_boundary_layer_over_Berlin.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Light_pollution_and_the_planetary_boundary_layer_over_Berlin.jpg/220px-Light_pollution_and_the_planetary_boundary_layer_over_Berlin.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Light_pollution_and_the_planetary_boundary_layer_over_Berlin.jpg/330px-Light_pollution_and_the_planetary_boundary_layer_over_Berlin.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Light_pollution_and_the_planetary_boundary_layer_over_Berlin.jpg/440px-Light_pollution_and_the_planetary_boundary_layer_over_Berlin.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Light_pollution_and_the_planetary_boundary_layer_over_Berlin.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The difference in the amount of aerosols below and above the boundary layer is easy to see in this aerial photograph. Light pollution from the city of Berlin is strongly scattered below the layer, but above the layer it mostly propagates out into space.</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">Wind shear</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind_gradient" title="Wind gradient">Wind gradient</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind_engineering" title="Wind engineering">Wind engineering</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ekman_layer" title="Ekman layer">Ekman layer</a></div>
<p>Typically, due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerodynamic" title="Aerodynamic">aerodynamic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drag_(force)" title="Drag (force)">drag</a>, there is a wind gradient in the wind flow just a few hundred meters above the Earth's surface—the <a href="/wiki/Surface_layer" title="Surface layer">surface layer</a> of the planetary boundary layer. Wind speed increases with increasing height above the ground, starting from zero<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wizelius_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wizelius-3">[3]</a></sup> due to the <a href="/wiki/No-slip_condition" title="No-slip condition">no-slip condition</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-4">[4]</a></sup> Flow near the surface encounters obstacles that reduce the wind speed, and introduce random vertical and horizontal velocity components at right angles to the main direction of flow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
This <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulence</a> causes vertical <a href="/wiki/Mixing_(physics)" title="Mixing (physics)">mixing</a> between the air moving horizontally at one level and the air at those levels immediately above and below it, which is important in dispersion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pollutants" title="Pollutants">pollutants</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hadlock_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hadlock-6">[6]</a></sup> and in <a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion" title="Soil erosion">soil erosion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lal_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lal-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The reduction in velocity near the surface is a function of surface roughness, so wind velocity profiles are quite different for different terrain types.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-4">[4]</a></sup> Rough, irregular ground, and man-made obstructions on the ground can reduce the <a href="/wiki/Geostrophic_wind" title="Geostrophic wind">geostrophic wind</a> speed by 40% to 50%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oke_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oke-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crawley_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crawley-9">[9]</a></sup> Over open water or ice, the reduction may be only 20% to 30%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Russell_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Russell-11">[11]</a></sup>  These effects are taken into account when siting <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">wind turbines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lubosny_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lubosny-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>For <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a> purposes, the wind gradient is modeled as a <a href="/wiki/Simple_shear" title="Simple shear">simple shear</a> exhibiting a vertical velocity profile varying according to a <a href="/wiki/Power_law" title="Power law">power law</a> with a constant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exponent" title="Exponent">exponential</a> coefficient based on surface type. The height above ground where surface friction has a negligible effect on wind speed is called the "gradient height" and the wind speed above this height is assumed to be a constant called the "gradient wind speed".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crawley_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Crawley-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gupta_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gupta-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> For example, typical values for the predicted gradient height are 457 m for large cities, 366 m for suburbs, 274 m for open terrain, and 213 m for open sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the power law exponent approximation is convenient, it has no theoretical basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> When the temperature profile is adiabatic, the wind speed should vary <a href="/wiki/Logarithm" title="Logarithm">logarithmically</a> with height,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Measurements over open terrain in 1961 showed good agreement with the <a href="/wiki/Log_wind_profile" title="Log wind profile">logarithmic fit</a> up to 100 m or so (within the <a href="/wiki/Surface_layer" title="Surface layer">surface layer</a>), with near constant average wind speed up through 1000 m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thuillier_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thuillier-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Shearing_(physics)" title="Shearing (physics)">shearing</a> of the wind is usually three-dimensional,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> that is, there is also a change in direction between the 'free' pressure-driven geostrophic wind and the wind close to the ground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> This is related to the <a href="/wiki/Ekman_spiral" title="Ekman spiral">Ekman spiral</a> effect. 
The cross-isobar angle of the diverted ageostrophic flow near the surface ranges from 10° over open water, to 30° over rough hilly terrain, and can increase to 40°-50° over land at night when the wind speed is very low.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Russell_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Russell-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>After sundown the wind gradient near the surface increases, with the increasing stability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Köpp_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Köpp-22">[22]</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_instability" title="Atmospheric instability">Atmospheric stability</a> occurring at night with <a href="/wiki/Radiative_cooling" title="Radiative cooling">radiative cooling</a> tends to contain turbulent eddies vertically, increasing the wind gradient.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lal_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lal-7">[7]</a></sup> The magnitude of the wind gradient is largely influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a>, principally atmospheric stability and the height of any convective boundary layer or <a href="/wiki/Capping_inversion" title="Capping inversion">Capping inversion</a>. This effect is even larger over the sea, where there is no diurnal variation of the height of the boundary layer as there is over land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johansson2002_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Johansson2002-23">[23]</a></sup>
In the convective boundary layer, strong mixing diminishes vertical wind gradient.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Constituent_layers">Constituent layers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_boundary_layer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Constituent layers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:20120629_atmospheric_thermocline.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="5184" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/20120629_atmospheric_thermocline.JPG/220px-20120629_atmospheric_thermocline.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/20120629_atmospheric_thermocline.JPG/330px-20120629_atmospheric_thermocline.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/20120629_atmospheric_thermocline.JPG/440px-20120629_atmospheric_thermocline.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:20120629_atmospheric_thermocline.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shelf_cloud" title="Shelf cloud">shelf cloud</a> at the leading edge of a thunderstorm complex on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Side_(Chicago)" title="South Side (Chicago)">South Side of Chicago</a> that extends from the <a href="/wiki/Hyde_Park,_Chicago" title="Hyde Park, Chicago">Hyde Park</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Community_areas_of_Chicago" title="Community areas of Chicago">community area</a> to over the <a href="/wiki/Regents_Park_(Chicago)" title="Regents Park (Chicago)">Regents Park</a> twin towers and out over <a href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan" title="Lake Michigan">Lake Michigan</a></div></div></div>
<p>As <a href="/wiki/Navier%E2%80%93Stokes_equations" title="Navier–Stokes equations">Navier–Stokes equations</a> suggest, the planetary boundary layer turbulence is produced in the layer with the largest velocity gradients that is at the very surface proximity. This layer – conventionally called a <a href="/wiki/Surface_layer" title="Surface layer">surface layer</a> – constitutes about 10% of the total PBL depth. Above the surface layer the PBL turbulence gradually dissipates, losing its kinetic energy to friction as well as converting the kinetic to potential energy in a density stratified flow. The balance between the rate of the turbulent kinetic energy production and its dissipation determines the planetary boundary layer depth. The PBL depth varies broadly. At a given wind speed, e.g. 8 m/s, and so at a given rate of the turbulence production, a PBL in wintertime Arctic could be as shallow as 50 m, a nocturnal PBL in mid-latitudes could be typically 300 m in thickness, and a tropical PBL in the trade-wind zone could grow to its full theoretical depth of 2000 m.
</p><p>In addition to the surface layer, the planetary boundary layer also comprises the PBL <i>core</i> (between 0.1 and 0.7 of the PBL depth) and the PBL top or <i>entrainment layer</i> or <i>capping inversion layer</i> (between 0.7 and 1 of the PBL depth). Four main external factors determine the PBL depth and its mean vertical structure: 
</p>
<ol><li>the free atmosphere wind speed;</li>
<li>the surface heat (more exactly buoyancy) balance;</li>
<li>the free atmosphere density stratification;</li>
<li>the free atmosphere vertical wind shear or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baroclinicity" title="Baroclinicity">baroclinicity</a>.</li></ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Principal_types">Principal types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_boundary_layer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Principal types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Atmospheric_boundary_layer.svg"><img alt="Atmospheric boundary layer.svg" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="345" data-file-width="660" height="115" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Atmospheric_boundary_layer.svg/220px-Atmospheric_boundary_layer.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Atmospheric_boundary_layer.svg/330px-Atmospheric_boundary_layer.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Atmospheric_boundary_layer.svg/440px-Atmospheric_boundary_layer.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Atmospheric_boundary_layer.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div>
<dl><dt>Convective planetary boundary layer</dt>
<dd>(CBL) is a PBL when positive buoyancy flux at the surface creates a thermal instability and thus generates additional or even major turbulence, (also known as having CAPE  or <a href="/wiki/Convective_available_potential_energy" title="Convective available potential energy">Convective available potential energy</a>); see <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_convection" title="Atmospheric convection">Atmospheric convection</a>. A CBL is typical in tropical and mid-latitudes during daytime. Solar heating assisted by the heat released from the water vapor condensation could create so strong convective turbulence that the <a href="/wiki/Free_convective_layer" title="Free convective layer">Free convective layer</a> comprises the entire troposphere up to the <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a> (the boundary in the Earth's atmosphere between the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>), which is at 10 km to 18 km in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intertropical_convergence_zone" title="Intertropical convergence zone">Intertropical convergence zone</a>.</dd>
<dt>Stably stratified planetary boundary layer</dt>
<dd>(SBL) is a PBL when negative buoyancy flux at the surface damps the turbulence; see <a href="/wiki/Convective_inhibition" title="Convective inhibition">Convective inhibition</a>. An SBL is solely driven by the wind shear turbulence and hence the SBL cannot exist without the free atmosphere wind. An SBL is typical in nighttime at all locations and even in daytime in places where the Earth's surface is colder than the air above. An SBL plays a particularly important role in high latitudes where it is often prolonged (days to months), resulting in very cold air temperatures.</dd></dl>
<p>Physical laws and equations of motions, which govern the planetary boundary layer dynamics and microphysics, are strongly non-linear and considerably influenced by properties of the Earth's surface and evolution of the processes in the free atmosphere. To deal with this complicity, the whole array of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turbulence_models" title="Turbulence models">turbulence modelling</a> has been proposed. However, they are often not accurate enough to meet practical requests. Significant improvements are expected from application of a <a href="/wiki/Large_eddy_simulation" title="Large eddy simulation">large eddy simulation</a> technique to problems related to the PBL.
</p><p>Perhaps the most important processes, which are critically dependent on the correct representation of the PBL in the atmospheric models (<a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_Model_Intercomparison_Project" title="Atmospheric Model Intercomparison Project">Atmospheric Model Intercomparison Project</a>), are turbulent transport of moisture (<a href="/wiki/Evapotranspiration" title="Evapotranspiration">evapotranspiration</a>) and pollutants (<a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollutants</a>). <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">Clouds</a> in the boundary layer influence <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trade_wind" title="Trade wind">trade winds</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrological_cycle" title="Hydrological cycle">hydrological cycle</a>, and energy exchange.
</p>
