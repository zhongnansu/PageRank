<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Leavening agent</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>A <b>leaven</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ɛ/: 'e' in 'dress'">ɛ</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="/ən/: 'on' in 'button'">ən</span></span>/</a></span></span>, often called a <b>leavening agent</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/ɛ/: 'e' in 'dress'">ɛ</span><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="/ən/: 'on' in 'button'">ən</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="/ŋ/: 'ng' in 'sing'">ŋ</span></span>/</a></span></span> (and also known as a <b>raising agent</b>), is any one of a number of substances used in <a href="/wiki/Dough" title="Dough">doughs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Batter_(cooking)" title="Batter (cooking)">batters</a> that cause a foaming action (gas bubbles) that lightens and softens the mixture. An alternative or supplement to leavening agents is mechanical action by which air is incorporated. Leavening agents can be biological or synthetic chemical compounds. The gas produced is often <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, or occasionally <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>. 
</p><p>When a dough or batter is mixed, the <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a> in the flour and the water in the dough form a matrix (often supported further by proteins like <a href="/wiki/Gluten" title="Gluten">gluten</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polysaccharides" title="Polysaccharides">polysaccharides</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Pentosan" title="Pentosan">pentosans</a> or <a href="/wiki/Xanthan_gum" title="Xanthan gum">xanthan gum</a>). Then the starch <a href="/wiki/Starch_gelatinization" title="Starch gelatinization">gelatinizes</a> and sets, leaving gas bubbles that remain.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_leavening_agents">Biological leavening agents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leavening_agent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biological leavening agents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Saccharomyces_cerevisiae" title="Saccharomyces cerevisiae">Saccharomyces cerevisiae</a></i> producing <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> found in:
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Baker%27s_yeast" title="Baker's yeast">baker's yeast</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">beer</a> (unpasteurised—live yeast)</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_leavens">Chemical leavens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leavening_agent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Chemical leavens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chemical leavens are mixtures or compounds that release gases when they react with each other, with moisture, or with heat.  Most are based on a combination of acid (usually a low molecular weight organic acid) and a <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salt</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> (HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>).  After they act, these compounds leave behind a chemical salt.  Chemical leavens are used in <a href="/wiki/Quick_bread" title="Quick bread">quick breads</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cake" title="Cake">cakes</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Cookie" title="Cookie">cookies</a> and numerous other applications where a long biological <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_fermentation" title="Food fermentation">fermentation</a> is impractical or undesirable.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leavening_agent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Chemical leavening were publicized by Amelia Simmons in her <i><a href="/wiki/American_Cookery" title="American Cookery">American Cookery</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> published in 1796, wherein she mentions the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pearl_ash" title="Pearl ash">pearl ash</a> as a leavening agent.
</p><p>Since chemical expertise is required to create a functional chemical leaven without producing off-flavors from the chemical precursors involved, such substances are often mixed into premeasured combinations for maximum results. These are generally referred to as <a href="/wiki/Baking_powder" title="Baking powder">baking powders</a>.  Sour milk and carbonates were used in the 1800s.  The breakthrough in chemical leavening agents occurred in the 1930s with the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Monocalcium_phosphate" title="Monocalcium phosphate">monocalcium phosphates</a> (Ca(H<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>). Other leavening agents developed include <a href="/wiki/Sodium_aluminium_sulfate" title="Sodium aluminium sulfate">sodium aluminium sulfate</a> (NaAl(SO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·12H<sub>2</sub>O), <a href="/wiki/Disodium_pyrophosphate" title="Disodium pyrophosphate">disodium pyrophosphate</a> (Na<sub>2</sub>H<sub>2</sub>P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>), and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_aluminium_phosphate" title="Sodium aluminium phosphate">sodium aluminium phosphates</a> (NaH<sub>14</sub>Al<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>8</sub>·4H<sub>2</sub>O and Na<sub>3</sub>H<sub>15</sub>Al<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>8</sub>).  These compounds combine with sodium bicarbonate to give <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> in a predictable manner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_leavens">Other leavens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leavening_agent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other leavens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">Steam</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a> are used as leavening agents when they expand upon heating. To take advantage of this style of leavening, the baking must be done at high enough temperatures to flash the water to steam, with a batter that is capable of holding the steam in until set. This effect is typically used in <a href="/wiki/Popover" title="Popover">popovers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_pudding" title="Yorkshire pudding">Yorkshire puddings</a>, and to a lesser extent in <a href="/wiki/Tempura" title="Tempura">tempura</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">Nitrous oxide</a> is used as a propellant in aerosol whip cream cans. Large densities of N<sub>2</sub>O are dissolved in cream at high pressure. When expelled from the can, the nitrous oxide escapes emulsion instantly, creating a temporary foam in the butterfat matrix of the cream.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanical_leavening">Mechanical leavening</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leavening_agent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mechanical leavening">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Creaming_(food)" title="Creaming (food)">Creaming</a> is the process of beating sugar crystals and solid fat (typically <a href="/wiki/Butter" title="Butter">butter</a>) together in a <a href="/wiki/Mixer_(cooking)" title="Mixer (cooking)">mixer</a>.  This integrates tiny air bubbles into the mixture, since the sugar crystals physically cut through the structure of the fat. Creamed mixtures are usually further leavened by a chemical leaven like baking soda.  This is often used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cookies" title="Cookies">cookies</a>.
</p><p>Using a <a href="/wiki/Whisk" title="Whisk">whisk</a> on certain liquids, notably <a href="/wiki/Cream" title="Cream">cream</a> or <a href="/wiki/Egg_white" title="Egg white">egg whites</a>, can also create foams through mechanical action. This is the method employed in the making of <a href="/wiki/Sponge_cake" title="Sponge cake">sponge cakes</a>, where an egg protein matrix produced by vigorous whipping provides almost all the structure of the finished product.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chorleywood_Bread_Process" title="Chorleywood Bread Process">Chorleywood Bread Process</a> uses a mix of biological and mechanical leavening to produce bread; while it is considered by food processors to be an effective way to deal with the soft wheat flours characteristic of British Isles agriculture, it is controversial due to a perceived lack of quality in the final product. The process has nevertheless been adapted by industrial bakers in other parts of the world.
</p>
