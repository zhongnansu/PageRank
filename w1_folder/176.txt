<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Dry-ice blasting</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rubbermold.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="221" data-file-width="220" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/df/Rubbermold.jpg" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rubbermold.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dry ice blasting used to clean a rubber mold</div></div></div>
<p><b>Dry ice blasting</b> is a form of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_cleaning" title="Carbon dioxide cleaning">carbon dioxide cleaning</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Dry_ice" title="Dry ice">dry ice</a>, the solid form of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, is accelerated in a pressurized air stream and directed at a surface in order to clean it.
</p><p>The method is similar to other forms of media blasting such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sand_blasting" title="Sand blasting">sand blasting</a>, plastic bead blasting, or <a href="/wiki/Sodablasting" title="Sodablasting">sodablasting</a> in that it cleans surfaces using a media accelerated in a pressurized air stream, but dry ice blasting uses dry ice as the blasting medium. Dry ice blasting is nonabrasive, non-conductive, nonflammable, and non-toxic.
</p><p>Dry ice blasting is an environmentally responsible<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> cleaning method. Dry ice is made of reclaimed carbon dioxide that is produced from other industrial processes, does not add additional greenhouse gases to the atmosphere, and is an approved media by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">EPA</a>, <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">FDA</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Agriculture" title="United States Department of Agriculture">USDA</a>. It also reduces or eliminates employee exposure to the use of chemical cleaning agents.
</p><p>Compared to other media blasting methods, dry ice blasting does not create secondary waste or chemical residues as dry ice <a href="/wiki/Sublimation_(phase_transition)" title="Sublimation (phase transition)">sublimates</a>, or converts back to a gaseous state, when it hits the surface that is being cleaned.  Dry ice blasting does not require clean-up of a blasting medium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  The waste products, which includes just the dislodged media, can be swept up, vacuumed or washed away depending on the containment.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Method">Method</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dry-ice_blasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Method">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dry_Ice_impact.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2400" data-file-width="3600" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Dry_Ice_impact.jpg/220px-Dry_Ice_impact.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Dry_Ice_impact.jpg/330px-Dry_Ice_impact.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Dry_Ice_impact.jpg/440px-Dry_Ice_impact.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dry_Ice_impact.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dry ice blasting illustration</div></div></div>
<p>Dry ice blasting involves propelling pellets at extremely high speeds. The actual dry ice pellets are quite soft, and much less dense than other media used in blast-cleaning (i.e. sand or plastic pellets). Upon impact, the pellet sublimates almost immediately, transferring minimal <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> to the surface on impact and producing minimal abrasion. The sublimation process absorbs a large volume of heat from the surface, producing <a href="/wiki/Shear_stress" title="Shear stress">shear stresses</a> due to <a href="/wiki/Thermal_shock" title="Thermal shock">thermal shock</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This is assumed to improve cleaning as the top layer of dirt or contaminant is expected to transfer more heat than the underlying <a href="/wiki/Substrate_(materials_science)" title="Substrate (materials science)">substrate</a> and flake off more easily. The efficiency and effectiveness of this process depends on the thermal conductivity of the substrate and contaminant. The rapid change in state from solid to gas also causes microscopic <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">shock waves</a>, which are also thought to assist in removing the contaminant.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Equipment">Equipment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dry-ice_blasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Equipment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The dry ice used can be in solid pellet form or shaved from a larger block of ice. The shaved ice block produces a less dense ice medium and is more delicate than the solid pellet system.  In addition, pellets may be made by either compressing dry ice snow, or using tanks of liquid CO2 to form solid pellets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  Dry ice made with compressed snow breaks apart more easily and is not as aggressive for cleaning.
</p><p>Dry ice blasting technology can trace its roots to conventional <a href="/wiki/Abrasive_blasting" title="Abrasive blasting">abrasive blasting</a>. The differences between an abrasive-blasting machine and a dry ice blasting machine are in how they handle the blast media. Unlike sand or other media, dry ice is generally used at its sublimation temperature. Other differences include systems for preventing the ice from forming <a href="/wiki/Snowball" title="Snowball">snowball</a>-like jams, and different materials to allow operation at very low temperatures.
</p><p>There are two methods of dry ice blasting, two-hose and single hose.  The single hose system is more aggressive for cleaning, since the particles are accelerated to faster speeds.
</p><p>Two-hose dry ice blasting was developed before the single-hose system.  The two-hose dry ice blasting approach is very similar to a suction-feed abrasive blast system. Compressed air is delivered in one hose, and ice pellets are sucked out of a second hose by the <a href="/wiki/Venturi_effect" title="Venturi effect">venturi effect</a>. Compared to a single-hose system, the two-hose system delivers ice particles less forcefully (approximately 5% for a given air supply). For a given amount of compressed air, two-hose systems can have less vertical distance between the machine and applicator. For most systems available today this limit is well in excess of 25 feet. Two-hose systems are generally cheaper to produce due to a simpler delivery system. These systems are rarely seen today as they are less efficient in most applications. Their principal advantage is in allowing finer particles of ice to be delivered to the applicator as the late combination of warm air with cold ice results in less sublimation in the hose. These systems allow for more delicate surfaces to be cleaned such as in semiconductor.
</p><p>The first dry ice blasting machine to be commercialized was a single-hose system.  It was developed by Cold Jet, LLC in 1986,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and uses a single hose to deliver air blasts and dry ice. Single-hose dry ice blasters share many of the advantages of single-hose abrasive-blast systems. To avoid the potential dangers of a pressurized hopper, single-hose dry ice blasters make use of a quickly cycling airlock. The single-hose system can use a longer hose than its double-hose counterpart without a significant drop in pressure when the ice leaves the hose. The additional power comes at the cost of increased complexity. Single-hose systems are used where more aggressive cleaning is an advantage. This allows heavier build-up to be cleaned and allows moderate buildup to be cleaned faster.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dry-ice_blasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bakerycleaning.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="220" data-file-width="220" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/a/ad/Bakerycleaning.jpg" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bakerycleaning.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dry ice blasting used to clean bakery equipment</div></div></div>
<p>Dry ice blasting is utilized in many different types of industries.  The unique properties of dry ice make it an ideal cleaning solution in many commercial and manufacturing settings.
</p><p>Dry ice blasting can clean numerous objects with differing, complex geometries at once, which is why cleaning <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastic</a> and rubber molds is a main application for the technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  Dry ice replaces traditional cleaning methods that rely on manual scrubbing and the use of chemical cleaning agents. Dry ice blasting cleans the molds in-place at operating temperature, which eliminates the need to shut production down for cleaning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Dry ice blasting can be used to clean <a href="/wiki/Food_processing" title="Food processing">food processing</a> equipment<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Food_Standards_Agency" title="Food Standards Agency">Food Standards Agency</a> documented the process to effectively decontaminate surfaces of <i><a href="/wiki/Salmonella_enterica_subsp._enterica" title="Salmonella enterica subsp. enterica">Salmonella enteritidis</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">E. coli</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Listeria_monocytogenes" title="Listeria monocytogenes">Listeria monocytogenes</a></i> such that these microorganisms are not detectable using conventional microbiological methods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> It may also be used to clean some equipment without disassembly and without producing fire or electrical hazards. The EPA recommends dry ice blasting as an alternative to many types of solvent-based cleaning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>The cleaning process is used for disaster remediation including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mold_remediation" title="Mold remediation">mold</a>, smoke, fire, and water damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Due to the nonabrasive nature of dry ice and the absence of secondary waste from the cleaning process, dry ice blasting is used in conservation and <a href="/wiki/Historic_preservation" title="Historic preservation">historical preservation</a> projects.  The cleaning process was used in the conservation of the <a href="/wiki/USS_Monitor" title="USS Monitor">USS Monitor</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Museum_of_Art" title="Philadelphia Museum of Art">Philadelphia Museum of Art</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Due to the blast media sublimating without residue, dry ice blasting finds use in the <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor_device_fabrication" title="Semiconductor device fabrication">semiconductor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aerospace" title="Aerospace">aerospace</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Medical_device_manufacturing" title="Medical device manufacturing">medical device manufacturing</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> industries.
</p><p>The cleaning process is also used in other manufacturing settings, such as cleaning production equipment on automated <a href="/wiki/Welding" title="Welding">weld</a> lines,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> cleaning <a href="/wiki/Composite_material" title="Composite material">composite</a> tooling,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> cleaning industrial <a href="/wiki/Printing_press" title="Printing press">printing presses</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> cleaning molds and equipment used in <a href="/wiki/Foundry" title="Foundry">foundries</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and to clean equipment and tooling in onshore and offshore environments in the oil and gas industry.
</p><p>Dry ice blasting is also used to <a href="/wiki/Burr_(edge)" title="Burr (edge)">deburr</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flash_(manufacturing)" title="Flash (manufacturing)">deflash</a> parts<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and surface preparation prior to painting.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dry-ice_blasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carbon dioxide is increasingly toxic starting at concentrations above 1%,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and can also displace <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> resulting in <a href="/wiki/Asphyxia" title="Asphyxia">asphyxia</a> if equipment is not used in a ventilated area. In addition, because carbon dioxide is heavier than air, exhaust vents are required to be at or near ground level to efficiently remove the gas. At normal pressure dry ice is −78 °C (−108 °F) and must be handled with insulated gloves. Eye and ear protection are required to safely use dry ice cleaning equipment.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dry-ice_blasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is believed the US Navy, in 1945, were the first to experiment with dry ice blasting.  They were interested in using the technology for various degreasing applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1959, Unilever filed a patent for using dry ice blasting (or water-ice blasting, or some combination of the two) as a method of removing meat from bone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1971, Chemotronics International Inc. filed a patent for using dry ice blasting for the purposes of deburring and deflashing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>A patent for dry ice blasting was filed by <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">Lockheed Martin</a> in 1974.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first patents regarding development and design of modern-day single-hose dry ice blasting technology were awarded to David Moore of Cold Jet, LLC in 1986, 1988 (<span><a class="external text" href="//www.google.com/patents/US4617064" rel="nofollow">U.S. Patent 4,617,064</a></span> and <span><a class="external text" href="//www.google.com/patents/US4744181" rel="nofollow">U.S. Patent 4,744,181</a></span>).
</p>
