<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Poultry</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Poultry_(disambiguation)" title="Poultry (disambiguation)">Poultry (disambiguation)</a>.</div>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Poultry_of_the_world.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="7104" data-file-width="5749" height="371" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Poultry_of_the_world.jpg/300px-Poultry_of_the_world.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Poultry_of_the_world.jpg/450px-Poultry_of_the_world.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Poultry_of_the_world.jpg/600px-Poultry_of_the_world.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Poultry_of_the_world.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Poultry of the World</div></div></div>
<p><b>Poultry</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="'r' in 'rye'">r</span><span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span>/</a></span></span>) are <a href="/wiki/Domestication" title="Domestication">domesticated</a> <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> kept by humans for their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_(food)" title="Egg (food)">eggs</a>, their <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">meat</a> or their <a href="/wiki/Feather" title="Feather">feathers</a>. These birds are most typically members of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superorder_(biology)" title="Superorder (biology)">superorder</a> <a href="/wiki/Fowl" title="Fowl">Galloanserae (fowl)</a>, especially the order <a href="/wiki/Galliformes" title="Galliformes">Galliformes</a> (which includes <a href="/wiki/Chicken" title="Chicken">chickens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quail" title="Quail">quails</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Domestic_turkey" title="Domestic turkey">turkeys</a>).
</p><p>Poultry also includes other birds that are killed for their meat, such as the young of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pigeon" title="Pigeon">pigeons</a> (known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Squab_(food)" title="Squab (food)">squabs</a>) but does not include similar wild birds hunted for sport or food and known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Game_(food)" title="Game (food)">game</a>. The word "poultry" comes from the French/Norman word <i>poule</i>, itself derived from the Latin word <i>pullus</i>, which means small animal.
</p><p>The domestication of poultry took place several thousand years ago. This may have originally been as a result of people hatching and rearing young birds from eggs collected from the wild, but later involved keeping the birds permanently in captivity. Domesticated chickens may have been used for <a href="/wiki/Cockfight" title="Cockfight">cockfighting</a> at first and quail kept for their songs, but soon it was realised how useful it was having a captive-bred source of food. 
</p><p>Selective breeding for fast growth, egg-laying ability, conformation, plumage and docility took place over the centuries, and modern <a href="/wiki/Breed" title="Breed">breeds</a> often look very different from their wild ancestors. Although some birds are still kept in small flocks in extensive systems, most birds available in the market today are reared in intensive commercial enterprises. 
</p><p>Together with pig meat, poultry is one of the two most widely eaten types of meat globally, with over 70% of the meat supply in 2012 between them;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> poultry provides nutritionally beneficial food containing high-quality protein accompanied by a low proportion of fat. All poultry meat should be properly handled and sufficiently cooked in order to reduce the risk of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_poisoning" title="Food poisoning">food poisoning</a>.
</p><p>The word "poultry" comes from the Middle English "pultrie", from Old French <i>pouletrie</i>, from <i>pouletier</i>, poultry dealer, from <i>poulet</i>, pullet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AH_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-AH-2">[2]</a></sup> The word "pullet" itself comes from Middle English <i>pulet</i>, from Old French <i>polet</i>, both from Latin <i>pullus</i>, a young fowl, young animal or chicken.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The word "fowl" is of Germanic origin (cf. Old English <i>Fugol</i>, German <i>Vogel</i>, Danish <i>Fugl</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Poultry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Poultry" is a term used for any kind of <a href="/wiki/Domestication" title="Domestication">domesticated</a> bird, captive-raised for its utility, and traditionally the word has been used to refer to wildfowl (<a href="/wiki/Galliformes" title="Galliformes">Galliformes</a>) and waterfowl (<a href="/wiki/Anseriformes" title="Anseriformes">Anseriformes</a>) but not to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cagebirds" title="Cagebirds">cagebirds</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Songbirds" title="Songbirds">songbirds</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parrots" title="Parrots">parrots</a>. "Poultry" can be defined as domestic fowls, including chickens, turkeys, geese and ducks, raised for the production of meat or eggs and the word is also used for the flesh of these birds used as food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AH_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-AH-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Encyclopædia Britannica lists the same bird groups but also includes guinea fowl and squabs (young pigeons).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-6">[6]</a></sup> In R. D. Crawford's <i>Poultry breeding and genetics</i>, squabs are omitted but <a href="/wiki/Japanese_quail" title="Japanese quail">Japanese quail</a> and <a href="/wiki/Common_pheasant" title="Common pheasant">common pheasant</a> are added to the list, the latter frequently being bred in captivity and released into the wild.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crawford_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crawford-7">[7]</a></sup> In his 1848 classic book on poultry, <i>Ornamental and Domestic Poultry: Their History, and Management</i>, Edmund Dixon included chapters on the <a href="/wiki/Peafowl" title="Peafowl">peafowl</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guinea_fowl" title="Guinea fowl">guinea fowl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mute_swan" title="Mute swan">mute swan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey_(bird)" title="Turkey (bird)">turkey</a>, various types of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geese" title="Geese">geese</a>, the muscovy duck, other ducks and all types of chickens including bantams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>In colloquial speech, the term "fowl" is often used near-synonymously with "domesticated chicken" (<i>Gallus gallus</i>), or with "poultry" or even just "bird", and many languages do not distinguish between "poultry" and "fowl". Both words are also used for the flesh of these birds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Poultry can be distinguished from "game", defined as wild birds or mammals hunted for food or sport, a word also used to describe the flesh of these when eaten.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chickens">Chickens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Poultry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chickens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chicken" title="Chicken">Chicken</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rooster_portrait2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="1334" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Rooster_portrait2.jpg/170px-Rooster_portrait2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Rooster_portrait2.jpg/255px-Rooster_portrait2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Rooster_portrait2.jpg/340px-Rooster_portrait2.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rooster_portrait2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cock with comb and wattles</div></div></div>
<p>Chickens are medium-sized, chunky birds with an upright stance and characterised by fleshy red <a href="/wiki/Comb_(anatomy)" title="Comb (anatomy)">combs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wattle_(anatomy)" title="Wattle (anatomy)">wattles</a> on their heads. Males, known as cocks, are usually larger, more boldly coloured, and have more exaggerated plumage than females (hens). Chickens are gregarious, <a href="/wiki/Omnivore" title="Omnivore">omnivorous</a>, ground-dwelling birds that in their natural surroundings search among the leaf litter for seeds, invertebrates, and other small animals. They seldom fly except as a result of perceived danger, preferring to run into the undergrowth if approached.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Card_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Card-11">[11]</a></sup> Today's domestic chicken (<i>Gallus gallus domesticus</i>) is mainly descended from the wild <a href="/wiki/Red_junglefowl" title="Red junglefowl">red junglefowl</a> of Asia, with some additional input from  <a href="/wiki/Grey_junglefowl" title="Grey junglefowl">grey junglefowl</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eriksson_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eriksson-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Domestication" title="Domestication">Domestication</a> is believed to have taken place between 7,000 and 10,000 years ago, and what are thought to be fossilized chicken bones have been found in northeastern China dated to around 5,400 BC. Archaeologists believe domestication was originally for the purpose of <a href="/wiki/Cockfight" title="Cockfight">cockfighting</a>, the male bird being a doughty fighter. By 4,000 years ago, chickens seem to have reached the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indus_Valley" title="Indus Valley">Indus Valley</a> and 250 years later, they arrived in Egypt. They were still used for fighting and were regarded as symbols of fertility. The Romans used them in <a href="/wiki/Divination" title="Divination">divination</a>, and the Egyptians made a breakthrough when they learned the difficult technique of artificial <a href="/wiki/Egg_incubation" title="Egg incubation">incubation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Since then, the keeping of chickens has spread around the world for the production of food with the domestic fowl being a valuable source of both eggs and meat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since their domestication, a large number of breeds of chickens have been established, but with the exception of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leghorn_(chicken)" title="Leghorn (chicken)">white Leghorn</a>, most commercial birds are of <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrid</a> origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Card_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Card-11">[11]</a></sup> In about 1800, chickens began to be kept on a larger scale, and modern high-output poultry farms were present in the United Kingdom from around 1920 and became established in the United States soon after the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a>. By the mid-20th century, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poultry_industry" title="Poultry industry">poultry meat-producing industry</a> was of greater importance than the egg-laying industry. Poultry breeding has produced breeds and strains to fulfil different needs; light-framed, egg-laying birds that can produce 300 eggs a year; fast-growing, fleshy birds destined for consumption at a young age, and utility birds which produce both an acceptable number of eggs and a well-fleshed carcase. Male birds are unwanted in the egg-laying industry and can often be identified as soon as they are hatch for subsequent culling. In meat breeds, these birds are sometimes castrated (often chemically) to prevent aggression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-6">[6]</a></sup>  The resulting bird, called a <a href="/wiki/Capon" title="Capon">capon</a>, has more tender and flavorful meat, as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Basley_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Basley-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cockfight_Santangelo_MAN_Napoli.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2800" data-file-width="2800" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Cockfight_Santangelo_MAN_Napoli.jpg/220px-Cockfight_Santangelo_MAN_Napoli.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Cockfight_Santangelo_MAN_Napoli.jpg/330px-Cockfight_Santangelo_MAN_Napoli.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Cockfight_Santangelo_MAN_Napoli.jpg/440px-Cockfight_Santangelo_MAN_Napoli.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cockfight_Santangelo_MAN_Napoli.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Roman mosaic depicting a cockfight</div></div></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Bantam_(poultry)" title="Bantam (poultry)">bantam</a> is a small variety of domestic chicken, either a miniature version of a member of a standard <a href="/wiki/Breed" title="Breed">breed</a>, or a "true bantam" with no larger counterpart. The name derives from the town of <a href="/wiki/Banten_(town)" title="Banten (town)">Bantam</a> in Java<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> where European sailors bought the local small chickens for their shipboard supplies. Bantams may be a quarter to a third of the size of standard birds and lay similarly small eggs. They are kept by small-holders and hobbyists for egg production, use as broody hens, ornamental purposes, and showing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cockfighting">Cockfighting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Poultry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cockfighting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cockfight" title="Cockfight">Cockfighting</a> is said to be the world's oldest spectator sport and may have originated in Persia 6,000 years ago. Two mature males (cocks or roosters) are set to fight each other, and will do so with great vigour until one is critically injured or killed. Breeds such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asil_(chicken)" title="Asil (chicken)">Aseel</a> were developed in the Indian subcontinent for their aggressive behaviour. The sport formed part of the culture of the ancient Indians, Chinese, Greeks, and Romans, and large sums were won or lost depending on the outcome of an encounter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Cockfighting has been banned in many countries during the last century on the grounds of cruelty to animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Britannica_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ducks">Ducks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Poultry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ducks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Domestic_duck" title="Domestic duck">Domestic duck</a></div>
<p>Ducks are medium-sized aquatic birds with broad bills, eyes on the side of the head, fairly long necks, short legs set far back on the body, and webbed feet. Males, known as drakes, are often larger than females (simply known as ducks) and are differently coloured in some breeds. Domestic ducks are <a href="/wiki/Omnivore" title="Omnivore">omnivores</a>, eating a variety of animal and plant materials such as aquatic insects, molluscs, worms, small amphibians, waterweeds, and grasses. They feed in shallow water by dabbling, with their heads underwater and their tails upended. Most domestic ducks are too heavy to fly, and they are social birds, preferring to live and move around together in groups. They keep their plumage waterproof by preening, a process that spreads the secretions of the <a href="/wiki/Uropygial_gland" title="Uropygial gland">preen gland</a> over their feathers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSPCA_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-RSPCA-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pekin_Ducks_cropped.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="376" data-file-width="530" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Pekin_Ducks_cropped.jpg/220px-Pekin_Ducks_cropped.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Pekin_Ducks_cropped.jpg/330px-Pekin_Ducks_cropped.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Pekin_Ducks_cropped.jpg/440px-Pekin_Ducks_cropped.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pekin_Ducks_cropped.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Pekin ducks</div></div></div>
<p>Clay models of ducks found in China dating back to 4000 BC may indicate the domestication of ducks took place there during the <a href="/wiki/Yangshao_culture" title="Yangshao culture">Yangshao culture</a>. Even if this is not the case, domestication of the duck took place in the Far East at least 1500 years earlier than in the West. <a href="/wiki/Columella" title="Columella">Lucius Columella</a>, writing in the first century BC, advised those who sought to rear ducks to collect wildfowl eggs and put them under a broody hen, because when raised in this way, the ducks "lay aside their wild nature and without hesitation breed when shut up in the bird pen". Despite this, ducks did not appear in agricultural texts in Western Europe until about 810 AD, when they began to be mentioned alongside geese, chickens, and peafowl as being used for rental payments made by tenants to landowners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cherry_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cherry-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is widely agreed that the <a href="/wiki/Mallard" title="Mallard">mallard</a> (<i>Anas platyrhynchos</i>) is the ancestor of all breeds of domestic duck (with the exception of the <a href="/wiki/Muscovy_duck" title="Muscovy duck">Muscovy duck</a> (<i>Cairina moschata</i>), which is not closely related to other ducks).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cherry_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cherry-20">[20]</a></sup> Ducks are farmed mainly for their meat, eggs, and <a href="/wiki/Down_feather" title="Down feather">down</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dean_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dean-21">[21]</a></sup> As is the case with chickens, various breeds have been developed, selected for egg-laying ability, fast growth, and a well-covered carcase. The most common commercial breed in the United Kingdom and the United States is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Pekin_duck" title="American Pekin duck">Pekin duck</a>, which can lay 200 eggs a year and can reach a weight of 3.5 kg (7.7 lb) in 44 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSPCA_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-RSPCA-19">[19]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western world</a>, ducks are not as popular as chickens, because the latter produce larger quantities of white, lean meat and are easier to keep intensively, making the price of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicken_meat" title="Chicken meat">chicken meat</a> lower than that of duck meat. While popular in <i><a href="/wiki/Haute_cuisine" title="Haute cuisine">haute cuisine</a></i>, duck appears less frequently in the mass-market food industry. However, things are different in the East. Ducks are more popular there than chickens and are mostly still herded in the traditional way and selected for their ability to find sufficient food in harvested rice fields and other wet environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dean_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dean-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geese">Geese</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Poultry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Geese">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Domestic_Goose.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1714" data-file-width="1296" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Domestic_Goose.jpg/170px-Domestic_Goose.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Domestic_Goose.jpg/255px-Domestic_Goose.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Domestic_Goose.jpg/340px-Domestic_Goose.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Domestic_Goose.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An <a href="/wiki/Emden_goose" title="Emden goose">Emden goose</a>, a descendant of the wild <a href="/wiki/Greylag_goose" title="Greylag goose">greylag goose</a></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Greylag_goose" title="Greylag goose">greylag goose</a> (<i>Anser anser</i>) was domesticated by the Egyptians at least 3000 years ago,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faogeese_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-faogeese-22">[22]</a></sup> and a different wild species, the <a href="/wiki/Swan_goose" title="Swan goose">swan goose</a> (<i>Anser cygnoides</i>), domesticated in Siberia about a thousand years later, is known as a <a href="/wiki/Chinese_goose" title="Chinese goose">Chinese goose</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The two <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridise</a> with each other and the large knob at the base of the beak, a noticeable feature of the Chinese goose, is present to a varying extent in these hybrids. The hybrids are fertile and have resulted in several of the modern breeds. Despite their early domestication, geese have never gained the commercial importance of chickens and ducks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faogeese_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-faogeese-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Domestic geese are much larger than their wild counterparts and tend to have thick necks, an upright posture, and large bodies with broad rear ends. The greylag-derived birds are large and fleshy and used for meat, while the Chinese geese have smaller frames and are mainly used for egg production. The fine down of both is valued for use in pillows and padded garments. They forage on grass and weeds, supplementing this with small invertebrates, and one of the attractions of rearing geese is their ability to grow and thrive on a grass-based system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CALU_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-CALU-24">[24]</a></sup> They are very gregarious and have good memories and can be allowed to roam widely in the knowledge that they will return home by dusk. The Chinese goose is more aggressive and noisy than other geese and can be used as a guard animal to warn of intruders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faogeese_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-faogeese-22">[22]</a></sup> The flesh of meat geese is dark-coloured and high in protein, but they deposit fat subcutaneously, although this fat contains mostly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monounsaturated_fats" title="Monounsaturated fats">monounsaturated fatty acids</a>. The birds are killed either around 10 or about 24 weeks. Between these ages, problems with dressing the carcase occur because of the presence of developing <a href="/wiki/Pin_feather" title="Pin feather">pin feathers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CALU_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-CALU-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>In some countries, geese and ducks are force-fed to produce livers with an exceptionally high fat content for the production of <i><a href="/wiki/Foie_gras" title="Foie gras">foie gras</a></i>. Over 75% of world production of this product occurs in France, with lesser industries in Hungary and Bulgaria and a growing production in China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <i>Foie gras</i> is considered a luxury in many parts of the world, but the process of feeding the birds in this way is banned in many countries on animal welfare grounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Turkeys">Turkeys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Poultry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Turkeys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domesticated_turkey" title="Domesticated turkey">Domesticated turkey</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Turkeybird.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1728" data-file-width="2304" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Turkeybird.JPG/220px-Turkeybird.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Turkeybird.JPG/330px-Turkeybird.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Turkeybird.JPG/440px-Turkeybird.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Turkeybird.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Male domesticated turkey sexually displaying by showing the snood hanging over the beak, the caruncles hanging from the throat, and the 'beard' of small, black, stiff feathers on the chest</div></div></div>
<p>Turkeys are large birds, their nearest relatives being the pheasant and the guineafowl. Males are larger than females and have spreading, fan-shaped tails and distinctive, fleshy <a href="/wiki/Wattle_(anatomy)" title="Wattle (anatomy)">wattles</a>, called a <a href="/wiki/Snood_(anatomy)" title="Snood (anatomy)">snood</a>, that hang from the top of the beak and are used in courtship display. Wild turkeys can fly, but seldom do so, preferring to run with a long, stratling gait. They roost in trees and forage on the ground, feeding on seeds, nuts, berries, grass, foliage, invertebrates, lizards, and small snakes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>The modern domesticated turkey is descended from one of six <a href="/wiki/Subspecies" title="Subspecies">subspecies</a> of <a href="/wiki/Wild_turkey" title="Wild turkey">wild turkey</a> (<i>Meleagris gallopavo</i>) found in the present Mexican states of <a href="/wiki/Jalisco" title="Jalisco">Jalisco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guerrero" title="Guerrero">Guerrero</a> and <a href="/wiki/Veracruz" title="Veracruz">Veracruz</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>  Pre-Aztec tribes in south-central Mexico first domesticated the bird around 800 BC, and Pueblo Indians inhabiting the Colorado Plateau in the United States did likewise around 200 BC. They used the feathers for robes, blankets, and ceremonial purposes.  More than 1,000 years later, they became an important food source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>  The first Europeans to encounter the bird misidentified it as a guineafowl, a bird known as a "turkey fowl" at that time because it had been introduced into Europe via Turkey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Commercial turkeys are usually reared indoors under controlled conditions. These are often large buildings, purpose-built to provide ventilation and low light intensities (this reduces the birds' activity and thereby increases the rate of weight gain).  The lights can be switched on for 24-hrs/day, or a range of step-wise light regimens to encourage the birds to feed often and therefore grow rapidly. Females achieve slaughter weight at about 15 weeks of age and males at about 19. Mature commercial birds may be twice as heavy as their wild counterparts. Many different breeds have been developed, but the majority of commercial birds are white, as this improves the appearance of the dressed carcass, the <a href="/wiki/Pin_feather" title="Pin feather">pin feathers</a> being less visible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Turkeys were at one time mainly consumed on special occasions such as <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a> (10 million birds in the United Kingdom) or <a href="/wiki/Thanksgiving" title="Thanksgiving">Thanksgiving</a> (60 million birds in the United States). However, they are increasingly becoming part of the everyday diet in many parts of the world.
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quail">Quail</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Poultry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Quail">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Japanese_Quail.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2193" data-file-width="1776" height="210" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Japanese_Quail.jpg/170px-Japanese_Quail.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Japanese_Quail.jpg/255px-Japanese_Quail.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Japanese_Quail.jpg/340px-Japanese_Quail.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Japanese_Quail.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Japanese quail</div></div></div>
<p>The quail is a small to medium-sized, cryptically coloured bird. In its natural environment, it is found in bushy places, in rough grassland, among agricultural crops, and in other places with dense cover. It feeds on seeds, insects, and other small invertebrates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ILAR_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-ILAR-32">[32]</a></sup> Being a largely ground-dwelling, gregarious bird, domestication of the quail was not difficult, although many of its wild instincts are retained in captivity. It was known to the Egyptians long before the arrival of chickens and was depicted in <a href="/wiki/Hieroglyph" title="Hieroglyph">hieroglyphs</a> from 2575 BC. It <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrated</a> across Egypt in vast flocks and the birds could sometimes be picked up off the ground by hand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> These were the <a href="/wiki/Common_quail" title="Common quail">common quail</a> (<i>Coturnix coturnix</i>), but modern domesticated flocks are mostly of <a href="/wiki/Japanese_quail" title="Japanese quail">Japanese quail</a> (<i>Coturnix japonica</i>) which was probably domesticated as early as the 11th century AD in Japan. They were originally kept as songbirds, and they are thought to have been regularly used in song contests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mills_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mills-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the early 20th century, Japanese breeders began to selectively breed for increased egg production. By 1940, the quail egg industry was flourishing, but the events of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> led to the complete loss of quail lines bred for their song type, as well as almost all of those bred for egg production. After the war, the few surviving domesticated quail were used to rebuild the industry, and all current commercial and laboratory lines are considered to have originated from this population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mills_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mills-34">[34]</a></sup> Modern birds can lay upward of 300 eggs a year and countries such as Japan, India, China, Italy, Russia, and the United States have established commercial Japanese quail farming industries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hubrecht_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hubrecht-35">[35]</a></sup> Japanese quail are also used in biomedical research in fields such as <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Embryology" title="Embryology">embryology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nutrition" title="Nutrition">nutrition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">physiology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pathology" title="Pathology">pathology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxicity</a> studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> These quail are closely related to the common quail, and many young <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrid</a> birds are released into the wild each year to replenish dwindling wild populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_poultry">Other poultry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Poultry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other poultry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Guinea fowl originated in southern Africa, and the species most often kept as poultry is the <a href="/wiki/Helmeted_guineafowl" title="Helmeted guineafowl">helmeted guineafowl</a> (<i>Numida meleagris</i>). It is a medium-sized grey or speckled bird with a small naked head with colourful wattles and a knob on top, and was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domesticated_guineafowl" title="Domesticated guineafowl">domesticated</a> by the time of the ancient Greeks and Romans. Guinea fowl are hardy, sociable birds that subsist mainly on insects, but also consume grasses and seeds. They will keep a vegetable garden clear of pests and will eat the ticks that carry <a href="/wiki/Lyme_disease" title="Lyme disease">Lyme disease</a>. They happily roost in trees and give a loud vocal warning of the approach of predators. Their flesh and eggs can be eaten in the same way as chickens, young birds being ready for the table at the age of about four months.
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Squab_(food)" title="Squab (food)">squab</a> is the name given to the young of domestic pigeons that are destined for the table. Like other domesticated pigeons, birds used for this purpose are descended from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rock_pigeon" title="Rock pigeon">rock pigeon</a> (<i>Columba livia</i>). Special <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Utility_pigeon" title="Utility pigeon">utility breeds</a> with desirable characteristics are used. Two eggs are laid and incubated for about 17 days. When they hatch, the squabs are fed by both parents on "pigeon's milk", a thick secretion high in protein produced by the <a href="/wiki/Crop_(anatomy)" title="Crop (anatomy)">crop</a>. Squabs grow rapidly, but are slow to fledge and are ready to leave the nest at 26 to 30 days weighing about 500 g (18 oz). By this time, the adult pigeons will have laid and be incubating another pair of eggs and a prolific pair should produce two squabs every four weeks during a  breeding season lasting several months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Poultry_farming">Poultry farming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Poultry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Poultry farming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Poultry_farming" title="Poultry farming">Poultry farming</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Duck_farm_in_Hainan_02.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Duck_farm_in_Hainan_02.jpg/220px-Duck_farm_in_Hainan_02.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Duck_farm_in_Hainan_02.jpg/330px-Duck_farm_in_Hainan_02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Duck_farm_in_Hainan_02.jpg/440px-Duck_farm_in_Hainan_02.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Duck_farm_in_Hainan_02.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Free-range ducks in Hainan Province, China</div></div></div>
<p>Worldwide, more chickens are kept than any other type of poultry, with over 50 billion birds being raised each year as a source of meat and eggs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Compassion_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Compassion-40">[40]</a></sup> Traditionally, such birds would have been kept extensively in small flocks, foraging during the day and housed at night. This is still the case in developing countries, where the women often make important contributions to family livelihoods through keeping poultry. However, rising world populations and urbanization have led to the bulk of production being in larger, more intensive specialist units. These are often situated close to where the feed is grown or near to where the meat is needed, and result in cheap, safe food being made available for urban communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Profitability of production depends very much on the price of feed, which has been rising. High feed costs could limit further development of poultry production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free-range" title="Free-range">free-range</a> husbandry, the birds can roam freely outdoors for at least part of the day. Often, this is in large enclosures, but the birds have access to natural conditions and can exhibit their normal behaviours. A more intensive system is <a href="/wiki/Yarding" title="Yarding">yarding</a>, in which the birds have access to a fenced yard and <a href="/wiki/Chicken_coop" title="Chicken coop">poultry house</a> at a higher stocking rate. Poultry can also be kept in a barn system, with no access to the open air,  but with the ability to move around freely inside the building. The most intensive system for egg-laying chickens is battery cages, often set in multiple tiers. In these, several birds share a small cage which restricts their ability to move around and behave in a normal manner. The eggs are laid on the floor of the cage and roll into troughs outside for ease of collection. Battery cages for hens have been illegal in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">EU</a> since January 1, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Compassion_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-Compassion-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>Chickens raised intensively for their meat are known as "broilers". Breeds have been developed that can grow to an acceptable carcass size (2 kg (4.4 lb)) in six weeks or less.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Browne_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Browne-43">[43]</a></sup>  Broilers grow so fast, their legs cannot always support their weight and their hearts and respiratory systems may not be able to supply enough oxygen to their developing muscles. Mortality rates at 1% are much higher than for less-intensively reared laying birds which take 18 weeks to reach similar weights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Browne_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Browne-43">[43]</a></sup> Processing the birds is done automatically with conveyor-belt efficiency. They are hung by their feet, stunned, killed, bled, scalded, plucked, have their heads and feet removed, eviscerated, washed, chilled, drained, weighed, and packed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> all within the course of little over two hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Browne_43-2"><a href="#cite_note-Browne-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>Both intensive and free-range farming have animal welfare concerns. In intensive systems, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cannibalism_(poultry)" title="Cannibalism (poultry)">cannibalism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Feather_pecking" title="Feather pecking">feather pecking</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vent_pecking" title="Vent pecking">vent pecking</a> can be common, with some farmers using <a href="/wiki/Debeaking" title="Debeaking">beak trimming</a> as a preventative measure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherwin_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sherwin-45">[45]</a></sup> Diseases can also be common and spread rapidly through the flock. In extensive systems, the birds are exposed to adverse weather conditions and are vulnerable to predators and disease-carrying wild birds. Barn systems have been found to have the worst bird welfare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sherwin_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sherwin-45">[45]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, a lack of disease control in free-range farming has been associated with outbreaks of <a href="/wiki/Avian_influenza" title="Avian influenza">avian influenza</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSPA_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-WSPA-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Poultry_shows">Poultry shows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Poultry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Poultry shows">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In many countries, national and regional poultry shows are held where enthusiasts exhibit their birds which are judged on certain <a href="/wiki/Phenotype" title="Phenotype">phenotypical</a> <a href="/wiki/Breed" title="Breed">breed</a> traits as specified by their respective <a href="/wiki/Breed_standard" title="Breed standard">breed standards</a>. The idea of poultry exhibition may have originated after cockfighting was made illegal, as a way of maintaining a competitive element in poultry husbandry. Breed standards were drawn up for egg-laying, meat-type, and purely ornamental birds, aiming for uniformity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Sometimes, poultry shows are part of general <a href="/wiki/Livestock_show" title="Livestock show">livestock shows</a>, and sometimes they are separate events such as the annual "National Championship Show" in the United Kingdom organised by the <a href="/wiki/Poultry_Club_of_Great_Britain" title="Poultry Club of Great Britain">Poultry Club of Great Britain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Poultry_as_food">Poultry as food</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Poultry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Poultry as food">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domesticated_turkey" title="Domesticated turkey">Domesticated turkey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chicken_as_food" title="Chicken as food">Chicken as food</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duck_as_food" title="Duck as food">Duck as food</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Squab_as_food" title="Squab as food">Squab as food</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trade">Trade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Poultry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Trade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HK_Central_%E7%B5%90%E5%BF%97%E8%A1%97_Gage_Street_market_%E9%9B%9E%E8%9B%8B_Chicken_n_%E9%B4%A8%E8%9B%8B_Duck_Eggs_on_sale_March-2012.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1936" data-file-width="2592" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/HK_Central_%E7%B5%90%E5%BF%97%E8%A1%97_Gage_Street_market_%E9%9B%9E%E8%9B%8B_Chicken_n_%E9%B4%A8%E8%9B%8B_Duck_Eggs_on_sale_March-2012.jpg/220px-HK_Central_%E7%B5%90%E5%BF%97%E8%A1%97_Gage_Street_market_%E9%9B%9E%E8%9B%8B_Chicken_n_%E9%B4%A8%E8%9B%8B_Duck_Eggs_on_sale_March-2012.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/HK_Central_%E7%B5%90%E5%BF%97%E8%A1%97_Gage_Street_market_%E9%9B%9E%E8%9B%8B_Chicken_n_%E9%B4%A8%E8%9B%8B_Duck_Eggs_on_sale_March-2012.jpg/330px-HK_Central_%E7%B5%90%E5%BF%97%E8%A1%97_Gage_Street_market_%E9%9B%9E%E8%9B%8B_Chicken_n_%E9%B4%A8%E8%9B%8B_Duck_Eggs_on_sale_March-2012.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/HK_Central_%E7%B5%90%E5%BF%97%E8%A1%97_Gage_Street_market_%E9%9B%9E%E8%9B%8B_Chicken_n_%E9%B4%A8%E8%9B%8B_Duck_Eggs_on_sale_March-2012.jpg/440px-HK_Central_%E7%B5%90%E5%BF%97%E8%A1%97_Gage_Street_market_%E9%9B%9E%E8%9B%8B_Chicken_n_%E9%B4%A8%E8%9B%8B_Duck_Eggs_on_sale_March-2012.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HK_Central_%E7%B5%90%E5%BF%97%E8%A1%97_Gage_Street_market_%E9%9B%9E%E8%9B%8B_Chicken_n_%E9%B4%A8%E8%9B%8B_Duck_Eggs_on_sale_March-2012.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chicken and duck eggs on sale in Hong Kong</div></div></div>
<p>Poultry is the second most widely eaten type of meat in the world, accounting for about 30% of total meat production worldwide compared to <a href="/wiki/Pork" title="Pork">pork</a> at 38%. Sixteen billion birds are raised annually for consumption, more than half of these in industrialised, factory-like production units.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciencenews.org_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-sciencenews.org-49">[49]</a></sup> Global broiler meat production rose to 84.6 million tonnes in 2013. The largest producers were the United States (20%), China (16.6%), Brazil (15.1%) and the European Union (11.3%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> There are two distinct models of production; the European Union <a href="/wiki/Supply_chain" title="Supply chain">supply chain</a> model seeks to supply products which can be traced back to the farm of origin. This model faces the increasing costs of implementing additional food safety requirements, welfare issues and environmental regulations. In contrast, the United States model turns the product into a commodity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>World production of duck meat was about 4.2 million tonnes in 2011 with China producing two thirds of the total,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DuckGoose_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-DuckGoose-52">[52]</a></sup>  some 1.7 billion birds. Other notable duck-producing countries in the Far East include Vietnam, Thailand, Malaysia, Myanmar, Indonesia and South Korea (12% in total). France (3.5%) is the largest producer in the West, followed by other EU nations (3%) and North America (1.7%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cherry_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cherry-20">[20]</a></sup> China was also by far the largest producer of goose and guinea fowl meat, with a 94% share of the 2.6 million tonne global market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DuckGoose_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-DuckGoose-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>Global egg production was expected to reach 65.5 million tonnes in 2013, surpassing all previous years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trend_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trend-53">[53]</a></sup> Between 2000 and 2010, egg production was growing globally at around 2% per year, but since then growth has slowed down to nearer 1%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trend_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-Trend-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cuts_of_poultry">Cuts of poultry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Poultry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Cuts of poultry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MIN_Rungis_volaille.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2584" data-file-width="3736" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/MIN_Rungis_volaille.jpg/220px-MIN_Rungis_volaille.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/MIN_Rungis_volaille.jpg/330px-MIN_Rungis_volaille.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/MIN_Rungis_volaille.jpg/440px-MIN_Rungis_volaille.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MIN_Rungis_volaille.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In the poultry pavilion of the  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/March%C3%A9_d%27Int%C3%A9r%C3%AAt_National_de_Rungis" title="Marché d'Intérêt National de Rungis">Rungis International Market</a>, France</div></div></div>
<p>Poultry is available fresh or frozen, as whole birds or as joints (cuts), bone-in or deboned, seasoned in various ways, raw or ready cooked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FeedCo_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-FeedCo-54">[54]</a></sup> The meatiest parts of a bird are the <a href="/wiki/Flight" title="Flight">flight</a> <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">muscles</a> on its chest, called "breast" meat, and the <a href="/wiki/Walking" title="Walking">walking</a> muscles on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leg_(anatomy)" title="Leg (anatomy)">legs</a>, called the "thigh" and "drumstick". The wings are also eaten (<a href="/wiki/Buffalo_wing" title="Buffalo wing">Buffalo wings</a> are a popular example in the United States) and may be split into three segments, the meatier "drumette", the "wingette" (also called the "flat"), and the wing tip (also called the "flapper").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FeedCo_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-FeedCo-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> In Japan, the wing is frequently separated, and these parts are referred to as 手羽元 (<i>teba-moto</i> "wing base") and 手羽先 (<i>teba-saki</i> "wing tip").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>Dark meat, which avian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myologist" title="Myologist">myologists</a> refer to as "red muscle", is used for sustained activity—chiefly walking, in the case of a chicken. The dark colour comes from the protein <a href="/wiki/Myoglobin" title="Myoglobin">myoglobin</a>, which plays a key role in oxygen uptake and storage within cells. White muscle, in contrast, is suitable only for short bursts of activity such as, for chickens, flying. Thus, the chicken's leg and thigh meat are dark, while its breast meat (which makes up the primary flight muscles) is white. Other birds with breast muscle more suitable for sustained flight, such as ducks and geese, have red muscle (and therefore dark meat) throughout.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>  Some cuts of meat including poultry expose the microscopic regular structure of intracellular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myofibrils" title="Myofibrils">muscle fibrils</a> which can <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">diffract</a> light and produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iridescent" title="Iridescent">iridescent</a> colours, an optical phenomenon sometimes called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Structural_colouration" title="Structural colouration">structural colouration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foods_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-foods-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Health_and_disease_.28humans.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_and_disease_(humans)">Health and disease (humans)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Poultry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Health and disease (humans)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LemonChicken2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="396" data-file-width="640" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/LemonChicken2.jpg/220px-LemonChicken2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/LemonChicken2.jpg/330px-LemonChicken2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/LemonChicken2.jpg/440px-LemonChicken2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LemonChicken2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cuts from plucked <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicken_(food)" title="Chicken (food)">chickens</a></div></div></div>
<p>Poultry meat and eggs provide nutritionally beneficial food containing protein of high quality. This is accompanied by low levels of fat which have a favourable mix of fatty acids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAOhealth_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAOhealth-59">[59]</a></sup> Chicken meat contains about two to three times as much <a href="/wiki/Polyunsaturated_fat" title="Polyunsaturated fat">polyunsaturated fat</a> as most types of <a href="/wiki/Red_meat" title="Red meat">red meat</a> when measured by weight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> However, for boneless, skinless chicken breast, the amount is much lower. A 100-g serving of baked chicken breast contains 4 g of fat and 31 g of protein, compared to 10 g of fat and 27 g of protein for the same portion of broiled, lean skirt steak.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2011 study by the <a href="/wiki/Translational_Genomics_Research_Institute" title="Translational Genomics Research Institute">Translational Genomics Research Institute</a> showed that 47% of the <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">meat</a> and poultry sold in <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> grocery stores was contaminated with <i><a href="/wiki/Staphylococcus_aureus" title="Staphylococcus aureus">Staphylococcus aureus</a></i>, and 52% of the bacteria concerned showed resistance to at least three groups of antibiotics. Thorough cooking of the product would kill these bacteria, but a risk of cross-contamination from improper handling of the raw product is still present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>  Also, some risk is present for consumers of poultry meat and eggs to bacterial infections such as <i><a href="/wiki/Salmonella" title="Salmonella">Salmonella</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Campylobacter" title="Campylobacter">Campylobacter</a></i>. Poultry products may become contaminated by these bacteria during handling, processing, marketing, or storage, resulting in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food-borne_illness" title="Food-borne illness">food-borne illness</a> if the product is improperly cooked or handled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAOhealth_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-FAOhealth-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>In general, <a href="/wiki/Avian_influenza" title="Avian influenza">avian influenza</a> is a disease of birds caused by bird-specific <a href="/wiki/Influenza_A_virus" title="Influenza A virus">influenza A virus</a> that is not normally transferred to people; however, people in contact with live poultry are at the greatest risk of becoming infected with the virus and this is of particular concern in areas such as Southeast Asia, where the disease is <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> in the wild bird population and domestic poultry can become infected. The virus possibly could mutate to become highly virulent and infectious in humans and cause an <a href="/wiki/Influenza_pandemic" title="Influenza pandemic">influenza pandemic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p><p>Bacteria can be grown in the laboratory on nutrient culture media, but viruses need living cells in which to replicate. Many <a href="/wiki/Vaccine" title="Vaccine">vaccines</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infectious_disease" title="Infectious disease">infectious diseases</a> can be grown in fertilised chicken eggs. Millions of eggs are used each year to generate the annual flu vaccine requirements, a complex process that takes about six months after the decision is made as to what strains of virus to include in the new vaccine. A problem with using eggs for this purpose is that people with egg <a href="/wiki/Allergy" title="Allergy">allergies</a> are unable to be immunised, but this disadvantage may be overcome as new techniques for cell-based rather than egg-based culture become available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Cell-based culture will also be useful in a <a href="/wiki/Pandemic" title="Pandemic">pandemic</a> when it may be difficult to acquire a sufficiently large quantity of suitable sterile, fertile eggs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p>
