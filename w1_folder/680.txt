<p><b>Sulfur hexafluoride</b> (SF<sub>6</sub>) is an  <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic</a>, colorless, odorless, non-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flammable" title="Flammable">flammable</a>, extremely potent <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a>, and an excellent <a href="/wiki/Insulator_(electricity)" title="Insulator (electricity)">electrical insulator</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT61313_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT61313-4">[4]</a></sup> <span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span>  has an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Octahedral_geometry" title="Octahedral geometry">octahedral geometry</a>, consisting of six <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a> atoms attached to a central <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> atom. It is a <a href="/wiki/Hypervalent_molecule" title="Hypervalent molecule">hypervalent molecule</a>. Typical for a nonpolar gas, it is poorly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soluble" title="Soluble">soluble</a> in water but quite soluble in nonpolar organic solvents. It is generally transported as a <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_gas" title="Liquefied gas">liquefied compressed gas</a>. It has a density of 6.12 g/L at sea level conditions, considerably higher than the <a href="/wiki/Density_of_air" title="Density of air">density of air</a> (1.225 g/L).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis_and_reactions">Synthesis and reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_hexafluoride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Synthesis and reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> can be prepared from the elements through exposure of <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur"><span class="chemf nowrap">S<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">8</sub></span></span></a> to <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine"><span class="chemf nowrap">F<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span></a>. This was also the method used by the discoverers <a href="/wiki/Henri_Moissan" title="Henri Moissan">Henri Moissan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Lebeau" title="Paul Lebeau">Paul Lebeau</a> in 1901. Some other sulfur fluorides are cogenerated, but these are removed by heating the mixture to <a href="/wiki/Disproportionation" title="Disproportionation">disproportionate</a> any <a href="/wiki/Disulfur_decafluoride" title="Disulfur decafluoride"><span class="chemf nowrap">S<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>F<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">10</sub></span></span></a> (which is highly toxic) and then scrubbing the product with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">NaOH</a> to destroy remaining <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_tetrafluoride" title="Sulfur tetrafluoride"><span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span></a>.
</p><p>Alternatively, utilizing <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">bromine</a>, sulfur hexafluoride can be synthesized from SF<sub>4</sub> and CoF<sub>3</sub> at lower temperatures (e.g. 100 °C), as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2CoF<sub>3</sub>  +  SF<sub>4</sub>  +  (Br<sub>2</sub>)  →  SF<sub>6</sub>  +  2CoF<sub>2</sub>  +  (Br<sub>2</sub>)</dd></dl>
<p>There is virtually no reaction chemistry for <span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span>. A main contribution to the inertness of SF<sub>6</sub> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steric_hindrance" title="Steric hindrance">steric hindrance</a> of the sulfur atom, whereas its heavier group 16 counterparts, such as <a href="/wiki/Selenium_hexafluoride" title="Selenium hexafluoride">SeF<sub>6</sub></a> are more reactive than SF<sub>6</sub> as a result of less steric hindrance (See hydrolysis example).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> It does not react with molten <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> below its boiling point,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> but reacts <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermically</a> with <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_hexafluoride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>More than 10,000 tons of <span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> are produced per year, most  of which (over 8,000 tons) is used as a <a href="/wiki/Dielectric_gas" title="Dielectric gas">gaseous dielectric medium</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_power_industry" title="Electrical power industry">electrical industry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Other main uses include an <a href="/wiki/Inert_gas" title="Inert gas">inert gas</a> for the casting of <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>, and as an inert filling for <a href="/wiki/Insulated_glazing" title="Insulated glazing">insulated glazing</a> windows.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dielectric_medium">Dielectric medium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_hexafluoride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dielectric medium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span>  is used in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_power_industry" title="Electrical power industry">electrical industry</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Dielectric_gas" title="Dielectric gas">gaseous dielectric medium</a> for high-voltage <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_hexafluoride_circuit_breaker" title="Sulfur hexafluoride circuit breaker">circuit breakers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switchgear" title="Switchgear">switchgear</a>, and other <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electrical</a> equipment, often replacing oil filled circuit breakers (OCBs) that can contain harmful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PCBs" title="PCBs">PCBs</a>. <span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> gas under <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> is used as an insulator in <a href="/wiki/Switchgear" title="Switchgear">gas insulated switchgear</a> (GIS) because it has a much higher <a href="/wiki/Dielectric_strength" title="Dielectric strength">dielectric strength</a> than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a> or dry <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>. The high dielectric strength is a result of the gas's high <a href="/wiki/Electronegativity" title="Electronegativity">electronegativity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a>. This property makes it possible to significantly reduce the size of electrical gear. This makes GIS more suitable for certain purposes such as indoor placement, as opposed to air-insulated electrical gear, which takes up considerably more room. Gas-insulated electrical gear is also more resistant to the effects of pollution and climate, as well as being more reliable in long-term operation because of its controlled operating environment. Exposure to an arc chemically breaks down <span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> though most of the decomposition products tend to quickly re-form <span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span>, a process termed "self-healing".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_arc" title="Electrical arc">Arcing</a> or <a href="/wiki/Corona_discharge" title="Corona discharge">corona</a> can produce <a href="/wiki/Disulfur_decafluoride" title="Disulfur decafluoride">disulfur decafluoride</a> (<a href="/wiki/Disulfur_decafluoride" title="Disulfur decafluoride"><span class="chemf nowrap">S<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>F<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">10</sub></span></span></a>), a highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toxic" title="Toxic">toxic</a> gas, with toxicity similar to <a href="/wiki/Phosgene" title="Phosgene">phosgene</a>. <span class="chemf nowrap">S<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>F<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">10</sub></span></span> was considered a potential <a href="/wiki/Chemical_warfare" title="Chemical warfare">chemical warfare</a> agent in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> because it does not produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lacrimation" title="Lacrimation">lacrimation</a> or skin irritation, thus providing little warning of exposure.
</p><p><span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> is also commonly encountered as a high voltage dielectric in the high voltage supplies of <a href="/wiki/Particle_accelerator" title="Particle accelerator">particle accelerators</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Van_de_Graaff_generator" title="Van de Graaff generator">Van de Graaff generators</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pelletron" title="Pelletron">Pelletrons</a> and high voltage transmission <a href="/wiki/Electron_microscope" title="Electron microscope">electron microscopes</a>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_use">Medical use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_hexafluoride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Medical use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> is used to provide a <a href="/wiki/Tamponade" title="Tamponade">tamponade</a> or plug of a retinal hole in <a href="/wiki/Retinal_detachment" title="Retinal detachment">retinal detachment</a> repair operations<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> in the form of a gas bubble. It is inert in the <a href="/wiki/Vitreous_chamber" title="Vitreous chamber">vitreous chamber</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and initially doubles its volume in 36 hours before being absorbed in the blood in 10–14 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p><span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> is used as a contrast agent for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_ultrasonography" title="Medical ultrasonography">ultrasound</a> imaging. Sulfur hexafluoride microbubbles are administered in solution through injection into a peripheral vein. These microbubbles enhance the visibility of blood vessels to ultrasound. This application has been used to examine the vascularity of tumours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid18376462_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid18376462-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tracer_compound">Tracer compound</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_hexafluoride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tracer compound">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sulfur hexafluoride was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tracer_gas" title="Tracer gas">tracer gas</a> used in the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roadway_air_dispersion_model" title="Roadway air dispersion model">roadway air dispersion model</a> calibration; this research program was sponsored by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="U.S. Environmental Protection Agency">U.S. Environmental Protection Agency</a> and conducted in <a href="/wiki/Sunnyvale,_California" title="Sunnyvale, California">Sunnyvale, California</a> on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Highway_101" title="U.S. Highway 101">U.S. Highway 101</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Gaseous <span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> is used as a tracer gas in short-term experiments of <a href="/wiki/Ventilation_(architecture)" title="Ventilation (architecture)">ventilation</a> efficiency in buildings and indoor enclosures, and for determining <a href="/wiki/Infiltration_(HVAC)" title="Infiltration (HVAC)">infiltration</a> rates. Two major factors recommend its use: its concentration can be measured with satisfactory accuracy at very low concentrations, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a> has a negligible concentration of <span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span>.
</p><p>Sulfur hexafluoride was used as a non-toxic test gas in an experiment at <a href="/wiki/St._John%27s_Wood_tube_station" title="St. John's Wood tube station">St. John's Wood tube station</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, United Kingdom on 25 March 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The gas was released throughout the station, and monitored as it drifted around.  The purpose of the experiment, which had been announced earlier in March by the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Transport" title="Secretary of State for Transport">Secretary of State for Transport</a> <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Alexander" title="Douglas Alexander">Douglas Alexander</a>, was to investigate how toxic gas might spread throughout <a href="/wiki/London_Underground" title="London Underground">London Underground</a> stations and buildings during a terrorist attack.
</p><p>Sulfur hexafluoride is also routinely used as a tracer gas in laboratory fume hood containment testing.  The gas is used in the final stage of <a href="/wiki/ASHRAE" title="ASHRAE">ASHRAE</a> 110 fume hood qualification.  A plume of gas is generated inside of the fume hood and a battery of tests are performed while a gas analyzer arranged outside of the hood samples for SF<sub>6</sub> to verify the containment properties of the fume hood.
</p><p>It has been used successfully as a tracer in <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">oceanography</a> to study <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/diapycnal" title="wikt:diapycnal">diapycnal</a> mixing and air-sea gas exchange.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_hexafluoride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Mark_50_torpedo" title="Mark 50 torpedo">Mark 50 torpedo</a> closed <a href="/wiki/Rankine_cycle" title="Rankine cycle">Rankine-cycle</a> propulsion system is powered by sulfur hexafluoride in an <a href="/wiki/Exothermic_reaction" title="Exothermic reaction">exothermic reaction</a> with solid <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li><span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a> is also used in the <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> industry as an <a href="/wiki/Etching_(microfabrication)" title="Etching (microfabrication)">etchant</a>.  <span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> breaks down in the plasma into sulfur and fluorine, the fluorine plasma performing the etching.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> industry uses large amounts of <span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> as inert gas to fill casting forms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></li>
<li>Pressurizes <a href="/wiki/Waveguide" title="Waveguide">waveguides</a> in high-power <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">microwave</a> systems.  The gas insulates the waveguide, preventing internal arcing.</li>
<li>Has been used in <a href="/wiki/Electrostatic_loudspeaker#Dayton_Wright" title="Electrostatic loudspeaker">electrostatic loudspeakers</a> because of its high dielectric strength and high molecular weight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li>Was used to fill Nike Air bags in all of their shoes from 1992-2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feedstock" title="Feedstock">Feedstock</a> for production of the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_weapon" title="Chemical weapon">chemical weapon</a> <a href="/wiki/Disulfur_decafluoride" title="Disulfur decafluoride">disulfur decafluoride</a>.</li>
<li>For entertainment purposes, when breathed, <span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> causes the voice to become significantly deeper, due to its density being so much higher than air, as seen in this video, <a class="external text" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=A7XdOyZIkko" rel="nofollow">here</a>. This is related to the more well-known effect of breathing low-density <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>, which causes someone's voice to become much higher.  Both of these effects should only be attempted with caution as these gases displace <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> that the lungs are attempting to extract from the air. Sulfur hexafluoride is also mildly anesthetic. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li>
<li>For science demonstrations / magic as "invisible water" since a light foil boat can be floated in a tank, as will an air filled balloon</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenhouse_gas">Greenhouse gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_hexafluoride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Greenhouse gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mauna_Loa_Sulfur_Hexafluoride.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="700" data-file-width="1000" height="154" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Mauna_Loa_Sulfur_Hexafluoride.png/220px-Mauna_Loa_Sulfur_Hexafluoride.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Mauna_Loa_Sulfur_Hexafluoride.png/330px-Mauna_Loa_Sulfur_Hexafluoride.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Mauna_Loa_Sulfur_Hexafluoride.png/440px-Mauna_Loa_Sulfur_Hexafluoride.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mauna_Loa_Sulfur_Hexafluoride.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mauna Loa sulfur hexafluoride timeseries.</div></div></div>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a>, <span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> is the most potent <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> that it has evaluated, with a <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_potential" title="Global warming potential">global warming potential</a> of 23,900<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> times that of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide"><span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span></a> when compared over a 100-year period.  Measurements of SF<sub>6</sub> show that its global average mixing ratio has increased by about 0.2 ppt (<a href="/wiki/Parts-per_notation" title="Parts-per notation">parts per trillion</a>) per year to over 9 ppt as of February 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Sulfur hexafluoride is also extremely long-lived, is inert in the troposphere and stratosphere and has an estimated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_lifetime" title="Atmospheric lifetime">atmospheric lifetime</a> of 800–3200 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> <span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> is very stable (for countries reporting their emissions to the UNFCCC, a GWP of 23,900 for <span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> was suggested at the third Conference of the Parties: <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_potential#GWP_used_in_Kyoto_protocol" title="Global warming potential">GWP used in Kyoto protocol</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Average global SF<sub>6</sub> concentrations increased by about seven percent per year during the 1980s and 1990s, mostly as the result of its use in the magnesium production industry, and by electrical utilities and electronics manufacturers. Given the small amounts of SF<sub>6</sub> released compared to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, its overall contribution to global warming is estimated to be less than 0.2 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>In Europe, <span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> falls under the F-Gas directive which ban or control its use for several applications. Since 1 January 2006, <span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> is banned as a tracer gas and in all applications except <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-voltage_switchgear" title="High-voltage switchgear">high-voltage switchgear</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> It was reported in 2013 that a three-year effort by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">United States Department of Energy</a> to identify and fix leaks at its laboratories in the United States such as the <a href="/wiki/Princeton_Plasma_Physics_Laboratory" title="Princeton Plasma Physics Laboratory">Princeton Plasma Physics Laboratory</a>, where the gas is used as a high voltage insulator, had been productive, cutting annual leaks by 35,000 pounds. This was done by comparing purchases with inventory, assuming the difference was leaked, then locating and fixing the leaks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT61313_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT61313-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physiological_effects_and_precautions">Physiological effects and precautions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_hexafluoride&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Physiological effects and precautions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like xenon, sulfur hexafluoride is a non-toxic gas, yet by displacing oxygen in the lungs, it also carries the risk of <a href="/wiki/Asphyxia" title="Asphyxia">asphyxia</a> if too much is inhaled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Being more dense than air, if a substantial quantity of gas is released it will settle in low-lying areas and present a significant risk of asphyxiation if the area is entered. This is particularly relevant to its use as an insulator in electrical equipment where workers may be in trenches or pits below equipment containing <span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>As with all gases, the density of <span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> affects the resonance frequencies of the vocal tract, thus changing drastically the vocal sound qualities, or timbre, of those who inhale it. It does not affect the vibrations of the vocal folds. The density of sulfur hexafluoride is relatively high at room temperature and pressure due to the gas's large <a href="/wiki/Molar_mass" title="Molar mass">molar mass</a>. Unlike <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>, which has a molar mass of about 4 grams/mol and gives the voice a childish and a "<a href="/wiki/Alvin_and_the_Chipmunks" title="Alvin and the Chipmunks">chipmunk</a> like" quality, <span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> has a molar mass of about 146 g/mol, and the speed of sound through the gas is about 134 m/s at room temperature, giving the voice a "demonic" quality when <span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> is inhaled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIST_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-NIST-33">[33]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> For comparison, the molar mass of air, which is about 80% nitrogen and 20% oxygen, is approximately 30 g/mol which leads to a speed of sound of 343 m/s. Inhalation of <span class="chemf nowrap">SF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> causes a lowering of the <a href="/wiki/Timbre" title="Timbre">timbre</a>, or frequency of the formants, of the vocal tract, by contrast with inhalation of helium, which raises it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wolfe_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wolfe-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sulfur hexafluoride has an <a href="/wiki/Anesthetic" title="Anesthetic">anesthetic</a> potency slightly lower than <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
