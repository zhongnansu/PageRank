<p><b>Mississippi Power</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NYSE" title="NYSE">NYSE</a>: <a class="external text" href="https://www.nyse.com/about/listed/mpj.html/" rel="nofollow">MPJ</a>) is an investor-owned electric utility and a wholly owned subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Southern_Company" title="Southern Company">Southern Company</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Mississippi Power Company (MPC) is headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Gulfport,_Mississippi" title="Gulfport, Mississippi">Gulfport, Mississippi</a>.
</p><p>Mississippi Power has 1,253 employees and serves most of the cities, towns, and communities within the 23 counties of southeast Mississippi. The utility also serves six <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rural_Electrification_Administration" title="Rural Electrification Administration">Rural Electrification Administration</a>-financed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_cooperative" title="Electric cooperative">electric cooperatives</a>: Coast EPA (Electric Power Association), Singing River EPA, Southern Pine EPA, Dixie EPA, Pearl River EPA, and East Mississippi EPA - and one municipality, <a href="/wiki/Collins,_Mississippi" title="Collins, Mississippi">City of Collins</a>, with wholesale electric power which, in turn, they resell to customers in southeast Mississippi. At .20 cents per KwH they are one of the highest priced power companies in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mississippi_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mississippi Power Company was founded in 1925. In 1949, <a href="/wiki/Southern_Company" title="Southern Company">Southern Company</a> was established as a holding company for four utilities, one of which included Mississippi Power Company.
</p><p>Formerly known as Mississippi Power Company from 1925 to 1976, the company shortened to Mississippi Power, and has maintained that name ever since. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mississippi_Power_-_History_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mississippi_Power_-_History-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurricane_Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mississippi_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hurricane Katrina">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>August 29, 2005, Hurricane Katrina struck, taking down the company’s electric systems and leaving every single customer without service. With a team of 12,000 - employees and crews from every state and Canada - they were able to restore service to all who could receive it in only 12 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mississippi_Power_-_History_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mississippi_Power_-_History-4">[4]</a></sup> The severity of the storm has cautioned Mississippi Power with every future investment it has made. Most noticeably is the location of the <a href="/wiki/Kemper_Project" title="Kemper Project">Kemper Project</a>, which was purposefully selected to be comfortably located miles from the Gulf.  It is no wonder that the Kemper Project, officially named Plant Ratcliffe, was named after the 2005 Southern Company CEO, David Ratcliffe.
</p><p>in 2007, Mississippi Power teamed with the Mississippi Department of Wildlife and Fisheries to begin restocking the Pascagoula River after Hurricane Katrina’s massive fish kill by releasing more than 2,500 largemouth bass advanced fingerlings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mississippi_Power_-_History_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mississippi_Power_-_History-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transmission_and_distribution_facilities">Transmission and distribution facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mississippi_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Transmission and distribution facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mississippi Power maintains 147 substations, 2,118 miles of transmission lines, 4,213 miles of primary overhead lines and 560 miles of primary underground lines. Total generating capacity is 3,098,692 kW.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kemper_Project">Kemper Project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mississippi_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Kemper Project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mississippi Power is currently constructing the Kemper County energy facility, commonly shortened to the Kemper Project, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kemper_County" title="Kemper County">Kemper County</a>, Mississippi. Construction began in June 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sequestration.mit.edu_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-sequestration.mit.edu-7">[7]</a></sup> The Kemper Project was intended to use an integrated gasification combined cycle (IGCC) to convert <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a> coal to gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">Lignite</a>, an abundant natural resource in Mississippi and commonly referred to as "brown coal", is very low grade coal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> To meet increasing energy demands, Mississippi Power and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">Department of Energy</a> invested in technology to turn <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a> coal into a viable energy source.  The Kemper Project hoped to capture 65% of the carbon dioxide emissions, a byproduct of the chemical gasification process.  This technology is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_sequestration" title="Carbon capture and sequestration">Carbon Capture &amp; Sequestration (CCS)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Kemper Coal Plant was built by Mississippi Power in order to diverse its energy portfolio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> With the majority of its investments in <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> (a fuel with high price <a href="/wiki/Volatility_(finance)" title="Volatility (finance)">volatility</a>), they determined <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a> would be a better long-term fuel source than <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The plant missed all its targets and plans for "clean coal" generation were abandoned in July 2017. The plant is expected to go ahead burning natural gas only.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mississippi_Power_Supreme_Court_Ruling">Mississippi Power Supreme Court Ruling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mississippi_Power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Mississippi Power Supreme Court Ruling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In February 2015, the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Mississippi" title="Supreme Court of Mississippi">Supreme Court of Mississippi</a> found the agreement to raise rates during construction of the plant between the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Public_Service_Commission" title="Mississippi Public Service Commission">Mississippi Public Service Commission</a> (MPSC) and Mississippi Power to be unlawful. The Court cited the MPSC's failure to give proper notice to the public about the rate increase as one of the main reasons for the 5-4 ruling.  Mississippi Power disagrees with the ruling, and officially announced it plans to file for a rehearing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PowerMagSC_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-PowerMagSC-15">[15]</a></sup>  The original basis for the agreement between MPSC and Mississippi Power in March 2013 was a result of powers granted in the state's <a class="external text" href="http://billstatus.ls.state.ms.us/2008/pdf/history/SB/SB2793.xml" rel="nofollow">2008 Baseload Act</a>. This act allows public utilities to collect Construction Work in Progress (CWIP) funds to encourage long term investments by public utility companies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PowerMagSC_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-PowerMagSC-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
