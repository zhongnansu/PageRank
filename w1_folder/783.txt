<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Surface runoff</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Runoff.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="328" data-file-width="224" height="322" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Runoff.jpg/220px-Runoff.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b0/Runoff.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Runoff.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Runoff flowing into a <a href="/wiki/Storm_drain" title="Storm drain">stormwater drain</a></div></div></div>
<p><b>Surface runoff</b> (also known as <b>overland flow</b>) is the flow of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> that occurs when excess <a href="/wiki/Stormwater" title="Stormwater">stormwater</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meltwater" title="Meltwater">meltwater</a>, or other sources flows over the <a href="/wiki/Earth#Surface" title="Earth">Earth's surface</a>. This might occur because <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> is <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff#Saturation_excess_overland_flow" title="Surface runoff">saturated</a> to full capacity, because rain arrives <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff#Infiltration_excess_overland_flow" title="Surface runoff">more quickly than soil can absorb</a> it, or because <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/impervious#Adjective" title="wikt:impervious">impervious</a> areas (<a href="/wiki/Roof" title="Roof">roofs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Road_surface" title="Road surface">pavement</a>) send their runoff to surrounding soil that cannot absorb all of it. Surface runoff is a major component of the <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">water cycle</a>. It is the primary agent in <a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion#Rainfall_and_runoff" title="Soil erosion">soil erosion by water</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Runoff that occurs on the ground surface before reaching a <a href="/wiki/Channel_(geography)" title="Channel (geography)">channel</a> is also called a <a href="/wiki/Nonpoint_source_pollution" title="Nonpoint source pollution">nonpoint source</a>.  If a nonpoint source contains man-made contaminants, or natural forms of pollution (such as rotting leaves) the runoff is called <a href="/wiki/Nonpoint_source_pollution" title="Nonpoint source pollution">nonpoint source pollution</a>.  A land area which produces runoff that drains to a common point is called a <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">drainage basin</a>.  When runoff flows along the ground, it can pick up <a href="/wiki/Soil_contamination" title="Soil contamination">soil contaminants</a> including <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a> that become <a href="/wiki/Discharge_(hydrology)" title="Discharge (hydrology)">discharge</a> or nonpoint source pollution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition to causing water erosion and pollution, surface <a href="/wiki/Urban_runoff" title="Urban runoff">runoff in urban areas</a> is a primary cause of <a href="/wiki/Flood#Urban_flooding" title="Flood">urban flooding</a> which can result in property damage, damp and mold in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basements" title="Basements">basements</a>, and street flooding.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Generation">Generation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface_runoff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Generation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Runoffrazorback.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2112" data-file-width="2816" height="195" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Runoffrazorback.jpg/260px-Runoffrazorback.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Runoffrazorback.jpg/390px-Runoffrazorback.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Runoffrazorback.jpg/520px-Runoffrazorback.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Runoffrazorback.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Surface runoff from a hillside after soil is saturated</div></div></div><p> Surface runoff can be generated either by rainfall, snowfall or by the melting of snow, or glaciers.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Snowmelt" title="Snowmelt">Snow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glacier</a> melt occur only in areas cold enough for these to form permanently. Typically <a href="/wiki/Snowmelt" title="Snowmelt">snowmelt</a> will peak in the spring and glacier melt in the summer, leading to pronounced flow maxima in rivers affected by them. The determining factor of the rate of melting of snow or glaciers is both air temperature and the duration of sunlight. In high mountain regions, streams frequently rise on sunny days and fall on cloudy ones for this reason.
</p><p>In areas where there is no snow, runoff will come from rainfall. However, not all rainfall will produce runoff because storage from soils can absorb light showers. On the extremely ancient soils of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southern_Africa" title="Southern Africa">Southern Africa</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proteoid_root" title="Proteoid root">proteoid roots</a> with their extremely dense networks of root hairs can absorb so much rainwater as to prevent runoff even when substantial amounts of rain fall. In these regions, even on less infertile <a href="/wiki/Vertisol" title="Vertisol">cracking clay soils</a>, high amounts of rainfall and  potential evaporation are needed to generate any surface runoff, leading to specialised adaptations to extremely variable (usually ephemeral) streams.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infiltration_excess_overland_flow">Infiltration excess overland flow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface_runoff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Infiltration excess overland flow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>This occurs when the rate of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainfall" title="Rainfall">rainfall</a> on a surface exceeds the rate at which water can <a href="/wiki/Infiltration_(hydrology)" title="Infiltration (hydrology)">infiltrate</a> the ground, and any depression storage has already been filled. This is called flooding excess overland flow, Hortonian overland flow (after <a href="/wiki/Robert_E._Horton" title="Robert E. Horton">Robert E. Horton</a>), or unsaturated overland flow. This more commonly occurs in <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-arid" title="Semi-arid">semi-arid</a> regions, where rainfall intensities are high and the <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infiltration_capacity" title="Infiltration capacity">infiltration capacity</a> is reduced because of surface sealing, or in paved areas. This occurs largely in city areas where pavements prevent water from flooding.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Saturation_excess_overland_flow">Saturation excess overland flow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface_runoff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Saturation excess overland flow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When the <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> is saturated and the depression storage filled, and rain continues to fall, the rainfall will immediately produce surface runoff. The level of antecedent soil moisture is one factor affecting the time until soil becomes saturated. This runoff is called saturation excess overland flow, saturated overland flow or Dunne runoff.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antecedent_soil_moisture">Antecedent soil moisture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface_runoff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Antecedent soil moisture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Soil retains a degree of moisture after a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainfall" title="Rainfall">rainfall</a>. This residual water moisture affects the soil's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infiltration_capacity" title="Infiltration capacity">infiltration capacity</a>. During the next rainfall event, the infiltration capacity will cause the soil to be saturated at a different rate. The higher the level of antecedent soil moisture, the more quickly the soil becomes saturated. Once the soil is saturated, runoff occurs.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsurface_return_flow">Subsurface return flow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface_runoff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Subsurface return flow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After water infiltrates the soil on an up-slope portion of a hill, the water may flow laterally through the soil, and exfiltrate (flow out of the soil) closer to a channel. This is called subsurface return flow or <a href="/wiki/Throughflow" title="Throughflow">throughflow</a>.
</p><p>As it flows, the amount of runoff may be reduced in a number of possible ways: a small portion of it may <a href="/wiki/Evapotranspiration" title="Evapotranspiration">evapotranspire</a>; water may become temporarily stored in microtopographic depressions; and a portion of it may <a href="/wiki/Infiltration_(hydrology)" title="Infiltration (hydrology)">infiltrate</a> as it flows overland. Any remaining surface water eventually flows into a <a href="/wiki/Surface_water" title="Surface water">receiving water</a> body such as a <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuary</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_influence">Human influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface_runoff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Human influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:View_of_urban_runoff_discharging_to_coastal_waters.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="694" height="231" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/View_of_urban_runoff_discharging_to_coastal_waters.jpg/200px-View_of_urban_runoff_discharging_to_coastal_waters.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/View_of_urban_runoff_discharging_to_coastal_waters.jpg/300px-View_of_urban_runoff_discharging_to_coastal_waters.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/View_of_urban_runoff_discharging_to_coastal_waters.jpg/400px-View_of_urban_runoff_discharging_to_coastal_waters.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:View_of_urban_runoff_discharging_to_coastal_waters.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Urban surface water runoff</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">Urbanization</a> increases surface runoff by creating more <a href="/wiki/Impervious_surface" title="Impervious surface">impervious surfaces</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pavement_(roads)" title="Pavement (roads)">pavement</a> and buildings that do not allow <a href="/wiki/Percolation" title="Percolation">percolation</a> of the water down through the soil to the <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifer</a>. It is instead forced directly into streams or <a href="/wiki/Storm_drain" title="Storm drain">storm water runoff drains</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siltation" title="Siltation">siltation</a> can be major problems, even when flooding is not. Increased runoff reduces <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> recharge, thus lowering the <a href="/wiki/Water_table" title="Water table">water table</a> and making <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">droughts</a> worse, especially for agricultural farmers and others who depend on the <a href="/wiki/Water_well" title="Water well">water wells</a>.
</p><p>When anthropogenic contaminants are dissolved or suspended in runoff, the human impact is expanded to create <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">water pollution</a>. This pollutant load can reach various receiving waters such as streams, rivers, lakes, estuaries and oceans with resultant water chemistry changes to these water systems and their related ecosystems.
</p><p>A 2008 report by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Research_Council" title="United States National Research Council">United States National Research Council</a> identified urban stormwater as a leading source of <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">water quality</a> problems in the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>As humans continue to alter the climate through the addition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gases</a> to the atmosphere, precipitation patterns are expected to change as the atmospheric capacity for water vapor increases. This will have direct consequences on runoff amounts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Urban_runoff" title="Urban runoff">Urban runoff</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting" title="Rainwater harvesting">Rainwater harvesting</a></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_surface_runoff">Effects of surface runoff</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface_runoff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Effects of surface runoff">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Erosion_and_deposition">Erosion and deposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface_runoff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Erosion and deposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Surface runoff can cause <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> of the Earth's surface; <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">eroded material</a> may be <a href="/wiki/Deposition_(geology)" title="Deposition (geology)">deposited</a> a considerable distance away. There are four main types of <a href="/wiki/Erosion#Rainfall_and_runoff" title="Erosion">soil erosion by water</a>: splash erosion, sheet erosion, <a href="/wiki/Rill" title="Rill">rill</a> erosion and <a href="/wiki/Gully" title="Gully">gully</a> erosion. Splash erosion is the result of mechanical collision of raindrops with the soil surface: soil particles which are dislodged by the impact then move with the surface runoff. Sheet erosion is the overland transport of <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a> by runoff without a well defined channel. Soil surface roughness causes may cause runoff to become concentrated into narrower flow paths: as these incise, the small but well-defined channels which are formed are known as <a href="/wiki/Rill" title="Rill">rills</a>. These channels can be as small as one centimeter wide or as large as several meters. If runoff continue to incise and enlarge rills, they may eventually grow to become <a href="/wiki/Gully" title="Gully">gullies</a>. Gully erosion can transport large amounts of eroded material in a small time period. 
</p>

<p>Reduced crop productivity usually results from erosion, and these effects are studied in the field of <a href="/wiki/Soil_conservation" title="Soil conservation">soil conservation</a>. The soil particles carried in runoff vary in size from about .001 millimeter to 1.0 millimeter in diameter. Larger particles <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitate" title="Precipitate">settle</a> over short transport distances, whereas small particles can be carried over long distances suspended in the <a href="/wiki/Water_column" title="Water column">water column</a>. Erosion of silty soils that contain smaller particles generates <a href="/wiki/Turbidity" title="Turbidity">turbidity</a> and diminishes light transmission, which disrupts <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_ecosystem" title="Aquatic ecosystem">aquatic ecosystems</a>.
</p><p>Entire sections of countries have been rendered unproductive by erosion. On the high central <a href="/wiki/Plateau" title="Plateau">plateau</a> of <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>, approximately ten percent of that country's land area, virtually the entire landscape is devoid of <a href="/wiki/Vegetation" title="Vegetation">vegetation</a>, with erosive gully furrows typically in excess of 50 meters deep and one kilometer wide.  <a href="/wiki/Shifting_cultivation" title="Shifting cultivation">Shifting cultivation</a> is a farming system which sometimes incorporates the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slash_and_burn" title="Slash and burn">slash and burn</a> method in some regions of the world. Erosion causes loss of the fertile top soil and reduces its fertility and quality of the agricultural produce.
</p><p>Modern industrial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Farming" title="Farming">farming</a> is another major cause of erosion. In some areas in the American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corn_belt" title="Corn belt">corn belt</a>, more than 50 percent of the original <a href="/wiki/Topsoil" title="Topsoil">topsoil</a> has been carried away within the last 100 years.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_effects">Environmental effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface_runoff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Environmental effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The principal environmental issues associated with runoff are the impacts to surface water, <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> through transport of water pollutants to these systems. Ultimately these consequences translate into human health risk, ecosystem disturbance and aesthetic impact to water resources. Some of the contaminants that create the greatest impact to surface waters arising from runoff are <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> substances, <a href="/wiki/Herbicide" title="Herbicide">herbicides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a>. Quantitative uptake by surface runoff of pesticides and other contaminants has been studied since the 1960s, and early on contact of pesticides with water was known to enhance <a href="/wiki/Phytotoxicity" title="Phytotoxicity">phytotoxicity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>  In the case of surface waters, the impacts translate to <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">water pollution</a>, since the streams and rivers have received runoff carrying various chemicals or sediments. When surface waters are used as <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">potable water</a> supplies, they can be compromised regarding <a href="/wiki/Risk_assessment" title="Risk assessment">health risks</a> and drinking water <a href="/wiki/Aesthetics" title="Aesthetics">aesthetics</a> (that is, odor, color and <a href="/wiki/Turbidity" title="Turbidity">turbidity</a> effects). Contaminated surface waters risk altering the metabolic processes of the aquatic <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> that they host; these alterations can lead to death, such as <a href="/wiki/Fish_kill" title="Fish kill">fish kills</a>, or alter the balance of populations present. Other specific impacts are on animal mating, spawning, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" title="Egg (biology)">egg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Larva" title="Larva">larvae</a> viability, juvenile survival and plant productivity.  Some researches show surface runoff of pesticides, such as <a href="/wiki/DDT" title="DDT">DDT</a>, can alter the gender of fish species genetically, which transforms male into female fish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Surface runoff occurring within forests can supply lakes with high loads of mineral nitrogen and phosphorus leading to <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">eutrophication</a>. Runoff waters within <a href="/wiki/Temperate_coniferous_forest" title="Temperate coniferous forest">coniferous forests</a> are also enriched with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humic_acid" title="Humic acid">humic acids</a> and can lead to <a href="/wiki/Humus" title="Humus">humification</a> of water bodies <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Additionally, high standing and young islands in the tropics and subtropics can undergo high soil erosion rates and also contribute large material fluxes to the coastal ocean. Such land derived runoff of sediment nutrients, carbon, and contaminants can have large impacts on global biogeochemical cycles and marine and coastal ecosystems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the case of groundwater, the main issue is contamination of drinking water, if the <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifer</a> is abstracted for human use. Regarding <a href="/wiki/Soil_contamination" title="Soil contamination">soil contamination</a>, runoff waters can have two important pathways of concern. Firstly, runoff water can extract soil contaminants and carry them in the form of water pollution to even more sensitive aquatic habitats. Secondly, runoff can deposit contaminants on pristine soils, creating health or ecological consequences.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Agricultural_issues">Agricultural issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface_runoff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Agricultural issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The other context of agricultural issues involves the transport of agricultural chemicals (nitrates, phosphates, pesticides, herbicides etc.) via surface runoff. This result occurs when chemical use is excessive or poorly timed with respect to high precipitation. The resulting contaminated runoff represents not only a waste of agricultural chemicals, but also an environmental threat to downstream ecosystems.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flooding">Flooding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface_runoff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Flooding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">Flooding</a> occurs when a watercourse is unable to convey the quantity of runoff flowing downstream. The frequency with which this occurs is described by a <a href="/wiki/Return_period" title="Return period">return period</a>. Flooding is a natural process, which maintains ecosystem composition and processes, but it can also be altered by <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land use</a> changes such as river engineering. Floods can be both beneficial to societies or cause damage. Agriculture along the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a> floodplain took advantage of the seasonal flooding that deposited nutrients beneficial for crops. However, as the number and susceptibility of settlements increase, flooding increasingly becomes a natural hazard. In urban areas, surface runoff is the primary cause of urban flooding, known for its repetitive and costly impact on communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Adverse impacts span loss of life, property damage, contamination of water supplies, loss of crops, and social dislocation and temporary homelessness. Floods are among the most devastating of natural disasters.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mitigation_and_treatment">Mitigation and treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface_runoff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Mitigation and treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:North-Bend-Uplands-Runoff-pond-3942.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/North-Bend-Uplands-Runoff-pond-3942.jpg/220px-North-Bend-Uplands-Runoff-pond-3942.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/North-Bend-Uplands-Runoff-pond-3942.jpg/330px-North-Bend-Uplands-Runoff-pond-3942.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/North-Bend-Uplands-Runoff-pond-3942.jpg/440px-North-Bend-Uplands-Runoff-pond-3942.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:North-Bend-Uplands-Runoff-pond-3942.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Runoff holding ponds (Uplands neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/North_Bend,_Washington" title="North Bend, Washington">North Bend, Washington</a>)</div></div></div>
<p>Mitigation of adverse impacts of runoff can take several forms:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">Land use</a> development controls aimed at minimizing <a href="/wiki/Impervious_surface" title="Impervious surface">impervious surfaces</a> in urban areas</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">Erosion</a> controls for <a href="/wiki/Farm" title="Farm">farms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">construction</a> sites</li>
<li>Flood control and retrofit programs, such as <a href="/wiki/Green_infrastructure" title="Green infrastructure">green infrastructure</a></li>
<li>Chemical use and handling controls in <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Landscape_maintenance" title="Landscape maintenance">landscape maintenance</a>, industrial use, etc.</li></ul>
<p><b>Land use controls.</b> Many world regulatory agencies have encouraged research on methods of minimizing total surface runoff by avoiding unnecessary <a href="/wiki/Hardscape" title="Hardscape">hardscape</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Many municipalities have produced guidelines and codes (<a href="/wiki/Zoning" title="Zoning">zoning</a> and related <a href="/wiki/Local_ordinance" title="Local ordinance">ordinances</a>) for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_developer" title="Land developer">land developers</a> that encourage minimum width sidewalks, use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paving_stone_(flooring)" title="Paving stone (flooring)">pavers</a> set in earth for <a href="/wiki/Driveway" title="Driveway">driveways</a> and <a href="/wiki/Walkway" title="Walkway">walkways</a> and other design techniques to allow maximum water <a href="/wiki/Infiltration_(hydrology)" title="Infiltration (hydrology)">infiltration</a> in urban settings. An example land use control program can be seen in the city of <a href="/wiki/Santa_Monica,_California" title="Santa Monica, California">Santa Monica, California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> </p><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low-impact_development_(Canada/US)" title="Low-impact development (Canada/US)">Low-impact development (Canada/US)</a></div> <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting" title="Rainwater harvesting">Rainwater harvesting</a></div>
<p><b>Erosion controls</b> have appeared since medieval times when farmers realized the importance of contour farming to protect soil resources. Beginning in the 1950s these agricultural methods became increasingly more sophisticated. In the 1960s some <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">state</a> and <a href="/wiki/Local_government" title="Local government">local governments</a> began to focus their efforts on mitigation of construction runoff by requiring builders to implement <a href="/wiki/Erosion_control" title="Erosion control">erosion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sediment_control" title="Sediment control">sediment controls</a> (ESCs).  This included such techniques as: use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Straw_bale" title="Straw bale">straw bales</a> and barriers to slow runoff on slopes, installation of <a href="/wiki/Silt_fence" title="Silt fence">silt fences</a>, programming construction for months that have less rainfall and minimizing extent and duration of exposed graded areas. <a href="/wiki/Montgomery_County,_Maryland" title="Montgomery County, Maryland">Montgomery County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> implemented the first local government sediment control program in 1965, and this was followed by a statewide program in Maryland in 1970.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Flood control programs</b> as early as the first half of the twentieth century became quantitative in predicting peak flows of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Riverine" title="Riverine">riverine</a> systems. Progressively strategies have been developed to minimize peak flows and also to reduce channel velocities. Some of the techniques commonly applied are: provision of holding ponds (also called <a href="/wiki/Detention_basin" title="Detention basin">detention basins</a>) to buffer riverine peak flows, use of energy dissipators in channels to reduce stream velocity and land use controls to minimize runoff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical" title="Chemical">Chemical</a> use and handling.</b> Following enactment of the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Resource_Conservation_and_Recovery_Act" title="Resource Conservation and Recovery Act">Resource Conservation and Recovery Act</a> (RCRA) in 1976, and later the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Water_Act" title="Clean Water Act">Water Quality Act of 1987</a>, states and cities have become more vigilant in controlling the containment and storage of toxic chemicals, thus preventing releases and leakage. Methods commonly applied are: requirements for double containment of <a href="/wiki/Underground_storage_tank" title="Underground storage tank">underground storage tanks</a>, registration of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hazardous_materials" title="Hazardous materials">hazardous materials</a> usage, reduction in numbers of allowed pesticides and more stringent regulation of fertilizers and herbicides in landscape maintenance. In many industrial cases, pretreatment of wastes is required, to minimize escape of pollutants into <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_sewer" title="Sanitary sewer">sanitary</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Storm_sewer" title="Storm sewer">stormwater sewers</a>.
</p><p>The U.S. <a href="/wiki/Clean_Water_Act" title="Clean Water Act">Clean Water Act</a> (CWA) requires that local governments in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urbanized_areas" title="Urbanized areas">urbanized areas</a> (as defined by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Census_Bureau" title="Census Bureau">Census Bureau</a>) obtain <a href="/wiki/Stormwater" title="Stormwater">stormwater</a> discharge permits for their drainage systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Essentially this means that the locality must operate a <a href="/wiki/Stormwater#Stormwater_management" title="Stormwater">stormwater management</a> program for all surface runoff that enters the municipal separate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Storm_sewer" title="Storm sewer">storm sewer</a> system ("MS4"). EPA and state regulations and related publications outline six basic components that each local program must contain:
</p>
<ul><li>Public education (informing individuals, households, businesses about ways to avoid stormwater pollution)</li>
<li>Public involvement (support public participation in implementation of local programs)</li>
<li>Illicit discharge detection &amp; elimination (removing <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_sewer" title="Sanitary sewer">sanitary sewer</a> or other non-stormwater connections to the MS4)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">Construction</a> site runoff controls (i.e. erosion &amp; sediment controls)</li>
<li>Post-construction (i.e. permanent) stormwater management controls</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pollution_prevention" title="Pollution prevention">Pollution prevention</a> and "good housekeeping" measures (e.g. system maintenance).</li></ul>
<p>Other property owners which operate storm drain systems similar to municipalities, such as state highway systems, universities, military bases and prisons, are also subject to the MS4 permit requirements.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement_and_mathematical_modeling">Measurement and mathematical modeling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surface_runoff&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Measurement and mathematical modeling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Runoff is analyzed by using <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_model" title="Mathematical model">mathematical models</a> in combination with various <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">water quality</a> sampling methods. Measurements can be made using continuous automated water quality analysis instruments targeted on pollutants such as specific <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inorganic_chemical" title="Inorganic chemical">inorganic chemicals</a>, <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a>, turbidity etc. or targeted on secondary indicators such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dissolved_oxygen" title="Dissolved oxygen">dissolved oxygen</a>. Measurements can also be made in batch form by extracting a single water sample and conducting any number of chemical or physical tests on that sample.
</p><p>In the 1950s or earlier <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrology_transport_model" title="Hydrology transport model">hydrology transport models</a> appeared to calculate quantities of runoff, primarily for flood forecasting. Beginning in the early 1970s computer models were developed to analyze the transport of runoff carrying water pollutants, which considered <a href="/wiki/Solvation" title="Solvation">dissolution</a> rates of various chemicals, <a href="/wiki/Infiltration_(hydrology)" title="Infiltration (hydrology)">infiltration</a> into soils and ultimate pollutant load delivered to <a href="/wiki/Surface_water" title="Surface water">receiving waters</a>.
One of the earliest models addressing chemical dissolution in runoff and resulting transport was developed in the early 1970s under contract to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>  This <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_model" title="Computer model">computer model</a> formed the basis of much of the mitigation study that led to strategies for <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land use</a> and chemical handling controls.
</p><p>Other computer models have been developed (such as the <a href="/wiki/DSSAM_Model" title="DSSAM Model">DSSAM Model</a>) that allow surface runoff to be tracked through a river course as reactive water pollutants. In this case the surface runoff may be considered to be a <a href="/wiki/Line_source" title="Line source">line source</a> of <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">water pollution</a> to the receiving waters.
</p>
