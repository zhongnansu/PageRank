<p><i><b>The Limits to Growth</b></i> (<i><b>LTG</b></i>) is a 1972 report<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meadows-1972_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Meadows-1972-1">[1]</a></sup> on the <a href="/wiki/Computer_simulation" title="Computer simulation">computer simulation</a> of exponential <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Population_growth" title="Population growth">population growth</a> with a finite supply of resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Funded by the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Foundation" title="Volkswagen Foundation">Volkswagen Foundation</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and commissioned by the <a href="/wiki/Club_of_Rome" title="Club of Rome">Club of Rome</a>, the findings of the study were first presented at international gatherings in Moscow and Rio de Janeiro in the summer of 1971.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meadows-1972_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Meadows-1972-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>186</span></sup> The report's authors are <a href="/wiki/Donella_Meadows" title="Donella Meadows">Donella H. Meadows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Meadows" title="Dennis Meadows">Dennis L. Meadows</a>, <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B8rgen_Randers" title="Jørgen Randers">Jørgen Randers</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=William_W._Behrens_III&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="William W. </p><p>Since its publication, some 30 million copies of the book in 30 languages have been purchased.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Solutions_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Solutions-4">[4]</a></sup> It continues to generate debate and has been the subject of several subsequent publications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Farley-n.d._5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Farley-n.d.-5">[5]</a></sup> Most recently, <i>The Limits to Growth: The 30-Year Update</i> was published on June 1, 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LtG+30-2004_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-LtG+30-2004-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Purpose">Purpose</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Limits_to_Growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Purpose">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In commissioning the MIT team to undertake the project that resulted in <i>LTG</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Club_of_Rome" title="Club of Rome">Club of Rome</a> had two objectives:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meadows-1972_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Meadows-1972-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>185</span></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>Gain insights into the limits of our world system and the constraints it puts on human numbers and activity.</li>
<li>Identify and study the dominant elements, and their interactions, that influence the long-term behavior of world systems.</li></ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methodology">Methodology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Limits_to_Growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Methodology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The study used the <a href="/wiki/World3" title="World3">World3</a> computer model to simulate the consequence of interactions between the earth and human systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The model was based on the work of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jay_Forrester" title="Jay Forrester">Jay Forrester</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MIT" title="MIT">MIT</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meadows-1972_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Meadows-1972-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>21</span></sup> as described in his book <i>World Dynamics</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The model was based on five variables: "<a href="/wiki/World_population" title="World population">population</a>, food production, industrialization, pollution, and <a href="/wiki/Resource_depletion" title="Resource depletion">consumption of nonrenewable natural resources</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meadows-1972_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Meadows-1972-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>25</span></sup> At the time of the study, all these variables were increasing and were assumed to continue to grow <a href="/wiki/Exponential_growth" title="Exponential growth">exponentially</a>, while the ability of technology to increase resources grew only <a href="/wiki/Linear_function" title="Linear function">linearly</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meadows-1972_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Meadows-1972-1">[1]</a></sup> The authors intended to explore the possibility of a sustainable feedback pattern that would be achieved by altering growth trends among the five variables under three scenarios. They noted that their projections for the values of the variables in each scenario were predictions "only in the most limited sense of the word", and were only indications of the system's behavioral tendencies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peter_A._Victor_2008_pp._92-93_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Peter_A._Victor_2008_pp._92-93-10">[10]</a></sup> Two of the scenarios saw "overshoot and collapse" of the global system by the mid- to latter-part of the <a href="/wiki/21st_century" title="21st century">21st century</a>, while a third scenario resulted in a "stabilized world".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSIRO_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-CSIRO-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>11</span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exponential_reserve_index">Exponential reserve index</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Limits_to_Growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Exponential reserve index">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The static reserve numbers assume that the usage is constant, and the exponential reserve assumes that the growth rate is constant.
</p><p>The exponential index has been interpreted as a prediction of the number of years until the world would "run out" of various resources, both by environmentalist groups calling for greater conservation and restrictions on use, and by skeptics criticizing the index when supplies failed to run out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> What <i>The Limits to Growth</i> actually has is the above table, which has the <i>current reserves</i> (that is no new sources of oil are found) for oil running out in 1992 assuming constant exponential growth.  In <i>Limits to Growth: The Thirty Year Update</i> there are several pages explaining that new resources are found over time and that the <i>current reserves</i> therefore change but that ultimately resources are finite.  (Earlier editions did explain this as well, but not in as much detail.) The standard model includes a resource base of double that of what they have calculated, but the book includes model runs where the assumed resources are infinite, but those model runs still result in overshoot and collapse from other factors.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conclusions">Conclusions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Limits_to_Growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Conclusions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After reviewing their computer simulations, the research team came to the following conclusions:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meadows-1972_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-Meadows-1972-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>23–24</span></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>Given business as usual, i.e., no changes to historical growth trends, the limits to growth on earth would become evident by 2072, leading to "sudden and uncontrollable decline in both population and industrial capacity".</li>
<li>Growth trends existing in 1972 could be altered so that sustainable ecological and economic stability could be achieved.</li>
<li>The sooner the world's people start striving for the second outcome above, the better the chance of achieving it.</li></ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Limits_to_Growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Criticism of <i>LTG</i> was immediate. Peter Passell and two co-authors published a 2 April 1972 article in the <i>New York Times</i> describing <i>LTG</i> as "...an empty and misleading work .... best summarized ... as a rediscovery of the oldest maxim of computer science: Garbage In, Garbage Out." Passell found the study's simulations to be simplistic, while assigning little value to the role of technological progress in solving the problems of resource depletion, pollution, and food production. They charged that all <i>LTG</i> simulations ended in collapse, predicted the imminent end of irreplaceable resources, and, finally, that the entire endeavor was motivated by a hidden agenda: to halt growth in its tracks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Passell-1972_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Passell-1972-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1973, a group of researchers at the Science Policy Research Unit at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Sussex" title="University of Sussex">University of Sussex</a>, published <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Models_of_Doom;_A_Critique_of_The_Limits_to_Growth&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Models of Doom; A Critique of The Limits to Growth (page does not exist)">Models of Doom; A Critique of The Limits to Growth</a></i>. The Sussex group examined the structure and assumptions of the MIT models. They concluded that the simulations were very sensitive to a few key assumptions and suggest that the MIT assumptions were unduly pessimistic. The Sussex scientists concluded that the MIT methodology, data, and projections were faulty and do not accurately reflect reality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Claiming that the Sussex critics  applied "micro reasoning to macro problems", the <i>LTG</i> team, in "A Response to Sussex", described and analyzed five major areas of disagreement between themselves and the Sussex authors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>The report has been criticized by academics, economists and businessmen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meyer-2010_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Meyer-2010-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cole-1973_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cole-1973-21">[21]</a></sup> Critics claimed that history proved the projections to be incorrect, which was specifically based on the popular belief that <i>The Limits to Growth</i> predicted resource depletion and associated economic collapse by the end of the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vanVuuren-2009_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-vanVuuren-2009-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>23</span></sup> <i>The Limits to Growth</i> faced ridicule as early as the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Attacks were made on the methodology, the computer, the conclusions, the rhetoric and the people behind the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alan201013_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-alan201013-25">[25]</a></sup>  Yale economist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_C._Wallich" title="Henry C. Wallich">Henry C. Wallich</a> agreed that growth could not continue indefinitely, but that a natural end to growth was preferable to intervention. Wallich stated that technology could solve all the problems the report was concerned about, but only if growth continued apace. By stopping growth too soon, Wallich warned, the world would be "consigning billions to permanent poverty".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alan201013_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-alan201013-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Julian_Simon" title="Julian Simon">Julian Simon</a>, a professor at the Universities of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Illinois" title="University of Illinois">Illinois</a> and, later, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Maryland" title="University of Maryland">Maryland</a>, argued that the fundamental underlying concepts of the <i>LTG</i> scenarios were faulty, because the very idea of what constitutes a "resource" varies over time. For instance, wood was the primary shipbuilding resource until the 1800s, and there were concerns about prospective wood shortages from the 1500s on. But then boats began to be made of iron, later steel, and the shortage issue disappeared. Simon argued in his book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ultimate_Resource" title="The Ultimate Resource">The Ultimate Resource</a></i> that human ingenuity creates new resources as required from the raw materials of the universe. For instance, copper will never "run out". History demonstrates that as it becomes scarcer its price will rise and more will be found, more will be recycled, new techniques will use less of it, and at some point a better substitute will be found for it altogether.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simon-1981_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Simon-1981-26">[26]</a></sup> His book was revised and reissued in 1996 as <i>The Ultimate Resource 2</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simon-1996_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Simon-1996-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Robert_Solow" title="Robert Solow">Robert Solow</a> from <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">MIT</a> argued that prediction in <i>The Limits to Growth</i> was based on a weak foundation of data. Allen Kneese and Ronald Riker of <a href="/wiki/Resources_for_the_Future" title="Resources for the Future">Resources for the Future</a> (RFF) stated, "The authors load their case by letting some things grow exponentially and others not. Population, capital and pollution grow exponentially in all models, but technologies for expanding resources and controlling pollution are permitted to grow, if at all, only in discrete increments."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Critics also argue that the authors of the report claimed to accept that the then-known resources of minerals and energy could, and would, grow in the future, and consumption growth rates could also decline. The theoretical expiry time for each resource would therefore need to be updated as new discoveries, technologies and trends came to light. Writing in <i>Forbes</i>, one critic pointed out that "...while we do indeed face hard limits to the availability of metals and or minerals these hard limits are so far away that they're not...something we should worry about..." and that "...everything is substitutable. Absolutely everything is, no exceptions." Thus substituting metals and minerals for fossil fuels is the very basis of renewable energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Worstall-2012_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Worstall-2012-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>By "...the 1990s LTG had become everyone's laughing stock....In short, Chicken Little with a computer."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bardi-2008_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bardi-2008-29">[29]</a></sup> In 1997, the Italian economist Giorgio Nebbia, observed that the negative reaction to the <i>LTG</i> study came from at least four sources: those who saw the book as a threat to their business or industry; professional economists, who saw <i>LTG</i> as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Credentialism" title="Credentialism">uncredentialed</a> encroachment on their professional <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/perquisite" title="wikt:perquisite">perquisites</a>; the Catholic church, which bridled at the suggestion that overpopulation was one of mankind's major problems; finally, the political left, which saw the <i>LTG</i> study as a scam by the elites designed to trick workers into believing that a proletarian paradise was a <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pipe_dream" title="wikt:pipe dream">pipe dream</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bardi-2008_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bardi-2008-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Positive_reviews">Positive reviews</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Limits_to_Growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Positive reviews">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:25%; ; background-color: #f8f8f8;"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303512">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{margin:0.5em auto 0.8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft p,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright p{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:larger;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" “ ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" ” ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:360px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{min-width:100%;margin:0 0 0.8em!important;float:none!important}}</style>
<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">With few exceptions, economics as a discipline has been dominated by a perception of living in an unlimited world, where resource and pollution problems in one area were solved by moving resources or people to other parts. The very hint of any global limitation as suggested in the report <i>The Limits to Growth</i> was met with disbelief and rejection by businesses and most economists. However, this conclusion was mostly based on false premises.</div>
<p><cite class="right-aligned" style="">– <a href="#CITEREFMeyerNørgård2010">Meyer &amp; Nørgård (2010)</a>.</cite>
</p>
</div>
<p>In the early years of the 21st century, the tide of opinion regarding <i>LTG</i> began to swing in a positive direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bardi-2008_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bardi-2008-29">[29]</a></sup> Reading <i>LTG</i> for the first time in 2000, influential energy economist <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Simmons" title="Matthew Simmons">Matthew Simmons</a> concluded his views on the report by saying, "In hindsight, The <a href="/wiki/Club_of_Rome" title="Club of Rome">Club of Rome</a> turned out to be right. We simply wasted 30 important years ignoring this work."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simmons-2000_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Simmons-2000-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2008, Graham Turner of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Scientific_and_Industrial_Research_Organisation" title="Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation">Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation</a> (CSIRO) found that the observed historical data from 1970 to 2000 closely match the simulated results of the "standard run" limits of growth model for almost all the outputs reported. "The comparison is well within uncertainty bounds of nearly all the data in terms of both magnitude and the trends over time." Turner also examined a number of reports, particularly by economists, which over the years have purported to discredit the limits-to-growth model. Turner says these reports are flawed, and reflect misunderstandings about the model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSIRO_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-CSIRO-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>37</span></sup>
</p><p>In 2010, Nørgård, Peet and Ragnarsdóttir called the book a "pioneering report", and said that it "has withstood the test of time and, indeed, has only become more relevant."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Solutions_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Solutions-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2014, <i>The Guardian</i> published an article showing that data collected since LTG's publication in 1972 supports the accuracy of the 1972 projections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Turner-2014_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Turner-2014-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Christian_Parenti" title="Christian Parenti">Christian Parenti</a>, writing in 2012, sees parallels between the reception of <i>LTG</i> and the contemporary climate debate, "That said, <i>The Limits to Growth</i> was a scientifically rigorous and credible warning that was actively rejected by the intellectual watchdogs of powerful economic interests. A similar story is playing out now around climate science."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Parenti-2012_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Parenti-2012-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Limits_to_Growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Club_of_Rome" title="Club of Rome">Club of Rome</a> has persisted after <i>The Limits of Growth</i> and has generally provided comprehensive updates to the book every five years.
</p><p>An independent retrospective on the public debate over <i>The Limits to Growth</i> concluded in 1978 that optimistic attitudes had won out, causing a general loss of momentum in the environmental movement. While summarizing a large number of opposing arguments, the article concluded that "scientific arguments for and against each position ... have, it would seem, played only a small part in the general acceptance of alternative perspectives."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1989, a symposium was held in <a href="/wiki/Hanover" title="Hanover">Hanover</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lower_Saxony" title="Lower Saxony">Lower Saxony</a>, entitled "Beyond the Limits to Growth: Global Industrial Society, Vision or Nightmare?" In 1993, <i><a href="/wiki/Beyond_the_Limits" title="Beyond the Limits">Beyond the Limits</a></i> was published as a 20-year update on the original material. <i>Limits to Growth: The 30-Year Update</i> was published in 2004.
</p><p>In 2008, Graham Turner at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Scientific_and_Industrial_Research_Organisation" title="Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation">Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation</a> (CSIRO) in Australia published a paper called "A Comparison of 'The Limits to Growth' with Thirty Years of Reality".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSIRO_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-CSIRO-11">[11]</a></sup> It compared the past thirty years of data with the scenarios laid out in the 1972 book and found that changes in industrial production, food production, and pollution are all congruent with one of the book's three scenarios—that of "business as usual". This scenario in <i>Limits</i> points to <a href="/wiki/Economic_collapse" title="Economic collapse">economic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Societal_collapse" title="Societal collapse">societal collapse</a> in the 21st century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>  In 2010, Nørgård, Peet, and Ragnarsdóttir called the book a "pioneering report". They said that, "its approach remains useful and that its conclusions are still surprisingly valid ... unfortunately the report has been largely dismissed by critics as a doomsday prophecy that has not held up to scrutiny."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Solutions_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Solutions-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Also in 2008, researcher Peter A. Victor wrote, that even though the <i>Limits</i> team probably underestimated <a href="/wiki/Price_mechanism" title="Price mechanism">price mechanism</a>'s role in adjusting outcomes, their critics have overestimated it. He states that <i>Limits to Growth</i> has had a significant impact on the conception of environmental issues and notes that the models in the book were meant to be taken as predictions "only in the most limited sense of the word".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peter_A._Victor_2008_pp._92-93_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Peter_A._Victor_2008_pp._92-93-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>In a 2009 article published in <i><a href="/wiki/American_Scientist" title="American Scientist">American Scientist</a></i> titled "Revisiting the Limits to Growth After Peak Oil", Hall and Day noted that "the values predicted by the limits-to-growth model and actual data for 2008 are very close."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-American_Scientist_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-American_Scientist-35">[35]</a></sup> These findings are consistent with the 2008 CSIRO study which concluded: "The analysis shows that 30 years of historical data compares favorably with key features ... [of the <i>Limits to Growth</i>] "standard run" scenario, which results in collapse of the global system midway through the 21st Century."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSIRO_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-CSIRO-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In 2011, <a href="/wiki/Ugo_Bardi" title="Ugo Bardi">Ugo Bardi</a> published a book-length academic study of <i>The Limits to Growth</i>, its methods and historical reception and concluded that "The warnings that we received in 1972 ... are becoming increasingly more worrisome as reality seems to be following closely the curves that the ... scenario had generated."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bardi-2011_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bardi-2011-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>3</span></sup> A popular analysis of the accuracy of the report by science writer <a href="/wiki/Richard_Heinberg" title="Richard Heinberg">Richard Heinberg</a> was also published.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2012, the <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Institution" title="Smithsonian Institution">Smithsonian Institution</a> held a symposium entitled "Perspectives on <i>Limits to Growth</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI-20120301_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-SI-20120301-38">[38]</a></sup> Another symposium was held in <a href="/wiki/Hanover" title="Hanover">Hanover</a>, Germany entitled "Already Beyond?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Limits to Growth</i> did not receive an official update in 2012, but one of its coauthors, <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B8rgen_Randers" title="Jørgen Randers">Jørgen Randers</a>, published a book, <i><a href="/wiki/2052:_A_Global_Forecast_for_the_Next_Forty_Years" title="2052: A Global Forecast for the Next Forty Years">2052: A Global Forecast for the Next Forty Years</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Randers-2012_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Randers-2012-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Randers-2052_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Randers-2052-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2012, writing in <i>American Scientist</i>, Brian Hayes stated that the model is "more a polemical tool than a scientific instrument". He went on to say that the graphs generated by the computer program should not, as the authors note, be used as predictions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2014, Graham Turner concluded that "preparing for a collapsing global system could be even more important than trying to avoid collapse."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-turner2014_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-turner2014-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2016, a report published by the UK <a class="external text" href="http://limits2growth.org.uk/" rel="nofollow">All-Party Parliamentary Group on Limits to Growth</a> concluded that "there is unsettling evidence that society is still following the 'standard run' of the original study – in which overshoot leads to an eventual collapse of production and living standards".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The report also points out that some issues not fully addressed in the original 1972 report, such as <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, present additional challenges for human development.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_books">Related books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Limits_to_Growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Related books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many books about humanity's uncertain future have appeared regularly over the years. Precursors to <i>Limits to Growth</i> included <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Robert_Malthus" title="Thomas Robert Malthus">Thomas Malthus's</a> <i><a href="/wiki/An_Essay_on_the_Principle_of_Population" title="An Essay on the Principle of Population">An Essay on the Principle of Population</a></i> (1798), <a href="/wiki/Harrison_Brown" title="Harrison Brown">Harrison Brown</a>'s <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Challenge_of_Man%27s_Future&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Challenge of Man's Future (page does not exist)">The Challenge of Man's Future</a></i> (1956), <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Carson" title="Rachel Carson">Rachel Carson</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Silent_Spring" title="Silent Spring">Silent Spring</a></i> (1962) and <a href="/wiki/Paul_R._Ehrlich" title="Paul R. Ehrlich">Paul R. Ehrlich</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Population_Bomb" title="The Population Bomb">The Population Bomb</a></i> (1968).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alan2010_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-alan2010-45">[45]</a></sup>
Some of the notable books published after 1972 include the <i><a href="/wiki/State_of_the_World_(book_series)" title="State of the World (book series)">State of the World</a></i> reports issued by the <a href="/wiki/Worldwatch_Institute" title="Worldwatch Institute">Worldwatch Institute</a> (produced annually since 1984); the influential <i><a href="/wiki/Our_Common_Future" title="Our Common Future">Our Common Future</a></i>, published by the UN’s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Commission_on_Environment_and_Development" title="World Commission on Environment and Development">World Commission on Environment and Development</a> (1987); <i><a href="/wiki/Earth_in_the_Balance" title="Earth in the Balance">Earth in the Balance</a></i>, written by then-US senator <a href="/wiki/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">Al Gore</a> (1992); and <i>Earth Odyssey</i> by journalist <a href="/wiki/Mark_Hertsgaard" title="Mark Hertsgaard">Mark Hertsgaard</a> (1999).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alan2010_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-alan2010-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
