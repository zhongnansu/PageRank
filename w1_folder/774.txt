<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Home</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SearsHouse115.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1004" data-file-width="926" height="325" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/SearsHouse115.jpg/300px-SearsHouse115.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/SearsHouse115.jpg/450px-SearsHouse115.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/SearsHouse115.jpg/600px-SearsHouse115.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SearsHouse115.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Plans for a detached <a href="/wiki/House" title="House">house</a> showing the social functions for each room</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>home</b>, or <b>domicile</b>, is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Living_space" title="Living space">living space</a> used as a permanent or semi-permanent residence for an individual, <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">family</a>, <a href="/wiki/Household" title="Household">household</a> or <a href="/wiki/Longhouse" title="Longhouse">several families in a tribe</a>. It is often a <a href="/wiki/House" title="House">house</a>, <a href="/wiki/Apartment" title="Apartment">apartment</a>, or other <a href="/wiki/Building" title="Building">building</a>, or alternatively a <a href="/wiki/Mobile_home" title="Mobile home">mobile home</a>, <a href="/wiki/Houseboat" title="Houseboat">houseboat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yurt" title="Yurt">yurt</a> or any other portable shelter. A principle of <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_law" title="Constitutional law">constitutional law</a> in many countries, related to the <a href="/wiki/Right_to_privacy" title="Right to privacy">right to privacy</a> enshrined in article 12 of the <a href="/wiki/Universal_Declaration_of_Human_Rights" title="Universal Declaration of Human Rights">Universal Declaration of Human Rights</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is the inviolability of the home as an individual's <a href="/wiki/Shelter_(building)" title="Shelter (building)">place of shelter and refuge</a>.
</p><p>Homes typically provide areas and facilities for sleeping, preparing food, eating and hygiene. Larger groups may live in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nursing_home" title="Nursing home">nursing home</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orphanage" title="Orphanage">children's home</a>, <a href="/wiki/Convent" title="Convent">convent</a> or any similar institution. A <a href="/wiki/Homestead_(buildings)" title="Homestead (buildings)">homestead</a> also includes agricultural land and facilities for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domesticated_animals" title="Domesticated animals">domesticated animals</a>. Where more secure <a href="/wiki/Dwelling" title="Dwelling">dwellings</a> are not available, people may live in the informal and sometimes illegal <a href="/wiki/Shack" title="Shack">shacks</a> found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slums" title="Slums">slums</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shanty_town" title="Shanty town">shanty towns</a>. More generally, "home" may be considered to be a geographic area, such as a <a href="/wiki/Town" title="Town">town</a>, <a href="/wiki/Village" title="Village">village</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suburb" title="Suburb">suburb</a>, <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">city</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Country" title="Country">country</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg"><img alt="[icon]" data-file-height="31" data-file-width="44" height="14" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" width="20"/></a></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs expansion</b>. <small>You can help by <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Home&amp;action=edit&amp;section=">adding to it</a>.</small> <small><i>(November 2013)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table><p>
The earliest homes that humans inhabited were likely naturally occurring features such as <a href="/wiki/Cave" title="Cave">caves</a>. </p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Taino_petroglyph_in_cave.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1016" data-file-width="1013" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Taino_petroglyph_in_cave.jpg/220px-Taino_petroglyph_in_cave.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Taino_petroglyph_in_cave.jpg/330px-Taino_petroglyph_in_cave.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Taino_petroglyph_in_cave.jpg/440px-Taino_petroglyph_in_cave.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Taino_petroglyph_in_cave.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno_people" title="Taíno people">Taíno</a> <a href="/wiki/Petroglyph" title="Petroglyph">petroglyphs</a> in a cave in Puerto Rico</div></div></div>
<p>Throughout history, primitive peoples have made use of caves. The earliest human <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a> found in caves come from a series of caves near Krugersdorp and Mokopane in South Africa. The cave sites of <a href="/wiki/Sterkfontein" title="Sterkfontein">Sterkfontein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swartkrans" title="Swartkrans">Swartkrans</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kromdraai" title="Kromdraai">Kromdraai</a> B, <a href="/wiki/Drimolen" title="Drimolen">Drimolen</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malapa" title="Malapa">Malapa</a>, Cooper's D, Gladysvale, Gondolin and Makapansgat have yielded a range of early human species dating back to between three and one million years ago, including <i><a href="/wiki/Australopithecus_africanus" title="Australopithecus africanus">Australopithecus africanus</a></i>, <i>7<a href="/wiki/Australopithecus_sediba" title="Australopithecus sediba">Australopithecus sediba</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Paranthropus_robustus" title="Paranthropus robustus">Paranthropus robustus</a></i>. However, it is not generally thought that these early humans were living in the caves, but that they were brought into the caves by carnivores that had killed them.
</p><p>The first early hominid ever found in Africa, the <a href="/wiki/Taung_Child" title="Taung Child">Taung Child</a> in 1924, was also thought for many years to come from a cave, where it had been deposited after being preyed upon by an eagle. However, this is now debated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Caves do form in the dolomite of the Ghaap Plateau, including the Early, Middle and Later Stone Age site of <a href="/wiki/Wonderwerk_Cave" title="Wonderwerk Cave">Wonderwerk Cave</a>; however, the caves that form along the escarpment's edge, like that hypothesised for the Taung Child, are formed within a secondary limestone deposit called <a href="/wiki/Tufa" title="Tufa">tufa</a>. There is numerous evidence for other early human species inhabiting caves from at least one million years ago in different parts of the world, including <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_erectus" title="Homo erectus">Homo erectus</a></i> in China at <a href="/wiki/Zhoukoudian" title="Zhoukoudian">Zhoukoudian</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_rhodesiensis" title="Homo rhodesiensis">Homo rhodesiensis</a></i> in South Africa at the Cave of Hearths (<a href="/wiki/Makapansgat" title="Makapansgat">Makapansgat</a>), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homo_neandertalensis" title="Homo neandertalensis">Homo neandertalensis</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_heidelbergensis" title="Homo heidelbergensis">Homo heidelbergensis</a></i> in Europe at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaeological_Site_of_Atapuerca" title="Archaeological Site of Atapuerca">Archaeological Site of Atapuerca</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Homo_floresiensis" title="Homo floresiensis">Homo floresiensis</a></i> in Indonesia, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denisovans" title="Denisovans">Denisovans</a> in southern Siberia.
</p><p>In southern Africa, early modern humans regularly used sea caves as shelter starting about 180,000 years ago when they learned to exploit the sea for the first time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The oldest known site is PP13B at <a href="/wiki/Pinnacle_Point" title="Pinnacle Point">Pinnacle Point</a>. This may have allowed rapid expansion of humans out of Africa and colonization of areas of the world such as Australia by 60-50,000 years ago. Throughout southern Africa, Australia, and Europe, early modern humans used caves and rock shelters as sites for rock art, such as those at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giants_Castle" title="Giants Castle">Giants Castle</a>. Caves such as the <a href="/wiki/Yaodong" title="Yaodong">yaodong</a> in China were used for shelter; other caves were used for burials (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rock-cut_tombs" title="Rock-cut tombs">rock-cut tombs</a>), or as religious sites (such as <a href="/wiki/Category:Buddhist_caves" title="Category:Buddhist caves">Buddhist caves</a>). Among the known sacred caves are China's Cave of a Thousand Buddhas<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Sacred_caves_of_Crete" title="Sacred caves of Crete">sacred caves of Crete</a>.
</p><p>As technology progressed, humans and other hominids began constructing their own dwellings. Buildings such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hut_(dwelling)" title="Hut (dwelling)">huts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Longhouse" title="Longhouse">longhouses</a> have been used for living since the late <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Skara_Brae_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Skara_Brae-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is unknown when the first mansion was built because there is no way to know for sure, but it is believed to have origins as a type of palace during the Mesopotamian period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Quora.com_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Quora.com-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Buildings">Buildings</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hancock-homestead-settler.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="937" data-file-width="1392" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Hancock-homestead-settler.jpg/220px-Hancock-homestead-settler.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Hancock-homestead-settler.jpg/330px-Hancock-homestead-settler.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Hancock-homestead-settler.jpg/440px-Hancock-homestead-settler.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hancock-homestead-settler.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1910 American homestead</div></div></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/House" title="House">house</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Building" title="Building">building</a> that functions as a home for <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> ranging from simple <a href="/wiki/Dwelling" title="Dwelling">dwellings</a> such as rudimentary huts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nomadic_tribes" title="Nomadic tribes">nomadic tribes</a> to complex, fixed structures of wood, brick, or other materials containing plumbing, ventilation and electrical systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schoenauer_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-schoenauer-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Most conventional modern houses will at least contain a <a href="/wiki/Bedroom" title="Bedroom">bedroom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bathroom" title="Bathroom">bathroom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kitchen" title="Kitchen">kitchen</a> or cooking area, and a <a href="/wiki/Living_room" title="Living room">living room</a>. In traditional agriculture-oriented societies, <a href="/wiki/List_of_domesticated_animals" title="List of domesticated animals">domestic animals</a> such as chickens or larger livestock (like cattle) may share part of the house with humans. The social unit that lives in a house is known as a <a href="/wiki/Household" title="Household">household</a>. Most commonly, a household is a <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">family</a> unit of some kind, although households may also be other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Group_(sociology)" title="Group (sociology)">social groups</a> or individuals. The design and structure of homes is also subject to change as a consequence of globalization, <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">urbanization</a> and other social, economic, demographic, and technological reasons. Various other cultural factors also influence the building style and patterns of domestic space.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Terraced_house" title="Terraced house">terraced house</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[a]</a></sup> is a style of <a href="/wiki/Medium-density_housing" title="Medium-density housing">medium-density housing</a> where a row of identical or mirror-image houses share side walls, while <a href="/wiki/Semi-detached" title="Semi-detached">semi-detached</a> housing consists of pairs of houses built side-by-side or (less commonly) back-to-back,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> sharing a <a href="/wiki/Party_wall" title="Party wall">party wall</a> and with mirrored layouts.
</p><p>An <a href="/wiki/Apartment" title="Apartment">apartment</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Comparison_of_American_and_British_English#Levels_of_buildings" title="Comparison of American and British English">American English</a>) or a flat (in <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a>) is a self-contained housing unit (a type of residential <a href="/wiki/Real_estate" title="Real estate">real estate</a>) that occupies only part of a <a href="/wiki/Building" title="Building">building</a>. Such a building may be called an <i>apartment building</i>, <i>apartment house</i> (in American English), <i>block of flats</i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tower_block" title="Tower block">tower block</a></i>, <i>high-rise</i> or, occasionally <i>mansion block</i> (in British English), especially if it consists of many apartments for <a href="/wiki/Renting" title="Renting">rent</a>. In Scotland it is called a block of flats or if it's a traditional sandstone building a <i>tenement</i>, which has a pejorative connotation elsewhere. Apartments may be owned by an <i>owner/occupier</i> by leasehold tenure or rented by <i>tenants</i> (two types of <a href="/wiki/Housing_tenure" title="Housing tenure">housing tenure</a>).
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Homestead_(buildings)" title="Homestead (buildings)">homestead</a> consists of a <a href="/wiki/Dwelling" title="Dwelling">dwelling</a>, often a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Farm_house" title="Farm house">farm house</a>, together with other buildings and associated land, and facilities for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domesticated_animals" title="Domesticated animals">domesticated animals</a>.
</p>

<p>The financial arrangements under which someone has the right to live in a home are, most frequently, <a href="/wiki/Leasehold_estate" title="Leasehold estate">tenancy</a>, in which <a href="/wiki/Renting" title="Renting">rent</a> is paid by the tenant to a <a href="/wiki/Landlord" title="Landlord">landlord</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Owner-occupancy" title="Owner-occupancy">owner-occupancy</a>.  Mixed forms of tenure are also possible.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Homelessness">Homelessness</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Homelessness" title="Homelessness">homelessness</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Street_dwellers_in_San%27ya_district_in_Tokyo_Japan.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1440" data-file-width="1920" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Street_dwellers_in_San%27ya_district_in_Tokyo_Japan.jpg/220px-Street_dwellers_in_San%27ya_district_in_Tokyo_Japan.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Street_dwellers_in_San%27ya_district_in_Tokyo_Japan.jpg/330px-Street_dwellers_in_San%27ya_district_in_Tokyo_Japan.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Street_dwellers_in_San%27ya_district_in_Tokyo_Japan.jpg/440px-Street_dwellers_in_San%27ya_district_in_Tokyo_Japan.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Street_dwellers_in_San%27ya_district_in_Tokyo_Japan.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Homeless people in <a href="/wiki/San%27ya" title="San'ya">San'ya</a> district, <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a></div></div></div>
<p>Article 25 of the <a href="/wiki/Universal_Declaration_of_Human_Rights" title="Universal Declaration of Human Rights">Universal Declaration of Human Rights</a>, adopted in 1948 contains the following text regarding housing and quality of living: "Everyone has the right to a standard of living adequate for the health and well-being of himself and of his family, including food, clothing, housing and medical care and necessary social services..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2004, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>, Department of Economic and Social Affairs, defined a homeless <a href="/wiki/Household" title="Household">household</a> as "<i>those households without a shelter that would fall within the scope of living quarters. They carry their few possessions with them, sleeping in the streets, in doorways or on piers, or in another space, on a more or less random basis.</i>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2009, at the United Nations Economic Commission for Europe Conference of European Statisticians recommended that homeless people are classified in two broad groups (noting that this would not provide a complete definition):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>(a) Primary homelessness (or rooflessness). This category includes persons living in the streets without a shelter that would fall within the scope of living quarters.</li>
<li>(b) Secondary homelessness. This category may include persons with no place of usual residence who move frequently between various types of accommodations (including dwellings, shelters, and institutions for the homeless or other living quarters). This category includes persons living in private dwellings but reporting ‘no usual address’ on their census form.</li></ul>
<p>In 2005, 100 million people worldwide were estimated to be <a href="/wiki/Homelessness" title="Homelessness">homeless</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> although some prefer the term 'houseless' or 'unsheltered'.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Psychological_significance">Psychological significance</span></h2>
<p>A home is generally a place that is close to the heart of the owner, and can become a prized possession. It has been argued that psychologically "<i>The strongest sense of home commonly coincides geographically with a dwelling. Usually, the sense of home attenuates as one moves away from that point, but it does not do so in a fixed or regular way.</i>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup> Since it can be said that humans are generally creatures of <a href="/wiki/Habituation" title="Habituation">habit</a>, the state of a person's home has been known to physiologically influence their <a href="/wiki/Behavior" title="Behavior">behavior</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emotion" title="Emotion">emotions</a>, and overall <a href="/wiki/Mental_health" title="Mental health">mental health</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> People may become <a href="/wiki/Homesickness" title="Homesickness">homesick</a> when they leave their home over an extended period of time. Places like homes can trigger self-reflection, thoughts about who someone is or used to be or who they might become.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup> These types of reflections also occur in places where there is a collective historical identity, such as <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Gettysburg" title="Battle of Gettysburg">Gettysburg</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ground_Zero" title="Ground Zero">Ground Zero</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Popular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sayings" title="Sayings">sayings</a> include  "<i>a man's home is his castle</i>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> "<i>there is no place like home</i>", "<i>home sweet home</i>", "<i>to be at home</i>", "<i>home away from home</i>", "<i>make yourself at home</i>", "<i>you can never go home again</i>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> "<i>home is where the heart is</i>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup> and "<i>home is where you hang your hat</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>The word <i>home</i> can be used for various types of residential community <a href="/wiki/Institution" title="Institution">institutions</a> in which people can live, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nursing_home" title="Nursing home">nursing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Retirement_home" title="Retirement home">retirement homes</a> for seniors, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foster_home" title="Foster home">foster homes</a>, etc. Short-term accommodation in a treatment facility for several weeks is unlikely to be considered 'home'.
</p><p>Homes may be lost in ways ranging from the upheavals of <a href="/wiki/Natural_disaster" title="Natural disaster">natural disasters</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup> fraud/theft, <a href="/wiki/Arson" title="Arson">arson</a>, or war-related destruction, to the more common voluntary sale, loss for one or more occupants on relationship breakdown, <a href="/wiki/Eminent_domain" title="Eminent domain">expropriation</a> by government or legislated cause, repossession/<a href="/wiki/Foreclosure" title="Foreclosure">foreclosure</a> to pay secured debts, <a href="/wiki/Eviction" title="Eviction">eviction</a> by landlords, disposal by time-limited means – <a href="/wiki/Lease" title="Lease">lease</a>, or absolute gift.  Jurisdiction-dependent means of home loss include <a href="/wiki/Adverse_possession" title="Adverse possession">adverse possession</a>, unpaid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Property_taxation" title="Property taxation">property taxation</a> and corruption such as in circumstances of a <a href="/wiki/Failed_state" title="Failed state">failed state</a>.  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personal_insolvency" title="Personal insolvency">Personal insolvency</a>, development or sustaining of <a href="/wiki/Mental_disorder" title="Mental disorder">mental illness</a> or severe physical incapacity without affordable <a href="/wiki/Care_work" title="Care work">domestic care</a> commonly lead to a change of home.  The underlying character of a home may be debased by <a href="/wiki/Structural_integrity_and_failure" title="Structural integrity and failure">structural defects</a>, natural <a href="/wiki/Subsidence" title="Subsidence">subsidence</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Property_maintenance" title="Property maintenance">neglect</a> or <a href="/wiki/Soil_contamination" title="Soil contamination">soil contamination</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Refugee" title="Refugee">Refugees</a> are people who have fled their homes due to violence or <a href="/wiki/Persecution" title="Persecution">persecution</a>. They may seek temporary housing in a shelter or they may claim <a href="/wiki/Right_of_asylum" title="Right of asylum">asylum</a> in another country in an attempt to relocate permanently.
</p>
