<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Membrane gas separation</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>Gas mixtures can be effectively separated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthetic_membranes" title="Synthetic membranes">synthetic membranes</a> made from polymers such as <a href="/wiki/Polyamide" title="Polyamide">polyamide</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cellulose_acetate" title="Cellulose acetate">cellulose acetate</a>, or from ceramic materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-handbook_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-handbook-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Flux_distribution_inside_the_fiber.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="1284" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Flux_distribution_inside_the_fiber.jpg/220px-Flux_distribution_inside_the_fiber.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Flux_distribution_inside_the_fiber.jpg/330px-Flux_distribution_inside_the_fiber.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Flux_distribution_inside_the_fiber.jpg/440px-Flux_distribution_inside_the_fiber.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Flux_distribution_inside_the_fiber.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Membrane cartridge</div></div></div>
<p>While polymeric membranes are economical and technologically useful, they are bounded by their performance, known as the Robeson limit (permeability must be sacrificed for selectivity and vice versa).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> This limit affects polymeric membrane use for CO<sub>2</sub> separation from flue gas streams, since mass transport becomes limiting and CO2 separation becomes very expensive due to low permeabilities. Membrane materials have expanded into the realm of <a href="/wiki/Silicon_dioxide" title="Silicon dioxide">silica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zeolite" title="Zeolite">zeolites</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal-organic_framework" title="Metal-organic framework">metal-organic frameworks</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Perovskite" title="Perovskite">perovskites</a> due to their strong thermal and chemical resistance as well as high tunability (ability to be modified and functionalized), leading to increased permeability and selectivity. <a href="/wiki/Membrane" title="Membrane">Membranes</a> can be used for separating gas mixtures where they act as a permeable barrier through which different compounds move across at different rates or not move at all. The membranes can be nanoporous, polymer, etc. and the gas molecules penetrate according to their size, <a href="/wiki/Diffusivity" title="Diffusivity">diffusivity</a>, or solubility.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Membrane_Governing_Methodology">Membrane Governing Methodology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Membrane_gas_separation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Membrane Governing Methodology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:752px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mechanisms_of_transport_in_membranes.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="415" data-file-width="1401" height="222" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Mechanisms_of_transport_in_membranes.jpg/750px-Mechanisms_of_transport_in_membranes.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Mechanisms_of_transport_in_membranes.jpg/1125px-Mechanisms_of_transport_in_membranes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1e/Mechanisms_of_transport_in_membranes.jpg 2x" width="750"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mechanisms_of_transport_in_membranes.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>(a) Bulk flow through pores; (b) Knudsen diffusion through pores; (c) molecular sieving; (d) solution diffusion through dense membranes.</div></div></div>
<p>There are 3 main <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">diffusion</a> mechanisms, the first (b), <a href="/wiki/Knudsen_diffusion" title="Knudsen diffusion">Knudsen diffusion</a> holds at very low pressures where lighter molecules can move across a membrane faster than heavy ones, in a material with reasonably large pores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smit_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smit-3">[3]</a></sup> The second (c), <a href="/wiki/Molecular_sieve" title="Molecular sieve">molecular sieving</a>, is the case where the pores of the membrane are too small to let one component pass, a process which is typically not practical in gas applications, as the molecules are too small to design relevant pores. In these cases the movement of molecules is best described by pressure-driven convective flow through capillaries, which is quantified by <a href="/wiki/Darcy%27s_law" title="Darcy's law">Darcy’s Law</a>. However, the more general model in gas applications is the solution-diffusion (d) where particles are first dissolved onto the membrane and then diffuse through it both at different rates. This model is employed when the pores in the polymer membrane appear and disappear faster relative to the movement of the particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In a typical membrane system the incoming feed stream is separated into two components: permeant and retentate. Permeant is the gas that travels across the membrane and the retentate is what is left of the feed. On both sides of the membrane, a gradient of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_potential" title="Chemical potential">chemical potential</a> is maintained by a pressure difference which is the driving force for the gas molecules to pass through. The ease of transport of each species is quantified by the <a href="/wiki/Permeation" title="Permeation">permeability</a>, P<sub>i</sub>. With the assumptions of ideal mixing on both sides of the membrane, <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas_law" title="Ideal gas law">ideal gas law</a>, constant diffusion coefficient and <a href="/wiki/Henry%27s_law" title="Henry's law">Henry’s Law</a>, the flux of a species can be related to the pressure difference by <a href="/wiki/Fick%27s_laws_of_diffusion" title="Fick's laws of diffusion">Fick’s Law</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smit_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smit-3">[3]</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Membrane_Materials_for_Carbon_Capture_in_Flue_Gas_Streams">Membrane Materials for Carbon Capture in Flue Gas Streams</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Membrane_gas_separation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Membrane Materials for Carbon Capture in Flue Gas Streams">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The material of the membrane plays an important role in its ability to provide the desired performance characteristics. It is optimal to have a membrane with a high permeability and sufficient selectivity and it is also important to match the membrane properties to that of the system operating conditions (for example pressures and gas composition).
</p><p>Synthetic membranes are made from a variety of polymers including polyethylene, polyamides, polyimides, cellulose acetate, polysulphone and polydimethylsiloxan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gas_sep_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-gas_sep-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polymer_Membranes">Polymer Membranes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Membrane_gas_separation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Polymer Membranes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polymeric_membranes" title="Polymeric membranes">Polymeric membranes</a> are a common option for use in the capture of CO<sub>2</sub> from flue gas because of the maturity of the technology in a variety of industries, namely petrochemicals. The ideal polymer membrane has both a high <a href="/wiki/Reactivity%E2%80%93selectivity_principle" title="Reactivity–selectivity principle">selectivity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Semipermeable_membrane" title="Semipermeable membrane">permeability</a>. Polymer membranes are examples of systems that are dominated by the solution-diffusion mechanism. The membrane is considered to have holes which the gas can dissolve (solubility) and the molecules can move from one cavity to the other (diffusion).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smit_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Smit-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>It was discovered by Robeson in the early 1990s that polymers with a high selectivity have a low permeability and opposite is true; materials with a low selectivity have a high permeability. This is best illustrated in a Robeson plot where the selectivity is plotted as a function of the CO<sub>2</sub> permeation. In this plot, the upper bound of selectivity is approximately a linear function of the permeability. It was found that the solubility in polymers is mostly constant but the diffusion coefficients vary significantly and this is where the engineering of the material occurs. Somewhat intuitively, the materials with the highest diffusion coefficients have a more open pore structure, thus losing selectivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> There are two methods that researchers are using to break the Robeson limit, one of these is the use of glassy polymers whose phase transition and changes in mechanical properties make it appear that the material is absorbing molecules and thus surpasses the upper limit. The second method of pushing the boundaries of the Robeson limit is by the facilitated transport method. As previously stated, the solubility of polymers is typically fairly constant but the facilitated transport method uses a chemical reaction to enhance the permeability of one component without changing the selectivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merkel_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Merkel-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nanoporous_Membranes">Nanoporous Membranes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Membrane_gas_separation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nanoporous Membranes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:442px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Microscopic_model_of_a_nanoporous_membrane.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="360" data-file-width="926" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Microscopic_model_of_a_nanoporous_membrane.jpg/440px-Microscopic_model_of_a_nanoporous_membrane.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Microscopic_model_of_a_nanoporous_membrane.jpg/660px-Microscopic_model_of_a_nanoporous_membrane.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Microscopic_model_of_a_nanoporous_membrane.jpg/880px-Microscopic_model_of_a_nanoporous_membrane.jpg 2x" width="440"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Microscopic_model_of_a_nanoporous_membrane.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Microscopic model of a nanoporous membrane. The white open area represents the area the molecule can pass through and the dark blue areas represent the membrane walls. The membrane channels consists of cavities and windows. The energy of the molecules in the cavity is U<sub>c</sub> and the energy of a particle in the window is U<sub>w</sub>.</div></div></div>
<p>Nanoporous membranes are fundamentally different than polymer-based membranes in that their chemistry is different and that they do not follow the Robeson limit for a variety of reasons. The simplified figure of a nanoporous membrane shows a small portion of an example membrane structure with cavities and windows. The white portion represents the area where the molecule can move and the blue shaded areas represent the walls of the structure. In the engineering of these membranes, the size of the cavity (L<sub>cy</sub> x L<sub>cz</sub>) and window region (L<sub>wy</sub> x L<sub>wz</sub>) can be modified so that the desired permeation is achieved. It has been shown that the permeability of a membrane is the production of adsorption and diffusion. In low loading conditions, the adsorption can be computed by the Henry coefficient.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smit_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Smit-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>If the assumption is made that the energy of a particle does not change when moving through this structure, only the entropy of the molecules changes based on the size of the openings. If we first consider changes the cavity geometry, the larger the cavity, the larger the entropy of the absorbed molecules which thus makes the Henry coefficient larger. For diffusion, an increase in entropy will lead to a decrease in free energy which in turn leads to a decrease in the diffusion coefficient. Conversely, changing the window geometry will primarily effect the diffusion of the molecules and not the Henry coefficient.
</p><p>In summary, by using the above simplified analysis, it is possible to understand why the upper limit of the Robeson line does not hold for nanostructures. In the analysis, both the diffusion and Henry coefficients can be modified without affecting the permeability of the material which thus can exceed the upper limit for polymer membranes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smit_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Smit-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Silica_Membranes">Silica Membranes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Membrane_gas_separation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Silica Membranes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Silica membranes are <a href="/wiki/Mesoporous_material" title="Mesoporous material">mesoporous</a> and can be made with high uniformity (the same structure throughout the membrane). The high porosity of these membranes gives them very high permeabilities. Synthesized membranes have smooth surfaces and can be modified on the surface to drastically improve selectivity. Functionalizing silica membrane surfaces with amine containing molecules (on the surface <a href="/wiki/Silanol" title="Silanol">silanol</a> groups) allows the membranes to separate CO<sub>2</sub> from flue gas streams more effectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> Surface functionalization (and thus chemistry) can be tuned to be more efficient for wet flue gas streams as compared to dry flue gas streams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> While previously, silica membranes were impractical due to their technical scalability and cost (they are very difficult to produce in an economical manner on a large scale), there have been demonstrations of a simple method of producing silica membranes on hollow polymeric supports. These demonstrations indicate that economical materials and methods can effectively separate CO<sub>2</sub> and N<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-11">[11]</a></sup> Ordered mesoporous silica membranes have shown considerable potential for surface modification that allows for ease of CO<sub>2</sub> separation. Surface functionalization with <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amines</a> leads to the reversible formation of <a href="/wiki/Carbamate" title="Carbamate">carbamates</a> (during CO<sub>2</sub> flow), increasing CO<sub>2</sub> selectivity significantly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Zeolite_Membranes">Zeolite Membranes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Membrane_gas_separation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Zeolite Membranes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Zeolite-ZSM-5-vdW.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1420" data-file-width="1876" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Zeolite-ZSM-5-vdW.png/220px-Zeolite-ZSM-5-vdW.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Zeolite-ZSM-5-vdW.png/330px-Zeolite-ZSM-5-vdW.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Zeolite-ZSM-5-vdW.png/440px-Zeolite-ZSM-5-vdW.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Zeolite-ZSM-5-vdW.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A typical zeolite. Thin layers of this crystalline zeolite structure can act as a membrane, since CO<sub>2</sub> can adsorb inside of the pores.</div></div></div>
<p>Zeolites are crystalline <a href="/wiki/Aluminosilicate" title="Aluminosilicate">aluminosilicates</a> with a regular repeating structure of molecular-sized pores. Zeolite membranes selectively separate molecules based on pore size and polarity and are thus highly tunable to specific gas separation processes. In general, smaller molecules and those with stronger zeolite-<a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">adsorption</a> properties are adsorbed onto zeolite membranes with larger selectivity. The capacity to discriminate based on both molecular size and adsorption affinity makes zeolite membranes an attractive candidate for CO<sub>2</sub> separation from N<sub>2</sub>, CH<sub>4</sub>, and H<sub>2</sub>.
</p><p>Scientists have found that the gas-phase enthalpy (heat) of adsorption on zeolites increases as follows: H<sub>2</sub> &lt; CH<sub>4</sub> &lt; N<sub>2</sub> &lt; CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> It is generally accepted that CO<sub>2</sub> has the largest adsorption energy because it has the largest <a href="/wiki/Quadrupole" title="Quadrupole">quadrupole moment</a>, thereby increasing its affinity for charged or polar zeolite pores. At low temperatures, zeolite adsorption-capacity is large and the high concentration of adsorbed CO<sub>2</sub> molecules blocks the flow of other gases. Therefore, at lower temperatures, CO<sub>2</sub> selectively permeates through zeolite pores. Several recent research efforts have focused on developing new zeolite membranes that maximize the CO<sub>2</sub> selectivity by taking advantage of the low-temperature blocking phenomena.
</p><p>Researchers have synthesized Y-type (Si:Al&gt;3) zeolite membranes which achieve room-temperature separation factors of 100 and 21 for CO<sub>2</sub>/N<sub>2</sub> and CO<sub>2</sub>/CH<sub>4</sub> mixtures respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> <a class="external text" href="http://america.iza-structure.org/IZA-SC/framework.php?STC=DDR" rel="nofollow">DDR-type</a> and <a class="external text" href="http://www.acsmaterial.com/sapo-250.html" rel="nofollow">SAPO-34</a> membranes have also shown promise in separating CO<sub>2</sub> and CH<sub>4</sub> at a variety of pressures and feed compositions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Metal-organic_Framework_.28MOF.29_Membranes"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Metal-organic_Framework_(MOF)_Membranes">Metal-organic Framework (MOF) Membranes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Membrane_gas_separation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Metal-organic Framework (MOF) Membranes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There have been advances in <a href="/wiki/Zeolitic_imidazolate_framework" title="Zeolitic imidazolate framework">zeolitic-imidazolate frameworks</a> (ZIFs), a subclass of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal-organic_framework" title="Metal-organic framework">metal-organic frameworks</a> (MOFs), that have allowed them to be useful for carbon dioxide separation from flue gas streams. Extensive modeling has been performed to demonstrate the value of using MOFs as membranes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> MOF materials are adsorption-based, and thus can be tuned to achieve selectivity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The drawback to MOF systems is stability in water and other compounds present in flue gas streams. Select materials, such as ZIF-8, have demonstrated stability in water and benzene, contents often present in flue gas mixtures. ZIF-8 can be synthesized as a membrane on a porous alumina support and has proven to be effective at separating CO<sub>2</sub> from flue gas streams. At similar CO<sub>2</sub>/CH<sub>4</sub> selectivity to Y-type zeolite membranes, ZIF-8 membranes achieve unprecedented CO<sub>2</sub> permeance, two orders of magnitude above the previous standard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CMR1.PNG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="369" data-file-width="563" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/CMR1.PNG/220px-CMR1.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/CMR1.PNG/330px-CMR1.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/CMR1.PNG/440px-CMR1.PNG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CMR1.PNG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Structure of a perovskite. A membrane would consist of a thin layer of the perovskite structure.</div></div></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Perovskite_Membranes">Perovskite Membranes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Membrane_gas_separation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Perovskite Membranes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Perovskite" title="Perovskite">Perovskite</a> are mixed metal oxide with a well-defined cubic structure and a general formula of ABO<sub>3</sub>, where A is an <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_earth_metal" title="Alkaline earth metal">alkaline earth</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanide</a> element and B is a <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metal</a>. These materials are attractive for CO<sub>2</sub> separation because of the tunability of the metal sites as well as their stabilities at elevated temperatures.
</p><p>The separation of CO<sub>2</sub> from N<sub>2</sub> was investigated with an α-alumina membrane impregnated with BaTiO<sub>3</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> It was found that adsorption of CO<sub>2</sub> was favorable at high temperatures due to an endothermic interaction between CO<sub>2</sub> and the material, promoting mobile CO<sub>2</sub> that enhanced CO<sub>2</sub> adsorption-desorption rate and surface diffusion. The experimental separation factor of CO<sub>2</sub> to N<sub>2</sub> was found to be 1.1-1.2 at 100<sup>o</sup>C to 500<sup>o</sup>C, which is higher than the separation factor limit of 0.8 predicted by <a href="/wiki/Knudsen_diffusion" title="Knudsen diffusion">Knudsen diffusion</a>. Though the separation factor was low due to pinholes observed in the membrane, this demonstrates the potential of perovskite materials in their selective surface chemistry for CO<sub>2</sub> separation.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_Membrane_Technologies">Other Membrane Technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Membrane_gas_separation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other Membrane Technologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In special cases other materials can be utilized; for example, <b>palladium membranes</b> permit transport solely of hydrogen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In addition to palladium membranes (which are typically palladium silver alloys to stop embrittlement of the alloy at lower temperature) there is also a significant research effort looking into finding non-precious metal alternatives. Although slow kinetics of exchange on the surface of the membrane and tendency for the membranes to crack or disintegrate after a number of duty cycles or during cooling are problems yet to be fully solved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_Status_of_CO2_Capture_with_Membranes">Current Status of CO<sub>2</sub> Capture with Membranes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Membrane_gas_separation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Current Status of CO2 Capture with Membranes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A great deal of research has been undertaken to utilize membranes instead of absorption or adsorption for carbon capture from flue gas streams, however, no current projects exist that utilize membranes. Process engineering along with new developments in materials have shown that membranes have the greatest potential for low energy penalty and cost compared to competing technologies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smit_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-Smit-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merkel_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Merkel-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brunetti_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brunetti-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Membrane_gas_separation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Today, membranes are used for commercial separations involving: N<sub>2</sub> from air, H<sub>2</sub> from ammonia in the <a href="/wiki/Haber_process" title="Haber process">Haber-Bosch process</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural-gas_processing" title="Natural-gas processing">natural gas purification</a>, and tertiary-level <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">Enhanced Oil Recovery</a> supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bernardo_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bernardo-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Single-stage membrane operations involve a single membrane with one selectivity value. Single-stage membranes were first used in natural gas purification, separating CO<sub>2</sub> from methane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bernardo_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bernardo-24">[24]</a></sup> A disadvantage of single-stage membranes is the loss of product in the permeate due to the constraints imposed by the single selectivity value. Increasing the selectivity reduces the amount of product lost in the permeate, but comes at the cost of requiring a larger pressure difference to process an equivalent amount of a flue stream. In practice, the maximum pressure ratio economically possible is around 5:1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baker_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>To combat the loss of product in the membrane permeate, engineers use “cascade processes” in which the permeate is recompressed and interfaced with additional, higher selectivity membranes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bernardo_24-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bernardo-24">[24]</a></sup> The retentate streams can be recycled, which achieves a better yield of product.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Need_for_multi-stage_process">Need for multi-stage process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Membrane_gas_separation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Need for multi-stage process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Single stage membranes devices are not feasible for obtaining a high concentration of separated material in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Permeate" title="Permeate">permeate</a> stream. This is due to the pressure ratio limit that is economically unrealistic to exceed. Therefore, the use of multi stage membranes is required to concentrate the permeate stream. The use of a second stage allows for less membrane area and power to be used. This is because of the higher concentration that passes the second stage, as well as the lower volume of gas for the pump to process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baker_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merkel_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Merkel-9">[9]</a></sup> Other factors, such as adding another stage that uses air to concentrate the stream further reduces cost by increasing concentration within the feed stream.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merkel_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Merkel-9">[9]</a></sup> Additional methods such as combining multiple types of separation methods allow for variation in creating economical process designs.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Membrane_Use_in_Hybrid_Processes">Membrane Use in Hybrid Processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Membrane_gas_separation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Membrane Use in Hybrid Processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hybrid processes have long standing history with gas separation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>  Typically, membranes are integrated into already existing processes such that they can be retrofitted into already existing carbon capture systems.
</p><p>MTR, Membrane Technology and Research Inc., and <a href="/wiki/University_of_Texas_at_Austin" title="University of Texas at Austin">UT Austin</a> have worked to create hybrid processes, utilizing both absorption and membranes, for CO<sub>2</sub> capture.  First, an <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_scrubber" title="Carbon dioxide scrubber">absorption</a> column using <a href="/wiki/Piperazine" title="Piperazine">piperazine</a> as a solvent absorbs about half the carbon dioxide in the flue gas, then the use of a membrane results in 90% capture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freeman_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Freeman-27">[27]</a></sup>  A parallel setup is also, with the membrane and absorption processes occurring simultaneously.  Generally, these processes are most effective when the highest content of carbon dioxide enters the amine absorption column. Incorporating hybrid design processes allows for retrofitting into <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> power plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freeman_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Freeman-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hybrid processes can also use <a href="/wiki/Air_separation" title="Air separation">cryogenic distillation</a> and membranes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lin_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lin-28">[28]</a></sup>  For example, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> can be separated, first using cryogenic gas separation, whereby most of the carbon dioxide exits first, then using a membrane process to separate the remaining carbon dioxide, after which it is recycled for further attempts at cryogenic separation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lin_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lin-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cost_Analysis_of_Current_Membrane_Technology_for_CCS_Relative_to_Well_Established_Techniques">Cost Analysis of Current Membrane Technology for CCS Relative to Well Established Techniques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Membrane_gas_separation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Cost Analysis of Current Membrane Technology for CCS Relative to Well Established Techniques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cost limits the pressure ratio in a membrane <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> separation stage to a value of 5; higher pressure rations eliminate any economic viability for CO<sub>2</sub> capture using membrane processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merkel_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-Merkel-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huang_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Huang-29">[29]</a></sup>  Recent studies have demonstrated that multi-stage CO<sub>2</sub> capture/separation processes using membranes can be economically competitive with older and more common technologies such as amine-based <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_scrubber" title="Carbon dioxide scrubber">absorption</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merkel_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-Merkel-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lin_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lin-28">[28]</a></sup> Currently, both membrane and amine-based absorption processes can be designed to yield a 90% CO<sub>2</sub> capture rate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brunetti_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brunetti-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merkel_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-Merkel-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huang_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Huang-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hao_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hao-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freeman_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-Freeman-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lin_28-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lin-28">[28]</a></sup> For <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture</a> at an average 600 MW coal-fired power plant, the cost of CO<sub>2</sub> capture using amine-based absorption is in the $40–100 per ton of CO<sub>2</sub> range, while the cost of CO<sub>2</sub> capture using current membrane technology (including current process design schemes) is about $23 per ton of CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merkel_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-Merkel-9">[9]</a></sup> Additionally, running an amine-based absorption process at an average 600 MW coal-fired power plant consumes about 30% of the energy generated by the power plant, while running a membrane process requires about 16% of the energy generated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merkel_9-8"><a href="#cite_note-Merkel-9">[9]</a></sup> CO<sub>2</sub> transport (e.g. to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">geologic sequestration</a> sites, or to be used for <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">EOR</a>) costs about $2–5 per ton of CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merkel_9-9"><a href="#cite_note-Merkel-9">[9]</a></sup> This cost is the same for all types of CO<sub>2</sub> capture/separation processes such as membrane separation and absorption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merkel_9-10"><a href="#cite_note-Merkel-9">[9]</a></sup> In terms of dollars per ton of  captured CO<sub>2</sub>, the least expensive membrane processes being studied at this time are multi-step <a href="/wiki/Countercurrent_exchange" title="Countercurrent exchange">counter-current</a> flow/sweep processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brunetti_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Brunetti-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merkel_9-11"><a href="#cite_note-Merkel-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huang_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-Huang-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hao_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hao-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freeman_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-Freeman-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lin_28-4"><a href="#cite_note-Lin-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
