<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Findhorn Ecovillage</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>
<b>Findhorn Ecovillage</b> is an experimental architectural community project based at The Park, in <a href="/wiki/Moray" title="Moray">Moray</a>, Scotland, near the village of <a href="/wiki/Findhorn" title="Findhorn">Findhorn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-local_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-local-1">[1]</a></sup> The project's main aim is to demonstrate a <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a> in environmental, social, and economic terms. Work began in the early 1980s under the auspices of the <a href="/wiki/Findhorn_Foundation" title="Findhorn Foundation">Findhorn Foundation</a> but now includes a wide diversity of organisations and activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lovie_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lovie-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ecovillage_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ecovillage-3">[3]</a></sup> Numerous different ecological techniques are in use, and the project has won a variety of awards, including the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Human_Settlements_Programme" title="United Nations Human Settlements Programme">UN-Habitat</a> Best Practice Designation in 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burns_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burns-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Barrel_House.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Barrel_House.jpg/220px-Barrel_House.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Barrel_House.jpg/330px-Barrel_House.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Barrel_House.jpg/440px-Barrel_House.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Barrel_House.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Barrel House—the first new dwelling to be created at Findhorn Ecovillage.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ScotlandMoray.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3411" data-file-width="2733" height="275" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/ScotlandMoray.png/220px-ScotlandMoray.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/ScotlandMoray.png/330px-ScotlandMoray.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/ScotlandMoray.png/440px-ScotlandMoray.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ScotlandMoray.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Moray's location in Scotland.</div></div></div>
<p>A recent independent study<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SDRC_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-SDRC-5">[5]</a></sup> concludes that the residents have the lowest <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a> of any community measured so far in the industrialised world and is also half of the UK average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Although the project has attracted some controversy,  the growing profile of <a href="/wiki/Environmental_movement" title="Environmental movement">environmental issues</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> has led to a degree of mainstream acceptance of its ecological ethos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stern_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stern-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guggenheim_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guggenheim-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cavendish_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cavendish-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Beginnings">Beginnings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Findhorn_Ecovillage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Beginnings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The October 1982 Conference ‘Building a Planetary Village’<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scott_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scott-10">[10]</a></sup> hosted by the <a href="/wiki/Findhorn_Foundation" title="Findhorn Foundation">Findhorn Foundation</a> marked the beginning of serious attempts by the <a href="/wiki/Intentional_community" title="Intentional community">intentional community</a>, which had existed at <a href="/wiki/Findhorn" title="Findhorn">Findhorn</a> since 1962<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riddell_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Riddell-11">[11]</a></sup> to demonstrate a human settlement that could be considered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainable" title="Sustainable">sustainable</a> in environmental, social, and economic terms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McLaughlin_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-McLaughlin-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The term ‘<a href="/wiki/Ecovillage" title="Ecovillage">ecovillage</a>’ later came to be used to describe such experiments and in 1995 the first international conference of ecovillages, <i>Ecovillages and Sustainable Communities for the 21st Century</i>, was held in Findhorn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bang_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bang-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dawson_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dawson-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>At first almost all of the activities this involved, such as eco-house construction, a 75 kW <a href="/wiki/Vestas" title="Vestas">Vestas</a> wind  turbine and an ecological waste water treatment system were undertaken by the Findhorn Foundation itself, or its trading company New Findhorn Directions Ltd.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walker_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-walker-15">[15]</a></sup> However, from 1990 onwards a growing number of independent charities, businesses, small sister communities, independent practitioners and community bodies have grown up and significantly extended the size and diversity of ecological projects,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-findhorn_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-findhorn-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Metcalf_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Metcalf-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pewters_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pewters-18">[18]</a></sup> some of which are listed below. As of 2005 the Ecovillage has around 450 members centred around The Park (the main campus on the southern edge of Findhorn), but also based at numerous locations in the nearby town of <a href="/wiki/Forres" title="Forres">Forres</a> and elsewhere in <a href="/wiki/Moray" title="Moray">Moray</a>. The project supports approximately 300 jobs in the Findhorn/Forres area and provides a total aggregate economic impact in excess of £5 million per annum in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Highlands" title="Scottish Highlands">Highlands</a> of Scotland as a whole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burns2003_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burns2003-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_of_eco-projects">Examples of eco-projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Findhorn_Ecovillage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Examples of eco-projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ecovillage" title="Ecovillage">Ecovillages</a> usually rely on a wide variety of approaches to minimise their ecological footprint.  Some of the most important used at Findhorn are listed here although it is important to bear in mind that in any given situation the single largest factor is likely to be the attitudes of the residents rather than the technologies themselves.  The national scale is far better researched than the micro scale, and at this level it is easy to observe that countries with similar levels of wealth may have quite different footprints — see for example Chambers (2004).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_building_codes">Ecological building codes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Findhorn_Ecovillage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecological building codes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PA120016.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/PA120016.JPG/220px-PA120016.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/PA120016.JPG/330px-PA120016.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/PA120016.JPG/440px-PA120016.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PA120016.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An eco-house at Findhorn with turf roof and <a href="/wiki/Solar_panel" title="Solar panel">solar panels</a></div></div></div>
<p>The site has a comprehensive building code that all new buildings are required to meet. It goes far beyond current UK building standards<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Talbott1993_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Talbott1993-20">[20]</a></sup> and includes features such as very efficient insulation (typically using products made from recycled paper), non-toxic organic paints, wood preservatives and boarding manufactured without the use of toxic glues or resins and roofing with natural clay tiles.
</p><p>There are a wide variety of other options used including low-energy light bulbs, 'breathing wall' construction allowing a controlled exchange of air &amp; vapour water conservation features and locally sourced materials. Shared facilities such as laundry, kitchens, lounges etc. are encouraged, thus avoiding unnecessary duplication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Talbott2001_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Talbott2001-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Most new buildings incorporate design features that invite passive solar radiation to reduce building heating needs, such as south-facing windows, conservatories and minimal wall openings on north walls. Sustainably harvested wood provides space heating for many homes both old and new, and an Ecovillage company supplies solar panels for domestic hot water heating to residential and commercial customers throughout the UK.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aessolar_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-aessolar-22">[22]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Highlands_and_Islands_Community_Energy_Company" title="Highlands and Islands Community Energy Company">Highlands and Islands Community Energy Company</a> recently awarded a grant for the installation of ground 
source heating for the proposed new Moray Arts Centre based at Findhorn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HIE_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-HIE-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Simply Build Green</i> — Talbott (1993) — is based on research undertaken at Findhorn Ecovillage and was the UK’s first technical guide to ecological housing.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eco-architecture">Eco-architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Findhorn_Ecovillage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Eco-architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first new dwelling to be constructed at the Ecovillage was a small house in the round made from a recycled <a href="/wiki/Whisky" title="Whisky">whisky</a> barrel. The idea was later developed and several buildings on this theme were constructed. Other innovative styles include a <a href="/wiki/Straw-bale_construction" title="Straw-bale construction">strawbale house</a>, and an <a href="/wiki/Earthship" title="Earthship">earthship</a> electricity sub-station. Over fifty new houses have now been constructed, some in exotic styles, others more prosaic in appearance.  The rigorous building codes contrast with an apparent absence of visual guidelines and the result is a diversity of design rather than a consistent approach to aesthetics.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Living_Machine">Living Machine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Findhorn_Ecovillage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Living Machine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FindhornLM.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/FindhornLM.jpg/220px-FindhornLM.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/FindhornLM.jpg/330px-FindhornLM.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/FindhornLM.jpg/440px-FindhornLM.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FindhornLM.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Living Machine wastewater treatment plant at Findhorn.</div></div></div>
<p>In 1995 <a href="/wiki/Jonathon_Porritt" title="Jonathon Porritt">Jonathon Porritt</a> opened Europe's first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Living_Machine" title="Living Machine">Living Machine</a> (also known as Eco-Restorers in The UK) at The Park campus. This is an ecologically engineered waste water treatment system which is designed to treat sewage from a population of up to 350 people and in common with a number of other such systems<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ltl_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-ltl-24">[24]</a></sup> also provides a research and educational facility to promote the technology.  It was constructed with assistance from the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>. The invention of Canadian scientist <a href="/wiki/John_Todd_(Canadian_biologist)" title="John Todd (Canadian biologist)">John Todd</a>, they use tanks containing diverse communities of bacteria, algae, micro-organisms, numerous species of plants and trees, snails, fish and other living creatures to treat the water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ltl_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-ltl-24">[24]</a></sup> At the end of the series of tanks, the resulting water is pure enough to be returned to the local water table. Plans to use the water for irrigation have been considered but not implemented to date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-West_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-West-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organic_food_production">Organic food production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Findhorn_Ecovillage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Organic food production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the most significant factors in the Ecovillage's low eco-footprint (see below) is its attitude to food production and consumption. Various smallholdings associated with the Ecovillage contribute to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Community_supported_agriculture" title="Community supported agriculture">community supported agriculture</a> or 'box' scheme which provides <a href="/wiki/Organic_food" title="Organic food">organic produce</a> for the local area, some of it grown using <a href="/wiki/Permaculture" title="Permaculture">permaculture</a> techniques. This horticulture '"provides more than 70% of the community's fresh food requirements"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-food_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-food-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EarthShare_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-EarthShare-27">[27]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_Community_Stores" title="Phoenix Community Stores">Phoenix Community Stores</a>, based at the Park, is one of the largest retailers of organic produce in northern Scotland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Phœnix_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Phœnix-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_park">Wind park</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Findhorn_Ecovillage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Wind park">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another reason for the low ecological impact of the settlement is the presence of four <a href="/wiki/Vestas" title="Vestas">Vestas</a> wind turbines which can generate up to 750 kW. These make the Park settlement net exporters of electricity produced from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renewable" title="Renewable">renewable</a> resources. The first V17 generator was installed in 1989 and three additional V29s were installed in March 2006. The original site was a <a href="/wiki/Trailer_park" title="Trailer park">caravan park</a>  and as a result the Ecovillage has its own private electricity grid. Most of the generation is used on-site with any surplus exported to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Grid_(UK)" title="National Grid (UK)">National Grid</a>.
</p>

<p>Since 2002 a <a href="/wiki/Local_currency" title="Local currency">local currency</a> has been in operation. Launched by Ekopia, the community’s <a href="/wiki/Development_trust" title="Development trust">development trust</a>, it is accepted by almost all Ecovillage organizations. There are roughly £20,000 of notes in circulation and issuing them has enabled Ekopia to make low interest loans and donations to support various initiatives including an ecological guest facility, the wind park (above) and the local Youth Project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The current issue, which is due to expire in 2011, is at par with sterling i.e. 1 Eko = £1, and notes are in one, five, ten and twenty denominations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eko_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eko-30">[30]</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_Footprint_Study">Ecological Footprint Study</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Findhorn_Ecovillage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Ecological Footprint Study">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Findhorn_wind_turbines.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Findhorn_wind_turbines.jpg/220px-Findhorn_wind_turbines.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Findhorn_wind_turbines.jpg/330px-Findhorn_wind_turbines.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Findhorn_wind_turbines.jpg/440px-Findhorn_wind_turbines.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Findhorn_wind_turbines.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The wind turbines at Findhorn, which make the Ecovillage a net exporter of electricity.</div></div></div>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a> is an attempt to measure the total environmental impact of human activities in a given area. The results measure the amount of land and water that the population requires to provide the resources they utilise and to absorb their wastes. The calculations used converts this into a measure of land area, global hectares (gha). It is widely accepted<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SDRC_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-SDRC-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chambers_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chambers-31">[31]</a></sup> that the human impact on planet Earth now exceeds the available capacity of natural systems to sustain it, and that western countries typically have a resource usage that ‘would require three planets’ to maintain if every citizen on Earth had a similar impact. In October 2006 preliminary results of the ecological footprint study<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SDRC_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-SDRC-5">[5]</a></sup> for the Findhorn Ecovillage undertaken by The <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Development_Research_Centre" title="Sustainable Development Research Centre">Sustainable Development Research Centre</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UHI_Millennium_Institute" title="UHI Millennium Institute">UHI Millennium Institute</a> in collaboration with the <a href="/wiki/Stockholm_Environment_Institute" title="Stockholm Environment Institute">Stockholm Environment Institute</a> confirmed that ecovillages can tread significantly more lightly on the Earth than more conventional communities.
</p><p>The study found that residents of The Park and the nearby campus of <a href="/wiki/Cluny_Hill" title="Cluny Hill">Cluny Hill</a> College have, on average, a footprint of 2.71 hectares per capita, a little over half the UK national average of 5.4 hectares. (By comparison the comparable figure for the United States is 9.5gha, whilst <a href="/wiki/Uzbekistan" title="Uzbekistan">Uzbekistan</a> is rated at the average 'global earthshare' of 1.9gha).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chambers_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chambers-31">[31]</a></sup> Of particular significance are the results relating to food, ‘home and energy' use, and  'consumables and personal possessions' which have 35%, 27% and 44% of the national average respectively. Findhorn residents have an eco-footprint some 13% lower than those at the London eco-housing development, <a href="/wiki/BedZED" title="BedZED">BedZED</a>. The study is also intending to measure the additional impact caused by visitors to the Ecovillage but results are not yet available.
</p>
<h2><span id="Awards.2C_UN_connections_and_critiques"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards,_UN_connections_and_critiques">Awards, UN connections and critiques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Findhorn_Ecovillage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Awards, UN connections and critiques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At a time when resource depletion and global warming are international concerns it is not perhaps surprising that these activities should receive various accolades.
</p>
<ul><li>In September 1995 The Findhorn Ecological Village Project was awarded the 'We the Peoples 50 Communities' award given by the Friends of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> as part of the 50th Anniversary of the UN Celebrations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burns_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Burns-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>In October 1998 the Ecovillage Project, together with other 100 leadership Initiatives "which are inspiring innovative action on Earth", received Best Practice Designation from the United Nations Centre for Human Settlements — UNCHS (Habitat) and Dubai Municipality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burns_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Burns-4">[4]</a></sup></li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PA120021.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/PA120021.jpg/220px-PA120021.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/PA120021.jpg/330px-PA120021.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/PA120021.jpg/440px-PA120021.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PA120021.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A larger, 200 m² plus Eco-house.</div></div></div>
<ul><li>In September 2000 the conservation charity Trees for Life received the Millennium Marque Award in recognition of its work in helping to restore the Caledonian Forest in <a href="/wiki/Glen_Affric" title="Glen Affric">Glen Affric</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trees_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trees-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
<li>The following year the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNEP" title="UNEP">United Nations Environment Programme</a> noted that "most of the problems identified by <a href="/wiki/Agenda_21" title="Agenda 21">Agenda 21</a> have their roots at the local level, therefore UNEP appreciates your significant contribution at the local level to models for holistic and sustainable living in harmony with the environment."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burns_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Burns-4">[4]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Various critiques exist in print, although these tend to concentrate on the eclectic spirituality of the <a href="/wiki/Findhorn_Foundation" title="Findhorn Foundation">Findhorn Foundation</a> rather than the environmental aspects of the Ecovillage.  There is a statement of core values for the Ecovillage and its associated community called ‘Common Ground’<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Common_Ground_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Common_Ground-33">[33]</a></sup> but other than affirming a commitment to "active spiritual practice" (which is diversely understood and interpreted<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walker_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-walker-15">[15]</a></sup>) it concentrates largely on preferred interpersonal behaviours rather than prescribing a spiritual philosophy.  Objections were raised by a local pressure group against the expansion of the wind park<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elgin_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Elgin-34">[34]</a></sup> but the concerns of local people<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-local_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-local-1">[1]</a></sup> about the Ecovillage appear to have reduced, partly due to the proactive stance of the ecovillagers themselves,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walker_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-walker-15">[15]</a></sup> especially perhaps as a result of the gift of some amenity land to a local <a href="/wiki/Land_trust" title="Land trust">land trust</a> by Duneland Ltd., an Ecovillage company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duneland_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Duneland-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bay_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-bay-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>While many houses are modest in size some are large, exceeding 200 m² in built area for a single household. Diversity of lifestyle may well be part of any human settlement but the magnitude of such structures hints that the commitment to ‘living lightly on the Earth’ is not as whole-hearted as first appearances may suggest. Nonetheless, working examples of how a western lifestyle might be possible whilst living within the means of the Earth's renewable resources to support it are likely to be of interest at a time when the consensus of mainstream opinion is moving  towards the view that ‘business as usual’ may no longer be an option.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stern_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stern-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
