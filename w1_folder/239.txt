<p>The <b>standard atmosphere</b> (symbol: atm) is a unit of <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> defined as <span style="white-space: nowrap">101<span style="margin-left: 0.25em">325</span></span> <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">Pa</a> (<span style="white-space: nowrap">1.013<span style="margin-left: 0.25em">25</span></span> bar). It is sometimes used as a <i>reference</i> or <i>standard pressure</i>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmosphere_(unit)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It was originally defined as the pressure exerted by 760 mm of mercury at 0 °C and standard gravity (<i>g</i> = <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7000980665000000000♠</span>9.806<span style="margin-left:.25em;">65</span> m/s<sup>2</sup></span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Resnick_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Resnick-1">[1]</a></sup>  It was used as a reference condition for physical and chemical properties, and was implicit in the definition of the Centigrade (later <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">Celsius</a>) scale of temperature by defining 100 °C as being the boiling point of water at this pressure. In 1954, the 10th Conférence Générale des Poids et Mesures (<a href="/wiki/General_Conference_on_Weights_and_Measures" title="General Conference on Weights and Measures">CGPM</a>) adopted <i>standard atmosphere</i> for general use and affirmed its definition of being precisely equal to <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7006101325000000000♠</span>1<span style="margin-left:.25em;">013</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">250</span></span> <a href="/wiki/Dyne" title="Dyne">dynes</a> per <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Square_centimetre" title="Square centimetre">square centimetre</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7005101325000000000♠</span>101<span style="margin-left:.25em;">325</span> <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">Pa</a></span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIPM_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-BIPM-2">[2]</a></sup> This defined both temperature and pressure independent of the properties of particular substance.  In addition (the CGPM noted) there had been some misapprehension that it "led some physicists to believe that this definition of the standard atmosphere was valid only for accurate work in thermometry."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIPM_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-BIPM-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> and in various industries, the reference pressure referred to in “<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_Temperature_and_Pressure" title="Standard Temperature and Pressure">Standard Temperature and Pressure</a>” (STP) was commonly 1 atm (101.325 kPa) but standards have since diverged; in 1982, the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Pure_and_Applied_Chemistry" title="International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry">International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry</a> (IUPAC) recommended that for the purposes of specifying the physical properties of substances, “<i>standard pressure</i>” should be precisely 100 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">kPa</a> (1 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">bar</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<dd>= 1 ata (atmosphere absolute). The ata unit is used in place of atm to indicate that the pressure shown is the total ambient pressure, compared to vacuum, of the system being calculated or measured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[4]</a></sup> For example, for underwater pressures, a pressure of 3.1 ata would mean that the 1 atm of the air above water is included in this value and the pressure due to water would total 2.1 atm.</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>Notes:</dd></dl>
<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-4"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-4">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Torr and mm-Hg, 0°C are often taken to be identical. For most practical purposes (to 5 significant digits), they are interchangeable.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-n2-5"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-n2_5-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-n2_5-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-n2_5-2"><sup><i><b>c</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">This is the customarily accepted value for cm–H<sub>2</sub>O, 4 °C. It is precisely the product of 1 kg-force per square centimeter (one technical atmosphere) times 1.013 25 (bar/atmosphere) divided by 0.980 665 (one gram-force). It is not accepted practice to define the value for water column based on a true physical realization of water (which would be 99.997 495% of this value because the true maximum density of <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Standard_Mean_Ocean_Water" title="Vienna Standard Mean Ocean Water">Vienna Standard Mean Ocean Water</a> is 0.999 974 95 kg/l at 3.984 °C). Also, this “physical realization” would <i>still</i> ignore the 8.285 cm–H<sub>2</sub>O reduction that would actually occur in a true physical realization due to the vapor pressure over water at 3.984 °C.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-n3-6"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-n3_6-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-n3_6-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NIST" title="NIST">NIST</a> value of 13.595 078(5) g/ml assumed for the density of Hg at 0 °C</span>
</li>
</ol></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_applications">Other applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmosphere_(unit)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scuba_diver" title="Scuba diver">Scuba divers</a> and others use the word <i>atmosphere</i> and "atm" in relation to pressures that are relative to mean atmospheric pressure at sea level (1.013 bar).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> For example, a partial pressure of oxygen is calibrated typically using air at sea level, so is expressed in units of atm.
</p><p>The old European unit <a href="/wiki/Technical_atmosphere" title="Technical atmosphere">technical atmosphere</a> (at) is equal to 1 <a href="/wiki/Kilogram-force_per_square_centimetre" title="Kilogram-force per square centimetre">kilogram-force per square centimetre</a> (kgf/cm<sup>2</sup>), <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7004980665000000000♠</span>98<span style="margin-left:.25em;">066</span>.5</span> Pa.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
