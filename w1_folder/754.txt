<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Balance of trade</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>balance of trade</b>, <b>commercial balance</b>, or <b>net exports</b> (sometimes symbolized as <b>NX</b>), is the difference between the monetary value of a nation's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exports" title="Exports">exports</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Imports" title="Imports">imports</a> over a certain period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Sometimes a distinction is made between a balance of trade for goods versus one for services. "Balance of trade" can be a misleading term because trade measures a <i>flow</i> of exports and imports over a given period of time, rather than a <i>balance</i> of exports and imports at a given point in time. Also, balance of trade does not mean that exports and imports are "in balance" with each other or anything else.
</p><p>If a country exports a greater value than it imports, it has a <b>trade surplus</b> or <b>positive balance</b>, and conversely, if a country imports a greater value than it exports, it has a <b>trade deficit</b> or <b>negative balance.</b> <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_net_exports" title="List of countries by net exports">About 60 out of 200 countries have a trade surplus</a>. The notion that bilateral trade deficits are bad in and of themselves is overwhelmingly rejected by trade experts and economists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:16_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:16-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:15_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:15-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The balance of trade forms part of the <a href="/wiki/Current_account" title="Current account">current account</a>, which includes other transactions such as income from the <a href="/wiki/Net_international_investment_position" title="Net international investment position">net international investment position</a> as well as international aid. If the current account is in surplus, the country's net international asset position increases correspondingly. Equally, a deficit decreases the net international asset position.
</p><p>The trade balance is identical to the difference between a country's output and its domestic demand (the difference between what goods a country produces and how many goods it buys from abroad; this does not include money re-spent on foreign stock, nor does it factor in the concept of importing goods to produce for the domestic market).
</p><p>Measuring the balance of trade can be problematic because of problems with recording and collecting data. As an illustration of this problem, when official data for all the world's countries are added up, exports exceed imports by almost 1%; it appears the world is running a positive balance of trade with itself. This cannot be true, because all transactions involve an equal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Credit_(finance)" title="Credit (finance)">credit</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Debit" title="Debit">debit</a> in the account of each nation. The discrepancy is widely believed to be explained by transactions intended to launder money or evade taxes, smuggling and other visibility problems. Especially for developing countries, the transaction statistics are likely to be inaccurate.
</p><p>Factors that can affect the balance of trade include:
</p>
<ul><li>The cost of production (land, labor, capital, taxes, incentives, etc.) in the exporting economy <i>vis-à-vis</i> those in the importing economy;</li>
<li>The cost and availability of raw materials, intermediate goods and other inputs;</li>
<li>Currency exchange rate movements;</li>
<li>Multilateral, bilateral and unilateral taxes or restrictions on trade;</li>
<li>Non-tariff barriers such as environmental, health or safety standards;</li>
<li>The availability of adequate foreign exchange with which to pay for imports; and</li>
<li>Prices of goods manufactured at home (influenced by the responsiveness of supply)</li></ul>
<p>In addition, the trade balance is likely to differ across the <a href="/wiki/Business_cycle" title="Business cycle">business cycle</a>. In export-led growth (such as oil and early industrial goods), the balance of trade will shift towards exports during an economic expansion.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, with domestic demand-led growth (as in the United States and Australia) the trade balance will shift towards imports at the same stage in the business cycle.
</p><p>The monetary balance of trade is different from the physical balance of trade<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> (which is expressed in amount of raw materials, known also as Total Material Consumption). Developed countries usually import a lot of raw materials from developing countries. Typically, these imported materials are transformed into finished products, and might be exported after adding value. Financial trade balance statistics conceal material flow. Most developed countries have a large physical trade deficit, because they consume more raw materials than they produce. Many<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (May 2016)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> civil society organisations claim this imbalance is predatory and campaign for <a href="/wiki/Ecological_debt" title="Ecological debt">ecological debt</a> repayment.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_examples">Historical examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balance_of_trade&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historical examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Foreign_trade_of_the_United_States" title="Foreign trade of the United States">Foreign trade of the United States</a></div>
<p>Many countries in early modern Europe adopted a policy of <a href="/wiki/Mercantilism" title="Mercantilism">mercantilism</a>, which theorized that a trade surplus was beneficial to a country, among other elements such as <a href="/wiki/Colonialism" title="Colonialism">colonialism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trade_barrier" title="Trade barrier">trade barriers</a> with other countries and their colonies.  (<a href="/wiki/Bullionism" title="Bullionism">Bullionism</a> was an early philosophy supporting mercantilism.)
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Merchandise_exports_(1870-1992).png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="526" data-file-width="779" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Merchandise_exports_%281870-1992%29.png/220px-Merchandise_exports_%281870-1992%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Merchandise_exports_%281870-1992%29.png/330px-Merchandise_exports_%281870-1992%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Merchandise_exports_%281870-1992%29.png/440px-Merchandise_exports_%281870-1992%29.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Merchandise_exports_(1870-1992).png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Merchandise exports (1870–1992)</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Trade_policy,_exports_and_growth_in_selected_European_countries.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="615" data-file-width="833" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Trade_policy%2C_exports_and_growth_in_selected_European_countries.png/220px-Trade_policy%2C_exports_and_growth_in_selected_European_countries.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Trade_policy%2C_exports_and_growth_in_selected_European_countries.png/330px-Trade_policy%2C_exports_and_growth_in_selected_European_countries.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Trade_policy%2C_exports_and_growth_in_selected_European_countries.png/440px-Trade_policy%2C_exports_and_growth_in_selected_European_countries.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Trade_policy,_exports_and_growth_in_selected_European_countries.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Trade policy, exports and growth in selected European countries</div></div></div>
<p>The practices and abuses of mercantilism led the natural resources and cash crops of <a href="/wiki/British_North_America" title="British North America">British North America</a> to be exported in exchange for finished goods from Great Britain, a factor leading to the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>. An early statement appeared in <i>Discourse of the Common Wealth of this Realm of England</i>, 1549: "We must always take heed that we buy no more from strangers than we sell them, for so should we impoverish ourselves and enrich them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Similarly a systematic and coherent explanation of balance of trade was made public through <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Mun" title="Thomas Mun">Thomas Mun</a>'s 1630 "England's treasure by foreign trade, or, The balance of our foreign trade is the rule of our treasure"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since the mid-1980s, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Balance_of_trade" title="United States Balance of trade">United States has had a growing deficit in tradeable goods</a>, especially with Asian nations (China and Japan) which now hold large sums of U.S debt that has in part funded the consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bivens_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bivens-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-debt_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-debt-11">[11]</a></sup> The U.S. has a trade surplus with nations such as Australia. The issue of trade deficits can be complex. Trade deficits generated in tradeable goods such as manufactured goods or software may impact domestic employment to different degrees than do trade deficits in raw materials.
</p><p>Economies which have savings surpluses, such as Japan and Germany, typically run trade surpluses. China, a high-growth economy, has tended to run trade surpluses. A higher savings rate generally corresponds to a trade surplus. Correspondingly, the U.S. with its lower savings rate has tended to run high trade deficits, especially with Asian nations.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Views_on_economic_impact">Views on economic impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balance_of_trade&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Views on economic impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The notion that bilateral trade deficits are bad in and of themselves is overwhelmingly rejected by trade experts and economists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:16_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:16-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:15_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-:15-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-6">[6]</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">IMF</a> trade deficits can cause a <a href="/wiki/Balance_of_payments" title="Balance of payments">balance of payments</a> problem, which can affect foreign exchange shortages and hurt countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> On the other hand, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stiglitz" title="Joseph Stiglitz">Joseph Stiglitz</a> points out that countries running surpluses exert a "negative externality" on trading partners, and pose a threat to global prosperity, far more than those in deficit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ben_Bernanke" title="Ben Bernanke">Ben Bernanke</a> argues that "persistent imbalances within the euro zone are... unhealthy, as they lead to financial imbalances as well as to unbalanced growth. The fact that Germany is selling so much more than it is buying redirects demand from its neighbors (as well as from other countries around the world), reducing output and employment outside Germany."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Several countries consider the trade balance as an important factor: Some have said that China pursues a mercantilist economic policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Russia pursues a policy based on protectionism, according to which international trade is not a "win-win" game but a zero-sum game: surplus countries get richer at the expense of deficit countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_theory">Classical theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balance_of_trade&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Classical theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Adam_Smith_on_the_balance_of_trade">Adam Smith on the balance of trade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balance_of_trade&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Adam Smith on the balance of trade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_List" title="Friedrich List">Friedrich List</a></div>
<p>"In the foregoing part of this chapter I have endeavoured to show, even upon the principles of the commercial system, how unnecessary it is to lay extraordinary restraints upon the importation of goods from those countries with which the balance of trade is supposed to be disadvantageous.
Nothing, however, can be more absurd than this whole doctrine of the balance of trade, upon which, not only these restraints, but almost all the other regulations of commerce are founded. When two places trade with one another, this [absurd] doctrine supposes that, if the balance be even, neither of them either loses or gains; but if it leans in any degree to one side, that one of them loses and the other gains in proportion to its declension from the exact equilibrium." (Smith, 1776, book IV, ch. iii, part ii)
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Keynesian_theory">Keynesian theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balance_of_trade&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Keynesian theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the last few years of his life, <a href="/wiki/John_Maynard_Keynes" title="John Maynard Keynes">John Maynard Keynes</a> was much preoccupied with the question of balance in international trade. He was the leader of the British delegation to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Monetary_and_Financial_Conference" title="United Nations Monetary and Financial Conference">United Nations Monetary and Financial Conference</a> in 1944 that established the <a href="/wiki/Bretton_Woods_system" title="Bretton Woods system">Bretton Woods system</a> of international currency management.
He was the principal author of a proposal – the so-called Keynes Plan – for an <a href="/wiki/International_Clearing_Union" title="International Clearing Union">International Clearing Union</a>. The two governing principles of the plan were that the problem of settling outstanding balances should be solved by 'creating' additional 'international money', and that debtor and creditor should be treated almost alike as disturbers of equilibrium. In the event, though, the plans were rejected, in part because "American opinion was naturally reluctant to accept the principle of equality of treatment so novel in debtor-creditor relationships".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>The new system is not founded on free-trade (liberalisation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> of foreign trade<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>) but rather on the regulation of international trade, in order to eliminate trade imbalances: the nations with a surplus would have a powerful incentive to get rid of it, and in doing so they would automatically clear other nations deficits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> He proposed a global bank that would issue its own currency – the bancor – which was exchangeable with national currencies at fixed rates of exchange and would become the unit of account between nations, which means it would be used to measure a country's trade deficit or trade surplus. Every country would have an overdraft facility in its bancor account at the International Clearing Union. He pointed out that surpluses lead to weak global aggregate demand – countries running surpluses exert a "negative externality" on trading partners, and posed far more than those in deficit, a threat to global prosperity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
In <i>"National Self-Sufficiency" The Yale Review, Vol. 22, no. 4 (June 1933)</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> he already highlighted the problems created by free trade .
</p><p>His view, supported by many economists and commentators at the time, was that creditor nations may be just as responsible as debtor nations for disequilibrium in exchanges and that both should be under an obligation to bring trade back into a state of balance. Failure for them to do so could have serious consequences. In the words of <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Crowther,_Baron_Crowther" title="Geoffrey Crowther, Baron Crowther">Geoffrey Crowther</a>, then editor of <a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a>, "If the economic relationships between nations are not, by one means or another, brought fairly close to balance, then there is no set of financial arrangements that can rescue the world from the impoverishing results of chaos."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>These ideas were informed by events prior to the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> when – in the opinion of Keynes and others – international lending, primarily by the U.S., exceeded the capacity of sound investment and so got diverted into non-productive and speculative uses, which in turn invited default and a sudden stop to the process of lending.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Influenced by Keynes, economics texts in the immediate post-war period put a significant emphasis on balance in trade. For example, the second edition of the popular introductory textbook, <i>An Outline of Money</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> devoted the last three of its ten chapters to questions of foreign exchange management and in particular the 'problem of balance'. However, in more recent years, since the end of the <a href="/wiki/Bretton_Woods_system" title="Bretton Woods system">Bretton Woods system</a> in 1971, with the increasing influence of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monetarist" title="Monetarist">monetarist</a> schools of thought in the 1980s, and particularly in the face of large sustained trade imbalances, these concerns – and particularly concerns about the destabilising effects of large trade surpluses – have largely disappeared from <a href="/wiki/Mainstream_economics" title="Mainstream economics">mainstream economics</a> discourse<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and Keynes' insights have slipped from view.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> They are receiving some attention again in the wake of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007%E2%80%9308" title="Financial crisis of 2007–08">financial crisis of 2007–08</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Monetarist_theory">Monetarist theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balance_of_trade&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Monetarist theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Prior to 20th century monetarist theory, the 19th century economist and philosopher <a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Bastiat" title="Frédéric Bastiat">Frédéric Bastiat</a> expressed the idea that trade deficits actually were a manifestation of profit, rather than a loss. He proposed as an example to suppose that he, a Frenchman, exported French wine and imported British coal, turning a profit. He supposed he was in France, and sent a cask of wine which was worth 50 francs to England. The customhouse would record an export of 50 francs. If, in England, the wine sold for 70 francs (or the pound equivalent), which he then used to buy coal, which he imported into France, and was found to be worth 90 francs in France, he would have made a profit of 40 francs. But the customhouse would say that the value of imports exceeded that of exports and was trade deficit against the ledger of France.
</p><p>By <i><a href="/wiki/Reductio_ad_absurdum" title="Reductio ad absurdum">reductio ad absurdum</a></i>, Bastiat argued that the national trade deficit was an indicator of a successful economy, rather than a failing one. Bastiat predicted that a successful, growing economy would result in greater trade deficits, and an unsuccessful, shrinking economy would result in lower trade deficits. This was later, in the 20th century, echoed by economist <a href="/wiki/Milton_Friedman" title="Milton Friedman">Milton Friedman</a>.
</p><p>In the 1980s, <a href="/wiki/Milton_Friedman" title="Milton Friedman">Milton Friedman</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Memorial_Prize_in_Economic_Sciences" title="Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences">Nobel Memorial Prize</a>-winning economist and a proponent of <a href="/wiki/Monetarism" title="Monetarism">monetarism</a>, contended that some of the concerns of trade deficits are unfair criticisms in an attempt to push macroeconomic policies favorable to exporting industries.
</p><p>Friedman argued that trade deficits are not necessarily important, as high exports raise the value of the currency, reducing aforementioned exports, and vice versa for imports, thus naturally removing trade deficits <i>not due to investment</i>. Since 1971, when the Nixon administration decided to abolish fixed exchange rates, America's Current Account accumulated trade deficits have totaled $7.75 trillion as of 2010. This deficit exists as it is matched by investment coming into the United States – purely by the definition of the balance of payments, any current account deficit that exists is matched by an inflow of foreign investment.
</p><p>In the late 1970s and early 1980s, the U.S. had experienced high inflation and Friedman's policy positions tended to defend the stronger dollar at that time. He stated his belief that these trade deficits were not necessarily harmful to the economy at the time since the currency comes back to the country (country A sells to country B, country B sells to country C who buys from country A, but the trade deficit only includes A and B). However, it may be in one form or another including the possible tradeoff of foreign control of assets. In his view, the "worst-case scenario" of the currency never returning to the country of origin was actually the best possible outcome: the country actually purchased its goods by exchanging them for pieces of cheaply made paper. As Friedman put it, this would be the same result as if the exporting country burned the dollars it earned, never returning it to market circulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>This position is a more refined version of the theorem first discovered by <a href="/wiki/David_Hume" title="David Hume">David Hume</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Hume argued that England could not permanently gain from exports, because hoarding gold (i.e., currency) would make gold more plentiful in England; therefore, the prices of English goods would rise, making them less attractive exports and making foreign goods more attractive imports. In this way, countries' trade balances would balance out.
</p><p>Friedman presented his analysis of the balance of trade in <i><a href="/wiki/Free_to_Choose" title="Free to Choose">Free to Choose</a></i>, widely considered his most significant popular work.
</p>
<h3><span id="Trade_balance.E2.80.99s_effects_upon_a_nation.27s_GDP"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Trade_balance’s_effects_upon_a_nation's_GDP">Trade balance’s effects upon a nation's GDP</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balance_of_trade&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Trade balance’s effects upon a nation's GDP">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Exports directly increase and imports directly reduce a nation's balance of trade (i.e. net exports). A trade surplus is a positive net balance of trade, and a trade deficit is a negative net balance of trade. Due to the balance of trade being explicitly added to the calculation of the nation's gross domestic product using the expenditure method of calculating gross domestic product (i.e. GDP), trade surpluses are contributions and trade deficits are "drags" upon their nation's GDP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
