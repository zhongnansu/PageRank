<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Environmental accounting</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Environmental accounting</b> is a subset of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accountancy" title="Accountancy">accounting</a> proper, its target being to incorporate both economic and environmental information. It can be conducted at the corporate level or at the level of a national economy through the <a href="/wiki/System_of_Integrated_Environmental_and_Economic_Accounting" title="System of Integrated Environmental and Economic Accounting">System of Integrated Environmental and Economic Accounting</a>, a satellite system to the National Accounts of Countries<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_UN1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_accounting#endnote_UN1">[1]</a></sup> (among other things, the National Accounts produce the estimates of Gross Domestic Product otherwise known as GDP).
</p><p>Environmental accounting is a field that identifies resource use, measures and communicates costs of a company’s or national economic impact on the environment.  Costs include costs to clean up or <a href="/wiki/Environmental_remediation" title="Environmental remediation">remediate</a> contaminated sites, environmental fines, penalties and taxes, purchase of pollution prevention technologies and waste management costs.
</p><p>An environmental accounting system consists of environmentally differentiated conventional accounting and ecological accounting. Environmentally differentiated accounting measures effects of the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">natural environment</a> on a <a href="/wiki/Company" title="Company">company</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monetary" title="Monetary">monetary</a> terms. Ecological accounting measures the influence a company has on the environment, but in physical measurements.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reasons_for_use">Reasons for use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Reasons for use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are several advantages environmental accounting brings to business; notably, the complete costs, including environmental remediation and long term environmental consequences and externalities can be quantified and addressed.
</p><p>More information about the statistical system of environmental accounts are available here: <a href="/wiki/System_of_Integrated_Environmental_and_Economic_Accounting" title="System of Integrated Environmental and Economic Accounting">System of Integrated Environmental and Economic Accounting</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subfields">Subfields</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subfields">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Environmental accounting is organized in three sub-disciplines: global, national, and corporate environmental accounting, respectively. Corporate environmental accounting can be further sub-divided into environmental management accounting and environmental financial accounting.
</p>
<ul><li><b>Global environmental accounting</b> is an accounting methodology that deals areas includes energetics, ecology and economics at a worldwide level.</li>
<li><b>National environmental accounting</b> is an accounting approach that deals with economics on a country's level.</li></ul>
<dl><dd>Internationally, environmental accounting has been formalised into the System of Integrated Environmental and Economic Accounting, known as SEEA.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_UN1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_accounting#endnote_UN1">[2]</a></sup> SEEA grows out of the System of National Accounts. The SEEA records the flows of raw materials (water, energy, minerals, wood, etc.) from the environment to the economy, the exchanges of these materials within the economy and the returns of wastes and pollutants to the environment. Also recorded are the prices or shadow prices for these materials as are environment protection expenditures. SEEA is used by 49 countries around the world.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_UN2"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_accounting#endnote_UN2">[3]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<ul><li><b>Corporate environmental accounting</b> focuses on the cost structure and environmental performance of a company.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_EA"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_accounting#endnote_EA">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Environmental management accounting</b> focuses on making internal business strategy decisions.  It can be defined as:</li></ul>
<dl><dd>"<i>..the identification, collection, analysis, and use of two types of information for internal decision making:</i></dd>
<dd>1) Physical information on the use, flows and fates of energy, water and materials (including wastes) and</dd>
<dd>2) Monetary information on environmentally related costs, earnings and savings."<i><sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_Jasch"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_accounting#endnote_Jasch">[5]</a></sup></i></dd></dl>
<dl><dd>As part of an environmental management accounting project in the State of Victoria, Australia, four case studies were undertaken in 2002 involving a school (Methodist Ladies College, Perth), plastics manufacturing company (Cormack Manufacturing Pty Ltd, Sydney), provider of office services (a service division of AMP, Australia wide) and wool processing (GH Michell &amp; Sons Pty Ltd, Adelaide). Four major accounting professionals and firms were involved in the project; <a href="/wiki/KPMG" title="KPMG">KPMG</a> (Melbourne), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Price_Waterhouse_Coopers" title="Price Waterhouse Coopers">Price Waterhouse Coopers</a> (Sydney), Professor Craig Deegan, <a href="/wiki/RMIT_University" title="RMIT University">RMIT University</a> (Melbourne) and BDO Consultants Pty Ltd (Perth). In February 2003, John Thwaites, The Victorian Minister for the Environment launched the report which summarised the results of the studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<dl><dd>These studies were supported by the Department of Environment and Heritage of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Federal_Government" title="Australian Federal Government">Australian Federal Government</a>, and appear to have applied some of the principles outlined in the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> Division for Sustainable Development publication, <i>Environmental Management Accounting Procedures and Principles</i> (2001).</dd></dl>
<ul><li><b>Environmental financial accounting</b> is used to provide information needed by external <a href="/wiki/Stakeholder_(corporate)" title="Stakeholder (corporate)">stakeholders</a> on a company’s financial performance.  This type of accounting allows companies to prepare financial reports for investors, lenders and other interested parties.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_EPA"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_accounting#endnote_EPA">[6]</a></sup></li></ul>
