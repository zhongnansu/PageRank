<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Exothermic process</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Not to be confused with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ectothermic" title="Ectothermic">Ectothermic</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Explosion1.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="552" data-file-width="743" height="223" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Explosion1.JPG/300px-Explosion1.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Explosion1.JPG/450px-Explosion1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Explosion1.JPG/600px-Explosion1.JPG 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Explosion1.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Explosions are some of the most violent exothermic reactions.</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>, the term <b>exothermic process</b> (exo- : "outside") describes a process or reaction that releases <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> from the system to its surroundings, usually in the form of <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a>, but also in a form of <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> (e.g. a spark, flame, or flash), <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> (e.g. a battery), or <a href="/wiki/Sound" title="Sound">sound</a> (e.g. explosion heard when burning hydrogen). Its etymology stems from the Greek prefix <i>έξω</i> (exō, which means "outwards") and the Greek word <i>θερμικός</i> (thermikόs, which means "thermal").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The term <i>exothermic</i> was first coined by <a href="/wiki/Marcellin_Berthelot" title="Marcellin Berthelot">Marcellin Berthelot</a>. The opposite of an exothermic process is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endothermic" title="Endothermic">endothermic</a> process, one that absorbs energy in the form of heat.
</p><p>The concept is frequently applied in the <a href="/wiki/Outline_of_physical_science" title="Outline of physical science">physical sciences</a> to <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reactions</a>, where as in chemical <a href="/wiki/Bond_energy" title="Bond energy">bond energy</a> that will be converted to <a href="/wiki/Thermal_energy" title="Thermal energy">thermal energy</a> (heat).
</p><p>Exothermic (and endothermic) describe two types of chemical reactions or systems found in nature, as follows.
</p><p>Simply stated, after an <a href="/wiki/Exothermic_reaction" title="Exothermic reaction">exothermic reaction</a>, more energy has been released to the surroundings than was absorbed to initiate and maintain the reaction. An example would be the burning of a candle, wherein the sum of calories produced by combustion (found by looking at radiant heating of the surroundings and visible light produced, including increase in temperature of the fuel (wax) itself, which with oxygen, have become hot CO<sub>2</sub> and water vapor,) exceeds the number of calories absorbed initially in lighting the flame and in the flame maintaining itself. (i.e. some energy produced by combustion is reabsorbed and used in melting, then vaporizing the wax, etc. but is (far) outstripped by the energy produced in breaking carbon-hydrogen bonds and combination of oxygen with the resulting carbon and hydrogen).
</p><p>On the other hand, in an <a href="/wiki/Endothermic_process" title="Endothermic process">endothermic reaction</a> or system, energy is taken from the surroundings in the course of the reaction. An example of an endothermic reaction is a first aid cold pack, in which the reaction of two chemicals, or dissolving of one in another, requires calories from the surroundings, and the reaction cools the pouch and surroundings by absorbing heat from them. An endothermic system is seen in the production of wood: trees absorb radiant energy, from the sun, use it in endothermic reactions such as taking apart CO<sub>2</sub> and H<sub>2</sub>O and combining the carbon and hydrogen generated to produce cellulose and other organic chemicals. These products, in the form of wood, say, may later be burned in a fireplace, exothermically, producing CO<sub>2</sub> and water, and releasing energy in the form of heat and light to their surroundings, e.g., to a home's interior and chimney gasses.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exothermic_process&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Exothermic refers to a transformation in which a system releases energy (heat) to the surroundings, expressed by
</p>
<dl><dd><i>Q &lt; 0</i>.</dd></dl>
<p>When the transformation occurs at constant pressure, one has for the <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy" title="Enthalpy">enthalpy</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><i>∆H &lt; 0</i>,</dd></dl>
<p>and constant volume, one has for the internal energy<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (March 2015)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata"> – <a href="/wiki/Talk:Exothermic_process#Dubious" title="Talk:Exothermic process">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><i>∆U &lt; 0</i>.</dd></dl>
<p>In an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adiabatic" title="Adiabatic">adiabatic</a> system (i.e. a system that does not exchange heat with the surroundings), an exothermic process results in an increase in temperature of the system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In exothermic chemical reactions, the heat that is released by the reaction takes the form of electromagnetic energy. The transition of electrons from one quantum energy level to another causes light to be released. This light is equivalent in energy to the stabilization energy of the energy for the chemical reaction, i.e. the bond energy. This light that is released can be absorbed by other molecules in solution to give rise to molecular vibrations or rotations, which gives rise to the classical understanding of heat. In contrast, when endothermic reactions occur, energy is absorbed to place an electron in a higher energy state, such that the electron can associate with another atom to form a chemical complex. Net energy is absorbed by an endothermic reaction. In an exothermic reaction, the energy needed to start the reaction is less than the energy that is subsequently released, so there is a net release of energy. This is the physical understanding of exothermic and endothermic reactions.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Implications_for_chemical_reactions">Implications for chemical reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exothermic_process&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Implications for chemical reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Exothermic_reaction" title="Exothermic reaction">Exothermic reaction</a></div>
<p>Chemical exothermic reactions are generally more spontaneous than their counterparts, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endothermic_reaction" title="Endothermic reaction">endothermic reactions</a>. 
</p><p>In a thermochemical reaction that is exothermic, the heat may be listed among the products of the reaction.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contrast_between_thermodynamic_and_biological_terminology">Contrast between thermodynamic and biological terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exothermic_process&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Contrast between thermodynamic and biological terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because of historical accident, students encounter a source of possible confusion between the terminology of physics and biology. Whereas the <i><b>thermodynamic</b></i> terms "exothermic" and "endothermic" respectively refer to processes that give out heat energy and processes that absorb heat energy, in <i><b>biology</b></i> the sense is effectively inverted. The <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolic</a> terms "<a href="/wiki/Ectotherm" title="Ectotherm">ectothermic</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Endotherm" title="Endotherm">endothermic</a>" respectively refer to organisms that rely largely on external heat to achieve a full working temperature, and to organisms that produce heat from within as a major factor in controlling their bodily temperature.
</p>
