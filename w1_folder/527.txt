<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Aroma compound</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>An <b>aroma compound</b>, also known as an <b>odorant</b>, <b>aroma</b>, <b>fragrance</b>, or <b><a href="/wiki/Flavor" title="Flavor">flavor</a></b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> that has a smell or <a href="/wiki/Odor" title="Odor">odor</a>. A chemical compound has a smell or odor when it is sufficiently <a href="/wiki/Volatility_(chemistry)" title="Volatility (chemistry)">volatile</a> to be transported to the <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_system" title="Olfactory system">olfactory system</a> in the upper part of the nose.
</p><p>Generally molecules meeting this specification have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_weight" title="Molecular weight">molecular weights</a> of less than 300. Flavors affect both the sense of <a href="/wiki/Taste" title="Taste">taste</a> and <a href="/wiki/Olfaction" title="Olfaction">smell</a>, whereas fragrances affect only smell.  Flavors tend to be naturally occurring, and fragrances tend to be synthetic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Aroma compounds can be found in <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spices" title="Spices">spices</a>, <a href="/wiki/Floral_scent" title="Floral scent">floral scent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Perfume" title="Perfume">perfumes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fragrance_oil" title="Fragrance oil">fragrance oils</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Essential_oil" title="Essential oil">essential oils</a>. For example, many form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biochemical" title="Biochemical">biochemically</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Ripening" title="Ripening">ripening</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruits</a> and other crops. In wines, most form as byproducts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(wine)" title="Fermentation (wine)">fermentation</a>. Also, many of the aroma compounds play a significant role in the production of flavorants, which are used in the food service industry to flavor, improve, and generally increase the appeal of their products.
</p><p>An <a href="/wiki/Odorizer" title="Odorizer">odorizer</a> may add a detectable odor to a dangerous odorless substance, like <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_odorant" title="Hydrogen odorant">hydrogen</a>, as a safety measure.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aroma-compound_receptors">Aroma-compound receptors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aroma_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Aroma-compound receptors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Animals that are capable of <a href="/wiki/Olfaction" title="Olfaction">smell</a> detect aroma compounds with their <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_receptor" title="Olfactory receptor">olfactory receptors</a>. Olfactory receptors are cell-membrane <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Receptor_(biology)" title="Receptor (biology)">receptors</a> on the surface of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sensory_neurons" title="Sensory neurons">sensory neurons</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Olfactory_system" title="Olfactory system">olfactory system</a> that detect airborne aroma compounds.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>, olfactory receptors are <a href="/wiki/Gene_expression" title="Gene expression">expressed</a> on the surface of the olfactory epithelium in the <a href="/wiki/Nasal_cavity" title="Nasal cavity">nasal cavity</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aroma_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Epikutanni-test.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Epikutanni-test.jpg/220px-Epikutanni-test.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Epikutanni-test.jpg/330px-Epikutanni-test.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Epikutanni-test.jpg/440px-Epikutanni-test.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Epikutanni-test.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Patch_test" title="Patch test">Patch test</a></div></div></div>
<p>In 2005–06, fragrance mix was the third-most-prevalent <a href="/wiki/Allergen" title="Allergen">allergen</a> in <a href="/wiki/Patch_test" title="Patch test">patch tests</a> (11.5%).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> 'Fragrance' was voted <a href="/wiki/Allergen_of_the_Year" title="Allergen of the Year">Allergen of the Year</a> in 2007 by the American Contact Dermatitis Society. A recent academic study in the United States has shown that "34.7 % of the population reported health problems, such as migraine headaches and respiratory difficulties, when exposed to fragranced products".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The composition of fragrances is usually not disclosed in the label of products, hiding the actual chemicals of the formula, which raises concerns among some consumers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fragrances are regulated in the United States by the <a href="/wiki/Toxic_Substances_Control_Act_of_1976" title="Toxic Substances Control Act of 1976">Toxic Substances Control Act of 1976</a> that "grandfathered" existing chemicals without further review or testing and put the burden of proof that a new substance is not safe on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="Environmental Protection Agency">EPA</a>. The EPA, however, does not conduct independent safety testing but relies on data provided by the manufacturer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_chemicals_used_as_fragrances">List of chemicals used as fragrances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aroma_compound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: List of chemicals used as fragrances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2010 the <a href="/wiki/International_Fragrance_Association" title="International Fragrance Association">International Fragrance Association</a>  published a list of  3,059  chemicals used in 2011 based on a voluntary survey of its members. It was estimated to represent about 90% of the world's production volume of fragrances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
