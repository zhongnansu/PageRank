<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Carbon cycle</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Biogeochemical cycle by which carbon is exchanged among the biosphere, pedosphere, geosphere, hydrosphere, and atmosphere,</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For the thermonuclear reaction involving carbon that powers some stars, see <a href="/wiki/CNO_cycle" title="CNO cycle">CNO cycle</a>. For organic chemical ring-shaped structures, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclic_compounds" title="Cyclic compounds">Cyclic compounds</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carbon_cycle.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2192" data-file-width="2600" height="337" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Carbon_cycle.jpg/400px-Carbon_cycle.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Carbon_cycle.jpg/600px-Carbon_cycle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Carbon_cycle.jpg/800px-Carbon_cycle.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Carbon_cycle.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Movement of carbon between land, atmosphere, and ocean in billions of tons per year. Yellow numbers are natural fluxes, red are human contributions, white are stored carbon.  The effects of volcanic and tectonic activity are not included.</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>carbon cycle</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Biogeochemical_cycle" title="Biogeochemical cycle">biogeochemical cycle</a> by which <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> is exchanged among the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pedosphere" title="Pedosphere">pedosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geosphere" title="Geosphere">geosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere of the Earth</a>. Carbon is the main component of biological compounds as well as a major component of many minerals such as limestone. Along with the <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_cycle" title="Nitrogen cycle">nitrogen cycle</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">water cycle</a>, the carbon cycle comprises a sequence of events that are key to make Earth capable of sustaining life. It describes the movement of carbon as it is recycled and reused throughout the biosphere, as well as long-term processes of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">carbon sequestration</a> to and release from <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sink" title="Carbon sink">carbon sinks</a>.
</p><p>The carbon cycle was discovered by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">Joseph Priestley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Lavoisier" title="Antoine Lavoisier">Antoine Lavoisier</a>, and popularized by <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Davy" title="Humphry Davy">Humphry Davy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOW_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-AOW-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

It remains unclear if carbon absorption will continue at this rate.</div></div></div>
<p>The global carbon cycle is now usually divided into the following major reservoirs of carbon interconnected by pathways of exchange:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>5–6</span></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a></li>
<li>The terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/World_Ocean" title="World Ocean">ocean</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Total_inorganic_carbon" title="Total inorganic carbon">dissolved inorganic carbon</a> and living and non-living marine biota</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediments</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>, fresh water systems and non-living organic material.</li>
<li>The Earth's interior (<a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a>. These carbon stores interact with the other components through geological processes.</li></ul>
<p>The carbon exchanges between reservoirs occur as the result of various chemical, physical, geological, and biological processes. The ocean contains the largest active pool of carbon near the surface of the Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GlobalCarbonCycle_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-GlobalCarbonCycle-2">[2]</a></sup>
The natural flows of carbon between the atmosphere, ocean, terrestrial ecosystems, and sediments is fairly balanced, so that carbon levels would be roughly stable without human influence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prentice_etal_2001_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prentice_etal_2001-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-U_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-U-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmosphere">Atmosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Atmosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_carbon_cycle" title="Atmospheric carbon cycle">Atmospheric carbon cycle</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ep%C3%ADfitas_en_los_cables_de_la_luz_el%C3%A9ctrica.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Ep%C3%ADfitas_en_los_cables_de_la_luz_el%C3%A9ctrica.JPG/220px-Ep%C3%ADfitas_en_los_cables_de_la_luz_el%C3%A9ctrica.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Ep%C3%ADfitas_en_los_cables_de_la_luz_el%C3%A9ctrica.JPG/330px-Ep%C3%ADfitas_en_los_cables_de_la_luz_el%C3%A9ctrica.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Ep%C3%ADfitas_en_los_cables_de_la_luz_el%C3%A9ctrica.JPG/440px-Ep%C3%ADfitas_en_los_cables_de_la_luz_el%C3%A9ctrica.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ep%C3%ADfitas_en_los_cables_de_la_luz_el%C3%A9ctrica.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Epiphyte" title="Epiphyte">Epiphytes</a> on electric wires. This kind of plant takes both CO<sub>2</sub> and water from the atmosphere for living and growing.</div></div></div>
<p>Carbon in the Earth's atmosphere exists in two main forms: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>. Both of these gases absorb and retain heat in the atmosphere and are partially responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GlobalCarbonCycle_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-GlobalCarbonCycle-2">[2]</a></sup> Methane produces a larger greenhouse effect per volume as compared to carbon dioxide, but it exists in much lower concentrations and is more short-lived than carbon dioxide, making carbon dioxide the more important greenhouse gas of the two.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forster2007_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Forster2007-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Carbon dioxide is removed from the atmosphere primarily through <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> and enters the terrestrial and oceanic biospheres. Carbon dioxide also dissolves directly from the atmosphere into bodies of water (ocean, lakes, etc.), as well as dissolving in precipitation as raindrops fall through the atmosphere. When dissolved in water, carbon dioxide reacts with water molecules and forms <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a>, which contributes to ocean acidity. It can then be absorbed by rocks through weathering. It also can acidify other surfaces it touches or be washed into the ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Planet_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Planet-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Human activities over the past two centuries have significantly increased the amount of carbon in the atmosphere, mainly in the form of carbon dioxide, both by modifying ecosystems' ability to extract carbon dioxide from the atmosphere and by emitting it directly, e.g., by burning fossil fuels and manufacturing concrete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GlobalCarbonCycle_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-GlobalCarbonCycle-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terrestrial_biosphere">Terrestrial biosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Terrestrial biosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_biological_carbon_cycle" title="Terrestrial biological carbon cycle">Terrestrial biological carbon cycle</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SRS1000_being_used_to_measure_soil_respiration_in_the_field..jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1330" data-file-width="2000" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/SRS1000_being_used_to_measure_soil_respiration_in_the_field..jpg/220px-SRS1000_being_used_to_measure_soil_respiration_in_the_field..jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/SRS1000_being_used_to_measure_soil_respiration_in_the_field..jpg/330px-SRS1000_being_used_to_measure_soil_respiration_in_the_field..jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/SRS1000_being_used_to_measure_soil_respiration_in_the_field..jpg/440px-SRS1000_being_used_to_measure_soil_respiration_in_the_field..jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SRS1000_being_used_to_measure_soil_respiration_in_the_field..jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A portable soil respiration system measuring soil CO<sub>2</sub> flux</div></div></div>
<p>The terrestrial biosphere includes the organic carbon in all land-living organisms, both alive and dead, as well as carbon stored in <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soils</a>. About 500 gigatons of carbon are stored above ground in plants and other living organisms,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prentice_etal_2001_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Prentice_etal_2001-4">[4]</a></sup> while soil holds approximately 1,500 gigatons of carbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Most carbon in the terrestrial biosphere is organic carbon,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>  while about a third of soil carbon is stored in inorganic forms, such as <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lal-2008_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lal-2008-10">[10]</a></sup> Organic carbon is a major component of all organisms living on earth. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autotrophs" title="Autotrophs">Autotrophs</a> extract it from the air in the form of carbon dioxide, converting it into organic carbon, while <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterotrophs" title="Heterotrophs">heterotrophs</a> receive carbon by consuming other organisms.
</p><p>Because carbon uptake in the terrestrial biosphere is dependent on biotic factors, it follows a diurnal and seasonal cycle. In CO<sub>2</sub> measurements, this feature is apparent in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keeling_curve" title="Keeling curve">Keeling curve</a>. It is strongest in the northern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hemisphere_of_the_Earth" title="Hemisphere of the Earth">hemisphere</a>, because this hemisphere has more land mass than the southern hemisphere and thus more room for ecosystems to absorb and emit carbon.
</p><p>Carbon leaves the terrestrial biosphere in several ways and on different time scales. The <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">respiration</a> of organic carbon releases it rapidly into the atmosphere. It can also be exported into the ocean through rivers or remain sequestered in soils in the form of inert carbon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Carbon stored in soil can remain there for up to thousands of years before being washed into rivers by <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> or released into the atmosphere through <a href="/wiki/Soil_respiration" title="Soil respiration">soil respiration</a>. Between 1989 and 2008 soil respiration increased by about 0.1% per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 2008, the global total of CO<sub>2</sub> released by soil respiration was roughly 98 billion tonnes, about 10 times more carbon than humans are now putting into the atmosphere each year by burning fossil fuel (this does not represent a net transfer of carbon from soil to atmosphere, as the respiration is largely offset by inputs to soil carbon). There are a few plausible explanations for this trend, but the most likely explanation is that increasing temperatures have increased rates of decomposition of <a href="/wiki/Soil_organic_matter" title="Soil organic matter">soil organic matter</a>, which has increased the flow of CO<sub>2</sub>. The length of carbon sequestering in soil is dependent on local climatic conditions and thus changes in the course of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ocean">Ocean</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ocean">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_carbon_cycle" title="Oceanic carbon cycle">Oceanic carbon cycle</a></div>
<p>The ocean can be conceptually divided into a <a href="/wiki/Surface_layer" title="Surface layer">surface layer</a> within which water makes frequent (daily to annual) contact with the atmosphere, and a deep layer below the typical <a href="/wiki/Mixed_layer" title="Mixed layer">mixed layer</a> depth of a few hundred meters or less, within which the time between consecutive contacts may be centuries. The  dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) in the surface layer is exchanged rapidly with the atmosphere, maintaining equilibrium. Partly because its concentration of DIC is about 15% higher<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sarmiento_and_Gruber_2006_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sarmiento_and_Gruber_2006-13">[13]</a></sup> but mainly due to its larger volume, the deep ocean contains far more carbon—it's the largest pool of actively cycled carbon in the world, containing 50 times more than the atmosphere<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GlobalCarbonCycle_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-GlobalCarbonCycle-2">[2]</a></sup>—but the timescale to reach equilibrium with the atmosphere is hundreds of years: the exchange of carbon between the two layers, driven by <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a>, is slow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GlobalCarbonCycle_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-GlobalCarbonCycle-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Carbon enters the ocean mainly through the dissolution of atmospheric carbon dioxide, a small fraction of which is converted into <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonate</a>. It can also enter the ocean through rivers as <a href="/wiki/Dissolved_organic_carbon" title="Dissolved organic carbon">dissolved organic carbon</a>. It is converted by organisms into organic carbon through <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> and can either be exchanged throughout the food chain or precipitated into the ocean's deeper, more carbon rich layers as dead soft tissue or in shells as <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>. It circulates in this layer for long periods of time before either being deposited as sediment or, eventually, returned to the surface waters through thermohaline circulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prentice_etal_2001_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Prentice_etal_2001-4">[4]</a></sup> Oceans are basic (~pH 8.2), hence CO<sub>2</sub> acidification shifts the pH of the ocean towards neutral.   
</p><p>Oceanic absorption of CO<sub>2</sub> is one of the most important forms of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestering" title="Carbon sequestering">carbon sequestering</a> limiting the human-caused rise of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere. However, this process is limited by a number of factors.  CO<sub>2</sub> absorption makes water more acidic, which affects ocean biosystems. The projected rate of increasing <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">oceanic acidity</a> could slow the biological precipitation of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonates</a>, thus decreasing the ocean's capacity to absorb carbon dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klyepas1999_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Klyepas1999-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langdon2000_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Langdon2000-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Earth.27s_interior"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth's_interior">Earth's interior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Earth's interior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The geologic component of the carbon cycle operates slowly in comparison to the other parts of the global carbon cycle. It is one of the most important determinants of the amount of carbon in the atmosphere, and thus of global temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Most of the earth's carbon is stored inertly in the earth's <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GlobalCarbonCycle_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-GlobalCarbonCycle-2">[2]</a></sup> Much of the carbon stored in the earth's mantle was stored there when the earth formed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DiVenere2012_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-DiVenere2012-17">[17]</a></sup> Some of it was deposited in the form of organic carbon from the biosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berner1999_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Berner1999-18">[18]</a></sup> Of the carbon stored in the geosphere, about 80% is <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> and its derivatives, which form from the sedimentation of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> stored in the shells of marine organisms. The remaining 20% is stored as <a href="/wiki/Kerogen" title="Kerogen">kerogens</a> formed through the sedimentation and burial of terrestrial organisms under high heat and pressure. Organic carbon stored in the geosphere can remain there for millions of years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Carbon can leave the geosphere in several ways. Carbon dioxide is released during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metamorphism_(geology)" title="Metamorphism (geology)">metamorphosis</a> of carbonate rocks when they are <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subducted</a> into the earth's mantle. This carbon dioxide can be released into the atmosphere and ocean through <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">volcanoes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hotspot_(geology)" title="Hotspot (geology)">hotspots</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DiVenere2012_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-DiVenere2012-17">[17]</a></sup> It can also be removed by humans through the direct extraction of kerogens in the form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a>. After extraction, fossil fuels are burned to release energy, thus emitting the carbon they store into the atmosphere
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_influence">Human influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Human influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">Global warming</a></div>

<p>Since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a>, human activity has modified the carbon cycle by changing its components' functions and directly adding carbon to the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GlobalCarbonCycle_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-GlobalCarbonCycle-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The largest human impact on the carbon cycle is through direct emissions from burning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a>, which transfers carbon from the geosphere into the atmosphere. The rest of this increase is caused mostly by changes in land-use, <a href="/wiki/Deforestation_and_climate_change" title="Deforestation and climate change">particularly deforestation</a>.
</p><p>Another direct human impact on the carbon cycle is the chemical process of <a href="/wiki/Calcination" title="Calcination">calcination</a> of <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> for <a href="/wiki/Clinker_(cement)" title="Clinker (cement)">clinker</a> production, which releases CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Clinker is an industrial <a href="/wiki/Precursor_(chemistry)" title="Precursor (chemistry)">precursor</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">cement</a>.
</p><p>Humans also influence the carbon cycle indirectly by changing the terrestrial and oceanic biosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">[24]</a></sup> Over the past several centuries, direct and indirect human-caused <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land use</a> and land cover change (LUCC) has led to the <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_loss" title="Biodiversity loss">loss of biodiversity</a>, which lowers ecosystems' resilience to environmental stresses and decreases their ability to remove carbon from the atmosphere. More directly, it often leads to the release of carbon from terrestrial ecosystems into the atmosphere. <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">Deforestation</a> for agricultural purposes removes forests, which hold large amounts of carbon, and replaces them, generally with agricultural or urban areas. Both of these replacement land cover types store comparatively small amounts of carbon, so that the net product of the process is that more carbon stays in the atmosphere.
</p><p>Other human-caused changes to the environment change ecosystems' productivity and their ability to remove carbon from the atmosphere. <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">Air pollution</a>, for example, damages plants and soils, while many agricultural and land use practices lead to higher <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> rates, washing carbon out of soils and decreasing plant productivity.
</p><p>Humans also affect the oceanic carbon cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">[24]</a></sup> Current trends in climate change lead to higher ocean temperatures, thus modifying ecosystems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Also, acid rain and polluted runoff from agriculture and industry change the ocean's chemical composition. Such changes can have dramatic effects on highly sensitive ecosystems such as <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reefs</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> thus limiting the ocean's ability to absorb carbon from the atmosphere on a regional scale and reducing oceanic biodiversity globally.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Arctic_methane_emissions" title="Arctic methane emissions">Arctic methane emissions</a> indirectly caused by anthropogenic global warming also affect the carbon cycle, and contribute to further warming in what is known as <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_feedback" title="Climate change feedback">climate change feedback</a>.
</p><p>On 12 November 2015, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> scientists reported that human-made carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>) <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Carbon dioxide in Earth's atmosphere">continues to increase</a>, reaching levels not seen in hundreds of thousands of years: currently, the rate carbon dioxide release by the burning of fossil fuels is about double the net uptake by vegetation and the ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20151112-ab_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20151112-ab-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20151112b_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20151112b-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20151110_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20151110-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP-20151109_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-AP-20151109-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
