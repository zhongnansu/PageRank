<p>A <b>truck</b> or <b>lorry</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Motor_vehicle" title="Motor vehicle">motor vehicle</a> designed to transport <a href="/wiki/Cargo" title="Cargo">cargo</a>. Trucks vary greatly in size, power, and configuration; smaller varieties may be mechanically similar to some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">automobiles</a>. Commercial trucks can be very large and powerful, and may be configured to mount specialized equipment, such as in the case of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fire_truck" title="Fire truck">fire trucks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Concrete_mixer" title="Concrete mixer">concrete mixers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suction_excavator" title="Suction excavator">suction excavators</a>.
</p><p>Modern trucks are largely powered by <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">diesel engines</a>, although small to medium size trucks with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gasoline_engine" title="Gasoline engine">gasoline engines</a> exist in the US, Canada, and Mexico. In the European Union, vehicles with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gross_combination_mass" title="Gross combination mass">gross combination mass</a> of up to 3.5 t (7,700 lb) are known as <a href="/wiki/Light_commercial_vehicle" title="Light commercial vehicle">light commercial vehicles</a>, and those over as <a href="/wiki/Large_goods_vehicle" title="Large goods vehicle">large goods vehicles</a>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Steam_wagons">Steam wagons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Steam wagons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Steam_wagon" title="Steam wagon">Steam wagon</a></div>
<p>Trucks and cars have a common ancestor: the steam-powered <i>fardier</i> <a href="/wiki/Nicolas-Joseph_Cugnot" title="Nicolas-Joseph Cugnot">Nicolas-Joseph Cugnot</a> built in 1769.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Steam_wagon" title="Steam wagon">steam wagons</a> were not common until the mid-1800s. The roads of the time, built for horse and carriages, limited these vehicles to very short hauls, usually from a factory to the nearest railway station. The first <a href="/wiki/Semi-trailer" title="Semi-trailer">semi-trailer</a> appeared in 1881,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> towed by a <a href="/wiki/Steam_tractor" title="Steam tractor">steam tractor</a> manufactured by <a href="/wiki/De_Dion-Bouton" title="De Dion-Bouton">De Dion-Bouton</a>. Steam-powered wagons were sold in France and the United States until the eve of World War I, and 1935 in the United Kingdom, when a change in road tax rules made them uneconomic against the new diesel lorries.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internal_combustion">Internal combustion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Internal combustion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DMG-Lastwagen_von_1896.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3003" data-file-width="7025" height="107" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/DMG-Lastwagen_von_1896.jpg/250px-DMG-Lastwagen_von_1896.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/DMG-Lastwagen_von_1896.jpg/375px-DMG-Lastwagen_von_1896.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/DMG-Lastwagen_von_1896.jpg/500px-DMG-Lastwagen_von_1896.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DMG-Lastwagen_von_1896.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Daimler Motor-Lastwagen from 1898</div></div></div>
<p>In 1895 <a href="/wiki/Karl_Benz" title="Karl Benz">Karl Benz</a> designed and built the first truck in history using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internal_combustion" title="Internal combustion">internal combustion</a> engine. Later that year some of Benz's trucks were modified to become the first bus by the <i>Netphener</i>, the first motorbus company in history. A year later, in 1896, another internal combustion engine truck was built by <a href="/wiki/Gottlieb_Daimler" title="Gottlieb Daimler">Gottlieb Daimler</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Other companies, such as <a href="/wiki/Peugeot" title="Peugeot">Peugeot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Renault" title="Renault">Renault</a> and <a href="/wiki/B%C3%BCssing" title="Büssing">Büssing</a>, also built their own versions. The first truck in the United States was built by <a href="/wiki/Autocar_Company" title="Autocar Company">Autocar</a> in 1899 and was available with optional 5 or 8 horsepower motors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Trucks of the era mostly used <a href="/wiki/Straight_engine" title="Straight engine">two-cylinder</a> engines and had a carrying capacity of 3,300 to 4,400 lb (1.5 to 2 t). In 1904, 700 heavy trucks were built in the United States, 1000 in 1907, 6000 in 1910, and 25000 in 1914.
</p><p>After World War I, several advances were made: pneumatic <a href="/wiki/Tire" title="Tire">tires</a> replaced the previously common full rubber versions. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Starter_motor" title="Starter motor">Electric starters</a>, power <a href="/wiki/Brake" title="Brake">brakes</a>, 4, 6, and 8 cylinder engines, closed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cabin_(truck)" title="Cabin (truck)">cabs</a>, and electric lighting followed. The first modern <a href="/wiki/Semi-trailer_truck" title="Semi-trailer truck">semi-trailer trucks</a> also appeared. Touring car builders such as <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford</a> and Renault entered the heavy truck market.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diesel_engines">Diesel engines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diesel engines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although it had been invented in 1897, the <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">diesel engine</a> did not appear in production trucks until Benz introduced it in 1923.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
The diesel engine was not common in trucks in Europe until the 1930s. In the United States, it took much longer for diesel engines to be accepted: gasoline engines were still in use on heavy trucks in the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Truck</i> is used in <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a>, and is common in <a href="/wiki/Canadian_English" title="Canadian English">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australian_English" title="Australian English">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_English" title="New Zealand English">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistani_English" title="Pakistani English">Pakistan</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_African_English" title="South African English">South Africa</a>, while  <i>lorry</i> is the equivalent in <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a>, and is the usual term in countries like the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hiberno-English" title="Hiberno-English">Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_English" title="Malaysian English">Malaysia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Singaporean_English" title="Singaporean English">Singapore</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Indian_English" title="Indian English">India</a>.
</p><p>The word "truck" might come from a back-formation of "truckle", meaning "small wheel" or "pulley", from <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> <i>trokell</i>, in turn from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>trochlea</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Another possible source is the Latin <i>trochus</i>, meaning "iron hoop". In turn, both sources emanate from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>trokhos</i> (τροχός), meaning "wheel", from <i>trekhein</i> (τρέχειν, "to run").
</p><p>The first known usage of "truck" was in 1611, when it referred to the small strong wheels on ships' cannon carriages. In its extended usage it came to refer to carts for carrying heavy loads, a meaning known since 1771. Its expanded application to "motor-powered load carrier" has been in usage since 1930, shortened from "motor truck", which dates back to 1901.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>"Lorry" has a more uncertain origin, but probably has its roots in the <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport" title="Rail transport">rail transport</a> industry, where the word is known to have been used in 1838 to refer to a type of truck (a <a href="/wiki/Railroad_car" title="Railroad car">goods wagon</a> as in British usage, not a <a href="/wiki/Bogie" title="Bogie">bogie</a> as in the American), specifically a large flat wagon. It probably derives from the verb <i>lurry</i> (to pull, tug) of uncertain origin. Its expanded meaning, "self-propelled vehicle for carrying goods", has been in usage since 1911.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Before that,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (March 2016)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> the word "lorry" was used for a sort of big horse-drawn goods wagon.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_variance">International variance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: International variance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/List_of_truck_types" title="List of truck types">List of truck types</a></div>
<p>In the United States, Canada, and the Philippines "truck" is usually reserved for commercial vehicles larger than normal cars, and includes pickups and other vehicles having an open load bed. In Australia, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_English" title="New Zealand English">New Zealand</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_African_English" title="South African English">South Africa</a>, the word "truck" is mostly reserved for larger vehicles; in Australia and New Zealand, a pickup truck is usually called a <a href="/wiki/Ute_(vehicle)" title="Ute (vehicle)"><i>ute</i></a> (short for "utility"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> while in South Africa it is called a <i>bakkie</i> (<a href="/wiki/Afrikaans" title="Afrikaans">Afrikaans</a>: "small open container"). In the United Kingdom, India, Malaysia, Singapore, Ireland and <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> <i>lorry</i> is used instead of <i>truck</i>, but only for the medium and heavy types.
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Might-E_Truck.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3456" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Might-E_Truck.jpg/220px-Might-E_Truck.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Might-E_Truck.jpg/330px-Might-E_Truck.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Might-E_Truck.jpg/440px-Might-E_Truck.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Might-E_Truck.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Might-E_Truck" title="Might-E Truck">Might-E Truck</a> from <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Electric_Vehicles" title="Canadian Electric Vehicles">Canadian Electric Vehicles</a></div></div></div>
<p>Often produced as variations of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Golf_car" title="Golf car">golf cars</a>, with internal combustion or <a href="/wiki/Battery_electric_vehicle" title="Battery electric vehicle">battery electric drive</a>, these are used typically for off-highway use on estates, golf courses, and parks. While not suitable for highway use some variations may be licensed as slow speed vehicles for operation on streets, generally as a body variation of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neighborhood_electric_vehicle" title="Neighborhood electric vehicle">neighborhood electric vehicle</a>. A few manufactures produce specialized chassis for this type of vehicle, while <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zap_Motors" title="Zap Motors">Zap Motors</a> markets a version of their xebra electric tricycle (licensable in the U.S. as a motorcycle).
</p>

<p>Popular in Europe and Asia, many mini trucks are factory redesigns of light automobiles, usually with <a href="/wiki/Monocoque" title="Monocoque">monocoque</a> bodies. Specialized designs with substantial frames such as the Italian Piaggio shown here are based upon Japanese designs (in this case by <a href="/wiki/Daihatsu" title="Daihatsu">Daihatsu</a>) and are popular for use in "old town" sections of European cities that often have very narrow alleyways.
</p><p>Regardless of name, these small trucks serve a wide range of uses. In Japan, they are regulated under the <a href="/wiki/Kei_car" title="Kei car">Kei car</a> laws, which allow vehicle owners a break in taxes for buying a smaller and less-powerful vehicle (currently, the engine is limited to 660 cc displacement). These vehicles are used as on-road utility vehicles in Japan. These Japanese-made mini trucks that were manufactured for on-road use are competing with off-road ATVs in the United States, and import regulations require that these mini trucks have a 25 mph (40 km/h) speed governor as they are classified as low speed vehicles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> These vehicles have found uses in construction, large campuses (government, university, and industrial), agriculture, cattle ranches, amusement parks, and replacements for golf carts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Light_trucks">Light trucks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Light trucks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dongfeng_Rich_II_China_2016-04-07.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="812" data-file-width="1300" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Dongfeng_Rich_II_China_2016-04-07.jpg/220px-Dongfeng_Rich_II_China_2016-04-07.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Dongfeng_Rich_II_China_2016-04-07.jpg/330px-Dongfeng_Rich_II_China_2016-04-07.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Dongfeng_Rich_II_China_2016-04-07.jpg/440px-Dongfeng_Rich_II_China_2016-04-07.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dongfeng_Rich_II_China_2016-04-07.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dongfeng pickup</div></div></div>
<p>Light trucks are car-sized (in the US, no more than 13,900 lb (6.3 t) and are used by individuals and businesses alike. In the EU they may not weigh more than 3.5 t (7,700 lb), and are allowed to be driven with a <a href="/wiki/Driving_licence_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Driving licence in the United Kingdom">driving licence</a> for cars. <a href="/wiki/Pickup_truck" title="Pickup truck">Pickup trucks</a>, called utes in Australia and New Zealand, are common in North America and some regions of Latin America, Asia and Africa, but not so in Europe, where this size of commercial vehicle is most often made as vans.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medium_trucks">Medium trucks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Medium trucks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fuso_Canter_3C13,_8th_Generation.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1680" data-file-width="2700" height="137" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Fuso_Canter_3C13%2C_8th_Generation.jpg/220px-Fuso_Canter_3C13%2C_8th_Generation.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Fuso_Canter_3C13%2C_8th_Generation.jpg/330px-Fuso_Canter_3C13%2C_8th_Generation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Fuso_Canter_3C13%2C_8th_Generation.jpg/440px-Fuso_Canter_3C13%2C_8th_Generation.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fuso_Canter_3C13,_8th_Generation.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fuso Canter 3C13, 8th Generation in Spain.</div></div></div>
<p>Medium trucks are larger than light but smaller than heavy trucks. In the US, they are defined as weighing between 13,000 and 33,000 lb (5.9 and 15.0 t).
For the UK and the EU the weight is between 3.5 to 7.5 t (7,700 to 16,500 lb).  Local delivery and public service (<a href="/wiki/Dump_truck" title="Dump truck">dump trucks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Garbage_truck" title="Garbage truck">garbage trucks</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fire_apparatus" title="Fire apparatus">fire-fighting trucks</a>) are normally around this size.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heavy_trucks">Heavy trucks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Heavy trucks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Heavy trucks are the largest on-road trucks, <a href="/wiki/Truck_classification#Class_8" title="Truck classification">Class 8</a>.  These include vocational applications such as heavy dump trucks, concrete pump trucks, and refuse hauling, as well as ubiquitous long-haul 4x2 and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/6x4_(drivetrain)" title="6x4 (drivetrain)">6×4</a> <a href="/wiki/Tractor_unit" title="Tractor unit">tractor units</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Road damage and wear increase very rapidly with the axle weight. The number of steering axles and the suspension type also influence the amount of the road wear. In many countries with good roads a six-axle truck may have a maximum weight of 44 t (97,000 lb) or more.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Off-road_trucks">Off-road trucks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Off-road trucks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:An_ALMA_Antenna_on_the_Move.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="5184" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/An_ALMA_Antenna_on_the_Move.jpg/220px-An_ALMA_Antenna_on_the_Move.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/An_ALMA_Antenna_on_the_Move.jpg/330px-An_ALMA_Antenna_on_the_Move.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/An_ALMA_Antenna_on_the_Move.jpg/440px-An_ALMA_Antenna_on_the_Move.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:An_ALMA_Antenna_on_the_Move.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Atacama_Large_Millimeter_Array" title="Atacama Large Millimeter Array">ALMA</a> antenna transporters are 20 m long, 10 m wide, weigh 130 t (290,000 lb) and drive on 28 tires.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Off-road trucks include standard, extra heavy-duty highway-legal trucks, typically outfitted with off-road features such as a front driving axle and special tires for applications such as logging and construction, and purpose-built off-road vehicles unconstrained by weight limits, such as the <a href="/wiki/Liebherr_T_282B" title="Liebherr T 282B">Liebherr T 282B</a> mining truck.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Maximum_sizes_by_country">Maximum sizes by country</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Maximum sizes by country">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Australia</b> has complex regulations over weight and length, including axle spacing, type of axle/axle group, rear overhang, kingpin to rear of trailer, drawbar length, ground clearance, as well as height and width laws. These limits are some of the highest in the world, a B-double can weigh 62.5 t (138,000 lb) and be 25 m (82 ft) long, and road trains used in the <a href="/wiki/Outback" title="Outback">outback</a> can weigh 172 t (379,000 lb) and be 53.5 m (176 ft) long.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aus1_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-aus1-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aus6_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-aus6-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <b>European Union</b> also has complex regulations. The number and spacing of axles, steering, single or dual tires, and suspension type all affect maximum weights. Length of a truck, of a trailer, from axle to hitch point, kingpin to rear of trailer, and turning radius are all regulated. In additions, there are special rules for carrying containers, and countries can set their own rules for local traffic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eurlex_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-eurlex-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <b>United States</b> Federal Bridge Law deals with the relation between the gross weight of the truck, the number of axles, the weight on and the spacing between the axles that the truck can have on the Interstate highway system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Each State determines the maximum permissible <a href="/wiki/Gross_vehicle_weight_rating" title="Gross vehicle weight rating">vehicle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gross_combined_weight_rating" title="Gross combined weight rating">combination</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gross_axle_weight_rating" title="Gross axle weight rating">axle</a> weight on state and local roads.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Almost all trucks share a common construction: they are made of a <a href="/wiki/Chassis" title="Chassis">chassis</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cabin_(truck)" title="Cabin (truck)">cab</a>, an area for placing <a href="/wiki/Cargo" title="Cargo">cargo</a> or equipment, <a href="/wiki/Axle" title="Axle">axles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suspension_(vehicle)" title="Suspension (vehicle)">suspension</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wheel" title="Wheel">roadwheels</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Engine" title="Engine">engine</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Powertrain" title="Powertrain">drivetrain</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pneumatic" title="Pneumatic">Pneumatic</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydraulic" title="Hydraulic">hydraulic</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Engine_cooling" title="Engine cooling">water</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical" title="Electrical">electrical</a> systems may also be present. Many also tow one or more <a href="/wiki/Trailer_(vehicle)" title="Trailer (vehicle)">trailers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Semi-trailer" title="Semi-trailer">semi-trailers</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cab">Cab</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Cab">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Scania_R470_topline.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="768" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Scania_R470_topline.JPG/170px-Scania_R470_topline.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Scania_R470_topline.JPG/255px-Scania_R470_topline.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Scania_R470_topline.JPG/340px-Scania_R470_topline.JPG 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Scania_R470_topline.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Flat nose truck</div></div></div><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kenworth_T2000,_Kenworth_Dealer_Hall_of_Fame,_2015.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3126" data-file-width="3127" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Kenworth_T2000%2C_Kenworth_Dealer_Hall_of_Fame%2C_2015.JPG/220px-Kenworth_T2000%2C_Kenworth_Dealer_Hall_of_Fame%2C_2015.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Kenworth_T2000%2C_Kenworth_Dealer_Hall_of_Fame%2C_2015.JPG/330px-Kenworth_T2000%2C_Kenworth_Dealer_Hall_of_Fame%2C_2015.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Kenworth_T2000%2C_Kenworth_Dealer_Hall_of_Fame%2C_2015.JPG/440px-Kenworth_T2000%2C_Kenworth_Dealer_Hall_of_Fame%2C_2015.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kenworth_T2000,_Kenworth_Dealer_Hall_of_Fame,_2015.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Streamlined conventional cab</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kuwaiti_BM-30_Smerch.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3582" data-file-width="2646" height="298" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Kuwaiti_BM-30_Smerch.jpg/220px-Kuwaiti_BM-30_Smerch.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Kuwaiti_BM-30_Smerch.jpg/330px-Kuwaiti_BM-30_Smerch.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Kuwaiti_BM-30_Smerch.jpg/440px-Kuwaiti_BM-30_Smerch.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kuwaiti_BM-30_Smerch.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cab beside engine</div></div></div>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cabin_(truck)" title="Cabin (truck)">cab</a> is an enclosed space where the driver is seated. A "sleeper" is a compartment attached to or integral with the cab where the driver can rest while not driving, sometimes seen in <a href="/wiki/Semi-trailer_truck" title="Semi-trailer truck">semi-trailer trucks</a>.
</p><p>There are several possible cab configurations:
</p>
<ul><li>"<a href="/wiki/Cab_over" title="Cab over">Cab over</a> engine" (COE) or "flat nose"; where the driver is seated above the front axle and the <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">engine</a>. This design is almost ubiquitous in Europe, where overall truck lengths are strictly regulated, but also widely used in the rest of the world as well. They were common in North American heavy duty trucks, but lost prominence when permitted length was extended in the early 1980s. Nevertheless, this design is still popular in North America among medium and light duty trucks. To reach the engine, the whole cab tilts forward, earning this design the name of "tilt-cab". This type of cab is especially suited to the delivery conditions in Europe where many roads follow the layout of much more ancient paths and trackways which require the additional turning capability given by the short wheelbase of the cab over engine type.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavies200058–61_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavies200058–61-26">[26]</a></sup> The COE design was invented by <a href="/wiki/Viktor_Schreckengost" title="Viktor Schreckengost">Viktor Schreckengost</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conventional_truck" title="Conventional truck">Conventional</a></b> cabs are the most common in North America and Australia, and are known in the UK as "American cabs" and in the Netherlands as "torpedo cabs". The driver is seated behind the engine, as in most passenger cars or pickup trucks. Many new cabs are very <a class="extiw" href="https://meta.wikimedia.org/wiki/wikt:streamline" title="m:wikt:streamline">streamlined</a>, with a sloped hood and other features to lower drag.</li>
<li><b>Cab beside engine</b> designs also exist, but are rather rare and are mainly used inside <a href="/wiki/Shipyard" title="Shipyard">shipping yards</a>, or other specialist uses that require the vehicle to carry long loads such as pipes, metal rods, flat iron and other construction materials. This type is often custom made from a regular cabover truck that gets the upper half of its cab removed on the passenger side and replaced by an extended section of the bed.</li></ul>
<p>A further step from this is the side loading <a href="/wiki/Forklift" title="Forklift">forklift</a> that can be described as a specially fabricated vehicle with the same properties as a truck of this type, in addition to the ability to pick up its own load.
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Engine">Engine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Engine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cummins_Engine_(LKW).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1806" data-file-width="2171" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Cummins_Engine_%28LKW%29.jpg/220px-Cummins_Engine_%28LKW%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Cummins_Engine_%28LKW%29.jpg/330px-Cummins_Engine_%28LKW%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Cummins_Engine_%28LKW%29.jpg/440px-Cummins_Engine_%28LKW%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cummins_Engine_(LKW).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Cummins_B_Series_engine#B6.7" title="Cummins B Series engine">Cummins ISB</a> 6.7L medium duty truck diesel engine</div></div></div>
<p>Most small trucks such as <a href="/wiki/Sport_utility_vehicle" title="Sport utility vehicle">sport utility vehicles</a> (SUVs) or <a href="/wiki/Pickup_truck" title="Pickup truck">pickups</a>, and even light medium-duty trucks in North America, China, and Russia use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gasoline_engine" title="Gasoline engine">gasoline engines</a> (petrol engines), but many <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">diesel engined</a> models are now being produced. Most of the heavier trucks use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Four_stroke_cycle" title="Four stroke cycle">four-stroke</a> diesel engines with a <a href="/wiki/Turbocharger" title="Turbocharger">turbocharger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Intercooler" title="Intercooler">intercooler</a>. Huge off-highway trucks use locomotive-type engines such as a <a href="/wiki/V12_engine" title="V12 engine">V12</a> <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Diesel" title="Detroit Diesel">Detroit Diesel</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Two_stroke_cycle" title="Two stroke cycle">two stroke</a> engine. Diesel engines are becoming the engine of choice for trucks ranging from class 3 to 8 GVWs. A large proportion of refuse trucks in the United States employ <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CNG" title="CNG">CNG</a> (compressed natural gas) engines for their low fuel cost and reduced carbon emissions.
</p><p>North American manufactured highway trucks often use an engine built by a third party, such as <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar_Inc." title="Caterpillar Inc.">CAT</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cummins" title="Cummins">Cummins</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Diesel" title="Detroit Diesel">Detroit Diesel</a>, but both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mack_Truck" title="Mack Truck">Mack</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Navistar" title="Navistar">Navistar</a> offer their own engines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the European Union, all new truck engines must comply with <a href="/wiki/European_emission_standards" title="European emission standards">Euro VI</a> emission regulations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Drivetrain">Drivetrain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Drivetrain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rear_axles_tandem.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1524" data-file-width="2032" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Rear_axles_tandem.jpg/220px-Rear_axles_tandem.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Rear_axles_tandem.jpg/330px-Rear_axles_tandem.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Rear_axles_tandem.jpg/440px-Rear_axles_tandem.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rear_axles_tandem.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A truck rear suspension and drive axles overview</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eaton_Autoshift.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Eaton_Autoshift.jpg/220px-Eaton_Autoshift.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Eaton_Autoshift.jpg/330px-Eaton_Autoshift.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Eaton_Autoshift.jpg/440px-Eaton_Autoshift.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eaton_Autoshift.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eaton Roadranger 18 speed "crash box" with automated gearshift</div></div></div>
<p>Small trucks use the same type of <a href="/wiki/Transmission_(mechanics)" title="Transmission (mechanics)">transmissions</a> as almost all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">cars</a>, having either an automatic transmission or a manual transmission with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synchromesh" title="Synchromesh">synchromesh</a> (synchronizers). Bigger trucks often use manual transmissions without synchronizers, saving bulk and weight, although synchromesh transmissions are used in larger trucks as well. Transmissions without synchronizers, known as "crash boxes", require <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Double-clutching" title="Double-clutching">double-clutching</a> for each shift, (which can lead to repetitive motion injuries), or a technique known colloquially as "floating", a method of changing gears which doesn't use the clutch, except for starts and stops, due to the physical effort of double clutching, especially with non-power-assisted clutches, faster shifts, and less clutch wear.
</p><p>Double-clutching allows the driver to control the engine and transmission revolutions to synchronize, so that a smooth shift can be made; for example, when upshifting, the accelerator pedal is released and the clutch pedal is depressed while the gear lever is moved into neutral, the clutch pedal is then released and quickly pushed down again while the gear lever is moved to the next higher gear. Finally, the clutch pedal is released and the accelerator pedal pushed down to obtain required engine speed. Although this is a relatively fast movement, perhaps a second or so while transmission is in neutral, it allows the engine speed to drop and synchronize engine and transmission revolutions relative to the road speed. Downshifting is performed in a similar fashion, except the engine speed is now required to increase (while transmission is in neutral) just the right amount in order to achieve the synchronization for a smooth, non-collision gear change. "Skip changing" is also widely used; in principle operation is the same as double-clutching, but it requires neutral be held slightly longer than a single-gear change.
</p><p>Common North American setups include 9, 10, 13, 15, and 18 speeds. <a href="/wiki/Automatic_transmission" title="Automatic transmission">Automatic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Semi-automatic_transmission" title="Semi-automatic transmission">semi-automatic transmissions</a> for heavy trucks are becoming more and more common, due to advances both in transmission and engine power. In Europe, 8, 10, 12 and 16 gears are common on larger trucks with manual transmission, while automatic or semi-automatic transmissions would have anything from 5 to 12 gears. Almost all heavy truck transmissions are of the "range and split" (double H shift pattern) type, where range change and so‑called half gears or splits are air operated and always preselected before the main gear selection.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Frame">Frame</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Frame">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A_truck_chassis_section.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/A_truck_chassis_section.jpg/220px-A_truck_chassis_section.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/A_truck_chassis_section.jpg/330px-A_truck_chassis_section.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/A_truck_chassis_section.jpg/440px-A_truck_chassis_section.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A_truck_chassis_section.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A truck rear frame (chassis) section view</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ToyotaTundraChassis.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/ToyotaTundraChassis.jpg/220px-ToyotaTundraChassis.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/ToyotaTundraChassis.jpg/330px-ToyotaTundraChassis.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/ToyotaTundraChassis.jpg/440px-ToyotaTundraChassis.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ToyotaTundraChassis.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Pickup truck frame (right rear view)</div></div></div>
<p>A truck <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frame_(vehicle)" title="Frame (vehicle)">frame</a> consists of two parallel boxed (tubular) or C‑shaped rails, or beams, held together by <a href="/wiki/Crossmember" title="Crossmember">crossmembers</a>. These frames are referred to as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ladder_frame" title="Ladder frame">ladder frames</a> due to their resemblance to a ladder if tipped on end. The rails consist of a tall vertical section (two if boxed) and two shorter horizontal flanges. The height of the vertical section provides opposition to vertical flex when weight is applied to the top of the frame (beam resistance). Though typically flat the whole length on heavy duty trucks, the rails may sometimes be tapered or arched for clearance around the engine or over the axles. The holes in rails are used either for mounting vehicle components and running wires and hoses, or measuring and adjusting the orientation of the rails at the factory or repair shop.
</p><p>The frame is usually made of <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>, but can be made (whole or in part) of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum" title="Aluminum">aluminum</a> for a lighter weight. A <a href="/wiki/Drawbar_(haulage)" title="Drawbar (haulage)">tow bar</a> may be found attached at one or both ends, but heavy trucks almost always make use of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fifth_wheel" title="Fifth wheel">fifth wheel</a> hitch.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Body_types">Body types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Body types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Refrigerator_truck" title="Refrigerator truck">Refrigerator trucks</a></b> have insulated panels as walls and a roof and floor, used for transporting fresh and frozen cargo such as ice cream, food, vegetables, and prescription drugs. They are mostly equipped with double-wing rear doors, but a side door is sometimes fitted.
</p><p><b><a href="/wiki/Box_truck" title="Box truck">Box trucks</a></b> ("tilts" in the UK) have walls and a roof, making an enclosed load space. The rear has doors for unloading; a side door is sometimes fitted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavies200030–31_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavies200030–31-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p><b><a href="/wiki/Concrete_mixer" title="Concrete mixer">Concrete mixers</a></b> have a rotating drum on an inclined axis, rotating in one direction to mix, and in the other to discharge the concrete down chutes. Because of the weight and power requirements of the drum body and rough construction sites, mixers have to be very heavy duty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p><b><a href="/wiki/Dump_truck" title="Dump truck">Dump trucks</a></b> ("tippers" in the UK) transport loose material such as sand, gravel, or dirt for construction. A typical dump truck has an open-box bed, which is hinged at the rear and lifts at the front, allowing the material in the bed to be unloaded ("dumped") on the ground behind the truck.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavies200034–35_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavies200034–35-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p><b><a href="/wiki/Flatbed_truck" title="Flatbed truck">Flatbed trucks</a></b> have an entirely flat, level platform body. This allows for quick and easy loading but has no protection for the load. Hanging or removable sides are sometimes fitted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavies200028–29_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavies200028–29-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p><b><a href="/wiki/Tractor_unit" title="Tractor unit">Semi-tractors</a></b> ("artics" in the UK) have a fifth wheel for towing a semi-trailer instead of a body.
</p><p><b><a href="/wiki/Tank_truck" title="Tank truck">Tank trucks</a></b> ("tankers" in the UK) are designed to carry <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquids" title="Liquids">liquids</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gases</a>. They usually have a cylindrical tank lying horizontally on the chassis. Many variants exist due to the wide variety of liquids and gases that can be transported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavies200032–33_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavies200032–33-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p><b><a href="/wiki/Tow_truck" title="Tow truck">Wreckers</a></b> ("recovery lorries" in the UK) are used to recover and/or tow disabled vehicles. They are normally equipped with a boom with a cable; wheel/chassis lifts are becoming common on newer trucks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavies200046–47_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavies200046–47-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Driving">Driving</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Driving">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In many countries, driving a truck requires a special driving license. The requirements and limitations vary with each different jurisdiction.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Inside_Mack_Granite.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Inside_Mack_Granite.JPG/220px-Inside_Mack_Granite.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Inside_Mack_Granite.JPG/330px-Inside_Mack_Granite.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Inside_Mack_Granite.JPG/440px-Inside_Mack_Granite.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Inside_Mack_Granite.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Inside a Mack truck</div></div></div>
<p>In Australia, a <a href="/wiki/Truck_driver" title="Truck driver">truck driver</a>'s license is required for any motor vehicle with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gross_Vehicle_Mass" title="Gross Vehicle Mass">Gross Vehicle Mass</a> (GVM) exceeding 4.5 t (9,900 lb). The motor vehicles classes are further expanded as:
</p>
<dl><dt>Combination</dt></dl>
<ul><li><b>HC</b>: Heavy Combination, a typical prime mover plus semi-trailer combination.</li>
<li><b>MC</b>: Multi Combination, e.g., B Doubles/road trains</li></ul>
<dl><dt>Rigid</dt></dl>
<ul><li><b>LR</b>: Light rigid: a rigid vehicle with a GVM of more than 4.5 t (9,900 lb) but not more than 8 t (17,600 lb). Any towed trailer must not weigh more than 9 t (20,000 lb) GVM.</li>
<li><b>MR</b>: Medium rigid: a rigid vehicle with 2 axles and a GVM of more than 8 t (17,600 lb). Any towed trailer must not weigh more than 9 t (20,000 lb) GVM. Also includes vehicles in class <i>LR</i>.</li>
<li><b>HR</b>: Heavy Rigid: a rigid vehicle with three or more axles and a GVM of more than 8 t (18,000 lb). Any towed trailer must not weigh more than 9 t (20,000 lb) GVM. Also includes articulated buses and vehicles in class <i>MR</i>.</li></ul>
<dl><dt>Heavy vehicle transmission</dt></dl>
<p>There is also a heavy vehicle transmission condition for a license class <i>HC</i>, <i>HR</i>, or <i>MC</i> test passed in a vehicle fitted with an automatic or synchromesh transmission; a driver's license will be restricted to vehicles of that class fitted with a synchromesh or automatic transmission. To have the condition removed, a person needs to pass a practical driving test in a vehicle with non-synchromesh transmission (constant mesh or crash box).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mercedes-Benz_LKW_Lenkrad.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Mercedes-Benz_LKW_Lenkrad.jpg/220px-Mercedes-Benz_LKW_Lenkrad.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Mercedes-Benz_LKW_Lenkrad.jpg/330px-Mercedes-Benz_LKW_Lenkrad.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Mercedes-Benz_LKW_Lenkrad.jpg/440px-Mercedes-Benz_LKW_Lenkrad.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mercedes-Benz_LKW_Lenkrad.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Inside a Mercedes-Benz truck</div></div></div>
<p>Driving licensing has been harmonized throughout the European Union (and practically all European non-member states), so that common rules apply within Europe (see <a href="/wiki/European_driving_licence" title="European driving licence">European driving licence</a>). As an overview, to drive a vehicle weighing more than 7.5 t (16,500 lb) for commercial purposes requires a specialist license (the type varies depending on the use of the vehicle and number of seats). For licenses first acquired after 1997, that weight was reduced to 3.5 t (7,700 lb), not including trailers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are around 5 million truck drivers in India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa">South Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: South Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To drive any vehicle with a GVM exceeding 3.5 t (7,700 lb), a code C1 drivers license is required.  Furthermore, if the vehicle exceeds  16 t (35,300 lb) a code C license becomes necessary.
</p><p>To drive any vehicle in South Africa towing a trailer with a GVM more than 7.5 t (16,500 lb), further restrictions apply and the driver must possess a license suitable for the GVM of the total combination as well as an articulated endorsement. This is indicated with the letter "E" prefixing the license code.
</p><p>In addition, any vehicle designed to carry goods or passengers may only be driven by a driver possessing a Public Driver's Permit, (or PrDP) of the applicable type.  This is an additional license that is added to the DL card of the operator and subject to annual renewal unlike the five-year renewal period of a normal license.
</p><p>The requirements for obtaining the different classes are below.
</p>
<ul><li>"G": Required for the transport of general goods, requires a criminal record check and a fee on issuing and renewal.</li>
<li>"P": Required for the transport of paying passengers, requires a more stringent criminal record check on, additionally the driver must be over the age of 21 at time of issue. A G class PrDP will be issued at the same time.</li>
<li>"D": Required for the transport of dangerous materials,  requires all of the same checks as class P. In addition the driver must be over 25 at time of issue.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Truck_cab.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1704" data-file-width="2272" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Truck_cab.JPG/220px-Truck_cab.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Truck_cab.JPG/330px-Truck_cab.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Truck_cab.JPG/440px-Truck_cab.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Truck_cab.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Inside a Navistar 9000</div></div></div>
<p>In the United States, a <a href="/wiki/Commercial_driver%27s_license" title="Commercial driver's license">commercial driver's license</a> is required to drive any type of commercial vehicle weighing 26,001 lb (11.8 t) or more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The federal government regulates how many hours a driver may be on the clock, how much rest and sleep time is required (e.g., 11 hours driving/14 hours on-duty followed by 10 hours off, with a maximum of 70 hours/8 days or 60 hours/7 days, 34 hours restart )<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Violations are often subject to significant penalties. Instruments to track each driver's hours must sometimes be fitted.
In 2006, the <a href="/wiki/Trucking_industry_in_the_United_States" title="Trucking industry in the United States">US trucking industry</a> employed 1.8 million drivers of heavy trucks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_effects">Environmental effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Environmental effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Diesel_exhaust" title="Diesel exhaust">Diesel exhaust</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Truck.car.transporter.arp.750pix.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="573" data-file-width="750" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Truck.car.transporter.arp.750pix.jpg/220px-Truck.car.transporter.arp.750pix.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Truck.car.transporter.arp.750pix.jpg/330px-Truck.car.transporter.arp.750pix.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Truck.car.transporter.arp.750pix.jpg/440px-Truck.car.transporter.arp.750pix.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Truck.car.transporter.arp.750pix.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>DAF <a href="/wiki/Tractor_unit" title="Tractor unit">tractor</a> with an auto-transport <a href="/wiki/Semi-trailer" title="Semi-trailer">semi-trailer</a> carrying <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Octavia" title="Škoda Octavia">Škoda Octavia</a> cars in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardiff,_Wales" title="Cardiff, Wales">Cardiff, Wales</a></div></div></div>
<p>Trucks contribute to air, noise, and <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">water pollution</a> similarly to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">automobiles</a>. Trucks may emit lower <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a> emissions than cars per equivalent vehicle mass, although the absolute level per vehicle distance traveled is higher, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diesel_particulate_matter" title="Diesel particulate matter">diesel particulate matter</a> is especially problematic for health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> EPA measures pollution from trucks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> With respect to <a href="/wiki/Noise_pollution" title="Noise pollution">noise pollution</a>, trucks emit considerably higher sound levels at all speeds compared to typical cars; this contrast is particularly strong with heavy-duty trucks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> There are several aspects of truck operations that contribute to the overall sound that is emitted. Continuous sounds are those from <a href="/wiki/Tire" title="Tire">tires</a> rolling on the roadway, and the constant hum of their diesel engines at highway speeds. Less frequent noises, but perhaps more noticeable, are things like the repeated sharp-pitched whistle of a <a href="/wiki/Turbocharger" title="Turbocharger">turbocharger</a> on acceleration, or the abrupt blare of an <a href="/wiki/Exhaust_brake" title="Exhaust brake">exhaust brake</a> <a href="/wiki/Retarder_(mechanical_engineering)" title="Retarder (mechanical engineering)">retarder</a> when traversing a downgrade. There has been <a href="/wiki/Noise_regulation" title="Noise regulation">noise regulation</a> put in place to help control where and when the use of <a href="/wiki/Engine_braking" title="Engine braking">engine braking</a> retarders are allowed.
</p><p>Concerns have been raised about the effect of trucking on the environment, particularly as part of the debate on <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. In the period from 1990 to 2003, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> emissions from transportation sources increased by 20%, despite improvements in vehicle fuel efficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Renault_Magnum-Palifor_(B)-2004.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Renault_Magnum-Palifor_%28B%29-2004.jpg/220px-Renault_Magnum-Palifor_%28B%29-2004.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Renault_Magnum-Palifor_%28B%29-2004.jpg/330px-Renault_Magnum-Palifor_%28B%29-2004.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Renault_Magnum-Palifor_%28B%29-2004.jpg/440px-Renault_Magnum-Palifor_%28B%29-2004.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Renault_Magnum-Palifor_(B)-2004.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Volvo's subsidiary <a href="/wiki/Renault_Magnum" title="Renault Magnum">Renault Magnum</a> <a href="/wiki/Tractor_unit" title="Tractor unit">Tractor unit</a></div></div></div>
<p>In 2005, transportation accounted for 27% of U.S. <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emission, increasing faster than any other sector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>Between 1985 and 2004, in the U.S., energy consumption in freight transportation grew nearly 53%,
while the number of ton-miles carried increased only 43%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to a 1995 U.S. government estimate, the energy cost of carrying one <a href="/wiki/Ton" title="Ton">ton</a> of freight a distance of one kilometer averages 337 kJ for water, 221 kJ for rail, 2,000 kJ for trucks, and nearly 13,000 kJ for air transport.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Many environmental organizations favor laws and incentives to encourage the switch from road to rail, especially in Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> is moving to ensure that charges on heavy-goods vehicles should be based in part on the air and <a href="/wiki/Noise_pollution" title="Noise pollution">noise pollution</a> they produce and the congestion they cause, according to legislation approved by the Transport Committee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eurovignette" title="Eurovignette">Eurovignette</a> scheme has been proposed, whereby new charges would be potentially levied against things such as noise and air pollution and also weight related damages from the lorries themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 60-ton tractor &amp; trailer at 80 km/h needs 168 kW : 41% (68 kW) to overcome the <a href="/wiki/Rolling_resistance" title="Rolling resistance">rolling resistance</a>, 38% (64 kW) for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_drag" title="Aerodynamic drag">aerodynamic drag</a>,  9% (15 kW) for the auxiliaries, 7% (12 kW) for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Driveline" title="Driveline">driveline</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Tire" title="Tire">tire</a> and 6% (10 kW) is lost in uphill/downhill <a href="/wiki/Hysteresis" title="Hysteresis">hysteresis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations_issues">Operations issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Operations issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxes">Taxes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Taxes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Commercial trucks in the US pay higher road use taxes on a state level than other road vehicles, and are subject to extensive regulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> A few reasons commercial trucks pay higher road use taxes: they are bigger and heavier than most other vehicles, and cause more wear and tear per hour on roadways; and trucks and their drivers are on the road for more hours per day. Rules on use taxes differ among jurisdictions.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Damage_to_pavement">Damage to pavement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Damage to pavement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The life of a pavement is measured by the number of passes of a vehicle axle. It may be evaluated using the Load Equivalency Factor,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> which states that the damage by the pass of a vehicle axle is proportional to the 4th power of the weight, so a ten-ton axle consumes 10,000 times the life of the pavement as a one-ton axle. For that reason, loaded trucks cost the same as thousands of cars in pavement costs, and are subject to higher taxes and highway tolls.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_insurance">Commercial insurance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Commercial insurance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Primary liability insurance coverage protects the truck from damage or injuries to other people as a result of a truck accident. This truck insurance coverage is mandated by U.S. state and federal agencies, and proof of coverage is required to be sent to them. Interstate trucks in the U.S. are required to have a minimum of $75,000 in liability insurance. This includes motor carriers operating vehicles with a gross weight rating in excess of 10,000 lb (4.5 t) (which transport non-hazardous materials). All motor carriers operating vehicles transporting materials classified as hazardous, and which have a gross weight rating in excess of 10,000 lb (4.5 t) must have a minimum of $1,000,000 in liability insurance. All motor carriers operating vehicles such as hopper-type cargo vehicles or tankers with a capacity in excess of 3,500 US gal (13,000 l) must have a minimum of $5,000,000 in liability insurance. Pricing is dependent on region, driving records, and history of the trucking operation.
</p><p>Motor truck cargo insurance protects the transporter for his responsibility in the event of damaged or lost freight. The policy is purchased with a maximum load limit per vehicle. Cargo insurance coverage limits can range from $10,000 to $100,000 or more. Pricing for this insurance is mainly dependent on the type of cargo being hauled.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trucking_accidents">Trucking accidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Trucking accidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Elst_(Overbetuwe)_2012-03-23_Trucking_accident_(1).JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Elst_%28Overbetuwe%29_2012-03-23_Trucking_accident_%281%29.JPG/220px-Elst_%28Overbetuwe%29_2012-03-23_Trucking_accident_%281%29.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Elst_%28Overbetuwe%29_2012-03-23_Trucking_accident_%281%29.JPG/330px-Elst_%28Overbetuwe%29_2012-03-23_Trucking_accident_%281%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Elst_%28Overbetuwe%29_2012-03-23_Trucking_accident_%281%29.JPG/440px-Elst_%28Overbetuwe%29_2012-03-23_Trucking_accident_%281%29.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Elst_(Overbetuwe)_2012-03-23_Trucking_accident_(1).JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Trucking accident at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elst,_Overbetuwe" title="Elst, Overbetuwe">Elst (Netherlands)</a> 2012-03-23</div></div></div>
<p>In 2002 and 2004, there were over 5,000 fatalities related to trucking accidents in the United States. The trucking industry has since made significant efforts in increasing safety regulations. In 2008 the industry had successfully lowered the fatality rate to just over 4,000 deaths. But trucking accidents are still an issue that causes thousands of deaths and injuries each year. Approximately 6,000 trucking accident fatalities occur annually in the United States. Fatalities are not the only issue caused by trucking accidents. Here are some of the environmental issues that arise with trucking accidents:
</p>
<ul><li>14.4% of trucking accidents cause cargo to spill</li>
<li>6.5% cause open flames</li></ul>
<p>Following increased pressure from <i>The Times</i> "Cities Fit For Cycling" campaign and from other media in Spring 2012, warning signs are now displayed on the backs of many HGVs. These signs are directed against a common type of accident which occurs when the large vehicle turns left at a junction: a cyclist trying to pass on the nearside can be crushed against the HGV's wheels, especially if the driver cannot see the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclist" title="Cyclist">cyclist</a>. The signs, such as the winning design of the <a class="external text" href="http://www.intandemcompetition.com" rel="nofollow">InTANDEM road safety competition</a> launched in March 2012, advocate extra care when passing a large vehicle on the nearside.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Truck_shows">Truck shows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Truck&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Truck shows">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Volvo_FH_at_a_Yorkshire_event.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1360" data-file-width="2048" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Volvo_FH_at_a_Yorkshire_event.jpg/220px-Volvo_FH_at_a_Yorkshire_event.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Volvo_FH_at_a_Yorkshire_event.jpg/330px-Volvo_FH_at_a_Yorkshire_event.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Volvo_FH_at_a_Yorkshire_event.jpg/440px-Volvo_FH_at_a_Yorkshire_event.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Volvo_FH_at_a_Yorkshire_event.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Custom truck at a Yorkshire truck show</div></div></div>
<p>In the UK, three truck shows are popular – Shropshire Truck Show<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> in Oswestry Showground during May, The UK Truck Show held in June at Santa Pod Raceway, and FIA European Drag Racing Championships from the home of European Drag-Racing. The UK Truck Show features drag-racing with 6-tonne trucks from the British Truck Racing Association, plus other diesel-powered entertainment.
</p><p>In Mexico, the ANPACT Autotransporte – Truck Show is well known as one of the biggest of the region; 2013 edition features trucker celebrity <a href="/wiki/Lisa_Kelly_(trucker)" title="Lisa Kelly (trucker)">Lisa Kelly</a>.
</p><p>Truck shows provide operators with an opportunity to win awards for their trucks.
</p>
