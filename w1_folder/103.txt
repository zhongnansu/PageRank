<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">PubMed</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>PubMed</b> is a free search engine accessing primarily the  <a href="/wiki/MEDLINE" title="MEDLINE">MEDLINE</a> <a href="/wiki/Bibliographic_database" title="Bibliographic database">database</a> of references and abstracts on life sciences and biomedical topics. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Library_of_Medicine" title="United States National Library of Medicine">United States National Library of Medicine</a> (NLM) at the <a href="/wiki/National_Institutes_of_Health" title="National Institutes of Health">National Institutes of Health</a> maintains the database as part of the <a href="/wiki/Entrez" title="Entrez">Entrez</a> system of <a href="/wiki/Information_retrieval" title="Information retrieval">information retrieval</a>.
</p><p>From 1971 to 1997, MEDLINE online access to the <a href="/wiki/MEDLINE" title="MEDLINE">MEDLARS Online</a> computerized database primarily had been through institutional facilities, such as <a href="/wiki/Academic_library" title="Academic library">university libraries</a>. PubMed, first released in January 1996, ushered in the era of private, free, home- and office-based MEDLINE searching.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The PubMed system was offered free to the public starting in June 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid11185333_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid11185333-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Content">Content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PubMed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to MEDLINE, PubMed provides access to:
</p>
<ul><li>older references from the print version of <i><a href="/wiki/Index_Medicus" title="Index Medicus">Index Medicus</a></i>, back to 1951 and earlier</li>
<li>references to some journals before they were indexed in Index Medicus and MEDLINE, for instance <a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)"><i>Science</i></a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BMJ" title="BMJ">BMJ</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Annals_of_Surgery" title="Annals of Surgery">Annals of Surgery</a></i></li>
<li>very recent entries to records for an article before it is indexed with <a href="/wiki/Medical_Subject_Headings" title="Medical Subject Headings">Medical Subject Headings</a> (MeSH) and added to MEDLINE</li>
<li>a collection of books available full-text and other subsets of NLM records<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/PubMed_Central" title="PubMed Central">PMC</a> citations</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Biotechnology_Information#NCBI_Bookshelf" title="National Center for Biotechnology Information">NCBI Bookshelf</a></li></ul>
<p>Many PubMed records contain links to full text articles, some of which are freely available, often in <a href="/wiki/PubMed_Central" title="PubMed Central">PubMed Central</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and local mirrors such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK_PubMed_Central" title="UK PubMed Central">UK PubMed Central</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Information about the journals indexed in MEDLINE, and available through PubMed, is found in the NLM Catalog.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of  11 July 2017<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=PubMed&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, PubMed has more than 27.3 million records going back to 1966, selectively to the year 1865, and very selectively to 1809; about 500,000 new records are added each year. As of the same date<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=PubMed&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, 13.1 million of PubMed's records are listed with their abstracts, and 14.2 million articles have links to full-text (of which 3.8 million articles are available, full-text for free for any user).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Approximately 12% of the records in PubMed correspond to cancer-related entries, which have grown from 6% in the 1950s to 16% in 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> 
Other significant proportion of records correspond to “Chemistry” (8.69%), “Therapy” (8.39%) and "Infection" (5%).
</p><p>In 2016, NLM changed the indexing system so that publishers will be able to directly correct typos and errors in PubMed indexed articles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PubMed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Standard_searches">Standard searches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PubMed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Standard searches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Simple searches on PubMed can be carried out by entering key aspects of a subject into PubMed's search window.
</p><p>PubMed translates this initial search formulation and automatically adds field names, relevant MeSH (Medical Subject Headings) terms, synonyms, Boolean operators, and 'nests' the resulting terms appropriately, enhancing the search formulation significantly, in particular by routinely combining (using the OR operator) textwords and MeSH terms.
</p><p>The examples given in a PubMed tutorial<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> demonstrate how this automatic process works:
</p>
<dl><dd><b>Causes Sleep Walking</b> is translated as ("etiology"[Subheading] OR "etiology"[All Fields] OR "causes"[All Fields] OR "causality"[MeSH Terms] OR "causality"[All Fields]) <b>AND</b> ("somnambulism"[MeSH Terms] OR "somnambulism"[All Fields] OR ("sleep"[All Fields] AND "walking"[All Fields]) OR "sleep walking"[All Fields])</dd></dl>
<p>Likewise,
</p>
<dl><dd><b>Heart Attack Aspirin Prevention</b> is translated as ("myocardial infarction"[MeSH Terms] OR ("myocardial"[All Fields] AND "infarction"[All Fields]) OR "myocardial infarction"[All Fields] OR ("heart"[All Fields] AND "attack"[All Fields]) OR "heart attack"[All Fields]) <b>AND</b> ("aspirin"[MeSH Terms] OR "aspirin"[All Fields]) <b>AND</b> ("prevention and control"[Subheading] OR ("prevention"[All Fields] AND "control"[All Fields]) OR "prevention and control"[All Fields] OR "prevention"[All Fields])</dd></dl>
<p>A new PubMed interface was launched in October 2009 and encouraged the use of such quick, Google-like search formulations; they have also been described as 'telegram' searches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid10968827_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid10968827-11">[11]</a></sup>  By default the results are sorted by Most Recent, but this changed to Best Match, Publication Date, First Author, Last Author, Journal, or Title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comprehensive_searches">Comprehensive searches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PubMed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Comprehensive searches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For optimal searches in PubMed, it is necessary to understand its core component, MEDLINE, and especially of the MeSH (Medical Subject Headings) controlled vocabulary used to index MEDLINE articles. They may also require complex search strategies, use of field names (tags), proper use of limits and other features; reference librarians and search specialists offer search services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid8343701_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid8343701-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid10507188_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid10507188-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Journal_article_parameters">Journal article parameters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PubMed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Journal article parameters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When a journal article is indexed, numerous article parameters are extracted and stored as structured information. Such parameters are: Article Type (MeSH terms, e.g., "Clinical Trial"), Secondary identifiers, (MeSH terms), Language, Country of the Journal or publication history (e-publication date, print journal publication date).
</p>
<h4><span id="Publication_Type:_Clinical_queries.2Fsystematic_reviews"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Publication_Type:_Clinical_queries/systematic_reviews">Publication Type: Clinical queries/systematic reviews</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PubMed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Publication Type: Clinical queries/systematic reviews">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Publication type parameter allows searching by the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_journal#Types_of_articles" title="Scientific journal">type of publication</a>, including reports of various kinds of clinical research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Secondary_ID">Secondary ID</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PubMed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Secondary ID">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Since July 2005, the MEDLINE article indexing process extracts identifiers from the article abstract and puts those in a field called Secondary Identifier (SI). The secondary identifier field is to store accession numbers to various databases of molecular sequence data, gene expression or chemical compounds and clinical trial IDs. For clinical trials, PubMed extracts trial IDs for the two largest trial registries: ClinicalTrials.gov (NCT identifier) and the International Standard Randomized Controlled Trial Number Register (IRCTN identifier).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adherence_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-adherence-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PubMed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A reference which is judged particularly relevant can be marked and "related articles" can be identified. If relevant, several studies can be selected and related articles to all of them can be generated (on PubMed or any of the other NCBI Entrez databases) using the 'Find related data' option. The related articles are then listed in order of "relatedness". To create these lists of related articles, PubMed compares words from the title and abstract of each citation, as well as the MeSH headings assigned, using a powerful word-weighted algorithm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The 'related articles' function has been judged to be so precise that the authors of a paper suggested it can be used instead of a full search.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid16467730_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid16467730-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mapping_to_MeSH">Mapping to MeSH</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PubMed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Mapping to MeSH">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>PubMed automatically links to MeSH terms and subheadings. Examples would be: "bad breath" links to (and includes in the search) "halitosis", "heart attack" to "myocardial infarction", "breast cancer" to "breast neoplasms". Where appropriate, these MeSH terms are automatically "expanded", that is, include more specific terms. Terms like "nursing" are automatically linked to "Nursing [MeSH]" or "Nursing [Subheading]". This feature is called Auto Term Mapping and is enacted, by default, in free text searching but not exact phrase searching (i.e. enclosing the search query with double quotes).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fatehi_102–112_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fatehi_102–112-19">[19]</a></sup> This feature makes PubMed searches more sensitive and avoids false-negative (missed) hits by compensating for the diversity of medical terminology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fatehi_102–112_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fatehi_102–112-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="My_NCBI">My NCBI</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PubMed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: My NCBI">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The PubMed optional facility "My NCBI" (with free registration) provides tools for
</p>
<ul><li>saving searches</li>
<li>filtering search results</li>
<li>setting up automatic updates sent by e-mail</li>
<li>saving sets of references retrieved as part of a PubMed search</li>
<li>configuring display formats or highlighting search terms</li></ul>
<p>and a wide range of other options.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The "My NCBI" area can be accessed from any computer with web-access.
An earlier version of "My NCBI" was called "PubMed Cubby".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="LinkOut">LinkOut</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PubMed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: LinkOut">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>LinkOut, a NLM facility to link (and make available full-text) local journal holdings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Some 3,200 sites (mainly academic institutions) participate in this NLM facility (as of  March 2010<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=PubMed&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>), from <a href="/wiki/Aalborg_University" title="Aalborg University">Aalborg University</a> in Denmark to <a href="/wiki/ZymoGenetics" title="ZymoGenetics">ZymoGenetics</a> in Seattle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Users at these institutions see their institutions logo within the PubMed search result (if the journal is held at that institution) and can access the full-text.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="PubMed_Commons">PubMed Commons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PubMed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: PubMed Commons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2016, PubMed allows authors of articles to comment on articles indexed by PubMed. This feature was initially  tested in a pilot mode (since 2013) and was made permanent in 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> In February 2018, PubMed Commons was discontinued due to the fact that "usage has remained minimal".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="PubMed_for_handhelds.2Fmobiles"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="PubMed_for_handhelds/mobiles">PubMed for handhelds/mobiles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PubMed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: PubMed for handhelds/mobiles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>PubMed/MEDLINE can be accessed via handheld devices, using for instance the <a href="/wiki/PICO_process" title="PICO process">"PICO"</a> option (for focused clinical questions) created by the NLM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> A "PubMed Mobile" option, providing access to a mobile friendly, simplified PubMed version, is also available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="askMEDLINE">askMEDLINE</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PubMed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: askMEDLINE">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>askMEDLINE, a free-text, natural language query tool for MEDLINE/PubMed, developed by the NLM, also suitable for handhelds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="PubMed_identifier"><span id="PubMed_identifier">PubMed identifier</span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PubMed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: PubMed identifier">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For help using PubMed identifiers within Wikipedia, see <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:PMID" title="Wikipedia:PMID">Wikipedia:PMID</a>.</div>
<p>A <b>PMID</b> (PubMed identifier or PubMed unique identifier)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Unique_identifier" title="Unique identifier">unique integer value</a>, starting at <code>1</code>, assigned to each PubMed record. A PMID is not the same as a PMCID which is the identifier for all works published in the free-to-access <a href="/wiki/PubMed_Central" title="PubMed Central">PubMed Central</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PMCID_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-PMCID-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>The assignment of a PMID or PMCID to a publication tells the reader nothing about the type or quality of the content. PMIDs are assigned to <a href="/wiki/Letter_to_the_editor" title="Letter to the editor">letters to the editor</a>, editorial opinions, <a href="/wiki/Op-ed" title="Op-ed">op-ed</a> columns, and any other piece that the editor chooses to include in the journal, as well as peer-reviewed papers. The existence of the identification number is also not proof that the papers have not been <a href="/wiki/Retraction" title="Retraction">retracted</a> for fraud, incompetence, or misconduct. The announcement about any <a href="/wiki/Correction_(newspaper)" title="Correction (newspaper)">corrections</a> to original papers may be assigned a PMID.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_interfaces">Alternative interfaces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PubMed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Alternative interfaces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MEDLINE_via_PubMed.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="584" data-file-width="743" height="244" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/MEDLINE_via_PubMed.png/310px-MEDLINE_via_PubMed.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/MEDLINE_via_PubMed.png/465px-MEDLINE_via_PubMed.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/MEDLINE_via_PubMed.png/620px-MEDLINE_via_PubMed.png 2x" width="310"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MEDLINE_via_PubMed.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>MEDLINE is one of the databases which are accessible via PubMed. Several companies provide access to MEDLINE through their platforms.</div></div></div>
<p>The National Library of Medicine leases the MEDLINE information to a number of private vendors such as <a href="/wiki/Embase" title="Embase">Embase</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ovid_Technologies" title="Ovid Technologies">Ovid</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dialog_(online_database)" title="Dialog (online database)">Dialog</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EBSCO_Publishing" title="EBSCO Publishing">EBSCO</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Knowledge_Finder&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Knowledge Finder (page does not exist)">Knowledge Finder</a> and many other commercial, non-commercial, and academic providers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> As of  October 2008<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=PubMed&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, more than 500 licenses had been issued, more than 200 of them to  providers outside the United States. As licenses to use MEDLINE data are available for free, the NLM in effect provides a free testing ground for a wide range<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cite_pmid|_21245076_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-cite_pmid|_21245076-33">[33]</a></sup> of alternative interfaces and 3rd party additions to PubMed, one of a very few large, professionally curated databases which offers this option.
</p><p>Lu<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cite_pmid|_21245076_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-cite_pmid|_21245076-33">[33]</a></sup> identifies a sample of 28 current and free Web-based PubMed versions, requiring no installation or registration, which are grouped into four categories:
</p>
<ul><li>Ranking search results, for instance: <a href="/wiki/ETBLAST" title="ETBLAST">eTBLAST</a>; <a href="/wiki/Hakia" title="Hakia">Hakia</a>; MedlineRanker;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cite_pmid|_19429696_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-cite_pmid|_19429696-34">[34]</a></sup> MiSearch;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cite_pmid|_18326507_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-cite_pmid|_18326507-35">[35]</a></sup></li>
<li>Clustering results by topics, authors, journals etc., for instance: <a href="/wiki/Anne_O%27Tate" title="Anne O'Tate">Anne O'Tate</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cite_pmid|_18279519_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-cite_pmid|_18279519-36">[36]</a></sup> ClusterMed;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> MedPPP;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></li>
<li>Enhancing semantics and visualization, for instance: EBIMed;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cite_pmid|_17237098_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-cite_pmid|_17237098-39">[39]</a></sup> MedEvi;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cite_pmid|_18400773_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-cite_pmid|_18400773-40">[40]</a></sup> <a class="external text" href="http://www.scicurve.com" rel="nofollow">SciCurve</a> (Note: <i>CiteXplore</i> was withdrawn from service on 15 February 2013,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> replaced by <a href="/wiki/Europe_PubMed_Central" title="Europe PubMed Central">Europe PubMed Central</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>)</li>
<li>Improved search interface and retrieval experience, for instance, askMEDLINE<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cite_pmid|_17238542_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-cite_pmid|_17238542-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cite_pmid|_16779244_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-cite_pmid|_16779244-44">[44]</a></sup> BabelMeSH;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cite_pmid|_17911830_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-cite_pmid|_17911830-45">[45]</a></sup> and PubCrawler.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cite_pmid|_15215341_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-cite_pmid|_15215341-46">[46]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/GoPubMed" title="GoPubMed">GoPubMed</a> is a knowledge-based (<a class="external text" href="http://www.geneontology.org/" rel="nofollow">Gene Ontology</a> and <a class="external text" href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/mesh" rel="nofollow">MeSH</a>) search engine for PubMed. <a class="external text" href="http://www.GoPubMed.com" rel="nofollow">GoPubMed</a> claims to be a <a href="/wiki/Semantic_search" title="Semantic search">semantic search</a> engine, but searches return exactly the same results as PubMed itself.</li>
<li>Expertscape provides search and ranking of medical and biomedical expertise by specific diagnosis, technique, or other terminology. Results are based on analysis derived from most recent ten years of PubMed data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></li>
<li>Search term forwarders like <cite class="citation web"><a class="external text" href="http://sm.kidney.de" rel="nofollow">"OssiPubMed online"</a>. O. Groth.</cite><span class="Z3988" title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Abook&amp;rft.genre=unknown&amp;rft.btitle=OssiPubMed+online&amp;rft.pub=O.+Groth&amp;rft_id=http%3A%2F%2Fsm.kidney.de&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3APubMed"></span><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r861714446">.mw-parser-output cite.citation{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output q{quotes:"\"""\"""'""'"}.mw-parser-output code.cs1-code{color:inherit;background:inherit;border:inherit;padding:inherit}.mw-parser-output .cs1-lock-free a{background:url("//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Lock-green.svg/9px-Lock-green.svg.png")no-repeat;background-position:right .1em center}.mw-parser-output .cs1-lock-limited a,.mw-parser-output .cs1-lock-registration a{background:url("//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Lock-gray-alt-2.svg/9px-Lock-gray-alt-2.svg.png")no-repeat;background-position:right .1em center}.mw-parser-output .cs1-lock-subscription a{background:url("//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Lock-red-alt-2.svg/9px-Lock-red-alt-2.svg.png")no-repeat;background-position:right .1em center}.mw-parser-output .cs1-subscription,.mw-parser-output .cs1-registration{color:#555}.mw-parser-output .cs1-subscription span,.mw-parser-output .cs1-registration span{border-bottom:1px dotted;cursor:help}.mw-parser-output .cs1-hidden-error{display:none;font-size:100%}.mw-parser-output .cs1-visible-error{font-size:100%}.mw-parser-output .cs1-subscription,.mw-parser-output .cs1-registration,.mw-parser-output .cs1-format{font-size:95%}.mw-parser-output .cs1-kern-left,.mw-parser-output .cs1-kern-wl-left{padding-left:0.2em}.mw-parser-output .cs1-kern-right,.mw-parser-output .cs1-kern-wl-right{padding-right:0.2em}</style>, which runs searches on multiple external platforms derived from the original boolean search terms.</li>
<li>Reference-to-PubMed transcriptors like <cite class="citation web"><a class="external text" href="http://sm.kidney.de" rel="nofollow">"OssiPubMed online"</a>. O. Groth.</cite><span class="Z3988" title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Abook&amp;rft.genre=unknown&amp;rft.btitle=OssiPubMed+online&amp;rft.pub=O.+Groth&amp;rft_id=http%3A%2F%2Fsm.kidney.de&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3APubMed"></span><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r861714446" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/>, which retrieves the PMID from one-letter coded journal abbreviations to get the full-text articles.</li>
<li>Link-Out arborizers <cite class="citation web"><a class="external text" href="http://sm.kidney.de" rel="nofollow">"OssiPubMed online"</a>. O. Groth.</cite><span class="Z3988" title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Abook&amp;rft.genre=unknown&amp;rft.btitle=OssiPubMed+online&amp;rft.pub=O.+Groth&amp;rft_id=http%3A%2F%2Fsm.kidney.de&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fen.wikipedia.org%3APubMed"></span><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r861714446" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/>, which tries to retrieve available PDF's from additional hosts.</li></ul>
<p>As most of these and other alternatives rely essentially on PubMed/MEDLINE data leased under license from the NLM/PubMed, the term "PubMed derivatives" has been suggested.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cite_pmid|_21245076_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-cite_pmid|_21245076-33">[33]</a></sup> Without the need to store about 90 GB of original PubMed Datasets, anybody can write PubMed applications using the eutils-application program interface as described in "The E-utilities In-Depth: Parameters, Syntax and More", by Eric Sayers, PhD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Various citation format generators, taking PMID numbers as input, are examples of web applications making use of the eutils-application program interface. Sample web pages include <a class="external text" href="https://mickschroeder.com/citation/" rel="nofollow">Citation Generator - Mick Schroeder</a>, <a class="external text" href="https://www.ultrasoundoftheweek.com/citation/" rel="nofollow">Pubmed Citation Generator - Ultrasound of the Week</a>, <a class="external text" href="https://www.pmid2cite.com/" rel="nofollow">PMID2cite</a>, and <a class="external text" href="http://www.citethisforme.com/citation-generator/ama" rel="nofollow">Cite this for me</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Data_mining_of_PubMed">Data mining of PubMed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=PubMed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Data mining of PubMed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Alternative methods to mine the data in PubMed use programming environments such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matlab" title="Matlab">Matlab</a>, <a href="/wiki/Python_(programming_language)" title="Python (programming language)">Python</a> or <a href="/wiki/R_(programming_language)" title="R (programming language)">R</a>. In these cases, queries of PubMed are written as lines of code and passed to PubMed and the response is then processed directly in the programming environment.  Code can be automated to systematically queries with different keywords such as disease, year, organs, etc. A recent publication (2017) found that the proportion of cancer-related entries in PubMed has risen from 6% in the 1950s to 16% in 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>The data accessible by PubMed can be mirrored locally using an unofficial tool such as MEDOC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MEDOC_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-MEDOC-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p>
