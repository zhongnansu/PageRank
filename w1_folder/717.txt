<p>The <b>Peabody Trust</b> was founded in 1862 as the 'Peabody Donation Fund' and now brands itself simply as <b>Peabody</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fin2009_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-fin2009-1">[1]</a></sup>. It is one of <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>'s oldest and largest <a href="/wiki/Housing_association" title="Housing association">housing associations</a> with around 55,000 properties across London and the South East. It is also a community benefit society and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urban_regeneration" title="Urban regeneration">urban regeneration</a> agency, a developer with a focus on regeneration, and a provider of an extensive range of community programmes.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peabody_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Peabody_Trust_estate_Horseferry_Road.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Peabody_Trust_estate_Horseferry_Road.jpg/220px-Peabody_Trust_estate_Horseferry_Road.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Peabody_Trust_estate_Horseferry_Road.jpg/330px-Peabody_Trust_estate_Horseferry_Road.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Peabody_Trust_estate_Horseferry_Road.jpg/440px-Peabody_Trust_estate_Horseferry_Road.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Peabody_Trust_estate_Horseferry_Road.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Peabody Trust estate in <a href="/wiki/Horseferry_Road" title="Horseferry Road">Horseferry Road</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The Trust was founded in 1862 by London-based American banker <a href="/wiki/George_Peabody" title="George Peabody">George Peabody</a>, who in the 1850s had developed a great affection for London, and determined to make a charitable gift to benefit it. His initial ideas included a system of drinking fountains (comparable to the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Drinking_Fountain_and_Cattle_Trough_Association" title="Metropolitan Drinking Fountain and Cattle Trough Association">Metropolitan Drinking Fountain and Cattle Trough Association</a> scheme actually set up by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Gurney_(MP)" title="Samuel Gurney (MP)">Samuel Gurney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edward_Thomas_Wakefield" title="Edward Thomas Wakefield">Edward Thomas Wakefield</a> in 1859), or a contribution to the "<a href="/wiki/Ragged_school" title="Ragged school">ragged schools</a>" of the <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Ashley-Cooper,_7th_Earl_of_Shaftesbury" title="Anthony Ashley-Cooper, 7th Earl of Shaftesbury">Earl of Shaftesbury</a>. In March 1859, however, he settled on establishing a <a href="/wiki/Model_dwellings_company" title="Model dwellings company">model dwellings company</a>. Three years later, in a letter to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> on 26 March 1862, he launched the Peabody Donation Fund, with an initial gift of £150,000. The aim of the organisation, he said, would be to "ameliorate the condition of the poor and needy of this great metropolis, and to promote their comfort and happiness". The paper reported, "We have today to announce an act of beneficence unexampled in its largeness and in the time and manner of the gift".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Shortly before his death in 1869, Peabody increased his gift to a munificent £500,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Peabody Trust was later constituted by <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Parliament" title="Act of Parliament">Act of Parliament</a>, stipulating its objectives to work solely within London for the relief of <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a>. This was to be expressed through the provision of <a href="/wiki/Model_dwellings_company" title="Model dwellings company">model dwellings</a> for the capital's poor.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Peabody_Buildings_1863.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1193" data-file-width="1753" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/ff/Peabody_Buildings_1863.jpg/220px-Peabody_Buildings_1863.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/ff/Peabody_Buildings_1863.jpg/330px-Peabody_Buildings_1863.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/ff/Peabody_Buildings_1863.jpg/440px-Peabody_Buildings_1863.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Peabody_Buildings_1863.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The first block of Peabody dwellings in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commercial_Street_(London)" title="Commercial Street (London)">Commercial Street</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spitalfields" title="Spitalfields">Spitalfields</a>. A wood-engraving published in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Illustrated_London_News" title="Illustrated London News">Illustrated London News</a></i> in 1863, shortly before the building opened.</div></div></div>
<p>The first block, designed by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Darbishire" title="Henry Darbishire">H.A. Darbishire</a> in a red-brick <a href="/wiki/Jacobethan" title="Jacobethan">Jacobethan</a> style, opened in <a href="/wiki/Commercial_Street,_London" title="Commercial Street, London">Commercial Street</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spitalfields" title="Spitalfields">Spitalfields</a>, on 29 February 1864. It cost £22,000 to build, and contained 57 "dwellings" (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Apartment" title="Apartment">flats</a>) for the poor, nine shops with accommodation for the shopkeepers, and baths and laundry facilities on the upper floor. <a href="/wiki/Flush_toilet" title="Flush toilet">Water-closets</a> were grouped in pairs by the staircases, with one shared between every two flats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This first block was followed by larger estates in <a href="/wiki/Islington" title="Islington">Islington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poplar,_London" title="Poplar, London">Poplar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shadwell" title="Shadwell">Shadwell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chelsea,_London" title="Chelsea, London">Chelsea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Westminster" title="Westminster">Westminster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bermondsey" title="Bermondsey">Bermondsey</a>, and elsewhere. By 1882 the Trust housed more than 14,600 people in 3,500 dwellings. By 1939 it owned more than 8,000 dwellings.
</p><p>In its early days, the Trust imposed strict rules to ensure that its tenants were of good moral character. Rents were to be paid weekly and punctually; there was a night-time curfew and a set of moral standards to be adhered to; and the dwellings could not be used for certain trades.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_mission">Current mission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peabody_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Current mission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Peabody wants to help people make the most of their lives by providing good-quality affordable homes, work with communities and promote wellbeing. It will: put the most vulnerable first; create great places at scale, where people want to live (Thamesmead and larger developments like St John’s Hill are examples of these); and build resilience in people and communities<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thamesmead_Regeneration">Thamesmead Regeneration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peabody_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Thamesmead Regeneration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Peabody is leading a £1 billion regeneration of Thamesmead, Abbey Wood and Plumstead in South-East London. The Group worked with the Royal Borough of Greenwich and the London Borough of Bexley to successfully bid for Greater London Authority housing zone status, which led to a c£80 million investment.
</p><p>As well as providing thousands of new homes in the area, Peabody says it will provide firm foundations for Thamesmead’s long term, sustainable rejuvenation by investing in existing homes, infrastructure and services to improve the quality of life for existing residents. Going beyond "bricks and mortar", Peabody says its work with partners will enhance employment, enterprise, cultural, social and leisure opportunities for people which is supported by socio-economic outputs. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Locally" title="Locally">locally</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Thamesmead and the surrounding area is a major growth and opportunity area for London. Crossrail will open at nearby Abbey Wood from 2019, and Peabody has said it will capitalise on this, ensuring the right amenities, infrastructure and transport connections are available for the long term sustainability of the area.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Peabody_Group">Peabody Group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peabody_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Peabody Group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2014 Gallions, Trust Thamesmead and Tilfen Land became part of the Peabody Group. The Peabody Group now comprises two housing associations, Peabody and Gallions, and a number of trading companies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fin2009_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-fin2009-1">[1]</a></sup>.
</p><p>In July 2017 Peabody merged with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Family_Mosaic" title="Family Mosaic">Family Mosaic</a> housing association under the "Peabody" name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mereger_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-mereger-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Funding">Funding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peabody_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Funding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Housing associations borrow money to pay for new homes and improvements. In March 2011, Peabody raised £200 million on a <a href="/wiki/Corporate_bond" title="Corporate bond">corporate bond</a>, at a rate among the best secured by a housing association borrowing in its own name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 2013 it issued a public bond for £350 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_Mosaic">Family Mosaic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peabody_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Family Mosaic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Family Mosaic is a <a href="/wiki/Housing_association" title="Housing association">housing association</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RobLoretta2014_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-RobLoretta2014-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> It has over 25,000 homes housing over 45,000 people<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-18">[18]</a></sup> and is one of the largest housing providers in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, Essex and the South  East of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-18">[18]</a></sup> Family Mosaic also provide care and support services to over 8,000 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Family Mosaic works with young people, helping them into work <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and promoting better health to young people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition to offering specialist housing for people with support needs and social housing for general needs tenants, a third strand of Family Mosaic is its role in assisting people to get onto the property ladder through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leasehold" title="Leasehold">leasehold</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shared_ownership" title="Shared ownership">shared ownership</a> properties.  In April 2017, Family Mosaic announced that it would be launching a 111 home development for private rental tenants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Family Mosaic also runs employment training courses and activity groups for its residents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> It also offers welfare benefits advice.
</p><p>Family Mosaic is a member of the <a href="/wiki/G15_(housing_associations)" title="G15 (housing associations)">G15</a> group of housing associations in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Committee2010_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Committee2010-25">[25]</a></sup> In July 2017 Family Mosaic merged with Peabody Trust housing association under the "Peabody" name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mereger_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-mereger-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsidiaries">Subsidiaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peabody_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Subsidiaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<li>Old Oak Housing Association – Owns and manages 669 homes in the <a href="/wiki/London_Borough_of_Hammersmith_and_Fulham" title="London Borough of Hammersmith and Fulham">London Borough of Hammersmith and Fulham</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-26">[26]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peabody_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2012, Family Mosaic was paid an out of court settlement by a north London council because of the council's failure to pay additional costs for two of the care home's residents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> 
A number of complaints have been made against Family Mosaic in the national and local press, including heating being cut-off for five months of the year,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> a ceiling collapsing,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> poor administration at one of its care homes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>  and a rodent infestation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> In 2014, Family Mosaic took the unusual step of apologising to its tenants after a malfunctioning computer system led to an increase in complaints about its repairs service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
