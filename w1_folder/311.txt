<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Beaujolais</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Beaujolais</b> (<small>French pronunciation: ​</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">[bo.ʒɔ.lɛ]</a></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/French_wine" title="French wine">French</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Appellation_d%27Origine_Contr%C3%B4l%C3%A9e" title="Appellation d'Origine Contrôlée">Appellation d'Origine Contrôlée</a></i> (AOC) <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a> generally made of the <a href="/wiki/Gamay" title="Gamay">Gamay</a> <a href="/wiki/Grape" title="Grape">grape</a> which has a thin skin and is low in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grape_tannins" title="Grape tannins">tannins</a>. Like most AOC wines they are not <a href="/wiki/Wine_label" title="Wine label">labeled</a> <a href="/wiki/Varietal" title="Varietal">varietally</a>. Whites from the region, which make up only 1% of its production, are made mostly with <a href="/wiki/Chardonnay" title="Chardonnay">Chardonnay</a> grapes though <a href="/wiki/Aligot%C3%A9" title="Aligoté">Aligoté</a> is also permitted until 2024 (on condition the vines were planted before 2004). Beaujolais tends to be a very <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body_(wine)" title="Body (wine)">light-bodied</a> red wine, with relatively high amounts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acidity" title="Acidity">acidity</a>. In some <a href="/wiki/Vintage" title="Vintage">vintages</a>, Beaujolais produces more wine than the Burgundy wine regions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chablis_(wine)" title="Chablis (wine)">Chablis</a>, <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B4te_d%27Or_(escarpment)" title="Côte d'Or (escarpment)">Côte d'Or</a>, <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B4te_Chalonnaise" title="Côte Chalonnaise">Côte Chalonnaise</a> and <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A2connais" title="Mâconnais">Mâconnais</a> put together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_pg_72-74_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_pg_72-74-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The wine takes its name from the historical <a href="/wiki/Beaujolais_(province)" title="Beaujolais (province)">Province of Beaujolais</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wine-producing_region" title="Wine-producing region">wine-producing region</a>. It is located north of <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">Lyon</a>, and covers parts of the north of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rh%C3%B4ne_(d%C3%A9partement)" title="Rhône (département)">Rhône</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D%C3%A9partement_in_France" title="Département in France">département</a></i> of the <a href="/wiki/Rh%C3%B4ne-Alpes" title="Rhône-Alpes">Rhône-Alpes</a> region and southern areas of the <a href="/wiki/Sa%C3%B4ne-et-Loire" title="Saône-et-Loire">Saône-et-Loire</a> <i>département</i> of <a href="/wiki/Burgundy" title="Burgundy">Burgundy</a>. While administratively considered part of the <a href="/wiki/Burgundy_wine" title="Burgundy wine">Burgundy wine</a> region, the climate is closer to the <a href="/wiki/Rh%C3%B4ne_wine" title="Rhône wine">Rhône</a> and the wine is sufficiently individual in character to be considered separately from Burgundy and Rhône. The region is known internationally for its long tradition of <a href="/wiki/Winemaking" title="Winemaking">winemaking</a>, for the use of <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_maceration" title="Carbonic maceration">carbonic maceration</a>, and more recently for the popular <a href="/wiki/Beaujolais_nouveau" title="Beaujolais nouveau">Beaujolais nouveau</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beaujolais&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The region of Beaujolais was first cultivated by the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a> who planted the areas along its trading route up the <a href="/wiki/Sa%C3%B4ne" title="Saône">Saône</a> valley. The most noticeable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome_(wine)" title="Ancient Rome (wine)">Roman vineyard</a> was <i>Brulliacus</i> located on the hillside of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mont_Brouilly&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mont Brouilly (page does not exist)">Mont Brouilly</a>. The Romans also planted vineyards in the area <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Morgon_(Beaujolais)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Morgon (Beaujolais) (page does not exist)">Morgon</a>. From the 7th century through the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, most of the <a href="/wiki/Viticulture" title="Viticulture">viticulture</a> and winemaking was done by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benedictine" title="Benedictine">Benedictine</a> monks. In the 10th century, the region got its name from the town of <a href="/wiki/Beaujeu,_Rh%C3%B4ne" title="Beaujeu, Rhône">Beaujeu, Rhône</a> and was ruled by the Lords of <b>Beaujeu</b> till the 15th century when it was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceded" title="Ceded">ceded</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Burgundy" title="Duchy of Burgundy">Duchy of Burgundy</a>. The wines from Beaujolais were mostly confined to the markets along the Saône and <a href="/wiki/Rh%C3%B4ne" title="Rhône">Rhône</a>, particularly in the town of Lyon. The expansion of the <a href="/wiki/History_of_rail_transport_in_France" title="History of rail transport in France">French railroad system</a> in the 19th century opened up the lucrative <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> market. The first mention of Beaujolais wines in English followed soon after when <a href="/wiki/Cyrus_Redding" title="Cyrus Redding">Cyrus Redding</a> described the wines of Moulin-à-Vent and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint-Amour_wine" title="Saint-Amour wine">Saint-Amour</a> as being low priced and best consumed young.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_pg_72-74_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_pg_72-74-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1980s, Beaujolais hit a peak of popularity in the world's wine market with its <a href="/wiki/Beaujolais_nouveau" title="Beaujolais nouveau">Beaujolais nouveau</a> wine. Spurred on by the creative marketing from <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/N%C3%A9gociant" title="Négociant">négociants</a></i> like <a href="/wiki/Georges_Duboeuf" title="Georges Duboeuf">Georges Duboeuf</a>, demand outpaced supply for the easy drinking, fruity wines. As more Beaujolais producers tried to capitalize on the "Nouveau craze", production of regular Beaujolais dropped and an eventual backlash occurred in the late 1990s and early 21st century. By this point, the whole of Beaujolais wine had developed a negative reputation among consumers who associated Gamay based wines with the slightly sweet, simple light bodied wines that characterized Beaujolais Nouveau. Producers were left with a <a href="/wiki/Wine_lake" title="Wine lake">wine lake</a> surplus that French authorities compelled them to reduce through mandatory <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distillation</a>. In response, there has been renewed emphasis on the production of more complex wines that are aged longer in <a href="/wiki/Oak_(wine)" title="Oak (wine)">oak barrels</a> prior to release. Recent years have seen a rise in the number of <a href="/wiki/Terroir" title="Terroir">terroir</a> driven estate-bottled wines made from single vineyards or in one of the <a href="/wiki/Cru_(wine)" title="Cru (wine)">Cru</a> Beaujolais communes, where the name of the commune is allowed to be displayed on the label.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_pg_72-74_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_pg_72-74-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gamay_grape">Gamay grape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beaujolais&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Gamay grape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:16th-century_unknown_painters_-_Philip_the_Bold_-_WGA23677.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="615" height="286" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/16th-century_unknown_painters_-_Philip_the_Bold_-_WGA23677.jpg/220px-16th-century_unknown_painters_-_Philip_the_Bold_-_WGA23677.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/16th-century_unknown_painters_-_Philip_the_Bold_-_WGA23677.jpg/330px-16th-century_unknown_painters_-_Philip_the_Bold_-_WGA23677.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/16th-century_unknown_painters_-_Philip_the_Bold_-_WGA23677.jpg/440px-16th-century_unknown_painters_-_Philip_the_Bold_-_WGA23677.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:16th-century_unknown_painters_-_Philip_the_Bold_-_WGA23677.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>When Philippe the Bold outlawed the cultivation of Gamay in Burgundy, it pushed the grape south to the Beaujolais region.</div></div></div>
<p>Gamay noir is now known to be a cross of Pinot noir and the ancient white variety Gouais, the latter a Central European variety that was probably introduced to northeastern France by the Romans. The grape brought relief to the village growers following the decline of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a>. In contrast to the <a href="/wiki/Pinot_noir" title="Pinot noir">Pinot noir</a> variety, Gamay <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ripened_(wine)" title="Ripened (wine)">ripened</a> two weeks earlier and was less difficult to cultivate. It also produced a strong, fruitier wine in a much larger abundance. In July 1395, the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Burgundy" title="Duke of Burgundy">Duke of Burgundy</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philippe_the_Bold" title="Philippe the Bold">Philippe the Bold</a> outlawed the cultivation of Gamay as being "a very bad and disloyal plant", due in part to the variety occupying land that could be used for the more "elegant" Pinot noir. Sixty years later, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philip_III,_Duke_of_Burgundy" title="Philip III, Duke of Burgundy">Philippe the Good</a> issued another edict against Gamay, in which he stated the reason for the ban was that "The Dukes of Burgundy are known as the lords of the best wines in Christendom. We will maintain our reputation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnson_pg_133-134_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Johnson_pg_133-134-2">[2]</a></sup> The edicts had the effect of pushing Gamay plantings southward, out of the main region of Burgundy and into the <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a> based soils of Beaujolais where the grape thrived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_pg_72-74_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_pg_72-74-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scandals">Scandals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beaujolais&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Scandals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following the 2001 vintage, over 1.1 million cases of Beaujolais wine (most of it Beaujolais Nouveau) had to be destroyed or distilled due to lackluster sale as part of a consumer backlash against the popularity of Beaujolais Nouveau. French wine critic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Mauss" title="François Mauss">François Mauss</a> claimed, in an interview given to a local newspaper <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lyon_Mag&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lyon Mag (page does not exist)">Lyon Mag</a></i>, that the reason for the backlash was the poor quality of Beaujolais Nouveau that had flooded the market in recent decades. He claimed that Beaujolais producers had long ignored the warning signs that such a backlash was coming and continued to produce what Mauss termed <i>vin de merde</i> (<a href="/wiki/Shit" title="Shit">shit</a> wine).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deca605_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-deca605-3">[3]</a></sup> This triggered an outcry among Beaujolais producers followed by an association of 56 cooperative producers filing a lawsuit against the <i>Lyon Mag</i> for publishing Mauss' comments.
</p><p>Rather than sue for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Libel" title="Libel">libel</a>, the producers sued under an obscure French law that punishes the denigration of French products. In January 2003, the court in Villefranche-sur-Saône found in favor of the Beaujolais producers and awarded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USD" title="USD">USD</a>$350,000 which would put the small, employee owned publication out of business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sotheby_pg_165-168_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sotheby_pg_165-168-4">[4]</a></sup> The bad publicity garnered from the "Shit wine case" was extensive, with several publications such as <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Monde" title="Le Monde">Le Monde</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> and the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Herald_Tribune" title="International Herald Tribune">Herald Tribune</a></i> running critical or satirical articles on the court's decision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deca605_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-deca605-3">[3]</a></sup> In 2005, the highest court of appeal reversed and found that there was no cause of action against the publication, and the Beaujolais winemakers were ordered to pay <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">€</a>2,000 (US$2,442) in court costs to <i>Lyon Mag</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deca605_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-deca605-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2005, the Vins Georges Duboeuf company was charged with mixing low-grade wine with better vintages after a patchy 2004 harvest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-samuel2006_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-samuel2006-5">[5]</a></sup> Georges Duboeuf denied wrongdoing, blaming human error and pointing out that none of the affected wine was released to consumers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-samuel2006_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-samuel2006-5">[5]</a></sup> The production manager directly responsible admitted his actions and resigned, and a court found that both "fraud and attempted fraud concerning the origin and quality of wines" had been committed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-samuel2006_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-samuel2006-5">[5]</a></sup> Fewer than 200,000 liters of the company's annual 270 million liter production were implicated, but L'Affaire Duboeuf, as it was called, was considered a serious scandal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>In December 2007, five people were arrested after reportedly selling nearly 600 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tonnes" title="Tonnes">tonnes</a> of sugar to growers in Beaujolais. Up to 100 growers were accused of using the sugar for illegal <a href="/wiki/Chaptalization" title="Chaptalization">chaptalization</a> and also of exceeding volume quotas between 2004 and 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_and_geography">Climate and geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beaujolais&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Climate and geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Regniechurch1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="278" data-file-width="640" height="96" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/69/Regniechurch1.jpg/220px-Regniechurch1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/69/Regniechurch1.jpg/330px-Regniechurch1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/69/Regniechurch1.jpg/440px-Regniechurch1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Regniechurch1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cru Beaujolais region of Régnié</div></div></div>
<p>Beaujolais is a large wine producing region, larger than any single district of Burgundy. There are over 18,000 <a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">hectares</a> (44,000 <a href="/wiki/Acre" title="Acre">acres</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> of vines planted in a 34 miles (55 km) stretch of land that is between 7 and 9 miles (14 km) wide (11 to 14 km).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dummies_pg_112-120_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dummies_pg_112-120-9">[9]</a></sup> The historical capital of the province is Beaujeu (<i>Bôjor</i> /<i>Biôjœr</i> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arpitan" title="Arpitan">Arpitan</a>) and the economic capital of the area is <a href="/wiki/Villefranche-sur-Sa%C3%B4ne" title="Villefranche-sur-Saône">Villefranche-sur-Saône</a> (<i>Velafranche</i>). Many of Beaujolais vineyards are found in the hillside on the outskirt of Lyons in the eastern portion of the region along the Saône valley. The <a href="/wiki/Massif_Central" title="Massif Central">Massif Central</a> is located to the west and has a tempering influence on Beaujolais' climate. The region is located south of the Burgundy wine region <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A2connais" title="Mâconnais">Mâconnais</a> with nearly 100 <i><a href="/wiki/Communes_of_France" title="Communes of France">communes</a></i> in the northern region of Beaujolais overlapping between the AOC boundaries Beaujolais and the Maconnais region of <a href="/wiki/Saint-V%C3%A9ran_AOC" title="Saint-Véran AOC">Saint-Véran</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_pg_72-74_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_pg_72-74-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The climate of Beaujolais is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-continental_(wine)" title="Semi-continental (wine)">semi-continental</a> with some <a href="/wiki/Temperate_climate" title="Temperate climate">temperate influences</a>. The proximity of the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a> does impart some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean_influence_(wine)" title="Mediterranean influence (wine)">Mediterranean influence</a> on the climate. The region is overall, warmer than Burgundy with vines that consistently fully ripen grapes. By the time that the Beaujolais Nouveau is released in late November, the foothills in the western regions will have normally seen snow. A common viticultural hazard is spring time <a href="/wiki/Frost" title="Frost">frost</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_pg_72-74_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_pg_72-74-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_vineyard_soils" title="List of vineyard soils">soils</a> of Beaujolais divide the region into a northern and southern half, with the town of Villefranche serving as a near dividing point. The northern half of Beaujolais, where most of the Cru Beaujolais communes are located, includes rolling hills of <a href="/wiki/Schist" title="Schist">schist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a> based soils with some <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a>. On hillsides, most of the granite and schist is found in the upper slopes with the lower slopes having more stone and <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> composition. The southern half of the region, also known as the <i>Bas Beaujolais</i>, has flatter terrain with richer, <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> and clay based soils with some limestone patches. The Gamay grape fares differently in both regions-producing more structured, complex wines in the north and more lighter, fruity wines in the south. The angle of the hillside vineyards in the north exposes the grapes to more sunshine which leads to <a href="/wiki/Harvest_(wine)" title="Harvest (wine)">harvest</a> at an earlier time than the vineyards in the south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_pg_72-74_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_pg_72-74-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Appellations">Appellations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beaujolais&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Appellations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The new rules for Beaujolais appellations were issued by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/INAO" title="INAO">INAO</a> in 2011<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDC_BEAUJOLAIS_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDC_BEAUJOLAIS-10">[10]</a></sup>
There are twelve main appellations of Beaujolais wines covering the production of more than 96 villages in the Beaujolais region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacNeil_pg_220-225_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeil_pg_220-225-11">[11]</a></sup> They were originally established in 1936, with additional crus being promoted in 1938 and 1946, plus <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=R%C3%A9gni%C3%A9_(Beaujolais)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Régnié (Beaujolais) (page does not exist)">Régnié</a> in 1988. About half of all Beaujolais wine is sold under the basic Beaujolais AOC designation. The majority of this wine is produced in the southern <i>Bas Beaujolais</i> region located around the town of <a href="/wiki/Belleville,_Rh%C3%B4ne" title="Belleville, Rhône">Belleville</a>. The minimum natural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcohol_level" title="Alcohol level">alcohol level</a> for the grapes is 10%,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDC_BEAUJOLAIS_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-CDC_BEAUJOLAIS-10">[10]</a></sup> and the maximum <a href="/wiki/Yield_(wine)" title="Yield (wine)">yield</a> is 60 hl/ha (65 hl/ha for a <a href="/wiki/Bumper_crop" title="Bumper crop">bumper crop</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDC_BEAUJOLAIS_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-CDC_BEAUJOLAIS-10">[10]</a></sup> The wine may be labeled as <i>Beaujolais Supérieur</i> in case the minimum natural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcohol_level" title="Alcohol level">alcohol level</a> for the grapes is 10,5%,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDC_BEAUJOLAIS_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-CDC_BEAUJOLAIS-10">[10]</a></sup> and the maximum <a href="/wiki/Yield_(wine)" title="Yield (wine)">yield</a> is 58 hl/ha (63 hl/ha for a <a href="/wiki/Bumper_crop" title="Bumper crop">bumper crop</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDC_BEAUJOLAIS_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-CDC_BEAUJOLAIS-10">[10]</a></sup> Exactly the same limits are effective for <i>Beaujolais-Villages</i>. Maximum <a href="/wiki/Chaptalization" title="Chaptalization">chaptalization</a> levels are established at 3 g/l (<a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> + <a href="/wiki/Fructose" title="Fructose">fructose</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDC_BEAUJOLAIS_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-CDC_BEAUJOLAIS-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b><span id="Beaujolais_AOC">Beaujolais AOC</span></b>  is the most extended appellation allowed to be used in any of the 96 villages, but essentially covering 60 villages, and refers to all basic Beaujolais wines. A large portion of the wine produced under this appellation is sold as Beaujolais Nouveau. Annually, this appellation averages around 75 million bottles a year in production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dummies_pg_112-120_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dummies_pg_112-120-9">[9]</a></sup> Maximum level of <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> in the Nouveau is limited at 100 mg/l.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDC_BEAUJOLAIS_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-CDC_BEAUJOLAIS-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><span id="[[Beaujolais-Villages_AOC]]"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beaujolais-Villages_AOC" title="Beaujolais-Villages AOC">Beaujolais-Villages AOC</a></span></b>, the intermediate category in terms of classification, covers 39 communes/villages in the Haut Beaujolais, the northern part of the region accounting for a quarter of production. Some is sold as Beaujolais-Villages Nouveau, but it is not common. Most of the wines are released in the following March after the harvest. The terrain of this region is hillier with more schist and granite soil composition than what is found in the regions of the Beaujolais AOC and the wine has the potential to be of higher quality. If the grapes come from the area of a single vineyard or <i>commune</i>, producers can affix the name of their particular village to the Beaujolais-Villages designation. Since most of the villages of Beaujolais, outside of those classified as Cru Beaujolais, villages have little international name recognitions most producers choose to maintain the Beaujolais-Villages designation. The maximum permitted yields for this AOC is 50 hl/ha.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_pg_72-74_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_pg_72-74-1">[1]</a></sup> These wines are meant to be consumed young, within two years of their harvest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dummies_pg_112-120_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dummies_pg_112-120-9">[9]</a></sup> Several of the communes in the Beaujolais-Villages AOC also qualify to produce their wines under the Mâconnais and Saint-Véran AOCs. The Beaujolais producers that produce a red wine under the Beaujolais-Villages appellation will often produce their white wine under the more internationally recognized names of Mâcon-Villages or Saint-Véran.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sotheby_pg_165-168_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sotheby_pg_165-168-4">[4]</a></sup></li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Louis_Jadot_Cru_Beaujolais_in_glass.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1080" data-file-width="720" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Louis_Jadot_Cru_Beaujolais_in_glass.jpg/220px-Louis_Jadot_Cru_Beaujolais_in_glass.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Louis_Jadot_Cru_Beaujolais_in_glass.jpg/330px-Louis_Jadot_Cru_Beaujolais_in_glass.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Louis_Jadot_Cru_Beaujolais_in_glass.jpg/440px-Louis_Jadot_Cru_Beaujolais_in_glass.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Louis_Jadot_Cru_Beaujolais_in_glass.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cru Beaujolais from Brouilly.</div></div></div>
<ul><li><b>Cru Beaujolais</b>, the highest category of classification in Beaujolais, account for the production within ten villages/areas in the foothills of the Beaujolais mountains. Unlike Burgundy and <a href="/wiki/Alsace_wine" title="Alsace wine">Alsace</a>, the phrase <i>cru</i> in Beaujolais refers to an entire wine producing area rather than an individual vineyard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacNeil_pg_220-225_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeil_pg_220-225-11">[11]</a></sup> Seven of the Crus relate to actual villages while Brouilly and Côte de Brouilly refer to the vineyards areas around Mont Brouilly and Moulin-à-Vent is named for a local <a href="/wiki/Windmill" title="Windmill">windmill</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dummies_pg_112-120_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Dummies_pg_112-120-9">[9]</a></sup> These wines do not usually show the word "Beaujolais" on the label, in an attempt to separate themselves from mass-produced Nouveau; in fact vineyards in the cru villages are not allowed to produce Nouveau. The maximum yields for Cru Beaujolais wine is 48 hl/ha. Their wines can be more full-bodied, darker in color, and significantly longer-lived. From north to south the Beaujolais crus are- Saint-Amour, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juli%C3%A9nas_AOC" title="Juliénas AOC">Juliénas</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A9nas_AOC" title="Chénas AOC">Chénas</a>, Moulin-à-Vent, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fleurie_AOC" title="Fleurie AOC">Fleurie</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chiroubles_AOC" title="Chiroubles AOC">Chiroubles</a>, Morgon, Régnié, Brouilly and Côte de Brouilly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_pg_72-74_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_pg_72-74-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Beaujolais Blanc</b> &amp; <b>Beaujolais Rosé</b> - A small amount of white wine made from <a href="/wiki/Chardonnay" title="Chardonnay">Chardonnay</a> is grown in the region and used to produce Beaujolais Blanc or Beaujolais-Villages Blanc. The vineyards to produces these wines are normally found in the <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> soils of the far northern extremities of the region. Part of the reason for the small production of these wines is that many of the vineyards overlap into the <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A2connais" title="Mâconnais">Mâconnais</a> regions and producers will usually choose to label their wines under the more marketable and well known Mâcon Blanc designation. There is also regulations in several Beaujolais <i>communes</i> restricting growers to dedicating no more than 10% of their vineyard space to white wine <a href="/wiki/List_of_grape_varieties" title="List of grape varieties">grape varieties</a>. Beaujolais Rosé made from Gamay is permitted in the Beaujolais AOC but is rarely produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_pg_72-74_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_pg_72-74-1">[1]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beaujolais_Crus">Beaujolais Crus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beaujolais&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Beaujolais Crus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cote_de_Brouilly_bottle_of_Beaujolais_wine.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3376" data-file-width="1376" height="368" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Cote_de_Brouilly_bottle_of_Beaujolais_wine.jpg/150px-Cote_de_Brouilly_bottle_of_Beaujolais_wine.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Cote_de_Brouilly_bottle_of_Beaujolais_wine.jpg/225px-Cote_de_Brouilly_bottle_of_Beaujolais_wine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Cote_de_Brouilly_bottle_of_Beaujolais_wine.jpg/300px-Cote_de_Brouilly_bottle_of_Beaujolais_wine.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cote_de_Brouilly_bottle_of_Beaujolais_wine.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bottle of Côte de Brouilly wine.</div></div></div>
<p>The ten Beaujolais Crus differ in character. The following three crus produce the lightest bodied Cru Beaujolais and are typically meant to be consumed within three years of the vintage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dummies_pg_112-120_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-Dummies_pg_112-120-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Brouilly</b> - The largest Cru in Beaujolais, situated around Mont Brouilly and contains within its boundaries the sub-district of Côte de Brouilly. The wines are noted for their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aromas_(wine)" title="Aromas (wine)">aromas</a> of <a href="/wiki/Blueberry" title="Blueberry">blueberries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cherry" title="Cherry">cherries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raspberry" title="Raspberry">raspberries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ribes" title="Ribes">currants</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacNeil_pg_220-225_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeil_pg_220-225-11">[11]</a></sup> Along with Côte de Brouilly, this is the only Cru Beaujolais region that permits grapes other than Gamay to be produced in the area with vineyards growing Chardonnay, Aligote and <a href="/wiki/Melon_de_Bourgogne" title="Melon de Bourgogne">Melon de Bourgogne</a> as well. The Brouilly cru also contains the famous <i>Pisse Vieille</i> vineyard (roughly translated as "<a href="/wiki/Urination" title="Urination">piss</a> old woman!") which received its name from a local legend of a devout <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> woman who misheard the local priest's <a href="/wiki/Absolution" title="Absolution">absolution</a> to <i>"Allez! Et ne péchez plus."</i> (Go! And sin no more.) as <i>"Allez! Et ne pissez plus."</i> (Go! And piss no more). The vineyard name is the admonishment that her husband gave to her upon learning of the priest's words.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sotheby_pg_165-168_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sotheby_pg_165-168-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Régnié</b> - The most recently recognized Cru, graduating from a Beaujolais-Villages area to Cru Beaujolais in 1988. One of the more fuller bodied crus in this category. It is noted for its redcurrant and raspberry flavors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacNeil_pg_220-225_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeil_pg_220-225-11">[11]</a></sup> Local lore in the region states that this Cru was the site of the first vineyards planted in Beaujolais by the Romans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sotheby_pg_165-168_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Sotheby_pg_165-168-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Chiroubles</b> - This cru has vineyards at some of the highest altitudes among the Cru Beaujolais. Chiroubles cru are noted for their delicate perfume that often includes aromas of <a href="/wiki/Viola_(plant)" title="Viola (plant)">violets</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacNeil_pg_220-225_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeil_pg_220-225-11">[11]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The next three crus produce more medium bodied Cru Beaujolais that <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Wine" title="Master of Wine">Master of Wine</a> <a href="/wiki/Mary_Ewing-Mulligan" title="Mary Ewing-Mulligan">Mary Ewing-Mulligan</a> recommend needs at least a year aging in the bottle and to be consumed within four years of the vintage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dummies_pg_112-120_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-Dummies_pg_112-120-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Côte de Brouilly</b> - Located on the higher slopes of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extinct_volcano" title="Extinct volcano">extinct volcano</a> Mont Brouilly within the Brouilly Cru Beaujolais. The wines from this region are more deeply concentrated with less earthiness than Brouilly wine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacNeil_pg_220-225_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeil_pg_220-225-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Fleurie</b> - One of the most widely exported Cru Beaujolais into the United States. These wines often have a velvet texture with fruity and floral bouquet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacNeil_pg_220-225_11-6"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeil_pg_220-225-11">[11]</a></sup> In ideal vintages, a <i>vin de garde</i> (wine for aging) is produced that is meant to age at least four years before consuming and can last up to 16 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sotheby_pg_165-168_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Sotheby_pg_165-168-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Saint-Amour</b> - Local lore suggest that this region was named after a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_soldier" title="Roman soldier">Roman soldier</a> (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=St._Amateur&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="St. Amateur (page does not exist)">St. Amateur</a>) who converted to <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> after escaping death and established a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mission_(Christian)" title="Mission (Christian)">mission</a> near the area. The wines from Saint-Amour are noted for their spicy flavors with aromas of <a href="/wiki/Peach" title="Peach">peaches</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacNeil_pg_220-225_11-7"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeil_pg_220-225-11">[11]</a></sup> The <i>vin de garde</i> wines require at least four year aging and can last up to twelve years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sotheby_pg_165-168_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-Sotheby_pg_165-168-4">[4]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The last four crus produce the fullest bodied examples of Cru Beaujolais that need the most time aging in the bottle and are usually meant to be consumed between four and ten years after harvest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dummies_pg_112-120_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-Dummies_pg_112-120-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Chénas</b> - Once contained many of the vineyards that are now sold under the Moulin-à-Vent designation. It is now the smallest Cru Beaujolais with wines that are noted for their aroma of wild <a href="/wiki/Rose" title="Rose">roses</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacNeil_pg_220-225_11-8"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeil_pg_220-225-11">[11]</a></sup> In ideal vintages, a <i>vin de garde</i> is produced that is meant to age at least five years before consuming and last up to 15. The area named is derived from the forest of French <a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">oak</a> trees <i>(chêne)</i> that used to dot the hillside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sotheby_pg_165-168_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-Sotheby_pg_165-168-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Juliénas</b>-This cru is based around the village named after <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a>. The wines made from this area are noted for their richness and spice with aromas reminiscent of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peonies" title="Peonies">peonies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacNeil_pg_220-225_11-9"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeil_pg_220-225-11">[11]</a></sup> In contrast to the claims of Régnié, Juliénas growers believe that this area was the site of the first vineyards planted in Beaujolais by the Romans during this conquest of <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sotheby_pg_165-168_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-Sotheby_pg_165-168-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Morgon</b> - Produces earthy wines that can take on a Burgundian character of silky texture after five years aging. These wines are generally the deepest color and most rich Cru Beaujolais with aromas of <a href="/wiki/Apricot" title="Apricot">apricots</a> and peaches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacNeil_pg_220-225_11-10"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeil_pg_220-225-11">[11]</a></sup> Within this Cru there is a particular hillside, known as Cote du Py, in the center of Morgon that produces the most powerful examples of Morgon wines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sotheby_pg_165-168_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-Sotheby_pg_165-168-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Moulin-%C3%A0-Vent_AOC" title="Moulin-à-Vent AOC">Moulin-à-Vent</a></b> - Wines are very similar to the nearby Chénas Cru Beaujolais. This region produces some of the longest-lasting examples of Beaujolais wine, with some wines lasting up to ten years. Some producers will age their Moulin-à-Vent in <a href="/wiki/Oak_(wine)" title="Oak (wine)">oak</a> which gives these wines more tannin and structure than other Beaujolais wines. The phrase <i>fûts de chêne</i> (oak casks) will sometimes appear on the <a href="/wiki/Wine_label" title="Wine label">wine label</a> of these oak aged wines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dummies_pg_112-120_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-Dummies_pg_112-120-9">[9]</a></sup> The region is noted for the high level of <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a> that is in the soil, which can be toxic to grape vines in high levels. The level of toxicity in Moulin-à-Vent does not kill the vine but is enough to cause <a href="/wiki/Chlorosis" title="Chlorosis">chlorosis</a> and alter the vine's <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a> to reduce yields severely. The resulting wine from Moulin-à-Vent are the most full bodied and powerful examples in Beaujolais. The <i>vin de garde</i> styles require at least 6 years <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aging_(wine)" title="Aging (wine)">aging</a> and can last up to 20 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sotheby_pg_165-168_4-9"><a href="#cite_note-Sotheby_pg_165-168-4">[4]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beaujolais_Nouveau">Beaujolais Nouveau</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beaujolais&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Beaujolais Nouveau">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beaujolais_Nouveau" title="Beaujolais Nouveau">Beaujolais Nouveau</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Beaujolais_nouveau_wine.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="1936" height="295" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Beaujolais_nouveau_wine.jpg/220px-Beaujolais_nouveau_wine.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Beaujolais_nouveau_wine.jpg/330px-Beaujolais_nouveau_wine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Beaujolais_nouveau_wine.jpg/440px-Beaujolais_nouveau_wine.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Beaujolais_nouveau_wine.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A glass and bottle of Beaujolais Nouveau from the 2013 vintage.</div></div></div>
<p>The early history of Beaujolais Nouveau can trace its roots to 19th century when the first wines of the vintage were sent down the Saône to the early bistros of Lyon. Upon their arrival signs would be put out proclaiming <i>"Le Beaujolais Est Arrivé!"</i> and its consumption was seen as a celebration of another successful harvest. In the 1960s, this style of simple Beaujolais became increasingly popular worldwide with more than half a million cases of being sold. In 1985 the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Institut_National_des_Appellations_d%27Origine" title="Institut National des Appellations d'Origine">Institut National des Appellations d'Origine</a></i> (INAO) established the third Thursday of November to allow for a uniform release date for the wine. Wines are typically shipped a few days earlier to locations around the world where they must be held in a <a href="/wiki/Surety_bond" title="Surety bond">bonded</a> warehouse till 12:01am when the wines can be first opened and consumed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacNeil_pg_220-225_11-11"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeil_pg_220-225-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Today, about a third of the region's production is sold as Beaujolais Nouveau, a marketing name created by George Duboeuf for the local <i>vin de l'année</i>. It is the lightest, fruitiest style of Beaujolais and meant for simple <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Quaffing&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Quaffing (page does not exist)">quaffing</a>. Any Beaujolais or Beaujolais-Villages AOC vineyard can produce Beaujolais Nouveau. The grapes are harvested between late August and early September. It is fermented for just a few days and released to the public on the third Thursday of November - "Beaujolais Nouveau Day". It is the first French wine to be released for each vintage year. In 1992, at its peak, more than half of all Beaujolais wine was sold as "Beaujolais Nouveau".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_pg_72-74_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_pg_72-74-1">[1]</a></sup> The wines are meant to be drunk as young as possible, when they are at their freshest and fruitiest. They can last up to one or two years but will have lost most of their characteristic flavors by that point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dummies_pg_112-120_9-8"><a href="#cite_note-Dummies_pg_112-120-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Viticulture_and_grape_varieties">Viticulture and grape varieties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beaujolais&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Viticulture and grape varieties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Beaujolais region has one of the highest vine density ratios of any major worldwide wine region, with anywhere from 9000 to 13,000 vines per hectare. Most vines are trained in the traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gobelets" title="Gobelets">gobelet</a> style, where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spur_(biology)" title="Spur (biology)">spurs</a> of the vines are pushed upwards and arranged in a circle, resembling a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chalice_(cup)" title="Chalice (cup)">chalice</a>. This method has its roots in the Roman style of <a href="/wiki/Vine_training" title="Vine training">vine training</a> and has only recently begun to fall out of favor for the <a href="/wiki/Jules_Guyot" title="Jules Guyot">guyot</a> method which involves taking a single or double spur and training it out horizontally. Harvest usually occurs in late September and is almost universally done by hand rather than with the use of mechanical harvesters. This is because the Beaujolais wine-making style of carbonic maceration utilizes whole bunches of grape clusters that normally get broken and separated by a mechanical harvester.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_pg_72-74_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_pg_72-74-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Gamay grape, more accurately known as Gamay noir à Jus blanc to distinguish it from the Gamay <a href="/wiki/Teinturier" title="Teinturier">teinturier</a> grapes with red juice and different from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napa_Gamay" title="Napa Gamay">Napa Gamay</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Gamay_Beaujolais" title="Gamay Beaujolais">Gamay Beaujolais</a> grapes of <a href="/wiki/California_wine" title="California wine">California</a>, is the most widely planted grape in Beaujolais, accounting for nearly 98% of all plantings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dummies_pg_112-120_9-9"><a href="#cite_note-Dummies_pg_112-120-9">[9]</a></sup> The remaining plantings are mostly Chardonnay. Aligote vines that were planted prior to 2004 are permitted in wine production, but the entire grape variety is being phased out of the region by 2024. According to AOC regulation, up to 15% of white wine grape varieties can be included in all Beaujolais red wines from the basic Beaujolais AOC to the Cru Beaujolais wines, but in practice the wines are almost always 100% Gamay. Pinot noir, which has very small plantings, is also permitted, but that grape is being phased out by 2015 as Beaujolais winemakers continue to focus their winemaking identity on the Gamay grape.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_pg_72-74_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_pg_72-74-1">[1]</a></sup> The characteristics that the Gamay grapes adds to Beaujolais are a deep bluish-red color, low <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acidity" title="Acidity">acidity</a>, moderate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grape_tannins" title="Grape tannins">tannins</a>, and light to medium <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body_(wine)" title="Body (wine)">body</a>. The aroma associated with the grape itself is typically red berries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dummies_pg_112-120_9-10"><a href="#cite_note-Dummies_pg_112-120-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since the 1960s, more focus has been placed on the choice of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rootstocks" title="Rootstocks">rootstocks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cloning#Horticultural" title="Cloning">clonal selection</a>, with six approved clones of Gamay for the wine region. In recent years the rootstock Vialla has gained popularity due to its propensity to produce well in granite soils. The SO4 and 3309 rootstocks also account for significant plantings. Clonal selection of the Gamay grape has shifted towards an emphasis on smaller, thicker-skinned berries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_pg_72-74_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_pg_72-74-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Winemaking_and_style">Winemaking and style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beaujolais&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Winemaking and style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_maceration" title="Carbonic maceration">Carbonic maceration</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Beaujolais_glass.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="640" data-file-width="480" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Beaujolais_glass.jpg/150px-Beaujolais_glass.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Beaujolais_glass.jpg/225px-Beaujolais_glass.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Beaujolais_glass.jpg/300px-Beaujolais_glass.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Beaujolais_glass.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Beaujolais_nouveau" title="Beaujolais nouveau">Beaujolais nouveau</a></div></div></div>
<p>Beaujolais wines are produced by the winemaking technique of semi-carbonic maceration. Whole grape clusters are put in cement or stainless steel tanks with capacities between 4,000–30,000 litres (1,100–7,900 US gal). The bottom third of the grapes gets crushed under the weight of gravity and resulting must begins normal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yeast_(wine)" title="Yeast (wine)">yeast</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(wine)" title="Fermentation (wine)">fermentation</a> with ambient yeasts found naturally on the skins of the grapes. <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">Carbon dioxide</a> is released as a byproduct of this fermentation and begins to saturate the individual intact grape berries that remain in the barrel. The carbon dioxide seeps into the skin of the grape and begin to stimulate fermentation at an <a href="/wiki/Intracellular" title="Intracellular">intracellular</a> level. This is caused, in part, by the absence of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> in the winemaking environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dummies_pg_112-120_9-11"><a href="#cite_note-Dummies_pg_112-120-9">[9]</a></sup> This results in a fruity wine without much tannin. In the case of Beaujolais nouveau, this process is completed in as little as four days, with the other AOCs being allowed longer time to ferment. As the grapes ferment longer, they develop more tannins and a fuller body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_pg_72-74_1-14"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_pg_72-74-1">[1]</a></sup> Maximum length of the <i>cuvaison</i> for Nouveau wines is limited to 10 days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDC_BEAUJOLAIS_10-7"><a href="#cite_note-CDC_BEAUJOLAIS-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>After fermentation, the must is normally high in <a href="/wiki/Malic_acid" title="Malic acid">malic acid</a> and producers will put the wine through <a href="/wiki/Malolactic_fermentation" title="Malolactic fermentation">malolactic fermentation</a> to soften the wine. The process of chaptalization, adding sugar to the grape must to boost alcohol levels, has been a controversial issue for Beaujolais winemakers. Historically, Beaujolais producers would pick grapes at ripeness that were at minimum potential alcohol levels of 10-10.5% and then add sugar in order to artificially boost the alcohol levels to the near the maximum of 13-13.5%. This created wines lacking structure and balance to go with the high alcohol body and <a href="/wiki/Mouthfeel" title="Mouthfeel">mouthfeel</a>. The recent trend towards higher quality wine production has limited the use of chaptalization in the premium levels of Beaujolais wine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_pg_72-74_1-15"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_pg_72-74-1">[1]</a></sup> Filtering the wine in order to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stabilize_(wine)" title="Stabilize (wine)">stabilize</a> it is practiced to varying degrees by Beaujolais winemakers. Some producers who make Beaujolais on a large commercial scale will filter the wine aggressively to avoid any impurity or future chemical reactions. This can have the negative side effect of diminishing some of the wine's unique fruit character and leave a flavor that critics have described as <a href="/wiki/Jell-O" title="Jell-O">Jell-O</a>-like.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacNeil_pg_220-225_11-12"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeil_pg_220-225-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Basic Beaujolais is the classic <a href="/wiki/Bistro" title="Bistro">bistro</a> wine of Paris; a fruity, easy-drinking red traditionally served in 1 <a href="/wiki/Pint" title="Pint">pint</a> glass <a href="/wiki/Wine_bottle" title="Wine bottle">bottles</a> known as <i>pot</i>. This is epitomized in Beaujolais Nouveau, which is fermented for just a few days and can be dominated by <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">estery</a> flavors such as <a href="/wiki/Banana" title="Banana">bananas</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pear_drops" title="Pear drops">pear drops</a>. Basic Beaujolais and Beaujolais nouveau are meant to be drunk within a year of their harvest. Beaujolais-Villages are generally consumed within 2–3 years and Cru Beaujolais has the potential to age longer, some not even fully developing till at least 3 years after harvest. Premium examples from Chénas, Juliénas, Morgon and Moulin-à-Vent can spend up to 10 years continuing to develop in the bottle and in very good vintages can take on <a href="/wiki/Burgundy_wine" title="Burgundy wine">Burgundian</a> qualities of structure and complexity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_pg_72-74_1-16"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_pg_72-74-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wine_industry">Wine industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beaujolais&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Wine industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wine_yard_in_Beaujolais.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2322" data-file-width="4128" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Wine_yard_in_Beaujolais.jpg/220px-Wine_yard_in_Beaujolais.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Wine_yard_in_Beaujolais.jpg/330px-Wine_yard_in_Beaujolais.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Wine_yard_in_Beaujolais.jpg/440px-Wine_yard_in_Beaujolais.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wine_yard_in_Beaujolais.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Wine yard in Beaujolais</div></div></div>
<p>The Beaujolais wine industry is dominated by the more than 30 <i>négociants</i> who produce nearly 90% of the wine sold outside the Beaujolais region. Many of these <i>négociants</i> are based in Burgundy-such as <a href="/wiki/Maison_Louis_Jadot" title="Maison Louis Jadot">Maison Louis Jadot</a> ( through <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_des_jacques" title="Château des jacques">Château des Jacques</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Bouchard_P%C3%A8re_et_Fils" title="Bouchard Père et Fils">Bouchard Père et Fils</a>. One of the most well known Beaujolais producers is the <i>négociants</i> Georges Duboeuf. There are more than 4000 vineyard owners in Beaujolais and the fractional amount that is not sold to <i>négociants</i> are bottled by the nearly 20 village <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Co-operative" title="Co-operative">co-operatives</a> with a growing amount being estate bottled. Very little of the estate bottled Beaujolais wines are exported into the United States or <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> though a few exporters specialize in this small niche-the most notable being <a href="/wiki/Kermit_Lynch" title="Kermit Lynch">Kermit Lynch</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alain_Jugenet&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alain Jugenet (page does not exist)">Alain Jugenet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacNeil_pg_220-225_11-13"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeil_pg_220-225-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Serving_and_food_pairing">Serving and food pairing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beaujolais&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Serving and food pairing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Beaujolais_salad.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Beaujolais_salad.jpg/150px-Beaujolais_salad.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Beaujolais_salad.jpg/225px-Beaujolais_salad.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Beaujolais_salad.jpg/300px-Beaujolais_salad.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Beaujolais_salad.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Light bodied Beaujolais wine, such as Beaujolais-Villages pair well with lighter fare like salads.</div></div></div>
<p>Wine expert <a href="/wiki/Karen_MacNeil" title="Karen MacNeil">Karen MacNeil</a> has described Beaujolais as "the only white wine that happens to be red".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacNeil_pg_220-225_11-14"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeil_pg_220-225-11">[11]</a></sup> Similarly, Beaujolais is often treated like a white wine and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wine_serving_temperature" title="Wine serving temperature">served</a> slightly chilled to a lower temperature, the lighter the style. Beaujolais Nouveau, being the lightest style, is served at about 52 °F (11 °C). Beaujolais AOC and Beaujolais-Villages are generally served between 56–57 °F (13–14 °C). Cru Beaujolais, especially the fuller bodied examples, can be treated like red Burgundy wine and served at 60–62 °F (16–17 °C). The wines rarely need to be <a href="/wiki/Decanter" title="Decanter">decanted</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dummies_pg_112-120_9-12"><a href="#cite_note-Dummies_pg_112-120-9">[9]</a></sup> In Beaujolais, it is traditional to soak the bottles in buckets of ice water and bring them out to the center villages for picnics and games of <a href="/wiki/Boules" title="Boules">boules</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacNeil_pg_220-225_11-15"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeil_pg_220-225-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Beaujolais wine can <a href="/wiki/Wine_and_food_matching" title="Wine and food matching">be paired with</a> a variety of food according to the lightness and body of the wine. Beaujolais Nouveau is typically used as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ap%C3%A9ritif" title="Apéritif">apéritif</a> with basic Beaujolais and Beaujolais-Villages doing well with light fare, like picnics and salads. The lighter Cru Beaujolais pair well with poultry and the heavier Crus pairing better with red meats and hearty dishes like stews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dummies_pg_112-120_9-13"><a href="#cite_note-Dummies_pg_112-120-9">[9]</a></sup> In Norway, Beaujolais is a favorite with <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bacalhau" title="Bacalhau">bacalhau</a> dishes. According to Lyon chef <a href="/wiki/Paul_Bocuse" title="Paul Bocuse">Paul Bocuse</a>, Beaujolais wine is used to make a traditional regional dessert involving a glass of sliced peaches, topped with blackcurrants and drenched in chilled Beaujolais wine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacNeil_pg_220-225_11-16"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeil_pg_220-225-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
