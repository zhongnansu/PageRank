<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Water footprint</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:552px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:World_Water_Footprint.png"><img alt="World Water Footprint.png" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1205" data-file-width="1141" height="581" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/World_Water_Footprint.png/550px-World_Water_Footprint.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/World_Water_Footprint.png/825px-World_Water_Footprint.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/World_Water_Footprint.png/1100px-World_Water_Footprint.png 2x" width="550"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:World_Water_Footprint.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div>
<p>The <b>water footprint</b> shows the extent of water use in relation to <a href="/wiki/Consumption_(economics)" title="Consumption (economics)">consumption</a> by people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nations_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-nations-1">[1]</a></sup> The water footprint of an individual, community or business is defined as the total volume of <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">fresh water</a> used to produce the goods and services consumed by the individual or community or produced by the business. Water use is measured in water volume consumed (evaporated) and/or polluted per unit of time. A water footprint can be <a href="/wiki/Calculation" title="Calculation">calculated</a> for any well-defined group of <a href="/wiki/Consumer" title="Consumer">consumers</a> (e.g., an individual, family, village, city, province, state or nation) or <a href="/wiki/Production_(economics)" title="Production (economics)">producers</a> (e.g., a public organization, private enterprise or economic sector), for a single <a href="/wiki/Process" title="Process">process</a> (such as growing rice) or for any <a href="/wiki/Product_(business)" title="Product (business)">product</a> or <a href="/wiki/Service_(economics)" title="Service (economics)">service</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-waterfootprintorg_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-waterfootprintorg-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Traditionally, water use has been approached from the production side, by quantifying the following three columns of water use: water withdrawals in the <a href="/wiki/Home" title="Home">domestic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agricultural</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">industrial</a> sector. While this does provide valuable data, it is a limited way of looking at water use in a <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">globalised</a> world, in which products are not always consumed in their country of origin. International trade of agricultural and industrial products in effect creates a global flow of <a href="/wiki/Virtual_water" title="Virtual water">virtual water</a>, or <i>embodied water</i> (akin to the concept of <a href="/wiki/Embodied_energy" title="Embodied energy">embodied energy</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nations_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-nations-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2002, the water footprint concept was introduced in order to have a consumption-based <a href="/wiki/Economic_indicator" title="Economic indicator">indicator</a> of water use, that could provide useful information in addition to the traditional production-sector-based indicators of water use. It is analogous to the <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a> concept introduced in the 1990s. The water footprint is a geographically explicit indicator, not only showing volumes of water use and pollution, but also the locations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Thus, it gives a grasp on how economic choices and processes influence the availability of adequate water resources and other ecological realities across the globe (and vice versa).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_availability">Water availability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Water availability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Globally, about 4 percent of <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">precipitation</a> falling on land each year (about 117,000 km<sup>3</sup> (28,000 cu mi)),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> is used by rain-fed agriculture and about half is subject to evaporation and transpiration in <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a> and other natural or quasi-natural landscapes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>  The remainder,  which goes to <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> replenishment and <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">surface runoff</a>, is sometimes called “total actual renewable freshwater resources”.  Its magnitude was in 2012 estimated at 52,579 km<sup>3</sup> (12,614 cu mi)/year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frenken2012_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frenken2012-6">[6]</a></sup>  It represents water that can be used either in-stream or after withdrawal from surface and groundwater sources.  Of this remainder, about 3,918 km<sup>3</sup> (940 cu mi) were withdrawn in 2007, of which 2,722 km<sup>3</sup> (653 cu mi), or 69 percent, were used by agriculture, and 734 km<sup>3</sup> (176 cu mi), or 19 percent, by other industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  Most agricultural use of withdrawn water is for <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>, which uses about 5.1 percent of total actual renewable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a> resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frenken2012_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Frenken2012-6">[6]</a></sup>  World water use has been growing rapidly in the last hundred years (see <a class="external text" href="https://www.newscientist.com/data/images/archive/2670/26700101.jpg" rel="nofollow">graph</a> from <i><a href="/wiki/New_Scientist" title="New Scientist">New Scientist</a></i> article<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition_and_measures">Definition and measures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definition and measures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blue_water_footprint">Blue water footprint</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Blue water footprint">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>blue water footprint</i> is the volume of water that has been sourced from <a href="/wiki/Surface_water" title="Surface water">surface</a> or <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> resources (lakes, rivers, wetlands and aquifers) and has either evaporated (for example while <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigating</a> crops), incorporated into a product or taken from one body of water and returned to another, or returned at a different time. Irrigated agriculture, industry and domestic water use can each have a blue water footprint.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whatis_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-whatis-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_water_footprint">Green water footprint</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Green water footprint">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>green water footprint</i> is the amount of water from <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">precipitation</a> that, after having been stored in the root zone of the soil (green water), is either lost by <a href="/wiki/Evapotranspiration" title="Evapotranspiration">evapotranspiration</a> or incorporated by plants. It is particularly relevant for agricultural, <a href="/wiki/Horticulture" title="Horticulture">horticultural</a> and <a href="/wiki/Forestry" title="Forestry">forestry</a> products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whatis_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-whatis-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grey_water_footprint">Grey water footprint</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Grey water footprint">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>grey water footprint</i> is the volume of water that is required to dilute <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">pollutants</a> (industrial discharges, seepage from tailing ponds at mining operations, untreated municipal wastewater, or <a href="/wiki/Nonpoint_source_pollution" title="Nonpoint source pollution">nonpoint source pollution</a> such as agricultural runoff or <a href="/wiki/Urban_runoff" title="Urban runoff">urban runoff</a>) to such an extent that the quality of the water meets agreed water quality standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whatis_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-whatis-9">[9]</a></sup> It is calculated as:

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Calculation_for_different_actors">Calculation for different actors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Calculation for different actors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The water footprint of a process is expressed as <a href="/wiki/Volumetric_flow_rate" title="Volumetric flow rate">volumetric flow rate</a> of water. That of a product is the whole footprint (sum) of processes in its complete supply chain divided by the number of product units. For consumers, businesses and geographic area, water footprint is indicated as volume of water per time, in particular:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TheWaterFootprintAssessmentManual_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-TheWaterFootprintAssessmentManual-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>That of a consumer is the sum of footprint of all consumed products.</li>
<li>That of a community or a nation is the sum for all of its members resp. inhabitants.</li>
<li>That of a business is the footprint of all produced goods.</li>
<li>That of a geographically delineated area is the footprint of all processes undertaken in this area. The virtual water balance of an area is the net import of virtual water <i>V<sub>i, net</sub></i>, defined as the difference of the gross import <i>V<sub>i</sub></i> of virtual water from its gross export <i>V<sub>e</sub></i>. The water footprint of national consumption <i>WF<sub>area,nat</sub></i> results from this as the sum of the water footprint of national area and its virtual water balance.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The concept of a <i>water footprint</i> was coined in 2002, by Arjen Hoekstra, Professor in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_management" title="Water management">water management</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Twente" title="University of Twente">University of Twente</a>, Netherlands, and co-founder and scientific director of the Water Footprint Network, whilst working at the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO-IHE" title="UNESCO-IHE">UNESCO-IHE</a> Institute for Water Education, as a <a href="/wiki/System_of_measurement" title="System of measurement">metric</a> to measure the amount of water consumed and polluted to produce goods and services along their full supply chain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aims_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-aims-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>  Water footprint is one of a family of <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a> indicators, which also includes <a href="/wiki/Carbon_footprint" title="Carbon footprint">carbon footprint</a> and <a href="/wiki/Land_footprint" title="Land footprint">land footprint</a>. The water footprint concept is further related to the idea of <a href="/wiki/Virtual_water" title="Virtual water">virtual water</a> trade introduced in the early 1990's by Professor <a href="/wiki/John_Anthony_Allan" title="John Anthony Allan">John Allan</a> (2008 <a href="/wiki/Stockholm_Water_Prize" title="Stockholm Water Prize">Stockholm Water Prize Laureate</a>). The most elaborate publications on how to estimate water footprints are a 2004 report on the <i>Water footprint of nations</i> from <a href="/wiki/UNESCO-IHE" title="UNESCO-IHE">UNESCO-IHE</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> the 2008 book <i>Globalization of Water</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and the 2011 manual <i>The water footprint assessment manual: Setting the global standard</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>  Cooperation between global leading institutions in the field has led to the establishment of the Water Footprint Network in 2008.
</p>
<h3><span id="Water_Footprint_Network_.28WFN.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_Footprint_Network_(WFN)">Water Footprint Network (WFN)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Water Footprint Network (WFN)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><p>
The Water Footprint Network is an international learning community (<a href="/wiki/Nonprofit_organization" title="Nonprofit organization">non-profit foundation</a> under Dutch law) that serves as a platform for sharing knowledge, tools and innovations among governments, businesses and communities that are concerned about growing <a href="/wiki/Water_scarcity" title="Water scarcity">water scarcity</a> and increasing <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">water pollution</a> levels and their impacts on people and nature. The network consists of around 100 partners from all sectors – producers, investors, suppliers and regulators – as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organisation" title="Non-governmental organisation">non-governmental organisations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Academy" title="Academy">academia</a>. It describes its mission as follows: </p><blockquote><p> To provide <a href="/wiki/Evidence-based_practice" title="Evidence-based practice">science-based</a>, practical solutions and strategic insights that empower companies, governments, individuals and small-scale producers to transform the way we use and share fresh water within earth’s limits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aims_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-aims-11">[11]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_standard">International standard</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: International standard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In February 2011, the Water Footprint Network, in a global collaborative effort of environmental organizations, companies, research institutions and the UN, launched the <i>Global Water Footprint Standard</i>. In July 2014, the <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">International Organization for Standardization</a> issued <i>ISO 14046:2014, Environmental management—Water footprint—Principles, requirements and guidelines</i>, to provide practical guidance to practitioners from various backgrounds, such as large companies, public authorities, non-governmental organizations, academic and research groups as well as small and medium enterprises, for carrying out a water footprint assessment. The ISO standard is based on <a href="/wiki/Life-cycle_assessment" title="Life-cycle assessment">life-cycle assessment</a> (LCA) principles and can be applied for different sorts of assessment of products and companies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISO14046_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-ISO14046-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Life-cycle_assessment_of_water_use">Life-cycle assessment of water use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Life-cycle assessment of water use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Life-cycle_assessment" title="Life-cycle assessment">Life-cycle assessment</a> (LCA) is a systematic, phased approach to assessing the environmental aspects and potential impacts that are associated with a product, process or service.  “Life cycle” refers to the major activities connected with the product’s life-span, from its manufacture, use, and maintenance, to its final disposal, and also including the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Raw_material" title="Raw material">raw material</a> required to manufacture the product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>  Thus a method for assessing the environmental impacts of freshwater consumption was developed.  It specifically looks at the damage to three areas of protection: human health, ecosystem quality, and resources.  The consideration of water consumption is crucial where water-intensive products (for example agricultural goods) are concerned that need to therefore undergo a life-cycle assessment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>  In addition, regional assessments are equally as necessary as the impact of water use depends on its location.  In short, LCA is important as it identifies the impact of water use in certain products, consumers, companies, nations, etc. which can help reduce the amount of water used.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_footprint_of_products">Water footprint of products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Water footprint of products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_footprint_of_companies">Water footprint of companies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Water footprint of companies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The water footprint of a business, the 'corporate water footprint', is defined as the total volume of freshwater that is used directly or indirectly to run and support a business. It is the total volume of water use to be associated with the use of the business outputs. The water footprint of a business consists of water used for producing/manufacturing or for supporting activities and the indirect water use in the producer’s supply chain.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Carbon_Trust" title="The Carbon Trust">The Carbon Trust</a> argue that a more robust approach is for businesses to go beyond simple volumetric measurement to assess the full range of water impact from all sites.  Its work with leading global pharmaceutical company <a href="/wiki/GlaxoSmithKline" title="GlaxoSmithKline">GlaxoSmithKline</a> (GSK) analysed four key categories: water availability, water quality, health impacts, and licence to operate (including reputational and regulatory risks) in order to enable GSK to quantitatively measure, and credibly reduce, its year-on-year water impact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coca-Cola_Company" title="Coca-Cola Company">Coca-Cola Company</a> operates over a thousand manufacturing plants in about 200 countries. Making its drink uses a lot of water. Critics say its water footprint has been large. Coca-Cola has started to look at its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_sustainability" title="Water sustainability">water sustainability</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>  It has now set out goals to reduce its water footprint such as treating the water it uses so it goes back into the environment in a clean state. Another goal is to find sustainable sources for the raw materials it uses in its drinks, such as <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oranges" title="Oranges">oranges</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corn" title="Corn">corn</a>. By making its water footprint better, the company can reduce costs, improve the environment, and benefit the communities in which it operates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naumann_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-naumann-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_footprint_of_nations">Water footprint of nations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Water footprint of nations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Water_Footprint_per_capita.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2457" data-file-width="1500" height="655" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Water_Footprint_per_capita.jpg/400px-Water_Footprint_per_capita.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Water_Footprint_per_capita.jpg/600px-Water_Footprint_per_capita.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Water_Footprint_per_capita.jpg/800px-Water_Footprint_per_capita.jpg 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Water_Footprint_per_capita.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Global view of national per capita water footprints</div></div></div>
<p>The water footprint of a nation is the amount of water used to produce the goods and services consumed by the inhabitants of that nation. Analysis of the water footprint of nations illustrates the global dimension of water consumption and pollution, by showing that several countries rely heavily on foreign water resources and that (consumption patterns in) many countries significantly and in various ways impact how, and how much, water is being consumed and polluted elsewhere on Earth. International water dependencies are substantial and are likely to increase with continued global <a href="/wiki/Free_trade" title="Free trade">trade liberalisation</a>. The largest share (76%) of the virtual water flows between countries is related to international trade in crops and derived crop products. Trade in animal products and industrial products contributed 12% each to the global virtual water flows. The four major direct factors determining the water footprint of a country are: volume of consumption (related to the <a href="/wiki/Gross_national_income" title="Gross national income">gross national income</a>); consumption pattern (e.g. high versus low <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_meat_consumption" title="List of countries by meat consumption">meat consumption</a>); climate (growth conditions); and agricultural practice (water use efficiency).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nations_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-nations-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Production_or_consumption">Production or consumption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Production or consumption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The assessment of total water use in connection to consumption can be approached from both ends of the <a href="/wiki/Supply_chain" title="Supply chain">supply chain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The water footprint of <b>production</b> estimates how much water from local sources is used or polluted in order to provide the goods and services produced in that country. The water footprint of <b>consumption</b> of a country looks at the amount of water used or polluted (locally, or in the case of imported goods, in other countries) in connection with all the goods and services that are consumed by the inhabitants of that country. The water footprint of production and that of consumption, can also be estimated for any administrative unit such as a city, province, river basin or the entire world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nations_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-nations-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Absolute_or_per_capita">Absolute or per capita</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Absolute or per capita">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The absolute water footprint is the total sum of water footprints of all people. A country's <a href="/wiki/Per_capita" title="Per capita">per capita</a> water footprint (that nation's water footprint divided by its number of inhabitants) can be used to compare its water footprint with those of other nations.
</p><p>The global water footprint in the period 1996–2005 was 9.087 Gm3/yr (Billion Cubic Metres per year, or 9.087.000.000.000.000 liters/year), of which 74% was and green, 11% blue, 15% grey. This is an average amount per capita of 1.385 Gm3/yr., or 3.800 liters per person per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morelli_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morelli-33">[33]</a></sup> On average 92% of this is embedded in agricultural products consumed, 4.4% in industrial products consumed, and 3.6% is domestic water use. The global water footprint related to producing goods for export is 1.762 Gm3∕y.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-humanity_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-humanity-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>In absolute terms, India is the country with the largest water footprint in the world, a total of 987 Gm3/yr. In relative terms (i.e. taking population size into account), the people of the USA have the largest water footprint, with 2480 m3/yr per capita, followed by the people in south European countries such as Greece, Italy and Spain (2300–2400 m3/yr per capita). High water footprints can also be found in Malaysia and Thailand. In contrast, the Chinese people have a relatively low per capita water footprint with an average of 700 m3/yr.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nations_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-nations-1">[1]</a></sup> (These numbers are also from the period 1996-2005)
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internal_or_external">Internal or external</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Internal or external">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:562px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GlobalWaterFootprint_by_sector.1500.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="3690" height="291" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/GlobalWaterFootprint_by_sector.1500.jpg/560px-GlobalWaterFootprint_by_sector.1500.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/GlobalWaterFootprint_by_sector.1500.jpg/840px-GlobalWaterFootprint_by_sector.1500.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/GlobalWaterFootprint_by_sector.1500.jpg/1120px-GlobalWaterFootprint_by_sector.1500.jpg 2x" width="560"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GlobalWaterFootprint_by_sector.1500.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Global average numbers and composition of all national water footprints, internal and external</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>internal water footprint</b> is the amount of water used from domestic water resources; the <b>external water footprint</b> is the amount of water used in other countries to produce goods and services imported and consumed by the inhabitants of the country. When assessing the water footprint of a nation, it is crucial to take into account the international flows of <a href="/wiki/Virtual_water" title="Virtual water">virtual water</a> (also called <i>embodied water</i>, i.e. the water used or polluted in connection to all agricultural and industrial commodities) leaving and entering the country. When taking the use of domestic water resources as a starting point for calculating a nation’s water footprint, one should subtract the virtual water flows that leave the country and add the virtual water flows that enter the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nations_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-nations-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The external part of a nation's water footprint varies strongly from country to country. Some African nations, such as Sudan, Mali, Nigeria, Ethiopia, Malawi and Chad have hardly any external water footprint, simply because they have little import. Some European countries on the other hand, e.g. Italy, Germany, the UK and the Netherlands have external water footprints that constitute 50–80% of their total water footprint. The agricultural products that on average contribute most to the external water footprints of nations are: bovine meat, soybean, wheat, cocoa, rice, cotton and maize.
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nations_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-nations-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The top 10 gross virtual water exporting nations, which together account for more than half of the global virtual water export, are the United States (314 Gm3∕year), China (143 Gm3∕year), India (125 Gm3∕year), Brazil (112 Gm3∕year), Argentina (98 Gm3∕year), Canada (91 Gm3∕year), Australia (89 Gm3∕year), Indonesia (72 Gm3∕year), France (65 Gm3∕year), and Germany (64 Gm3∕year).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-humanity_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-humanity-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>The top 10 gross virtual water importing nations are the United States (234 Gm3∕year), Japan (127 Gm3∕year), Germany (125 Gm3∕year), China (121 Gm3∕year), Italy (101 Gm3∕year), Mexico (92 Gm3∕year), France (78 Gm3∕year), the United Kingdom (77 Gm3∕year), and The Netherlands (71 Gm3∕year).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-humanity_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-humanity-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Droughts_in_Europe" title="Category:Droughts in Europe">Category:Droughts in Europe</a></div>
<p>Each <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_the_European_Union" title="Demographics of the European Union">EU citizen</a> consumes 4,815 litres of water per day on average; 44% is used in power production primarily to cool thermal plants or <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_in_the_European_Union" title="Nuclear power in the European Union">nuclear power</a> plants. Energy production annual water consumption in the EU 27 in 2011 was, in billion m³: for gas 0.53, coal 1.54 and nuclear 2.44. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wind_energy" title="Wind energy">Wind energy</a> avoided the use of 387 million cubic metres (mn m³) of water in 2012, avoiding a cost of €743 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_water_use">Environmental water use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Environmental water use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although agriculture’s water use includes provision of important terrestrial environmental values (as discussed in the “Water footprint of products” section above), and much “green water’ is used in maintaining forests and wild lands, there is also direct environmental use (e.g. of surface water) that may be allocated by governments. For example, in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, where water use issues are sometimes severe because of drought, about 48 percent of “dedicated water use” in an average water year is for the environment (somewhat more than for agriculture).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Such environmental water use is for keeping streams flowing, maintaining aquatic and riparian habitats, keeping wetlands wet, etc.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_of_water_footprint_and_virtual_water">Criticism of water footprint and virtual water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Criticism of water footprint and virtual water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Insufficient_consideration_of_consequences_of_proposed_water_saving_policies_to_farm_households">Insufficient consideration of consequences of proposed water saving policies to farm households</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Insufficient consideration of consequences of proposed water saving policies to farm households">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to Dennis Wichelns of the <a href="/wiki/International_Water_Management_Institute" title="International Water Management Institute">International Water Management Institute</a>: "Although one goal of virtual water analysis is to describe opportunities for improving water security, there is almost no mention of the potential impacts of the prescriptions arising from that analysis on farm households in industrialized or developing countries. It is essential to consider more carefully the inherent flaws in the virtual water and water footprint perspectives, particularly when seeking guidance regarding policy decisions."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_water_scarcity_should_be_taken_into_account_when_interpreting_water_footprint">Regional water scarcity should be taken into account when interpreting water footprint</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Regional water scarcity should be taken into account when interpreting water footprint">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The application and interpretation of water footprints may sometimes be used to promote industrial activities that lead to facile criticism of certain products. For example, the 140 litres required for coffee production for one cup<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-waterfootprintorg_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-waterfootprintorg-2">[2]</a></sup> might be of no harm to water resources if its cultivation occurs mainly in humid areas, but could be damaging in more arid regions. Other factors such as hydrology, climate, geology, topography, population and demographics should also be taken into account. Nevertheless, high water footprint calculations do suggest that environmental concern may be appropriate.
</p><p>The use of the term <i>footprint</i> can also confuse people familiar with the notion of a <a href="/wiki/Carbon_footprint" title="Carbon footprint">carbon footprint</a>, because the water footprint concept includes sums of water quantities without necessarily evaluating related impacts. This is in contrast to the carbon footprint, where carbon emissions are not simply summarized but normalized by CO<sub>2</sub> emissions, which are globally identical, to account for the environmental harm. The difference is due to the somewhat more complex nature of water; while involved in the global hydrological cycle, it is expressed in conditions both local and regional through various forms like river basins, watersheds, on down to groundwater (as part of larger aquifer systems).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_water_use">Sustainable water use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Sustainable water use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainable" title="Sustainable">Sustainable</a> water use involves the rigorous assessment of all source of clean water to establish the current and future rates of use, the impacts of that use both downstream and in the wider area where the water may be used and the impact of  contaminated water streams on the environment and economic well being of the area. It also involves the implementation of <a href="/wiki/Social_policy" title="Social policy">social policies</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Water_pricing" title="Water pricing">water pricing</a> in order to manage water demand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> In some localities, water may also have spiritual relevance and the use of such water may need to take account of such interests. For example, the <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="Māori people">Maori</a> believe that water is the source and foundation of all life and have many spiritual associations with water and places associated with water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>  On a national and global scale, water sustainability requires strategic and long term planning to ensure appropriate sources of clean water are identified and the environmental and economic impact of such choices are understood and accepted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> The re-use and reclamation of water is also part of sustainability including downstream impacts on both surface waters and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ground_water" title="Ground water">ground waters</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-naumann_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-naumann-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sectoral_distributions_of_withdrawn_water_use">Sectoral distributions of withdrawn water use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_footprint&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Sectoral distributions of withdrawn water use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several nations estimate sectoral distribution of use of water withdrawn from surface and groundwater sources. For example, in Canada, in 2005, 42 billion m³ of withdrawn water were used, of which about 38 billion m³ were freshwater. Distribution of this use among sectors was: thermoelectric power generation 66.2%, manufacturing 13.6%, residential 9.0%, agriculture 4.7%, commercial and institutional 2.7%, water treatment and distribution systems   2.3%, mining  1.1%, and oil and gas extraction 0.5%.  The 38 billion cu.m of freshwater withdrawn in that year can be compared with the nation’s annual freshwater yield (estimated as streamflow) of 3,472 billion cu.m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>   Sectoral distribution is different in many respects in the US, where agriculture accounts for about 39% of fresh water withdrawals, thermoelectric power generation 38%, industrial 4%, residential 1%, and mining (including oil and gas) 1%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maupin2014_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Maupin2014-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>Within the agricultural sector, withdrawn water use is for irrigation and for livestock.  Whereas all irrigation in the US (including loss in conveyance of irrigation water) is estimated to account for about 38 percent of US withdrawn freshwater use,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maupin2014_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Maupin2014-43">[43]</a></sup> the irrigation water used for production of livestock feed and forage has been estimated to account for about 9 percent,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> and other withdrawn freshwater use for the livestock sector (for drinking, washdown of facilities, etc.) is estimated at about 0.7 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maupin2014_43-2"><a href="#cite_note-Maupin2014-43">[43]</a></sup>  Because agriculture is a major user of withdrawn water, changes in the magnitude and efficiency of its water use are important.  In the US, from 1980 (when agriculture’s withdrawn water use peaked) to 2010, there was a 23 percent reduction in agriculture’s use of withdrawn water,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maupin2014_43-3"><a href="#cite_note-Maupin2014-43">[43]</a></sup> while US agricultural output increased by 49 percent over that period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the US, irrigation water application data are collected in the quinquennial Farm and Ranch Irrigation Survey, conducted as part of the Census of Agriculture.  Such data indicate great differences in irrigation water use within various agricultural sectors.  For example, about 14 percent of corn-for-grain land and 11 percent of soybean land in the US are irrigated, compared with 66 percent of vegetable land, 79 percent of orchard land and 97 percent of rice land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USDA2009_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-USDA2009-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
