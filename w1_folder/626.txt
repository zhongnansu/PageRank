<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Sediment</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Leman_img_0573.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2924" data-file-width="1922" height="259" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Leman_img_0573.jpg/170px-Leman_img_0573.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Leman_img_0573.jpg/255px-Leman_img_0573.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Leman_img_0573.jpg/340px-Leman_img_0573.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Leman_img_0573.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_Rh%C3%B4ne" title="River Rhône">River Rhône</a> flowing into <a href="/wiki/Lake_Geneva" title="Lake Geneva">Lake Geneva</a>. Sediments make the water appear brownish-grey; they are an indicator of increased <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff#Effects_of_surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">water runoff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Land_degradation" title="Land degradation">land degradation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> due to intensive industrialized land use, land sealing, and poor soil management.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Sediment</b> is a naturally occurring material that is broken down by processes of <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a> and <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>, and is subsequently <a href="/wiki/Sediment_transport" title="Sediment transport">transported</a> by the action of wind, water, or ice, and/or by the force of <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a> acting on the particles. For example, <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silt</a> can be carried in <a href="/wiki/Suspension_(chemistry)" title="Suspension (chemistry)">suspension</a> in river water and on reaching the sea bed deposited by <a href="/wiki/Sedimentation" title="Sedimentation">sedimentation</a> and if buried, may eventually become <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siltstone" title="Siltstone">siltstone</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rocks</a>).
</p><p>Sediments are most often transported by water (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluvial" title="Fluvial">fluvial processes</a>), but also wind (<a href="/wiki/Aeolian_processes" title="Aeolian processes">aeolian processes</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a>. Beach sands and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stream_channel" title="Stream channel">river channel</a> deposits are examples of fluvial transport and <a href="/wiki/Deposition_(geology)" title="Deposition (geology)">deposition</a>, though sediment also often settles out of slow-moving or standing water in lakes and oceans. Desert sand dunes and <a href="/wiki/Loess" title="Loess">loess</a> are examples of aeolian transport and deposition.  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glacial" title="Glacial">Glacial</a> <a href="/wiki/Moraine" title="Moraine">moraine</a> deposits and <a href="/wiki/Till" title="Till">till</a> are ice-transported sediments.
</p>

<p>Sediment can be classified based on its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particle_size_(grain_size)" title="Particle size (grain size)">grain size</a> and/or its composition.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sediment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Composition of sediment can be measured in terms of:
</p>
<ul><li>parent <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rock</a> <a href="/wiki/Lithology" title="Lithology">lithology</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> composition</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical" title="Chemical">chemical</a> make-up.</li></ul>
<p>This leads to an ambiguity in which <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> can be used as both a size-range and a composition (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clay_mineral" title="Clay mineral">clay minerals</a>).
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Glacial_Transportation_and_Deposition.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1360" data-file-width="2048" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Glacial_Transportation_and_Deposition.jpg/220px-Glacial_Transportation_and_Deposition.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Glacial_Transportation_and_Deposition.jpg/330px-Glacial_Transportation_and_Deposition.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Glacial_Transportation_and_Deposition.jpg/440px-Glacial_Transportation_and_Deposition.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Glacial_Transportation_and_Deposition.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Glacial transport of boulders. <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sediment_transport" title="Sediment transport">Sediment transport</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Rouse_number" title="Rouse number">Rouse number</a></div>
<p>Sediment is transported based on the strength of the flow that carries it and its own size, volume, density, and shape. Stronger flows will increase the lift and drag on the particle, causing it to rise, while larger or denser particles will be more likely to fall through the flow.
</p>
<h3><span id="Fluvial_processes:_rivers.2C_streams.2C_and_overland_flow"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Fluvial_processes:_rivers,_streams,_and_overland_flow">Fluvial processes: rivers, streams, and overland flow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sediment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fluvial processes: rivers, streams, and overland flow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>If the upwards velocity is approximately equal to the settling velocity, sediment will be transported downstream entirely as <a href="/wiki/Suspended_load" title="Suspended load">suspended load</a>. If the upwards velocity is much less than the settling velocity, but still high enough for the sediment to move (see <a href="/wiki/Sediment_transport#Initiation_of_motion" title="Sediment transport">Initiation of motion</a>), it will move along the bed as <a href="/wiki/Bed_load" title="Bed load">bed load</a> by rolling, sliding, and <a href="/wiki/Saltation_(geology)" title="Saltation (geology)">saltating</a> (jumping up into the flow, being transported a short distance then settling again). If the upwards velocity is higher than the settling velocity, the sediment will be transported high in the flow as <a href="/wiki/Wash_load" title="Wash load">wash load</a>.
</p><p>As there are generally a range of different particle sizes in the flow, it is common for material of different sizes to move through all areas of the flow for given stream conditions.
</p>

<p>Sediment motion can create self-organized structures such as <a href="/wiki/Ripple_marks" title="Ripple marks">ripples</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dune" title="Dune">dunes</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Antidune" title="Antidune">antidunes</a> on the river or stream <a href="/wiki/Stream_bed" title="Stream bed">bed</a>. These bedforms are often preserved in sedimentary rocks and can be used to estimate the direction and magnitude of the flow that deposited the sediment.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Surface_runoff">Surface runoff</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sediment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Surface runoff">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Overland_flow" title="Overland flow">Overland flow</a> can erode soil particles and transport them downslope. The erosion associated with overland flow may occur through different methods depending on meteorological and flow conditions.
</p>
<ul><li>If the initial impact of rain droplets dislodges soil, the phenomenon is called rainsplash erosion.</li>
<li>If overland flow is directly responsible for sediment entrainment but does not form gullies, it is called "sheet erosion".</li>
<li>If the flow and the substrate permit channelization, gullies may form; this is termed "gully erosion".</li></ul>

<li><a href="/wiki/Alluvial_fan" title="Alluvial fan">Alluvial fans</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Braided_river" title="Braided river">Braided rivers</a></li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mass_balance">Mass balance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sediment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Mass balance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Exner_equation" title="Exner equation">Exner equation</a></div>
<p>The overall balance between sediment in transport and sediment being deposited on the bed is given by the <a href="/wiki/Exner_equation" title="Exner equation">Exner equation</a>. This expression states that the rate of increase in bed elevation due to deposition is proportional to the amount of sediment that falls out of the flow. This equation is important in that changes in the power of the flow change the ability of the flow to carry sediment, and this is reflected in the patterns of erosion and deposition observed throughout a stream. This can be localized, and simply due to small obstacles; examples are scour holes behind boulders, where flow accelerates, and deposition on the inside of <a href="/wiki/Meander" title="Meander">meander</a> bends. Erosion and deposition can also be regional; erosion can occur due to <a href="/wiki/Dam_removal" title="Dam removal">dam removal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Base_level" title="Base level">base level</a> fall. Deposition can occur due to dam emplacement that causes the river to pool and deposit its entire load, or due to base level rise.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shores_and_shallow_seas">Shores and shallow seas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sediment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Shores and shallow seas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Seas, oceans, and lakes accumulate sediment over time. The sediment can consist of <i>terrigenous</i> material, which originates on land, but may be deposited in either terrestrial, marine, or lacustrine (lake) environments, or of sediments (often biological) originating in the body of water. Terrigenous material is often supplied by nearby rivers and streams or reworked <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_sediment" title="Marine sediment">marine sediment</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a>). In the mid-ocean, the exoskeletons of dead organisms are primarily responsible for sediment accumulation.
</p><p>Deposited sediments are the source of <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rocks</a>, which can contain <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossils</a> of the inhabitants of the body of water that were, upon death, covered by accumulating sediment. Lake bed sediments that have not solidified into rock can be used to determine past <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climatic</a> conditions.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Key_marine_depositional_environments">Key marine depositional environments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sediment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Key marine depositional environments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EolianiteLongIsland.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/EolianiteLongIsland.JPG/220px-EolianiteLongIsland.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/EolianiteLongIsland.JPG/330px-EolianiteLongIsland.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/EolianiteLongIsland.JPG/440px-EolianiteLongIsland.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EolianiteLongIsland.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> <a href="/wiki/Eolianite" title="Eolianite">eolianite</a> and a carbonate beach on <a href="/wiki/Long_Island,_Bahamas" title="Long Island, Bahamas">Long Island, Bahamas</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The major areas for deposition of sediments in the marine environment include:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Littoral" title="Littoral">Littoral</a> sands (e.g. beach sands, runoff river sands, coastal bars and spits, largely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clastic" title="Clastic">clastic</a> with little faunal content)</li>
<li>The continental shelf (<a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silty</a> <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clays</a>, increasing marine faunal content).</li>
<li>The shelf margin (low terrigenous supply, mostly <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcareous</a> faunal skeletons)</li>
<li>The shelf slope (much more fine-grained silts and clays)</li>
<li>Beds of estuaries with the resultant deposits called "<a href="/wiki/Bay_mud" title="Bay mud">bay mud</a>".</li></ul>
<p>One other depositional environment which is a mixture of fluvial and marine is the <a href="/wiki/Turbidite" title="Turbidite">turbidite</a> system, which is a major source of sediment to the deep <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_basin" title="Sedimentary basin">sedimentary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Abyssal_plain" title="Abyssal plain">abyssal basins</a> as well as the deep <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_trench" title="Oceanic trench">oceanic trenches</a>.
</p><p>Any depression in a marine environment where sediments accumulate over time is known as a <a href="/wiki/Sediment_trap_(geology)" title="Sediment trap (geology)">sediment trap</a>.
</p><p>The null point theory explains how sediment deposition undergoes a hydrodynamic sorting process within the marine environment leading to a seaward fining of sediment grain size.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_issues">Environmental issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sediment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Environmental issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Sediment_transport#Applications" title="Sediment transport">Sediment transport § Applications</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Erosion_and_agricultural_sediment_delivery_to_rivers">Erosion and agricultural sediment delivery to rivers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sediment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Erosion and agricultural sediment delivery to rivers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One cause of high sediment loads is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slash_and_burn" title="Slash and burn">slash and burn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shifting_cultivation" title="Shifting cultivation">shifting cultivation</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical" title="Tropical">tropical</a> forests. When the ground surface is stripped of vegetation and then seared of all living organisms, the upper soils are vulnerable to both wind and water erosion. In a number of regions of the earth, entire sectors of a country have become erodible. For example, on the <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a> high central <a href="/wiki/Plateau" title="Plateau">plateau</a>, which constitutes approximately ten percent of that country's land area, most of the land area is devegetated, and gullies have eroded into the underlying soil in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Furrows" title="Furrows">furrows</a> typically in excess of 50 meters deep and one kilometer wide.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This <a href="/wiki/Siltation" title="Siltation">siltation</a> results in discoloration of rivers to a dark red brown color and leads to fish kills.
</p><p>Erosion is also an issue in areas of modern farming, where the removal of native vegetation for the cultivation and harvesting of a single type of crop has left the soil unsupported. Many of these regions are near rivers and drainages. Loss of soil due to erosion removes useful farmland, adds to sediment loads, and can help transport anthropogenic fertilizers into the river system, which leads to <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">eutrophication</a>.
</p><p>The Sediment Delivery Ratio (SDR) is fraction of gross erosion (interill, rill, gully and stream erosion) that is expected to be delivered to the outlet of the river.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The sediment transfer and deposition can be modelled with sediment distribution models such as WaTEM/SEDEM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In Europe, according to WaTEM/SEDEM model estimates the Sediment Delivery Ratio is about 15%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coastal_Development_and_Sedimentation_near_Coral_Reefs">Coastal Development and Sedimentation near Coral Reefs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sediment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Coastal Development and Sedimentation near Coral Reefs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Watershed development near coral reefs is a primary cause of sediment-related coral stress.The stripping of natural vegetation in the watershed for development exposes soil to increased wind and rainfall, and as a result, can cause exposed sediment to become more susceptible to erosion and delivery to the marine environment during rainfall events. Sediment can negatively affect corals in many ways, such as by physically smothering them, abrading their surfaces, causing corals to expend energy during sediment removal, and causing algal blooms that can ultimately lead to less space on the seafloor where juvenile corals (polyps) can settle.
</p><p>When sediments are introduced into the coastal regions of the ocean, the proportion of land, marine and organic-derived sediment that characterizes the seafloor near sources of sediment output is altered.    In addition, because the source of sediment (i.e. land, ocean, or organically) is often correlated with how coarse or fine sediment grain sizes that characterize an area are on average, grain size distribution of sediment will shift according to relative input of land (typically fine), marine (typically coarse), and organically-derived (variable with age) sediment.  These alterations in marine sediment characterize the amount of sediment that is suspended in the water column at any given time and sediment-related coral stress.
</p>
