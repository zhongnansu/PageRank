<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Deputy prime minister</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>deputy prime minister</b> or <b>vice prime minister</b> is, in some countries, a <a href="/wiki/Minister_(government)" title="Minister (government)">government minister</a> who can take the position of acting <a href="/wiki/Prime_minister" title="Prime minister">prime minister</a> when the prime minister is temporarily absent. The position is often likened to that of a <a href="/wiki/Vice_president" title="Vice president">vice president</a>, but is significantly different even though both positions are "number two" offices. The position of deputy prime minister should not be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deputy_Minister_(Canada)" title="Deputy Minister (Canada)">Deputy Minister</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Canada" title="Prime Minister of Canada">Prime Minister of Canada</a>, a nonpolitical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civil_servant" title="Civil servant">civil servant</a> position. Also, the Deputy Prime Minister of Canada does not act as a "number two".
</p><p>The states of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and provinces of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> each have the analogous office of deputy premier. In the <a href="/wiki/Devolution" title="Devolution">devolved</a> administrations of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, an analogous position is that of the deputy first minister but the position in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> has the same powers as the First Minister.
</p><p>A deputy prime minister traditionally serves as acting prime minister when the real prime minister is temporarily absent or incapable of exercising his/her power. The deputy prime minister is often asked to succeed to the prime minister's office following the prime minister's sudden death or unexpected resignation, but that is not necessarily mandated by the constitution. This government position is often a job that is held simultaneously with another ministry, and is usually given to one of the most senior or fairly experienced ministers of the cabinet. The holder of this office may also be <a href="/wiki/Deputy_leader" title="Deputy leader">deputy leader</a> of the governing party, or perhaps even as leader of the junior party of a <a href="/wiki/Coalition_government" title="Coalition government">coalition government</a>.
</p><p>Little scholarly attention has focused on deputy prime ministers due to their nature as being less involved in the political power plays of government and more focus on the work at hand. A 2009 study in <i>Political Science</i> identified nine 'qualities' of deputy prime ministership: temperament; relationships with their Cabinet and caucus; relationships with their party; popularity with the public; media skills; achievements as deputy prime minister; relationship with the prime minister; leadership ambition; and method of succession.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>By contrast, the structure of the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Russia" title="Government of Russia">Government of Russia</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_Ministers_of_Ukraine" title="Cabinet of Ministers of Ukraine">Cabinet of Ministers of Ukraine</a> foresees the positions of several deputy prime ministers or vice prime ministers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In the case of the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Russia" title="Government of Russia">Russian government</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Russia" title="Prime Minister of Russia">Prime Minister</a> is responsible for defining the scope of the duties for each of his or her deputies,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> who also may head a specific ministry - e.g. the former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minister_of_Finance" title="Minister of Finance">Minister of Finance</a> of Russia, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexey_Kudrin" title="Alexey Kudrin">Alexey Kudrin</a> also serves as one of the deputies of the prime ministers or <i>vice-premiers</i>. One or two of these deputy prime ministers may hold the title of a <i>First Deputy Prime Minister</i>. The Russian federal law indicates that  in accordance with the order established in advance, <i>one of the deputy prime ministers</i> may temporarily substitute for the Prime Minister in his or her absence. Customarily, however, it is to one of the "First" Deputy Prime Ministers that the prime-ministerial duties may be delegated.  At the same time, in the case of Prime Minister's resignation, the law allows the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Russia" title="President of Russia">President of Russia</a> to choose <i>any</i> of the current vice-premiers to serve as an <i>acting</i> Prime Minister until the confirmation of the new government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<td>19 October 2016 <br/> 9 June 2017 <br/> 19 June 2017 <br/> 25 May 2018</td>
<td>Article 109 of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Croatia" title="Constitution of Croatia">Constitution</a> mandates that there be at least one Deputy Prime Minister in every cabinet. The position is usually held by senior government ministers or leaders of junior parties in a coalition government.
<p>The title <i>First Deputy Prime Minister</i> is occasionally used when the position is occupied either by the chair of a junior coalition party which has significant parliamentary representation, and may be key in maintaining a majority, or by a high-official within the larger party in a coalition (as was the case in the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_Tihomir_Ore%C5%A1kovi%C4%87" title="Cabinet of Tihomir Orešković">Orešković cabinet</a>).
