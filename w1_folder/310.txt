<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Carbonic maceration</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Beaujolais_nouveau_wine.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="1936" height="295" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Beaujolais_nouveau_wine.jpg/220px-Beaujolais_nouveau_wine.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Beaujolais_nouveau_wine.jpg/330px-Beaujolais_nouveau_wine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Beaujolais_nouveau_wine.jpg/440px-Beaujolais_nouveau_wine.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Beaujolais_nouveau_wine.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Beaujolais Nouveau made by carbonic maceration</div></div></div>
<p><b>Carbonic maceration</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Winemaking" title="Winemaking">winemaking</a> technique, often associated with the <a href="/wiki/French_wine" title="French wine">French wine</a> region of <a href="/wiki/Beaujolais" title="Beaujolais">Beaujolais</a>, in which whole <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grapes" title="Grapes">grapes</a> are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermentation_(wine)" title="Fermentation (wine)">fermented</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> rich environment prior to crushing. Conventional <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fermentation" title="Ethanol fermentation">alcoholic fermentation</a> involves crushing the grapes to free the juice and pulp from the skin with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yeast_(wine)" title="Yeast (wine)">yeast</a> serving to convert <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a> into <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>. Carbonic maceration ferments most of the juice while it is still inside the grape, although grapes at the bottom of the vessel are crushed by <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a> and undergo conventional fermentation. The resulting <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a> is fruity with very low <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grape_tannins" title="Grape tannins">tannins</a>. It is ready to drink quickly but lacks the structure for long-term aging. In the most extreme case, such as with <a href="/wiki/Beaujolais_nouveau" title="Beaujolais nouveau">Beaujolais nouveau</a>, the period between picking and bottling can be less than six weeks.
</p><p>During carbonic maceration, an <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/anaerobic" title="wikt:anaerobic">anaerobic</a> environment is created by pumping carbon dioxide into a sealed container filled with whole grape clusters. The carbon dioxide gas <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Permeate" title="Permeate">permeates</a> through the grape skins and begins to stimulate fermentation at an <a href="/wiki/Intracellular" title="Intracellular">intracellular</a> level. The entire process takes place inside each single, intact berry. Ethanol is produced as a by-product of this process but studies have shown that other unique chemical reactions take place that have a distinctive effect on the wine. 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonic_maceration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Beaujolais_salad.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Beaujolais_salad.jpg/220px-Beaujolais_salad.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Beaujolais_salad.jpg/330px-Beaujolais_salad.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Beaujolais_salad.jpg/440px-Beaujolais_salad.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Beaujolais_salad.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Beaujolais wine.</div></div></div>
<p>The process of carbonic maceration occurs naturally in a partial state without deliberate intervention and has occurred in some form throughout history. If grapes are stored in a closed container, the force of gravity will crush the grapes on the bottom, releasing grape juice. Ambient yeasts present on the grape skins will interact with the sugars in the grape juice to start conventional ethanol fermentation. Carbon dioxide is released as a by product and, being denser than <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, will push out the oxygen through any permeable surface (such as slight gaps between wood planks) creating a mostly anaerobic environment for the uncrushed grape clusters to go through carbonic maceration. Some of the earliest documented studies on the process were conducted by the French scientist <a href="/wiki/Louis_Pasteur" title="Louis Pasteur">Louis Pasteur</a> who noted in 1872 that grapes contained in an oxygen rich environment prior to crushing and fermentation produced wines of different flavors than grapes produced in a carbon dioxide rich environment. This was because the fermentation process had already started within the individual grape clusters prior to the introduction of yeasts during conventional fermentation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_pg_138_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_pg_138-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wine_production">Wine production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonic_maceration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Wine production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yeast_on_grapes.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1704" data-file-width="2272" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Yeast_on_grapes.jpg/220px-Yeast_on_grapes.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Yeast_on_grapes.jpg/330px-Yeast_on_grapes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Yeast_on_grapes.jpg/440px-Yeast_on_grapes.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yeast_on_grapes.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In carbonic maceration, fermentation takes place inside each individual grape berry.</div></div></div>
<p>The use of carbonic maceration is closely associated with the production of <a href="/wiki/Beaujolais_nouveau" title="Beaujolais nouveau">Beaujolais nouveau</a> wine from the <a href="/wiki/Gamay" title="Gamay">Gamay</a> grape in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beaujolais_wine" title="Beaujolais wine">Beaujolais region</a> and some wines in the <a href="/wiki/Rioja_(wine)" title="Rioja (wine)">Rioja</a> <a href="/wiki/%C3%81lava" title="Álava">Alavesa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jumilla_(DO)" title="Jumilla (DO)">Jumilla</a> areas of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spain_wine" title="Spain wine">Spain</a>. This grape lends itself well to the production of simple, fruity wines and Beaujolais winemakers have been able to create a unique identity based on this distinctive style.
</p><p>Producers in other parts of <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/New_World_wine" title="New World wine">New World</a> have frequently utilized carbonic maceration for their own Gamay production, or with other <a href="/wiki/List_of_grape_varieties" title="List of grape varieties">grape varieties</a>. Winemakers in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Languedoc_wine" title="Languedoc wine">Languedoc</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rh%C3%B4ne_wine" title="Rhône wine">Rhône wine</a> regions will sometimes employ the technique on coarse and tannic grapes like <a href="/wiki/Carignan" title="Carignan">Carignan</a>, especially if they are to be blended with other varieties.  In California, the process is sometimes used with <a href="/wiki/Valdigui%C3%A9" title="Valdiguié">Valdiguié</a> grapes, producing what is labeled a "<a href="/wiki/Nouveau" title="Nouveau">Nouveau</a>" wine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The process is almost always used in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/Red_wine" title="Red wine">red wine</a> production since some of the flavor compounds produced by volatile <a href="/wiki/Phenolic_content_in_wine" title="Phenolic content in wine">phenols</a> tend to form undesirable flavors with white wine <a href="/wiki/List_of_grape_varieties" title="List of grape varieties">grape varieties</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_pg_138_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_pg_138-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_techniques">Other techniques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonic_maceration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other techniques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Semi-carbonic maceration is the winemaking technique where grapes are put through a short period of carbonic maceration, followed by conventional yeast fermentations. This is the process used in the production of Beaujolais nouveau wines. To an extent, most wines were historically treated to some form of semi or partial carbonic fermentation (as noted in history section above) with the amount dependent on the shape and size of the vessel that the grapes were stored in prior to crushing. The deeper the vessel, the greater the proportion of grapes that could be exposed to an anaerobic environment caused by the release of carbon dioxide from the crushed grapes on the bottom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_pg_620_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_pg_620-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>An alternative name for carbonic maceration is "whole grape fermentation", which is distinct from the process known as "whole bunch fermentation" which is common in the <a href="/wiki/Burgundy_wine" title="Burgundy wine">Burgundy wine</a> production of <a href="/wiki/Pinot_noir" title="Pinot noir">Pinot noir</a>. With "whole bunch fermentation", entire clusters of grapes (including stems) are fermented before being crushed. This creates a large "cap" of grape skins with pathways created by the stems that allows juice to flow more evenly through caps, increasing the levels of skin contact or <a href="/wiki/Maceration_(wine)" title="Maceration (wine)">maceration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oxford_pg_767_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_pg_767-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
