<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Reciprocal length</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Reciprocal length</b> or <b>inverse length</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Measurement" title="Measurement">measurement</a> used in several branches of <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>. As the <a href="/wiki/Multiplicative_inverse" title="Multiplicative inverse">reciprocal</a> of <a href="/wiki/Length" title="Length">length</a>, common units used for this measurement include the <b>reciprocal metre</b> or <b>inverse metre</b> (m<sup>−1</sup>), the <b>reciprocal centimetre</b> or <b>inverse centimetre</b> (cm<sup>−1</sup>), and, in <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">optics</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Dioptre" title="Dioptre">dioptre</a>.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantities" title="Quantities">Quantities</a> measured in reciprocal length include:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absorption_coefficient" title="Absorption coefficient">absorption coefficient</a> or <a href="/wiki/Attenuation_coefficient" title="Attenuation coefficient">attenuation coefficient</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">materials science</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Curvature" title="Curvature">curvature</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Curve" title="Curve">line</a>, in mathematics</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gain_(laser)" title="Gain (laser)">gain</a>, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laser_physics" title="Laser physics">laser physics</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnitude_(vector)" title="Magnitude (vector)">magnitude</a> of <a href="/wiki/Vector_(mathematics_and_physics)" title="Vector (mathematics and physics)">vectors</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reciprocal_space" title="Reciprocal space">reciprocal space</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Crystallography" title="Crystallography">crystallography</a></li>
<li>more generally any <a href="/wiki/Spatial_frequency" title="Spatial frequency">spatial frequency</a> e.g. in cycles per unit length</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Optical_power" title="Optical power">optical power</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)" title="Lens (optics)">lens</a>, in optics</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotational_constant" title="Rotational constant">rotational constant</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Rigid_rotor" title="Rigid rotor">rigid rotor</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wavenumber" title="Wavenumber">wavenumber</a>, or magnitude of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wavevector" title="Wavevector">wavevector</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopy</a></li>
<li>density of a linear feature in <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrology</a> and other fields; see <a href="/wiki/Kilometre_per_square_kilometre" title="Kilometre per square kilometre">kilometre per square kilometre</a></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measure_of_energy">Measure of energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reciprocal_length&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Measure of energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Reciprocal length is used as a measure of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">frequency</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a> yields a certain <a href="/wiki/Photon_energy" title="Photon energy">photon energy</a>, according to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planck-Einstein_relation" title="Planck-Einstein relation">Planck-Einstein relation</a>. Therefore, as reciprocal length is a measure of frequency, it can also be used as a measure of energy. For example, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reciprocal_centimetre" title="Reciprocal centimetre">reciprocal centimetre</a>, cm<sup>−1</sup>, is an energy unit equaling the energy of a photon with 1 cm <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a>. That energy amounts to approximately <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6996124000000000000♠</span>1.24<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>−4</sup> <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">eV</a></span> or <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6977198600000000000♠</span>1.986<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>−23</sup> <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">J</a></span>.
</p><p>The higher the number of inverse length units, the lower the energy. For example, in terms of energy, one reciprocal metre equals <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">6998100000000000000♠</span>10<sup>−2</sup></span> (one hundredth) as much as a reciprocal centimetre. Five reciprocal metres are one-fifth as much energy as one reciprocal metre.
</p>
