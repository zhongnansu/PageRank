<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Medical tourism</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Medical tourism</b> refers to people traveling to a country other than their own to obtain medical treatment. In the past this usually referred to those who traveled from less-developed countries to major medical centers in highly developed countries for treatment unavailable at home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> However, in recent years it may equally refer to those from developed countries who travel to developing countries for lower priced medical treatments. The motivation may be also for medical services unavailable or illegal in the home country.
</p><p>Medical tourism most often is for <a href="/wiki/Surgery" title="Surgery">surgeries</a> (cosmetic or otherwise) or similar treatments, though people also travel for <a href="/wiki/Dental_tourism" title="Dental tourism">dental tourism</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fertility_tourism" title="Fertility tourism">fertility tourism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> People with rare conditions may travel to countries where the treatment is better understood. However, almost all types of health care are available, including <a href="/wiki/Psychiatry" title="Psychiatry">psychiatry</a>, alternative medicine, convalescent care, and even burial services.
</p><p><b>Health tourism</b> is a wider term for travel that focus on medical treatments and the use of healthcare services. It covers a wide field of health-oriented, tourism ranging from preventive and health-conductive treatment to rehabilitational and curative forms of travel. <a href="/wiki/Wellness_tourism" title="Wellness tourism">Wellness tourism</a> is a related field.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first recorded instance of people travelling for medical treatment dates back thousands of years to when <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greek</a> pilgrims traveled from the eastern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> to a small area in the <a href="/wiki/Saronic_Gulf" title="Saronic Gulf">Saronic Gulf</a> called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epidauria" title="Epidauria">Epidauria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This territory was the sanctuary of the healing god <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asklepios" title="Asklepios">Asklepios</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Spa_town" title="Spa town">Spa towns</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sanatorium" title="Sanatorium">sanitaria</a> were early forms of medical tourism. In 18th-century Europe patients visited spas because they were places with supposedly health-giving <a href="/wiki/Mineral_water" title="Mineral water">mineral waters</a>, treating diseases from <a href="/wiki/Gout" title="Gout">gout</a> to <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a> disorders and <a href="/wiki/Bronchitis" title="Bronchitis">bronchitis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gahlinger2008_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gahlinger2008-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Factors that have led to the increasing popularity of medical travel include the high cost of health care, long wait times for certain procedures, the ease and affordability of international travel, and improvements in both technology and standards of care in many countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Goering_March_28,_2008_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Goering_March_28,_2008-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  The avoidance of waiting times is the leading factor for medical tourism from the UK, whereas in the US, the main reason is cheaper prices abroad.
</p><p>Many surgery procedures performed in medical tourism destinations cost a fraction of the price they do in other countries. For example, in the United States, a liver transplant that may cost $300,000 USD, would generally cost about $91,000 USD in Taiwan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A large draw to medical travel is convenience and speed. Countries that operate public health-care systems often have long wait times for certain operations, for example, an estimated 782,936 Canadian patients spent an average waiting time of 9.4 weeks on medical waiting lists in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Canada has also set waiting time benchmarks for non urgent medical procedures, including a 26-week waiting period for a hip replacement and a 16-week wait for cataract surgery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>In First World countries such as the United States, medical tourism has large growth prospects and potentially destabilizing implications.  A forecast by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deloitte_Consulting" title="Deloitte Consulting">Deloitte Consulting</a> published in August 2008 projected that medical tourism originating in the US could jump by a factor of ten over the next decade.  An estimated 750,000 Americans went abroad for health care in 2007, and the report estimated that 1.5 million would seek health care outside the US in 2008.  The growth in medical tourism has the potential to cost US health care providers billions of dollars in lost revenue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>An authority at the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_School" title="Harvard Business School">Harvard Business School</a> stated that "medical tourism is promoted much more heavily in the United Kingdom than in the United States".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Additionally, some patients in some First World countries are finding that insurance either does not cover orthopedic surgery (such as knee or hip replacement) or limits the choice of the facility, surgeon, or prosthetics to be used.
</p><p>Popular medical travel worldwide destinations include: <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Popular destinations for cosmetic surgery include: <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>. According to the "Sociedad Boliviana de Cirugia Plastica y Reconstructiva", more than 70% of middle and upper class women in the country have had some form of plastic surgery. Other destination countries include <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some people travel for <a href="/wiki/Assisted_reproductive_technology" title="Assisted reproductive technology">assisted pregnancy</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In-vitro_fertilization" title="In-vitro fertilization">in-vitro fertilization</a>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surrogate_pregnancy" title="Surrogate pregnancy">surrogacy</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> or freezing embryos for retro-production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, perceptions of medical tourism are not always positive. In places like the US, which has high standards of quality, medical tourism is viewed as risky. In some parts of the world, wider political issues can influence where medical tourists will choose to seek out health care.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Health_tourism_provider" title="Health tourism provider">Health tourism providers</a> have developed as intermediaries which unite potential medical tourists with provider hospitals and other organizations. Companies that focus on medical value travel typically provide nurse case managers to assist patients with pre- and post-travel medical issues. They may also help provide resources for follow-up care upon the patient's return.
</p><p>Circumvention tourism is also an area of medical tourism that has grown. Circumvention tourism is travel in order to access medical services that are legal in the destination country but illegal in the home country. This can include travel for fertility treatments that aren’t yet approved in the home country, abortion, and doctor-assisted suicide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Circumvention_Tourism_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Circumvention_Tourism-17">[17]</a></sup> Abortion tourism can be found most commonly in Europe, where travel between countries is relatively simple. Ireland and Poland, two European countries with highly restrictive abortion laws, have the highest rates of circumvention tourism. In Poland especially, it is estimated that each year nearly 7,000 women travel to the UK, where abortion services are free through the National Health Service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> There are also efforts being made by independent organizations and doctors, such as with Women on Waves, to help women circumvent draconian laws in order to access medical services. With Women on Waves, the organization uses a mobile clinic aboard a ship to provide medical abortions in international waters, where the law of the country whose flag is flown applies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_healthcare_accreditation">International healthcare accreditation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: International healthcare accreditation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/International_healthcare_accreditation" title="International healthcare accreditation">International healthcare accreditation</a></div>
<p>International healthcare accreditation is the process of certifying a level of quality for healthcare providers and programs across multiple countries.  <a href="/wiki/List_of_international_healthcare_accreditation_organizations" title="List of international healthcare accreditation organizations">International healthcare accreditation organizations</a> certify a wide range of healthcare programs such as hospitals, primary care centers, medical transport, and ambulatory care services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>  There are a number of accreditation schemes available based in a number of different countries around the world.
</p><p>The oldest international accrediting body is Accreditation Canada, formerly known as the Canadian Council on Health Services Accreditation,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> which accredited the Bermuda Hospital Board as soon as 1968.  Since then, it has accredited hospitals and health service organizations in ten other countries.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GoldSeal_4color.jpg"><img alt="JCI Gold Seal, an example of international hospital standard accreditation" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="750" data-file-width="750" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/GoldSeal_4color.jpg/220px-GoldSeal_4color.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/GoldSeal_4color.jpg/330px-GoldSeal_4color.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/GoldSeal_4color.jpg/440px-GoldSeal_4color.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GoldSeal_4color.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>JCI Gold Seal, an example of international hospital standard  accreditation</div></div></div>
<p>In the United States, the accreditation group <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joint_Commission_International" title="Joint Commission International">Joint Commission International</a> (JCI) was formed in 1994 to provide international clients education and consulting services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Many international hospitals today see obtaining international accreditation as a way to attract American patients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Joint Commission International is a relative of the Joint Commission in the United States. Both are US-style independent private sector not-for-profit organizations that develop nationally and internationally recognized procedures and standards to help improve patient care and safety. They work with hospitals to help them meet Joint Commission standards for patient care and then accredit those hospitals meeting the standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>A British scheme, QHA Trent Accreditation, is an active independent holistic accreditation scheme, as well as GCR.org which monitors the success metrics and standards of almost 500,000 medical clinics worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>The different international healthcare accreditation schemes vary in quality, size, cost, intent and the skill and intensity of their marketing. They also vary in terms of cost to hospitals and healthcare institutions making use of them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Increasingly, some hospitals are looking towards dual international accreditation, perhaps having both JCI to cover potential US clientele, and Accreditation Canada or QHA Trent. As a result of competition between clinics for American medical tourists, there have been initiatives to rank hospitals based on patient-reported metrics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Risks">Risks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Risks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Medical tourism carries some risks that locally provided medical care either does not carry or carries to a much lesser degree.
</p><p>Some countries, such as South Africa, or Thailand have very different infectious disease-related epidemiology to Europe and North America. Exposure to diseases without having built up natural immunity can be a hazard for weakened individuals, specifically with respect to gastrointestinal diseases (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis_A" title="Hepatitis A">hepatitis A</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amoebic_dysentery" title="Amoebic dysentery">amoebic dysentery</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paratyphoid" title="Paratyphoid">paratyphoid</a>) which could weaken progress and expose the patient to <a href="/wiki/Mosquito-borne_disease" title="Mosquito-borne disease">mosquito-transmitted diseases</a>, <a href="/wiki/Influenza" title="Influenza">influenza</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>. However, because in poor tropical nations diseases run the gamut, doctors seem to be more open to the possibility of considering any infectious disease, including <a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">HIV</a>, TB, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typhoid" title="Typhoid">typhoid</a>, while there are cases in the West where patients were consistently misdiagnosed for years because such diseases are perceived to be "rare" in the West.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>The quality of post-operative care can also vary dramatically, depending on the hospital and country, and may be different from US or European standards. Also, traveling long distances soon after surgery can increase the risk of complications. Long flights and decreased mobility associated with window seats can predispose one towards developing <a href="/wiki/Deep_vein_thrombosis" title="Deep vein thrombosis">deep vein thrombosis</a> and potentially a <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_embolism" title="Pulmonary embolism">pulmonary embolism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Other vacation activities can be problematic as well — for example, scars may become darker and more noticeable if they are sunburned while healing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Also, health facilities treating medical tourists may lack an adequate complaints policy to deal appropriately and fairly with complaints made by dissatisfied patients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Differences in healthcare provider standards around the world have been recognised by the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a>, and in 2004 it launched the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=World_Alliance_for_Patient_Safety&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="World Alliance for Patient Safety (page does not exist)">World Alliance for Patient Safety</a>. This body assists hospitals and government around the world in setting patient safety policy and practices that can become particularly relevant when providing medical tourism services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>If there are complications, the patient may need to stay in the foreign country for longer than planned or if they have returned home, will not have easy access for follow up care.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ginger_Rough_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ginger_Rough-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>Patients sometimes travel to another country to obtain medical procedures that doctors in their home country refuse to perform because they believed that the risks of the procedure outweigh the benefits. Such patients may have difficulty getting insurance (whether public or private) to cover follow up medical costs should the feared complications indeed arise.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_issues">Legal issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legal issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Receiving medical care abroad may subject medical tourists to unfamiliar legal issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AHC_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-AHC-34">[34]</a></sup> The limited nature of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Litigation" title="Litigation">litigation</a> in various countries is a reason for accessbility of care overseas. While some countries currently presenting themselves as attractive medical tourism destinations provide some form of legal remedies for <a href="/wiki/Medical_malpractice" title="Medical malpractice">medical malpractice</a>, these legal avenues may be unappealing to the medical tourist. Should problems arise, patients might not be covered by adequate personal insurance or might be unable to seek compensation via <a href="/wiki/Malpractice" title="Malpractice">malpractice</a> lawsuits. Hospitals and/or doctors in some countries may be unable to pay the financial damages awarded by a court to a patient who has sued them, owing to the hospital and/or the doctor not possessing appropriate insurance cover and/or medical indemnity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>Issues can also arise for patients who seek out services that are illegal in their home country. In this case, some countries have the jurisdiction to prosecute their citizen once they have returned home, or in extreme cases extraterritorially arrest and prosecute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Circumvention_Tourism_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Circumvention_Tourism-17">[17]</a></sup> In Ireland, especially, in the 1980s-90s there were cases of young rape victims who were banned from traveling to Europe to get legal abortions. Ultimately, Ireland’s Supreme Court overturned the ban; they and many other countries have since created "right to travel" amendments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Circumvention_Tourism_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Circumvention_Tourism-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethical_issues">Ethical issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ethical issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There can be major ethical issues around medical tourism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AHC_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-AHC-34">[34]</a></sup> For example, the illegal purchase of organs and tissues for transplantation had been methodically documented and studied in countries such as India,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> China,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orgharv_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-orgharv-40">[40]</a></sup> Colombia<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> and the Philippines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_Istanbul" title="Declaration of Istanbul">Declaration of Istanbul</a> distinguishes between ethically problematic "transplant tourism" and "travel for transplantation".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>Medical tourism may raise broader ethical issues for the countries in which it is promoted. For example, in India, some argue that a "policy of 'medical tourism for the classes and health missions for the masses' will lead to a deepening of the inequities" already embedded in the health care system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> In Thailand, in 2008 it was stated that, "Doctors in Thailand have become so busy with foreigners that Thai patients are having trouble getting care".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Medical tourism centered on new technologies, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stem_cell_treatments" title="Stem cell treatments">stem cell treatments</a>, is often criticized on grounds of fraud, blatant lack of scientific rationale and patient safety. However, when pioneering advanced technologies, such as providing 'unproven' therapies to patients outside of regular clinical trials, it is often challenging to differentiate between acceptable medical innovation and unacceptable patient exploitation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Employer-sponsored_health_care_in_the_US">Employer-sponsored health care in the US</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Employer-sponsored health care in the US">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some US employers have begun exploring medical travel programs as a way to cut employee <a href="/wiki/Health_care_prices_in_the_United_States" title="Health care prices in the United States">health care costs</a>.  Such proposals have raised stormy debates between employers and <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">trade unions</a> representing workers, with one union stating that it deplored the "shocking new approach" of offering employees overseas treatment in return for a share of the company's savings.  The unions also raise the issues of <a href="/wiki/Legal_liability" title="Legal liability">legal liability</a> should something go wrong, and potential job losses in the US health care industry if treatment is <a href="/wiki/Outsourcing" title="Outsourcing">outsourced</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p>Employers may offer incentives such as paying for air travel and waiving out-of-pocket expenses for care outside of the US. For example, in January 2008, Hannaford Bros., a supermarket chain based in Maine, began paying the entire medical bill for employees to travel to Singapore for hip and knee replacements, including travel for the patient and companion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>  Medical travel packages can integrate with all types of <a href="/wiki/Health_insurance" title="Health insurance">health insurance</a>, including limited benefit plans,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Preferred_provider_organization" title="Preferred provider organization">preferred provider organizations</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_Deductible_Health_Plan" title="High Deductible Health Plan">high deductible health plans</a>.
</p><p>In 2000, Blue Shield of California began the United States' first cross-border health plan. Patients in California could travel to one of the three certified hospitals in Mexico for treatment under California Blue Shield.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> In 2007, a subsidiary of BlueCross BlueShield of South Carolina, Companion Global Healthcare, teamed up with hospitals in Thailand, Singapore, Turkey, Ireland, Costa Rica and India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> A 2008 article in <i><a href="/wiki/Fast_Company_(magazine)" title="Fast Company (magazine)">Fast Company</a></i> discusses the globalization of healthcare and describes how various players in the US healthcare market have begun to explore it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Destinations">Destinations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Destinations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa_and_the_Middle_East">Africa and the Middle East</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Africa and the Middle East">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Jordan">Jordan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Jordan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jordan" title="Jordan">Jordan</a>, through their Private Hospitals Association, attracted 250,000 international patients accompanied by more than 500,000 companions in 2012, with total revenues exceeding 1B US$.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JordanAward_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-JordanAward-54">[54]</a></sup> Jordan won the Medical Destination of the year award in 2014 in the IMTJ Medical Travel Awards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JordanAward_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-JordanAward-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Israel">Israel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Israel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Medical_tourism_in_Israel" title="Medical tourism in Israel">Medical tourism in Israel</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Healthcare_in_Israel" title="Healthcare in Israel">Israel</a> is a popular destination for medical tourism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haaretz_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haaretz-55">[55]</a></sup> Many medical tourists to Israel come from Europe, particularly the former Soviet Union, as well as the United States, Australia, Cyprus, and South Africa. Medical tourists come to Israel for a variety of surgical procedures and therapies, including bone marrow transplants, heart surgery and catheterization, oncological and neurological treatments, orthopedic procedures, car accident rehabilitation, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In-vitro_fertilization" title="In-vitro fertilization">in-vitro fertilization</a>. Israel's popularity as a destination for medical tourism stems from its status as a developed country with a high-quality level of medical care, while at the same time having lower medical costs than many other developed countries. Israel is particularly popular as a destination for bone marrow transplants among Cypriots, as the procedure is not available in Cyprus, and for orthopedic procedures among Americans, as the cost of orthopedic procedures in Israel is about half that of in the United States. Israel is a particularly popular destination for people seeking IFV treatments. Medical tourists in Israel use both public and private hospitals, and all major Israeli hospitals offer medical tourism packages which typically cost far less than comparable procedures than in facilities elsewhere with a similarly high standard of care. In 2014, it was estimated that roughly 50,000 medical tourists came to Israel annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> There are reports that these medical tourists obtain preferential treatment, to the detriment of local patients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haaretz_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-Haaretz-55">[55]</a></sup> In addition, some people come to Israel to visit health resorts at the <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea" title="Dead Sea">Dead Sea</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haaretz_55-2"><a href="#cite_note-Haaretz-55">[55]</a></sup> and on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Kinneret" title="Lake Kinneret">Lake Kinneret</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Iran">Iran</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Iran">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 2012, 30,000 people came to <a href="/wiki/Healthcare_in_Iran" title="Healthcare in Iran">Iran</a> to receive medical treatment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
In 2015, it is estimated that between 150,000 and 200,000 health tourists came to Iran, and this figure is expected to rise to 500,000 a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p><p>Iran has low endemicity for <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis_B_virus" title="Hepatitis B virus">hepatitis B virus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hepatitis_C_virus" title="Hepatitis C virus">hepatitis C virus</a> infections and there is a unique experience of control of these infections that can be presented to people in Middle East countries. The pharmaceutical companies in Iran produces the drugs needed for control of HCV and HBV infection such as <a href="/wiki/Tenofovir_disoproxil" title="Tenofovir disoproxil">Tenofovir disoproxil</a>, Peg Interferon, <a href="/wiki/Sofosbuvir/daclatasvir" title="Sofosbuvir/daclatasvir">Sofosbuvir/daclatasvir</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ledipasvir" title="Ledipasvir">Ledipasvir</a>  with very low prices and high efficacy. Sadeghi F, Salehi-Vaziri M, Almasi-Hashiani A, Gholami-Fesharaki M, Pakzad R, Alavian SM. Prevalence of Hepatitis C Virus Genotypes Among Patients in Countries of the Eastern Mediterranean Regional Office of WHO (EMRO): A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis. Hepat Mon. 2016;16(4):e35558.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa">South Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: South Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Healthcare_in_South_Africa" title="Healthcare in South Africa">South Africa</a> is the first country in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> to emerge as a medical tourism destination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Arab_Emirates">United Arab Emirates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: United Arab Emirates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>United Arab Emirates, especially Dubai, Abu Dhabi, Ras Al Khaimah is a popular destination for medical tourism. The Dubai Health authority has been spearheading medical tourism into UAE, especially Dubai. However, hospitals providing medical tourism are spread all over the seven emirates. UAE has the distinction of having the maximum number of JCI accredited hospitals (under various heads).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> UAE has inbound medical tourism as well as people going out for medical treatment. The inbound tourism usually is from African countries like Nigeria, Kenya, Uganda, Rwanda, etc. The outbound can be categorised into two segments - the local population (citizens of UAE) and the expats. The locals prefer to go to European destinations like the U.K., Germany etc. The expats prefer to go back to their home countries for treatment.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Americas">Americas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Americas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazil">Brazil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Brazil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Healthcare_in_Brazil" title="Healthcare in Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein_Hospital" title="Albert Einstein Hospital">Albert Einstein Hospital</a> in São Paulo was the first JCI-accredited facility outside of the US,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> and more than a dozen Brazilian medical facilities have since been similarly accredited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JCI_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-JCI-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Mexico</b>
</p><p>Mexico has 98 hospitals accredited by the country’s Federal Health Ministry and 7 hospitals which are JCI-accredited. Mexico is most reputed for advanced care in dentistry and cosmetic surgery. Medical care in Mexico saves a patient 40% to 65% compared to the cost of similar services in the US.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In comparison to US health costs, patients can save 30 to 60 percent on health costs in <a href="/wiki/Healthcare_in_Canada" title="Healthcare in Canada">Canada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the early 1990s, Americans illegally using counterfeit, borrowed, or fraudulently obtained Canadian health insurance cards to obtain free healthcare in Canada became a serious issue due to the high costs it imposed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Costa_Rica">Costa Rica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Costa Rica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Costa Rica, there are two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joint_Commission_on_Accreditation_of_Healthcare_Organizations#International_accreditation" title="Joint Commission on Accreditation of Healthcare Organizations">Joint Commission International</a> accredited (JCI) hospitals. Both are in San Jose, Costa Rica. When the World Health Organization (WHO) ranked the world's health systems in the year 2000, Costa Rica was ranked as no. 26, which was higher than the U.S., and together with Dominica it dominated the list amongst the Central American countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Deloitte Center for Health Solutions reported a cost savings average of between 30-70% of US prices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Health_care_in_the_United_States" title="Health care in the United States">Health care in the United States</a></div>
<p>A report of McKinsey and Co. from 2008 found that between 60,000 and 85,000 medical tourists were traveling to the United States for the purpose of receiving in-patient medical care.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forbes08_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes08-70">[70]</a></sup> The same McKinsey study estimated that 750,000 American medical tourists traveled from the United States to other countries in 2007 (up from 500,000 in 2006).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hansen08_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hansen08-71">[71]</a></sup>  The availability of advanced medical technology and sophisticated training of physicians are cited as driving motivators for growth in foreigners traveling to the U.S. for medical care,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forbes08_70-1"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes08-70">[70]</a></sup> whereas the low costs for hospital stays and major/complex procedures at Western-accredited medical facilities abroad are cited as major motivators for American travelers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hansen08_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hansen08-71">[71]</a></sup> Also, the decline in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._currency#International_use" title="U.S. currency">value of the U.S. dollar</a> between 2007 and 2013 used to offer additional incentives for foreign travel to the U.S., although cost differences between the US and many locations in Asia are larger than any currency fluctuations.
</p><p>Several major medical centers and teaching hospitals offer international patient centers that cater to patients from foreign countries who seek medical treatment in the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> Many of these organizations offer service coordinators to assist international patients with arrangements for medical care, accommodations, finances and transportation including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_ambulance" title="Air ambulance">air ambulance</a> services.
</p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Healthcare_in_China" title="Healthcare in China">Healthcare in China</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ctrip" title="Ctrip">Ctrip</a>'s 2016 Online Medical Tourism Report indicates that the number of travelers who enroll in the oversea medical tourism through its platform increased fivefold over the previous year, and more than 500,000 Chinese visitors are expected to go on medical tourism. The top ten medical tourism destinations are Japan, Korea, the U.S., Taiwan, Germany, Singapore, Malaysia, Sweden, Thailand, and India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marce_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-marce-73">[73]</a></sup>  Regular health checks made up the majority share of Chinese medical tourism in 2016, representing over 50% of all medical tourism trips for tourists originating in China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marce_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-marce-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Medical_tourism_in_India" title="Medical tourism in India">Medical tourism in India</a></div>
<p>Medical tourism is a growing sector in <a href="/wiki/Healthcare_in_India" title="Healthcare in India">India</a>. India is becoming the 2nd medical tourism destination after Thailand. <a href="/wiki/Chennai" title="Chennai">Chennai</a> is regarded as "India's Health City" as it attracts 45% of health tourists visiting India and 40% of domestic health tourists.
</p><p>India’s medical tourism sector was expected to experience an annual growth rate of 30% from 2012, making it a $2 billion industry by 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MedicalCapitalsPlaceInHistory_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-MedicalCapitalsPlaceInHistory-76">[76]</a></sup> As medical treatment costs in the developed world balloon - with the United States leading the way - more and more Westerners are finding the prospect of international travel for medical care increasingly appealing.  An estimated 150,000 of these travel to India for low-priced healthcare procedures every year. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmetic_surgery" title="Cosmetic surgery">Cosmetic surgery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bariatric_surgery" title="Bariatric surgery">bariatric surgery</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knee_Cap" title="Knee Cap">knee cap replacements</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liver_transplantation" title="Liver transplantation">liver transplants</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Management_of_cancer" title="Management of cancer">cancer treatments</a> are some of the most sought out medical tourism procedures chosen by foreigners.
</p>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Health_care_in_New_Zealand" title="Health care in New Zealand">Health care in New Zealand</a></div>

<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Medical_tourism_in_Pakistan" title="Medical tourism in Pakistan">Medical tourism in Pakistan</a></div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Healthcare_in_Singapore" title="Healthcare in Singapore">Singapore</a> has a dozen hospitals and health centers with JCI accreditation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup>  In 2013 medical expenditure generated from medical tourists, mostly from more complex medical procedures, such as heart surgery, was S$832 million, a decline of 25% from 2012's S$1.11 billion, as the hospitals faced more competition from neighbouring countries for less complex work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Thailand">Thailand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Thailand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Health_in_Thailand" title="Health in Thailand">Health in Thailand</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_tourism_in_Thailand" title="Medical tourism in Thailand">Medical tourism in Thailand</a></div>
<p>Foreigners seeking treatment for everything from open-heart surgery to fertility treatmenst have made <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> and its 39 JCI-accredited hospitals a popular destination for medical tourism, attracting an estimated 2.81 million patients in 2015, up 10.2 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> In 2013, medical tourists spent as much as US$4.7 billion, according to government statistics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Thailand_Bangkok_Bumrungrad_International_Hospital_Campus.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1816" height="93" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Thailand_Bangkok_Bumrungrad_International_Hospital_Campus.jpg/220px-Thailand_Bangkok_Bumrungrad_International_Hospital_Campus.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Thailand_Bangkok_Bumrungrad_International_Hospital_Campus.jpg/330px-Thailand_Bangkok_Bumrungrad_International_Hospital_Campus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Thailand_Bangkok_Bumrungrad_International_Hospital_Campus.jpg/440px-Thailand_Bangkok_Bumrungrad_International_Hospital_Campus.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Thailand_Bangkok_Bumrungrad_International_Hospital_Campus.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Bumrungrad_International_Hospital" title="Bumrungrad International Hospital">Bumrungrad International Hospital</a> had been mentioned in the world press as a leading <a class="mw-selflink selflink">medical tourism</a> destination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Medical tourism is a large and growing sector within Thailand's extensive tourism and healthcare industries. The country is attractive to potential medical tourists and international patients for a number of important reasons: Thailand was the first Asian country to achieve <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joint_Commission_International" title="Joint Commission International">Joint Commission International</a> (JCI) accreditation in 2002. As of April 2014, 35 hospitals were JCI-accredited.;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> experienced, often Western-trained, medical professionals; the latest medical technology; and significantly lower costs of treatment when compared to corresponding procedures in the West. Taken together, all these factors—plus the country’s reputation as a popular tourist destination—have made Thailand one of the world's most popular medical tourism destinations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Siriraj_Hospital,Bangkok.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2394" data-file-width="4256" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Siriraj_Hospital%2CBangkok.jpg/300px-Siriraj_Hospital%2CBangkok.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Siriraj_Hospital%2CBangkok.jpg/450px-Siriraj_Hospital%2CBangkok.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Siriraj_Hospital%2CBangkok.jpg/600px-Siriraj_Hospital%2CBangkok.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Siriraj_Hospital,Bangkok.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Siriraj_Hospital" title="Siriraj Hospital">Siriraj Hospital</a>, Bangkok, the oldest and largest hospital in Thailand.</div></div></div>
<p>Some of the primary destinations for medical tourism in Thailand are Bangkok, Chiang Mai, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hua_Hin" title="Hua Hin">Hua Hin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ko_Samui" title="Ko Samui">Ko Samui</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pattaya" title="Pattaya">Pattaya</a>/<a href="/wiki/Chonburi_(city)" title="Chonburi (city)">Chonburi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Phuket_Province" title="Phuket Province">Phuket</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of  2006<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bumrungrad_International_Hospital" title="Bumrungrad International Hospital">Bumrungrad International Hospital</a> had been mentioned in the world press as a leading <a class="mw-selflink selflink">medical tourism</a> destination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> It was mentioned by www.firstmedicaltourism.com in December 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup> According to bh.listedcompany.com the hospital, served 520,000 international patients from over 190 countries in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BuildingForFuture_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-BuildingForFuture-88">[88]</a></sup>
</p><p>Foreigners seeking treatment for everything from open-heart surgery to <a href="/wiki/Sex_reassignment_surgery" title="Sex reassignment surgery">gender reassignment</a> have placed Thailand's private hospitals among the world's top destinations for medical tourism<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, attracting an estimated 2.81 million patients in 2015, up 10.2 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> In 2013, medical tourists pumped as much as US$4.7 billion into the Thai economy, according to government statistics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup>
</p><p>The rise of Chinese tourism in Thailand, growing 31 percent per year since 2012, has lifted medical tourism in Thailand and throughout <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ASEAN" title="ASEAN">ASEAN</a>. Of the 8.7 Chinese tourists who visited Thailand in 2016, some 27,000 came for medical treatment. Numbers are expected to grow by 8 to 10 percent by 2023. Also popular among Chinese patients are Malaysia and Singapore, largely owing to the large numbers of Chinese speakers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2006, it was ruled that under the conditions of the E112 European health scheme, UK health authorities had to pay the bill if one of their patients could establish urgent medical reasons for seeking quicker treatment in another European union country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup>
</p><p>The European directive on the application of patients’ rights to cross-border healthcare was agreed in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Finland">Finland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Finland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On December 9, 2013, the City of Helsinki decided that all minors under the age of 18 and all pregnant mothers living in Helsinki without a valid visa or residence permit are <a href="/wiki/Healthcare_in_Finland" title="Healthcare in Finland">granted the right to the same health care</a> and at the same price as all citizens of the city. This service will be available sometime early year 2014. Volunteer doctors of Global Clinic have tried to help these people, for whom only acute care has been available. This means that the <a href="/wiki/Healthcare_in_Finland" title="Healthcare in Finland">Finnish health care system</a> is open for all people coming outside of the European Union. The service covers special child health care, maternity clinics and specialist medical care etc. practically for free. It is still unclear if this will increase so called health care tourism, because all you have to do is come to Helsinki as a tourist and let the visa expire.
</p><p>The Global Clinic in Turku offers health care for all undocumented immigrants for free.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>British NHS patients have been offered treatment in <a href="/wiki/Health_care_in_France" title="Health care in France">France</a> to reduce waiting lists for hip, knee and cataract surgery since 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> France is a popular tourist destination but also ranked the world's leading health care system by the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Justice" title="European Court of Justice">European Court of Justice</a> said that <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service_(England)" title="National Health Service (England)">National Health Service (England)</a> has to pay back British patients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup>
</p><p>The number of patients is growing, and in 2016, France scored # 7 in the Medical Tourism Index.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Serbia">Serbia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Serbia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Healthcare_in_Serbia" title="Healthcare in Serbia">Serbia</a> has a variety of clinics catering to medical tourists in areas of cosmetic surgery, dental care, fertility treatment and weight loss procedures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> The country is also a major international hub for gender reassignment surgery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Turkey">Turkey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Turkey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The cost of medical treatments in <a href="/wiki/Health_care_in_Turkey" title="Health care in Turkey">Turkey</a> is quite affordable compared to Western European countries. Therefore, thousands of people each year travel Turkey for their medical treatments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> Turkey is especially becoming a hub for hair transplant surgery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup>  In February 2018 it was reported that the country was the worst place for botched plastic surgery operations on British people with Dr Mehmet Kaya of <a href="/wiki/Marmaris" title="Marmaris">Marmaris</a> singled out for criticism. The president of the <a href="/wiki/British_Association_of_Aesthetic_Plastic_Surgeons" title="British Association of Aesthetic Plastic Surgeons">British Association of Aesthetic Plastic Surgeons</a> said operations were performed on people who were not appropriate for surgery, and that unscrupulous practitioners have endangered their health for profit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Medical_tourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service" title="National Health Service">National Health Service</a> is publicly owned. It attracts medical tourism principally to specialist centres in London. Some private hospitals and clinics in the United Kingdom are medical tourism destinations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> Very few UK private hospitals have gone through independent international accreditation (they have the mandatory registration with the UK's watchdog, the <a href="/wiki/Care_Quality_Commission" title="Care Quality Commission">Care Quality Commission</a>), so they have not as yet measured themselves against the best clinics and hospitals elsewhere in the world.
</p><p>It is alleged that health tourists in the UK often target the NHS for its free-at-the-point-of-care treatment, allegedly costing the NHS up to £200 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup>  A study in 2013 concluded that the UK was a net exporter of medical tourists, with 63,000 UK residents travelling abroad for treatment and about 52,000 patients getting treatment in UK.  Medical tourists treated as private patients by NHS trusts are more profitable than UK private patients, yielding close to a quarter of the revenue from only 7% of volume of cases.  UK dental patients largely go to Hungary and Poland. Fertility tourists mostly travel to Eastern Europe, Cyprus and Spain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the summer of 2015 immigration officers from the <a href="/wiki/Border_Force" title="Border Force">Border Force</a> were stationed in <a href="/wiki/St_George%27s_University_Hospitals_NHS_Foundation_Trust" title="St George's University Hospitals NHS Foundation Trust">St George's University Hospitals NHS Foundation Trust</a> to train staff to identify "potentially chargeable patients".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup>  In October 2016 the trust announced that it planned to require photo identity papers or proof of their right to remain in the UK such as asylum status or a visa for pregnant women. Those not able to provide satisfactory documents would be sent to the trust's overseas patient team "for specialist document screening, in liaison with the <a href="/wiki/UK_Border_Agency" title="UK Border Agency">UK Border Agency</a> and the Home Office.”  It was estimated that £4.6 million a year was spent on care for ineligible patients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup> A pilot scheme to check whether patients were entitled to free NHS care in 18 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NHS_trusts" title="NHS trusts">NHS trusts</a>,  11 in London, for two months in 2017 asked 8,894 people for two forms of ID prior to non-emergency care. Only 50 were not eligible for free NHS treatment. Campaigners claimed this was “part of the Government’s hostile environment policy”, and that in <a href="/wiki/Newham_University_Hospital" title="Newham University Hospital">Newham hospital</a> "you will see huge signs saying you may not be eligible for free NHS treatment".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup>
