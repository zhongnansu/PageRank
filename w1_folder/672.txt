<p><b>Forsterite</b> (Mg<sub>2</sub>SiO<sub>4</sub>; commonly abbreviated as <b>Fo</b>) is the magnesium-rich <a href="/wiki/Endmember" title="Endmember">end-member</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">olivine</a> <a href="/wiki/Solid_solution" title="Solid solution">solid solution</a> series. It is <a href="/wiki/Isomorphism_(crystallography)" title="Isomorphism (crystallography)">isomorphous</a> with the iron-rich end-member, <a href="/wiki/Fayalite" title="Fayalite">fayalite</a>. Forsterite crystallizes in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthorhombic" title="Orthorhombic">orthorhombic</a> system (<a href="/wiki/Space_group" title="Space group">space group</a> <i>Pbnm</i>) with cell parameters <i>a</i> 4.75 <a href="/wiki/%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="Ångström">Å</a> (0.475 <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">nm</a>), <i>b</i> 10.20 Å (1.020 nm) and <i>c</i> 5.98 Å (0.598 nm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klein_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Klein-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Forsterite is associated with <a href="/wiki/Igneous_rock" title="Igneous rock">igneous</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphic rocks</a> and has also been found in <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">meteorites</a>. In 2005 it was also found in <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">cometary</a> dust returned by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stardust_probe" title="Stardust probe">Stardust probe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 2011 it was observed as tiny crystals in the dusty clouds of gas around a forming star.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Two polymorphs of forsterite are known: <a href="/wiki/Wadsleyite" title="Wadsleyite">wadsleyite</a> (also orthorhombic) and <a href="/wiki/Ringwoodite" title="Ringwoodite">ringwoodite</a> (isometric). Both are mainly known from meteorites.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Peridot" title="Peridot">Peridot</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Gemstone" title="Gemstone">gemstone</a> variety of forsterite olivine.
</p><p>Forsterite reacts with <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a> to form the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthopyroxene" title="Orthopyroxene">orthopyroxene</a> mineral <a href="/wiki/Enstatite" title="Enstatite">enstatite</a> in the following reaction:
</p>
<dl><dd>Mg<sub>2</sub>SiO<sub>4</sub> + SiO<sub>2</sub>  →  2MgSiO<sub>3</sub>.</dd></dl>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forsterite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Forsterite_orange_-_Ochtendung,_Eifel,_Germany.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="692" data-file-width="922" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Forsterite_orange_-_Ochtendung%2C_Eifel%2C_Germany.jpg/220px-Forsterite_orange_-_Ochtendung%2C_Eifel%2C_Germany.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Forsterite_orange_-_Ochtendung%2C_Eifel%2C_Germany.jpg/330px-Forsterite_orange_-_Ochtendung%2C_Eifel%2C_Germany.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Forsterite_orange_-_Ochtendung%2C_Eifel%2C_Germany.jpg/440px-Forsterite_orange_-_Ochtendung%2C_Eifel%2C_Germany.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Forsterite_orange_-_Ochtendung,_Eifel,_Germany.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Orange forsterite with a portion of tephroite</div></div></div>
<p>Pure forsterite is composed of magnesium, oxygen and silicon. The chemical formula is Mg<sub>2</sub>SiO<sub>4</sub>. Forsterite, <a href="/wiki/Fayalite" title="Fayalite">fayalite</a> (Fe<sub>2</sub>SiO<sub>4</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Tephroite" title="Tephroite">tephroite</a> (Mn<sub>2</sub>SiO<sub>4</sub>) are the end-members of the olivine solid solution series; other elements such as Ni and Ca substitute for Fe and Mg in olivine, but only in minor proportions in natural occurrences. Other minerals such as <a href="/wiki/Monticellite" title="Monticellite">monticellite</a> (CaMgSiO<sub>4</sub>), an uncommon calcium-rich mineral, share the olivine structure, but solid solution between olivine and these other minerals is limited. Monticellite is found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contact_metamorphism" title="Contact metamorphism">contact metamorphosed</a> <a href="/wiki/Dolomite" title="Dolomite">dolomites</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klein_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Klein-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geologic_occurrence">Geologic occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forsterite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geologic occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Forsterite-rich olivine is the most abundant mineral in the <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a> above a depth of about 400 km (250 mi); <a href="/wiki/Pyroxene" title="Pyroxene">pyroxenes</a> are also important minerals in this upper part of the mantle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  Although pure forsterite does not occur in <a href="/wiki/Igneous_rock" title="Igneous rock">igneous rocks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dunite" title="Dunite">dunite</a> often contains olivine with forsterite contents at least as Mg-rich as Fo<sub>92</sub> (92% forsterite – 8% fayalite); common <a href="/wiki/Peridotite" title="Peridotite">peridotite</a> contains olivine typically at least as Mg-rich as Fo<sub>88</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>  Due to its high melting point, olivine crystals are the first minerals to precipitate from a magmatic melt in a <a href="/wiki/Cumulate_rock" title="Cumulate rock">cumulate</a> process, often with <a href="/wiki/Pyroxene" title="Pyroxene">orthopyroxenes</a>. Forsterite-rich olivine is a common crystallization product of mantle-derived <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a>. Olivine in <a href="/wiki/Mafic" title="Mafic">mafic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultramafic" title="Ultramafic">ultramafic</a> rocks typically is rich in the forsterite end-member.
</p><p>Forsterite also occurs in dolomitic <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a> which results from the metamorphism of high magnesium <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dolostone" title="Dolostone">dolostones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
Nearly pure forsterite occurs in some <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphosed</a> <a href="/wiki/Serpentinite" title="Serpentinite">serpentinites</a>. Fayalite-rich olivine is much less common. Nearly pure fayalite is a minor constituent in some <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a>-like rocks, and it is a major constituent of some <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphic</a> <a href="/wiki/Banded_iron_formation" title="Banded iron formation">banded iron formations</a>.
</p>
<h2><span id="Structure.2C_formation.2C_and_physical_properties"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure,_formation,_and_physical_properties">Structure, formation, and physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forsterite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Structure, formation, and physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Forsterite is mainly composed of the anion SiO<sub>4</sub><sup>4−</sup> and the cation Mg<sup>2+</sup> in a molar ratio 1:2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Silicon is the central atom in the SiO<sub>4</sub><sup>4−</sup> anion. Each oxygen atom is bonded to the silicon by a single covalent bond. The four oxygen atoms have a partial negative charge because of the covalent bond with silicon. Therefore, oxygen atoms need to stay far from each other in order to reduce the repulsive force between them. The best geometry to reduce the repulsion is a tetrahedral shape. The cations occupy two different octahedral sites which are M1 and M2 and form ionic bonds with the silicate anions. M1 and M2 are slightly different. M2 site is larger and more regular than M1 as shown in Fig. 1.  The packing in forsterite structure is dense. The space group of this structure is Pbnm and the point group is 2/m 2/m 2/m which is an orthorhombic crystal structure. 
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Atomic_structure_of_olivine_1.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="258" data-file-width="300" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Atomic_structure_of_olivine_1.png/220px-Atomic_structure_of_olivine_1.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cc/Atomic_structure_of_olivine_1.png 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Atomic_structure_of_olivine_1.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fig. 1: The atomic scale structure of forsterite looking along the <i>a</i> axis. Oxygen is shown in red, silicon in pink, and Mg in blue. A projection of the unit cell is shown by the black rectangle.</div></div></div>
<p>This structure of forsterite can form a complete <a href="/wiki/Solid_solution" title="Solid solution">solid solution</a> by replacing the magnesium with iron.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Iron can form two different cations which are Fe<sup>2+</sup> and Fe<sup>3+</sup>. The iron(II) ion has the same charge as magnesium ion and it has a very similar ionic radius to magnesium. Consequently, Fe<sup>2+</sup> can replace the magnesium ion in the olivine structure.
</p><p>One of the important factors that can increase the portion of forsterite in the olivine solid solution is the ratio of iron(II) ions to iron(III) ions in the magma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> As the iron(II) ions <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidize" title="Oxidize">oxidize</a> and become iron(III) ions, iron(III) ions cannot form olivine because of their 3+ charge. The occurrence of forsterite due to the oxidation of iron was observed in the <a href="/wiki/Stromboli" title="Stromboli">Stromboli</a> <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcano</a> in Italy. As the volcano fractured, gases and volatiles escaped from the magma chamber. The crystallization temperature of the magma increased as the gases escaped. Because iron(II) ions were oxidized in the Stromboli magma, little iron(II) was available to form Fe-rich olivine (<a href="/wiki/Fayalite" title="Fayalite">fayalite</a>). Hence, the crystallizing olivine was Mg-rich, and igneous rocks rich in forsterite were formed.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Forsterite-pV.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="540" data-file-width="720" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Forsterite-pV.svg/220px-Forsterite-pV.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Forsterite-pV.svg/330px-Forsterite-pV.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Forsterite-pV.svg/440px-Forsterite-pV.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Forsterite-pV.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Molar volume vs. pressure at room temperature</div></div></div>
<p>At high pressure, forsterite undergoes a phase transition into wadsleyite; under the conditions prevailing in the Earth's upper mantle, this transformation would occur at pressures of ca. 14–15 GPa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Presnall_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Presnall-13">[13]</a></sup> In high-pressure experiments, the transformation may be delayed so that forsterite can remain metastable at pressures up to almost 50 GPa (see fig.).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_name">Discovery and name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forsterite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Discovery and name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Forsterite was first described in 1824 for an occurrence at Mte. Somma, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vesuvius" title="Vesuvius">Vesuvius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>. It was named by <a href="/wiki/Armand_L%C3%A9vy_(mineralogist)" title="Armand Lévy (mineralogist)">Armand Lévy</a> in 1824 after the English naturalist and mineral collector <a href="/wiki/Adolarius_Jacob_Forster" title="Adolarius Jacob Forster">Adolarius Jacob Forster</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forsterite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Forsterite is being currently studied as a potential <a href="/wiki/Biomaterial" title="Biomaterial">biomaterial</a> for <a href="/wiki/Implant_(medicine)" title="Implant (medicine)">implants</a> owing to its superior mechanical properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
