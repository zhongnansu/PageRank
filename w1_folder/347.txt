<p><b>Dichlorodifluoromethane</b> (<b>R-12</b>) is a colorless gas usually sold under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brand_name" title="Brand name">brand name</a> <b>Freon-12</b>, and a <a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon" title="Chlorofluorocarbon">chlorofluorocarbon</a> <a href="/wiki/Halomethane" title="Halomethane">halomethane</a> (CFC) used as a <a href="/wiki/Refrigerant" title="Refrigerant">refrigerant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aerosol_spray#Propellant" title="Aerosol spray">aerosol spray propellant</a>. Complying with the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a>, its manufacture was banned in developed countries (non-article 5 countries) in 1996, and developing countries (article 5 countries) in 2010 due to concerns about its damaging impact to the <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rsc_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-rsc-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint noexcerpt Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="Wikipedia copy (July 2017)">better source needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Its only allowed usage is as fire retardant in submarines and aircraft. It is soluble in many organic solvents. Dichlorodifluoromethane was one of the original propellants for <a href="/wiki/Silly_String" title="Silly String">Silly String</a>. R-12 cylinders are colored <a href="/wiki/White" title="White">white</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dichlorodifluoromethane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It can be prepared by reacting <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride" title="Carbon tetrachloride">carbon tetrachloride</a> with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">hydrogen fluoride</a> in the presence of a <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalytic</a> amount of <a href="/wiki/Antimony_pentachloride" title="Antimony pentachloride">antimony pentachloride</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>CCl<sub>4</sub> + 2HF → CCl<sub>2</sub>F<sub>2</sub> + 2HCl</dd></dl>
<p>This reaction can also produce <a href="/wiki/Trichlorofluoromethane" title="Trichlorofluoromethane">trichlorofluoromethane</a> (CCl<sub>3</sub>F), <a href="/wiki/Chlorotrifluoromethane" title="Chlorotrifluoromethane">chlorotrifluoromethane</a> (CClF<sub>3</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Tetrafluoromethane" title="Tetrafluoromethane">tetrafluoromethane</a> (CF<sub>4</sub>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dichlorodifluoromethane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Charles_F._Kettering" title="Charles F. Kettering">Charles (Boss) Kettering</a>, vice president of General Motors Research Corporation, was seeking a refrigerant replacement that would be colorless, odorless, tasteless, nontoxic, and nonflammable. He assembled a team that included <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Midgley,_Jr." title="Thomas Midgley, Jr.">Thomas Midgley, Jr.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Albert_Leon_Henne" title="Albert Leon Henne">Albert Leon Henne</a>, and Robert McNary. From 1930 to 1935, they developed dichlorodifluoromethane (CCl<sub>2</sub>F<sub>2</sub> or R12), <a href="/wiki/Trichlorofluoromethane" title="Trichlorofluoromethane">trichlorofluoromethane</a> (CCl<sub>3</sub>F or R11), <a href="/wiki/Chlorodifluoromethane" title="Chlorodifluoromethane">chlorodifluoromethane</a> (CHClF<sub>2</sub> or R22), <a href="/wiki/Trichlorotrifluoroethane" title="Trichlorotrifluoroethane">trichlorotrifluoroethane</a> (CCl<sub>2</sub>FCClF<sub>2</sub> or R113), and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dichlorotetrafluoroethane" title="Dichlorotetrafluoroethane">dichlorotetrafluoroethane</a> (CClF<sub>2</sub>CClF<sub>2</sub> or R114), through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kinetic_Chemicals" title="Kinetic Chemicals">Kinetic Chemicals</a> which was a joint venture between <a href="/wiki/DuPont" title="DuPont">DuPont</a> and <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_History_of_Polytetrafluoroethylene:_Discovery_and_Development_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_History_of_Polytetrafluoroethylene:_Discovery_and_Development-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_as_an_aerosol">Use as an aerosol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dichlorodifluoromethane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Use as an aerosol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The use of chlorofluorocarbons as aerosols in medicine, such as <a href="/wiki/United_States_Pharmacopeia" title="United States Pharmacopeia">USP</a>-approved <a href="/wiki/Salbutamol" title="Salbutamol">salbutamol</a>, has been phased out by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="U.S. Food and Drug Administration">U.S. Food and Drug Administration</a>. A different propellant known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrofluoroalkane" title="Hydrofluoroalkane">hydrofluoroalkane</a>, or HFA, which is not known to harm the environment, was chosen to replace it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Retrofitting">Retrofitting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dichlorodifluoromethane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Retrofitting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dichlorodifluoromethane&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span> <small><i>(February 2016)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>R-12 was used in most refrigeration and vehicle air conditioning applications prior to 1994 before being replaced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1,1,1,2-tetrafluoroethane" title="1,1,1,2-tetrafluoroethane">1,1,1,2-tetrafluoroethane</a> (R-134a), which has an insignificant <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion_potential" title="Ozone depletion potential">ozone depletion potential</a>. Automobile manufacturers started using R-134a instead of R-12 in 1992–1994. When older units leak or require repair involving removal of the refrigerant, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrofit" title="Retrofit">retrofitment</a> to a refrigerant other than R-12 (most commonly R-134a) is required in some jurisdictions. The United States does not require automobile owners to retrofit their systems; however, taxes on ozone-depleting chemicals coupled with the relative scarcity of the original refrigerants on the open market make retrofitting the only economical option. Retrofitment requires system flush and a new filter/dryer or accumulator, and may also involve the installation of new seals and/or hoses made of materials compatible with the refrigerant being installed. Mineral oil used with R-12 is not compatible with R-134a. Some oils designed for conversion to R-134a are advertised as compatible with residual R-12 mineral oil.
</p>
