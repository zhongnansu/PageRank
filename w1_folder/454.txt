<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Haldane effect</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>Haldane effect</b> is a property of <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a> first described by <a href="/wiki/John_Scott_Haldane" title="John Scott Haldane">John Scott Haldane</a>. Oxygenation of blood in the lungs displaces carbon dioxide from hemoglobin which increases the removal of carbon dioxide. This property is the Haldane effect. Conversely, oxygenated blood has a reduced affinity for carbon dioxide. Thus, the Haldane effect describes hemoglobin’s ability to carry increased amounts of CO2 in the deoxygenated state as opposed to the oxygenated state.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbamino">Carbamino</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Haldane_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Carbamino">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carbon dioxide can bind to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amino" title="Amino">amino</a> groups, creating <a href="/wiki/Carbamino" title="Carbamino">carbamino</a> compounds. Amino groups are available for binding at the N-terminals and at side-chains of <a href="/wiki/Arginine" title="Arginine">arginine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lysine" title="Lysine">lysine</a> residues in haemoglobin. This forms <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbaminohaemoglobin" title="Carbaminohaemoglobin">carbaminohaemoglobin</a>. Carbaminohaemoglobin is the major contributor to the Haldane effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nunn_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nunn-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Buffering">Buffering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Haldane_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Buffering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Haldane_effect&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span> <small><i>(June 2015)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table><p><a href="/wiki/Histidine" title="Histidine">Histidine</a> residues in hemoglobin can accept protons and act as <a href="/wiki/Buffering_agent" title="Buffering agent">buffers</a>. Deoxygenated hemoglobin is a better <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">proton acceptor</a> than the oxygenated form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nunn_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nunn-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In red blood cells, the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_anhydrase" title="Carbonic anhydrase">carbonic anhydrase</a> catalyzes the conversion of dissolved carbon dioxide to <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a>, which rapidly dissociates to <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> and a free <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a>:<br/>CO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O → H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> → H<sup>+</sup> + HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup><br/>
By <a href="/wiki/Le_Chatelier%27s_principle" title="Le Chatelier's principle">Le Chatelier's principle</a>, anything that stabilizes the proton produced will cause the reaction to shift to the right, thus the enhanced affinity of deoxyhemoglobin for protons enhances synthesis of bicarbonate and accordingly increases capacity of deoxygenated blood for carbon dioxide.  The majority of carbon dioxide in the blood is in the form of bicarbonate.  Only a very small amount is actually dissolved as carbon dioxide, and the remaining amount of carbon dioxide is bound to hemoglobin.
</p><p>In addition to enhancing removal of carbon dioxide from oxygen-consuming tissues, the Haldane effect promotes dissociation of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> from hemoglobin in the presence of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>. In the oxygen-rich capillaries of the lung, this property causes the displacement of carbon dioxide to plasma as low-oxygen blood enters the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolus" title="Alveolus">alveolus</a> and is vital for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_gas_exchange" title="Alveolar gas exchange">alveolar gas exchange</a>.
</p><p>The general equation for the Haldane Effect is:
H<sup>+</sup> + HbO<sub>2</sub> ←→ H<sup>+</sup>Hb + O<sub>2</sub>; however, this equation is confusing as it reflects primarily the <a href="/wiki/Bohr_effect" title="Bohr effect">Bohr effect</a>.  The significance of this equation lies in realizing that oxygenation of Hb promotes dissociation of H<sup>+</sup> from Hb, which shifts the bicarbonate buffer equilibrium towards CO<sub>2</sub> formation; therefore, CO<sub>2</sub> is released from RBCs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Siggaard_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Siggaard-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_significance">Clinical significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Haldane_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Clinical significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In patients with lung disease, lungs may not be able to increase <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_ventilation" title="Alveolar ventilation">alveolar ventilation</a> in the face of increased amounts of dissolved CO<sub>2</sub>.
</p><p>This partially explains the observation that some patients with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emphysema" title="Emphysema">emphysema</a> might have an increase in P<sub>a</sub>CO<sub>2</sub> (partial pressure of arterial dissolved carbon dioxide) following administration of supplemental oxygen even if content of CO<sub>2</sub> stays equal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hanson_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hanson-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
