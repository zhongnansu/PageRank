<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Quantification (science)</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For formal and natural language features to indicate quantity, see <a href="/wiki/Quantifier_(logic)" title="Quantifier (logic)">Quantifier (logic)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quantifier_(linguistics)" title="Quantifier (linguistics)">Quantifier (linguistics)</a>.</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empirical_science" title="Empirical science">empirical science</a>, <b>quantification</b> (or <b>quantitation</b>) is the act of <a href="/wiki/Counting" title="Counting">counting</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Measuring" title="Measuring">measuring</a> that maps human sense <a href="/wiki/Observation" title="Observation">observations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Experience" title="Experience">experiences</a> into <a href="/wiki/Quantity" title="Quantity">quantities</a>. Quantification in this sense is fundamental to the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific method</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_science">Natural science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantification_(science)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Natural science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some measure of the undisputed general importance of quantification in the <a href="/wiki/Natural_science" title="Natural science">natural sciences</a> can be gleaned from the following comments:
</p>
<ul><li>"these are mere facts, but they are quantitative facts and the basis of science."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li>It seems to be held as universally true that "the foundation of quantification is measurement."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li>There is little doubt that "quantification provided a basis for the objectivity of science."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>In ancient times, "musicians and artists ... rejected quantification, but merchants, by definition, quantified their affairs, in order to survive, made them visible on parchment and paper."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>Any reasonable "comparison between Aristotle and Galileo shows clearly that there can be no unique lawfulness discovered without detailed quantification."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>Even today, "universities use imperfect instruments called '<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exams" title="Exams">exams</a>' to indirectly quantify something they call knowledge."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>This meaning of quantification comes under the heading of <a href="/wiki/Pragmatism" title="Pragmatism">pragmatics</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (October 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In some instances in the natural sciences a seemingly intangible concept may be quantified by creating a scale—for example, a <a href="/wiki/Pain_scale" title="Pain scale">pain scale</a> in medical research, or a discomfort scale at the intersection of <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_physiology" title="Human physiology">human physiology</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Heat_index" title="Heat index">heat index</a> measuring the combined perceived effect of heat and <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a>, or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wind_chill_factor" title="Wind chill factor">wind chill factor</a> measuring the combined perceived effects of cold and wind.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_sciences">Social sciences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantification_(science)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Social sciences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Society_for_Quantitative_Analysis_of_Behavior" title="Society for Quantitative Analysis of Behavior">Society for Quantitative Analysis of Behavior</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quantitative_psychological_research" title="Quantitative psychological research">Quantitative psychological research</a></div>
<p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_sciences" title="Social sciences">social sciences</a>, quantification is an integral part of <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a>. Both disciplines gather data – economics by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empirical_observation" title="Empirical observation">empirical observation</a> and psychology by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Experimentation" title="Experimentation">experimentation</a> – and both use <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistical techniques</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Regression_analysis" title="Regression analysis">regression analysis</a> to draw conclusions from it.
</p><p>In some instances a seemingly intangible property may be quantified by asking subjects to rate something on a <a href="/wiki/Scale_(social_sciences)" title="Scale (social sciences)">scale</a>—for example, a <a href="/wiki/Happiness_economics#Methodology" title="Happiness economics">happiness scale</a> or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self-Perceived_Quality_of_Life_Scale" title="Self-Perceived Quality of Life Scale">quality of life scale</a>—or by the construction of a scale by the researcher, as with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Index_of_economic_freedom" title="Index of economic freedom">index of economic freedom</a>. In other cases, an unobservable variable may be quantified by replacing it with a <a href="/wiki/Proxy_(statistics)" title="Proxy (statistics)">proxy variable</a> with which it is highly correlated—for example, per capita <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a> is often used as a proxy for <a href="/wiki/Standard_of_living" title="Standard of living">standard of living</a> or <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a>.
</p><p>Frequently in the use of regression, the presence or absence of a trait is quantified by employing a <a href="/wiki/Dummy_variable_(statistics)" title="Dummy variable (statistics)">dummy variable</a>, which takes on the value 1 in the presence of the trait or the value 0 in the absence of the trait.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Quantitative_linguistics" title="Quantitative linguistics">Quantitative linguistics</a> is an area of <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a> that relies on quantification. For example,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> indices of <a href="/wiki/Grammaticalization" title="Grammaticalization">grammaticalization</a> of <a href="/wiki/Morpheme" title="Morpheme">morphemes</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">phonological</a> shortness, dependence on surroundings, and fusion with the verb, have been developed and found to be significantly correlated across languages with stage of evolution of function of the morpheme.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hard_versus_soft_science">Hard versus soft science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Quantification_(science)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hard versus soft science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ease of quantification is one of the features used to distinguish <a href="/wiki/Hard_and_soft_science" title="Hard and soft science">hard and soft sciences</a> from each other. Scientists often consider hard sciences to be more scientific or rigorous, but this is disputed by social scientists who maintain that appropriate rigor includes the qualitative evaluation of the broader contexts of qualitative data. In some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_sciences" title="Social sciences">social sciences</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>, quantitative data are difficult to obtain, either because laboratory conditions are not present or because the issues involved are conceptual but not directly quantifiable. Thus in these cases <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qualitative_methods" title="Qualitative methods">qualitative methods</a> are preferred.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
