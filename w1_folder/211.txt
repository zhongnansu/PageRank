<p>In <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, <b>pH</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'p' in 'pie'">p</span><span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">iː</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/eɪ/: 'a' in 'face'">eɪ</span><span title="/tʃ/: 'ch' in 'China'">tʃ</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Logarithmic_scale" title="Logarithmic scale">logarithmic scale</a> used to specify the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acidity" title="Acidity">acidity</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basicity" title="Basicity">basicity</a> of an <a href="/wiki/Aqueous_solution" title="Aqueous solution">aqueous solution</a>.  It is approximately the negative of the base 10 <a href="/wiki/Logarithm" title="Logarithm">logarithm</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Molar_concentration" title="Molar concentration">molar concentration</a>, measured in units of <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">moles</a> per liter, of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_ion" title="Hydrogen ion">hydrogen ions</a>. More precisely it is the negative of the base 10 logarithm of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Activity_(chemistry)" title="Activity (chemistry)">activity</a> of the hydrogen ion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bates_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bates-1">[1]</a></sup> Solutions with a pH less than 7 are acidic and solutions with a pH greater than 7 are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basic_(chemistry)" title="Basic (chemistry)">basic</a>. <a href="/wiki/Properties_of_water" title="Properties of water">Pure water</a> is neutral, at pH 7 (25 °C), being neither an acid nor a base. Contrary to popular belief, the pH value can be less than 0 or greater than 14 for very strong acids and bases respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Measurements of pH are important in <a href="/wiki/Agronomy" title="Agronomy">agronomy</a>, medicine, chemistry, water treatment, and many other applications.
</p><p>The pH scale is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traceable" title="Traceable">traceable</a> to a set of standard solutions whose pH is established by international agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-covington_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-covington-3">[3]</a></sup>
Primary pH standard values are determined using a <a href="/wiki/Galvanic_cell" title="Galvanic cell">concentration cell with transference</a>, by measuring the potential difference between a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogen_electrode" title="Hydrogen electrode">hydrogen electrode</a> and a standard electrode such as the <a href="/wiki/Silver_chloride_electrode" title="Silver chloride electrode">silver chloride electrode</a>.
The pH of aqueous solutions can be measured with a <a href="/wiki/Glass_electrode" title="Glass electrode">glass electrode</a> and a <a href="/wiki/PH_meter" title="PH meter">pH meter</a>, or an <a href="/wiki/PH_indicator" title="PH indicator">indicator</a>.
</p><p>There are three current theories used to describe <a href="/wiki/Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction" title="Acid–base reaction">acid–base reactions</a>: Arrhenius, Bronsted-Lowry and Lewis when determining pH.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>The concept of  pH was first introduced by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Danish_people" title="Danish people">Danish</a> chemist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S%C3%B8ren_Peder_Lauritz_S%C3%B8rensen" title="Søren Peder Lauritz Sørensen">Søren Peder Lauritz Sørensen</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Carlsberg_Laboratory" title="Carlsberg Laboratory">Carlsberg Laboratory</a> in 1909<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and revised to the modern pH in 1924 to accommodate definitions and measurements in terms of electrochemical cells. In the first papers, the notation had the "H" as a subscript to the lowercase "p", as so: p<sub>H</sub>.
</p><p>The exact meaning of the "p" in "pH" is disputed, but according to the Carlsberg Foundation, pH stands for "<a href="/wiki/Exponentiation" title="Exponentiation">power</a> of hydrogen".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sor_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sor-5">[5]</a></sup> It has also been suggested that the "p" stands for the <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> <i>Potenz</i><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (December 2017)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> (meaning "power"), others refer to <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> <i>puissance</i> (also meaning "power", based on the fact that the Carlsberg Laboratory was French-speaking). 
Another suggestion is that the "p" stands for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin terms</a> <i>pondus hydrogenii</i> (quantity of hydrogen),  <i>potentia hydrogenii</i> (capacity of hydrogen), or potential hydrogen. It is also suggested that Sørensen used the letters "p" and "q" (commonly paired letters in mathematics) simply to label the test solution (p) and the reference solution (q).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Currently in <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, the p stands for "decimal <a href="/wiki/Cologarithm" title="Cologarithm">cologarithm</a> of", and is also used in the term p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub>, used for <a href="/wiki/Acid_dissociation_constant" title="Acid dissociation constant">acid dissociation constants</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jens_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jens-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Bacteriologist <a href="/wiki/Alice_Catherine_Evans" title="Alice Catherine Evans">Alice C. Evans</a>, famed for her work's influence on <a href="/wiki/Dairy" title="Dairy">dairying</a> and <a href="/wiki/Food_safety" title="Food safety">food safety</a>, credited William Mansfield Clark and colleagues (of whom she was one) with developing pH measuring methods in the 1910s, which had a wide influence on laboratory and industrial use thereafter. In her memoir, she does not mention how much, or how little, Clark and colleagues knew about Sørensen's work a few years prior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evans-Memoirs_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Evans-Memoirs-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>10</span></sup> She said: 
</p>
<blockquote><p>In these studies [of bacterial metabolism] Dr. Clark's attention was directed to the effect of acid on the growth of bacteria. He found that it is the intensity of the acid in terms of hydrogen-ion concentration that affects their growth. But existing methods of measuring acidity determined the quantity, not the intensity, of the acid. Next, with his collaborators, Dr. Clark developed accurate methods for measuring hydrogen-ion concentration. These methods replaced the inaccurate titration method of determining acid content in use in biologic laboratories throughout the world. Also they were found to be applicable in many industrial and other processes in which they came into wide usage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Evans-Memoirs_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Evans-Memoirs-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>10</span></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>The first <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronic</a> method for measuring pH was invented by <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Orville_Beckman" title="Arnold Orville Beckman">Arnold Orville Beckman</a>, a professor at <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">California Institute of Technology</a> in 1934.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> It was in response to local citrus grower <a href="/wiki/Sunkist_Growers,_Incorporated" title="Sunkist Growers, Incorporated">Sunkist</a> that wanted a better method for quickly testing the pH of lemons they were picking from their nearby orchards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>

Commercial standard buffer solutions usually come with information on the value at 25 °C and a correction factor to be applied for other temperatures.

<h3><span id="p.5BH.5D"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="p[H]">p[H]</span></h3>

One way to do this, which has been used extensively, is to <a href="/wiki/Titration" title="Titration">titrate</a> a solution of known concentration of a strong acid with a solution of known concentration of strong alkaline in the presence of a relatively high concentration of background electrolyte. Since the concentrations of acid and alkaline are known, it is easy to calculate the concentration of hydrogen ions so that the measured potential can be correlated with concentrations. As it is constant, its value can be set to one by defining the <a href="/wiki/Standard_state" title="Standard state">standard state</a> as being the solution containing the background electrolyte. Thus, the effect of using this procedure is to make activity equal to the numerical value of concentration.

For instance, if one wishes to measure the pH of a seawater sample, the electrode should be calibrated in a solution resembling seawater in its chemical composition, as detailed below.
</p><p>The difference between p[H] and pH is quite small. It has been stated<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> that pH = p[H] + 0.04. It is common practice to use the term "pH" for both types of measurement.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="pH_indicators">pH indicators</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Universal_indicator" title="Universal indicator">Universal indicator</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Universal_indicator_paper.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1185" data-file-width="1371" height="259" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Universal_indicator_paper.jpg/300px-Universal_indicator_paper.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Universal_indicator_paper.jpg/450px-Universal_indicator_paper.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Universal_indicator_paper.jpg/600px-Universal_indicator_paper.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Universal_indicator_paper.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chart showing the variation of color of universal indicator paper with pH</div></div></div>
<p>Indicators may be used to measure pH, by making use of the fact that their color changes with pH. Visual comparison of the color of a test solution with a standard color chart provides a means to measure pH accurate to the nearest whole number. More precise measurements are possible if the color is measured spectrophotometrically, using a <a href="/wiki/Colorimeter_(chemistry)" title="Colorimeter (chemistry)">colorimeter</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectrophotometer" title="Spectrophotometer">spectrophotometer</a>.
<a href="/wiki/Universal_indicator" title="Universal indicator">Universal indicator</a> consists of a mixture of indicators such that there is a continuous color change from about pH 2 to pH 10. Universal indicator paper is made from absorbent paper that has been impregnated with universal indicator. Another method of measuring pH is using an electronic pH meter.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extremes_of_pH">Extremes of pH</span></h3>
<p>Measurement of pH below about 2.5 (ca. 0.003 <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mol</a> dm<sup>−3</sup> acid) and above about 10.5 (ca. 0.0003 mol dm<sup>−3</sup> alkaline) requires special procedures because, when using the glass electrode, the <a href="/wiki/Nernst_equation" title="Nernst equation">Nernst law</a> breaks down under those conditions. Various factors contribute to this. It cannot be assumed that <a href="/wiki/Liquid_junction_potential" title="Liquid junction potential">liquid junction potentials</a> are independent of pH.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feldman_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Feldman-14">[14]</a></sup> Also, extreme pH implies that the solution is concentrated, so electrode potentials are affected by <a href="/wiki/Ionic_strength" title="Ionic strength">ionic strength</a> variation. At high pH the glass electrode may be affected by "alkaline error", because the electrode becomes sensitive to the concentration of cations such as Na<sup>+</sup> and K<sup>+</sup> in the solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Specially constructed electrodes are available which partly overcome these problems.
</p><p>Runoff from mines or mine tailings can produce some very low pH values.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unified_absolute_pH_scale">Unified absolute pH scale</span></h3>
<p>The concept of "unified pH scale" has been developed on the basis of the absolute chemical potential of the proton. This model uses the <a href="/wiki/Lewis_acids_and_bases" title="Lewis acids and bases">Lewis acid–base</a> definition. This scale applies to liquids, gases and even solids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krossing_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Krossing-19">[19]</a></sup>
In 2010, a new "unified absolute pH scale" has been proposed that would allow various pH ranges across different solutions to use a common proton reference standard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span></h2>
<p>Pure water is neutral. When an <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acid</a> is dissolved in water, the pH will be less than 7 (25 °C). When a <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">base</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Alkali" title="Alkali">alkali</a>, is dissolved in water, the pH will be greater than 7. A solution of a strong acid, such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>, at concentration 1 mol dm<sup>−3</sup> has a pH of 0. A solution of a strong alkali, such as <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a>, at concentration 1 mol dm<sup>−3</sup>, has a pH of 14. Thus, measured pH values will lie mostly in the range 0 to 14, though negative pH values and values above 14 are entirely possible. Since pH is a logarithmic scale, a difference of one pH unit is equivalent to a tenfold difference in hydrogen ion concentration. <br/>
The pH of neutrality is not exactly 7 (25 °C), although this is a good approximation in most cases. Neutrality is defined as the condition where [H<sup>+</sup>] = [OH<sup>−</sup>] (or the activities are equal). Since <a href="/wiki/Self-ionization_of_water" title="Self-ionization of water">self-ionization of water</a> holds the product of these concentration [H<sup>+</sup>]×[OH<sup>−</sup>] = K<sub>w</sub>, it can be seen that at neutrality [H<sup>+</sup>] = [OH<sup>−</sup>] = <span class="nowrap">√<span style="border-top:1px solid; padding:0 0.1em;">K<sub>w</sub></span></span>, or pH = pK<sub>w</sub>/2. pK<sub>w</sub> is approximately 14 but depends on ionic strength and temperature, and so the pH of neutrality does also. Pure water and a solution of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">NaCl</a> in pure water are both neutral, since <a href="/wiki/Self-ionization_of_water" title="Self-ionization of water">dissociation of water</a> produces equal numbers of both ions. However the pH of the neutral NaCl solution will be slightly different from that of neutral pure water because the hydrogen and hydroxide ions' activity is dependent on <a href="/wiki/Ionic_strength" title="Ionic strength">ionic strength</a>, so K<sub>w</sub> varies with ionic strength.
</p><p>If pure water is exposed to air it becomes mildly acidic. This is because water absorbs <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> from the air, which is then slowly converted into <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> and hydrogen ions (essentially creating <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a>).
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="pH_in_nature">pH in nature</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lemon.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="848" data-file-width="1200" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Lemon.jpg/220px-Lemon.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Lemon.jpg/330px-Lemon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Lemon.jpg/440px-Lemon.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lemon.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lemon_juice" title="Lemon juice">Lemon juice</a> tastes sour because it contains 5% to 6% <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid" title="Citric acid">citric acid</a> and has a pH of 2.2. (high acidity)</div></div></div>
<p>pH-dependent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plant_pigment" title="Plant pigment">plant pigments</a> that can be used as <a href="/wiki/PH_indicator" title="PH indicator">pH indicators</a> occur in many plants, including <a href="/wiki/Hibiscus" title="Hibiscus">hibiscus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Red_cabbage" title="Red cabbage">red cabbage</a> (<a href="/wiki/Anthocyanin" title="Anthocyanin">anthocyanin</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Red_wine" title="Red wine">red wine</a>. The juice of <a href="/wiki/Citrus" title="Citrus">citrus</a> fruits is acidic mainly because it contains <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid" title="Citric acid">citric acid</a>. Other <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxylic acids</a> occur in many living systems. For example, <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactic acid</a> is produced by <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">muscle</a> activity. The state of protonation of <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a> derivatives, such as <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a>, is pH-dependent. The functioning of the oxygen-transport enzyme <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a> is affected by pH in a process known as the <a href="/wiki/Root_effect" title="Root effect">Root effect</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seawater">Seawater</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">Ocean acidification</a></div>
<p>The pH of <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a> is typically limited to a range between 7.5 and 8.4.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chester_Marine_Geochem_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chester_Marine_Geochem-22">[22]</a></sup> It plays an important role in the ocean's <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle#In_the_ocean" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a>, and there is evidence of ongoing <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a> caused by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_emission#Greenhouse_gas_emissions" title="Carbon dioxide emission">carbon dioxide emissions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raven05_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-raven05-23">[23]</a></sup> However, pH measurement is complicated by the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_property" title="Chemical property">chemical properties</a> of seawater, and several distinct pH scales exist in <a href="/wiki/Chemical_oceanography" title="Chemical oceanography">chemical oceanography</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zeebe_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-zeebe-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>As part of its <a href="/wiki/Operational_definition" title="Operational definition">operational definition</a> of the pH scale, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUPAC" title="IUPAC">IUPAC</a> defines a series of <a href="/wiki/Buffer_solution" title="Buffer solution">buffer solutions</a> across a range of pH values (often denoted with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Bureau_of_Standards" title="National Bureau of Standards">NBS</a> or <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">NIST</a> designation). These solutions have a relatively low <a href="/wiki/Ionic_strength" title="Ionic strength">ionic strength</a> (≈0.1) compared to that of seawater (≈0.7), and, as a consequence, are not recommended for use in characterizing the pH of seawater, since the ionic strength differences cause changes in <a href="/wiki/Standard_electrode_potential" title="Standard electrode potential">electrode potential</a>. To resolve this problem, an alternative series of buffers based on <a href="/wiki/Artificial_seawater" title="Artificial seawater">artificial seawater</a> was developed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> This new series resolves the problem of ionic strength differences between samples and the buffers, and the new pH scale is referred to as the 'total scale', often denoted as pH<sub>T</sub>. The total scale was defined using a medium containing <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfate</a> ions. These ions experience <a href="/wiki/Protonation" title="Protonation">protonation</a>, H<sup>+</sup> + SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span> ⇌ HSO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>, such that the total scale includes the effect of both <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> (free hydrogen ions) and hydrogen sulfate ions:
</p>
<dl><dd>[H<sup>+</sup>]<sub>T</sub> = [H<sup>+</sup>]<sub>F</sub> + [HSO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>]</dd></dl>
<p>An alternative scale, the 'free scale', often denoted 'pH<sub>F</sub>', omits this consideration and focuses solely on [H<sup>+</sup>]<sub>F</sub>, in principle making it a simpler representation of hydrogen ion concentration. Only [H<sup>+</sup>]<sub>T</sub> can be determined,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> therefore [H<sup>+</sup>]<sub>F</sub> must be estimated using the [SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>] and the stability constant of HSO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>, <span class="nowrap">K<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">*</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">S</sub></span></span>:
</p>
<dl><dd>[H<sup>+</sup>]<sub>F</sub> = [H<sup>+</sup>]<sub>T</sub> − [HSO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>] = [H<sup>+</sup>]<sub>T</sub> ( 1 + [SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>] / K<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">*</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">S</sub></span> )<sup>−1</sup></dd></dl>
<p>However, it is difficult to estimate K<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">*</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">S</sub></span> in seawater, limiting the utility of the otherwise more straightforward free scale.
</p><p>Another scale, known as the 'seawater scale', often denoted 'pH<sub>SWS</sub>', takes account of a further protonation relationship between hydrogen ions and <a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">fluoride</a> ions, H<sup>+</sup> + F<sup>−</sup> ⇌ HF. Resulting in the following expression for [H<sup>+</sup>]<sub>SWS</sub>:
</p>
<dl><dd>[H<sup>+</sup>]<sub>SWS</sub> = [H<sup>+</sup>]<sub>F</sub> + [HSO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>] + [HF]</dd></dl>
<p>However, the advantage of considering this additional complexity is dependent upon the abundance of fluoride in the medium. In seawater, for instance, sulfate ions occur at much greater concentrations (&gt;400 times) than those of fluoride. As a consequence, for most practical purposes, the difference between the total and seawater scales is very small.
</p><p>The following three equations summarise the three scales of pH:
</p>
<dl><dd>pH<sub>F</sub> = − log [H<sup>+</sup>]<sub>F</sub></dd>
<dd>pH<sub>T</sub> = − log ( [H<sup>+</sup>]<sub>F</sub> + [HSO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>] ) = − log [H<sup>+</sup>]<sub>T</sub></dd>
<dd>pH<sub>SWS</sub> = − log ( [H<sup>+</sup>]<sub>F</sub> + [HSO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>] + [HF] ) = − log [H<sup>+</sup>]<sub>SWS</sub></dd></dl>
<p>In practical terms, the three seawater pH scales differ in their values by up to 0.12 pH units, differences that are much larger than the accuracy of pH measurements typically required, in particular, in relation to the ocean's <a href="/wiki/Total_inorganic_carbon" title="Total inorganic carbon">carbonate system</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zeebe_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-zeebe-24">[24]</a></sup> Since it omits consideration of sulfate and fluoride ions, the free scale is significantly different from both the total and seawater scales. Because of the relative unimportance of the fluoride ion, the total and seawater scales differ only very slightly.
</p>

<p>The pH of different cellular compartments, <a href="/wiki/Body_fluid" title="Body fluid">body fluids</a>, and organs is usually tightly regulated in a process called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acid-base_homeostasis" title="Acid-base homeostasis">acid-base homeostasis</a>. The most common disorder in acid-base homeostasis is <a href="/wiki/Acidosis" title="Acidosis">acidosis</a>, which means an acid overload in the body, generally defined by pH falling below 7.35. <a href="/wiki/Alkalosis" title="Alkalosis">Alkalosis</a> is the opposite condition, with blood pH being excessively high.
</p><p>The pH of blood is usually slightly basic with a value of pH 7.365. This value is often referred to as physiological pH in biology and medicine. <a href="/wiki/Dental_plaque" title="Dental plaque">Plaque</a> can create a local acidic environment that can result in <a href="/wiki/Tooth_decay" title="Tooth decay">tooth decay</a> by demineralization. <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">Enzymes</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a> have an optimum pH range and can become inactivated or <a href="/wiki/Denaturation_(biochemistry)" title="Denaturation (biochemistry)">denatured</a> outside this range.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Calculations_of_pH">Calculations of pH</span></h2>
<p>The calculation of the pH of a solution containing acids and/or bases is an example of a chemical speciation calculation, that is, a mathematical procedure for calculating the concentrations of all chemical species that are present in the solution. The complexity of the procedure depends on the nature of the solution. For strong acids and bases no calculations are necessary except in extreme situations. The pH of a solution containing a weak acid requires the solution of a <a href="/wiki/Quadratic_equation" title="Quadratic equation">quadratic equation</a>. The pH of a solution containing a weak base may require the solution of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cubic_equation" title="Cubic equation">cubic equation</a>. The general case requires the solution of a set of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-linear" title="Non-linear">non-linear</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simultaneous_equation" title="Simultaneous equation">simultaneous equations</a>.
</p><p>A complicating factor is that water itself is a weak acid and a weak base (see <a href="/wiki/Amphoterism" title="Amphoterism">amphoterism</a>). It <a href="/wiki/Self-ionization_of_water" title="Self-ionization of water">dissociates</a> according to the equilibrium

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strong_acids_and_bases">Strong acids and bases</span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strong_acid" title="Strong acid">Strong acids</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strong_base" title="Strong base">bases</a> are compounds that, for practical purposes, are completely dissociated in water. Under normal circumstances this means that the concentration of hydrogen ions in acidic solution can be taken to be equal to the concentration of the acid. The pH is then equal to minus the logarithm of the concentration value. <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">Hydrochloric acid</a> (HCl) is an example of a strong acid. The pH of a 0.01M solution of HCl is equal to −log<sub>10</sub>(0.01), that is, pH = 2. <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">Sodium hydroxide</a>, NaOH, is an example of a strong base. The p[OH] value of a 0.01M solution of NaOH is equal to −log<sub>10</sub>(0.01), that is, p[OH] = 2. From the definition of p[OH] above, this means that the pH is equal to about 12. For solutions of sodium hydroxide at higher concentrations the self-ionization equilibrium must be taken into account.
</p><p>Self-ionization must also be considered when concentrations are extremely low. Consider, for example, a solution of hydrochloric acid at a concentration of 5×10<sup>−8</sup>M.  The simple procedure given above would suggest that it has a pH of 7.3. This is clearly wrong as an acid solution should have a pH of less than 7. Treating the system as a mixture of hydrochloric acid and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphoteric" title="Amphoteric">amphoteric</a> substance water, a pH of 6.89 results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Note that there are no approximations involved in these equations, except that each stability constant is defined as a quotient of concentrations, not activities. Much more complicated expressions are required if activities are to be used.
</p><p>There are 3 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-linear" title="Non-linear">non-linear</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simultaneous_equation" title="Simultaneous equation">simultaneous equations</a> in the three unknowns, [A], [B] and [H]. Because the equations are non-linear, and because concentrations may range over many powers of 10, the solution of these equations is not straightforward. However, many computer programs are available which can be used to perform these calculations. There may be more than three reagents. The calculation of hydrogen ion concentrations, using this formalism, is a key element in the <a href="/wiki/Determination_of_equilibrium_constants" title="Determination of equilibrium constants">determination of equilibrium constants</a> by potentiometric titration.
</p>
