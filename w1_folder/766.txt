<p>Consumption is a major concept in economics and is also studied in many other social sciences.
</p><p>Economists are particularly interested in the relationship between consumption and income, as modeled with the <i>consumption function</i>.
</p><p>Different schools of economists define <a href="/wiki/Production_(economics)" title="Production (economics)">production</a> and consumption differently. According to <a href="/wiki/Mainstream_economics" title="Mainstream economics">mainstream economists</a>, only the final purchase of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Good_(economics)" title="Good (economics)">goods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Service_(economics)" title="Service (economics)">services</a> by individuals constitutes consumption, while other types of expenditure — in particular, <a href="/wiki/Fixed_investment" title="Fixed investment">fixed investment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Intermediate_consumption" title="Intermediate consumption">intermediate consumption</a>, and government spending — are placed in separate categories (See <a href="/wiki/Consumer_choice" title="Consumer choice">consumer choice</a>). Other economists define consumption much more broadly, as the aggregate of all economic activity that does not entail the design, production and <a href="/wiki/Marketing" title="Marketing">marketing</a> of <a href="/wiki/Goods_and_services" title="Goods and services">goods and services</a> (e.g. the selection, adoption, use, disposal and recycling of goods and services).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consumption_function">Consumption function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consumption_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Consumption function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Consumption_function" title="Consumption function">Consumption function</a></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavioural_economics_and_consumption">Behavioural economics and consumption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consumption_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Behavioural economics and consumption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Keynesian <a href="/wiki/Consumption_function" title="Consumption function">consumption function</a> is also known as the <a href="/wiki/Absolute_income_hypothesis" title="Absolute income hypothesis">absolute income hypothesis</a>, as it only bases consumption on current income and ignores potential future income (or lack of).  Criticism of this assumption led to the development of <a href="/wiki/Milton_Friedman" title="Milton Friedman">Milton Friedman</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Permanent_income_hypothesis" title="Permanent income hypothesis">permanent income hypothesis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franco_Modigliani" title="Franco Modigliani">Franco Modigliani</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_cycle_hypothesis" title="Life cycle hypothesis">life cycle hypothesis</a>. More recent theoretical approaches are based on <a href="/wiki/Behavioral_economics" title="Behavioral economics">behavioral economics</a> and suggest that a number of behavioural principles can be taken as microeconomic foundations for a behaviourally-based aggregate consumption function.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consumption_and_household_production">Consumption and household production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consumption_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Consumption and household production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aggregate consumption is a component of <a href="/wiki/Aggregate_demand" title="Aggregate demand">aggregate demand</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Consumption is defined in part by comparison to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Production_theory_basics" title="Production theory basics">production</a>.
In the tradition of the Columbia School of Household <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">Economics</a>, also known as the <a href="/wiki/New_Home_Economics" title="New Home Economics">New Home Economics</a>, commercial consumption has to be analyzed in the context of household production. The opportunity cost of time affects the cost of home-produced substitutes and therefore demand for commercial goods and services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The elasticity of demand for consumption goods is also a function of who performs chores in households and how their spouses compensate them for opportunity costs of home production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Different schools of economists define <a href="/wiki/Production_(economics)" title="Production (economics)">production</a> and consumption differently. According to <a href="/wiki/Mainstream_economics" title="Mainstream economics">mainstream economists</a>, only the final purchase of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Good_(economics)" title="Good (economics)">goods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Service_(economics)" title="Service (economics)">services</a> by individuals constitutes consumption, while other types of expenditure — in particular, <a href="/wiki/Fixed_investment" title="Fixed investment">fixed investment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Intermediate_consumption" title="Intermediate consumption">intermediate consumption</a>, and government spending — are placed in separate categories (See <a href="/wiki/Consumer_choice" title="Consumer choice">consumer choice</a>). Other economists define consumption much more broadly, as the aggregate of all economic activity that does not entail the design, production and <a href="/wiki/Marketing" title="Marketing">marketing</a> of <a href="/wiki/Goods_and_services" title="Goods and services">goods and services</a> (e.g. the selection, adoption, use, disposal and recycling of goods and services).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Consumption can also be measured by a variety of different ways such as <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> in <a href="/wiki/Energy_economics" title="Energy economics">energy economics</a> metrics.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Old-age_spending">Old-age spending</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Consumption_(economics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Old-age spending">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Spending the Kids' Inheritance</i> (originally the title of a book on the subject by <a href="/wiki/Annie_Hulley" title="Annie Hulley">Annie Hulley</a>) and the acronyms SKI and SKI'ing refer to the growing number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retire" title="Retire">older people</a> in <a href="/wiki/Western_culture" title="Western culture">Western society</a> spending their <a href="/wiki/Money" title="Money">money</a> on <a href="/wiki/Travel" title="Travel">travel</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cars" title="Cars">cars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Property" title="Property">property</a>, in contrast to previous <a href="/wiki/Generation" title="Generation">generations</a> who tended to leave that money to their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Children" title="Children">children</a>.
</p><p><i>Die Broke</i> (from the book <i>Die Broke: A Radical Four-Part Financial Plan</i> by Stephen Pollan and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Levine_(economist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mark Levine (economist) (page does not exist)">Mark Levine</a>) is a similar idea.
</p>
