<p>The <b>International Maritime Organization</b> (<b>IMO</b>), known as the <b>Inter-Governmental Maritime Consultative Organization</b> (<b>IMCO</b>) until 1982,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-about-3">[3]</a></sup> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Specialised_agency_of_the_United_Nations" title="Specialised agency of the United Nations">specialised agency of the United Nations</a> responsible for regulating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ship_transport" title="Ship transport">shipping</a>.  The IMO was established following agreement at a UN conference held in <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a> in 1948<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and the IMO came into existence ten years later, meeting for the first time in 1959.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">[5]</a></sup> Headquartered in London, United Kingdom, the IMO currently has 174 Member States and three Associate Members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-about-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The IMO's primary purpose is to develop and maintain a comprehensive regulatory framework for shipping and its remit today includes safety, environmental concerns, legal matters, technical co-operation, maritime security and the efficiency of shipping. IMO is governed by an assembly of members and is financially administered by a council of members elected from the assembly. The work of IMO is conducted through five committees and these are supported by technical subcommittees. Other UN organisations may observe the proceedings of the IMO. Observer status is granted to qualified non-governmental organisations.
</p><p>IMO is supported by a permanent secretariat of employees who are representative of the organisation's members. The secretariat is composed of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secretary-General" title="Secretary-General">Secretary-General</a> who is periodically elected by the assembly, and various divisions such as those for marine safety, environmental protection and a conference section.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Maritime_Organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:International_Maritime_Organization_Headquarters.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3024" data-file-width="4032" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/International_Maritime_Organization_Headquarters.jpg/220px-International_Maritime_Organization_Headquarters.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/International_Maritime_Organization_Headquarters.jpg/330px-International_Maritime_Organization_Headquarters.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/International_Maritime_Organization_Headquarters.jpg/440px-International_Maritime_Organization_Headquarters.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:International_Maritime_Organization_Headquarters.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Headquarters of the IMO are located on Albert Embankment, <a href="/wiki/Lambeth" title="Lambeth">Lambeth</a>, London.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="SOLAS">SOLAS</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Maritime_Organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: SOLAS">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/SOLAS_Convention" title="SOLAS Convention">SOLAS Convention</a></div>
<p>Inter-Governmental Maritime Consultative Organization (IMCO) was formed in order to bring the regulation of the safety of shipping into an international framework, for which the creation of the United Nations provided an opportunity. Hitherto such international conventions had been initiated piecemeal, notably the Safety of Life at Sea Convention (SOLAS), first adopted in 1914 following the <i><a href="/wiki/RMS_Titanic" title="RMS Titanic">Titanic</a></i> disaster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-about_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-about-3">[3]</a></sup> IMCO's first task was to update that Convention; the resulting 1960 Convention was subsequently recast and updated in 1974 and it is that Convention that has been subsequently modified and updated to adapt to changes in safety requirements and technology.
</p><p>When IMCO began its operations in 1959 certain other pre-existing conventions were brought under its aegis, most notable the <a href="/wiki/International_Convention_for_the_Prevention_of_Pollution_of_the_Sea_by_Oil" title="International Convention for the Prevention of Pollution of the Sea by Oil">International Convention for the Prevention of Pollution of the Sea by Oil</a> (OILPOL) 1954. The first meetings of the newly formed IMCO were held in London in 1959.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMOHistoryInPics_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-IMOHistoryInPics-6">[6]</a></sup> Throughout its existence IMCO, later renamed the IMO in 1982, has continued to produce new and updated conventions across a wide range of maritime issues covering not only safety of life and marine pollution but also encompassing safe navigation, search and rescue, wreck removal, tonnage measurement, liability and compensation, ship recycling, the training and certification of seafarers, and piracy. More recently SOLAS has been amended to bring an increased focus on maritime security through the <a href="/wiki/International_Ship_and_Port_Facility_Security_Code" title="International Ship and Port Facility Security Code">International Ship and Port Facility Security (ISPS) Code</a>. The IMO has also increased its focus on smoke emissions from ships.
</p><p>In January 1959, IMO began to maintain and promote the 1954 OILPOL Convention. Under the guidance of IMO, the convention was amended in 1962, 1969, and 1971.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Torrey_Canyon">Torrey Canyon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Maritime_Organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Torrey Canyon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Torrey_Canyon_oil_spill" title="Torrey Canyon oil spill">Torrey Canyon oil spill</a></div>
<p>As oil trade and industry developed, many people in the industry began to recognise a need for further improvements in regards to oil pollution prevention at sea. This became increasingly apparent in 1967, when the tanker <i><a href="/wiki/SS_Torrey_Canyon" title="SS Torrey Canyon">Torrey Canyon</a></i> spilled 120,000 tons of crude oil when it ran aground entering the English Channel<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <i>Torrey Canyon</i> grounding was the largest oil pollution incident recorded up to that time. This incident prompted a series of new conventions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="International_Convention_for_the_Prevention_of_Pollution_by_Ship_73.2F78"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="International_Convention_for_the_Prevention_of_Pollution_by_Ship_73/78">International Convention for the Prevention of Pollution by Ship 73/78</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Maritime_Organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: International Convention for the Prevention of Pollution by Ship 73/78">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/MARPOL_73/78" title="MARPOL 73/78">MARPOL 73/78</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:IMO_Secretaries-General.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2340" data-file-width="3904" height="132" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/IMO_Secretaries-General.jpg/220px-IMO_Secretaries-General.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/IMO_Secretaries-General.jpg/330px-IMO_Secretaries-General.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/IMO_Secretaries-General.jpg/440px-IMO_Secretaries-General.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:IMO_Secretaries-General.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Current Secretary-General Kitack Lim (left), with predecessor Secretaries-General O'Neill, Mitropoulos and Sekimizu</div></div></div>
<p>IMO held an emergency session of its Council to deal with the need to readdress regulations pertaining to maritime pollution. In 1969, the IMO Assembly decided to host an international gathering in 1973 dedicated to this issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup> The goal at hand was to develop an international agreement for controlling general environmental contamination by ships when out at sea.
</p><p>During the next few years IMO brought to the forefront a series of measures designed to prevent large ship accidents and to minimise their effects.  It also detailed how to deal with the environmental threat caused by routine ship duties such as the cleaning of oil cargo tanks or the disposal of engine room wastes.  By tonnage, the aforementioned was a bigger problem than accidental pollution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The most significant thing to come out of this conference was the <a href="/wiki/MARPOL_73/78" title="MARPOL 73/78">International Convention for the Prevention of Pollution from Ships, 1973</a>.  It covers not only accidental and operational oil pollution but also different types of pollution by chemicals, goods in packaged form, sewage, garbage and air pollution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The original <a href="/wiki/MARPOL_73/78" title="MARPOL 73/78">MARPOL</a> was signed on 17 February 1973, but did not come into force due to lack of ratifications. The current convention is a combination of <a href="/wiki/MARPOL_73/78" title="MARPOL 73/78">1973 Convention</a> and the 1978 Protocol. It entered into force on 2 October 1983. As of May 2013, 152 states, representing 99.2 per cent of the world's shipping tonnage, are involved in the convention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1983 the IMO established the <a href="/wiki/World_Maritime_University" title="World Maritime University">World Maritime University</a> in Malmö, Sweden.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Headquarters">Headquarters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Maritime_Organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Headquarters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The IMO headquarters are located in a large purpose-built building facing the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Albert_Embankment" title="Albert Embankment">Albert Embankment</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Lambeth" title="Lambeth">Lambeth</a>, London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HistoryofIMO_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-HistoryofIMO-8">[8]</a></sup> The organisation moved into its new headquarters in late 1982, with the building being officially opened by <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Queen Elizabeth II</a> on 17 May 1983.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HistoryofIMO_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-HistoryofIMO-8">[8]</a></sup> The architects of the building were Douglass Marriott, Worby &amp; Robinson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MancHistory_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-MancHistory-9">[9]</a></sup> The front of the building is dominated by a seven-metre high, ten-tonne bronze sculpture of the bow of a ship, with a lone seafarer maintaining a look-out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MancHistory_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-MancHistory-9">[9]</a></sup> The previous headquarters of IMO were at 101 <a href="/wiki/Piccadilly" title="Piccadilly">Piccadilly</a> (now the home of the <a href="/wiki/Embassy_of_Japan,_London" title="Embassy of Japan, London">Embassy of Japan</a>), prior to that at 22 Berners Street in <a href="/wiki/Fitzrovia" title="Fitzrovia">Fitzrovia</a> and originally in <a href="/wiki/Chancery_Lane" title="Chancery Lane">Chancery Lane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMOHistoryInPics_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-IMOHistoryInPics-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Membership">Membership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Maritime_Organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Membership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:472px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:IMO_Participation.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="415" data-file-width="940" height="208" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/IMO_Participation.svg/470px-IMO_Participation.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/IMO_Participation.svg/705px-IMO_Participation.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/IMO_Participation.svg/940px-IMO_Participation.svg.png 2x" width="470"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:IMO_Participation.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>International Maritime Organization as of 2014: <div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #00AA00; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;"> </span> member states </div> <div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #00FF00; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;"> </span> associate members</div></div></div></div>
<p>To become a member of the IMO, a state ratifies a multilateral treaty known as the Convention on the International Maritime Organization. As of 2018, there are 173 member states of the IMO, which includes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_United_Nations_member_states" title="List of United Nations member states">172 of the UN member states</a> plus the <a href="/wiki/Cook_Islands" title="Cook Islands">Cook Islands</a>. The first state to ratify the convention was the United Kingdom in 1949. The most recent members to join were <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nauru" title="Nauru">Nauru</a>, which became IMO members in January and May 2018, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMO_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-IMO-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The three associate members of the IMO are the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>, Hong Kong and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macao" title="Macao">Macao</a>.
</p><p>Most UN member states that are not members of IMO are landlocked countries. These include Afghanistan, Andorra, Bhutan, Botswana, Burkina Faso, Burundi, Central African Republic, Chad, Kyrgyzstan, Laos, Lesotho, Liechtenstein, Mali, Niger, Rwanda, South Sudan, Swaziland, Tajikistan and Uzbekistan.  However, the Federated States of Micronesia, an island nation in the Pacific Ocean, is also a non-member.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Maritime_Organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The IMO consists of an Assembly, a Council and five main Committees: the Maritime Safety Committee; the Marine Environment Protection Committee; the Legal Committee; the Technical Co-operation Committee and the Facilitation Committee. A number of Sub-Committees support the work of the main technical committees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>IMO is the source of approximately 60 legal instruments that guide the regulatory development of its member states to improve safety at sea, facilitate trade among seafaring states and protect the maritime environment. The most well known is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Convention_for_the_Safety_of_Life_at_Sea" title="International Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea">International Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea</a> (SOLAS), as well as <a href="/wiki/International_Convention_on_Oil_Pollution_Preparedness,_Response_and_Co-operation" title="International Convention on Oil Pollution Preparedness, Response and Co-operation">International Convention on Oil Pollution Preparedness, Response and Co-operation</a> (OPRC). Others include the International Oil Pollution Compensation Funds (IOPC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iocp_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-iocp-12">[12]</a></sup> It also functions as a depository of yet to be ratified treaties, such as the International Convention on Liability and Compensation for Damage in Connection with the Carriage of Hazardous and Noxious Substances by Sea, 1996 (<a href="/wiki/HNS_Convention" title="HNS Convention">HNS Convention</a>) and Nairobi International Convention of Removal of Wrecks (2007).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Removal_of_Wrecks_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Removal_of_Wrecks-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>IMO regularly enacts regulations, which are broadly enforced by national and local maritime authorities in member countries, such as the <a href="/wiki/International_Regulations_for_Preventing_Collisions_at_Sea" title="International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea">International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea</a> (COLREG). The IMO has also enacted a <a href="/wiki/Port_State_Control" title="Port State Control">Port State Control</a> (PSC) authority, allowing domestic maritime authorities such as <a href="/wiki/Coast_guard" title="Coast guard">coast guards</a> to inspect foreign-flag ships calling at ports of the many port states. Memoranda of Understanding (protocols) were signed by some countries unifying Port State Control procedures among the signatories.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_issues">Current issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Maritime_Organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Current issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Recent initiatives at the IMO have included amendments to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Convention_for_the_Safety_of_Life_at_Sea" title="International Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea">SOLAS</a>, which upgraded <a href="/wiki/Fire_protection" title="Fire protection">fire protection</a> standards on <a href="/wiki/Passenger_ship" title="Passenger ship">passenger ships</a>, the <a href="/wiki/STCW_Convention" title="STCW Convention">International Convention on Standards of Training, Certification and Watchkeeping for Seafarers (STCW)</a> which establishes basic requirements on training, certification and watchkeeping for seafarers and to the Convention on the Prevention of Maritime Pollution (<a href="/wiki/MARPOL_73/78" title="MARPOL 73/78">MARPOL 73/78</a>), which required double hulls on all <a href="/wiki/Tanker_(ship)" title="Tanker (ship)">tankers</a>.
</p><p>In December 2002, new amendments to the 1974 SOLAS Convention were enacted. These amendments gave rise to the <a href="/wiki/International_Ship_and_Port_Facility_Security_Code" title="International Ship and Port Facility Security Code">International Ship and Port Facility Security (ISPS) Code</a>, which went into effect on 1 July 2004. The concept of the code is to provide layered and redundant defences against smuggling, terrorism, piracy, stowaways, etc. The ISPS Code required most ships and port facilities engaged in international trade to establish and maintain strict security procedures as specified in ship and port specific Ship Security Plans and Port Facility Security Plans.
</p><p>The IMO has a role in tackling international <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>. The First Intersessional Meeting of IMO's Working Group on Greenhouse Gas Emissions from Ships took place in Oslo, Norway (23–27 June 2008), tasked with developing the technical basis for the reduction mechanisms that may form part of a future IMO regime to control <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions from international shipping, and a draft of the actual reduction mechanisms themselves, for further consideration by IMO's Marine Environment Protection Committee (MEPC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The IMO participated in the <a href="/wiki/2015_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference">2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference</a> in Paris seeking to establish itself as the "appropriate international body to address greenhouse gas emissions from ships engaged in international trade".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Nonetheless, there has been widespread criticism of the IMO's relative inaction since the conclusion of the Paris conference, with the initial data-gathering step of a three-stage process to reduce maritime greenhouse emissions expected to last until 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The IMO has also taken action to mitigate the global effects of ballast water and sediment discharge, through the 2004 <a href="/wiki/Ballast_Water_Management_Convention" title="Ballast Water Management Convention">Ballast Water Management Convention</a>, which entered into force in September 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMO-BWM-media_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-IMO-BWM-media-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>The IMO is also responsible for publishing the <a href="/wiki/International_Code_of_Signals" title="International Code of Signals">International Code of Signals</a> for use between merchant and naval vessels. IMO has harmonised information available to seafarers and shore-side traffic services called <a href="/wiki/E-Navigation" title="E-Navigation">e-Navigation</a>. An e-Navigation strategy was ratified in 2005, and an implementation plan was developed through three IMO sub-committees. The plan was completed by 2014 and implemented in November of that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> IMO has also served as a key partner and enabler of US international and interagency efforts to establish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maritime_Domain_Awareness" title="Maritime Domain Awareness">Maritime Domain Awareness</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance_of_IMO">Governance of IMO</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Maritime_Organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Governance of IMO">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The governing body of the International Maritime Organization is the Assembly which meets every two years. In between Assembly sessions a Council, consisting of 40 Member States elected by the Assembly, acts as the governing body. The technical work of the International Maritime Organization is carried out by a series of Committees. The Secretariat consists of some 300 international civil servants headed by a Secretary-General.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marine.gov.uk_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-marine.gov.uk-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Secretary-General">Secretary-General</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Maritime_Organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Secretary-General">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The current Secretary-General is Kitack Lim (South Korea), elected for a four-year term at the 106th session of the IMO Council in June 2015 and at the 27th session of the IMO's Assembly in November 2015. His mandate started on 1 January 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewSG1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NewSG1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewSG2_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-NewSG2-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Previous Secretaries-General:
</p>
<ul><li>1959 Ove Nielsen (Denmark)</li>
<li>1961 William Graham (United Kingdom; acting, following death of Mr Nielsen)</li>
<li>1963 Jean Roullier (France)</li>
<li>1968 Colin Goad (United Kingdom)</li>
<li>1974 <a href="/wiki/Chandrika_Prasad_Srivastava" title="Chandrika Prasad Srivastava">Chandrika Prasad Srivastava</a> (India)</li>
<li>1990 William O'Neil (Canada)</li>
<li>2003 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Efthimios_E._Mitropoulos" title="Efthimios E. Mitropoulos">Efthimios E. Mitropoulos</a> (Greece)</li>
<li>2011 Koji Sekimizu (Japan)</li>
<li>2015 <a href="/wiki/Kitack_Lim" title="Kitack Lim">Kitack Lim</a> (South Korea)</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_committees">Technical committees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Maritime_Organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Technical committees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:International_Maritime_Organization_Committee.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3024" data-file-width="4032" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/International_Maritime_Organization_Committee.jpg/220px-International_Maritime_Organization_Committee.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/International_Maritime_Organization_Committee.jpg/330px-International_Maritime_Organization_Committee.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/International_Maritime_Organization_Committee.jpg/440px-International_Maritime_Organization_Committee.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:International_Maritime_Organization_Committee.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An image of the main hall assembly chamber, where the MSC and MEPC committees of the International Maritime Organization meet each year.</div></div></div>
<p>The technical work of the International Maritime Organisation is carried out by a series of Committees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMOStructure_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-IMOStructure-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marine.gov.uk_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-marine.gov.uk-19">[19]</a></sup> These include:
</p>
<ul><li>The Maritime Safety Committee (MSC)</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_environment" title="Marine environment">Marine environment</a> Protection Committee (MEPC)</li>
<li>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Legal_Committee&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Legal Committee (page does not exist)">Legal Committee</a></li>
<li>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Technical_Cooperation_Committee&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Technical Cooperation Committee (page does not exist)">Technical Cooperation Committee</a>, for <a href="/wiki/Capacity_building" title="Capacity building">capacity building</a></li>
<li>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Facilitation_Committee&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Facilitation Committee (page does not exist)">Facilitation Committee</a>, to simplify the documentation and formalities required in international shipping.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Maritime_Safety_Committee">Maritime Safety Committee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Maritime_Organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Maritime Safety Committee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It is regulated in the Article 28(a) of the Convention on the IMO:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>ARTICLE 28
</p><p>(a) The Maritime Safety Committee shall consider any matter within the scope of the Organization concerned with aids to navigation, construction and equipment of vessels, manning from a safety standpoint, rules for the prevention of collisions, handling of dangerous cargoes, maritime safety procedures and requirements, hydrographic information, log-books and navigational records, marine casualty investigation, salvage and rescue, and any other matters directly affecting maritime safety.
</p><p>(b) The Maritime Safety Committee shall provide machinery for performing any duties assigned to it by this Convention, the Assembly or the Council, or any duty within the scope of this Article which may be assigned to it by or under any other international instrument and accepted by the Organization.
</p><p>(c) Having regard to the provisions of Article 25, the Maritime Safety Committee, upon request by the Assembly or the Council or, if it deems such action useful in the interests of its own work, shall maintain such close relationship with other bodies as may further the purposes of the Organization
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The Maritime Safety Committee is the most senior of these and is the main Technical Committee; it oversees the work of its nine sub-committees and initiates new topics. One broad topic it deals with is the effect of the human element on <a href="/wiki/Casualty_insurance" title="Casualty insurance">casualties</a>; this work has been put to all of the sub-committees, but meanwhile, the Maritime Safety Committee has developed a code for the management of ships which will ensure that agreed operational procedures are in place and followed by the ship and shore-side staff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marine.gov.uk_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-marine.gov.uk-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sub-Committees">Sub-Committees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Maritime_Organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Sub-Committees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The MSC and MEPC are assisted in their work by a number of sub-committees which are open to all Member States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMOStructure_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-IMOStructure-20">[20]</a></sup> The committees are:
</p>
<ul><li>Sub-Committee on Human Element, Training and Watchkeeping (HTW)</li>
<li>Sub-Committee on Implementation of IMO Instruments (III)</li>
<li>Sub-Committee on Navigation, Communications and Search and Rescue (NCSR)</li>
<li>Sub-Committee on Pollution Prevention and Response (PPR)</li>
<li>Sub-Committee on Ship Design and Construction (SDC)</li>
<li>Sub-Committee on Ship Systems and Equipment (SSE)</li>
<li>Sub-Committee on Carriage of Cargoes and Containers (CCC)</li></ul>
<p>The names of the IMO sub-committees were changed in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMOStructure_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-IMOStructure-20">[20]</a></sup> Prior to 2013 there were nine Sub-Committees as follows:
</p>
<ul><li>Bulk Liquids and Gases (BLG)</li>
<li>Carriage of Dangerous Goods, Solid Cargoes and Containers(DSC)</li>
<li>Fire Protection (FP)</li>
<li>Radio-communications and Search and Rescue (COMSAR)</li>
<li>Safety of Navigation (NAV)</li>
<li>Ship Design and Equipment (DE)</li>
<li>Stability and Load Lines and Fishing Vessels Safety (SLF)</li>
<li>Standards of Training and Watchkeeping (STW)</li>
<li>Flag State Implementation (FSI)</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Resolutions">Resolutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Maritime_Organization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Resolutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Resolution MSC.255(84), of 16 May 2008, adopts the <i>Code of the International Standards and Recommended Practices for a Safety Investigation into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_casualty" title="Marine casualty">Marine casualty</a> or Marine Incident</i>.  It is also known as the <b>Casualty Investigation Code</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
