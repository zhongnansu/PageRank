<p><b>Kemper County</b> is a <a href="/wiki/County_(United_States)" title="County (United States)">county</a> located on the central eastern border of the <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>. As of the <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_Census" title="2010 United States Census">2010 census</a>, the population was 10,456.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-QF_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-QF-1">[1]</a></sup> Its <a href="/wiki/County_seat" title="County seat">county seat</a> is <a href="/wiki/De_Kalb,_Mississippi" title="De Kalb, Mississippi">De Kalb</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR6_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-GR6-2">[2]</a></sup> The county is named in honor of <a href="/wiki/Reuben_Kemper" title="Reuben Kemper">Reuben Kemper</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The county is part of the <a href="/wiki/Meridian,_Mississippi" title="Meridian, Mississippi">Meridian</a>, MS <a href="/wiki/Meridian_micropolitan_area" title="Meridian micropolitan area">Micropolitan Statistical Area</a>. In 2010 the Mississippi Public Service Commission approved construction of the <a href="/wiki/Kemper_Project" title="Kemper Project">Kemper Project</a>, designed to use "clean coal" to produce electricity for 23 counties in the eastern part of the state. As of February 2017, it was not completed and had cost overruns. It is designed as a model project to use gasification and carbon-capture technologies at this scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Senator" title="United States Senator">United States Senator</a> <a href="/wiki/John_C._Stennis" title="John C. Stennis">John C. Stennis</a>, who served from 1947 to 1988, was born in Kemper County. Another Kemper County native was <a href="/wiki/Texas_Ranger_Division" title="Texas Ranger Division">Texas Ranger</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain_Bill_McDonald" title="Captain Bill McDonald">Captain Bill McDonald</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/East_Mississippi_Community_College" title="East Mississippi Community College">East Mississippi Community College</a> is located in Kemper County in the town of <a href="/wiki/Scooba,_Mississippi" title="Scooba, Mississippi">Scooba</a>, at the junction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_45" title="US 45">US 45</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Highway_16" title="Mississippi Highway 16">Mississippi Highway 16</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kemper_County,_Mississippi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In this county the land was developed in the 19th century by white planters for cotton cultivation, based on the labor of enslaved African Americans. It continues to be largely rural. After the American Civil War and Reconstruction, racial violence increased as whites struggled to regain power over the majority population of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freedmen" title="Freedmen">freedmen</a>. In the period from 1877 to 1950, Kemper County had 24 documented <a href="/wiki/Lynching_in_the_United_States" title="Lynching in the United States">lynchings of African Americans</a>, third to <a href="/wiki/Carroll_County,_Mississippi" title="Carroll County, Mississippi">Hinds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leflore_County,_Mississippi" title="Leflore County, Mississippi">Leflore</a> counties, which had 29 and 48, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eji_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-eji-5">[5]</a></sup> This form of racial terrorism was at its height in the decades around the turn of the 20th century,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eji_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-eji-5">[5]</a></sup> which followed the state's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disfranchisement_after_Reconstruction_era" title="Disfranchisement after Reconstruction era">disenfranchisement of most blacks</a> in 1890 through creating barriers to voter registration. In 1890, blacks made up the majority of the county' population: 10,084 blacks to 7,845 whites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Beginning in late December 1906, there were several days of racial terror in the county. After violent incidents on the railroad between conductors and black passengers, whites attacked blacks at the rural towns of <a href="/wiki/Wahalak,_Mississippi" title="Wahalak, Mississippi">Wahalak</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scooba,_Mississippi" title="Scooba, Mississippi">Scooba</a>; by December 27, a total of 13 blacks had been killed in rioting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-riot_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-riot-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pens_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-pens-8">[8]</a></sup> The events started with a physical confrontation between a conductor and an African-American man on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mobile_%26_Ohio_Railroad" title="Mobile &amp; Ohio Railroad">Mobile &amp; Ohio Railroad</a> train. The conductor was cut, and he fatally shot two black men. George Simpson, another African American thought to be involved, escaped from the train. When captured in Wahalak by a posse, he killed a white constable and was quickly lynched by the other whites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-riot_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-riot-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>As reported by <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, 
</p>
<blockquote><p>Not satisfied with the punishment of this man, the whites immediately set out to strike terror into the negroes, who had been getting defiant of late. They found two sons of Simpson and lynched them, filling their bodies with bullets. Two other negroes who had behaved defiantly were treated in similar fashion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-riot_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-riot-7">[7]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Whites worried about blacks gathering to take revenge at Wahalak, where they had already been abused by lynchings, authorities called for state militia. Their commanding officer took his troops away from Wahalak while there was still unrest because he felt they were not being treated properly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-riot_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-riot-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>By the end of the day on December 26, white men in Scooba had killed another five black men. The county sheriff arrested several whites for these murders, and called for the state militia to go to Scooba. "All the men killed at Scooba today are said to be innocent of any crime, having been shot down merely as a matter of revenge by the rough whites."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-riot_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-riot-7">[7]</a></sup> There had been a conflict on another train, in which a black man mortally shot a conductor, George Harrison. The yardmaster shot and killed the African American. The rioting by whites in Scooba started after Harrison died.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-riot_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-riot-7">[7]</a></sup> Governor <a href="/wiki/James_K._Vardaman" title="James K. Vardaman">James K. Vardaman</a> went to Scooba with militia to establish control. He left a force of 20 there commanded by Adjutant General Fridge and returned to the state capital on the evening of December 27. That day the body of another murdered African-American man was found in the woods, bringing the total killed in Scooba to six.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pens_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-pens-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1934, three African-American suspects in Kemper County were repeatedly whipped in order to force them to confess to murder. In <i><a href="/wiki/Brown_v._Mississippi" title="Brown v. Mississippi">Brown v. Mississippi</a></i> (1936), the U.S. Supreme Court unanimously ruled such forced confessions violated the <a href="/wiki/Due_Process_Clause" title="Due Process Clause">Due Process Clause</a> of the Fourteenth Amendment, and were inadmissible at trial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The peak of population in the county was in 1930. Mechanization of agriculture decreased the need for farm labor. From 1940 to 1970, the population declined markedly, as may be seen on the table below, as people moved to other areas for work. This was also the period of the second wave of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Migration_(African_American)" title="Great Migration (African American)">Great Migration</a> of 5 million African Americans out of the South to the North and to the West Coast, where the defense industry had many jobs, beginning during World War II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brookings.edu_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-brookings.edu-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_Census" title="2010 United States Census">2010 United States Census</a>, there were 10,456 people residing in the county. 60.1% were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American" title="African American">Black or African American</a>, 35.3% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_American" title="White American">White</a>, 3.7% <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native American</a>, 0.1% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_American" title="Asian American">Asian</a>, 0.1% of some other race and 0.7% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multiracial_American" title="Multiracial American">of two or more races</a>. 0.5% were <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_and_Latino_Americans" title="Hispanic and Latino Americans">Hispanic or Latino</a> (of any race).
</p><p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR2_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-GR2-17">[17]</a></sup> of 2000, there were 10,453 people, 3,909 households, and 2,787 families residing in the county.  The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 14 people per square mile (5/km²).  There were 4,533 housing units at an average density of 6 per square mile (2/km²).  The racial makeup of the county was 58.13% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Black</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">African American</a>, 39.03% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">White</a>, 2.06% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Native American</a>, 0.08% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Asian</a>, 0.03% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 0.11% from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 0.57% from two or more races.  0.73% of the population were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">Latino</a> of any race.
</p><p>There were 3,909 households out of which 32.20% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 46.70% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 20.20% had a female householder with no husband present, and 28.70% were non-families. 26.40% of all households were made up of individuals and 12.60% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older.  The average household size was 2.57 and the average family size was 3.11.
</p><p>In the county, the population was spread out with 25.40% under the age of 18, 12.50% from 18 to 24, 25.20% from 25 to 44, 21.80% from 45 to 64, and 15.10% who were 65 years of age or older.  The median age was 35 years. For every 100 females there were 92.20 males.  For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 88.30 males.
</p><p>The median income for a household in the county was $23,998, and the median income for a family was $30,248. Males had a median income of $24,431 versus $18,199 for females. The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the county was $11,985.  About 21.20% of families and 26.00% of the population were below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_line" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including 35.30% of those under age 18 and 26.70% of those age 65 or over.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kemper_County,_Mississippi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kemper County is within the service area of the <a href="/wiki/East_Mississippi_Community_College" title="East Mississippi Community College">East Mississippi Community College</a> system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coursecat0709p3_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coursecat0709p3-18">[18]</a></sup> The main campus of EMCC, including the college system's administrative headquarters, is in the Scooba Campus in <a href="/wiki/Scooba,_Mississippi" title="Scooba, Mississippi">Scooba</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coursecat0709p8_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coursecat0709p8-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government">Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kemper_County,_Mississippi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The county is governed by a five-member elected Board of Supervisors, who are elected from single-member districts. The County Sheriff, Chancery Clerk, Circuit Clerk, and Tax Assessor are also elected to office.
</p><p>Kemper County generally votes for candidates of the Democratic Party; the Republican presidential nominee has won it only four times in the past century.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electric_power_plant">Electric power plant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kemper_County,_Mississippi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Electric power plant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kemper_Project" title="Kemper Project">Kemper Project</a></div>
<p>In 2010, the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Public_Service_Commission" title="Mississippi Public Service Commission">Mississippi Public Service Commission</a> approved the construction of a <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">lignite</a> <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> plant in Kemper County to be financed by electricity customers in twenty-three southeastern Mississippi counties being served by <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Power" title="Mississippi Power">Mississippi Power Company</a>. It is designed as a model project to use gasification and carbon-capture technologies at this scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The plant was strongly opposed by former <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a> State Chairman <a href="/wiki/Clarke_Reed" title="Clarke Reed">Clarke Reed</a> of <a href="/wiki/Greenville,_Mississippi" title="Greenville, Mississippi">Greenville</a>, who favored a less-expensive <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>-fueled plant. Reed called the project "...a horrible thing." He said it would be a political issue that could be used against Republicans for years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Kemper Project was scheduled to open in the third quarter of 2016, more than two years behind schedule. Its cost increased to $6.6 billion—three times original cost estimate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amy2_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Amy2-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABC_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-23">[23]</a></sup> As of February 2017, the project was still not in service, and the cost had increased to $7.1 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
