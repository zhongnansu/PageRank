<p><b>Calcium oxide</b> (<b>CaO</b>), commonly known as <b>quicklime</b> or <b>burnt lime</b>, is a widely used <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a>. It is a white, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caustic_(substance)" title="Caustic (substance)">caustic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alkali" title="Alkali">alkaline</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystalline</a> solid at room temperature. The broadly used term <i><a href="/wiki/Lime_(material)" title="Lime (material)">lime</a></i> connotes calcium-containing inorganic materials, in which carbonates, oxides and hydroxides of calcium, silicon, magnesium, aluminium, and iron predominate. By contrast, <i>quicklime</i>  specifically applies to the single chemical compound calcium oxide. Calcium oxide that survives processing without reacting in building products such as cement is called <b>free lime</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Quicklime is relatively inexpensive. Both it and a chemical derivative (<a href="/wiki/Calcium_hydroxide" title="Calcium hydroxide">calcium hydroxide</a>, of which quicklime is the <a href="/wiki/Base_anhydride" title="Base anhydride">base anhydride</a>) are important commodity chemicals.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Calcium oxide is usually made by the thermal decomposition of materials, such as <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> or <a href="/wiki/Seashell" title="Seashell">seashells</a>, that contain <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> (CaCO<sub>3</sub>; mineral <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a>) in a <a href="/wiki/Lime_kiln" title="Lime kiln">lime kiln</a>. This is accomplished by heating the material to above 825 °C (1,517 °F),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-merck_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-merck-6">[6]</a></sup> a process called <a href="/wiki/Calcination" title="Calcination">calcination</a> or <i>lime-burning</i>, to liberate a molecule of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>), leaving quicklime. 
</p>
<dl><dd>CaCO<sub>3</sub>(s) → CaO(s) + CO<sub>2</sub>(g)</dd></dl>
<p>The quicklime is not stable and, when cooled, will <a href="/wiki/Spontaneous_process" title="Spontaneous process">spontaneously</a> react with CO<sub>2</sub> from the air until, after enough time, it will be completely converted back to calcium carbonate unless slaked with water to set as <a href="/wiki/Lime_plaster" title="Lime plaster">lime plaster</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lime_mortar" title="Lime mortar">lime mortar</a>.
</p><p>Annual worldwide production of quicklime is around 283 million tonnes. China is by far the world's largest producer, with a total of around 170 million tonnes per year. The United States is the next largest, with around 20 million tonnes per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Approximately 1.8<span class="nowrap"> </span>t of limestone is required per 1.0<span class="nowrap"> </span>t of quicklime. Quicklime has a high affinity for water and is a more efficient <a href="/wiki/Desiccant" title="Desiccant">desiccant</a> than <a href="/wiki/Silica_gel" title="Silica gel">silica gel</a>. The reaction of quicklime with water is associated with an increase in volume by a factor of at least 2.5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-a-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>

<ul><li>The major use of quicklime is in the <a href="/wiki/Basic_oxygen_steelmaking" title="Basic oxygen steelmaking">basic oxygen steelmaking</a> (BOS) process. Its usage varies from about 30–50 kg/t of steel. The quicklime neutralizes the acidic oxides, <a href="/wiki/Silicon_dioxide" title="Silicon dioxide">SiO<sub>2</sub></a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aluminum_oxide" title="Aluminum oxide">Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></a>, and <a href="/wiki/Iron(III)_oxide" title="Iron(III) oxide">Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></a>, to produce a basic molten slag.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-a-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>Ground quicklime is used in the production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerated_concrete" title="Aerated concrete">aerated concrete</a> blocks, with densities of ca. 0.6–1.0<span class="nowrap"> </span>g/cm³.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-a-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>Quicklime and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrated_lime" title="Hydrated lime">hydrated lime</a> can considerably increase the load carrying capacity of clay-containing soils. They do this by reacting with finely divided silica and alumina to produce calcium silicates and aluminates, which possess cementing properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-a-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>Small quantities of quicklime are used in other processes; e.g., the production of glass, calcium aluminate cement, and organic chemicals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-a-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>Heat: Quicklime releases <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">Thermal</a> <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> by the formation of the hydrate, <a href="/wiki/Calcium_hydroxide" title="Calcium hydroxide">calcium hydroxide</a>, by the following equation:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-patent_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-patent-9">[9]</a></sup></li></ul>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>CaO (s) + H<sub>2</sub>O (l) ⇌ Ca(OH)<sub>2</sub> (aq) (ΔH<sub>r</sub> = −63.7<span class="nowrap"> </span>kJ/mol of CaO)</dd></dl></dd>
<dd>As it hydrates, an exothermic reaction results and the solid puffs up. The hydrate can be reconverted to quicklime by removing the water by heating it to redness to reverse the hydration reaction. One litre of water combines with approximately 3.1 kilograms (6.8 lb) of quicklime to give calcium hydroxide plus 3.54 <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">MJ</a> of energy. This process can be used to provide a convenient portable source of heat, as for on-the-spot food warming in a <a href="/wiki/Self-heating_can" title="Self-heating can">self-heating can</a>, cooking, and heating water without open flames. Several companies sell cooking kits using this heating method.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<ul><li>Light: When quicklime is heated to 2,400 °C (4,350 °F), it emits an intense glow. This form of illumination is known as a <a href="/wiki/Limelight" title="Limelight">limelight</a>, and was used broadly in theatrical productions prior to the invention of electric lighting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>Cement: Calcium oxide is a key ingredient for the process of making <a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">cement</a>.</li>
<li>As a cheap and widely available alkali. About 50% of the total quicklime production is converted to <a href="/wiki/Calcium_hydroxide" title="Calcium hydroxide">calcium hydroxide</a> before use. Both quick- and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrated_lime" title="Hydrated lime">hydrated lime</a> are used in the treatment of drinking water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-a-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>Petroleum industry: Water detection pastes contain a mix of calcium oxide and <a href="/wiki/Phenolphthalein" title="Phenolphthalein">phenolphthalein</a>. Should this paste come into contact with water in a fuel storage tank, the CaO reacts with the water to form calcium hydroxide. Calcium hydroxide has a high enough pH to turn the phenolphthalein a vivid purplish-pink color, thus indicating the presence of water.</li>
<li>Paper: Calcium oxide is used to regenerate sodium hydroxide from sodium carbonate in the chemical recovery at Kraft pulp mills.</li>
<li>Plaster: There is archeological evidence that <a href="/wiki/Pre-Pottery_Neolithic_B" title="Pre-Pottery Neolithic B">Pre-Pottery Neolithic B</a> humans used limestone-based <a href="/wiki/Plaster" title="Plaster">plaster</a> for flooring and other uses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Such <a href="/wiki/Lime-ash_floor" title="Lime-ash floor">Lime-ash floor</a> remained in use until the late nineteenth century.</li>
<li>Chemical or power production: Solid sprays or slurries of calcium oxide can be used to remove <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> from exhaust streams in a process called <a href="/wiki/Flue-gas_desulfurization" title="Flue-gas desulfurization">flue-gas desulfurization</a>.</li>
<li>Mining: <i>Compressed lime cartridges</i> exploit the exothermic properties of quicklime to break rock. A <a href="/wiki/Drilling_and_blasting#Procedure" title="Drilling and blasting">shot hole</a> is drilled into the rock in the usual way and a sealed cartridge of quicklime is placed within and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tamp" title="wikt:tamp">tamped</a>. A quantity of water is then injected into the cartridge and the resulting release of steam, together with the greater volume of the residual hydrated solid, breaks the rock apart. The method does not work if the rock is particularly hard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="As_a_weapon">As a weapon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: As a weapon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 80 BC, the Roman general <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sertorius" title="Sertorius">Sertorius</a> deployed choking clouds of caustic lime powder to defeat the Characitani of <a href="/wiki/Hispania" title="Hispania">Hispania</a>, who had taken refuge in inaccessible caves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> A similar dust was used in China to quell an armed peasant revolt in 178 AD, when <i>lime chariots</i> equipped with bellows blew limestone powder into the crowds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/David_Hume" title="David Hume">David Hume</a>, in his <a href="/wiki/The_History_of_England_(Hume)" title="The History of England (Hume)">History of England</a>, recounts that early in the reign of <a href="/wiki/Henry_III_of_England" title="Henry III of England">Henry III</a>, the English Navy destroyed an invading French fleet by blinding the enemy fleet with quicklime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Quicklime may have been used in medieval naval warfare - up to the use of "lime-mortars" to throw it at the enemy ships.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Quicklime is also thought to have been a component of <a href="/wiki/Greek_fire" title="Greek fire">Greek fire</a>. Upon contact with water, quicklime would increase its temperature above 150 °C and ignite the fuel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because of vigorous reaction of quicklime with water, quicklime causes severe irritation when inhaled or placed in contact with moist skin or eyes. Inhalation may cause coughing, sneezing, labored breathing. It may then evolve into burns with perforation of the nasal septum, abdominal pain, nausea and vomiting. Although quicklime is not considered a fire hazard, its reaction with water can release enough heat to ignite combustible materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
