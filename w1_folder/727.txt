<p><b>Hinterland</b> is a German word meaning "the land behind" (a <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">city</a>, a port, or similar).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The term's use in English was first documented by geographer <a href="/wiki/George_Chisholm_(geographer)" title="George Chisholm (geographer)">George Chisholm</a> in his <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Handbook_of_Commercial_Geography&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Handbook of Commercial Geography (page does not exist)">Handbook of Commercial Geography</a></i> (1888).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_usage">Etymology and usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hinterland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>hinterland</i> has several meanings. It may refer to:
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographic_region">Geographic region</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hinterland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geographic region">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>An area behind a coast or the shoreline of a river. Specifically, by the <i>doctrine of the hinterland,</i> the hinterland is the inland region lying behind a port and is claimed by the state that owns the coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kerr2008_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kerr2008-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>The area from which products are delivered to a port for shipping elsewhere is that port's hinterland.</li>
<li>The term is also used to refer to the area around a city or town.</li>
<li>More generally, <i>hinterland</i> can refer to the rural area economically tied to an urban <a href="/wiki/Catchment_area" title="Catchment area">catchment area</a>. The size of a hinterland can depend on geography, or on the ease, speed, and cost of transportation between the port and the hinterland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shipping" title="Shipping">shipping</a> usage, a port's hinterland is the area that it serves, both for imports and for exports.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/History_of_colonialism" title="History of colonialism">colonial</a> usage, the term was applied to the surrounding areas of former European colonies in Africa, which, although not part of the colony itself, were influenced by the colony. By analogous general economic usage, hinterland can refer to the area surrounding a service from which customers are attracted, also called the market area.</li>
<li>In German, <i>Hinterland</i> is sometimes used more generally to describe any sparsely populated area where the infrastructure is underdeveloped, although <i>Provinz</i> (analogous to <i>province</i>) is more common. In the United States, and particularly in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Midwest" title="American Midwest">American Midwest</a> (a region of German cultural heritage located far from ocean ports), it is this meaning and not the one relating to ports that predominates in common use. Analogous terms include  "the countryside", "the sticks", "the boonies", <i><a href="/wiki/Backcountry" title="Backcountry">backcountry</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Boondocks" title="Boondocks">boondocks</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/The_Bush_(Alaska)" title="The Bush (Alaska)">the Bush</a> (in Alaskan usage), and the <a href="/wiki/Outback" title="Outback">outback</a> (in Australian usage).</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breadth_of_knowledge">Breadth of knowledge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hinterland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Breadth of knowledge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A further sense in which the term is commonly applied, especially by British politicians, is in talking about an individual's depth and breadth of knowledge of other matters (or lack thereof), specifically of academic, artistic, cultural, literary and scientific pursuits. For instance, one could say, "X has a vast hinterland", or "Y has no hinterland". The spread of this usage is usually credited to <a href="/wiki/Denis_Healey" title="Denis Healey">Denis Healey</a> (British <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Defence" title="Secretary of State for Defence">Defence Secretary</a> 1964–1970, <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_the_Exchequer" title="Chancellor of the Exchequer">Chancellor of the Exchequer</a> 1974–1979) and his wife <a href="/wiki/Edna_Healey" title="Edna Healey">Edna Healey</a>, initially in the context of the supposed lack of hinterland of former <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
