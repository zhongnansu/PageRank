<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Carbon sink</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:422px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CO2_pump_hg.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="505" data-file-width="720" height="295" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/CO2_pump_hg.svg/420px-CO2_pump_hg.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/CO2_pump_hg.svg/630px-CO2_pump_hg.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/CO2_pump_hg.svg/840px-CO2_pump_hg.svg.png 2x" width="420"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CO2_pump_hg.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Air-sea exchange of CO<sub>2</sub></div></div></div>
<p>A <b>carbon sink</b> is a natural or artificial reservoir that accumulates and stores some <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>-containing chemical compound for an indefinite period. The process by which carbon sinks remove carbon dioxide (<a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a>) from the atmosphere is known as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">carbon sequestration</a>. Public awareness of the significance of CO<sub>2</sub> sinks has grown since passage of the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a>, which promotes their use as a form of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_offset" title="Carbon offset">carbon offset</a>. There are also different strategies used to enhance this process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General">General</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: General">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Increase in atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> means increase in global temperature. The amount of carbon dioxide varies naturally. The natural sinks are:
</p>
<ul><li>Trees serve as carbon sinks during growing seasons.</li>
<li>Absorption of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> by the oceans via <a href="/wiki/Solubility_pump" title="Solubility pump">physicochemical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biological_pump" title="Biological pump">biological</a> processes</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">Photosynthesis</a> by terrestrial plants</li></ul>
<p>Whilst the creation of artificial sinks has been discussed, no major artificial systems <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_removal" title="Carbon dioxide removal">remove carbon from the atmosphere</a> on a material scale.
</p><p>Carbon sources include:
</p>
<ul><li>Combustion of fossil fuels (coal, natural gas, and oil) by humans for energy and transportation <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li>Farmland (by animal respiration); there are proposals for improvements in farming practices to reverse this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kyoto_Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Kyoto Protocol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because growing vegetation takes in <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> allows <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change#Annex_I_countries" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">Annex I countries</a> with large areas of growing forests to issue <a href="/wiki/Removal_Units" title="Removal Units">Removal Units</a> to recognize the sequestration of carbon. The additional units make it easier for them to achieve their target emission levels. It is estimated that forests absorb between 10 and 20 <a href="/wiki/Ton" title="Ton">tons</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> per <a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">hectare</a> each year, through photosynthetic conversion into <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lignin" title="Lignin">lignin</a>, and wooden <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>. While this has been well documented for temperate forests and plantations, the <a href="/wiki/Fauna" title="Fauna">fauna</a> of the tropical forests place some limitations for such global estimates.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Some countries seek to trade emission rights in carbon emission markets, purchasing the unused carbon emission allowances of other countries. If overall limits on greenhouse gas emission are put into place, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cap_and_trade" title="Cap and trade">cap and trade</a> market mechanisms are purported to find cost-effective ways to reduce emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  There is as yet no <a href="/wiki/Carbon_audit_regime" title="Carbon audit regime">carbon audit regime</a> for all such markets globally, and none is specified in the Kyoto Protocol.  National carbon emissions are self-declared.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Development_Mechanism" title="Clean Development Mechanism">Clean Development Mechanism</a>, only <a href="/wiki/Afforestation" title="Afforestation">afforestation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reforestation" title="Reforestation">reforestation</a> are eligible to produce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Certified_emission_reduction" title="Certified emission reduction">certified emission reductions</a> (CERs) in the first commitment period of the Kyoto Protocol (2008–2012). Forest conservation activities or activities avoiding <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, which would result in emission reduction through the conservation of existing carbon stocks, are not eligible at this time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Also, agricultural carbon sequestration is not possible yet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Storage_in_terrestrial_and_marine_environments">Storage in terrestrial and marine environments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Storage in terrestrial and marine environments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soils">Soils</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Soils">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soils" title="Soils">Soils</a> represent a short to long-term carbon storage medium, and contain more carbon than all terrestrial vegetation and the atmosphere combined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BatjesGeoderma2016_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-BatjesGeoderma2016-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Plant_litter" title="Plant litter">Plant litter</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> including <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a> accumulates as <a href="/wiki/Soil_organic_matter" title="Soil organic matter">organic matter</a> in soils, and is degraded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_weathering" title="Chemical weathering">chemical weathering</a> and biological <a href="/wiki/Biodegradation" title="Biodegradation">degradation</a>.  More recalcitrant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_material" title="Organic material">organic</a> <a href="/wiki/Biopolymer" title="Biopolymer">carbon polymers</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hemi-cellulose" title="Hemi-cellulose">hemi-cellulose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lignin" title="Lignin">lignin</a>, aliphatic compounds, waxes and <a href="/wiki/Terpenoid" title="Terpenoid">terpenoids</a> are collectively retained as <a href="/wiki/Humus" title="Humus">humus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>  Organic matter tends to accumulate in litter and soils of colder regions such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boreal_forest" title="Boreal forest">boreal forests</a> of North America and the <a href="/wiki/Taiga" title="Taiga">Taiga</a> of <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leaf_litter" title="Leaf litter">Leaf litter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Humus" title="Humus">humus</a> are rapidly oxidized and poorly retained in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sub-tropical" title="Sub-tropical">sub-tropical</a> and tropical <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> conditions due to high temperatures and extensive leaching by rainfall. Areas where <a href="/wiki/Shifting_cultivation" title="Shifting cultivation">shifting cultivation</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slash_and_burn" title="Slash and burn">slash and burn</a> agriculture are practiced are generally only fertile for 2–3 years before they are abandoned.  These tropical jungles are similar to coral reefs in that they are highly efficient at conserving and circulating necessary nutrients, which explains their lushness in a nutrient desert.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  Much <a href="/wiki/Soil_carbon" title="Soil carbon">organic carbon</a> retained in many agricultural areas worldwide has been severely depleted due to <a href="/wiki/Intensive_farming" title="Intensive farming">intensive farming</a> practices]].
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">Grasslands</a> contribute to <a href="/wiki/Soil_organic_matter" title="Soil organic matter">soil organic matter</a>, stored mainly in their extensive fibrous root mats. Due in part to the climatic conditions of these regions (e.g. cooler temperatures and semi-arid to arid conditions), these soils can accumulate significant quantities of organic matter. This can vary based on rainfall, the length of the winter season, and the frequency of naturally occurring lightning-induced <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">grass-fires</a>.  While these fires release carbon dioxide, they improve the quality of the grasslands overall, in turn increasing the amount of carbon retained in the humic material.  They also deposit carbon directly to the soil in the form of <a href="/wiki/Biochar" title="Biochar">char</a> that does not significantly degrade back to carbon dioxide.
</p><p>Forest fires release absorbed carbon back into the atmosphere,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> as does deforestation due to rapidly increased oxidation of soil organic matter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Organic matter in <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a> bogs undergoes slow <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anaerobic_decomposition" title="Anaerobic decomposition">anaerobic decomposition</a> below the surface.  This process is slow enough that in many cases the bog grows rapidly and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_fixation" title="Carbon fixation">fixes</a> more carbon from the atmosphere than is released.  Over time, the peat grows deeper.  Peat bogs hold approximately one-quarter of the carbon stored in land plants and soils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Under some conditions, forests and peat bogs may become sources of CO<sub>2</sub>, such as when a forest is flooded by the construction of a hydroelectric dam. Unless the forests and peat are harvested before flooding, the rotting vegetation is a source of CO<sub>2</sub> and <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> comparable in magnitude to the amount of carbon released by a fossil-fuel powered plant of equivalent power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regenerative_agriculture">Regenerative agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Regenerative agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Biosequestration" title="Biosequestration">Biosequestration</a></div>
<p>Current agricultural practices lead to carbon loss from soils. It has been suggested that improved farming practices could return the soils to being a carbon sink. Present worldwide practises of overgrazing are substantially reducing many grasslands' performance as carbon sinks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/The_Rodale_Institute" title="The Rodale Institute">The Rodale Institute</a> says that <a href="/wiki/Regenerative_agriculture" title="Regenerative agriculture">regenerative agriculture</a>, if practiced on the planet’s 3.6 billion tillable acres, could sequester up to 40% of current CO<sub>2</sub> emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> They claim that agricultural carbon sequestration has the potential to mitigate global warming. When using biologically based <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Regenerative_practices&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Regenerative practices (page does not exist)">regenerative practices</a>, this dramatic benefit can be accomplished with no decrease in yields or farmer profits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Organically managed soils can convert carbon dioxide from a greenhouse gas into a food-producing asset.
</p><p>In 2006, U.S. carbon dioxide emissions, largely from fossil fuel combustion, were estimated at nearly 6.5 billion tons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> If a 2,000 (lb/ac)/year sequestration rate was achieved on all 434,000,000 acres (1,760,000 km<sup>2</sup>) of cropland in the United States, nearly 1.6 billion tons of carbon dioxide would be sequestered per year, mitigating close to one quarter of the country's total fossil fuel emissions.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceans">Oceans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Oceans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Presently, oceans are CO<sub>2</sub> sinks, and represent the largest active carbon sink on Earth, absorbing more than a quarter of the carbon dioxide that humans put into the air.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EarthInst_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-EarthInst-19">[19]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Solubility_pump" title="Solubility pump">solubility pump</a> is the primary mechanism responsible for the CO2 absorption by the oceans.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Biological_pump" title="Biological pump">biological pump</a> plays a negligible role, because of the limitation to pump by ambient light and nutrients required by the <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a> that ultimately drive it. <a href="/wiki/Total_inorganic_carbon" title="Total inorganic carbon">Total inorganic carbon</a> is not believed to limit <a href="/wiki/Primary_production" title="Primary production">primary production</a> in the oceans, so its increasing availability in the ocean does not directly affect production (the situation on land is different, since enhanced atmospheric levels of <a href="/wiki/CO2_fertilization_effect" title="CO2 fertilization effect">CO<sub>2</sub> essentially "fertilize"</a> land plant growth to some threshold). However, <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a> by invading anthropogenic CO<sub>2</sub> may affect the biological pump by negatively impacting <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcifying</a> organisms such as <a href="/wiki/Coccolithophore" title="Coccolithophore">coccolithophores</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foraminiferans" title="Foraminiferans">foraminiferans</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pteropod" title="Pteropod">pteropods</a>. <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">Climate change</a> may also affect the biological pump in the future by warming and <a href="/wiki/Stratification_(water)" title="Stratification (water)">stratifying</a> the surface ocean, thus reducing the supply of limiting nutrients to surface waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2008 study found that CO<sub>2</sub> could potentially increase primary productivity, particularly in eel grasses in coastal and estuarine habitats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>In January 2009, the <a href="/wiki/Monterey_Bay_Aquarium_Research_Institute" title="Monterey Bay Aquarium Research Institute">Monterey Bay Aquarium Research Institute</a> and the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> announced a joint study to determine whether the ocean off the California coast was serving as a carbon source or a carbon sink.  Principal instrumentation for the study will be self-contained CO<sub>2</sub> monitors placed on <a href="/wiki/Buoy" title="Buoy">buoys</a> in the ocean.  They will measure the partial pressure of CO<sub>2</sub> in the ocean and the atmosphere just above the water surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2009, Science Daily reported that the Southern Indian Ocean is becoming less effective at absorbing carbon dioxide due to changes to the region's climate which include higher wind speeds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>On longer timescales, oceans may be both sources and sinks – during <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice ages</a> CO<sub>2</sub> levels decrease to ≈180 ppmv, and much of this is believed to be stored in the oceans. As ice ages end, CO<sub>2</sub> is released from the oceans and CO<sub>2</sub> levels during previous interglacials have been around ≈280 ppmv. This role as a sink for CO<sub>2</sub> is driven by two processes, the <a href="/wiki/Solubility_pump" title="Solubility pump">solubility pump</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Biological_pump" title="Biological pump">biological pump</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>  The former is primarily a function of differential CO<sub>2</sub> solubility in <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a>, while the latter is the sum of a series of biological processes that transport carbon (in <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inorganic_chemistry_of_carbon" title="Inorganic chemistry of carbon">inorganic</a> forms) from the surface <a href="/wiki/Photic_zone" title="Photic zone">euphotic zone</a> to the ocean's interior.  A small fraction of the organic carbon transported by the biological pump to the <a href="/wiki/Seabed" title="Seabed">seafloor</a> is buried in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anoxic_sea_water" title="Anoxic sea water">anoxic</a> conditions under sediments and ultimately forms <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">oil</a> and natural gas.
</p><p>At the end of glacials with sea level rapidly rising, corals tend to grow slower due to increased ocean temperature as seen on the Showtime series "Years of Living Dangerously".  The calcium carbonate from which coral skeletons are made is just over 60% carbon dioxide.  If we postulate that coral reefs were eroded down to the glacial sea level, then coral reefs have grown 120m upward since the end of the recent glacial.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Enhancing_natural_sequestration">Enhancing natural sequestration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Enhancing natural sequestration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forests">Forests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Forests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Biosequestration" title="Biosequestration">Biosequestration</a></div>
<p>Forests can be carbon stores,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and they are carbon dioxide sinks when they are increasing in density or area. In <a href="/wiki/Boreal_forest_of_Canada" title="Boreal forest of Canada">Canada's boreal forests</a> as much as 80% of the total carbon is stored in the soils as dead organic matter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfs.nrcan.gc.ca_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-cfs.nrcan.gc.ca-27">[27]</a></sup> A 40-year study of African, Asian, and South American <a href="/wiki/Tropical_forest" title="Tropical forest">tropical forests</a> by the University of Leeds, shows tropical forests absorb about 18% of all carbon dioxide added by fossil fuels. Truly mature tropical forests, by definition, grow rapidly as each tree produces at least 10 new trees each year. Based on studies of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FAO" title="FAO">FAO</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNEP" title="UNEP">UNEP</a> it has been estimated that Asian forests absorb about 5 tonnes of carbon dioxide per hectare each year. The global cooling effect of carbon sequestration by forests is partially counterbalanced in that reforestation can decrease the reflection of sunlight (<a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a>). Mid-to-high latitude forests have a much lower <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> during snow seasons than flat ground, thus contributing to warming. Modeling that compares the effects of albedo differences between forests and grasslands suggests that expanding the land area of forests in temperate zones offers only a temporary cooling benefit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States in 2004 (the most recent year for which EPA statistics<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> are available), forests sequestered 10.6% (637 MegaTonnes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa1_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-epa1-33">[33]</a></sup>) of the carbon dioxide released in the United States by the combustion of fossil fuels (coal, oil and natural gas; 5657 MegaTonnes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa2_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-epa2-34">[34]</a></sup>).  Urban trees sequestered another 1.5% (88 MegaTonnes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa1_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-epa1-33">[33]</a></sup>).  To further reduce U.S. carbon dioxide emissions by 7%, as stipulated by the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a>, would require the planting of "an area the size of Texas [8% of the area of Brazil] every 30 years".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_offset" title="Carbon offset">Carbon offset</a> programs are planting millions of fast-growing trees per year to reforest tropical lands, for as little as $0.10 per tree; over their typical 40-year lifetime, one million of these trees will fix 1 to 2 MegaTonnes of carbon dioxide.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  In Canada, reducing timber harvesting would have very little impact on carbon dioxide emissions because of the combination of harvest and stored carbon in manufactured wood products along with the regrowth of the harvested forests. Additionally, the amount of carbon released from harvesting is small compared to the amount of carbon lost each year to forest fires and other natural disturbances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cfs.nrcan.gc.ca_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-cfs.nrcan.gc.ca-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> concluded that "a sustainable forest management strategy aimed at maintaining or increasing forest carbon stocks, while producing an annual sustained yield of timber fibre or energy from the forest, will generate the largest sustained mitigation benefit".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Sustainable management practices keep forests growing at a higher rate over a potentially longer period of time, thus providing net sequestration benefits in addition to those of unmanaged forests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Life expectancy of forests varies throughout the world, influenced by tree species, site conditions and natural disturbance patterns.  In some forests carbon may be stored for centuries, while in other forests carbon is released with frequent stand replacing fires. Forests that are harvested prior to stand replacing events allow for the retention  of carbon in manufactured forest products such as lumber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>  However, only a portion of the carbon removed from logged forests ends up as durable goods and buildings.  The remainder ends up as sawmill by-products such as pulp, paper and pallets, which often end with incineration (resulting in carbon release into the atmosphere) at the end of their lifecycle. For instance, of the 1,692 MegaTonnes of carbon harvested from forests in Oregon and Washington (U.S) from 1900 to 1992, only 23% is in long-term storage in forest products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceans_2">Oceans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Oceans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Iron_fertilization" title="Iron fertilization">Iron fertilization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_weathering" title="Enhanced weathering">Enhanced weathering</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_nourishment" title="Ocean nourishment">Ocean nourishment</a></div>
<p>One way to increase the carbon sequestration efficiency of the oceans is to add micrometre-sized iron particles in the form of either <a href="/wiki/Hematite" title="Hematite">hematite</a> (iron oxide) or <a href="/wiki/Melanterite" title="Melanterite">melanterite</a> (iron sulfate) to certain regions of the ocean. This has the effect of stimulating growth of <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a>.  Iron is an important nutrient for <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a>, usually made available via upwelling along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_shelves" title="Continental shelves">continental shelves</a>, inflows from rivers and streams, as well as deposition of dust suspended in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">atmosphere</a>. Natural sources of ocean iron have been declining in recent decades, contributing to an overall decline in ocean productivity (NASA, 2003).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  Yet in the presence of iron nutrients plankton populations quickly grow, or 'bloom', expanding the base of <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> productivity throughout the region and removing significant quantities of CO<sub>2</sub> from the atmosphere via <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>.  A test in 2002 in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a> around <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> suggests that between 10,000 and 100,000 carbon atoms are sunk for each iron atom added to the water.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  More recent work in Germany (2005) suggests that any biomass carbon in the oceans, whether exported to depth or recycled in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euphotic_zone" title="Euphotic zone">euphotic zone</a>, represents long-term storage of carbon.  This means that application of iron nutrients in select parts of the oceans, at appropriate scales, could have the combined effect of restoring ocean productivity while at the same time mitigating the effects of human caused emissions of carbon dioxide to the atmosphere.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Because the effect of periodic small scale phytoplankton blooms on ocean ecosystems is unclear, more studies would be helpful.  Phytoplankton have a complex effect on cloud formation via the release of substances such as <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfide" title="Dimethyl sulfide">dimethyl sulfide</a> (DMS) that are converted to sulfate aerosols in the atmosphere, providing <a href="/wiki/Cloud_condensation_nuclei" title="Cloud condensation nuclei">cloud condensation nuclei</a>, or CCN.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> But the effect of small scale plankton blooms on overall DMS production is unknown.
</p><p>Other nutrients such as nitrates, phosphates, and silica as well as iron may cause ocean fertilization.  There has been some speculation that using pulses of fertilization (around 20 days in length) may be more effective at getting carbon to ocean floor than sustained fertilization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>There is some controversy over seeding the oceans with iron however, due to the potential for increased toxic phytoplankton growth (e.g. "<a href="/wiki/Red_tide" title="Red tide">red tide</a>"), declining water quality due to overgrowth, and increasing anoxia in areas harming other sea-life such as zooplankton, fish, coral, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soils_2">Soils</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Soils">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since the 1850s, a large proportion of the world's grasslands have been tilled and converted to croplands, allowing the rapid oxidation of large quantities of soil organic carbon.  However, in the United States in 2004 (the most recent year for which EPA statistics are available), agricultural soils including pasture land sequestered 0.8% (46 teragrams<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa1_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-epa1-33">[33]</a></sup>) as much carbon as was released in the United States by the combustion of fossil fuels (5988 teragrams<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa2_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-epa2-34">[34]</a></sup>).  The annual amount of this sequestration has been gradually increasing since 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-epa1_33-3"><a href="#cite_note-epa1-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>Methods that significantly enhance carbon sequestration in soil include <a href="/wiki/No-till_farming" title="No-till farming">no-till farming</a>, residue mulching, <a href="/wiki/Cover_crop" title="Cover crop">cover cropping</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Crop_rotation" title="Crop rotation">crop rotation</a>, all of which are more widely used in <a href="/wiki/Organic_farming" title="Organic farming">organic farming</a> than in conventional farming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>  Because only 5% of US farmland currently uses no-till and residue mulching, there is a large potential for carbon sequestration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Conversion to pastureland, particularly with good management of grazing, can sequester even more carbon in the soil.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Terra_preta" title="Terra preta">Terra preta</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a>, high-carbon soil, is also being investigated as a sequestration mechanism.
By <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">pyrolysing</a> biomass, about half of its carbon can be reduced to <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a>, which can persist in the soil for centuries, and makes a useful soil amendment, especially in tropical soils (<i><a href="/wiki/Biochar" title="Biochar">biochar</a></i> or <i>agrichar</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abc.net.au/catalyst/s2012892_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-abc.net.au/catalyst/s2012892-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Savanna">Savanna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Savanna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Controlled_burn" title="Controlled burn">Controlled burns</a> on far north Australian <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savannas</a> can result in an overall carbon sink. One working example is the West Arnhem Fire Management Agreement, started to bring "strategic fire management across 28,000 km² of Western Arnhem Land". Deliberately starting controlled burns early in the dry season results in a mosaic of burnt and unburnt country which reduces the area of burning compared with stronger, late dry season fires. In the early dry season there are higher moisture levels, cooler temperatures, and lighter wind than later in the dry season; fires tend to go out overnight. Early controlled burns also results in a smaller proportion of the grass and tree biomass being burnt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-savanna.ntu.edu.au/arnhem_fire_proj_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-savanna.ntu.edu.au/arnhem_fire_proj-49">[49]</a></sup> Emission reductions of 256,000 tonnes of CO<sub>2</sub> have been made as of 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-savanna.ntu.edu.au/Eureka_arnhem_fire_proj_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-savanna.ntu.edu.au/Eureka_arnhem_fire_proj-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Artificial_sequestration">Artificial sequestration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Artificial sequestration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For carbon to be sequestered artificially (i.e. not using the natural processes of the carbon cycle) it must first be captured, <i>or</i> it must be significantly delayed or prevented from being re-released into the atmosphere (by combustion, decay, etc.) from an existing carbon-rich material, by being incorporated into an enduring usage (such as in construction). Thereafter it can be passively stored <i>or</i> remain productively utilized over time in a variety of ways.
</p><p>For example, upon harvesting, wood (as a carbon-rich material) can be immediately burned or otherwise serve as a fuel, returning its carbon to the atmosphere, <i>or</i> it can be incorporated into construction or a range of other durable products, thus sequestering its carbon over years or even centuries.
</p><p>Indeed, a very carefully designed and durable, energy-efficient and energy-capturing building has the potential to sequester (in its carbon-rich construction materials), as much as or more carbon than was released by the acquisition and incorporation of all its materials and than will be released by building-function "energy-imports" during the structure's (potentially multi-century) existence.  Such a structure might be termed "carbon neutral" or even "carbon negative". Building construction and operation (electricity usage, heating, etc.) are estimated to contribute nearly <i>half</i> of the annual human-caused carbon additions to the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural-gas" title="Natural-gas">Natural-gas</a> purification plants often already have to remove carbon dioxide, either to avoid <a href="/wiki/Dry_ice" title="Dry ice">dry ice</a> clogging gas tankers or to prevent carbon-dioxide concentrations exceeding the 3% maximum permitted on the natural-gas distribution grid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>Beyond this, one of the most likely early applications of carbon capture is the capture of carbon dioxide from <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">flue gases</a> at <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">power stations</a> (in the case of coal, this <a href="/wiki/Coal_pollution_mitigation" title="Coal pollution mitigation">coal pollution mitigation</a> is sometimes known as "clean coal"). A typical new 1000 MW <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal-fired_power_station" title="Coal-fired power station">coal-fired power station</a> produces around 6 million tons of carbon dioxide annually. Adding carbon capture to existing plants can add significantly to the costs of energy production; scrubbing costs aside, a 1000 MW coal plant will require the storage of about 50 million barrels (7,900,000 m<sup>3</sup>) of carbon dioxide a year. However, scrubbing is relatively affordable when added to new plants based on <a href="/wiki/Coal_gasification" title="Coal gasification">coal gasification</a> technology, where it is estimated to raise energy costs for households in the United States using only coal-fired electricity sources from 10 cents per kW·h to 12 cents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_capture">Carbon capture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Carbon capture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">Carbon capture and storage</a></div>
<p>Currently, capture of carbon dioxide is performed on a large scale by absorption of carbon dioxide onto various <a href="/wiki/Amine_gas_treating" title="Amine gas treating">amine-based solvents</a>. Other techniques are currently being investigated, such as <a href="/wiki/Pressure_swing_adsorption" title="Pressure swing adsorption">pressure swing adsorption</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Temperature_swing_adsorption&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Temperature swing adsorption (page does not exist)">temperature swing adsorption</a>, <a href="/wiki/Membrane_gas_separation" title="Membrane gas separation">gas separation membranes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Distillation#Industrial_distillation" title="Distillation">cryogenics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">flue capture</a>.
</p><p>In coal-fired power stations, the main alternatives to retrofitting amine-based absorbers to existing power stations are two new technologies: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integrated_Gasification_Combined_Cycle" title="Integrated Gasification Combined Cycle">coal gasification combined-cycle</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxy-fuel_combustion" title="Oxy-fuel combustion">oxy-fuel combustion</a>. Gasification first produces a "<a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">syngas</a>" primarily of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>, which is burned, with carbon dioxide filtered from the flue gas. Oxy-fuel combustion burns the coal in <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> instead of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air#Composition" title="Air">air</a>, producing only carbon dioxide and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_vapour" title="Water vapour">water vapour</a>, which are relatively easily separated. Some of the combustion products must be returned to the combustion chamber, either before or after separation, otherwise the temperatures would be too high for the turbine.
</p><p>Another long-term option is carbon capture directly from the air using <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxides</a>. The air would literally be scrubbed of its CO<sub>2</sub> content.  This idea offers an alternative to non-<a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">carbon-based fuels</a> for the transportation sector.
</p><p>Examples of carbon sequestration at coal plants include converting carbon from smokestacks into baking soda,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> and algae-based carbon capture, circumventing storage by converting algae into fuel or feed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceans_3">Oceans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Oceans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another proposed form of carbon sequestration in the ocean is direct injection.  In this method, carbon dioxide is pumped directly into the water at depth, and expected to form "lakes" of liquid CO<sub>2</sub> at the bottom. Experiments carried out in moderate to deep waters (350–3600 m) indicate that the liquid CO<sub>2</sub> reacts to form solid CO<sub>2</sub> <a href="/wiki/Clathrate_hydrate" title="Clathrate hydrate">clathrate hydrates</a>, which gradually dissolve in the surrounding waters.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>This method, too, has potentially dangerous environmental consequences.  The carbon dioxide does react with the water to form <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a>, H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>; however, most (as much as 99%) remains as dissolved molecular CO<sub>2</sub>. The equilibrium would no doubt be quite different under the high pressure conditions in the deep ocean. In addition, if deep-sea bacterial <a href="/wiki/Methanogen" title="Methanogen">methanogens</a> that reduce carbon dioxide were to encounter the carbon dioxide sinks, levels of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> gas may increase, leading to the generation of an even worse greenhouse gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
The resulting environmental effects on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benthic" title="Benthic">benthic</a> life forms of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bathypelagic" title="Bathypelagic">bathypelagic</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abyssopelagic" title="Abyssopelagic">abyssopelagic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hadopelagic" title="Hadopelagic">hadopelagic</a> zones are unknown. Even though life appears to be rather sparse in the deep ocean basins, energy and chemical effects in these deep basins could have far-reaching implications. Much more work is needed here to define the extent of the potential problems.
</p><p>Carbon storage in or under oceans may not be compatible with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_Prevention_of_Marine_Pollution_by_Dumping_of_Wastes_and_Other_Matter" title="Convention on the Prevention of Marine Pollution by Dumping of Wastes and Other Matter">Convention on the Prevention of Marine Pollution by Dumping of Wastes and Other Matter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>An additional method of long-term ocean-based sequestration is to gather <a href="/wiki/Crop_residue" title="Crop residue">crop residue</a> such as corn stalks or excess hay into large weighted bales of biomass and deposit it in the <a href="/wiki/Alluvial_fan" title="Alluvial fan">alluvial fan</a> areas of the deep <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_basin" title="Ocean basin">ocean basin</a>.  Dropping these residues in alluvial fans would cause the residues to be quickly buried in silt on the sea floor, sequestering the biomass for very long time spans.  Alluvial fans exist in all of the world's oceans and seas where river deltas fall off the edge of the <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_Delta" title="Mississippi Delta">Mississippi alluvial fan</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">gulf of Mexico</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Nile_Delta" title="Nile Delta">Nile alluvial fan</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>. A downside, however, would be an increase in aerobic bacteria growth due to the introduction of biomass, leading to more competition for oxygen resources in the deep sea, similar to the <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_minimum_zone" title="Oxygen minimum zone">oxygen minimum zone</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geological_sequestration">Geological sequestration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Geological sequestration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The  method of  <i>geo-sequestration</i> or <i>geological storage</i> involves injecting carbon dioxide directly into underground geological formations.  Declining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_field" title="Oil field">oil fields</a>, saline <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifers</a>, and unminable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_seam" title="Coal seam">coal seams</a> have been suggested as storage sites.  Caverns and old mines that are commonly used to store natural gas are not considered, because of a lack of storage safety.
</p><p>CO<sub>2</sub> has been injected into declining oil fields for more than 40 years, to increase oil recovery.  This option is attractive because the storage costs are offset by the sale of additional oil that is recovered. Typically, 10–15% additional recovery of the original oil in place is possible. Further benefits are the existing infrastructure and the geophysical and geological information about the oil field that is available from the oil exploration. Another benefit of injecting CO<sub>2</sub> into Oil fields is that CO<sub>2</sub> is soluble in oil.  Dissolving CO<sub>2</sub> in oil lowers the viscosity of the oil and reduces its interfacial tension which increases the oils mobility. All oil fields have a geological barrier preventing upward migration of oil. As most oil and gas has been in place for millions to tens of millions of years, depleted oil and gas reservoirs can contain carbon dioxide for millennia. Identified possible problems are the many 'leak' opportunities provided by old oil wells, the need for high injection pressures and acidification which can damage the geological barrier. Other disadvantages of old oil fields are their limited geographic distribution and depths, which require high injection pressures for sequestration.  Below a depth of about 1000 m, carbon dioxide is injected as a supercritical fluid, a material with the density of a liquid, but the viscosity and diffusivity of a gas.
Unminable coal seams can be used to store CO<sub>2</sub>, because CO<sub>2</sub> absorbs to the coal surface, ensuring safe long-term storage.  In the process it releases methane that was previously adsorbed to the coal surface and that may be recovered.  Again the sale of the methane can be used to offset the cost of the CO<sub>2</sub> storage. Release or burning of methane would of course at least partially offset the obtained sequestration result – except when the gas is allowed to escape into the atmosphere in significant quantities: methane has a higher <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_potential" title="Global warming potential">global warming potential</a> than CO<sub>2</sub>.
</p><p>Saline aquifers contain highly mineralized brines and have so far been considered of no benefit to humans except in a few cases where they have been used for the storage of chemical waste.  Their advantages include a large potential storage volume and relatively common occurrence reducing the distance over which CO<sub>2</sub> has to be transported. The major disadvantage of saline aquifers is that relatively little is known about them compared to oil fields. Another disadvantage of saline aquifers is that as the salinity of the water increases, less CO<sub>2</sub> can be dissolved into aqueous solution. To keep the cost of storage acceptable the geophysical exploration may be limited, resulting in larger uncertainty about the structure of a given aquifer. Unlike storage in oil fields or coal beds, no side product will offset the storage cost. Leakage of CO<sub>2</sub> back into the atmosphere may be a problem in saline-aquifer storage.  However, current research shows that several <i>trapping mechanisms</i> immobilize the CO<sub>2</sub> underground, reducing the risk of leakage.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>A major research project examining the geological sequestration of carbon dioxide is currently being performed at an oil field at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weyburn,_Saskatchewan" title="Weyburn, Saskatchewan">Weyburn</a> in south-eastern <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>, Norway's <a href="/wiki/Equinor" title="Equinor">Equinor</a> natural-gas platform <a href="/wiki/Sleipner_A" title="Sleipner A">Sleipner</a> strips carbon dioxide out of the natural gas with amine solvents and disposes of this carbon dioxide by geological sequestration. Sleipner reduces emissions of carbon dioxide by approximately one million tonnes a year. The cost of geological sequestration is minor relative to the overall running costs. As of April 2005, <a href="/wiki/BP" title="BP">BP</a> is considering a trial of large-scale sequestration of carbon dioxide stripped from power plant emissions in the <a href="/wiki/Miller_oilfield" title="Miller oilfield">Miller oilfield</a> as its reserves are depleted.
</p><p>In October 2007, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Economic_Geology" title="Bureau of Economic Geology">Bureau of Economic Geology</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_University_of_Texas_at_Austin" title="The University of Texas at Austin">The University of Texas at Austin</a> received a 10-year, $38 million subcontract to conduct the first intensively monitored, long-term project in the United States studying the feasibility of injecting a large volume of CO<sub>2</sub> for underground storage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> The project is a research program of the <a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20080719035649/http://www.sseb.org/currentprograms/cpa_cmi.htm" rel="nofollow">Southeast Regional Carbon Sequestration Partnership (SECARB)</a>, funded by the <a href="/wiki/National_Energy_Technology_Laboratory" title="National Energy Technology Laboratory">National Energy Technology Laboratory</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">U.S. Department of Energy (DOE)</a>. The SECARB partnership will demonstrate CO<sub>2</sub> injection rate and storage capacity in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tuscaloosa-Woodbine&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tuscaloosa-Woodbine (page does not exist)">Tuscaloosa-Woodbine</a> geologic system that stretches from Texas to Florida. Beginning in fall 2007, the project will inject CO<sub>2</sub> at the rate of one million tons<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness"><span title="This information is too vague. (February 2009)">vague</span></a></i>]</sup> per year, for up to 1.5 years, into brine up to 10,000 feet (3,000 m) below the land surface near the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cranfield_oil_field&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cranfield oil field (page does not exist)">Cranfield oil field</a> about 15 miles (24 km) east of <a href="/wiki/Natchez,_Mississippi" title="Natchez, Mississippi">Natchez, Mississippi</a>. Experimental equipment will measure the ability of the subsurface to accept and retain CO<sub>2</sub>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mineral_sequestration">Mineral sequestration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Mineral sequestration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mineral sequestration aims to trap carbon in the form of solid <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonate</a> salts. This process occurs slowly in nature and is responsible for the deposition and accumulation of <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> over geologic time. <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">Carbonic acid</a> in groundwater slowly reacts with complex <a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">silicates</a> to dissolve <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alkali" title="Alkali">alkalis</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">silica</a> and leave a residue of <a href="/wiki/Clay_minerals" title="Clay minerals">clay minerals</a>. The dissolved calcium and magnesium react with <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> to precipitate calcium and magnesium carbonates, a process that organisms use to make shells. When the organisms die, their shells are deposited as sediment and eventually turn into limestone. Limestones have accumulated over billions of years of geologic time and contain much of Earth's carbon. Ongoing research aims to speed up similar reactions involving alkali carbonates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>Several serpentinite deposits are being investigated as potentially large scale CO<sub>2</sub> storage sinks such as those found in NSW, Australia, where the first mineral carbonation pilot plant project is underway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> Beneficial re-use of magnesium carbonate from this process could provide feedstock for new products developed for the built environment and agriculture without returning the carbon into the atmosphere and so acting as a carbon sink.
</p><p>One proposed reaction is that of the olivine-rich rock <a href="/wiki/Dunite" title="Dunite">dunite</a>, or its hydrated equivalent <a href="/wiki/Serpentinite" title="Serpentinite">serpentinite</a> with carbon dioxide to form the carbonate mineral <a href="/wiki/Magnesite" title="Magnesite">magnesite</a>, plus silica and iron oxide (<a href="/wiki/Magnetite" title="Magnetite">magnetite</a>).
</p><p>Serpentinite sequestration is favored because of the non-toxic and stable nature of magnesium carbonate. The ideal reactions involve the magnesium endmember components of the <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">olivine</a> (reaction 1) or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serpentine_group" title="Serpentine group">serpentine</a> (reaction 2), the latter derived from earlier olivine by hydration and silicification (reaction 3). The presence of iron in the olivine or serpentine reduces the efficiency of sequestration, since the iron components of these minerals break down to iron oxide and silica (reaction 4).
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Serpentinite_reactions">Serpentinite reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Serpentinite reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><b>Reaction 1</b><br/>
<i>Mg-olivine  + carbon dioxide → magnesite + silica + water</i>
</p>
<dl><dd>Mg<sub>2</sub>SiO<sub>4</sub> + 2CO<sub>2</sub> → 2MgCO<sub>3</sub> + SiO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p><b>Reaction 2</b><br/>
<i>Serpentine + carbon dioxide → magnesite  + silica + water</i>
</p>
<dl><dd>Mg<sub>3</sub>[Si<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>(OH)<sub>4</sub>] + 3CO<sub>2</sub> →  3MgCO<sub>3</sub> + 2SiO<sub>2</sub> + 2H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p><b>Reaction 3</b><br/>
<i>Mg-olivine  + water + silica → serpentine </i>
</p>
<dl><dd>3Mg<sub>2</sub>SiO<sub>4</sub> + 2SiO<sub>2</sub> + 4H<sub>2</sub>O → 2Mg<sub>3</sub>[Si<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>(OH)<sub>4</sub>]</dd></dl>
<p><b>Reaction 4</b><br/>
<i>Fe-olivine  + water → magnetite + silica + hydrogen </i>
</p>
<dl><dd>3Fe<sub>2</sub>SiO<sub>4</sub> + 2H<sub>2</sub>O → 2Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> + 3SiO<sub>2</sub> + 2H<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Zeolitic_imidazolate_frameworks">Zeolitic imidazolate frameworks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Zeolitic imidazolate frameworks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zeolitic_imidazolate_frameworks" title="Zeolitic imidazolate frameworks">Zeolitic imidazolate frameworks</a></div>
<p>Zeolitic imidazolate frameworks is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal-organic_framework" title="Metal-organic framework">metal-organic framework</a> carbon dioxide sink which could be used to keep industrial emissions of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> out of the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trends_in_sink_performance">Trends in sink performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_sink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Trends in sink performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One study in 2009 found that the fraction of fossil-fuel emissions absorbed by the oceans may have declined by up to 10% since 2000, indicating oceanic sequestration may be sublinear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EarthInst_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-EarthInst-19">[19]</a></sup> However, another study by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wolfgang_Knorr&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wolfgang Knorr (page does not exist)">Wolfgang Knorr</a> indicated that the fraction of CO<sub>2</sub> absorbed by carbon sinks has not changed since 1850.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p>
