<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Halogenation</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Fluorination" redirects here. For the addition of fluoride to drinking water, see <a href="/wiki/Water_fluoridation" title="Water fluoridation">Water fluoridation</a>.</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For the addition of chlorine, hypochlorite, etc. to drinking water, see <a href="/wiki/Water_chlorination" title="Water chlorination">Water chlorination</a>.</div>
<p><b>Halogenation</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reaction</a> that involves the addition of one or more <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogens</a> to a compound or material. The pathway and <a href="/wiki/Stoichiometry" title="Stoichiometry">stoichiometry</a> of halogenation depends on the structural features and functional groups of the organic substrate, as well as on the specific halogen. Inorganic compounds such as metals also undergo halogenation.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organic_chemistry">Organic chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogenation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Organic chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Halogenation_by_reaction_type">Halogenation by reaction type</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogenation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Halogenation by reaction type">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several pathways exist for the halogenation of organic compounds, including <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Free_halogenation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Free halogenation (page does not exist)">free halogenation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ketone_halogenation" title="Ketone halogenation">ketone halogenation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrophilic_halogenation" title="Electrophilic halogenation">electrophilic halogenation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Halogen_addition_reaction" title="Halogen addition reaction">halogen addition reaction</a>. The structure of the substrate is one factor that determines the pathway.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Free_radical_halogenation">Free radical halogenation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogenation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Free radical halogenation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Saturated hydrocarbons typically do not add halogens but undergo free radical halogenation, involving substitution of hydrogen atoms by halogen. The regiochemistry of the halogenation of alkanes is usually determined by the relative weakness of the available C–H bonds. The preference for reaction at tertiary and secondary positions results from greater stability of the corresponding free radicals and the transition state leading to them. Free radical halogenation is used for the industrial production of chlorinated methanes:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>CH<sub>4</sub> + Cl<sub>2</sub> → CH<sub>3</sub>Cl + HCl</dd></dl>
<p>Rearrangement often accompany such free radical reactions.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Addition_of_halogens_to_alkenes_and_alkynes">Addition of halogens to alkenes and alkynes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogenation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Addition of halogens to alkenes and alkynes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Unsaturated compounds, especially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkenes" title="Alkenes">alkenes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkynes" title="Alkynes">alkynes</a>, add halogens:
</p>
<dl><dd>RCH=CHR′ + X<sub>2</sub> → RCHX–CHXR′</dd></dl>
<p>The addition of halogens to alkenes proceeds via intermediate <a href="/wiki/Halonium_ion" title="Halonium ion">halonium ions</a>. In special cases, such intermediates have been isolated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Biadamantylidene-bromonium-ion-from-xtal-1994-2D-skeletal.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="712" data-file-width="1100" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Biadamantylidene-bromonium-ion-from-xtal-1994-2D-skeletal.png/170px-Biadamantylidene-bromonium-ion-from-xtal-1994-2D-skeletal.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Biadamantylidene-bromonium-ion-from-xtal-1994-2D-skeletal.png/255px-Biadamantylidene-bromonium-ion-from-xtal-1994-2D-skeletal.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Biadamantylidene-bromonium-ion-from-xtal-1994-2D-skeletal.png/340px-Biadamantylidene-bromonium-ion-from-xtal-1994-2D-skeletal.png 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Biadamantylidene-bromonium-ion-from-xtal-1994-2D-skeletal.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Structure of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bromonium_ion" title="Bromonium ion">bromonium ion</a></div></div></div></dd></dl>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Halogenation_of_aromatic_compounds">Halogenation of aromatic compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogenation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Halogenation of aromatic compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Aromatic compounds are subject to electrophilic halogenation:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>RC<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub> + X<sub>2</sub> → HX + RC<sub>6</sub>H<sub>4</sub>X</dd></dl>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_halogenation_methods">Other halogenation methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogenation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other halogenation methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Hunsdiecker_reaction" title="Hunsdiecker reaction">Hunsdiecker reaction</a>, from carboxylic acids are converted to the chain-shortened halide. The carboxylic acid is first converted to its silver salt, which is then oxidized with halogen:
</p>
<dl><dd>RCO<sub>2</sub>Ag + Br<sub>2</sub> → RBr + CO<sub>2</sub> + AgBr</dd></dl>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sandmeyer_reaction" title="Sandmeyer reaction">Sandmeyer reaction</a> is used to give aryl halides from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diazonium_salt" title="Diazonium salt">diazonium salts</a>, which are obtained from <a href="/wiki/Aniline" title="Aniline">anilines</a>.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Hell%E2%80%93Volhard%E2%80%93Zelinsky_halogenation" title="Hell–Volhard–Zelinsky halogenation">Hell–Volhard–Zelinsky halogenation</a>, carboxylic acids are alpha-halogenated.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Oxychlorination" title="Oxychlorination">oxychlorination</a>, the combination of hydrogen chloride and oxygen serves as the equivalent of chlorine, as illustrated by this route to dichloroethane:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 HCl + CH<sub>2</sub>=CH<sub>2</sub> + ​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> O<sub>2</sub> → ClCH<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>Cl + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Halogenation_by_halogen_type">Halogenation by halogen type</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogenation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Halogenation by halogen type">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The facility of halogenation is influenced by the halogen. <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">Fluorine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> are more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrophilic" title="Electrophilic">electrophilic</a> and are more aggressive halogenating agents. <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">Bromine</a> is a weaker halogenating agent than both fluorine and chlorine, while <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a> is the least reactive of them all. The facility of dehydrohalogenation follows the reverse trend: iodine is most easily removed from organic compounds, and organofluorine compounds are highly stable. 
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fluorination">Fluorination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogenation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Fluorination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Organic compounds, saturated and unsaturated alike, react readily, usually explosively, with fluorine. Fluorination with elemental fluorine (F<sub>2</sub>) requires highly specialised conditions and apparatus. Many commercially important organic compounds are fluorinated electrochemically using <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">hydrogen fluoride</a> as the source of fluorine. The method is called <a href="/wiki/Electrochemical_fluorination" title="Electrochemical fluorination">electrochemical fluorination</a>. Aside from F<sub>2</sub> and its electrochemically generated equivalent, a variety of fluorinating <a href="/wiki/Reagent" title="Reagent">reagents</a> are known such as <a href="/wiki/Xenon_difluoride" title="Xenon difluoride">xenon difluoride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cobalt(III)_fluoride" title="Cobalt(III) fluoride">cobalt(III) fluoride</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Chlorination">Chlorination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogenation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Chlorination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<dl><dd>See also: <a href="/wiki/Photochlorination" title="Photochlorination">Photochlorination</a></dd></dl>
<p>Chlorination is generally highly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermic</a>. Both saturated and unsaturated compounds react directly with chlorine, the former usually requiring UV light to initiate <a href="/wiki/Homolysis_(chemistry)" title="Homolysis (chemistry)">homolysis</a> of chlorine. Chlorination is conducted on a large scale industrially; major processes include routes to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1,2-dichloroethane" title="1,2-dichloroethane">1,2-dichloroethane</a> (a precursor to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PVC" title="PVC">PVC</a>), as well as various chlorinated ethanes, as solvents.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bromination">Bromination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogenation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Bromination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><b>Bromination</b> is more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_selectivity" title="Chemical selectivity">selective</a> than chlorination because the reaction is less <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exothermic" title="Exothermic">exothermic</a>. Most commonly bromination is conducted by the addition of Br<sub>2</sub> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkenes" title="Alkenes">alkenes</a>. An example of bromination is the <a href="/wiki/Organic_synthesis" title="Organic synthesis">organic synthesis</a> of the anesthetic <a href="/wiki/Halothane" title="Halothane">halothane</a> from <a href="/wiki/Trichloroethylene" title="Trichloroethylene">trichloroethylene</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Halothane_synthesis.png" title="Halothane synthesis"><img alt="Halothane synthesis" data-file-height="90" data-file-width="761" height="71" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Halothane_synthesis.png/600px-Halothane_synthesis.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/43/Halothane_synthesis.png 1.5x" width="600"/></a></div></div></dd></dl>
<p>Organobromine compounds are the most common organohalides in nature. Their formation is catalyzed by the enzyme <a href="/wiki/Bromoperoxidase" title="Bromoperoxidase">bromoperoxidase</a> which utilizes bromide in combination with oxygen as an oxidant. The oceans are estimated to release 1–2 million tons of bromoform and 56,000 tons of bromomethane annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Iodination">Iodination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogenation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Iodination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Iodine is the least reactive halogen and is reluctant to react with most organic compounds. The addition of iodine to alkenes is the basis of the analytical method called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iodine_number" title="Iodine number">iodine number</a>, a measure of the degree of unsaturation for <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fats</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iodoform_reaction" title="Iodoform reaction">iodoform reaction</a> involves degradation of methyl ketones.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Inorganic_chemistry">Inorganic chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halogenation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Inorganic chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All elements aside from argon, neon, and helium form fluorides by direct reaction with <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a>. Chlorine is slightly more selective, but still reacts with most metals and heavier nonmetals. Following the usual trend, bromine is less reactive and iodine least of all. Of the many reactions possible, illustrative is the formation of <a href="/wiki/Gold(III)_chloride" title="Gold(III) chloride">gold(III) chloride</a> by the chlorination of <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>. The chlorination of metals is usually not very important industrially since the chlorides are more easily made from the oxides and the hydrogen halide. Where chlorination of inorganic compounds is practiced on a relatively large scale is for the production of <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus_trichloride" title="Phosphorus trichloride">phosphorus trichloride</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulfur_monochloride" title="Sulfur monochloride">sulfur monochloride</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
