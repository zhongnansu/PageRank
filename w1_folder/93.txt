<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Life-cycle assessment</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"Cradle-to-grave" redirects here. For other uses, see <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Cradle_to_the_grave_(disambiguation)" title="Cradle to the grave (disambiguation)">Cradle to the grave (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about the environment impacts of products. For the ultimate cost of business decisions, see <a href="/wiki/Life-cycle_cost_analysis" title="Life-cycle cost analysis">Life-cycle cost analysis</a>.</div>
<p><b>Life-cycle assessment</b> (<b>LCA</b>, also known as <b>life-cycle analysis</b>, <b>ecobalance</b>, and <b>cradle-to-grave analysis</b>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is a technique to assess environmental impacts associated with all the stages of a product's life from raw material extraction through materials processing, manufacture, distribution, use, repair and maintenance, and disposal or recycling. Designers use this process to help critique their products. LCAs can help avoid a narrow outlook on environmental concerns by:
</p>
<ul><li>Compiling an inventory of relevant energy and material inputs and environmental releases;</li>
<li>Evaluating the potential impacts associated with identified inputs and releases;</li>
<li>Interpreting the results to help make a more informed decision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Goals_and_purpose">Goals and purpose</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life-cycle_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Goals and purpose">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The goal of LCA is to compare the full range of environmental effects assignable to products and services by quantifying all inputs and outputs of material flows and assessing how these material flows affect the environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This information is used to improve processes, support policy and provide a sound basis for informed decisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ecometrica.com_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-ecometrica.com-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The term <i>life cycle</i> refers to the notion that a fair, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holistic" title="Holistic">holistic</a> assessment requires the assessment of raw-material production, manufacture, <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_distribution" title="Sustainable distribution">distribution</a>, use and <a href="/wiki/Waste_management" title="Waste management">disposal</a> including all intervening transportation steps necessary or caused by the product's existence.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>There are two main types of LCA. Attributional LCAs seek to establish (or attribute) the burdens associated with the production and use of a product, or with a specific service or process, at a point in time (typically the recent past). Consequential LCAs seek to identify the environmental consequences of a decision or a proposed change in a system under study (oriented to the future), which means that market and economic implications of a decision may have to be taken into account. Social LCA is under development<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> as a different approach to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_Cycle_Thinking" title="Life Cycle Thinking">life cycle thinking</a> intended to assess social implications or potential impacts. Social LCA should be considered as an approach that is complementary to environmental LCA.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The procedures of life cycle assessment (LCA) are part of the <a href="/wiki/ISO_14000" title="ISO 14000">ISO 14000</a> environmental management standards: in ISO 14040:2006 and 14044:2006. (ISO 14044 replaced earlier versions of ISO 14041 to ISO 14043.) <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">GHG</a> product life cycle assessments can also comply with specifications such as <a href="/wiki/Publicly_Available_Specification" title="Publicly Available Specification">PAS</a> 2050 and the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_emissions_reporting#Greenhouse_Gas_Protocol._28GHGP.29" title="Carbon emissions reporting">GHG Protocol Life Cycle Accounting and Reporting Standard</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ecometrica.com_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-ecometrica.com-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Four_main_phases">Four main phases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life-cycle_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Four main phases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PhasesOfLifeCycleAnalysis.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="359" data-file-width="340" height="264" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/PhasesOfLifeCycleAnalysis.png/250px-PhasesOfLifeCycleAnalysis.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ea/PhasesOfLifeCycleAnalysis.png 1.5x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PhasesOfLifeCycleAnalysis.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illustration of LCA phases</div></div></div>
<p>According to the ISO 14040<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and 14044<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> standards, a Life Cycle Assessment is carried out in four distinct phases as illustrated in the figure shown to the right. The phases are often interdependent in that the results of one phase will inform how other phases are completed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Goal_and_scope">Goal and scope</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life-cycle_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Goal and scope">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An LCA starts with an explicit statement of the goal and scope of the study, which sets out the context of the study and explains how and to whom the results are to be communicated. This is a key step and the ISO standards require that the goal and scope of an LCA be clearly defined and consistent with the intended application. The goal and scope document therefore includes technical details that guide subsequent work:
</p>
<ul><li><b>the functional unit</b>, which defines what precisely is being studied and quantifies the service delivered by the product system, providing a reference to which the inputs and outputs can be related. Further, the functional unit is an important basis that enables alternative goods, or services, to be compared and analyzed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> So to explain this a functional system which is inputs, processes and outputs contains a functional unit, that fulfills a function, for example paint is covering a wall, making a functional unit of 1m² covered for 10 years. The functional flow would be the items necessary for that function, so this would be a brush, tin of paint and the paint itself.</li>
<li><b>the system boundaries</b>; which are delimitations of which processes that should be included in the analysis of a product system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>any assumptions and limitations</b>;<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><b>the allocation methods</b> used to partition the environmental load of a process when several products or functions share the same process; allocation is commonly dealt with in one of three ways: system expansion, substitution and partition. Doing this is not easy and different methods may give different results<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li></ul>
<p>and
</p>
<ul><li><b>the impact categories</b> chosen for example human <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxicity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Smog" title="Smog">smog</a>, <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">eutrophication</a>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_cycle_inventory">Life cycle inventory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life-cycle_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Life cycle inventory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LCI_Diagram.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="628" data-file-width="1020" height="135" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/LCI_Diagram.png/220px-LCI_Diagram.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/LCI_Diagram.png/330px-LCI_Diagram.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/LCI_Diagram.png/440px-LCI_Diagram.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LCI_Diagram.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This is an example of a Life-cycle inventory (LCI) diagram</div></div></div>
<p>Life Cycle Inventory (LCI) analysis involves creating an inventory of flows from and to nature for a product system. Inventory flows include inputs of water, energy, and raw materials, and releases to air, land, and water. To develop the inventory, a flow model of the technical system is constructed using data on inputs and outputs. The flow model is typically illustrated with a flow chart that includes the activities that are going to be assessed in the relevant supply chain and gives a clear picture of the technical system boundaries. The input and output data needed for the construction of the model are collected for all activities within the system boundary, including from the supply chain (referred to as inputs from the technosphere).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The data must be related to the functional unit defined in the goal and scope definition. Data can be presented in tables and some interpretations can be made already at this stage. The results of the inventory is an LCI which provides information about all inputs and outputs in the form of elementary flow to and from the environment from all the unit processes involved in the study.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Inventory flows can number in the hundreds depending on the system boundary. For product LCAs at either the generic (i.e., representative industry averages) or brand-specific level, that data is typically collected through survey questionnaires. At an industry level, care has to be taken to ensure that questionnaires are completed by a representative sample of producers, leaning toward neither the best nor the worst, and fully representing any regional differences due to energy use, material sourcing or other factors. The questionnaires cover the full range of inputs and outputs, typically aiming to account for 99% of the mass of a product, 99% of the energy used in its production and any environmentally sensitive flows, even if they fall within the 1% level of inputs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>One area where data access is likely to be difficult is flows from the technosphere. The technosphere is more simply defined as the man-made world. Considered by geologists as secondary resources, these resources are in theory 100% recyclable; however, in a practical sense, the primary goal is salvage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> For an LCI, these technosphere products (supply chain products) are those that have been produced by man and unfortunately those completing a questionnaire about a process which uses a man-made product as a means to an end will be unable to specify how much of a given input they use. Typically, they will not have access to data concerning inputs and outputs for previous production processes of the product. The entity undertaking the LCA must then turn to secondary sources if it does not already have that data from its own previous studies. National databases or data sets that come with LCA-practitioner tools, or that can be readily accessed, are the usual sources for that information. Care must then be taken to ensure that the secondary data source properly reflects regional or national conditions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_cycle_impact_assessment">Life cycle impact assessment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life-cycle_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Life cycle impact assessment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Inventory analysis is followed by impact assessment. This phase of LCA is aimed at evaluating the significance of potential environmental impacts based on the LCI flow results. Classical life cycle impact assessment (LCIA) consists of the following mandatory elements:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<ul><li>selection of impact categories, category indicators, and characterization models;</li>
<li>the classification stage, where the inventory parameters are sorted and assigned to specific impact categories; and</li>
<li>impact measurement, where the categorized LCI flows are characterized, using one of many possible LCIA methodologies, into common equivalence units that are then summed to provide an overall impact category total.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li></ul>
<p>In many LCAs, characterization concludes the LCIA analysis; this is also the last compulsory stage according to ISO 14044:2006. However, in addition to the above mandatory LCIA steps, other optional LCIA elements – <a href="/wiki/Normalization_(statistics)" title="Normalization (statistics)">normalization</a>, grouping, and weighting – may be conducted depending on the goal and scope of the LCA study. In normalization, the results of the impact categories from the study are usually compared with the total impacts in the region of interest, the U.S. for example. <a href="/wiki/Grouped_data" title="Grouped data">Grouping</a> consists of sorting and possibly <a href="/wiki/Ranking" title="Ranking">ranking</a> the impact categories. During <a href="/wiki/Weighting" title="Weighting">weighting</a>, the different environmental impacts are weighted relative to each other so that they can then be summed to get a single number for the total environmental impact. ISO 14044:2006 generally advises against weighting, stating that “weighting, shall not be used in LCA studies intended to be used in comparative assertions intended to be disclosed to the public”. This advice is often ignored, resulting in comparisons that can reflect a high degree of subjectivity as a result of weighting.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Life cycle impacts can also be categorized under the several phases of the development, production, use, and disposal of a product. Broadly speaking, these impacts can be divided into "First Impacts,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> use impacts, and end of life impacts. "First Impacts" include extraction of raw materials, manufacturing (conversion of raw materials into a product), transportation of the product to a market or site, construction/installation, and the beginning of the use or occupancy. Use impacts include physical impacts of operating the product or facility (such as energy, water, etc.), maintenance, renovation and repairs (required to continue to use the product or facility). End of life impacts include demolition and processing of waste or recyclable materials.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretation">Interpretation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life-cycle_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Interpretation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Life Cycle Interpretation is a systematic technique to identify, quantify, check, and evaluate information from the results of the life cycle inventory and/or the life cycle impact assessment. The results from the inventory analysis and impact assessment are summarized during the interpretation phase. The outcome of the interpretation phase is a set of conclusions and recommendations for the study. According to ISO 14040:2006, the interpretation should include:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<ul><li>identification of significant issues based on the results of the LCI and LCIA phases of an LCA;</li>
<li>evaluation of the study considering completeness, sensitivity and consistency checks; and</li>
<li>conclusions, limitations and recommendations.</li></ul>
<p>A key purpose of performing life cycle interpretation is to determine the level of confidence in the final results and communicate them in a fair, complete, and accurate manner. Interpreting the results of an LCA is not as simple as "3 is better than 2, therefore Alternative A is the best choice"! Interpreting the results of an LCA starts with understanding the accuracy of the results, and ensuring they meet the goal of the study. This is accomplished by identifying the data elements that contribute significantly to each impact category, <a href="/wiki/Sensitivity_analysis" title="Sensitivity analysis">evaluating the sensitivity</a> of these significant data elements, assessing the completeness and consistency of the study, and drawing conclusions and recommendations based on a clear understanding of how the LCA was conducted and the results were developed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Reference_test">Reference test</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life-cycle_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reference test">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>More specifically, the best alternative is the one that the LCA shows to have the least cradle-to-grave environmental negative impact on land, sea, and air resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="LCA_uses">LCA uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life-cycle_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: LCA uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Based on a survey of LCA practitioners carried out in 2006<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> 
LCA is mostly used to support business strategy (18%) and R&amp;D (18%), as input to product or process design (15%), in education (13%) and for labeling or product declarations (11%). LCA will be continuously integrated into the built environment as tools such as the European ENSLIC Building project guidelines for buildings or developed and implemented, which provide practitioners guidance on methods to implement LCI data into the planning and design process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Major corporations all over the world are either undertaking LCA in house or commissioning studies, while governments support the development of national databases to support LCA. Of particular note is the growing use of LCA for ISO Type III labels called Environmental Product Declarations, defined as "quantified environmental data for a product with pre-set categories of parameters based on the ISO 14040 series of standards, but not excluding additional environmental information".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Singh&amp;baksshi_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Singh&amp;baksshi-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> These third-party certified LCA-based labels provide an increasingly important basis for assessing the relative environmental merits of competing products. Third-party certification plays a major role in today's industry. Independent certification can show a company's dedication to safer and environmental friendlier products to customers and NGOs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>LCA also has major roles in <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_assessment" title="Environmental impact assessment">environmental impact assessment</a>, integrated <a href="/wiki/Waste_management" title="Waste management">waste management</a> and pollution studies. A recent study evaluated the LCA of a laboratory scale plant for oxygen enriched air production coupled with its economic evaluation in an holistic eco-design standpoint.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> LCA has also been used to assess the environmental impacts of pavement maintenance, repair, and rehabilitation activities <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Data_analysis">Data analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life-cycle_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Data analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A life cycle analysis is only as valid as its <a href="/wiki/Data" title="Data">data</a>; therefore, it is crucial that data used for the completion of a life cycle analysis are accurate and current. When comparing different life cycle analyses with one another, it is crucial that equivalent data are available for both products or processes in question. If one product has a much higher availability of data, it cannot be justly compared to another product which has less detailed data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are two basic types of LCA data – unit process data and environmental input-output data (EIO), where the latter is based on national economic input-output data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greet_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-greet-22">[22]</a></sup> Unit process data are derived from direct surveys of companies or plants producing the product of interest, carried out at a unit process level defined by the system boundaries for the study.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Data validity is an ongoing concern for life cycle analyses. Due to <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">globalization</a> and the rapid pace of <a href="/wiki/Research_and_development" title="Research and development">research and development</a>, new materials and manufacturing methods are continually being introduced to the market. This makes it both very important and very difficult to use up-to-date information when performing an LCA. If an LCA’s conclusions are to be valid, the data must be recent; however, the data-gathering process takes time. If a product and its related processes have not undergone significant revisions since the last LCA data was collected, data validity is not a problem. However, <a href="/wiki/Consumer_electronics" title="Consumer electronics">consumer electronics</a> such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cell_phones" title="Cell phones">cell phones</a> can be redesigned as often as every 9 to 12 months,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> creating a need for ongoing data collection.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The life cycle considered usually consists of a number of stages including: materials extraction, processing and manufacturing, product use, and product disposal. If the most environmentally harmful of these stages can be determined, then impact on the environment can be efficiently reduced by focusing on making changes for that particular phase. For example, the most energy-intensive life phase of an airplane or car is during use due to fuel consumption. One of the most effective ways to increase fuel efficiency is to decrease vehicle weight, and thus, car and airplane manufacturers can decrease environmental impact in a significant way by replacing heavier materials with lighter ones such as aluminium or carbon fiber-reinforced elements. The reduction during the use phase should be more than enough to balance additional raw material or <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing_cost" title="Manufacturing cost">manufacturing cost</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Data sources are typically large databases, it is not appropriate to compare two options if different data sources have been used to source the data. 
Data sources include:

This can range from a simple spreadsheet, where the user enters the data manually to a fully automated program, where the user is not aware of the source data.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">Variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life-cycle_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cradle-to-grave">Cradle-to-grave</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life-cycle_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Cradle-to-grave">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cradle-to-grave is the full Life Cycle Assessment from resource extraction ('cradle') to use phase and disposal phase ('grave'). For example, trees produce paper, which can be recycled into low-energy production <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a> (fiberised paper) <a href="/wiki/Building_insulation" title="Building insulation">insulation</a>, then used as an energy-saving device in the ceiling of a home for 40 years, saving 2,000 times the <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil-fuel</a> energy used in its production. After 40 years the <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a> fibers are replaced and the old fibers are disposed of, possibly incinerated. All inputs and outputs are considered for all the phases of the life cycle.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cradle-to-gate">Cradle-to-gate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life-cycle_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Cradle-to-gate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cradle-to-gate is an assessment of a <i>partial</i> product life cycle from resource extraction (<i>cradle</i>) to the factory gate (i.e., before it is transported to the consumer). The use phase and disposal phase of the product are omitted in this case. Cradle-to-gate assessments are sometimes the basis for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_product_declarations" title="Environmental product declarations">environmental product declarations</a> (EPD) termed business-to-business EDPs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> One of the significant uses of the cradle-to-gate approach compiles the life cycle inventory (LCI) using cradle-to-gate. This allows the LCA to collect all of the impacts leading up to resources being purchased by the facility. They can then add the steps involved in their transport to plant and manufacture process to more easily produce their own cradle-to-gate values for their products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cradle-to-cradle_or_closed_loop_production">Cradle-to-cradle or closed loop production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life-cycle_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Cradle-to-cradle or closed loop production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cradle_to_Cradle_Design" title="Cradle to Cradle Design">Cradle to Cradle Design</a></div>
<p>Cradle-to-cradle is a specific kind of cradle-to-grave assessment, where the end-of-life disposal step for the product is a <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">recycling</a> process. It is a method used to minimize the environmental impact of products by employing sustainable production, operation, and disposal practices and aims to incorporate social responsibility into product development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> From the recycling process originate new, identical products (e.g., asphalt pavement from discarded asphalt pavement, glass bottles from collected glass bottles), or different products (e.g., glass wool insulation from collected glass bottles).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Allocation of burden for products in open loop production systems presents considerable challenges for LCA. Various methods, such as the <a href="/wiki/Avoided_burden" title="Avoided burden">avoided burden</a> approach have been proposed to deal with the issues involved.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gate-to-gate">Gate-to-gate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life-cycle_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Gate-to-gate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gate-to-gate is a partial LCA looking at only one value-added process in the entire production chain. Gate-to-gate modules may also later be linked in their appropriate production chain to form a complete cradle-to-gate evaluation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Well-to-wheel">Well-to-wheel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life-cycle_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Well-to-wheel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Well-to-wheel is the specific LCA used for <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">transport</a> <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuels</a> and vehicles. The analysis is often broken down into stages entitled "well-to-station", or "well-to-tank", and "station-to-wheel" or "tank-to-wheel", or "plug-to-wheel". The first stage, which incorporates the feedstock or fuel production and processing and fuel delivery or energy transmission, and is called the "upstream" stage, while the stage that deals with vehicle operation itself is sometimes called the "downstream" stage. The well-to-wheel analysis is commonly used to assess total energy consumption, or the <a href="/wiki/Energy_conversion_efficiency" title="Energy conversion efficiency">energy conversion efficiency</a> and <a href="/wiki/Exhaust_gas" title="Exhaust gas">emissions</a> impact of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marine_vessel" title="Marine vessel">marine vessels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> and <a href="/wiki/Motor_vehicle" title="Motor vehicle">motor vehicles</a>, including their <a href="/wiki/Carbon_footprint" title="Carbon footprint">carbon footprint</a>, and the fuels used in each of these transport modes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VDI_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-VDI-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> WtW analysis is useful for reflecting the different efficiencies and emissions of energy technologies and fuels at both the upstream and downstream stages, giving a more complete picture of real emissions.
</p><p>The well-to-wheel variant has a significant input on a model developed by the <a href="/wiki/Argonne_National_Laboratory" title="Argonne National Laboratory">Argonne National Laboratory</a>. The Greenhouse gases, Regulated Emissions, and Energy use in Transportation (GREET) model was developed to evaluate the impacts of new fuels and vehicle technologies. The model evaluates the impacts of fuel use using a well-to-wheel evaluation while a traditional cradle-to-grave approach is used to determine the impacts from the vehicle itself. The model reports energy use, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emission" title="Greenhouse gas emission">greenhouse gas emissions</a>, and six additional pollutants: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compounds" title="Volatile organic compounds">volatile organic compounds</a> (VOCs), <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> (CO), <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxide</a> (NOx), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">particulate matter</a> with size smaller than 10 micrometre (PM10), particulate matter with size smaller than 2.5 micrometre (PM2.5), and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_oxide" title="Sulfur oxide">sulfur oxides</a> (SOx).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greet_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-greet-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Quantitative values of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions calculated with the WTW or with the LCA method can differ, since the LCA is considering more emission sources. In example, while assessing the GHG emissions of a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Battery_Electric_Vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Battery Electric Vehicle (page does not exist)">Battery Electric Vehicle</a> in comparison with a conventional internal combustion engine vehicle, the WTW (accounting only the GHG for manufacturing the fuels) finds out that an electric vehicle can save the 50-60% of GHG <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>, while an hybrid LCA-WTW method, considering also the GHG due to the manufacturing and the end of life of the battery gives GHG emission savings 10-13% lower, compared to the WTW. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Economic_input.E2.80.93output_life_cycle_assessment"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_input–output_life_cycle_assessment">Economic input–output life cycle assessment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life-cycle_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Economic input–output life cycle assessment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Economic input–output LCA (<a href="/wiki/EIOLCA" title="EIOLCA">EIOLCA</a>) involves use of aggregate sector-level data on how much environmental impact can be attributed to each sector of the economy and how much each sector purchases from other sectors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Such analysis can account for long chains (for example, building an automobile requires energy, but producing energy requires vehicles, and building those vehicles requires energy, etc.), which somewhat alleviates the scoping problem of process LCA; however, EIOLCA relies on sector-level averages that may or may not be representative of the specific subset of the sector relevant to a particular product and therefore is not suitable for evaluating the environmental impacts of products. Additionally the translation of economic quantities into environmental impacts is not validated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecologically_based_LCA">Ecologically based LCA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life-cycle_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Ecologically based LCA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While a conventional LCA uses many of the same approaches and strategies as an Eco-LCA, the latter considers a much broader range of ecological impacts. It was designed to provide a guide to wise management of human activities by understanding the direct and indirect impacts on ecological resources and surrounding ecosystems. Developed by Ohio State University Center for resilience, Eco-LCA is a methodology that quantitatively takes into account regulating and supporting services during the life cycle of economic goods and products. In this approach services are categorized in four main groups: supporting, regulating, provisioning and cultural services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Singh&amp;baksshi_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Singh&amp;baksshi-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exergy_based_LCA">Exergy based LCA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life-cycle_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Exergy based LCA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Exergy" title="Exergy">Exergy</a> of a system is the maximum useful work possible during a process that brings the system into equilibrium with a heat reservoir.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Wall <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> clearly states the relation between exergy analysis and resource accounting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> This intuition confirmed by DeWulf <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> and Sciubba <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> lead to Exergo-economic accounting <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> and to methods specifically dedicated to LCA such as Exergetic material input per unit of service (EMIPS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The
concept of material input per unit of service (MIPS) is quantified in terms of the second law of thermodynamics, allowing the calculation of both resource input and service output in exergy terms. This exergetic material input per unit of service (EMIPS) has been elaborated for transport technology. The service not only takes into account the total mass to be transported
and the total distance, but also the mass per single transport and the delivery time.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_cycle_energy_analysis">Life cycle energy analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life-cycle_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Life cycle energy analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Life cycle energy analysis (LCEA) is an approach in which all <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> inputs to a product are accounted for, not only direct energy inputs during manufacture, but also all energy inputs needed to produce components, materials and services needed for the manufacturing process. An earlier term for the approach was <i>energy analysis</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>With LCEA, the <i>total life cycle energy input</i> is established.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_production">Energy production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life-cycle_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Energy production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_production" title="Energy production">energy production</a></div>
<p>It is recognized that much energy is lost in the production of energy commodities themselves, such as <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear energy</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" title="Photovoltaic">photovoltaic</a>
<a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> or high-quality <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petroleum_products" title="Petroleum products">petroleum products</a>. <i>Net energy content</i> is the energy content of the product minus energy input used
during extraction and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_conversion" title="Energy conversion">conversion</a>, directly or indirectly. A controversial early result of LCEA claimed that manufacturing
<a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">solar cells</a> requires more energy than can be recovered in using the solar cell<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. The result was refuted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
Another new concept that flows from life cycle assessments is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_Cannibalism" title="Energy Cannibalism">Energy Cannibalism</a>. Energy Cannibalism refers to an effect where rapid
growth of an entire energy-intensive industry creates a need for <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> that uses (or cannibalizes) the energy of existing power
plants. Thus during rapid growth the industry as a whole produces no energy because new energy is used to fuel the <a href="/wiki/Embodied_energy" title="Embodied energy">embodied energy</a> of
future power plants. Work has been undertaken in the UK to determine the life cycle energy (alongside full LCA) impacts of a number of renewable technologies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_recovery">Energy recovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life-cycle_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Energy recovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Energy_recovery" title="Energy recovery">energy recovery</a></div>
<p>If materials are incinerated during the disposal process, the energy released during burning can be harnessed and used for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electricity_production" title="Electricity production">electricity production</a>. This provides a low-impact energy source, especially when compared with <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> and natural gas<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> While <a href="/wiki/Incineration" title="Incineration">incineration</a> produces more <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions than <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfilling" title="Landfilling">landfilling</a>, the waste plants are well-fitted with filters to minimize this negative impact. A recent study comparing energy consumption and greenhouse gas emissions from landfilling (without energy recovery) against incineration (with energy recovery) found incineration to be superior in all cases except for when <a href="/wiki/Landfill_gas" title="Landfill gas">landfill gas</a> is recovered for electricity production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life-cycle_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It has also been argued that energy efficiency is only one consideration in deciding which alternative process to employ, and that it should not be elevated to the only criterion for determining environmental acceptability.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> For example, simple energy analysis does not take into account the renewability of energy flows or the toxicity of waste products;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>. Incorporating <b>Dynamic LCAs</b> of renewable energy technologies (using sensitivity analyses to project future improvements in renewable systems and their share of the power grid) may help mitigate this criticism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>In recent years, the literature on life cycle assessment of <a href="/wiki/Energy_technology" title="Energy technology">energy technology</a> has begun to reflect the interactions between the current <a href="/wiki/Electrical_grid" title="Electrical grid">electrical grid</a> and future <a href="/wiki/Energy_technology" title="Energy technology">energy technology</a>. Some papers have focused on <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> life cycle,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearce2_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pearce2-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearce1_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pearce1-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dynamic_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-dynamic-54">[54]</a></sup> while others have focused on <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gases</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RKCL_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-RKCL-55">[55]</a></sup> The essential critique given by these sources is that when considering <a href="/wiki/Energy_technology" title="Energy technology">energy technology</a>, the growing nature of the power grid must be taken into consideration. If this is not done, a given class of <a href="/wiki/Energy_technology" title="Energy technology">energy technology</a> may emit more CO<sub>2</sub> over its lifetime than it mitigates.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>A problem the energy analysis method cannot resolve is that different energy forms (<a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemical_energy" title="Chemical energy">chemical energy</a> etc.) have different quality and value even in natural sciences, as a consequence of the two main laws of <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>. A thermodynamic measure of the quality of energy is <a href="/wiki/Exergy" title="Exergy">exergy</a>. According to the <a href="/wiki/First_law_of_thermodynamics" title="First law of thermodynamics">first law of thermodynamics</a>, all energy inputs should be accounted with equal weight, whereas by the <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">second law</a> diverse energy forms should be accounted by different values.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The conflict is resolved in one of these ways:
</p>
<ul><li>value difference between energy inputs is ignored,</li>
<li>a value ratio is arbitrarily assigned (e.g., a <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">joule</a> of <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> is 2.6 times more valuable than a joule of heat or fuel input),</li>
<li>the analysis is supplemented by economic (monetary) cost analysis,</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Exergy" title="Exergy">exergy</a> instead of energy can be the metric used for the life cycle analysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critiques">Critiques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life-cycle_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Critiques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Life cycle assessment is a powerful tool for analyzing <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/commensurable" title="wikt:commensurable">commensurable</a> aspects of quantifiable systems. Not every factor, however, can be reduced to a number and inserted into a model. Rigid system boundaries make accounting for changes in the system difficult. This is sometimes referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Boundary_critique" title="Boundary critique">boundary critique</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Systems_thinking" title="Systems thinking">systems thinking</a>. The accuracy and availability of data can also contribute to inaccuracy. For instance, data from generic processes may be based on <a href="/wiki/Average" title="Average">averages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sampling_error" title="Sampling error">unrepresentative sampling</a>, or outdated results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> Additionally, social implications of products are generally lacking in LCAs. Comparative life-cycle analysis is often used to determine a better process or product to use. However, because of aspects like differing system boundaries, different statistical information, different product uses, etc., these studies can easily be swayed in favor of one product or process over another in one study and the opposite in another study based on varying parameters and different available data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> There are guidelines to help reduce such conflicts in results but the method still provides a lot of room for the researcher to decide what is important, how the product is typically manufactured, and how it is typically used.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>An in-depth review of 13 LCA studies of wood and paper products<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> found<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> a lack of consistency in the methods and assumptions used to track carbon during the <a href="/wiki/Product_lifecycle" title="Product lifecycle">product lifecycle</a>. A wide variety of methods and assumptions were used, leading to different and potentially contrary conclusions – particularly with regard to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">carbon sequestration</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methanogenesis" title="Methanogenesis">methane generation</a> in landfills and with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_accounting" title="Carbon accounting">carbon accounting</a> during forest growth and product use.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Streamline_LCA">Streamline LCA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life-cycle_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Streamline LCA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This process includes three steps.
First, a proper method should be selected to combine adequate accuracy with acceptable cost burden in order to guide decision making. Actually, in LCA process, besides streamline LCA, Eco-screening and complete LCA are usually considered as well. However, the former one only could provide limited details and the latter one with more detailed information is more expensive.
Second, single measure of stress should be selected. Typical LCA output includes <a href="/wiki/Resource_consumption" title="Resource consumption">resource consumption</a>, energy consumption, water consumption, emission of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a>, toxic residues and so on. One of these outputs is used as the main factor to measure in streamline LCA. Energy consumption and CO<sub>2</sub> emission are often regarded as “practical indicators”.
Last, stress selected in step 2 is used as standard to assess phase of life separately and identify the most damaging phase. For instance, for a family car, energy consumption could be used as the single stress factor to assess each phase of life. The result shows that the most energy intensive phase for a family car is the usage stage.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p><b>Life Cycle Assessment of Engineered Material in Service</b> plays a significant role in saving energy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chat1_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chat1-61">[61]</a></sup> conserving resources <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chat2_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chat2-62">[62]</a></sup> and saving billions by preventing premature<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chat2_62-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chat2-62">[62]</a></sup> failure of critical engineered component in a machine or equipment. LCA data of surface engineered materials<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> are used to improve life cycle of the engineered component. Life cycle improvement of industrial machineries and equipments including, manufacturing, power generation, transportations, etc. leads to improvement in energy efficiency, sustainability and negating global temperature rise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chat1_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chat1-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Estimated reduction in anthropogenic carbon emission is minimum 10% of the global emission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chat1_61-2"><a href="#cite_note-Chat1-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p>
