<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Gas chamber</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Gas_chamber_(disambiguation)" title="Gas chamber (disambiguation)">Gas chamber (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p><p>A <b>gas chamber</b> is an apparatus for killing humans or other animals with gas, consisting of a sealed chamber into which a <a href="/wiki/Poison" title="Poison">poisonous</a> or <a href="/wiki/Asphyxiant_gas" title="Asphyxiant gas">asphyxiant gas</a> is introduced. The most commonly used poisonous agent is <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide" title="Hydrogen cyanide">hydrogen cyanide</a>; <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> have also been used. Gas chambers were used as a method of execution for condemned prisoners in the United States beginning in the 1920s and continue to be a legal execution method in three states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> During <a href="/wiki/The_Holocaust" title="The Holocaust">the Holocaust</a>, large-scale gas chambers designed for mass killing were used by <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> as part of their <a href="/wiki/Genocide" title="Genocide">genocide</a> program. The use of gas chambers in <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a> has also been reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nazi_Germany">Nazi Germany</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Extermination_camp#Gassings" title="Extermination camp">Extermination camp § Gassings</a>; <a href="/wiki/The_Holocaust#Extermination_camps,_gas_chambers" title="The Holocaust">The Holocaust § Extermination camps, gas chambers</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Criticism_of_Holocaust_denial#Use_of_gas_chambers" title="Criticism of Holocaust denial">Criticism of Holocaust denial § Use of gas chambers</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Majdanek_Komora_Gazowa.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1664" data-file-width="2500" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Majdanek_Komora_Gazowa.JPG/220px-Majdanek_Komora_Gazowa.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Majdanek_Komora_Gazowa.JPG/330px-Majdanek_Komora_Gazowa.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Majdanek_Komora_Gazowa.JPG/440px-Majdanek_Komora_Gazowa.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Majdanek_Komora_Gazowa.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Interior of Majdanek gas chamber, showing <a href="/wiki/Prussian_blue" title="Prussian blue">Prussian blue</a> residue</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> made extensive use of various types of gas chamber for mass killing.
</p><p>Beginning in 1939, gas chambers were used as part of the <a href="/wiki/Aktion_T4" title="Aktion T4">Nazi euthanasia program</a> aimed at eliminating physically and intellectually disabled people. Experiments in the gassing of patients were conducted in October 1939 in occupied <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poznan" title="Poznan">Posen</a> in Poland. Hundreds of prisoners were killed by <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> poisoning in an improvised gas chamber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-browning_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-browning-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1940 gas chambers using bottled pure carbon monoxide were established at six euthanasia centres in Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USHMM-HE_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-USHMM-HE-4">[4]</a></sup> In addition to persons with disabilities, these centres were also used to kill prisoners transferred from concentration camps in Germany, Austria, and Poland. Killings of concentration camp inmates continued after the euthanasia program was officially shut down in 1941.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-klee_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-klee-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Operation_Barbarossa" title="Operation Barbarossa">invasion of Russia</a>, mass executions by <a href="/wiki/Exhaust_gas" title="Exhaust gas">exhaust gas</a> were performed by <i><a href="/wiki/Einsatzgruppen" title="Einsatzgruppen">Einsatzgruppen</a></i> using <a href="/wiki/Gas_van" title="Gas van">gas vans</a>, trucks modified to divert engine exhaust into a sealed interior gas chamber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USHMM-HE_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-USHMM-HE-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Starting in 1941, gas chambers were used at <a href="/wiki/Extermination_camp" title="Extermination camp">extermination camps</a> in Poland for the mass killing of <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">Roma</a>, and other victims of <a href="/wiki/The_Holocaust" title="The Holocaust">the Holocaust</a>. Gas vans were used at the <a href="/wiki/Che%C5%82mno_extermination_camp" title="Chełmno extermination camp">Chełmno extermination camp</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Operation_Reinhard" title="Operation Reinhard">Operation Reinhard</a> extermination camps at <a href="/wiki/Be%C5%82%C5%BCec_extermination_camp" title="Bełżec extermination camp">Bełżec</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sobib%C3%B3r_extermination_camp" title="Sobibór extermination camp">Sobibór</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Treblinka_extermination_camp" title="Treblinka extermination camp">Treblinka</a> used exhaust fumes from stationary <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">diesel engines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USHMM-HE_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-USHMM-HE-4">[4]</a></sup> In search of more efficient killing methods, the Nazis experimented with using the <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide" title="Hydrogen cyanide">hydrogen cyanide</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Fumigation" title="Fumigation">fumigant</a> <a href="/wiki/Zyklon_B" title="Zyklon B">Zyklon B</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Auschwitz_concentration_camp" title="Auschwitz concentration camp">Auschwitz concentration camp</a>. This method was adopted for mass killings at the Auschwitz and <a href="/wiki/Majdanek_concentration_camp" title="Majdanek concentration camp">Majdanek</a> camps. Up to 6000 victims were gassed with Zyklon-B each day at Auschwitz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USHMM-HE_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-USHMM-HE-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Most extermination camp gas chambers were dismantled or destroyed in the last months of the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> as <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> troops approached, except for those at <a href="/wiki/Dachau_concentration_camp" title="Dachau concentration camp">Dachau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sachsenhausen_concentration_camp" title="Sachsenhausen concentration camp">Sachsenhausen</a> and Majdanek. One destroyed gas chamber at Auschwitz was reconstructed after the war to stand as a memorial.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lithuania">Lithuania</span></h2>
<p>In 1937–1940, <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a> operated a gas chamber in <a href="/wiki/Aleksotas" title="Aleksotas">Aleksotas</a> within the First Fort of the <a href="/wiki/Kaunas_Fortress" title="Kaunas Fortress">Kaunas Fortress</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cernevic_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-cernevic-6">[6]</a></sup> Before, the executions were carried out by hanging or by shooting. However, these methods were viewed as brutal and in January 1937, the criminal code was amended to provide execution by gas which at the time was viewed as more civilized and humane. Lithuania considered and rejected execution by poison. The first execution was carried on July 27, 1937: Bronius Pogužinskas, age 37, convicted of murder of five people from a Jewish family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cernevic_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-cernevic-6">[6]</a></sup> Historian Sigita Černevičiūtė counted at least nine executions in the gas chamber, though records are incomplete and fragmentary. Of the nine, eight were convicted of murder. One, Aleksandras Maurušaitis, was in addition convicted of anti-government actions during the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=1935_Suvalkija_strike&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="1935 Suvalkija strike (page does not exist)">1935 Suvalkija strike</a>. The last known execution took place on May 19, 1940 for robbery. The fate of the gas chamber after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occupation_of_Lithuania_by_Soviet_Union_1940" title="Occupation of Lithuania by Soviet Union 1940">occupation by the Soviet Union</a> in June 1940 is unclear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cernevic_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-cernevic-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="North_Korea">North Korea</span></h2>
<p>Kwon Hyok, a former head of security at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Camp_22" title="Camp 22">Camp 22</a>, described laboratories equipped with gas chambers for <a href="/wiki/Asphyxiant_gas" title="Asphyxiant gas">suffocation gas</a> experiments, in which three or four people, normally a family, are the experimental subjects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>  After undergoing medical checks, the chambers are sealed and poison is injected through a tube, while scientists observe from above through glass. In a report reminiscent of an earlier account of a family of seven, Kwon claims to have watched one family of two parents, a son and a daughter die from suffocating gas, with the parents trying to save the children using <a href="/wiki/Mouth-to-mouth_resuscitation" title="Mouth-to-mouth resuscitation">mouth-to-mouth resuscitation</a> for as long as they had the strength. Kwon's testimony was supported by documents from Camp 22 describing the transfer of prisoners designated for the experiments. The documents were identified as genuine by Kim Sang Hun, a London-based expert on Korea and human rights activist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc01_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc01-9">[9]</a></sup> A press conference in <a href="/wiki/Pyongyang" title="Pyongyang">Pyongyang</a>, organized by North Korean authorities, denounced this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Soviet_Union">Soviet Union</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gas_van" title="Gas van">Gas van</a></div>
<p>The original invention of mobile gas chambers based on adapted vans with the storage compartment sealed and exhaust redirected inside are attributed to Soviet <a href="/wiki/NKVD" title="NKVD">NKVD</a> commander Isay Berg. These vans were used by NKVD from 1936 under disguise of bread vans (Russian: <span lang="ru">душегубка</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Gas chambers have been used for <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment_in_the_United_States" title="Capital punishment in the United States">capital punishment in the United States</a> to execute <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_death_row_inmates" title="List of United States death row inmates">death row inmates</a>. The first person to be executed in the United States by lethal gas was <a href="/wiki/Gee_Jon" title="Gee Jon">Gee Jon</a>, on February 8, 1924. An unsuccessful attempt to pump poison gas directly into his cell at <a href="/wiki/Nevada_State_Prison" title="Nevada State Prison">Nevada State Prison</a> led to the development of the first makeshift gas chamber to carry out Gee's death sentence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DPIC-Descriptions_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-DPIC-Descriptions-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>On December 3, 1948, <a href="/wiki/Miran_Edgar_Thompson" title="Miran Edgar Thompson">Miran Thompson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sam_Shockley" title="Sam Shockley">Sam Shockley</a> were executed in the gas chamber at <a href="/wiki/San_Quentin_State_Prison" title="San Quentin State Prison">San Quentin State Prison</a> for their role in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Alcatraz" title="Battle of Alcatraz">Battle of Alcatraz</a>.
</p><p>In 1957, <a href="/wiki/Burton_Abbott" title="Burton Abbott">Burton Abbott</a> was executed as the governor of California, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goodwin_J._Knight" title="Goodwin J. Knight">Goodwin J. Knight</a>, was on the telephone to stay the execution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Since the restoration of the death penalty in the United States in 1976, eleven executions by gas chamber have been conducted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNN-16">[16]</a></sup> By the 1980s, reports of suffering during gas chamber executions had led to controversy over the use of this method.
</p><p>At the September 2, 1983, execution of <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Lee_Gray" title="Jimmy Lee Gray">Jimmy Lee Gray</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>, officials cleared the viewing room after eight minutes while Gray was still alive and gasping for air. The decision to clear the room while he was still alive was criticized by his <a href="/wiki/Attorneys_in_the_United_States" title="Attorneys in the United States">attorney</a>. <a href="/wiki/David_Bruck" title="David Bruck">David Bruck</a>, an attorney specializing in death penalty cases, said, "Jimmy Lee Gray died banging his head against a steel pole in the gas chamber while reporters counted his moans."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Gray was convicted for the <a href="/wiki/Murder" title="Murder">murder</a> of three-year-old Deressa Jean Scales in 1976, after <a href="/wiki/Kidnapping" title="Kidnapping">kidnapping</a> and anally raping her.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the April 6, 1992, execution of <a href="/wiki/Donald_Harding_(murderer)" title="Donald Harding (murderer)">Donald Harding</a> in <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a>, it took 11 minutes for death to occur. The prison warden stated that he would quit if required to conduct another gas chamber execution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Following Harding's execution, Arizona voted that all persons condemned after November 1992 would be executed by lethal injection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-CNN-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Following the execution of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Alton_Harris" title="Robert Alton Harris">Robert Alton Harris</a>, a federal court declared that "execution by lethal gas under the California protocol is unconstitutionally <a href="/wiki/Cruel_and_unusual_punishment" title="Cruel and unusual punishment">cruel and unusual</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> By the late 20th century, most states had switched to methods considered to be more humane, such as <a href="/wiki/Lethal_injection" title="Lethal injection">lethal injection</a>. California's gas chamber at San Quentin State Prison was converted to an execution chamber for lethal injection.
</p><p>As of 2010, the last person to be executed in the gas chamber was German national <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walter_LaGrand" title="Walter LaGrand">Walter LaGrand</a>, sentenced to death before 1992, who was executed in <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a> on March 3, 1999. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Ninth_Circuit" title="U.S. Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit">U.S. Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit</a> had ruled that he could not be executed by gas chamber, but the decision was overturned by the United States Supreme Court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNN_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-CNN-16">[16]</a></sup> The gas chamber was formerly used in <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>. Six states, Arizona, California, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, Mississippi, <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wyoming" title="Wyoming">Wyoming</a>, authorize lethal gas if lethal injection cannot be administered, the condemned committed their crime before a certain date, or the condemned chooses to die in the gas chamber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In October 2010, <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_New_York" title="Governor of New York">Governor of New York</a> <a href="/wiki/David_Paterson" title="David Paterson">David Paterson</a> signed a bill rendering gas chambers illegal for use by <a href="/wiki/Humane_society" title="Humane society">humane societies</a> and other animal shelters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-humaneanimal_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-humaneanimal-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Method_of_use">Method of use</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Using_hydrogen_cyanide">Using hydrogen cyanide</span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Santa_Fe_gas_chamber.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="753" data-file-width="1000" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Santa_Fe_gas_chamber.jpg/220px-Santa_Fe_gas_chamber.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Santa_Fe_gas_chamber.jpg/330px-Santa_Fe_gas_chamber.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Santa_Fe_gas_chamber.jpg/440px-Santa_Fe_gas_chamber.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Santa_Fe_gas_chamber.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The former gas chamber at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Mexico_State_Penitentiary" title="New Mexico State Penitentiary">New Mexico State Penitentiary</a>, used only once in 1960 and later replaced by <a href="/wiki/Lethal_injection" title="Lethal injection">lethal injection</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:181px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gaschamber.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="236" data-file-width="179" height="236" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2e/Gaschamber.jpg" width="179"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption">Executions in California were carried out in the gas chamber at <a href="/wiki/San_Quentin_State_Prison" title="San Quentin State Prison">San Quentin State Prison</a>. It was modified for the use of <a href="/wiki/Lethal_injection" title="Lethal injection">lethal injection</a>, but has been returned to its original designated purpose, with the creation of a new chamber specifically for lethal injection.</div></div></div>
<p>As implemented in the United States, the gas chamber is considered to be the most dangerous, most complicated, and most expensive method of administering the death penalty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>  The condemned person is strapped into a chair within an airtight chamber, which is then sealed.  The executioner activates a mechanism which drops <a href="/wiki/Potassium_cyanide" title="Potassium cyanide">potassium cyanide</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Sodium_cyanide" title="Sodium cyanide">sodium cyanide</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>) pellets into a bath of <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> beneath the chair; the ensuing chemical reaction generates lethal <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide" title="Hydrogen cyanide">hydrogen cyanide</a> gas.  Because hydrogen cyanide gas condenses at approximately 78 °F (26 °C), the temperature in the chamber (when it is in use) is maintained at a minimum of 80 °F (27 °C).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>The gas is visible to the condemned, who is advised to take several deep breaths to speed unconsciousness. Nonetheless, there are often convulsions and excessive drooling.  There may also be urinating, defecating, and vomiting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Following the execution the chamber is purged with air, and any remnant gas is neutralized with <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">anhydrous ammonia</a>, after which the body can be removed (with great caution, as pockets of gas can be trapped in the victim's clothing).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HCN_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-HCN-31">[31]</a></sup>  Sometimes, as a safety precaution, the clothing worn by the executed person is destroyed by incineration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leuchter2_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-leuchter2-32">[32]</a></sup>  The undertaker who handles the body wears rubber gloves for protection against any trace amounts of cyanide that might still be present on or in the body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leuchter2_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-leuchter2-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Excluding_all_oxygen">Excluding all oxygen</span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Inert_gas_asphyxiation" title="Inert gas asphyxiation">Inert gas asphyxiation</a></div>
<p>Nitrogen gas or oxygen-depleted air has been considered for human execution, as it can induce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_asphyxiation" title="Nitrogen asphyxiation">nitrogen asphyxiation</a>. The victim detects little abnormal sensation as the oxygen level falls. This leads to <a href="/wiki/Asphyxia" title="Asphyxia">asphyxiation</a> (death from lack of oxygen) without the painful and traumatic feeling of suffocation, or the side effects of poisoning.
</p><p>In April 2015, <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Oklahoma" title="Governor of Oklahoma">Oklahoma Governor</a> <a href="/wiki/Mary_Fallin" title="Mary Fallin">Mary Fallin</a> approved a bill allowing nitrogen asphyxiation as an execution method.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newsok.com_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-newsok.com-33">[33]</a></sup> On March 14, 2018, Oklahoma Attorney General Mike Hunter and Corrections Director Joe M. Allbaugh announced a switch to nitrogen gas as the primary method of execution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Livestock">Livestock</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Controlled_atmosphere_killing" title="Controlled atmosphere killing">Controlled atmosphere killing</a></div>
<p>Gas chambers have also been used for <a href="/wiki/Animal_euthanasia" title="Animal euthanasia">animal euthanasia</a>, using <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> as the lethal agent. Sometimes a box filled with <a href="/wiki/Anesthetic" title="Anesthetic">anesthetic</a> gas is used to anesthetize small animals for surgery or euthanasia.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
