<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Mole (unit)</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about the chemistry unit "mole" (amount of substance). For other uses, see <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Mole_(disambiguation)" title="Mole (disambiguation)">Mole (disambiguation)</a>.</div>

<table class="infobox" style="width:22em;background:;"><tbody><tr><th colspan="2" style="text-align:center;font-size:125%;font-weight:bold">Mole</th></tr><tr><th scope="row"><a href="/wiki/System_of_measurement" title="System of measurement">Unit system</a></th><td>
<a href="/wiki/SI_base_unit" title="SI base unit">SI base unit</a></td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Unit of</th><td>
<a href="/wiki/Amount_of_substance" title="Amount of substance">Amount of substance</a></td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Symbol</th><td>
mol </td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The <b>mole</b> is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Units_of_measurement" title="Units of measurement">unit of measurement</a> for <a href="/wiki/Amount_of_substance" title="Amount of substance">amount of substance</a> in the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a> (SI). The unit is defined as the amount or sample of a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_substance" title="Chemical substance">chemical substance</a> that contains as many constitutive particles, e.g., <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecules</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photons</a>, as there are atoms in 12 <a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">grams</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carbon-12" title="Carbon-12">carbon-12</a> (<sup>12</sup>C), the <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> with <a href="/wiki/Standard_atomic_weight" title="Standard atomic weight">standard atomic weight</a> 12 by definition. This number is expressed by the <a href="/wiki/Avogadro_constant" title="Avogadro constant">Avogadro constant</a>, which has a value of approximately <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7023602214076000000♠</span>6.022<span style="margin-left:.25em;">140</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">76</span><span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>23</sup> mol<sup>−1</sup></span>. The mole is an <a href="/wiki/SI_base_unit" title="SI base unit">SI base unit</a>, with the unit symbol <b>mol</b>.
</p><p>At its next meeting in November 2018 the <a href="/wiki/General_Conference_on_Weights_and_Measures" title="General Conference on Weights and Measures">CGPM</a> is expected to accept the proposed redefinition of the mole, <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kilogram</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ampere" title="Ampere">ampere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">kelvin</a>, which will define the mole to have exactly <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7023602214076000000♠</span>6.022<span style="margin-left:.25em;">140</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">76</span><span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>23</sup></span> elementary entities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The mole is widely used in chemistry as a convenient way to express amounts of reactants and products of chemical reactions.  For example, the chemical equation <span class="nowrap">2 H<sub>2</sub> + O<sub>2</sub> → 2H<sub>2</sub>O</span> implies that 2 mol <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dihydrogen" title="Dihydrogen">dihydrogen</a> (H<sub>2</sub>) and 1 mol <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dioxygen" title="Dioxygen">dioxygen</a> (O<sub>2</sub>) react to form 2 mol water (H<sub>2</sub>O). The mole may also be used to represent the number of atoms, ions, or other entities in a given sample of a substance. The <a href="/wiki/Concentration" title="Concentration">concentration</a> of a solution is commonly expressed by its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molarity" title="Molarity">molarity</a>, defined as the amount of dissolved substance per unit volume of solution, for which the unit typically used is moles per <a href="/wiki/Litre" title="Litre">litre</a> (mol/l).
</p><p>The term <i>gram-molecule</i> was formerly used for essentially the same concept.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI114-15_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-SI114-15-2">[2]</a></sup> The term <i>gram-atom</i> has been used for a related but distinct concept, namely a quantity of a substance that contains Avogadro's number of <i>atoms</i>, whether isolated or combined in molecules. Thus, for example, 1 mole of MgBr<sub>2</sub> is 1 gram-molecule of MgBr<sub>2</sub> but 3 gram-atoms of MgBr<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition_and_related_concepts">Definition and related concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mole_(unit)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition and related concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Amount_of_substance" title="Amount of substance">Amount of substance</a> is a measure of the quantity of substance proportional to the number of its entities. As of  2011<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mole_(unit)&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the mole is defined by International Bureau of Weights and Measures as:
</p>
<ol><li>The mole is the amount of substance of a system which contains as many elementary entities as there are atoms in 0.012 kilogram of carbon 12.</li>
<li>When the mole is used, the elementary entities must be specified and may be atoms, molecules, ions, electrons, other particles, or specified groups of such particles.</li></ol>
<p>Thus, by definition, one mole of pure <sup>12</sup>C has a mass of <i>exactly</i> 12 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grams" title="Grams">g</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Molar_mass" title="Molar mass">molar mass</a> of a substance is the mass of a sample divided by the amount of substance in that sample. This is a constant for any given substance. Since the <a href="/wiki/Unified_atomic_mass_unit" title="Unified atomic mass unit">unified atomic mass unit</a> (symbol: u, or Da) is defined as 1/12 of the mass of the <sup>12</sup>C atom, it follows that the molar mass of a substance, measured in grams per mole, is numerically equal to its mean <a href="/wiki/Atomic_mass" title="Atomic mass">atomic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Molecular_mass" title="Molecular mass">molecular mass</a> expressed in Da.
</p><p>One can determine the amount of a known substance, in moles, by dividing the sample's mass by the substance's molar mass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIPMrealise_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-BIPMrealise-5">[5]</a></sup>  Other methods include the use of the <a href="/wiki/Molar_volume" title="Molar volume">molar volume</a> or the measurement of <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">electric charge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIPMrealise_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-BIPMrealise-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The mass of one mole of a substance depends not only on its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_formula" title="Molecular formula">molecular formula</a>, but also on the proportions within the sample of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotopes" title="Isotopes">isotopes</a> of each chemical element present in it. For example, one mole of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calcium-40" title="Calcium-40">calcium-40</a> is <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7001399625909800000♠</span>39.96259098<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>0.00000022 grams</span>, whereas one mole of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calcium-42" title="Calcium-42">calcium-42</a> is <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7001419586180100000♠</span>41.95861801<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>0.00000027 grams</span>, and one mole of <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> with the normal isotopic mix is <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7001400780000000000♠</span>40.078<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>0.004 grams</span>.
</p><p>Since the definition of the gram is not (as of  2011<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mole_(unit)&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>) mathematically tied to that of the atomic mass unit, the number of molecules per mole <i>N</i><sub><i>A</i></sub> (the Avogadro constant) must be determined experimentally. The value adopted by <a href="/wiki/Committee_on_Data_for_Science_and_Technology" title="Committee on Data for Science and Technology">CODATA</a> in 2010 is <i>N</i><sub><i>A</i></sub> = <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7023602214129000000♠</span>(6.02214129<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>0.00000027)<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>23</sup> mol<sup>−1</sup></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
In 2011 the measurement was refined to <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7023602214078000000♠</span>(6.02214078<span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">±</span>0.00000018)<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>23</sup> mol<sup>−1</sup></span>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mass and volume (properties of matter) are often used to quantify a sample of a substance. However, the volume changes with temperature and pressure. Similarly, due to relativistic effects, the mass of a sample changes with temperature, speed or gravity. This effect is very small at low temperature, speed or gravity, but at high speed like in a particle accelerator or theoretical space craft, the change is significant. The amount of substance remains the same regardless of temperature, pressure, speed or gravity, unless a (chemical or nuclear) reaction changes the number of particles.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mole_(unit)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The history of the mole is intertwined with that of <a href="/wiki/Molecular_mass" title="Molecular mass">molecular mass</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_mass_unit" title="Atomic mass unit">atomic mass unit</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avogadro_number" title="Avogadro number">Avogadro number</a> and related concepts.
</p><p>The first table of <a href="/wiki/Standard_atomic_weight" title="Standard atomic weight">standard atomic weight</a> (atomic mass) was published by <a href="/wiki/John_Dalton" title="John Dalton">John Dalton</a> (1766–1844) in 1805, based on a system in which the relative atomic mass of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> was defined as 1. These relative atomic masses were based on the <a href="/wiki/Stoichiometry" title="Stoichiometry">stoichiometric</a> proportions of chemical reaction and compounds, a fact that greatly aided their acceptance: It was not necessary for a chemist to subscribe to <a href="/wiki/Atomic_theory" title="Atomic theory">atomic theory</a> (an unproven hypothesis at the time) to make practical use of the tables. This would lead to some confusion between atomic masses (promoted by proponents of atomic theory) and <a href="/wiki/Equivalent_weight" title="Equivalent weight">equivalent weights</a> (promoted by its opponents and which sometimes differed from relative atomic masses by an integer factor), which would last throughout much of the nineteenth century.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6ns_Jacob_Berzelius" title="Jöns Jacob Berzelius">Jöns Jacob Berzelius</a> (1779–1848) was instrumental in the determination of relative atomic masses to ever-increasing accuracy. He was also the first chemist to use <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> as the standard to which other masses were referred. Oxygen is a useful standard, as, unlike hydrogen, it forms compounds with most other elements, especially <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metals" title="Metals">metals</a>. However, he chose to fix the atomic mass of oxygen as 100, which did not catch on.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Charles_Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Gerhardt" title="Charles Frédéric Gerhardt">Charles Frédéric Gerhardt</a> (1816–56), <a href="/wiki/Henri_Victor_Regnault" title="Henri Victor Regnault">Henri Victor Regnault</a> (1810–78) and <a href="/wiki/Stanislao_Cannizzaro" title="Stanislao Cannizzaro">Stanislao Cannizzaro</a> (1826–1910) expanded on Berzelius' works, resolving many of the problems of unknown stoichiometry of compounds, and the use of atomic masses attracted a large consensus by the time of the <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe_Congress" title="Karlsruhe Congress">Karlsruhe Congress</a> (1860). The convention had reverted to defining the atomic mass of hydrogen as 1, although at the level of precision of measurements at that time—relative uncertainties of around 1%—this was numerically equivalent to the later standard of oxygen = 16. However the chemical convenience of having oxygen as the primary atomic mass standard became ever more evident with advances in analytical chemistry and the need for ever more accurate atomic mass determinations.
</p><p>Developments in <a href="/wiki/Mass_spectrometry" title="Mass spectrometry">mass spectrometry</a> led to the adoption of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen-16" title="Oxygen-16">oxygen-16</a> as the standard substance, in lieu of natural oxygen.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The old definition of the mole, based on carbon-12, was approved during the 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SI114-15_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-SI114-15-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUPAChist_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-IUPAChist-8">[8]</a></sup> The four different definitions were equivalent to within 1%.
</p>

<p>The name <i>mole</i> is an 1897 translation of the <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> unit <i>Mol</i>, coined by the <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">chemist</a> <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Ostwald" title="Wilhelm Ostwald">Wilhelm Ostwald</a> in 1894 from the German word <i>Molekül</i> (<a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecule</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>  However, the related concept of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equivalent_mass" title="Equivalent mass">equivalent mass</a> had been in use at least a century earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The mole was made the seventh <a href="/wiki/SI_base_unit" title="SI base unit">SI base unit</a> in 1971 by the 14th CGPM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mole_(unit)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since its adoption into the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">International System of Units</a> in 1971, numerous criticisms of the concept of the mole as a unit like the <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metre</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">second</a> have arisen:
</p>
<ul><li>the number of molecules, etc. in a given amount of material is a fixed <a href="/wiki/Dimensionless_quantity" title="Dimensionless quantity">dimensionless quantity</a> that can be expressed simply as a number, not requiring a distinct base unit;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUPAChist_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-IUPAChist-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>the SI thermodynamic mole is irrelevant to analytical chemistry and could cause avoidable costs to advanced economies;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>the mole is not a true metric (i.e. measuring) unit, rather it is a <i>parametric</i> unit and amount of substance is a <i>parametric</i> base quantity;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>the SI defines numbers of entities as quantities of dimension one, and thus ignores the ontological distinction between <i>entities</i> and <i>units of continuous quantities</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>In chemistry, it has been known since <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Proust" title="Joseph Proust">Proust's</a> <a href="/wiki/Law_of_definite_proportions" title="Law of definite proportions">law of definite proportions</a> (1794) that knowledge of the mass of each of the components in a chemical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/System_(thermodynamics)" title="System (thermodynamics)">system</a> is not sufficient to define the system. Amount of substance can be described as mass divided by Proust's "definite proportions", and contains information that is missing from the measurement of mass alone. As demonstrated by <a href="/wiki/John_Dalton" title="John Dalton">Dalton's</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Law_of_partial_pressures" title="Law of partial pressures">law of partial pressures</a> (1803), a measurement of mass is not even necessary to measure the amount of substance (although in practice it is usual). There are many physical relationships between amount of substance and other physical quantities, the most notable one being the <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas_law" title="Ideal gas law">ideal gas law</a> (where the relationship was first demonstrated in 1857). The term "mole" was first used in a textbook describing these <a href="/wiki/Colligative_properties" title="Colligative properties">colligative properties</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Other_units_called_.22mole.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id='Other_units_called_"mole"'>Other units called "mole"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mole_(unit)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title='Edit section: Other units called "mole"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chemical engineers use the concept extensively, but the unit is rather small for industrial use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> For convenience in avoiding conversions in the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_units" title="Imperial units">Imperial</a> (or <a href="/wiki/United_States_customary_units" title="United States customary units">American customary units</a>), some engineers adopted the <i>pound-mole</i> (notation <i>lb-mol</i> or <i>lbmol</i>), which is defined as the number of entities in 12 <a href="/wiki/Pound_(mass)" title="Pound (mass)">lb</a> of <sup>12</sup>C.  One lb-mol is equal to <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7002453592370000000♠</span>453.59237 mol</span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Himmelblau_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Himmelblau-18">[18]</a></sup> which value is identical to the number of grams in an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_avoirdupois_pound" title="International avoirdupois pound">international avoirdupois pound</a>.
</p><p>In the metric system, chemical engineers once used the <i>kilogram-mole</i> (notation <i>kg-mol</i>), which is defined as the number of entities in 12 kg of <sup>12</sup>C, and often referred to the mole as the <i>gram-mole</i> (notation <i>g-mol</i>), when dealing with laboratory data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Himmelblau_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Himmelblau-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Late 20th century chemical engineering practice came to use the <i>kilomole</i> (kmol), which is numerically identical to the kilogram-mole, but whose name and symbol adopt the SI convention for standard multiples of metric units – thus, kmol means 1000 mol.  This is analogous to the use of kg instead of g. The use of kmol is not only for "magnitude convenience" but also makes the equations used for modelling chemical engineering systems <a href="/wiki/Coherence_(units_of_measurement)" title="Coherence (units of measurement)">coherent</a>. For example, the conversion of a flowrate of kg/s to kmol/s only requires the molecular mass not the factor 1000 unless the basic SI unit of mol/s were to be used.  Indeed, the appearance of any conversion factors in a model can cause confusion and is to be avoided; possibly a definition of coherence is the absence of conversion factors in sets of equations developed for modelling.
</p><p>Concentrations expressed as kmol/m<sup>3</sup> are numerically the same as those in mol/dm<sup>3</sup> i.e. the molarity conventionally used by chemists for bench measurements; this equality can be convenient for scale-up.
</p><p>Greenhouse and growth chamber lighting for plants is sometimes expressed in micromoles per square meter per second, where 1 mol photons = <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7023602000000000000♠</span>6.02<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>23</sup></span> photons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_redefinition">Proposed redefinition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mole_(unit)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Proposed redefinition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Proposed_redefinition_of_SI_base_units" title="Proposed redefinition of SI base units">Proposed redefinition of SI base units</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CODATA_2018" title="CODATA 2018">CODATA 2018</a></div>
<p>In 2011, the 24th meeting of the <a href="/wiki/General_Conference_on_Weights_and_Measures" title="General Conference on Weights and Measures">General Conference on Weights and Measures</a> (CGPM) agreed to a plan for a possible revision of the <a href="/wiki/SI_base_unit" title="SI base unit">SI base unit</a> definitions at an undetermined date. 
</p><p>The 26th meeting of the CGPM set for November 2018 has scheduled a formal vote on the proposed redefinition of SI base units mole, kilogram, ampere and kelvin.  The mole will have exactly 6.02214076 x10<sup>23</sup> specified "elementary entities".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The definition of the mole will no longer be based on mass. If the proposal is approved as expected, the new definitions will take effect 20 May 2019.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_units">Related units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mole_(unit)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Related units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The SI units for <a href="/wiki/Molar_concentration" title="Molar concentration">molar concentration</a> are mol/<a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">m</a><sup>3</sup>. However, most chemical literature traditionally uses mol/<a href="/wiki/Decimetre" title="Decimetre">dm</a><sup>3</sup>, or mol <a href="/wiki/Decimetre" title="Decimetre">dm</a><sup>−3</sup>, which is the same as mol/<a href="/wiki/Litre" title="Litre">L</a>. These traditional units are often denoted by a capital letter M (pronounced "molar"), sometimes preceded by an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_prefix" title="SI prefix">SI prefix</a>, for example, millimoles per <a href="/wiki/Litre" title="Litre">litre</a> (mmol/L) or millimolar (mM), micromoles/litre (µmol/L) or micromolar (µM), or nanomoles/L (nmol/L) or nanomolar (nM).
</p><p>The <i>demal</i> (D) is an obsolete unit for expressing the concentration of a <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solution</a>. It is equal to molar concentration at 0 °C, i.e., 1 D represents 1 mol of the solute present in one cubic decimeter of the solution at 0 °C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> It was first proposed in 1924 as a unit of concentration based on the decimeter rather than the liter; at the time there was a factor of 1.000028 difference between the liter and the cubic decimeter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jerrard_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jerrard-22">[22]</a></sup> The demal was used as a unit of concentration in electrolytic  conductivity primary standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pratt_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pratt-23">[23]</a></sup> These standards were later redefined in terms of molar concentration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shreiner2004_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shreiner2004-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mole_Day">Mole Day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mole_(unit)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mole Day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>October 23, denoted 10/23 in the US, is recognized by some as <a href="/wiki/Mole_Day" title="Mole Day">Mole Day</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> It is an informal holiday in honor of the unit among chemists. The date is derived from the Avogadro number, which is approximately <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7023602200000000000♠</span>6.022<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>23</sup></span>. It starts at 6:02 a.m. and ends at 6:02 p.m. Alternatively, some chemists celebrate June 2 or February 6, a reference to the 6.02 part of the constant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
