<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">British thermal unit</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"BTU" redirects here. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/BTU_(disambiguation)" title="BTU (disambiguation)">BTU (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>
The <b>British thermal unit</b> (<b>Btu</b> or <b>BTU</b>) is a traditional unit of <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a>; it is defined as the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of one <a href="/wiki/Pound_(mass)" title="Pound (mass)">pound</a> of water by one degree <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">Fahrenheit</a>. It is part of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_customary_units" title="United States customary units">United States customary units</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Its counterpart in the <a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">metric system</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Calorie" title="Calorie">calorie</a>, which is defined as the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of one gram of water by one degree <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">Celsius</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Heat is now known to be equivalent to energy, for which the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI" title="SI">SI</a> unit is the <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">joule</a>; one BTU is about 1055 joules. While units of heat are often supplanted by energy units in scientific work, they are still important in many fields. As examples, in the United States the <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas_prices" title="Natural gas prices">price of natural gas</a> is quoted in dollars per million BTUs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HenryHub_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-HenryHub-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_thermal_unit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A BTU was originally defined as the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of 1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avoirdupois" title="Avoirdupois">avoirdupois</a> pound of liquid water by 1 degree Fahrenheit at a constant pressure of one <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">atmosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-5">[5]</a></sup> There are several different definitions of the BTU that are now known to differ slightly. This reflects the fact that the temperature change of a mass of water due to the addition of a specific amount of heat (calculated in energy units, usually joules) depends slightly upon the water's initial temperature. As seen in the table below, definitions of the BTU based on different water temperatures vary by up to 0.5%. In the table, thermochemical and steam table (IT) values, which are now defined in terms of exact values in joules, have been rounded to four decimal places.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prefixes">Prefixes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_thermal_unit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Prefixes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Units kBtu are used in building energy use tracking and heating system sizing. Energy Use Index (EUI) represents kBtu per square foot of conditioned floor area. "k" stands for 1,000.
</p><p>The units <b>MBtu</b> and <b>MMBtu</b> are used in the natural gas and other industries to indicate 1,000 and 1,000,000 BTUs, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EIA_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-EIA-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Price_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Price-12">[12]</a></sup> There is an ambiguity in that the metric system uses the prefix "M" to indicate one million (1,000,000), and "MBtu" is also used to indicate one million BTUs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-APS_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-APS-13">[13]</a></sup> Because of this ambiguity, some authors have deprecated the use of MBtu.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Price_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Price-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Energy analysts accustomed to the metric "k" for 1,000 are more likely to use MBtu to represent one million, especially in documents where M represents one million in other energy or cost units, such as MW, MWh and $.
</p><p>The unit <i><a href="/wiki/Therm" title="Therm">therm</a></i> is used to represent 100,000 (or 10<sup>5</sup>) BTUs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EIA_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-EIA-11">[11]</a></sup> A decatherm is 10 therms or one MBtu. The unit <i><a href="/wiki/Quad_(unit)" title="Quad (unit)">quad</a></i> is commonly used to represent one quadrillion (10<sup>15</sup>) BTUs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-APS_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-APS-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conversions">Conversions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_thermal_unit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Conversions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One Btu is approximately:
</p>
<ul><li>1.054 to 1.060 kJ (kilo<a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">joules</a>)</li>
<li>0.2931 W⋅h (<a href="/wiki/Kilowatt_hour" title="Kilowatt hour">watt hours</a>)</li>
<li>252 to 253 cal (<a href="/wiki/Calorie" title="Calorie">calories</a>, or "little calories")</li>
<li>0.25 kcal (kilocalories, "large calories", or "food calories")</li>
<li>25,031 to 25,160 ft⋅pdl (<a href="/wiki/Foot-poundal" title="Foot-poundal">foot-poundal</a>)</li>
<li>778 to 782 ft⋅lbf (<a href="/wiki/Foot-pound_(energy)" title="Foot-pound (energy)">foot-pounds-force</a>)</li>
<li>5.40395 (lbf/in<sup>2</sup>)⋅ft<sup>3</sup></li></ul>
<p>A Btu can be approximated as the heat produced by burning a single wooden kitchen match or as the amount of energy it takes to lift a one-pound (0.45 kg) weight 778 feet (237 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="For_natural_gas">For natural gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_thermal_unit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: For natural gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas_prices" title="Natural gas prices">Natural gas prices</a></div>
<ul><li>In <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> pricing, the Canadian definition is that 1 MMBtu ≡ <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7006100000000000000♠</span>1<span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">000</span></span> Btu ≡ 1.054615 GJ.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alberta_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alberta-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>The energy content (high or low heating value) of a volume of natural gas varies with the composition of the natural gas, which means there is no universal conversion factor for the number of Btu to volume. 1 standard cubic foot of average natural gas yields ≈ 1030 Btu (between 1010 Btu and 1070 Btu, depending on quality, when burned)</li>
<li>As a coarse approximation, 1000 ft<sup>3</sup> of natural gas yields ≈ 1 MMBtu ≈ 1 GJ</li>
<li>For natural gas price conversion 1000 m<sup>3</sup> ≈ 36.906 MMBtu and 1 MMBtu ≈ 27.096 m<sup>3</sup></li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Associated_units">Associated units</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_thermal_unit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Associated units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>1 <i><a href="/wiki/Ton_of_refrigeration" title="Ton of refrigeration">ton of cooling</a></i>, a common unit in North American refrigeration and air conditioning applications, is 12,000 Btu/h (3.52 kW). It is the rate of heat transfer needed to freeze 1 short ton (907 kg) of water into ice in 24 hours.</li>
<li>In the US, the <a href="/wiki/R-value_(insulation)" title="R-value (insulation)">R-value</a> that describes the performance of thermal insulation is typically quoted in ft<sup>2</sup>⋅°F⋅hr/Btu. For one square foot of the insulation, one BTU per hour of heat flows across the insulator for each degree of temperature difference across it.</li>
<li>1 <i><a href="/wiki/Therm" title="Therm">therm</a></i> is defined in the United States and European Union as 100,000 Btu—but the U.S. uses the Btu<sub>59 °F</sub> while the EU uses the Btu<sub>IT</sub>. The therm is used in natural gas pricing in the United Kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></li>
<li>1 <i><a href="/wiki/Quad_(unit)" title="Quad (unit)">quad</a></i> (short for <a href="/wiki/Orders_of_magnitude_(numbers)#1015" title="Orders of magnitude (numbers)">quadrillion</a> Btu) is 10<sup>15</sup> Btu, which is about 1 exajoule (<span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7018105500000000000♠</span>1.055<span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">×</span>10<sup>18</sup> J</span>). Quads are used in the United States for representing the annual energy consumption of large economies: for example, the U.S. economy used 99.75 quads in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> One quad/year is about 33.43 gigawatts.</li></ul>
<p>The Btu should not be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Kilowatt_hour" title="Kilowatt hour">Board of Trade Unit</a> (B.O.T.U.), which is a much larger quantity of energy (1 kW⋅h or 3,412 Btu).
</p><p>The Btu is often used to express the conversion-efficiency of heat into electrical energy in power plants. Figures are quoted in terms of the quantity of heat in Btu required to generate 1 kW⋅h of electrical energy. A typical coal-fired power plant works at <span class="nowrap"><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7004105000000000000♠</span>10<span style="margin-left:.25em;">500</span></span> Btu/kW⋅h, an efficiency of 32–33%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>The centigrade heat unit (CHU) is the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of one pound of water by one degree <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">Celsius</a>. It is equal to 1.8 BTU or 1899 joules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>  This unit was sometimes used in the United Kingdom as an alternative to BTU but is now obsolete.
</p>
