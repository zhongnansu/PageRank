<p><b>Apnea</b> <a href="/wiki/American_and_British_English_spelling_differences#ae_and_oe" title="American and British English spelling differences">or</a> <b>apnoea</b> is the cessation of <a href="/wiki/Breathing" title="Breathing">breathing</a>.  During apnea, there is no movement of the <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">muscles</a> of <a href="/wiki/Inhalation" title="Inhalation">inhalation</a>, and the volume of the <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">lungs</a> initially remains unchanged. Depending on how blocked the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airway" title="Airway">airways</a> are (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/patency" title="wikt:patency">patency</a>), there may or may not be a flow of <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> between the lungs and the environment; <a href="/wiki/Gas_exchange" title="Gas exchange">gas exchange</a> within the lungs and <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">cellular respiration</a> is not affected. Voluntarily doing this is called <b>holding one's breath</b>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cause">Cause</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apnea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Cause">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Apnea can be involuntarily achieved, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_drug" title="Pharmaceutical drug">drug</a>-induced (such as <a href="/wiki/Opiate" title="Opiate">opiate</a> toxicity or <a href="/wiki/Tryptamine" title="Tryptamine">tryptamine</a> toxicity<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>), mechanically induced (for example, by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strangulation" title="Strangulation">strangulation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Choking" title="Choking">choking</a>), or it can occur as a consequence of neurological disease or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_trauma" title="Physical trauma">trauma</a>. During sleep in patients who are suffering from <a href="/wiki/Sleep_apnea" title="Sleep apnea">sleep apnea</a>, these events can occur up to 20–30 times per hour, every night.
</p><p>Apnea can also be observed during periods of heightened <a href="/wiki/Emotion" title="Emotion">emotion</a>, such as during <a href="/wiki/Crying" title="Crying">crying</a> or accompanied by the <a href="/wiki/Valsalva_maneuver" title="Valsalva maneuver">Valsalva maneuver</a> when a person <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laughs" title="Laughs">laughs</a>.
</p><p>Voluntary apnea can be achieved by closing the <a href="/wiki/Vocal_cords" title="Vocal cords">vocal cords</a>, simultaneously keeping the mouth closed and blocking the <a href="/wiki/Nasal_vestibule" title="Nasal vestibule">nasal vestibule</a>, or constantly activating expiratory muscles.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Complications">Complications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apnea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Complications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Under normal conditions, <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a> cannot store much <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> in the body. Prolonged apnea leads to severe lack of oxygen in the <a href="/wiki/Circulatory_system" title="Circulatory system">blood circulation</a>. Permanent <a href="/wiki/Brain_damage" title="Brain damage">brain damage</a> can occur after as little as three minutes and <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">death</a> will inevitably ensue after a few more minutes unless ventilation is restored. However, under special circumstances such as <a href="/wiki/Hypothermia" title="Hypothermia">hypothermia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperbaric_oxygenation" title="Hyperbaric oxygenation">hyperbaric oxygenation</a>, apneic oxygenation (see below), or <a href="/wiki/Extracorporeal_membrane_oxygenation" title="Extracorporeal membrane oxygenation">extracorporeal membrane oxygenation</a>, much longer periods of apnea may be tolerated without severe consequences.
</p><p>Untrained humans cannot sustain voluntary apnea for more than one or two minutes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The reason for the time limit of voluntary apnea is that the rate of breathing and the volume of each breath are tightly regulated to maintain constant values of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> tension and <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a>. In apnea, CO<sub>2</sub> is not removed through the lungs and accumulates in the blood. The consequent rise in CO<sub>2</sub> tension and drop in pH result in stimulation of the respiratory centre in the brain which eventually cannot be overcome voluntarily.
</p><p>When a person is immersed in water, physiological changes due to the mammalian <a href="/wiki/Diving_reflex" title="Diving reflex">diving reflex</a> enable somewhat longer tolerance of apnea even in untrained persons. Tolerance can in addition be trained. The ancient technique of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free-diving" title="Free-diving">free-diving</a> requires breath-holding, and world-class free-divers can hold their breath underwater up to depths of 214 metres and for more than four minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <b>Apneists</b>, in this context, are people who can hold their breath for a long time.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hyperventilation">Hyperventilation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apnea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hyperventilation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Voluntary <a href="/wiki/Hyperventilation" title="Hyperventilation">hyperventilation</a> before beginning voluntary apnea is commonly believed to allow the person involved to safely hold their breath for a longer period. In reality, it will give the impression that one does not need to breathe, while the body is actually experiencing a blood-oxygen level that would normally, and indirectly, invoke a strong <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dyspnea" title="Dyspnea">dyspnea</a>. Some have incorrectly attributed the effect of hyperventilation to increased oxygen in the blood, not realizing that it is actually due to a decrease in CO<sub>2</sub> in the blood and lungs. Blood leaving the lungs is normally fully saturated with oxygen, so hyperventilation of normal air cannot increase the amount of oxygen available. Lowering the CO<sub>2</sub> concentration increases the pH of the blood, thus increasing the time before the respiratory center becomes stimulated, as described above. While hyperventilation will yield slightly longer breath-holding times, any small time increase is at the expense of possible <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(medical)" title="Hypoxia (medical)">hypoxia</a>. One using this method can suddenly lose consciousness—a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shallow_water_blackout" title="Shallow water blackout">shallow water blackout</a>—as a result. If a person loses consciousness underwater, there is considerable danger that they will <a href="/wiki/Drowning" title="Drowning">drown</a>. An alert diving partner would be in the best position to rescue such a person. Static apnea blackout occurs at the surface when a motionless diver holds a breath long enough for the circulating oxygen to fall below that required for the brain to maintain consciousness. It involves no pressure changes in the body and is usually performed to enhance breath-hold time. It should never be practiced alone, but under strict safety protocols with a safety beside the diver.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Apneic_oxygenation">Apneic oxygenation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apnea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Apneic oxygenation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because the exchange of gases between the blood and airspace of the lungs is independent of the movement of gas to and from the lungs, enough oxygen can be delivered to the circulation even if a person is apneic. With the onset of apnea, low pressure develops in the airspace of the lungs, because more oxygen is absorbed than CO<sub>2</sub> is released. With the airways closed or obstructed, this will lead to a gradual collapse of the lungs. However, if the airways are open, any gas supplied to the upper airways will follow the pressure gradient and flow into the lungs to replace the oxygen consumed. If pure oxygen is supplied, this process will serve to replenish the oxygen stored in the lungs. The uptake of oxygen into the blood will then remain at the usual level and the normal functioning of the organs will not be affected.  A detriment to this hyperoxygenation is possible <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_washout" title="Nitrogen washout">nitrogen washout</a> occurring which can lead to absorption <a href="/wiki/Atelectasis" title="Atelectasis">atelectasis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, no CO<sub>2</sub> is removed during apnea. The <a href="/wiki/Partial_pressure" title="Partial pressure">partial pressure</a> of CO<sub>2</sub> in the airspace of the lungs will quickly equilibrate with that of the blood. As the blood is loaded with CO<sub>2</sub> from the metabolism, more and more CO<sub>2</sub> will accumulate and eventually displace oxygen and other gases from the airspace. CO<sub>2</sub> will also accumulate in the tissues of the body, resulting in <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_acidosis" title="Respiratory acidosis">respiratory acidosis</a>.
</p><p>Under ideal conditions (i.e., if pure oxygen is breathed before onset of apnea to remove all <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> from the lungs, and pure oxygen is <a href="/wiki/Insufflation_(medicine)" title="Insufflation (medicine)">insufflated</a>), apneic oxygenation could theoretically be sufficient to provide enough oxygen for survival of more than one hour's duration in a healthy adult.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, accumulation of carbon dioxide (described above) would remain the limiting factor.
</p><p>Apneic oxygenation is more than a physiologic curiosity. It can be employed to provide a sufficient amount of oxygen in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thoracic_surgery" title="Thoracic surgery">thoracic surgery</a> when apnea cannot be avoided, and during manipulations of the airways such as <a href="/wiki/Bronchoscopy" title="Bronchoscopy">bronchoscopy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Intubation" title="Intubation">intubation</a>, and surgery of the upper airways. However, because of the limitations described above, apneic oxygenation is inferior to extracorporal circulation using a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart-lung_machine" title="Heart-lung machine">heart-lung machine</a> and is therefore used only in emergencies and for short procedures.  Use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PEEP_valve" title="PEEP valve">PEEP valves</a> is also an accepted alternative, 5 cm H<sub>2</sub>O in average weight patients and 10 cm H<sub>2</sub>O significantly improved lung and chest wall compliance in morbidly obese patients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1959, Frumin described the use of apneic oxygenation during anesthesia and surgery. Of the eight test subjects in this landmark study, the highest recorded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arterial_blood_gas" title="Arterial blood gas">PaCO<sub>2</sub></a> was 250 <a href="/wiki/Torr" title="Torr">millimeters of mercury</a>, and the lowest arterial <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> was 6.72 after 53 minutes of apnea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Apnea_scientific_studies">Apnea scientific studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apnea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Apnea scientific studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Studies found spleen volume is reduced during short breath-hold apnea in healthy adults.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Apnea_test_in_determining_brain_death">Apnea test in determining brain death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apnea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Apnea test in determining brain death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A recommended practice for the clinical diagnosis of <a href="/wiki/Brain_death" title="Brain death">brain death</a> formulated by the <a href="/wiki/American_Academy_of_Neurology" title="American Academy of Neurology">American Academy of Neurology</a> hinges on the conjunction of three diagnostic criteria: <a href="/wiki/Coma" title="Coma">coma</a>, absence of <a href="/wiki/Brainstem" title="Brainstem">brainstem</a> reflexes, and apnea (defined as the inability of the patient to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breath" title="Breath">breathe</a> unaided, that is, with no <a href="/wiki/Life_support" title="Life support">life support</a> systems). The apnea test follows a delineated protocol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  Apnea testing is not suitable in patients whom are hemodynamically unstable with increasing vasopressor needs, metabolic acidosis, or (as in this patient) require high levels of ventilatory support.  Apnea testing carries the risk of arrhythmias, worsening hemodynamic instability, or metabolic acidosis beyond the level of recovery and can potentially make the patient unsuitable for organ donation.  In this situation a confirmatory test is warranted as it is unsafe to perform the apnea test.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_pronunciation">Etymology and pronunciation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Apnea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Etymology and pronunciation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <i>apnea</i> uses <a href="/wiki/Classical_compound" title="Classical compound">combining forms</a> of <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/a-#Prefix" title="wikt:a-">a-</a></i> + <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-pnea#Suffix" title="wikt:-pnea">-pnea</a></i>, from Greek <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el">ἄπνοια</span>, from ἀ-, <a href="/wiki/Privative" title="Privative">privative</a>, πνέειν, to breathe. <a href="/wiki/Shortness_of_breath#Collation_of_-pnea_transcriptions" title="Shortness of breath">See pronunciation information at <i>dyspnea</i>.</a>
</p>
