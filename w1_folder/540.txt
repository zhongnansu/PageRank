<p><b>Dimethyl sulfoxide</b> (<b>DMSO</b>) is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organosulfur_compound" title="Organosulfur compound">organosulfur compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methyl" title="Methyl">CH<sub>3</sub></a>)<sub>2</sub><a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">S</a><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a>.  This colorless liquid is an important <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_solvent" title="Polar solvent">polar</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aprotic_solvent" title="Aprotic solvent">aprotic solvent</a> that dissolves both <a href="/wiki/Chemical_polarity" title="Chemical polarity">polar and nonpolar</a> compounds and is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miscible" title="Miscible">miscible</a> in a wide range of organic solvents as well as water.  It has a relatively high melting point.  DMSO has the unusual property that many individuals perceive a <a href="/wiki/Garlic" title="Garlic">garlic</a>-like taste in the mouth after contact with the skin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In terms of chemical structure, the molecule has idealized <a href="/wiki/Molecular_symmetry#Common_point_groups" title="Molecular symmetry">C<sub>s</sub></a> <a href="/wiki/Symmetry_group" title="Symmetry group">symmetry</a>. It has a <a href="/wiki/Trigonal_pyramidal_molecular_geometry" title="Trigonal pyramidal molecular geometry">trigonal pyramidal molecular geometry</a> consistent with other three-coordinate S(IV) compounds,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> with a <a href="/wiki/Lone_pair" title="Lone pair">nonbonded electron pair</a> on the approximately <a href="/wiki/Tetrahedral_molecular_geometry" title="Tetrahedral molecular geometry">tetrahedral</a> sulfur atom.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis_and_production">Synthesis and production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimethyl_sulfoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Synthesis and production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It was first synthesized in 1866 by the Russian scientist <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Mikhaylovich_Zaytsev" title="Alexander Mikhaylovich Zaytsev">Alexander Zaytsev</a>, who reported his findings in 1867.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  Dimethyl sulfoxide is produced industrially from <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfide" title="Dimethyl sulfide">dimethyl sulfide</a>, a by-product of the <a href="/wiki/Kraft_process" title="Kraft process">Kraft process</a>, by oxidation with oxygen or <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide" title="Nitrogen dioxide">nitrogen dioxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimethyl_sulfoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions_with_electrophiles">Reactions with electrophiles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimethyl_sulfoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reactions with electrophiles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The sulfur center in DMSO is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nucleophilic" title="Nucleophilic">nucleophilic</a> toward soft <a href="/wiki/Electrophile" title="Electrophile">electrophiles</a> and the oxygen is nucleophilic toward hard electrophiles.  With <a href="/wiki/Methyl_iodide" title="Methyl iodide">methyl iodide</a> it forms <a href="/wiki/Trimethylsulfoxonium_iodide" title="Trimethylsulfoxonium iodide">trimethylsulfoxonium iodide</a>, [(CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>SO]I:
</p>
<dl><dd>(CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>SO  +  CH<sub>3</sub>I  →  [(CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>SO]I</dd></dl>
<p>This salt can be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deprotonated" title="Deprotonated">deprotonated</a> with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydride" title="Sodium hydride">sodium hydride</a> to form the <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> <a href="/wiki/Ylide" title="Ylide">ylide</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>[(CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>SO]I  +  NaH  → (CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>S(CH<sub>2</sub>)O  +  NaI  +  H<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acidity">Acidity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimethyl_sulfoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Acidity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The methyl groups of DMSO are only weakly acidic, with a <span class="nowrap">p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> = 35</span>.  For this reason, the basicities of many weakly basic organic compounds have been examined in this solvent.
</p><p>Deprotonation of DMSO requires strong bases like <a href="/wiki/Lithium_diisopropylamide" title="Lithium diisopropylamide">lithium diisopropylamide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydride" title="Sodium hydride">sodium hydride</a>.  Stabilization of the resultant <a href="/wiki/Carbanion" title="Carbanion">carbanion</a> is provided by the S(O)R group.  The sodium derivative of DMSO formed in this way is referred to as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dimsyl_sodium" title="Dimsyl sodium">dimsyl sodium</a></i>.  It is a base, e.g., for the deprotonation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ketones" title="Ketones">ketones</a> to form sodium <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enolates" title="Enolates">enolates</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphonium_salt" title="Phosphonium salt">phosphonium salts</a> to form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wittig_reagent" title="Wittig reagent">Wittig reagents</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formamidinium" title="Formamidinium">formamidinium</a> salts to form <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diaminocarbene" title="Diaminocarbene">diaminocarbenes</a>.  It is also a potent nucleophile.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxidant">Oxidant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimethyl_sulfoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Oxidant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Organic_synthesis" title="Organic synthesis">organic synthesis</a>, DMSO is used as a mild oxidant,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> as illustrated by the <a href="/wiki/Pfitzner%E2%80%93Moffatt_oxidation" title="Pfitzner–Moffatt oxidation">Pfitzner–Moffatt oxidation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Swern_oxidation" title="Swern oxidation">Swern oxidation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ligand">Ligand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimethyl_sulfoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ligand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Related to its ability to dissolve many salts, DMSO is a common <a href="/wiki/Ligand" title="Ligand">ligand</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coordination_chemistry" title="Coordination chemistry">coordination chemistry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Illustrative is the complex <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dichlorotetrakis(dimethyl_sulfoxide)ruthenium(II)" title="Dichlorotetrakis(dimethyl sulfoxide)ruthenium(II)">dichlorotetrakis(dimethyl sulfoxide)ruthenium(II)</a> (RuCl<sub>2</sub>(dmso)<sub>4</sub>). In this complex, three DMSO ligands are bonded to <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium" title="Ruthenium">ruthenium</a> through sulfur. The fourth DMSO is bonded through oxygen. In general, the oxygen-bonded mode is more common.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimethyl_sulfoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solvent">Solvent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimethyl_sulfoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Solvent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vacuum_distillation_of_DMSO_at_70C.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="450" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Vacuum_distillation_of_DMSO_at_70C.jpg/170px-Vacuum_distillation_of_DMSO_at_70C.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Vacuum_distillation_of_DMSO_at_70C.jpg/255px-Vacuum_distillation_of_DMSO_at_70C.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Vacuum_distillation_of_DMSO_at_70C.jpg/340px-Vacuum_distillation_of_DMSO_at_70C.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vacuum_distillation_of_DMSO_at_70C.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Distillation of DMSO requires a partial vacuum to achieve a lower boiling point.</div></div></div>
<p>DMSO is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_aprotic_solvent" title="Polar aprotic solvent">polar aprotic solvent</a> and is less toxic than other members of this class, such as <a href="/wiki/Dimethylformamide" title="Dimethylformamide">dimethylformamide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dimethylacetamide" title="Dimethylacetamide">dimethylacetamide</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methylpyrrolidone" title="Methylpyrrolidone"><i>N</i>-methyl-2-pyrrolidone</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hexamethylphosphoramide" title="Hexamethylphosphoramide">HMPA</a>. DMSO is frequently used as a <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvent</a> for chemical reactions involving salts, most notably <a href="/wiki/Finkelstein_reaction" title="Finkelstein reaction">Finkelstein reactions</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Nucleophilic_substitution" title="Nucleophilic substitution">nucleophilic substitutions</a>. It is also extensively used as an extractant in biochemistry and cell biology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Because DMSO is only weakly acidic, it tolerates relatively strong bases and as such has been extensively used in the study of <a href="/wiki/Carbanion" title="Carbanion">carbanions</a>.  A set of non-aqueous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PKa" title="PKa">pKa</a> values (C-H, O-H, S-H and N-H acidities) for thousands of organic compounds have been determined in DMSO solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Because of its high boiling point, 189 °C (372 °F), DMSO evaporates slowly at normal atmospheric pressure. Samples dissolved in DMSO cannot be as easily recovered compared to other solvents, as it is very difficult to remove all traces of DMSO by conventional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotary_evaporation" title="Rotary evaporation">rotary evaporation</a>. One technique to fully recover samples is removal of the organic solvent by evaporation followed by addition of water (to dissolve DMSO) and <a href="/wiki/Freeze-drying" title="Freeze-drying">cryodesiccation</a> to remove both DMSO and water. Reactions conducted in DMSO are often diluted with water to precipitate or phase-separate products. The relatively high freezing point of DMSO, 18.5 °C (65.3 °F), means that at, or just below, room temperature it is a solid, which can limit its utility in some chemical processes (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Crystallization" title="Crystallization">crystallization</a> with cooling).
</p><p>In its <a href="/wiki/Deuterium" title="Deuterium">deuterated</a> form (<a href="/wiki/Deuterated_DMSO" title="Deuterated DMSO">DMSO-d<sub>6</sub></a>), it is a useful solvent for <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance" title="Nuclear magnetic resonance">NMR</a> spectroscopy, again due to its ability to dissolve a wide range of analytes, the simplicity of its own spectrum, and its suitability for high-temperature NMR spectroscopic studies. Disadvantages to the use of <a href="/wiki/Deuterated_DMSO" title="Deuterated DMSO">DMSO-d<sub>6</sub></a> are its high viscosity, which broadens signals, and its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hygroscopicity" title="Hygroscopicity">hygroscopicity</a>, which leads to an overwhelming H<sub>2</sub>O resonance in the <sup>1</sup>H NMR spectrum. It is often mixed with <a href="/wiki/Deuterated_chloroform" title="Deuterated chloroform">CDCl<sub>3</sub></a> or <a href="/wiki/Deuterated_dichloromethane" title="Deuterated dichloromethane">CD<sub>2</sub>Cl<sub>2</sub></a> for lower viscosity and melting points.
</p><p>DMSO is finding increased use in manufacturing processes to produce microelectronic devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> It is widely used to strip photoresist in TFT-LCD 'flat panel' displays and advanced packaging applications (such as wafer-level packaging / solder bump patterning).  It also used in <a href="/wiki/Biopreservation" title="Biopreservation">biopreservation</a> especially stem cell banking. DMSO is an effective <a href="/wiki/Paint_stripper" title="Paint stripper">paint stripper</a>, being safer than many of the others such as <a href="/wiki/Nitromethane" title="Nitromethane">nitromethane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dichloromethane" title="Dichloromethane">dichloromethane</a>.
</p><p>Because of its ability to dissolve many kinds of compounds, DMSO plays a role in sample management and <a href="/wiki/High-throughput_screening" title="High-throughput screening">high-throughput screening</a> operations in drug design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biology">Biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimethyl_sulfoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>DMSO is used in <a href="/wiki/Polymerase_chain_reaction" title="Polymerase chain reaction">polymerase chain reaction</a> (PCR) to inhibit <a href="/wiki/Stem-loop" title="Stem-loop">secondary structures</a> in the <a href="/wiki/DNA#Polymerases" title="DNA">DNA template</a> or the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DNA_primer" title="DNA primer">DNA primers</a>. It is added to the PCR mix before reacting, where it interferes with the self-complementarity of the DNA, minimizing interfering reactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>DMSO in a PCR reaction is applicable for supercoiled plasmids (to relax before amplification) or DNA templates with high <a href="/wiki/GC-content" title="GC-content">GC-content</a> (to decrease <a href="/wiki/Thermostability" title="Thermostability">thermostability</a>). For example, 10% final concentration of DMSO in the PCR mixture with Phusion decreases primer annealing temperature (i.e. primer melting temperature) by 5.5–6.0 °C (9.9–10.8 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is well known as a reversible cell cycle arrester at phase G1 of human lymphoid cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>DMSO may also be used as a <a href="/wiki/Cryoprotectant" title="Cryoprotectant">cryoprotectant</a>, added to cell media to reduce ice formation and thereby prevent cell death during the freezing process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Approximately 10% may be used with a slow-freeze method, and the cells may be frozen at −80 °C (−112 °F) or stored in <a href="/wiki/Liquid_nitrogen" title="Liquid nitrogen">liquid nitrogen</a> safely.
</p><p>DMSO has been used as a co-solvent to assist absorption of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flavonol#Flavonols_glycosides" title="Flavonol">flavonol glycoside</a> <a href="/wiki/Icariin" title="Icariin">Icariin</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Caenorhabditis_elegans" title="Caenorhabditis elegans"><i>C. elegans</i></a> <a href="/wiki/Nematode" title="Nematode">nematode</a> worm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>In cell culture, DMSO is used to induce differentiation of P19 embryonic carcinoma cells into cardiomyocytes and skeletal muscle cells.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medicine">Medicine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimethyl_sulfoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Medicine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Use of DMSO in medicine dates from around 1963, when an <a href="/wiki/Oregon_Health_%26_Science_University" title="Oregon Health &amp; Science University">Oregon Health &amp; Science University</a> Medical School team, headed by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stanley_Jacob&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stanley Jacob (page does not exist)">Stanley Jacob</a>, discovered it could penetrate the skin and other membranes without damaging them and could carry other compounds into a biological system. In medicine, DMSO is predominantly used as a topical <a href="/wiki/Analgesic" title="Analgesic">analgesic</a>, a vehicle for topical application of pharmaceuticals, as an <a href="/wiki/Anti-inflammatory" title="Anti-inflammatory">anti-inflammatory</a>, and an <a href="/wiki/Antioxidant" title="Antioxidant">antioxidant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Because DMSO increases the rate of absorption of some compounds through organic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_tissue" title="Biological tissue">tissues</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Skin" title="Skin">skin</a>, it is used in some <a href="/wiki/Transdermal" title="Transdermal">transdermal</a> <a href="/wiki/Drug_delivery" title="Drug delivery">drug delivery</a> systems.  Its effect may be enhanced with the addition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EDTA" title="EDTA">EDTA</a>. It is frequently compounded with antifungal medications, enabling them to penetrate not just skin but also toenails and fingernails.
</p><p>DMSO has been examined for the treatment of numerous conditions and ailments, but the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> (FDA) has approved its use only for the symptomatic relief of patients with <a href="/wiki/Interstitial_cystitis" title="Interstitial cystitis">interstitial cystitis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> A 1978 study concluded that DMSO brought <a href="/wiki/Statistical_significance" title="Statistical significance">significant</a> relief to the majority of the 213 patients with inflammatory <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genitourinary" title="Genitourinary">genitourinary</a> disorders that were studied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The authors recommended DMSO for genitourinary inflammatory conditions not caused by infection or tumor in which symptoms were severe or patients failed to respond to conventional therapy.
</p><p>A gel containing DMSO, <a href="/wiki/Panthenol" title="Panthenol">dexpanthenol</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Heparin" title="Heparin">heparin</a>, is sold in Germany and eastern Europe (commercialized under the Dolobene<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> brand) for topical use in sprains, <a href="/wiki/Tendinitis" title="Tendinitis">tendinitis</a>, and local inflammation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Interventional_radiology" title="Interventional radiology">interventional radiology</a>, DMSO is used as a solvent for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EVOH" title="EVOH">ethylene vinyl alcohol</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Onyx_(Interventional_radiology)" title="Onyx (Interventional radiology)">Onyx</a> liquid embolic agent, which is used in <a href="/wiki/Embolization" title="Embolization">embolization</a>, the therapeutic occlusion of blood vessels.
</p><p>In medical research, DMSO is often used as a drug vehicle in <i><a href="/wiki/In_vivo" title="In vivo">in vivo</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/In_vitro" title="In vitro">in vitro</a></i> experiments. However, when a researcher is unaware of its <a href="/wiki/Pleiotropy_(drugs)" title="Pleiotropy (drugs)">pleiotropic</a> effects, or when the control groups are not carefully planned, a bias can occur; an effect of DMSO can be falsely attributed to the drug.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kelava_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-kelava-24">[24]</a></sup> For example, even a very low dose of DMSO has a powerful protective effect against <a href="/wiki/Paracetamol" title="Paracetamol">paracetamol</a> (acetaminophen)-induced liver injury in mice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PMID_20962895_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-PMID_20962895-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Cryobiology" title="Cryobiology">cryobiology</a> DMSO has been used as a <a href="/wiki/Cryoprotectant" title="Cryoprotectant">cryoprotectant</a> and is still an important constituent of cryoprotectant <a href="/wiki/Cryopreservation#Vitrification" title="Cryopreservation">vitrification</a> mixtures used to preserve organs, tissues, and cell suspensions. Without it, up to 90% of frozen cells will become inactive. It is particularly important in the freezing and long-term storage of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Embryonic_stem_cells" title="Embryonic stem cells">embryonic stem cells</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hematopoietic_stem_cell" title="Hematopoietic stem cell">hematopoietic stem cells</a>, which are often frozen in a mixture of 10% DMSO, a freezing medium, and 30% <a href="/wiki/Fetal_bovine_serum" title="Fetal bovine serum">fetal bovine serum</a>. In the cryogenic freezing of heteroploid cell lines (MDCK, VERO, etc.) a mixture of 10% DMSO with 90% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EMEM" title="EMEM">EMEM</a> (70% EMEM + 30% fetal bovine serum + antibiotic mixture) is used. As part of an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autologous" title="Autologous">autologous</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bone_marrow_transplant" title="Bone marrow transplant">bone marrow transplant</a> the DMSO is re-infused along with the patient's own <a href="/wiki/Hematopoietic_stem_cell" title="Hematopoietic stem cell">hematopoietic stem cells</a>.
</p><p>DMSO is metabolized by <a href="/wiki/Disproportionation" title="Disproportionation">disproportionation</a> to <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfide" title="Dimethyl sulfide">dimethyl sulfide</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfone" title="Dimethyl sulfone">dimethyl sulfone</a>. It is subject to renal and pulmonary excretion. A possible side effect of DMSO is therefore elevated blood dimethyl sulfide, which may cause a blood borne <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halitosis" title="Halitosis">halitosis</a> symptom.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_medicine">Alternative medicine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimethyl_sulfoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Alternative medicine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>DMSO is marketed as an <a href="/wiki/Alternative_medicine" title="Alternative medicine">alternative medicine</a>. Its popularity as an alternative cure is stated to stem from a <i><a href="/wiki/60_Minutes" title="60 Minutes">60 Minutes</a></i> documentary featuring an early proponent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCAHF_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCAHF-26">[26]</a></sup> However, DMSO is an ingredient in some products listed by the U.S. FDA as fake cancer cures<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FDA-fake_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-FDA-fake-27">[27]</a></sup> and the FDA has had a running battle with distributors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCAHF_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-NCAHF-26">[26]</a></sup> One such distributor is Mildred Miller, who promoted DMSO for a variety of disorders and was consequently convicted of <a href="/wiki/Medicare_fraud" title="Medicare fraud">Medicare fraud</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>The use of DMSO as an alternative treatment for cancer is of particular concern, as it has been shown to interfere with a variety of <a href="/wiki/Chemotherapy" title="Chemotherapy">chemotherapy</a> drugs, including <a href="/wiki/Cisplatin" title="Cisplatin">cisplatin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carboplatin" title="Carboplatin">carboplatin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Oxaliplatin" title="Oxaliplatin">oxaliplatin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> There is insufficient evidence to support the hypothesis that DMSO has any effect,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> and most sources agree that its history of side effects when tested warrants caution when using it as a dietary supplement, for which it is marketed heavily with the <a href="/wiki/Quack_Miranda_warning" title="Quack Miranda warning">usual disclaimer</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Veterinary_medicine">Veterinary medicine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimethyl_sulfoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Veterinary medicine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>DMSO is commonly used in veterinary medicine as a <a href="/wiki/Liniment" title="Liniment">liniment</a> for <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horses</a>, alone or in combination with other ingredients.  In the latter case, often, the intended function of the DMSO is as a solvent, to carry the other ingredients across the skin.  Also in horses, DMSO is used intravenously, again alone or in combination with other drugs.  It is used alone for the treatment of increased intracranial pressure and/or cerebral edema in horses.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Taste">Taste</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimethyl_sulfoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Taste">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The perceived garlic taste upon skin contact with DMSO may be due to <a href="/wiki/Olfaction" title="Olfaction">nonolfactory</a> activation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transient_receptor_potential_cation_channel,_member_A1" title="Transient receptor potential cation channel, member A1">TRPA1</a> receptors in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trigeminal_ganglia" title="Trigeminal ganglia">trigeminal ganglia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>  Unlike <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_disulfide" title="Dimethyl disulfide">dimethyl</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diallyl_disulfide" title="Diallyl disulfide">diallyl</a> disulfide (also with odors resembling garlic), the <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfide" title="Dimethyl sulfide">mono-</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_trisulfide" title="Dimethyl trisulfide">tri-</a> sulfides (typically with foul odors), and other similar structures, the pure chemical DMSO is odorless.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimethyl_sulfoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicity">Toxicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimethyl_sulfoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Toxicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>DMSO is a non-toxic solvent with a <a href="/wiki/Median_lethal_dose" title="Median lethal dose">median lethal dose</a> higher than ethanol (DMSO: LD<sub>50</sub>, oral, rat, 14,500 mg/kg;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> ethanol: LD<sub>50</sub>, oral, rat, 7,060 mg/kg<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>).
</p><p>Early clinical trials with DMSO were stopped because of questions about its safety, especially its ability to harm the eye. The most commonly reported side effects include headaches and burning and itching on contact with the skin. Strong allergic reactions have been reported.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A complete citation is needed (January 2013)">full citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> DMSO can cause contaminants, toxins, and medicines to be absorbed through the skin, which may cause unexpected effects. DMSO is thought to increase the effects of blood thinners, steroids, heart medicines, sedatives, and other drugs. In some cases this could be harmful or dangerous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACS_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-ACS-34">[34]</a></sup> It is a developmental neurotoxin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>In Australia, it is listed as a <a href="/wiki/Standard_for_the_Uniform_Scheduling_of_Medicines_and_Poisons" title="Standard for the Uniform Scheduling of Medicines and Poisons">Schedule 4 (S4)</a> Drug, and a company has been prosecuted for adding it to products as a preservative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>Because DMSO easily penetrates the <a href="/wiki/Skin" title="Skin">skin</a>, substances dissolved in DMSO may be quickly absorbed. <a href="/wiki/Glove" title="Glove">Glove</a> selection is important when working with DMSO. <a href="/wiki/Butyl_rubber" title="Butyl rubber">Butyl rubber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fluoroelastomer" title="Fluoroelastomer">fluoroelastomer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neoprene" title="Neoprene">neoprene</a>, or thick (15<span class="nowrap"> </span><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mil_(imperial)" title="Mil (imperial)">mil</a>) <a href="/wiki/Latex" title="Latex">latex</a> gloves are recommended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nitrile_rubber" title="Nitrile rubber">Nitrile</a> gloves, which are very commonly used in chemical laboratories, may protect from brief contact but have been found to degrade rapidly with exposure to DMSO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>On September 9, 1965, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a></i> reported that a manufacturer of the chemical warned that the death of an Irish woman after undergoing DMSO treatment for a sprained wrist may have been due to the treatment, although no autopsy was done, nor was a causal relationship established.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Clinical_research" title="Clinical research">Clinical research</a> using DMSO was halted and did not begin again until the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a> (NAS) published findings in favor of DMSO in 1972.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fda.gov_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-fda.gov-40">[40]</a></sup>  In 1978, the US FDA approved DMSO for treating <a href="/wiki/Interstitial_cystitis" title="Interstitial cystitis">interstitial cystitis</a>.  In 1980, the US Congress held hearings on claims that the FDA was slow in approving DMSO for other medical uses.  In 2007, the US FDA granted "fast track" designation on clinical studies of DMSO's use in reducing brain tissue swelling following <a href="/wiki/Traumatic_brain_injury" title="Traumatic brain injury">traumatic brain injury</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fda.gov_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-fda.gov-40">[40]</a></sup> DMSO exposure to developing mouse brains can produce brain degeneration. This <a href="/wiki/Neurotoxicity" title="Neurotoxicity">neurotoxicity</a> could be detected at <a href="/wiki/Dose_(biochemistry)" title="Dose (biochemistry)">doses</a> as low as 0.3<span class="nowrap"> </span>mL/kg, a level exceeded in children exposed to DMSO during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bone_marrow_transplant" title="Bone marrow transplant">bone marrow transplant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>DMSO disposed into <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_sewer" title="Sanitary sewer">sewers</a> can also cause odor problems in municipal effluents: waste water <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> transform DMSO under <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(environmental)" title="Hypoxia (environmental)">hypoxic</a> (anoxic) conditions into <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfide" title="Dimethyl sulfide">dimethyl sulfide</a> (DMS) that has a strong disagreeable odor, similar to rotten cabbage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> However, chemically pure DMSO is odorless because of the lack of C-S-C (<a href="/wiki/Sulfide#Organic_chemistry" title="Sulfide">sulfide</a>) and C-S-H (<a href="/wiki/Thiol" title="Thiol">mercaptan</a>) linkages. Deodorization of DMSO is achieved by removing the odorous impurities it contains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Explosion_hazard">Explosion hazard</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimethyl_sulfoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Explosion hazard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dimethyl sulfoxide can produce an explosive reaction when exposed to <a href="/wiki/Acyl_chloride" title="Acyl chloride">acyl chlorides</a>; at a low temperature, this reaction produces the <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_agent" title="Oxidizing agent">oxidant</a> for <a href="/wiki/Swern_oxidation" title="Swern oxidation">Swern oxidation</a>.
</p><p>DMSO can decompose at the boiling temperature of 189 °C at normal pressure,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roth/Weller_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roth/Weller-44">[44]</a></sup> possibly leading to an explosion. The decomposition is catalyzed by acids and bases and therefore can be relevant at even lower temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roth/Weller_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Roth/Weller-44">[44]</a></sup> A strong to explosive reaction also takes place in combination with halogen compounds, metal nitrides, metal perchlorates, sodium hydride, periodic acid and fluorinating agents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roth/Weller_44-2"><a href="#cite_note-Roth/Weller-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Römpp_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Römpp-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A complete citation is needed (January 2013)">full citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
