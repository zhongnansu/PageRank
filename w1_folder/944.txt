<p><b>Sandstone</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clastic_sedimentary_rock" title="Clastic sedimentary rock">clastic sedimentary rock</a> composed mainly of <a href="/wiki/Grain_size" title="Grain size">sand-sized</a> (0.0625 to 2 mm) <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> particles or <a href="/wiki/Rock_fragment" title="Rock fragment">rock fragments</a>.
</p><p>Most sandstone is composed of <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a> or <a href="/wiki/Feldspar" title="Feldspar">feldspar</a> (both <a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">silicates</a>) because they are the most resistant minerals to <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a> processes at the Earth's surface, as seen in <a href="/wiki/Bowen%27s_reaction_series" title="Bowen's reaction series">Bowen's reaction series</a>. Like uncemented <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a>, sandstone may be any color due to impurities within the minerals, but the most common colors are tan, brown, yellow, red, grey, pink, white, and black. Since sandstone beds often form highly visible cliffs and other <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topographic</a> features, certain colors of sandstone have been strongly identified with certain regions.
</p><p>Rock formations that are primarily composed of sandstone usually allow the <a href="/wiki/Percolation" title="Percolation">percolation</a> of water and other fluids and are <a href="/wiki/Porosity" title="Porosity">porous</a> enough to store large quantities, making them valuable <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_reservoir" title="Petroleum reservoir">petroleum reservoirs</a>. Fine-grained aquifers, such as sandstones, are better able to filter out pollutants from the surface than are rocks with cracks and crevices, such as <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> or other rocks fractured by <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">seismic activity</a>.
</p><p>Quartz-bearing sandstone can be converted into <a href="/wiki/Quartzite" title="Quartzite">quartzite</a> through <a href="/wiki/Metamorphism" title="Metamorphism">metamorphism</a>, usually related to tectonic compression within <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orogenic_belts" title="Orogenic belts">orogenic belts</a>.
</p>

<p>Sandstones are <i><a href="/wiki/Clastic_rock" title="Clastic rock">clastic</a></i> in origin (as opposed to either <i>organic</i>, like <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, or <i>chemical</i>, like <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jasper" title="Jasper">jasper</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LF_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-LF-1">[1]</a></sup>
They are formed from <a href="/wiki/Cementation_(geology)" title="Cementation (geology)">cemented</a> grains that may either be fragments of a pre-existing rock or be mono-minerallic <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystals</a>. The cements binding these grains together are typically <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clays</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">silica</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particle_size_(grain_size)" title="Particle size (grain size)">Grain sizes</a> in sands are defined (in geology) within the range of 0.0625 mm to 2 mm (0.002–0.079 inches). Clays and sediments with smaller grain sizes not visible with the naked eye, including <a href="/wiki/Siltstone" title="Siltstone">siltstones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shale" title="Shale">shales</a>, are typically called <i><a href="/wiki/Argillaceous_minerals" title="Argillaceous minerals">argillaceous</a></i> sediments; rocks with larger grain sizes, including <a href="/wiki/Breccia" title="Breccia">breccias</a> and <a href="/wiki/Conglomerate_(geology)" title="Conglomerate (geology)">conglomerates</a>, are termed <i>rudaceous</i> sediments.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lower_antelope_2_md.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1231" data-file-width="821" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Lower_antelope_2_md.jpg/170px-Lower_antelope_2_md.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Lower_antelope_2_md.jpg/255px-Lower_antelope_2_md.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Lower_antelope_2_md.jpg/340px-Lower_antelope_2_md.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lower_antelope_2_md.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Red sandstone interior of <a href="/wiki/Antelope_Canyon" title="Antelope Canyon">Lower Antelope Canyon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a>, worn smooth by erosion from <a href="/wiki/Flash_flood" title="Flash flood">flash flooding</a> over thousands of years.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tafoni_at_Twyfelfontein_(Namibia).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2974" data-file-width="4685" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Tafoni_at_Twyfelfontein_%28Namibia%29.jpg/240px-Tafoni_at_Twyfelfontein_%28Namibia%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Tafoni_at_Twyfelfontein_%28Namibia%29.jpg/360px-Tafoni_at_Twyfelfontein_%28Namibia%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Tafoni_at_Twyfelfontein_%28Namibia%29.jpg/480px-Tafoni_at_Twyfelfontein_%28Namibia%29.jpg 2x" width="240"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tafoni_at_Twyfelfontein_(Namibia).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Tafoni" title="Tafoni">Tafoni</a> at <a href="/wiki/Twyfelfontein" title="Twyfelfontein">Twyfelfontein</a> in <a href="/wiki/Namibia" title="Namibia">Namibia</a></div></div></div>
<p>The formation of sandstone involves two principal stages. First, a layer or layers of sand accumulates as the result of <a href="/wiki/Sedimentation" title="Sedimentation">sedimentation</a>, either from water (as in a stream, lake, or sea) or from air (as in a desert). Typically, sedimentation occurs by the sand settling out from suspension; i.e., ceasing to be rolled or bounced along the bottom of a body of water or ground surface (e.g., in a desert or <a href="/wiki/Erg_(landform)" title="Erg (landform)">erg</a>). Finally, once it has accumulated, the sand becomes sandstone when it is <a href="/wiki/Compaction_(geology)" title="Compaction (geology)">compacted</a> by the pressure of overlying deposits and cemented by the precipitation of minerals within the pore spaces between sand grains.
</p><p>The most common cementing materials are silica and <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>, which are often derived either from dissolution or from alteration of the sand after it was buried. Colours will usually be tan or yellow (from a blend of the clear quartz with the dark amber feldspar content of the sand). A predominant additional colourant in the southwestern United States is <a href="/wiki/Iron_oxide" title="Iron oxide">iron oxide</a>, which imparts reddish tints ranging from pink to dark red (<a href="/wiki/Terracotta" title="Terracotta">terracotta</a>), with additional <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a> imparting a purplish <a href="/wiki/Hue" title="Hue">hue</a>. Red sandstones are also seen in the Southwest and West of <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a>, as well as central <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>. The regularity of the latter favours use as a source for <a href="/wiki/Masonry" title="Masonry">masonry</a>, either as a primary building material or as a facing stone, over other construction.
</p><p>The environment where it is deposited is crucial in determining the characteristics of the resulting sandstone, which, in finer detail, include its <i>grain size</i>, <i>sorting</i>, and <i>composition</i> and, in more general detail, include the rock geometry and sedimentary structures. Principal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sedimentary_depositional_environment" title="Sedimentary depositional environment">environments of deposition</a> may be split between terrestrial and marine, as illustrated by the following broad groupings:
</p>
<ul><li>Terrestrial environments</li></ul>
<ol><li><a href="/wiki/River" title="River">Rivers</a> (<a href="/wiki/Levee" title="Levee">levees</a>, point bars, channel sands)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alluvial_fan" title="Alluvial fan">Alluvial fans</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glacial_outwash" title="Glacial outwash">Glacial outwash</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">Lakes</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deserts" title="Deserts">Deserts</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sand_dune" title="Sand dune">sand dunes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Erg_(landform)" title="Erg (landform)">ergs</a>)</li></ol>
<ul><li>Marine environments</li></ul>
<ol><li><a href="/wiki/River_delta" title="River delta">Deltas</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Beach" title="Beach">Beach</a> and shoreface sands</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidal_flat" title="Tidal flat">Tidal flats</a></li>
<li>Offshore bars and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sand_waves" title="Sand waves">sand waves</a></li>
<li>Storm deposits (tempestites)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Turbidite" title="Turbidite">Turbidites</a> (submarine channels and fans)</li></ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Components">Components</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sandstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Components">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Framework_grains">Framework grains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sandstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Framework grains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:(1)Saunders_Quarry-1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2304" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/%281%29Saunders_Quarry-1.jpg/240px-%281%29Saunders_Quarry-1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/%281%29Saunders_Quarry-1.jpg/360px-%281%29Saunders_Quarry-1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/%281%29Saunders_Quarry-1.jpg/480px-%281%29Saunders_Quarry-1.jpg 2x" width="240"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:(1)Saunders_Quarry-1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Paradise Quarry, <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a>, Australia</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GrusSand.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/GrusSand.JPG/220px-GrusSand.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/GrusSand.JPG/330px-GrusSand.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/GrusSand.JPG/440px-GrusSand.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GrusSand.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Grus_(geology)" title="Grus (geology)">Grus</a> sand and the granitoid from which it is derived</div></div></div>
<p>Framework grains are sand-sized (0.0625-to-2-millimetre (0.00246 to 0.07874 in) diameter) detrital fragments that make up the bulk of a sandstone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stow_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stow-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pettijohn_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pettijohn-3">[3]</a></sup> These grains can be classified into several different categories based on their mineral composition:
</p>
<ul><li>Quartz framework grains are the dominant minerals in most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clastic_sedimentary_rock" title="Clastic sedimentary rock">clastic sedimentary rocks</a>; this is because they have exceptional physical properties, such as hardness and chemical stability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup> These physical properties allow the quartz grains to survive multiple recycling events, while also allowing the grains to display some degree of rounding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup> Quartz grains evolve from plutonic rock, which are felsic in origin and also from older sandstones that have been recycled.</li>
<li>Feldspathic framework grains are commonly the second most abundant mineral in sandstones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup> Feldspar can be divided into two smaller subdivisions: alkali feldspars and plagioclase feldspars. The different types of feldspar can be distinguished under a petrographic microscope.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup> Below is a description of the different types of feldspar.</li></ul>
<dl><dd><dl><dd><ul><li><a href="/wiki/Alkali_feldspar" title="Alkali feldspar">Alkali feldspar</a> is a group of minerals in which the chemical composition of the mineral can range from KAlSi<sub>3</sub>O<sub>8</sub> to NaAlSi<sub>3</sub>O<sub>8</sub>, this represents a complete solid solution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup></li></ul></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><dl><dd><ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plagioclase_feldspar" title="Plagioclase feldspar">Plagioclase feldspar</a> is a complex group of solid solution minerals that range in composition from NaAlSi<sub>3</sub>O<sub>8</sub> to CaAl<sub>2</sub>Si<sub>2</sub>O<sub>8</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup></li></ul></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LvMS-Lvm.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2328" data-file-width="1770" height="289" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/LvMS-Lvm.jpg/220px-LvMS-Lvm.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/LvMS-Lvm.jpg/330px-LvMS-Lvm.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/LvMS-Lvm.jpg/440px-LvMS-Lvm.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LvMS-Lvm.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Photomicrograph of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic" title="Volcanic">volcanic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sand_grain" title="Sand grain">sand grain</a>; upper picture is plane-polarised light, bottom picture is cross-polarised light, scale box at left-centre is 0.25 millimetre. This type of grain would be a main component of a lithic sandstone.</div></div></div>
<ul><li>Lithic framework grains are pieces of ancient source rock that have yet to weather away to individual mineral grains, called lithic fragments or clasts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup> Lithic fragments can be any fine-grained or coarse-grained igneous, metamorphic, or sedimentary rock,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup> although the most common lithic fragments found in sedimentary rocks are clasts of volcanic rocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>Accessory minerals are all other mineral grains in a sandstone; commonly these minerals make up just a small percentage of the grains in a sandstone. Common accessory minerals include micas (<a href="/wiki/Muscovite" title="Muscovite">muscovite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biotite" title="Biotite">biotite</a>), <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">olivine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pyroxene" title="Pyroxene">pyroxene</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Corundum" title="Corundum">corundum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-9"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prothero_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero-5">[5]</a></sup> Many of these accessory grains are more dense than the silicates that make up the bulk of the rock. These <b>heavy minerals</b> are commonly resistant to weathering and can be used as an indicator of sandstone maturity through the <a href="/wiki/ZTR_index" title="ZTR index">ZTR index</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Common heavy minerals include <a href="/wiki/Zircon" title="Zircon">zircon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tourmaline" title="Tourmaline">tourmaline</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rutile" title="Rutile">rutile</a> (hence <i>ZTR</i>), <a href="/wiki/Garnet" title="Garnet">garnet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnetite" title="Magnetite">magnetite</a>, or other dense, resistant minerals derived from the source rock.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Matrix">Matrix</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sandstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Matrix">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Matrix_(geology)" title="Matrix (geology)">Matrix</a> is very fine material, which is present within interstitial pore space between the framework grains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-10"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup> The interstitial pore space can be classified into two varieties. One is to call the sandstone an arenite, and the other is to call it a wacke. Below is a definition of the differences between the two matrices:
</p>
<ul><li>Arenites are texturally <i>clean</i> sandstones that are free of or have very little matrix.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prothero_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Prothero-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>Wackes are texturally <i>dirty</i> sandstones that have a significant amount of matrix.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pettijohn_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pettijohn-3">[3]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cement">Cement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sandstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cement is what binds the siliciclastic framework grains together. Cement is a secondary mineral that forms after deposition and during burial of the sandstone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-11"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup> These cementing materials may be either silicate minerals or non-silicate minerals, such as calcite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-12"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Silica cement can consist of either quartz or <a href="/wiki/Opal" title="Opal">opal</a> minerals. Quartz is the most common silicate mineral that acts as cement. In sandstone where there is silica cement present, the quartz grains are attached to cement, which creates a rim around the quartz grain called overgrowth. The overgrowth retains the same crystallographic continuity of quartz framework grain that is being cemented. Opal cement is found in sandstones that are rich in volcanogenic materials, and very rarely is in other sandstones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-13"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>Calcite cement is the most common carbonate cement. Calcite cement is an assortment of smaller calcite crystals. The cement adheres itself to the framework grains, this adhesion is what causes the framework grains to be adhered together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-14"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>Other minerals that act as cements include: <a href="/wiki/Hematite" title="Hematite">hematite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Limonite" title="Limonite">limonite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Feldspar" title="Feldspar">feldspars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anhydrite" title="Anhydrite">anhydrite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barite" title="Barite">barite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clay_minerals" title="Clay minerals">clay minerals</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Zeolite" title="Zeolite">zeolite</a> minerals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-15"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pore_space">Pore space</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sandstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pore space">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pore space includes the open spaces within a rock or a soil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackson_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jackson-7">[7]</a></sup> The pore space in a rock has a direct relationship to the <a href="/wiki/Porosity" title="Porosity">porosity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Permeability_(earth_sciences)" title="Permeability (earth sciences)">permeability</a> of the rock. The porosity and permeability are directly influenced by the way the sand grains are packed together.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-16"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Porosity is the percentage of bulk volume that is inhabited by interstices within a given rock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackson_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jackson-7">[7]</a></sup> Porosity is directly influenced by the packing of even-sized spherical grains, rearranged from loosely packed to tightest packed in sandstones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-17"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>Permeability is the rate in which water or other fluids flow through the rock. For <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a>, work permeability may be measured in gallons per day through a one square foot cross section under a unit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydraulic_gradient" title="Hydraulic gradient">hydraulic gradient</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackson_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jackson-7">[7]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_sandstone">Types of sandstone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sandstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Types of sandstone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:QFLtriangle.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="380" data-file-width="381" height="219" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/QFLtriangle.jpg/220px-QFLtriangle.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/QFLtriangle.jpg/330px-QFLtriangle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c6/QFLtriangle.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:QFLtriangle.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Schematic QFL diagram showing tectonic provinces</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Logan_Formation_Cross_Bedding_Scour.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2140" data-file-width="2716" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Logan_Formation_Cross_Bedding_Scour.jpg/220px-Logan_Formation_Cross_Bedding_Scour.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Logan_Formation_Cross_Bedding_Scour.jpg/330px-Logan_Formation_Cross_Bedding_Scour.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Logan_Formation_Cross_Bedding_Scour.jpg/440px-Logan_Formation_Cross_Bedding_Scour.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Logan_Formation_Cross_Bedding_Scour.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cross-bedding and scour in sandstone of the <a href="/wiki/Logan_Formation" title="Logan Formation">Logan Formation</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lower_Carboniferous" title="Lower Carboniferous">Lower Carboniferous</a>) of Jackson County, <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a></div></div></div>
<p>All sandstones are composed of the same general minerals. These minerals make up the framework components of the sandstones. Such components are quartz, feldspars,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and lithic fragments. Matrix may also be present in the interstitial spaces between the framework grains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-18"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup> Below is a list of several major groups of sandstones. These groups are divided based on <a href="/wiki/Mineralogy" title="Mineralogy">mineralogy</a> and texture. Even though sandstones have very simple compositions which are based on framework grains, geologists have not been able to agree on a specific, right way, to classify sandstones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-19"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup> Sandstone classifications are typically done by point-counting a <a href="/wiki/Thin_section" title="Thin section">thin section</a> using a method like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gazzi-Dickinson_Method" title="Gazzi-Dickinson Method">Gazzi-Dickinson Method</a>. The composition of a sandstone can have important information regarding the genesis of the sediment when used with a triangular <i>Q</i>uartz, <i>F</i>eldspar, <i>L</i>ithic fragment (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/QFL_diagrams" title="QFL diagrams">QFL diagrams</a>). Many geologists, however, do not agree on how to separate the triangle parts into the single components so that the framework grains can be plotted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-20"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup> Therefore, there have been many published ways to classify sandstones, all of which are similar in their general format.
</p><p>Visual aids are diagrams that allow geologists to interpret different characteristics about a sandstone. The following QFL chart and the sandstone provenance model correspond with each other therefore, when the QFL chart is plotted those points can then be plotted on the sandstone provenance model. The stage of textural maturity chart illustrates the different stages that a sandstone goes through.
</p>
<ul><li>A QFL chart is a representation of the framework grains and matrix that is present in a sandstone. This chart is similar to those used in igneous petrology. When plotted correctly, this model of analysis creates for a meaningful quantitative classification of sandstones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carozzi_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carozzi-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>A sandstone provenance chart allows geologists to visually interpret the different types of places from which sandstones can originate.</li>
<li>A stage of textural maturity is a chart that shows the different stages of sandstones. This chart shows the difference between immature, submature, mature, and supermature sandstones. As the sandstone becomes more mature, grains become more rounded, and there is less clay in the matrix of the rock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-21"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span id="Dott.27s_classification_scheme"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Dott's_classification_scheme">Dott's classification scheme</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sandstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Dott's classification scheme">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dott's (1964) sandstone classification scheme is one of many such schemes used by geologists for classifying sandstones. Dott's scheme is a modification of Gilbert's classification of silicate sandstones, and it incorporates R.L. Folk's dual textural and compositional maturity concepts into one classification system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dott_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dott-10">[10]</a></sup> The philosophy behind combining Gilbert's and R. L. Folk's schemes is that it is better able to "portray the continuous nature of textural variation from mudstone to arenite and from stable to unstable grain composition".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dott_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dott-10">[10]</a></sup> Dott's classification scheme is based on the mineralogy of framework grains, and on the type of matrix present in between the framework grains.
</p><p>In this specific classification scheme, Dott has set the boundary between arenite and wackes at 15% matrix. In addition, Dott also breaks up the different types of framework grains that can be present in a sandstone into three major categories: quartz, feldspar, and lithic grains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-22"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Arenites are types of sandstone that have less than 15% clay matrix in between the framework grains.
<ul><li>Quartz arenites are sandstones that contain more than 90% of siliceous grains. Grains can include <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chert" title="Chert">chert</a> rock fragments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-23"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup> Quartz arenites are texturally mature to supermature sandstones. These pure quartz sands result from extensive <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a> that occurred before and during transport. This weathering removed everything but quartz grains, the most stable <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a>. They are commonly affiliated with rocks that are deposited in a stable cratonic environment, such as aeolian <a href="/wiki/Beach" title="Beach">beaches</a> or shelf environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-24"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup> Quartz arenites emanate from multiple recycling of quarts grains, generally as sedimentary source rocks and less regularly as first-cycle deposits derived from primary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Igneous" title="Igneous">igneous</a> or <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphic rocks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-25"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>Feldspathic arenites are sandstones that contain less than 90% quartz, and more feldspar than unstable lithic fragments, and minor accessory minerals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-26"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup> Feldspathic sandstones are commonly immature or sub-mature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-27"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup> These sandstones occur in association with cratonic or stable shelf settings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-28"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup> Feldspathic sandstones are derived from granitic-type, primary crystalline, rocks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-29"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup> If the sandstone is dominantly plagioclase, then it is igneous in origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-30"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>Lithic arenites are characterised by generally high content of unstable lithic fragments. Examples include volcanic and metamorphic clasts, though stable clasts such as chert are common in lithic arenites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-31"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup> This type of rock contains less than 90% quartz grains and more unstable rock fragments than feldspars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-32"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup> They are commonly immature to submature texturally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-33"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup> They are associated with fluvial conglomerates and other fluvial deposits, or in deeper water marine conglomerates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-34"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup> They are formed under conditions that produce large volumes of unstable material, derived from fine-grained rocks, mostly <a href="/wiki/Shale" title="Shale">shales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_rock" title="Volcanic rock">volcanic rocks</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphic rock</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-35"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup></li></ul></li>
<li>Wackes are sandstones that contain more than 15% clay matrix between framework grains.
<ul><li>Quartz wackes are uncommon because quartz arenites are texturally mature to supermature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-36"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>Felspathic wackes are feldspathic sandstone that contain a matrix that is greater than 15%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-37"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>Lithic wacke is a sandstone in which the matrix greater than 15%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boggs_4-38"><a href="#cite_note-Boggs-4">[4]</a></sup></li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Arkose" title="Arkose">Arkose</a> sandstones are more than 25 percent <a href="/wiki/Feldspar" title="Feldspar">feldspar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LF_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-LF-1">[1]</a></sup> The grains tend to be poorly rounded and less well sorted than those of pure quartz sandstones. These feldspar-rich sandstones come from rapidly eroding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Granitic" title="Granitic">granitic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphic</a> <a href="/wiki/Terrain" title="Terrain">terrains</a> where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_weathering" title="Chemical weathering">chemical weathering</a> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subordinate" title="Subordinate">subordinate</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_weathering" title="Physical weathering">physical weathering</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Greywacke" title="Greywacke">Greywacke</a> sandstones are a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterogeneous" title="Heterogeneous">heterogeneous</a> mixture of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lithic_Fragment_(geology)" title="Lithic Fragment (geology)">lithic fragments</a> and angular grains of quartz and feldspar or grains surrounded by a fine-grained <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clay_mineral" title="Clay mineral">clay</a> <a href="/wiki/Matrix_(geology)" title="Matrix (geology)">matrix</a>. Much of this matrix is formed by relatively soft fragments, such as <a href="/wiki/Shale" title="Shale">shale</a> and some volcanic rocks, that are chemically altered and physically compacted after deep burial of the sandstone formation.</li></ul>

<p>Sandstone has been used for domestic construction and housewares since prehistoric times, and continues to be used.
</p><p>Sandstone was a popular building material from ancient times. It is relatively soft, making it easy to carve. It has been widely used around the world in constructing temples, homes, and other buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> It has also been used for artistic purposes to create ornamental fountains and statues.
</p><p>Some sandstones are resistant to <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a>, yet are easy to work. This makes sandstone a common <a href="/wiki/Building_material" title="Building material">building</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pavement_(material)" title="Pavement (material)">paving</a> material including in <a href="/wiki/Asphalt_concrete" title="Asphalt concrete">asphalt concrete</a>. However, some that have been used in the past, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collyhurst_sandstone" title="Collyhurst sandstone">Collyhurst sandstone</a> used in <a href="/wiki/North_West_England" title="North West England">North West England</a>, have been found less resistant, necessitating repair and replacement in older buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Because of the hardness of individual grains, uniformity of grain size and <a href="/wiki/Friability" title="Friability">friability</a> of their structure, some types of sandstone are excellent materials from which to make <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grindstone_(tool)" title="Grindstone (tool)">grindstones</a>, for sharpening blades and other implements. Non-friable sandstone can be used to make grindstones for grinding grain, e.g., <a href="/wiki/Gritstone" title="Gritstone">gritstone</a>.
</p><p>A type of pure quartz sandstone, the orthoquartzite, with more of 90–95 percent of quartz,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> has been proposed for nomination to the <a href="/wiki/Global_Heritage_Stone_Resource" title="Global Heritage Stone Resource">Global Heritage Stone Resource</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-14">[14]</a></sup> In some regions of Argentina, the orthoquartzite-stoned <a href="/wiki/Facade" title="Facade">facade</a> is one of the main features of the <a href="/wiki/Mar_del_Plata_style" title="Mar del Plata style">Mar del Plata style</a> bungalows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
