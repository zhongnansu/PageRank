<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Industrial gas</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Gaseous materials produced for use in industry</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gas_regulator.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Gas_regulator.jpg/220px-Gas_regulator.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Gas_regulator.jpg/330px-Gas_regulator.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Gas_regulator.jpg/440px-Gas_regulator.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gas_regulator.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A gas regulator attached to a nitrogen cylinder.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Industrial gases</b> are <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gaseous</a> materials that are <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufactured</a> for use in <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">Industry</a>. The principal gases provided are <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Acetylene" title="Acetylene">acetylene</a>; although a huge variety of gases and mixtures are available in gas cylinders. The industry producing these gases is known as the industrial gases industry, which is seen as also encompassing the supply of equipment and technology to produce and use the gases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EIGA_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-EIGA-1">[1]</a></sup> Their production is a part of the wider <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_Industry" title="Chemical Industry">chemical Industry</a> (where industrial gases are often seen as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Specialty_chemicals" title="Specialty chemicals">specialty chemicals</a>").
</p><p>Industrial gases are used in a wide range of industries, which include <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_industry" title="Petroleum industry">oil and gas</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petrochemicals" title="Petrochemicals">petrochemicals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemical_industry" title="Chemical industry">chemicals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_industry" title="Electric power industry">power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steelmaking" title="Steelmaking">steelmaking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmental protection</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_drug" title="Pharmaceutical drug">pharmaceuticals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">biotechnology</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_Industry" title="Food Industry">food</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_industry" title="Water industry">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aerospace" title="Aerospace">aerospace</a>. Industrial gas is sold to other industrial enterprises; typically comprising large orders to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corporate" title="Corporate">corporate</a> industrial clients, covering a size range from building a process facility or pipeline down to cylinder gas supply.
</p><p>Some <a href="/wiki/Tradesman" title="Tradesman">trade</a> scale business is done, typically through tied <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agent_(law)" title="Agent (law)">local agents</a> who are supplied <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wholesale" title="Wholesale">wholesale</a>. This business covers the <a href="/wiki/Sales" title="Sales">sale</a> or <a href="/wiki/Renting" title="Renting">hire</a> of gas cylinders and associated equipment to <a href="/wiki/Tradesman" title="Tradesman">tradesmen</a> and occasionally the general public. This includes products such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tethered_helium_balloon" title="Tethered helium balloon">balloon helium</a>, dispensing gases for <a href="/wiki/Keg" title="Keg">beer kegs</a>, welding gases and welding equipment, LPG and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_therapy" title="Oxygen therapy">medical oxygen</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Retail" title="Retail">Retail</a> sales of small scale gas supply are not confined to just the industrial gas companies or their agents. A wide variety of hand-carried small gas containers, which may be called cylinders, bottles, cartridges, capsules or canisters are available to supply LPG, butane, propane, carbon dioxide or nitrous oxide.  Examples are <a href="/wiki/Whipped-cream_charger" title="Whipped-cream charger">Whipped-cream chargers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Powerlet" title="Powerlet">powerlets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Campingaz" title="Campingaz">campingaz</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sodastream" title="Sodastream">sodastream</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history_of_gases">Early history of gases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industrial_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early history of gases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Des8.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2964" data-file-width="3000" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Des8.jpg/220px-Des8.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Des8.jpg/330px-Des8.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Des8.jpg/440px-Des8.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Des8.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Blowing air at a spark</div></div></div><p> The first gas from the natural environment used by humans was almost certainly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a> when it was discovered that blowing on or fanning a fire made it burn brighter. Humans also used the <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">warm gases from a fire</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smoking_food" title="Smoking food">smoke</a> foods and <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a> from boiling water to cook foods.
</p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Weizenbier.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1486" data-file-width="750" height="337" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Weizenbier.jpg/170px-Weizenbier.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Weizenbier.jpg/255px-Weizenbier.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Weizenbier.jpg/340px-Weizenbier.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Weizenbier.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bubbles of carbon dioxide form a froth on fermenting liquids such as beer</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">Carbon dioxide</a> has been known from ancient times as the byproduct of <a href="/wiki/Fermentation_in_food_processing" title="Fermentation in food processing">fermentation</a>, particularly for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" title="Alcoholic beverage">beverages</a>, which was first documented dating from 7000–6600 B.C. in <a href="/wiki/Jiahu" title="Jiahu">Jiahu</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neolithic_China" title="Neolithic China">China</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcgovern_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcgovern-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">Natural gas</a> was used by the Chinese in about 500 B.C. when they discovered the potential to transport gas seeping from the ground in crude pipelines of bamboo to where it was used to boil sea water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">Sulfur dioxide</a> was used by the Romans in winemaking as it had been discovered that burning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Candles" title="Candles">candles</a> made of sulfur <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> inside empty wine vessels would keep them fresh and prevent them gaining a vinegar smell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hamburg_Museum_2010-1207-217.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="516" height="337" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Hamburg_Museum_2010-1207-217.jpg/170px-Hamburg_Museum_2010-1207-217.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Hamburg_Museum_2010-1207-217.jpg/255px-Hamburg_Museum_2010-1207-217.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Hamburg_Museum_2010-1207-217.jpg/340px-Hamburg_Museum_2010-1207-217.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hamburg_Museum_2010-1207-217.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/D%C3%B6bereiner%27s_lamp" title="Döbereiner's lamp">Döbereiner's hydrogen lamp</a> .</div></div></div>
<p>Early understanding consisted of <a href="/wiki/Empirical_evidence" title="Empirical evidence">empirical evidence</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Protoscience" title="Protoscience">protoscience</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">alchemy</a>; however with the advent of <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific method</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  and the <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> of <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, these gases became positively identified and understood.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kipp_Apparat.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="712" data-file-width="475" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Kipp_Apparat.jpg/220px-Kipp_Apparat.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Kipp_Apparat.jpg/330px-Kipp_Apparat.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Kipp_Apparat.jpg/440px-Kipp_Apparat.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kipp_Apparat.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Kipp's apparatus</div></div></div><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carbide_lamp_lit.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="998" data-file-width="904" height="243" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Carbide_lamp_lit.jpg/220px-Carbide_lamp_lit.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Carbide_lamp_lit.jpg/330px-Carbide_lamp_lit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Carbide_lamp_lit.jpg/440px-Carbide_lamp_lit.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Carbide_lamp_lit.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Acetylene flame <a href="/wiki/Carbide_lamp" title="Carbide lamp">carbide lamp</a></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/History_of_chemistry" title="History of chemistry">history of chemistry</a> tells us that a number of gases were identified and either discovered or first made in relatively pure form during the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> of the 18th and 19th centuries by notable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemists" title="Chemists">chemists</a> in their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laboratories" title="Laboratories">laboratories</a>. The timeline of attributed discovery for various gases are carbon dioxide (1754),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> hydrogen (1766),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-krogt_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-krogt-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pt_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-pt-9">[9]</a></sup> nitrogen (1772),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-krogt_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-krogt-8">[8]</a></sup> nitrous oxide (1772) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-N2O_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-N2O-10">[10]</a></sup>
, oxygen (1773) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-krogt_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-krogt-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
, ammonia (1774),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>  chlorine (1774),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-krogt_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-krogt-8">[8]</a></sup> methane (1776),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>  hydrogen sulfide (1777),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> carbon monoxide (1800),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> hydrogen chloride (1810),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> acetylene (1836),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> helium (1868) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-krogt_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-krogt-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Helium_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Helium-19">[19]</a></sup> fluorine (1886),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-krogt_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-krogt-8">[8]</a></sup> argon (1894),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-krogt_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-krogt-8">[8]</a></sup> krypton, neon and xenon (1898) 
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-krogt_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-krogt-8">[8]</a></sup> and radon (1899).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-krogt_8-8"><a href="#cite_note-krogt-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Carbon dioxide, hydrogen, nitrous oxide, oxygen, ammonia, chlorine, sulfur dioxide and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_manufactured_gas" title="History of manufactured gas">manufactured fuel gas</a> were already being used during the 19th century, and mainly had uses in <a href="/wiki/E_number" title="E number">food</a>, <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">refrigeration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medical_gas_supply" title="Medical gas supply">medicine</a>, and for <a href="/wiki/Fuel_gas" title="Fuel gas">fuel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gas_lighting" title="Gas lighting">gas lighting</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CGA100_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-CGA100-20">[20]</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/Carbonated_water" title="Carbonated water">carbonated water</a> was being made from 1772 and commercially from 1783, chlorine was first used to bleach textiles in 1785 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chlorine_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chlorine-21">[21]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a> was first used for dentistry anaethesia in 1844.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-N2O_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-N2O-10">[10]</a></sup> At this time gases were often generated for immediate use by <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reactions</a>. A notable example of a generator is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kipps_apparatus" title="Kipps apparatus">Kipps apparatus</a> which was invented in 1844 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> and could be used to generate gases such as hydrogen, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a>, chlorine, acetylene and carbon dioxide by simple <a href="/wiki/Gas_evolution_reaction" title="Gas evolution reaction">gas evolution reactions</a>. Acetylene was manufactured commercially from 1893 and acetylene generators were used from about 1898 to produce gas for <a href="/wiki/Gas_stove" title="Gas stove">gas cooking</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gas_lighting" title="Gas lighting">gas lighting</a>, however electricity took over as more practical for lighting and once LPG was produced commercially from 1912, the use of acetylene for cooking declined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CGA100_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-CGA100-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seltzogene.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="581" height="344" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Seltzogene.jpg/200px-Seltzogene.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Seltzogene.jpg/300px-Seltzogene.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Seltzogene.jpg/400px-Seltzogene.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Seltzogene.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Late Victorian <a href="/wiki/Gasogene" title="Gasogene">Gasogene</a> for producing carbonated water</div></div></div>
<p>Once gases had been discovered and produced in modest quantities, the process of <a href="/wiki/Industrialisation" title="Industrialisation">industrialisation</a> spurred on <a href="/wiki/Innovation" title="Innovation">innovation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Invention" title="Invention">invention</a> of <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a> to produce larger quantities of these gases. Notable developments in the industrial production of gases include the <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water" title="Electrolysis of water">electrolysis of water</a> to produce hydrogen (in 1869) and oxygen (from 1888), the <a href="/wiki/Brin_process" title="Brin process">Brin process</a> for oxygen production which was invented in the 1884, the <a href="/wiki/Chloralkali_process" title="Chloralkali process">chloralkali process</a> to produce chlorine in 1892 and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haber_Process" title="Haber Process">Haber Process</a> to produce ammonia in 1908.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NH3_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-NH3-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>The development of uses in refrigeration also enabled advances in <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">air conditioning</a> and the liquefaction of gases. Carbon dioxide was first liquefied in 1823. The first <a href="/wiki/Vapor-compression_refrigeration" title="Vapor-compression refrigeration">Vapor-compression refrigeration</a> cycle using <a href="/wiki/Ether" title="Ether">ether</a> was invented by <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Perkins" title="Jacob Perkins">Jacob Perkins</a> in 1834 and a similar cycle using <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> was invented in 1873 and another with sulfur dioxide in 1876.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CGA100_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-CGA100-20">[20]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">Liquid oxygen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liquid_nitrogen" title="Liquid nitrogen">Liquid nitrogen</a> were both first made in 1883; <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">Liquid hydrogen</a> was first made in 1898 and <a href="/wiki/Liquid_helium" title="Liquid helium">liquid helium</a> in 1908. <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_petroleum_gas" title="Liquefied petroleum gas">LPG</a> was first made in 1910. A patent for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LNG" title="LNG">LNG</a> was filed in 1914 with the first commercial production in 1917.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although no one event marks the beginning of the industrial gas industry, many would take it to be the 1880s with the construction of the first high pressure <a href="/wiki/Gas_cylinder" title="Gas cylinder">gas cylinders</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CGA100_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-CGA100-20">[20]</a></sup> Initially cylinders were mostly used for carbon dioxide in <a href="/wiki/Carbonation" title="Carbonation">carbonation</a> or dispensing of beverages. 
In 1895 refrigeration compression cycles were further developed to enable <a href="/wiki/Liquid_air" title="Liquid air">the liquefaction of air</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cool_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cool-25">[25]</a></sup> most notably by <a href="/wiki/Carl_von_Linde" title="Carl von Linde">Carl von Linde</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> allowing larger quantities of oxygen production and in 1896 the discovery that large quantities of acetylene could be dissolved in <a href="/wiki/Acetone" title="Acetone">acetone</a> and rendered nonexplosive allowed the safe bottling of acetylene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>A particularly important use was the development of <a href="/wiki/Welding" title="Welding">welding</a> and metal cutting done with oxygen and acetylene from the early 1900s.
As production processes for other gases were developed many more gases came to be sold in cylinders without the need for a <a href="/wiki/Gas_generator" title="Gas generator">gas generator</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_production_technology">Gas production technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industrial_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Gas production technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:472px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coldbox.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2616" data-file-width="3488" height="353" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Coldbox.JPG/470px-Coldbox.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Coldbox.JPG/705px-Coldbox.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Coldbox.JPG/940px-Coldbox.JPG 2x" width="470"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coldbox.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Distillation column in a cryogenic air separation plant</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Air_separation" title="Air separation">Air separation</a> plants <a href="/wiki/Refining" title="Refining">refine</a> air in a <a href="/wiki/Separation_process" title="Separation process">separation process</a> and so allow the bulk production of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a> in addition to oxygen - these three are often also produced as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryogenic" title="Cryogenic">cryogenic</a> <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a>. To achieve the required low <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distillation</a> temperatures, an Air Separation Unit (ASU) uses a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Refrigeration_cycle" title="Refrigeration cycle">refrigeration cycle</a> that operates by means of the <a href="/wiki/Joule%E2%80%93Thomson_effect" title="Joule–Thomson effect">Joule–Thomson effect</a>. 
In addition to the main air gases, air separation is also the only practical source for production of the <a href="/wiki/Trace_gas" title="Trace gas">rare</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noble_gases" title="Noble gases">noble gases</a> <a href="/wiki/Neon" title="Neon">neon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Krypton" title="Krypton">krypton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xenon" title="Xenon">xenon</a>.
</p><p>Cryogenic technologies also allow the <a href="/wiki/Liquefaction_of_gases" title="Liquefaction of gases">liquefaction</a>  of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LNG" title="LNG">natural gas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">hydrogen</a>  and <a href="/wiki/Liquid_helium" title="Liquid helium">helium</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Natural-gas_processing" title="Natural-gas processing">natural-gas processing</a>, cryogenic technologies are used to remove nitrogen from natural gas in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_Rejection_Unit" title="Nitrogen Rejection Unit">Nitrogen Rejection Unit</a>; a process that can also be used to produce <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> from natural gas - if the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_gas_fields" title="Natural gas fields">natural gas fields</a> contain sufficient helium to make this economic. The larger industrial gas companies have often invested in extensive <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a> libraries in all fields of their business, but particularly in cryogenics.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hydrogen.from.Coal.gasification_tampa.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="126" data-file-width="200" height="126" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4b/Hydrogen.from.Coal.gasification_tampa.jpg" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hydrogen.from.Coal.gasification_tampa.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Gasification</div></div></div>
<p>The other principal production <a href="/wiki/Chemical_engineering" title="Chemical engineering">technology</a> in the industry is Reforming. <a href="/wiki/Steam_reforming" title="Steam reforming">Steam reforming</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_process" title="Chemical process">chemical process</a> used to convert natural gas and <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a> into a <a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">syngas</a> containing <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byproduct" title="Byproduct">byproduct</a>. <a href="/wiki/Partial_oxidation" title="Partial oxidation">Partial oxidation</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autothermal_reforming" title="Autothermal reforming">autothermal reforming</a> are similar processes but these also require oxygen from an ASU. Synthesis gas is often a precursor to the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_synthesis" title="Chemical synthesis">chemical synthesis</a> of ammonia or <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>. The carbon dioxide produced is an <a href="/wiki/Acid_gas" title="Acid gas">acid gas</a> and is most commonly removed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amine_treating" title="Amine treating">amine treating</a>. This separated carbon dioxide can potentially be <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">sequestrated</a> to a <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture</a> <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_reservoir" title="Petroleum reservoir">reservoir</a> or used for <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">Enhanced oil recovery</a>.
</p><p>Air Separation and hydrogen reforming technologies are the cornerstone of the industrial gases industry and also form part of the technologies required for many fuel <a href="/wiki/Gasification" title="Gasification">gasification</a> ( including <a href="/wiki/Integrated_gasification_combined_cycle" title="Integrated gasification combined cycle">IGCC</a>), <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">cogeneration</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fischer%E2%80%93Tropsch_process" title="Fischer–Tropsch process">Fischer-Tropsch</a> <a href="/wiki/Gas_to_liquids" title="Gas to liquids">gas to liquids</a> schemes. Hydrogen has many <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_production" title="Hydrogen production">production methods</a> and is touted as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_neutral" title="Carbon neutral">carbon neutral</a> <a href="/wiki/Alternative_fuel" title="Alternative fuel">alternative fuel</a> to hydrocarbons, whilst <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">liquid hydrogen</a> is used by NASA in the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rocket_fuel" title="Rocket fuel">rocket fuel</a>; see <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_economy" title="Hydrogen economy">hydrogen economy</a> for more information on hydrogens uses.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gas_Control_Systems,_INC_PSA.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3264" data-file-width="2448" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Gas_Control_Systems%2C_INC_PSA.jpg/220px-Gas_Control_Systems%2C_INC_PSA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Gas_Control_Systems%2C_INC_PSA.jpg/330px-Gas_Control_Systems%2C_INC_PSA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Gas_Control_Systems%2C_INC_PSA.jpg/440px-Gas_Control_Systems%2C_INC_PSA.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gas_Control_Systems,_INC_PSA.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A nitrogen generator</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Membrane_nitrogen_generator.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="996" data-file-width="1000" height="219" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Membrane_nitrogen_generator.jpg/220px-Membrane_nitrogen_generator.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Membrane_nitrogen_generator.jpg/330px-Membrane_nitrogen_generator.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Membrane_nitrogen_generator.jpg/440px-Membrane_nitrogen_generator.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Membrane_nitrogen_generator.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Membrane nitrogen generator</div></div></div>
<p>Simpler <a href="/wiki/Gas_separation" title="Gas separation">gas separation</a> technologies, such as <a href="/wiki/Membrane_gas_separation" title="Membrane gas separation">membranes</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_sieves" title="Molecular sieves">molecular sieves</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Pressure_swing_adsorption" title="Pressure swing adsorption">pressure swing adsorption</a> or <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_swing_adsorption" title="Vacuum swing adsorption">vacuum swing adsorption</a> are also used to produce low purity air gases in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nitrogen_generators" title="Nitrogen generators">nitrogen generators</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_plant" title="Oxygen plant">oxygen plants</a>. Other examples producing smaller amounts of gas are  <a href="/wiki/Chemical_oxygen_generator" title="Chemical oxygen generator">chemical oxygen generators</a> or <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_concentrator" title="Oxygen concentrator">oxygen concentrators</a>.
</p><p>In addition to the major gases produced by air separation and syngas reforming, the industry provides many other gases. Some gases are simply byproducts from other industries and others are sometimes bought from other larger chemical producers, refined and repackaged; although a few have their own production processes. Examples are hydrogen chloride produced by burning hydrogen in chlorine, nitrous oxide produced by <a href="/wiki/Thermal_decomposition" title="Thermal decomposition">thermal decomposition</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_nitrate" title="Ammonium nitrate">ammonium nitrate</a> when gently heated, <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> for the production of fluorine, and electrical <a href="/wiki/Corona_discharge" title="Corona discharge">corona discharge</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> from air or oxygen.
</p><p>Related services and technology can be supplied such as <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a>, which is often provided in <a href="/wiki/Medical_gas_supply" title="Medical gas supply">hospital gas systems</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_purifiers" title="Air purifiers">purified</a> <a href="/wiki/Compressed_air" title="Compressed air">compressed air</a>; or <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">refrigeration</a>. Another unusual system is the <a href="/wiki/Inert_gas_generator" title="Inert gas generator">inert gas generator</a>. Some industrial gas companies may also supply related <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemicals" title="Chemicals">chemicals</a>, particularly liquids such as <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">bromine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethylene_oxide" title="Ethylene oxide">ethylene oxide</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_distribution">Gas distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industrial_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Gas distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mode_of_gas_supply">Mode of gas supply</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industrial_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mode of gas supply">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Compressed_hydrogen_tube_trailer.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="111" data-file-width="200" height="111" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/07/Compressed_hydrogen_tube_trailer.jpg" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Compressed_hydrogen_tube_trailer.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Compressed_hydrogen" title="Compressed hydrogen">Compressed hydrogen</a> tube trailer</div></div></div>
<p>Most materials that are gaseous at ambient temperature and pressure are supplied as compressed gas. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gas_compressor" title="Gas compressor">gas compressor</a> is used to compress the gas into storage <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pressure_vessels" title="Pressure vessels">pressure vessels</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Powerlet" title="Powerlet">gas canisters</a>, gas cylinders or <a href="/wiki/Compressed_hydrogen_tube_trailer" title="Compressed hydrogen tube trailer">tube trailers</a>) through <a href="/wiki/Piping" title="Piping">piping</a> systems.  Gas cylinders are by far the most common gas storage <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and large numbers are produced at a <a href="/wiki/Filling_carousel" title="Filling carousel">"cylinder fill"</a> facility.
</p><p>However not all industrial gases are supplied in the <a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">gaseous phase</a>. A few gases are <a href="/wiki/Vapor" title="Vapor">vapors</a> that can be liquefied at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ambient_temperature" title="Ambient temperature">ambient temperature</a> under <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> alone, so they can also be supplied as a liquid in an appropriate container.  This <a href="/wiki/Phase_transition" title="Phase transition">phase change</a> also makes these gases useful as ambient <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Refrigerants" title="Refrigerants">refrigerants</a> and the most significant industrial gases with this property are <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> (R717), <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a> (R290), <a href="/wiki/Butane" title="Butane">butane</a> (R600), and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> (R764). Chlorine also has this property but is too toxic, corrosive and reactive to ever have been used as a refrigerant. Some other gases exhibit this phase change if the ambient temperature is low enough; this includes <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">ethylene</a> (R1150), <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (R744), <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">ethane</a> (R170), <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a> (R744A), and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_hexafluoride" title="Sulfur hexafluoride">sulfur hexafluoride</a>; however, these can only be liquefied under pressure if kept below their <a href="/wiki/Critical_point_(thermodynamics)" title="Critical point (thermodynamics)">critical temperatures</a> which are 9 °C for C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>4</sub> ; 31 °C for CO<sub>2</sub> ; 32 °C for C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>6</sub> ; 36 °C for N<sub>2</sub>O ; 45 °C for SF<sub>6</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GENC_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-GENC-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>All of these substances are also provided as a <i>Gas</i> (not a vapor) at the 200 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">bar</a> pressure in a gas cylinder because that pressure is above their <a href="/wiki/Critical_point_(thermodynamics)" title="Critical point (thermodynamics)">critical pressure</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GENC_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-GENC-29">[29]</a></sup>  Other gases can only be supplied as liquid if also cooled. All gases can potentially be used as a refrigerant around the temperatures at which they are liquid; for example nitrogen (R728) and methane (R50) are used as refrigerant at cryogenic temperatures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cool_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cool-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Exceptionally <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> can be produced as a cold <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solid</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Dry_ice" title="Dry ice">dry ice</a>, which <a href="/wiki/Sublimation_(phase_transition)" title="Sublimation (phase transition)">sublimes</a> as it warms in ambient conditions, the properties of carbon dioxide are such that it cannot be liquid at a pressure below its <a href="/wiki/Triple_point" title="Triple point">triple point</a> of 5.1 bar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GENC_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-GENC-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Acetylene is also supplied differently. Since it is so unstable and explosive, this is supplied as a gas dissolved in acetone within a <a href="/wiki/Agamassan" title="Agamassan">packing mass</a> in a cylinder. Acetylene is also the only other common industrial gas that sublimes at atmospheric pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GENC_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-GENC-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_delivery">Gas delivery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industrial_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Gas delivery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:180px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Photos_Gas_Cabinet_Inventory.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="196" data-file-width="178" height="196" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0e/Photos_Gas_Cabinet_Inventory.jpg" width="178"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Photos_Gas_Cabinet_Inventory.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Photos gas cabinet inventory</div></div></div>
<p>The major industrial gases can be produced in bulk and delivered to customers by <a href="/wiki/Pipeline_transport" title="Pipeline transport">pipeline</a>, but can also be packaged and transported.
</p><p>Most gases are sold in <a href="/wiki/Gas_cylinder" title="Gas cylinder">gas cylinders</a> and some sold as liquid in appropriate containers (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Cryogenic_storage_dewar" title="Cryogenic storage dewar">Dewars</a>) or as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bulk_liquid" title="Bulk liquid">bulk liquid</a> delivered by truck. The industry originally supplied gases in cylinders to avoid the need for local gas generation; but for large customers such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steelworks" title="Steelworks">steelworks</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_refineries" title="Oil refineries">oil refineries</a>, a large gas production plant may be built nearby (typically called an "on-site" facility) to avoid using large numbers of cylinders <a href="/wiki/Cylinder_manifold" title="Cylinder manifold">manifolded together</a>. Alternatively, an industrial gas company may supply the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_plant" title="Chemical plant">plant and equipment</a> to produce the gas rather than the gas itself. An industrial gas company may also offer to act as <a href="/wiki/Plant_operator" title="Plant operator">plant operator</a> under an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maintenance,_repair,_and_operations" title="Maintenance, repair, and operations">operations and maintenance</a> contract for a gases facility for a customer, since it usually has the experience of running such facilities for the production or handling of gases for itself.
</p><p>Some materials are dangerous to use as a gas; for example, fluorine is highly reactive and industrial chemistry requiring fluorine often uses <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">hydrogen fluoride</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">hydrofluoric acid</a>) instead. Another approach to overcoming gas reactivity is to generate the gas as and when required, which is done, for example, with <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>.
</p><p>The delivery options are therefore local gas generation, <a href="/wiki/Pipeline_transport" title="Pipeline transport">pipelines</a>, bulk transport (<a href="/wiki/Road_transport#Trucking_and_hauling" title="Road transport">truck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport" title="Rail transport">rail</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ship_transport" title="Ship transport">ship</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Bottled_gas" title="Bottled gas">packaged gases</a> in gas cylinders or other containers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EIGA_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-EIGA-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Bulk liquid gases are often transferred to end user <a href="/wiki/Storage_tank" title="Storage tank">storage tanks</a>. Gas cylinders (and liquid gas containing vessels) are  often used by end users for their own small scale distribution systems. Toxic or flammable gas cylinders are often stored by end users in <a href="/wiki/Gas_cabinet" title="Gas cabinet">gas cabinets</a> for protection from external fire or from any leak.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="What_defines_an_industrial_gas">What defines an industrial gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industrial_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: What defines an industrial gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Industrial gas is a group of materials that are specifically manufactured for use in <a href="/wiki/Industrial_processes" title="Industrial processes">industry</a> and are also gaseous at ambient temperature and pressure.  They are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemicals" title="Chemicals">chemicals</a> which can be an <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">elemental gas</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> that is either <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic</a>, and tend to be low <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_weight" title="Molecular weight">molecular weight</a> molecules. They could also be a <a href="/wiki/Mixture" title="Mixture">mixture</a> of individual gases. They have value as a chemical; whether as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feedstock" title="Feedstock">feedstock</a>, in process enhancement, as a useful end product, or for a particular use; as opposed to having value as a "simple" <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a>.
</p><p>The term “industrial gases” <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BCGA_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-BCGA-30">[30]</a></sup> is sometimes narrowly defined as just the major gases sold, which are: nitrogen, oxygen, carbon dioxide, argon, hydrogen, acetylene and helium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>  Many names are given to gases outside of this main list by the different industrial gas companies, but generally the gases fall into the categories "specialty gases", “<a href="/wiki/Medical_gas_supply" title="Medical gas supply">medical gases</a>”, “<a href="/wiki/Fuel_gas" title="Fuel gas">fuel gases</a>” or “<a href="/wiki/List_of_refrigerants" title="List of refrigerants">refrigerant gases</a>”. However gases can also be known by their uses or industries that they serve, hence "welding gases" or "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breathing_gases" title="Breathing gases">breathing gases</a>", etc. ; or by their source, as in "air gases"; or by their mode of supply as in "packaged gases". The major gases might also be termed "bulk gases" or "tonnage gases".
</p><p>In principle any gas or gas mixture sold by the "industrial gases industry" probably has some industrial use and might be termed an "industrial gas". In practice, "industrial gases" are likely to be a pure compound or a mixture of precise <a href="/wiki/Chemical_composition" title="Chemical composition">chemical composition</a>, packaged or in small quantities, but with high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Purity_(gas)" title="Purity (gas)">purity</a> or tailored to a specific use (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxyacetylene" title="Oxyacetylene">oxyacetylene</a>).
Lists of the more significant gases are listed in "The Gases" below.
</p><p>There are cases when a gas is not usually termed an "industrial gas"; principally where the gas is <i>processed</i> for later use of its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calorific_value" title="Calorific value">energy</a> rather than <i>manufactured</i> for use as a chemical substance or preparation.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_and_gas" title="Oil and gas">oil and gas</a> industry is seen as distinct. So, whilst it is true that natural gas is a "gas" used in "industry" - often as a fuel, sometimes as a feedstock, and in this generic sense is an "industrial gas"; this term is not generally used by industrial enterprises for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrocarbons" title="Hydrocarbons">hydrocarbons</a> produced by the <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_industry" title="Petroleum industry">petroleum industry</a> directly from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_resources" title="Natural resources">natural resources</a> or in an <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">oil refinery</a>. Materials such as LPG and LNG are complex mixtures often without precise chemical composition that often also changes whilst stored.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Petrochemical_industry" title="Petrochemical industry">petrochemical industry</a> is also seen as distinct. So petrochemicals (chemicals derived from <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a>) such as <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">ethylene</a> are also generally not described as "industrial gases".
</p><p>Sometimes the chemical industry is thought of as distinct from industrial gases; so materials such as ammonia and chlorine might be considered "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_chemical" title="Industrial chemical">chemicals</a>" (especially if supplied as a liquid) instead of or sometimes as well as "industrial gases".
</p><p>Small scale gas supply of hand-carried containers is sometimes not considered to be industrial gas as the use is considered personal rather than industrial; and suppliers are not always gas specialists.
</p><p>These demarcations are based on perceived boundaries of these industries (although in practice there is some overlap), and an exact scientific definition is difficult. To illustrate "overlap" between industries:
</p><p>Manufactured <a href="/wiki/Fuel_gas" title="Fuel gas">fuel gas</a> (such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Town_gas" title="Town gas">town gas</a>) would historically have been considered an industrial gas. <a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">Syngas</a> is often considered to be a petrochemical; although its production is a core industrial gases technology. Similarly, projects harnessing <a href="/wiki/Landfill_gas" title="Landfill gas">Landfill gas</a> or <a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">biogas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waste-to-energy" title="Waste-to-energy">Waste-to-energy</a> schemes, as well as Hydrogen Production all exhibit overlapping technologies.
</p><p>Helium is an industrial gas, even though its source is from natural gas processing.
</p><p>Any gas is likely to be considered an industrial gas if it is put in a gas cylinder (except perhaps if it is used as a fuel)
</p><p>Propane would be considered an industrial gas when used as a refrigerant, but not when used as a refrigerant in LNG production, even though this is an overlapping technology.
</p>

<p>The known <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_elements" title="Chemical elements">chemical elements</a> which are, or can be obtained from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_resources" title="Natural resources">natural resources</a> and which are gaseous are hydrogen, nitrogen, oxygen, fluorine, chlorine, plus the noble gases; and are collectively referred to by chemists as the "elemental gases".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> These elements are all <a href="/wiki/Primordial_nuclide" title="Primordial nuclide">primordial</a> apart from the noble gas <a href="/wiki/Radon" title="Radon">radon</a> which is a <a href="/wiki/Trace_radioisotope" title="Trace radioisotope">trace radioisotope</a> which occurs <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural" title="Natural">naturally</a> since all isotopes are <a href="/wiki/Radiogenic_nuclide" title="Radiogenic nuclide">radiogenic nuclides</a> from <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive decay</a>. (It is not known if any <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_element" title="Synthetic element">synthetic elements</a> with <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> above 108 are gases.)
</p><p>The elements which are stable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diatomic" title="Diatomic">two atom</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homonuclear" title="Homonuclear">homonuclear</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecules" title="Molecules">molecules</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard temperature and pressure">standard temperature and pressure</a> (STP), are hydrogen (H<sub>2</sub>), nitrogen (N<sub>2</sub>) and oxygen (O<sub>2</sub>), plus the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halogens" title="Halogens">halogens</a> fluorine (F<sub>2</sub>) and chlorine (Cl<sub>2</sub>). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noble_gases" title="Noble gases">noble gases</a> are all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monatomic" title="Monatomic">monatomic</a>.
</p><p>In the industrial gases industry the term "elemental gases" (or sometimes less accurately "molecular gases") is used to distinguish these gases from molecules that are also <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_compounds" title="Chemical compounds">chemical compounds</a>. These elements are all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonmetals" title="Nonmetals">nonmetals</a>.
</p><p>Radon is chemically stable, but it is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> and does not have a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stable_isotope" title="Stable isotope">stable isotope</a>. Its most stable <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radon-222" title="Radon-222"><sup>222</sup>Rn</a>, has a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 3.8 days. Its uses are due to its radioactivity rather than its chemistry and it requires specialist handling outside of industrial gas industry norms. It can however be produced as a by-product of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uranium_ore_deposits" title="Uranium ore deposits">uraniferous ores</a> processing. Radon is a trace <a href="/wiki/Naturally_occurring_radioactive_material" title="Naturally occurring radioactive material">naturally occurring radioactive material</a> (NORM) encountered in the air processed in an ASU.
</p><p>Chlorine is the only elemental gas that is technically a <a href="/wiki/Vapor" title="Vapor">vapor</a> since STP is below its <a href="/wiki/Critical_point_(thermodynamics)" title="Critical point (thermodynamics)">critical temperature</a>; whilst 
<a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">bromine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> are liquid at STP, and so their vapor exists in equilibrium with their liquid at STP.
</p>
