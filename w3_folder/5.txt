<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Global warming potential</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Global warming potential</b> (<b>GWP</b>) is a relative measure of how much heat a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> traps in the atmosphere. It compares the amount of heat trapped by a certain mass of the <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> in question to the amount of heat trapped by a similar mass of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. A GWP is calculated over a specific time interval, commonly 20, 100, or 500 years. GWP is expressed as a factor of carbon dioxide (whose GWP is standardized to 1). In the Fifth Assessment Report of the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> has a lifetime of 12.4 years and with climate-carbon feedbacks a global warming potential of 86 over 20 years and 34 over 100 years in response to emissions. User related choices such as the time horizon can greatly affect the numerical values obtained for carbon dioxide equivalents. For a change in time horizon from 20 to 100 years, the GWP for methane decreases by a factor of approximately 2.5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
The substances subject to restrictions under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kyoto_protocol" title="Kyoto protocol">Kyoto protocol</a> are either rapidly increasing their concentrations in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a> or have a large GWP.
</p><p>The GWP depends on the following factors:
</p>
<ul><li>the absorption of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infrared_radiation" title="Infrared radiation">infrared radiation</a> by a given species</li>
<li>the spectral location of its absorbing wavelengths</li>
<li>the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_lifetime" title="Atmospheric lifetime">atmospheric lifetime</a> of the species</li></ul>
<p>Thus, a high GWP correlates with a large infrared absorption and a long atmospheric lifetime.  The dependence of GWP on the wavelength of absorption is more complicated. Even if a gas absorbs radiation efficiently at a certain wavelength, this may not affect its GWP much if the atmosphere already absorbs most radiation at that wavelength. A gas has the most effect if it absorbs in a "window" of wavelengths where the atmosphere is fairly transparent. The dependence of GWP as a function of wavelength has been found empirically and published as a graph.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Because the GWP of a greenhouse gas depends directly on its infrared spectrum, the use of <a href="/wiki/Infrared_spectroscopy" title="Infrared spectroscopy">infrared spectroscopy</a> to study greenhouse gases is centrally important in the effort to understand the impact of human activities on global <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Calculating_the_global_warming_potential">Calculating the global warming potential</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_potential&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Calculating the global warming potential">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Just as <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a> provides a simplified means of comparing the various factors that are believed to influence the climate system to one another, global warming potentials (GWPs) are one type of simplified index based upon radiative properties that can be used to estimate the potential future impacts of emissions of different gases upon the climate system in a relative sense. GWP is based on a number of factors, including the radiative efficiency (infrared-absorbing ability) of each gas relative to that of carbon dioxide, as well as the decay rate of each gas (the amount removed from the atmosphere over a given number of years) relative to that of carbon dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>

The radiative efficiencies a<sub>x</sub> and a<sub>r</sub> are not necessarily constant over time. While the absorption of infrared radiation by many greenhouse gases varies linearly with their abundance, a few important ones display non-linear behaviour for current and likely future abundances (e.g., CO<sub>2</sub>, CH<sub>4</sub>, and N<sub>2</sub>O). For those gases, the relative radiative forcing will depend upon abundance and hence upon the future scenario adopted.
</p><p>Since all GWP calculations are a comparison to CO<sub>2</sub> which is non-linear, all GWP values are affected. Assuming otherwise as is done above will lead to lower GWPs for other gases than a more detailed approach would. Clarifying this, while increasing CO<sub>2</sub> has less and less effect on radiative absorption as ppm concentrations rise, more powerful greenhouse gases like methane and nitrous oxide have different thermal absorption frequencies to CO<sub>2</sub> that are not filled up (saturated) as much as CO<sub>2</sub>, so rising ppms of these gases are far more significant.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_Kyoto_Protocol">Use in Kyoto Protocol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_potential&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Use in Kyoto Protocol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Under the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a>, the Conference of the Parties decided (decision 2/CP.3) that the values of GWP calculated for the <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Second_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Second Assessment Report">IPCC Second Assessment Report</a> are to be used for converting the various greenhouse gas emissions into comparable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Co2e" title="Co2e">CO<sub>2</sub> equivalents</a> when computing overall sources and sinks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Global_Temperature_change_Potential_.28GTP.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_Temperature_change_Potential_(GTP)">Global Temperature change Potential (GTP)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_potential&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Global Temperature change Potential (GTP)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>Global Temperature change Potential</i> is another way to quantify the ratio change from a substance relative to that of CO<sub>2</sub>, in global mean surface temperature, used for a specific time span.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Importance_of_time_horizon">Importance of time horizon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_potential&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Importance of time horizon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A substance's GWP depends on the timespan over which the potential is calculated. A gas which is quickly removed from the atmosphere may initially have a large effect, but for longer time periods, as it has been removed, it becomes less important. Thus methane has a potential of 34 over 100 years but 86 over 20 years; conversely <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_hexafluoride" title="Sulfur hexafluoride">sulfur hexafluoride</a> has a GWP of 22,800 over 100 years but 16,300 over 20 years (<a href="/wiki/IPCC_Third_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Third Assessment Report">IPCC Third Assessment Report</a>). The GWP value depends on how the gas concentration decays over time in the atmosphere. This is often not precisely known and hence the values should not be considered exact. For this reason when quoting a GWP it is important to give a reference to the calculation.
</p><p>The GWP for a mixture of gases can be obtained from the mass-fraction-weighted average of the GWPs of the individual gases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Commonly, a time horizon of 100 years is used by regulators (e.g., the <a href="/wiki/California_Air_Resources_Board" title="California Air Resources Board">California Air Resources Board</a>).
</p>

<p>The values given in the table assume the same mass of compound is released; different ratios will result from the conversion of one substance to another. For instance, burning <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> would reduce the global warming impact, but by a smaller factor than 25:1 because the mass of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> burned is less than the mass of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> released (ratio 1:2.74).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> If you started with 1 tonne of methane which has a GWP of 25, after combustion you would have 2.74 tonnes of CO<sub>2</sub>, each tonne of which has a GWP of 1. This is a net reduction of 22.26 tonnes of GWP, reducing the global warming effect by a ratio of 25:2.74 (approximately 9 times).
</p><p>The global warming potential of <a href="/wiki/Perfluorotributylamine" title="Perfluorotributylamine">perfluorotributylamine</a> (PFTBA) over a 100-year time horizon has been estimated to be approximately 7100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> It has been used by the electrical industry since the mid-20th century for electronic testing and as a heat transfer agent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> PFTBA has the highest <b>radiative efficiency</b> (relative effectiveness of greenhouse gases to restrict long-wave radiation from escaping back into space<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>) of any molecule detected in the atmosphere to date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The researchers found an average of 0.18 parts per trillion of PFTBA in Toronto air samples, whereas carbon dioxide exists around 400 parts per million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_vapour">Water vapour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_potential&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Water vapour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_vapour" title="Water vapour">Water vapour</a> has a profound infrared absorption spectrum with more and broader absorption bands than CO<sub>2</sub>, and also absorbs non-zero amounts of radiation in its low absorbing spectral regions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> (see <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> (GHG)), its GWP is therefore difficult to calculate.  Further, its concentration in the atmosphere depends on air temperature and water availability; using a global average temperature of ~16 °C, for example, creates an average humidity of ~18,000ppm at sea level (CO<sub>2</sub> is ~400ppm<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and so concentrations of [H<sub>2</sub>O]/[CO<sub>2</sub>] ~ 45x).  Another issue with calculating GWP is that, unlike other GHG, water vapor does not decay in the environment, so an average over some time period or some other measure consistent with "time dependent decay," q.v., above, must be used in lieu of the time dependent decay of artificial or excess CO<sub>2</sub>, molecules.  Other factors complicating its calculation are the Earth's temperature distribution, and the differing land masses in the Northern and Southern hemispheres.
</p>
