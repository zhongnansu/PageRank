<p>The <b>Greene–Marston House</b> (commonly known as <b>Termite Hall</b>) is a historic house in <a href="/wiki/Mobile,_Alabama" title="Mobile, Alabama">Mobile</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description_and_history">Description and history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greene%E2%80%93Marston_House&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description and history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Construction of the house began in about 1851, with a one-and-a-half-story cottage, built by the Greene family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nris_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-nris-1">[1]</a></sup> Martin Van Heuval built a much larger two-and-a-half-story <a href="/wiki/Victorian_architecture" title="Victorian architecture">Late Victorian</a> structure in 1903, incorporating the earlier house into the main block as a wing. The house was remodeled again in 1910 by William Syson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-termite_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-termite-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The house was sold to Regina DeMouy Rapier in 1919, after the DeMouy house was destroyed in a fire. Many of her DeMouy, Rapier, and Marston relatives lived in the house during this period. The house gained its name in the early twentieth century from an incident involving the Marston sisters, Adelaide and Eleanor. Family tradition maintains that one evening the children were sitting on a porch rail, and when they got up the rail disintegrated before their eyes. It was discovered that the porch had been infested with <a href="/wiki/Termite" title="Termite">termites</a>, from which it acquired its common name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-termite_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-termite-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>It became locally renowned as a literary gathering place during the tenancy of Adelaide Caroline Marston Trigg. In 1941, she co-founded Mobile's The Haunted Book Shop. The bookstore became a literary fixture that attracted the likes of <a href="/wiki/Harper_Lee" title="Harper Lee">Harper Lee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Mann" title="Thomas Mann">Thomas Mann</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Walter" title="Eugene Walter">Eugene Walter</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_March" title="William March">William March</a>. Several years after selling her interest in The Haunted Book Shop, she operated Far Corners Book Search out of the house.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adelaide_obit_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-adelaide_obit-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Adelaide and sister Eleanor were close friends with Eugene Walter. He was renowned as an author, poet, gourmet chef, translator, and friend of <a href="/wiki/Federico_Fellini" title="Federico Fellini">Federico Fellini</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-4">[4]</a></sup> Walter lived with them on several occasions and featured the house in his 1982 cookbook, <i>Delectable Dishes From Termite Hall</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-termite_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-termite-2">[2]</a></sup>
Adelaide Trigg died in 2008 at the age of 89.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adelaide_obit_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-adelaide_obit-3">[3]</a></sup> The novel, <i>In the Hope of Rising Again</i>, was written by Trigg's granddaughter, Helen Scully, and is set in the house.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inhope_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-inhope-5">[5]</a></sup> The house has remained in the Marston-Trigg family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-termite_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-termite-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>It was added to the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a> on January 11, 1983, due to its architectural significance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nris_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-nris-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
