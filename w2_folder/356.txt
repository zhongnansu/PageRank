<p><b>Community Greens</b>, sometimes referred to as <i>backyard commons</i>, <i>urban <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_lands" title="Common lands">commons</a></i>, or <a href="/wiki/Pocket_neighborhood" title="Pocket neighborhood">pocket neighborhoods</a>, are shared open green spaces on the inside of city blocks, created either when residents merge backyard space or reclaim underutilized urban land such as vacant lots and <a href="/wiki/Alley" title="Alley">alleyways</a>. These shared spaces are communally used and managed only by the residents whose homes abut them. They are not a public park, a private backyard, or a community garden; however, they can function as all three.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Community_Greens">Community Greens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Community_greens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Community Greens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Community Greens is an organization concerned with the development of shared green spaces in residential neighborhoods in American cities. These green spaces are community greens.  The Community Greens movement believes that such an approach presents the best opportunity to add usable green space to American cities, by converting under utilized backyards and dysfunctional alleys into functional and beautiful shared green spaces that are owned, managed, and enjoyed by the people who live around them.
</p><p>This has led communities in numerous American cities, including Boston, Sacramento, Baltimore, New York, and San Francisco, taking down their backyard fences, to create backyard commons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Luzerne_glover_green_alley.JPG"><img alt="Luzerne Glover Block Party to celebrate completion of gated and greened alley" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1704" data-file-width="2272" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Luzerne_glover_green_alley.JPG/220px-Luzerne_glover_green_alley.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Luzerne_glover_green_alley.JPG/330px-Luzerne_glover_green_alley.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Luzerne_glover_green_alley.JPG/440px-Luzerne_glover_green_alley.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Luzerne_glover_green_alley.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Community Green behind the Luzerne-Glover Block</div></div></div>
<p>Community Greens are multi-functional spaces for gardening, recreation, and leisure which are designed to provide social, economic, and environmental benefits to urban residents. The creation of  backyard commons can lead to an increased interaction with neighbors throughout the planning and implementation process, which may result in a stronger overall sense of community. Other possible social benefits that are claimed include decreased crime, from having more eyes on the street, and safe places where children can play and adults relax. Community Greens, like other types of urban green spaces, can significantly improve the ecological functioning of urban habitats. Vegetation and permeable pavement can slow storm water runoff and increase groundwater, which in turn can reduce pollutant flowing into nearby bodies of water during a storm. Urban environments are often significantly warmer than outlying suburbs, mostly due to the prevalence of  heat retentive concrete surfaces. City trees can mitigate this effect through shading, etc..<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The taking down  of backyards fences transforms fragmented habitats to connected corridors for urban wildlife. Furthermore because city dwellers recognize value in green space, often simply from an aesthetic standpoint, and this may be reflected through increased property values.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_beginnings_in_Maryland">The beginnings in Maryland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Community_greens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The beginnings in Maryland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>Community Greens was founded as an offshoot of Ashoka, by Ashoka founder <a href="/wiki/Bill_Drayton" title="Bill Drayton">Bill Drayton</a> in 2001.</li>
<li>Spearheaded a state wide legislature in <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, which changed the City Charter which allows the city to lease and gate alleys to homeowners.</li>
<li>Started the Alley Gating and Alley Greening pilot project in <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore</a> in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li>Local ordinance passed in the City of Baltimore in 2007.</li>
<li>As of 2009, there are 3 completely gated and greened alleys in Baltimore and over 70 applications from interested neighborhoods.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Baltimore:_The_pilot_project">Baltimore: The pilot project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Community_greens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Baltimore: The pilot project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2002, a group of residents from the <a href="/wiki/Patterson_Park" title="Patterson Park">Patterson Park</a> neighborhood approached the Patterson Park Community Development Corporation (CDC) looking for a way to improve the dirty, crime-ridden alley that ran behind their homes. Simultaneously, Community Greens also approached the Patterson Park CDC looking for an alley they could use as a pilot project in Baltimore, and these two groups were put into contact. The Luzerne-Glover block was granted a temporary permit from the city to gate their alleyway, despite the fact that it was not yet legal to gate a right-of-way. Community Greens, the Patterson Park CDC, the Patterson Park Neighborhood Association and the Luzurne‐Glover block group turned to University of Maryland law professor Barbara Bezdek, who enlisted the help of her law students, who researched existing laws and current uses of Baltimore alleyways.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Luzerne_and_Chandlers_Yard_Before.jpg"><img alt="Luzerne alleyway before gating and greening" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="1536" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Luzerne_and_Chandlers_Yard_Before.jpg/220px-Luzerne_and_Chandlers_Yard_Before.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Luzerne_and_Chandlers_Yard_Before.jpg/330px-Luzerne_and_Chandlers_Yard_Before.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Luzerne_and_Chandlers_Yard_Before.jpg/440px-Luzerne_and_Chandlers_Yard_Before.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Luzerne_and_Chandlers_Yard_Before.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Luzerne-Glover Block alley before gating and greening</div></div></div>
<p>Looking for a permanent solution, these groups aligned themselves with the Mayor's Department of Neighborhoods and took their case to Annapolis. Maryland Delegate <a href="/wiki/Peter_A._Hammen" title="Peter A. Hammen">Peter A. Hammen</a> sponsored House Bill 1533 to amend the Baltimore City Charter, allowing the City to close alleyways and lease them to interested parties. The Luzerne-Glover group testified before the Environmental Committee of the state legislature and House Bill 1533 passed in 2004. Community Greens then worked with law firm <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hogan_%26_Hartson" title="Hogan &amp; Hartson">Hogan &amp; Hartson</a>, in collaboration with Barbara Bezdek, to draft a city alley gating and leasing ordinance, which was then submitted to the City of Baltimore.
</p><p>In April 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_City_Council" title="Baltimore City Council">Baltimore City Council</a> under the leadership of newly elected Mayor <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Shelia_Dixon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Shelia Dixon (page does not exist)">Shelia Dixon</a>, passed the Gating and Greening Alleys ordinance, enabling Baltimore residents to legally gate and green the alleys behind their homes, contingent on a requirement that 80% of the homeowners on the alley submit consent forms and 100% of property owners to approve projects that impede traffic flow. Projects requiring 100% consent include removal of existing concrete and installation of permeable pavement and large tree plantings; beautification and smaller greening projects that do not impede traffic only require 80% approval.
</p><p>Interested communities need approval from several Baltimore City Departments, including Solid Waste, Transportation, Fire, and Police to ensure that proposed alley projects meet the necessary infrastructural conditions. Once initial approval is received, residents submit an application to the Department of Public Works, which includes the necessary consent forms from homeowners and a signed affidavit stating that attempts were made to obtain approval from all homeowners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Luzerne Glover neighborhood now has their green alleyway, and is the first community in Baltimore to use the Gating and Greening Alleys ordinance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> So far, seven Baltimore communities have successfully completed this process and have created eight useful Community Greens from the underutilized alleyways in their backyards.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_existing_greens">Other existing greens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Community_greens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other existing greens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Montgomery_Park_-_Boston.2C_Massachusetts"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Montgomery_Park_-_Boston,_Massachusetts"><a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20090628111701/http://communitygreens.org/ExistingGreens/montgomerypark/Montgomery%20Park.htm" rel="nofollow">Montgomery Park - Boston, Massachusetts</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Community_greens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Montgomery Park - Boston, Massachusetts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Montgomery Park</b> is a third of an acre common space shared by 85 households in Boston's South End neighborhood. From the time the neighborhood was first developed in the 1860s through the 1970s, the park was separated from the residents' backyards by an alley and fence. In more recent years, residents began to connect the park to their individual yards by replacing the alley separating their private backyards from the park with a narrower brick walkway and tearing down backyard fences. Additionally, residents have moved garbage collection to the front street and convinced utility companies to bury service lines. Residents now enjoy greater access to the shared Community Green, and use it for gardening and recreation.
</p>
<h3><span id="Stoney_Creek_Apartments_-_Livermore.2C_California"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Stoney_Creek_Apartments_-_Livermore,_California"><a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20090625143531/http://communitygreens.org/ExistingGreens/stoneycreek/stoneycreek.htm" rel="nofollow">Stoney Creek Apartments - Livermore, California</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Community_greens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Stoney Creek Apartments - Livermore, California">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Stoney Creek Apartments</b> were constructed in 1992 to address a lack of affordable housing in <a href="/wiki/Livermore,_California" title="Livermore, California">Livermore, California</a>. Ten residential buildings share five courtyards, accessible from a small alleyway. Homes have front and back patios in addition to the shared courtyards, and neighbors have the opportunity to enjoy this shared outdoor space and experience a heightened sense of community. The inward-facing position of these patios allows parents to keep a watchful eye on children playing in the courtyard.
</p>
<h3><span id="Chandlers_Yard_-_Baltimore.2C_Maryland"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Chandlers_Yard_-_Baltimore,_Maryland"><a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20090619124249/http://communitygreens.org/ExistingGreens/chandlersyard/chandlersyard.htm" rel="nofollow">Chandlers Yard - Baltimore, Maryland</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Community_greens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Chandlers Yard - Baltimore, Maryland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Located in the Federal Hill neighborhood of downtown Baltimore and hidden behind eleven narrow rowhouses, <b>Chandler's Yard</b> is a tree-shaded courtyard that was carved out of the backyards of the surrounding homes by developer Bill Struever who wanted to make living on the block more attractive to potential buyers. Struever convinced some of the property owners in this block to give up part of their backyards for this shared courtyard and pay a small construction fee. The result is a beautiful shared space for these residents, who enjoy a heightened sense of security and increased property values.
</p>
<h3><span id="Village_Homes_-_Davis.2C_California"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Village_Homes_-_Davis,_California"><a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20090218163042/http://communitygreens.org/ExistingGreens/villagehomes/villagehomes.htm" rel="nofollow">Village Homes - Davis, California</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Community_greens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Village Homes - Davis, California">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <b>Village Homes</b> development is situated on a sixty-acre parcel in suburban Davis, California. While home lots are smaller than the average for Davis, clusters of eight homes share common green spaces accessible from private backyards. In addition to these courtyards, Village Homes residents share two large parks, two vineyards, and numerous small orchards and community gardens. Gardens are irrigated by naturally-flowing creeks, which also serve as natural filters that eliminate the need for an expensive storm-water sewer system. Residents enjoy environmental and aesthetic benefits from their shared spaces, and the increased sense of community is evident; Village Homes residents on average know 40 neighbors, compared to an average of 17 acquaintances reported in a nearby traditional suburban development.
</p>
<h3><span id="Jackson_Heights_-_Queens.2C_New_York"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Jackson_Heights_-_Queens,_New_York"><a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20090630004718/http://www.communitygreens.org/ExistingGreens/jacksonheights/jacksonheights.htm" rel="nofollow">Jackson Heights - Queens, New York</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Community_greens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Jackson Heights - Queens, New York">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For nearly nine decades, the historic neighborhood of <b>Jackson Heights</b> in Queens has maintained its fourteen block-long shared interior courtyards, and is considered to be the first "garden apartments" constructed in the United States. These spaces have helped to sustain the blocks' distinctive appeal since their development in the early decades of the last century. Over the years, Jackson Heights residents fought to hold on to their gardens and green spaces in the midst of a city where high property values create an intense pressure to develop any available open space.  The preservation of these shared spaces has increased both community pride and residential property values; if these inner courtyards had been developed, Jackson Heights property values would have dropped by one-third. In 1993, Jackson Heights was officially declared a historic district by the NYC Landmark Preservation Commission, furthering sense of place for this community.
</p>
<h3><span id="St._Francis_Square_-_San_Francisco.2C_California"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="St._Francis_Square_-_San_Francisco,_California"><a class="external text" href="http://www.communitygreens.org/st-francis-square" rel="nofollow">St. Francis Square - San Francisco, California</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Community_greens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: St. Francis Square - San Francisco, California">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A cooperative apartment community, <b>St. Francis Square</b> consists of 299 apartments wrapped around three shared, open spaces. These community greens are full of gardens, basketball courts, playgrounds, and quiet places to relax.  St. Francis Square was established as a limited equity co-operative apartment, although it converted to market rate ownership in 2004.
</p>
<h3><span id="The_Hope_Community_-_Minneapolis.2C_MN"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Hope_Community_-_Minneapolis,_MN"><a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20090619124254/http://communitygreens.org/ExistingGreens/hopecommunity/hope.htm" rel="nofollow">The Hope Community - Minneapolis, MN</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Community_greens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: The Hope Community - Minneapolis, MN">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fifteen years ago, this community was situated in an area of Minneapolis plagued by urban flight and crime. Instrumental to the rehabilitation and renovation of this neighborhood is a local community development corporation, <a class="external text" href="http://www.hope-community.org/" rel="nofollow">Hope Community, Inc</a>. In the late 1980s, residents were selling their properties for one dollar because they had lost all value; this is when the Hope Community began purchasing houses in the hopes of developing affordable rental units clustered together on a single block. Today, nine rehabilitated houses abut community areas, a playground, and gardens. Property values are steadily increasing and the community itself is stabilizing. By following principles of Crime Prevention Through Environmental Design (CPTED) and creating defensible space, <b>the Hope Community</b> has created a sense of shared ownership and community in a troubled urban area.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_alleys">Green alleys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Community_greens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Green alleys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Something similar has taken place in various cities in North America, involving the <a href="/wiki/Greening" title="Greening">greening</a> of <a href="/wiki/Back_lane" title="Back lane">back lanes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Alley" title="Alley">alleys</a>. This includes <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chicago_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-chicago-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montr%C3%A9al" title="Montréal">Montréal</a>, Canada, who have started to reclaim their alleys from garbage and crime by <a href="/wiki/Greening" title="Greening">greening</a> the service lanes, or back ways, that run behind some houses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dc_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-dc-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p><b><a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a></b>, <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a> has about 1,900 miles (3,100 km) of alleyways.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chicago_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-chicago-7">[7]</a></sup> In 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Department_of_Transportation" title="Chicago Department of Transportation">Chicago Department of Transportation</a> started converting conventional alleys which were paved with <a href="/wiki/Asphalt" title="Asphalt">asphalt</a> into so called Green Alleys. This program, called the Green Alley Program, is supposed to enable easier water runoff, as the alleyways in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a> are not connected to the sewer system. With this program, the water will be able to seep through semi-permeable <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a> or <a href="/wiki/Asphalt" title="Asphalt">asphalt</a> in which a colony of fungi and bacteria will establish itself. The bacteria will help breakup oils before the water is absorbed into the ground. The lighter color of the pavement will also reflect more light, making the area next to the alley cooler.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The greening of such alleys or laneways can also involve the planting of native plants to further absorb rain water and moderate temperature.
</p>
