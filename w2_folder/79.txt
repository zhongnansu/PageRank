<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Evergreen</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about plant types. For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Evergreen_(disambiguation)" title="Evergreen (disambiguation)">Evergreen (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Abies_alba_02.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1122" data-file-width="1624" height="207" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Abies_alba_02.jpg/300px-Abies_alba_02.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Abies_alba_02.jpg/450px-Abies_alba_02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Abies_alba_02.jpg/600px-Abies_alba_02.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Abies_alba_02.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Abies_alba" title="Abies alba">silver fir</a> shoot showing three successive years of retained leaves.</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">botany</a>, an <b>evergreen</b> is a plant that has <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> throughout the year, always green. This is true even if the plant retains its foliage only in warm climates, and contrasts with <a href="/wiki/Deciduous" title="Deciduous">deciduous</a> plants, which completely lose their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foliage" title="Foliage">foliage</a> during the winter or <a href="/wiki/Dry_season" title="Dry season">dry season</a>. There are many different kinds of evergreen plants, both <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">trees</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrubs</a>. Evergreens include:
</p>
<ul><li>most species of <a href="/wiki/Pinophyta" title="Pinophyta">conifers</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tsuga" title="Tsuga">hemlock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blue_spruce" title="Blue spruce">blue spruce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thuja_plicata" title="Thuja plicata">red cedar</a>, and <a href="/wiki/List_of_Pinus_species#Subgenus_Strobus" title="List of Pinus species">white</a>/<a href="/wiki/Scots_pine" title="Scots pine">scots</a>/<a href="/wiki/Jack_pine" title="Jack pine">jack pine</a>), but not all (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Larch" title="Larch">larch</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Live_oak" title="Live oak">live oak</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holly" title="Holly">holly</a>, and "ancient" <a href="/wiki/Gymnosperm" title="Gymnosperm">gymnosperms</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Cycad" title="Cycad">cycads</a></li>
<li>most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angiosperm" title="Angiosperm">angiosperms</a> from frost-free climates, such as <a href="/wiki/Eucalypt" title="Eucalypt">eucalypts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforest</a> trees</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lycopodiopsida" title="Lycopodiopsida">clubmosses and relatives</a></li></ul>
<p>The Latin binomial term <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sempervirens" title="wikt:sempervirens">sempervirens</a></i>, meaning "always green", refers to the evergreen nature of the plant, for instance
</p>
<dl><dd><i><a href="/wiki/Cupressus_sempervirens" title="Cupressus sempervirens">Cupressus sempervirens</a></i> (a cypress)</dd>
<dd><i><a href="/wiki/Lonicera_sempervirens" title="Lonicera sempervirens">Lonicera sempervirens</a></i> (a honeysuckle)</dd>
<dd><i><a href="/wiki/Sequoia_sempervirens" title="Sequoia sempervirens">Sequoia sempervirens</a></i> (a sequoia)</dd></dl>
<p>Leaf persistence in evergreen plants varies from a few months to several decades (over thirty years in the <a href="/wiki/Pinus_longaeva" title="Pinus longaeva">Great Basin Bristlecone Pine</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reasons_for_being_evergreen_or_deciduous">Reasons for being evergreen or deciduous</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Reasons for being evergreen or deciduous">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Live_oak_Georgetown.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="225" data-file-width="300" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8c/Live_oak_Georgetown.jpg" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Live_oak_Georgetown.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Quercus_virginiana" title="Quercus virginiana">southern live oak</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a> during winter</div></div></div>
<p>Deciduous trees shed their leaves, usually as an adaptation to a cold or dry/wet season. Evergreen trees do lose leaves, but each tree loses its leaves gradually and not all at once. Most <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rainforest" title="Tropical rainforest">tropical rainforest</a> plants are considered to be evergreens, replacing their leaves gradually throughout the year as the leaves age and fall, whereas species growing in seasonally arid climates may be either evergreen or deciduous. Most warm <a href="/wiki/Temperate_climate" title="Temperate climate">temperate climate</a> plants are also evergreen. In cool temperate climates, fewer plants are evergreen, with a predominance of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conifers" title="Conifers">conifers</a>, as few evergreen <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">broadleaf</a> plants can <a href="/wiki/Psychrophile" title="Psychrophile">tolerate severe cold</a> below about −26 °C (−15 °F).
</p><p>In areas where there is a reason for being deciduous, e.g. a cold season or dry season, being evergreen is usually an adaptation to low nutrient levels. Deciduous trees lose nutrients whenever they lose their leaves. In warmer areas, species such as some <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cupressaceae" title="Cupressaceae">cypresses</a> grow on poor soils and disturbed ground. In <i><a href="/wiki/Rhododendron" title="Rhododendron">Rhododendron</a></i>, a genus with many broadleaf evergreens, several species grow in mature forests but are usually found on highly acidic soil where the nutrients are less available to plants. In <a href="/wiki/Taiga" title="Taiga">taiga</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boreal_forest" title="Boreal forest">boreal forests</a>, it is too cold for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organic_matter_in_the_soil" title="Organic matter in the soil">organic matter in the soil</a> to decay rapidly, so the nutrients in the soil are less easily available to plants, thus favouring evergreens.
</p><p>In temperate climates, evergreens can reinforce their own survival; evergreen leaf and needle litter has a higher carbon-nitrogen ratio than deciduous leaf litter, contributing to a higher soil acidity and lower soil nitrogen content. These conditions favour the growth of more evergreens and make it more difficult for deciduous plants to persist. In addition, the shelter provided by existing evergreen plants can make it easier for younger evergreen plants to survive cold and/or drought.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
