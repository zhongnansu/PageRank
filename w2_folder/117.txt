<p>The <b>Green–Meldrim House</b> is a historic house at 14 West Macon Street, on the northwest corner of <a href="/wiki/Squares_of_Savannah,_Georgia" title="Squares of Savannah, Georgia">Madison Square</a> in <a href="/wiki/Savannah,_Georgia" title="Savannah, Georgia">Savannah, Georgia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  Built in the 1850s, it was designated as a <a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Landmark" title="National Historic Landmark">National Historic Landmark</a> in 1976 as one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_South" title="American South">American South</a>'s finest and most lavish examples of <a href="/wiki/Gothic_Revival_architecture" title="Gothic Revival architecture">Gothic Revival architecture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  The house is owned by the adjacent St. John's Episcopal Church, which offers tours and uses it as a meeting and reception space.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green%E2%80%93Meldrim_House&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Green–Meldrim House is located on the west side of Madison Square in central Savannah, at the southwest corner of West Harris and Bull Streets.  West Macon Street, the house's address, is a spur street off Whitaker Street, which runs behind the property.  The house's principal facade faces south, with a porch and garden facing the square.  The house is among the best-known examples of the <a href="/wiki/Gothic_Revival_architecture" title="Gothic Revival architecture">Gothic Revival</a> style in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">South</a>, with a stuccoed brick exterior, cast-iron porch, oriel windows, and an imposing front cast-iron fence.  The main entrance has an iron portico believed to be unique in the United States, with octagonal posts supported a pair of arches.  A crenellated parapet rings the roof.  The interior of the house, following a center-hall plan, retains original woodwork, plaster, and ironwork, the latter featuring a freestanding staircase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhl_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-nhl-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green%E2%80%93Meldrim_House&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Entrance_Hall_of_Mr_Chas._Green%27s_house,_Savannah_Ga,_now_occupied_as_Head_Quarters_by_Gen_Sherman.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5007" data-file-width="5817" height="258" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Entrance_Hall_of_Mr_Chas._Green%27s_house%2C_Savannah_Ga%2C_now_occupied_as_Head_Quarters_by_Gen_Sherman.jpg/300px-Entrance_Hall_of_Mr_Chas._Green%27s_house%2C_Savannah_Ga%2C_now_occupied_as_Head_Quarters_by_Gen_Sherman.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Entrance_Hall_of_Mr_Chas._Green%27s_house%2C_Savannah_Ga%2C_now_occupied_as_Head_Quarters_by_Gen_Sherman.jpg/450px-Entrance_Hall_of_Mr_Chas._Green%27s_house%2C_Savannah_Ga%2C_now_occupied_as_Head_Quarters_by_Gen_Sherman.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Entrance_Hall_of_Mr_Chas._Green%27s_house%2C_Savannah_Ga%2C_now_occupied_as_Head_Quarters_by_Gen_Sherman.jpg/600px-Entrance_Hall_of_Mr_Chas._Green%27s_house%2C_Savannah_Ga%2C_now_occupied_as_Head_Quarters_by_Gen_Sherman.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Entrance_Hall_of_Mr_Chas._Green%27s_house,_Savannah_Ga,_now_occupied_as_Head_Quarters_by_Gen_Sherman.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Entrance Hall in 1864, when it was being used as General Sherman's Headquarters. 1864 sketch by <a href="/wiki/William_Waud" title="William Waud">William Waud</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The house was designed and built between 1853 and 1861 at a cost of $93,000 by the architect <a href="/wiki/John_S._Norris" title="John S. Norris">John Norris</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The property's first owner was Charles Green, a wealthy cotton merchant and grandfather of the writer <a href="/wiki/Julien_Green" title="Julien Green">Julien Green</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>At this time, none of the original furniture is on display at the house. After the Union troops captured Savannah in 1864, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_T._Sherman" title="William T. Sherman">Sherman</a> occupied the house and used it as a headquarters until the end of the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> It was in this house in December 1864 that Sherman composed his famous telegram to <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">President Lincoln</a>, in which he communicated his desire to present to the President "as a Christmas Gift the City of Savannah, with one hundred and fifty heavy guns and plenty of ammunition, and also about twenty-five thousand bales of cotton"; the cotton belonged to Charles Green, the owner of the House.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1892, local politician and judge <a href="/wiki/Peter_Meldrim" title="Peter Meldrim">Peter Meldrim</a> purchased the property and lived in it a number of decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In 1943, his heirs sold the house to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._John%27s_Church,_Savannah" title="St. John's Church, Savannah">St John's Church</a>, which is located next door. Tours of the house are given during the day, and the church uses it for wedding receptions and after-church events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
