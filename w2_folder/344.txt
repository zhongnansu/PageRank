<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">History of the Green Party of England and Wales</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

</p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_England_and_Wales" title="Green Party of England and Wales">Green Party of England and Wales</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_England_and_Wales" title="Green Party of England and Wales">Green Party of England and Wales</a> has its roots in the <a href="/wiki/PEOPLE_Party" title="PEOPLE Party">PEOPLE Party</a> started in Coventry in 1972/3 by four professional friends (Michael Benfield, Freda Sanders, Tony and Lesley Whittaker). It then changed its name to the more descriptive <b>Ecology Party</b> in 1975, to the <b>Green Party</b> ten years later.   In the 1990s, the Scottish and Northern Ireland wings of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(UK)" title="Green Party (UK)">Green Party</a> in the United Kingdom decided to separate amicably from the party in England and Wales, to form the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Green_Party" title="Scottish Green Party">Scottish Green Party</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Green Party in Northern Ireland">Green Party in Northern Ireland</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Wales_Green_Party" title="Wales Green Party">Wales Green Party</a> became an autonomous regional party and remained within the new Green Party of England and Wales.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1990_to_1997">1990 to 1997</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_England_and_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: 1990 to 1997">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1991 Green Party spokesman and TV sports presenter <a href="/wiki/David_Icke" title="David Icke">David Icke</a> created considerable embarrassment for the Party when he revealed his extreme political and spiritual beliefs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ronson4_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ronson4-1">[1]</a></sup> He was subsequently forced to leave the party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GPHistory_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-GPHistory-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Internal divisions over the direction of the party in the early 1990s meant that the Green Party fell out of the limelight and failed to maintain its electoral momentum. In 1991, attempts to streamline the Party Constitution were proposed by a group called <i><a href="/wiki/Green_2000" title="Green 2000">Green 2000</a></i>, who wanted to "modernise" the Party and make it into an organised electoral force that could become the ruling party in the UK by the year 2000. After the Green 2000 Constitution was adopted, a new Executive came into force to oversee the day-to-day business of the party. Many Green 2000 members were elected to the new Executive in 1991 but, by 1992, only two remained, with the others resigning or being recalled and forced to quit. These internal constitutional wranglings, and negative public statements released by supporters of both Green 2000 and decentralists who ran the recall campaigns, seriously hampered preparations for the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1992" title="United Kingdom general election, 1992">1992 General Election</a>, in which 253 Green candidates received 1.3% of the vote.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GPHistory_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-GPHistory-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sara_Parkin" title="Sara Parkin">Sara Parkin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jonathon_Porritt" title="Jonathon Porritt">Jonathon Porritt</a> left active involvement with the party.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The early and mid-1990s were difficult for the Greens, because of Britain's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plurality_electoral_system" title="Plurality electoral system">first-past-the-post electoral system</a>, the recession of 1992–93 and the squeeze caused by the rising popularity of <a href="/wiki/New_Labour" title="New Labour">New Labour</a>. Nevertheless, the party gained a handful of local councillors in <a href="/wiki/Stroud" title="Stroud">Stroud</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a>.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1992" title="United Kingdom general election, 1992">1992 General Election</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cynog_Dafis" title="Cynog Dafis">Cynog Dafis</a> was elected on a <a href="/wiki/Plaid_Cymru" title="Plaid Cymru">Plaid Cymru</a>–<a href="/wiki/Wales_Green_Party" title="Wales Green Party">Wales Green Party</a> ticket, gaining <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceredigion_and_Pembroke_North_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Ceredigion and Pembroke North (UK Parliament constituency)">Ceredigion and Pembroke North</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrats</a>. The Green Party argued that this made him the first Green representative in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a>. The electoral agreement that enabled this broke down by 1995.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1997_to_2009">1997 to 2009</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_England_and_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1997 to 2009">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Caroline_Lucas_keynote_Green_Party_England_and_Wales_2006-09-23.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1168" data-file-width="1760" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Caroline_Lucas_keynote_Green_Party_England_and_Wales_2006-09-23.jpg/220px-Caroline_Lucas_keynote_Green_Party_England_and_Wales_2006-09-23.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Caroline_Lucas_keynote_Green_Party_England_and_Wales_2006-09-23.jpg/330px-Caroline_Lucas_keynote_Green_Party_England_and_Wales_2006-09-23.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Caroline_Lucas_keynote_Green_Party_England_and_Wales_2006-09-23.jpg/440px-Caroline_Lucas_keynote_Green_Party_England_and_Wales_2006-09-23.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Caroline_Lucas_keynote_Green_Party_England_and_Wales_2006-09-23.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Caroline Lucas giving a keynote speech at the autumn conference of the Green Party of England and Wales, <a href="/wiki/Hove" title="Hove">Hove</a>, 2006</div></div></div>
<p>The election of a Labour government in 1997 created opportunities and focus for the Green Party. New democratic institutions were created that offered electoral possibilities for the Greens, such as the <a href="/wiki/London_Assembly" title="London Assembly">London Assembly</a>, the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="National Assembly for Wales">National Assembly for Wales</a> and – for the independent <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Green_Party" title="Scottish Green Party">Scottish Green Party</a> – the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a>, all of which use some form of <a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">proportional representation</a>, allowing smaller parties the chance of gaining representation. Labour also changed European Parliamentary elections to a form of proportional representation.
</p><p>Combined with gradual council gains, the party quietly gained successes.
</p><p>In the 1999 European elections, two Greens were elected <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_European_Parliament" title="Member of the European Parliament">Members of the European Parliament</a> (MEPs): <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Lucas" title="Caroline Lucas">Caroline Lucas</a> (<a href="/wiki/South_East_England_(European_Parliament_constituency)" title="South East England (European Parliament constituency)">South East England</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Caroline_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Caroline-3">[3]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Jean_Lambert" title="Jean Lambert">Jean Lambert</a> (<a href="/wiki/London_(European_Parliament_constituency)" title="London (European Parliament constituency)">London</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jean_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jean-4">[4]</a></sup> They retained their seats in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Parliament_Election,_2004_(UK)" title="European Parliament Election, 2004 (UK)">2004 European elections</a>, despite a reduction in number of seats available. Overall, the Party gained 1,033,093 votes in the 2004 European election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GPEWSite_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-GPEWSite-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>However the Greens did not manage to break through into other European electoral regions or the Welsh Assembly.
Three Greens were elected to the first <a href="/wiki/London_Assembly" title="London Assembly">London Assembly</a>.
</p><p>The Green Party achieved its best ever UK General Election result to date in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2005" title="United Kingdom general election, 2005">2005 General Election</a> with a total of 281,780 votes, with <a href="/wiki/Keith_Taylor_(British_politician)" title="Keith Taylor (British politician)">Keith Taylor</a> receiving 22% of the vote in <a href="/wiki/Brighton_Pavilion_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Brighton Pavilion (UK Parliament constituency)">Brighton Pavilion</a>.
</p><p>The Party had 116 <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Local government in the United Kingdom">local councillors</a> after a gain of 5 councillors during the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_local_elections,_2008" title="United Kingdom local elections, 2008">2008 local elections</a>. The Greens achieved significant representation on <a href="/wiki/Brighton_and_Hove" title="Brighton and Hove">Brighton &amp; Hove City Council</a>, <a href="/wiki/City_of_Lancaster" title="City of Lancaster">Lancaster City Council</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norwich" title="Norwich">Norwich</a>, <a href="/wiki/London_Borough_of_Lewisham" title="London Borough of Lewisham">Lewisham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxford_City_Council" title="Oxford City Council">Oxford City Council</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxfordshire_County_Council" title="Oxfordshire County Council">Oxfordshire County Council</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kirklees" title="Kirklees">Kirklees Council</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stroud_District_Council" title="Stroud District Council">Stroud District Council</a>. The Green Party were the official opposition on Norwich City Council; they formed part of the ruling coalition that controlled <a href="/wiki/City_of_Lancaster" title="City of Lancaster">Lancaster City Council</a> alongside the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a>, and shared control of <a href="/wiki/Castle_Morpeth" title="Castle Morpeth">Castle Morpeth</a> Council as part of an all party administration.
</p><p>In this period, the Green Party of England and Wales had representation in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>, the (unelected) upper chamber of <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament</a>: in the person of  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timothy_Beaumont,_Baron_Beaumont_of_Whitley" title="Timothy Beaumont, Baron Beaumont of Whitley">Lord Beaumont of Whitley</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GPEWSite_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-GPEWSite-5">[5]</a></sup> who died in 2008.
</p><p>According to MORI in 2010, Green issues were rated as importantly as during the Green Party's previous high point in the late 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MORI_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-MORI-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The party held its first ever leadership election in September 2008: <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Lucas" title="Caroline Lucas">Caroline Lucas</a> was elected to the position of Leader, and <a href="/wiki/Adrian_Ramsay" title="Adrian Ramsay">Adrian Ramsay</a> to the position of Deputy Leader.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Since_2010">Since 2010</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_England_and_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Since 2010">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Green Party fielded more than 300 candidates for the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2010" title="United Kingdom general election, 2010">2010 general election</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-Hopeful_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-Hopeful-7">[7]</a></sup>  Caroline Lucas became the first Green candidate to gain a seat in Westminster, after being elected MP for <a href="/wiki/Brighton_Pavilion_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Brighton Pavilion (UK Parliament constituency)">Brighton Pavilion</a> by a margin of 1,252 votes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-election2010-brightonpavillion_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-election2010-brightonpavillion-8">[8]</a></sup> The following week 600 new members had joined the Green Party bringing total membership to over 11,000. In previous years 600 new members was high for a whole year.
</p><p>On  24 May 2012 the  Six Green Party Councillors elected to Stroud District Council formed a power sharing administration  (a "constructive cooperation") with both the Labour Party and the Liberal Democrats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2014, <a href="/wiki/Molly_Scott_Cato" title="Molly Scott Cato">Molly Scott Cato</a> became the party's third <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_European_Parliament" title="Member of the European Parliament">MEP</a>.
</p>
