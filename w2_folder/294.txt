<p>A <b>Green party</b> is a formally organized <a href="/wiki/Political_party" title="Political party">political party</a> based on the principles of <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">green politics</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">social justice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nonviolence" title="Nonviolence">nonviolence</a>. Greens believe that these issues are inherently related to one another as a foundation for world peace. Green party platforms typically embrace social-democratic economic policies and forming coalitions with <a href="/wiki/Left-wing_politics" title="Left-wing politics">leftists</a>. Green parties exist in nearly 90 countries around the world; many are members of <a href="/wiki/Global_Greens" title="Global Greens">Global Greens</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are distinctions between "green" parties and "Green" parties. Any party, faction, or politician may be labeled "green" if it emphasizes environmental causes. Indeed, the term may even be used as a verb: it is not uncommon to hear of "greening" a party or a candidate.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In contrast, formally organized Green parties may follow a coherent <a href="/wiki/Ideology" title="Ideology">ideology</a> that includes not only <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalism</a>, but often also other concerns such as <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">social justice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Consensus_decision-making" title="Consensus decision-making">consensus decision-making</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nonviolence" title="Nonviolence">nonviolence</a>. Greens believe that these issues are inherently related to one another as a foundation for world peace. The best-known statement of the above Green values is the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Four_Pillars_of_the_Green_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Four Pillars of the Green Party (page does not exist)">Four Pillars of the Green Party</a>, adopted by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alliance_%2790/The_Greens" title="Alliance '90/The Greens">German Greens</a> in 1979–1980 (but forsaken since). The <a href="/wiki/Global_Greens_Charter" title="Global Greens Charter">Global Greens Charter</a> lists six guiding principles which are ecological wisdom, social justice, participatory democracy, nonviolence, sustainability and respect for diversity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Political parties campaigning on a predominantly environmental platform arose in the early 1970s in various parts of the world.
</p><p>The world's first political parties to campaign on a predominantly environmental platform were the <a href="/wiki/United_Tasmania_Group" title="United Tasmania Group">United Tasmania Group</a> contested the <a href="/wiki/Tasmanian_state_election,_1972" title="Tasmanian state election, 1972">April 1972 state election</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania, Australia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Values_Party" title="Values Party">Values Party</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, which contested the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1972" title="New Zealand general election, 1972">November 1972 New Zealand general election</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dann,_Christine_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dann,_Christine-2">[2]</a></sup>  Their use of the name 'Green' derived from the <a href="/wiki/Green_ban" title="Green ban">'Green Bans'</a>: an Australian movement of building workers who refused to build on sites of cultural and environmental significance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bevan,_RA_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bevan,_RA-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first green party in Europe was the Popular Movement for the Environment, founded in 1972 in the <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Swiss</a> canton of <a href="/wiki/Neuch%C3%A2tel" title="Neuchâtel">Neuchâtel</a>. The first national green party in Europe was <a href="/wiki/PEOPLE_Party" title="PEOPLE Party">PEOPLE</a>, founded in Britain in February 1973, which eventually turned into the <a href="/wiki/Ecology_Party" title="Ecology Party">Ecology Party</a>, and then the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(UK)" title="Green Party (UK)">Green Party</a>. Several other local political groups were founded in beginning of the 1970s and Fons Sprangers was probably the first Green mayor in the world, elected in 1970 in Meer, and active until 2006 for the Flemish Greens. The first political party to use the name "Green" seems to have been the Lower Saxon "<a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gr%C3%BCne_Liste_Umweltschutz" title="de:Grüne Liste Umweltschutz">Green List for Environmental Protection</a>", founded Sept. 1, 1977.
</p><p>The first Green Party to achieve national prominence was the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Green_Party" title="German Green Party">German Green Party</a>, famous for their opposition to <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a>, as well as an expression of anti-centralist and pacifist values traditional to greens. They were founded in 1980 and have been in coalition governments at <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">state</a> level for some years. They were in federal government with the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Party of Germany">Social Democratic Party of Germany</a> in a so-called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red-Green_alliance" title="Red-Green alliance">Red-Green alliance</a> from 1998 to 2005. In 2001, they reached an agreement to end reliance on nuclear power in Germany, and agreed to remain in coalition and support the government of Chancellor <a href="/wiki/Gerhard_Schr%C3%B6der" title="Gerhard Schröder">Gerhard Schröder</a> in the <a href="/wiki/War_in_Afghanistan_(2001%E2%80%93present)" title="War in Afghanistan (2001–present)">2001 Afghan War</a>. This put them at odds with many Greens worldwide.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, in 1995, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vihre%C3%A4_Liitto" title="Vihreä Liitto">Finnish Green Party</a> was the first European Green party to be part of a national Cabinet.  Other Green parties that have participated in government at national level include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Groen!" title="Groen!">Groen!</a> (formerly Agalev) and <a href="/wiki/Ecolo" title="Ecolo">Ecolo</a> in <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Greens_(France)" title="The Greens (France)">Les Verts</a> in France and the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(Ireland)" title="Green Party (Ireland)">Green Party</a> in Ireland. In the Netherlands <a href="/wiki/GroenLinks" title="GroenLinks">GroenLinks</a> ("GreenLeft") was founded in 1990 from four small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left-wing" title="Left-wing">left-wing</a> parties and is now a stable faction in the Dutch parliament.
</p><p>Around the world, individuals have formed many Green parties over the last thirty years. Green parties now exist in most countries with democratic systems: from <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> to <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>; from <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> to <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>; from <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> to <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>. There is Green representation at national, regional and local levels in many countries around the world.
</p><p>Most of the Green parties are formed to win elections, and so organize themselves by the presented electoral or political districts. But that does not apply universally: The <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Alaska" title="Green Party of Alaska">Green Party of Alaska</a> is organized along bioregional lines to practice <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioregional_democracy" title="Bioregional democracy">bioregional democracy</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alliances">Alliances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Alliances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Depending on local conditions or issues, platforms and alliances may vary. In line with the goal of  democracy, neighboring <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecoregions" title="Ecoregions">ecoregions</a> may require different policies or protections.
</p><p>Green parties are often formed in a given jurisdiction by a coalition of scientific <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecologists" title="Ecologists">ecologists</a>, community <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmentalists" title="Environmentalists">environmentalists</a>, and local (or national) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leftist" title="Leftist">leftist</a> groups or groups concerned with peace or citizens rights.
</p><p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red-Green_alliance" title="Red-Green alliance">Red-Green alliance</a> is an alliance between Green parties and <a href="/wiki/Social_democracy" title="Social democracy">social democratic</a> parties.  Such alliances are typically formed for the purpose of elections (mostly in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First-past-the-post" title="First-past-the-post">first-past-the-post</a> election systems), or, after elections, for the purpose of forming a government.
</p><p>Some Greens, such as those in <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>, find more effective alliances with more conservative groups (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue-Green_alliance" title="Blue-Green alliance">Blue-Green alliance</a>) or <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">indigenous peoples</a> - who seek to prevent disruption of traditional ways of life or to save ecological resources they depend on. Although Greens find much to support in fostering these types of alliances with groups of historically different backgrounds, they also feel strongly about forming diverse communities through encouragement of <a href="/wiki/Multiculturalism" title="Multiculturalism">diversity</a> in social and economic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demographics" title="Demographics">demographics</a> in communities, especially in the United States.
</p><p>Alliances often highlight strategic differences between participating in parties and advancing the values of the Green movement. For example, Greens became allied with centre-right parties to oust the centre-left ruling <a href="/wiki/Institutional_Revolutionary_Party" title="Institutional Revolutionary Party">Institutional Revolutionary Party</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Nader" title="Ralph Nader">Ralph Nader</a>, the 2000 presidential nominee of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Greens" title="U.S. Greens">U.S. Greens</a>, campaigned with ultra-conservative <a href="/wiki/Pat_Buchanan" title="Pat Buchanan">Pat Buchanan</a> on joint issues such as farm policy and bans on corporate funding of election campaigns, although this "alliance" between Nader and Buchanan was very specifically limited to the purpose of showing that there was broad support for certain specific issues, across the political spectrum.
</p><p>U.S. Greens grew dramatically throughout 2001. However, stable coalitions (such as that in Germany) tend to be formed between elections with left-wing parties on social issues, and 'the grassroots right' on such issues as irresponsible corporate subsidies and public ethics.
</p><p>On 13 June 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(Ireland)" title="Green Party (Ireland)">Irish Green Party</a>, represented by six members of parliament or <a href="/wiki/Teachta_D%C3%A1la" title="Teachta Dála">TDs</a>, agreed to go into a coalition government for the first time in their history, with <a href="/wiki/Fianna_F%C3%A1il" title="Fianna Fáil">Fianna Fáil</a>. Fianna Fáil and the Green Party were joined by the <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Democrats" title="Progressive Democrats">Progressive Democrats</a> in a coalition also supported by a number of independent members of parliament (the Progressive Democrats later dissolved as a party, though their members remained in parliament). The Green Party held two Cabinet seats, as well as two <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_State_(Ireland)" title="Minister of State (Ireland)">junior ministries</a>, until their withdrawal from government in January 2011. They lost all of their six seats in <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_2011" title="Irish general election, 2011">the following month's general election</a>, but regained two in <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_2016" title="Irish general election, 2016">the 2016 General Election</a>.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(Czech_Republic)" title="Green Party (Czech Republic)">Green Party</a> was part of the governing coalition, together with the conservative <a href="/wiki/Civic_Democratic_Party_(Czech_Republic)" title="Civic Democratic Party (Czech Republic)">Civic Democratic Party</a> (ODS) and the <a href="/wiki/Christian_and_Democratic_Union_%E2%80%93_Czechoslovak_People%27s_Party" title="Christian and Democratic Union – Czechoslovak People's Party">Christian Democrats</a> (KDU–ČSL) from January 2007 until the government collapsed in March 2009. The Finnish and French Green parties are now the only ones actively participating in government.
</p>

<p>As of 2002 the foundation of Green parties has been the most notable in Africa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The leader of the Kenyan Green Party, <a href="/wiki/Wangari_Maathai" title="Wangari Maathai">Wangari Maathai</a>, won the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Peace_Prize" title="Nobel Peace Prize">Nobel Peace Prize</a> in 2004, enhancing the image of Green parties across Africa and throughout the world.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia_and_Oceania">Asia and Oceania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Asia and Oceania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span id="Australia.2C_Fiji.2C_New_Zealand.2C_Papua_New_Guinea.2C_Vanuatu"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia,_Fiji,_New_Zealand,_Papua_New_Guinea,_Vanuatu">Australia, Fiji, New Zealand, Papua New Guinea, Vanuatu</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Australia, Fiji, New Zealand, Papua New Guinea, Vanuatu">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Asia_Pacific_Greens_Federation" title="Asia Pacific Greens Federation">Asia Pacific Greens Federation</a></div>
<p>Green parties have achieved national or state parliamentary representation in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vanuatu" title="Vanuatu">Vanuatu</a>. In New Zealand the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_New_Zealand" title="Green Party of New Zealand">Green Party of New Zealand</a> currently holds 14 seats in the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_House_of_Representatives" title="New Zealand House of Representatives">New Zealand House of Representatives</a> after the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2011" title="New Zealand general election, 2011">2011 general election</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Australian_Greens" title="Australian Greens">Australian Greens</a> hold 10 seats in the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Senate" title="Australian Senate">Australian Senate</a> and one seat in the <a href="/wiki/Australian_House_of_Representatives" title="Australian House of Representatives">Australian House of Representatives</a>. Since <a href="/wiki/Australian_federal_election,_2004" title="Australian federal election, 2004">2004</a>, they have received more votes than any other third party in every federal election. They also have representation in the upper and lower houses of <a href="/wiki/Parliaments_of_the_Australian_states_and_territories" title="Parliaments of the Australian states and territories">state parliaments</a> of five states and in the unicameral chamber of one territory. Greens also hold representative positions in local government across New Zealand and Australia (where a number of local government authorities are controlled by Green councilors). The Greens took the seat of Melbourne from the Australian Labor Party in 2010 with candidate <a href="/wiki/Adam_Bandt" title="Adam Bandt">Adam Bandt</a>. This is the first time the Greens have won a Lower House seat at a general election (although they have previously won two seats at by-elections).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">Proportional representation</a> in the Australian Senate and in New Zealand has strengthened the position of the Australian Greens and the Green Party of New Zealand and enabled them to participate directly in legislatures and policy-making committees. In countries following British-style '<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Majoritarian" title="Majoritarian">first past the post</a>' electoral rules, Green parties face barriers to gaining federal or provincial/regional/state seats. The <a href="/wiki/Australian_Labor_Party" title="Australian Labor Party">Australian Labor Party</a>'s practice of allocating a portion of ALP <a href="/wiki/Group_voting_ticket" title="Group voting ticket">ticket votes</a> to Australian Greens has helped bring AG candidates into parliament.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Capital_Territory_general_election,_2008" title="Australian Capital Territory general election, 2008">2008 ACT election</a> in Australia, the Greens won 15.6% of the vote, winning 4 out of 17 seats. Shane Rattenbury was elected the speaker of the assembly, the first time a Green party member had held such a position in any parliament or assembly in Australia. However, they retained only one seat at the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Capital_Territory_general_election,_2012" title="Australian Capital Territory general election, 2012">2012 election</a> in the same territory.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Green_Confederation" title="Green Confederation">Green Confederation</a> (<i>Confédération Verte</i>) in <a href="/wiki/Vanuatu" title="Vanuatu">Vanuatu</a> won 3 out of 52 seats in <a href="/wiki/Vanuatuan_general_election,_2012" title="Vanuatuan general election, 2012">the last general election</a> in October 2012. Its most prominent member is <a href="/wiki/Moana_Carcasses_Kalosil" title="Moana Carcasses Kalosil">Moana Carcasses Kalosil</a>, who became <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Vanuatu" title="Prime Minister of Vanuatu">Prime Minister</a> in March 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Carcasses, a <a href="/wiki/Green_liberalism" title="Green liberalism">Green liberal</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> does not lead a Green government, but a broad coalition government in which he is the only Green minister.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>There is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea_Greens_Party" title="Papua New Guinea Greens Party">Papua New Guinea Greens Party</a>, but it does not have any members in Parliament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> There was briefly a <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Fiji" title="Green Party of Fiji">Green Party in Fiji</a> from 2008 to 2013; as Parliament was suspended by the military regime during this time, the party was not able to take part in any election before all parties were deregistered in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="India.2C_Pakistan.2C_Saudi_Arabia"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="India,_Pakistan,_Saudi_Arabia">India, Pakistan, Saudi Arabia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: India, Pakistan, Saudi Arabia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Pakistan" title="Green Party of Pakistan">Green Party of Pakistan</a> was created in 2002, the Green Party of India emerged in 2010 <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_India&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Green Party of India (page does not exist)">Green Party of India</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="Green Party of Saudi Arabia">Green Party of Saudi Arabia</a> also emerged at that time. However, due to political repression, some South and Southwest Asian Green parties are <a href="/wiki/Subculture" title="Subculture">underground</a> organizations.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lebanon">Lebanon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Lebanon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Lebanon" title="Green Party of Lebanon">Green Party of Lebanon</a> was founded in 2008 as a secular party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>  Its first president was Philippe Skaff, CEO of Grey Advertising.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>  The party debuted with the May 2010 municipal elections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>  In 2011, the party became the first political party in <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a> to elect a female leader when Nada Zaarour was elected its president.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Belgian_and_German_roots">Belgian and German roots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Belgian and German roots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The first green parties in Europe were founded in the late 1970s, following the rise of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_awareness" title="Environmental awareness">environmental awareness</a> and the development of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_social_movement" title="New social movement">new social movements</a>. Green parties in Belgium first made a breakthrough.  Belgium had Green members of parliament elected first in the 1970s, and with seats on the local council, held the balance of power in the city of Liege, so were the first to go into coalition with the ruling party on that council. In 1979 political campaigns and dissident groups feeling underrepresented in west German politics formed a coalition to contest the 1979 elections to the European Parliament.
</p><p>Although they did not win any seats, the groups in this association formally agreed to become a party and won a breakthrough in the German national elections of 1983. They were not the first Green Party in Europe to have members elected nationally but the impression was created that they had been, because they attracted the most media attention.  This was partly due to their charismatic leader <a href="/wiki/Petra_Kelly" title="Petra Kelly">Petra Kelly</a>, a German who was of interest to the American media because she had an American step-father. Since its foundation in 1980 and merger with <a href="/wiki/Alliance_90" title="Alliance 90">Alliance 90</a> after the German reunification, Kelly's party, now named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alliance_%2790/The_Greens" title="Alliance '90/The Greens">Alliance '90/The Greens</a>, has become one of Europe's most important Green parties. It played an important role in the formation of national-level Green parties in other countries <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Confederation_of_The_Greens" title="Confederation of The Greens">such as Spain</a> as well. The forerunner of the Green Party in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> was the <a href="/wiki/PEOPLE_Party" title="PEOPLE Party">PEOPLE Party</a>, formed in <a href="/wiki/Coventry" title="Coventry">Coventry</a> in 1972. It changed its name to the Ecology Party in 1973 and the Green Party in 1985.
</p>
<h4><span id="1984.E2.80.931989:_A_new_political_force"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1984–1989:_A_new_political_force">1984–1989: A new political force</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 1984–1989: A new political force">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1984 Greens agreed a common platform for the European Parliament Elections and the first Green Members of the European Parliament were elected here. Germany, a stronghold of the Green movement, elected seven MEPs; two more came from Belgium and two from the Netherlands. As those nine MEPs did not entitle the Greens to form a parliamentary group on their own, they concluded an alliance with MEPs from Italy, Denmark, and regionalists from Flanders and Ireland to form the GRAEL (Green Alternative European Link) group, also known as the <a href="/wiki/Rainbow_Group_(1984%E2%80%9389)" title="Rainbow Group (1984–89)">Rainbow Group</a>. Politically they engaged in the fight against environmental pollution, nuclear energy (1986 saw the Chernobyl disaster), the promotion of animal protection and the campaign against the demolition of Brussels by speculation fuelled by the presence of the European institutions.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Since_the_1990s">Since the 1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Since the 1990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Flickr_-_Saeima_-_9.Saeimas_deput%C4%81ts_Indulis_Emsis.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3008" data-file-width="2000" height="226" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Flickr_-_Saeima_-_9.Saeimas_deput%C4%81ts_Indulis_Emsis.jpg/150px-Flickr_-_Saeima_-_9.Saeimas_deput%C4%81ts_Indulis_Emsis.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Flickr_-_Saeima_-_9.Saeimas_deput%C4%81ts_Indulis_Emsis.jpg/225px-Flickr_-_Saeima_-_9.Saeimas_deput%C4%81ts_Indulis_Emsis.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Flickr_-_Saeima_-_9.Saeimas_deput%C4%81ts_Indulis_Emsis.jpg/300px-Flickr_-_Saeima_-_9.Saeimas_deput%C4%81ts_Indulis_Emsis.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Flickr_-_Saeima_-_9.Saeimas_deput%C4%81ts_Indulis_Emsis.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Indulis_Emsis" title="Indulis Emsis">Indulis Emsis</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Latvian_Green_Party" title="Latvian Green Party">Latvian Green Party</a> became <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Latvia" title="Prime Minister of Latvia">Prime Minister of Latvia</a> in 2004, and the world's first green <a href="/wiki/Head_of_government" title="Head of government">head of government</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>After years of co-operation between the national Green parties, they formed a pan-European alliance that unites most European Green parties. The Greens are a party within the European parliament with 46 seats, as of June 2009. It has a long-standing alliance with the <a href="/wiki/European_Free_Alliance" title="European Free Alliance">European Free Alliance</a> (EFA), an alliance of "stateless nations", such as the Welsh nationalist <a href="/wiki/Plaid_Cymru" title="Plaid Cymru">Plaid Cymru</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scottish_National_Party" title="Scottish National Party">Scottish National Party</a>. Together European Green Party/EFA have 58 seats and they are the fourth largest party in the European Parliament.
</p><p>While on many issues European Greens practice the same policies, one issue divides European Green parties: the European Union. Some Green parties, like the Dutch <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GreenLeft" title="GreenLeft">GreenLeft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_England_and_Wales" title="Green Party of England and Wales">the Green Party of England and Wales</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Switzerland" title="Green Party of Switzerland">Swiss Green Party</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(Ireland)" title="Green Party (Ireland)">Irish Green Party</a> and the German Alliance '90/The Greens, are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pro-European" title="Pro-European">pro-European</a> while some, like the Green party in <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(Sweden)" title="Green Party (Sweden)">Sweden</a>, are moderately <a href="/wiki/Euroscepticism" title="Euroscepticism">eurosceptic</a>.
</p><p>Some Green parties have been part of governing coalitions. The first one was the Finnish <a href="/wiki/Green_League" title="Green League">Green League</a> that entered government in 1995. The Italian <a href="/wiki/Federation_of_the_Greens" title="Federation of the Greens">Federation of the Greens</a>, the <a href="/wiki/The_Greens_(France)" title="The Greens (France)">French Greens</a>, the German Alliance '90/The Greens and both Belgian Green parties, the French-speaking <a href="/wiki/Ecolo" title="Ecolo">Ecolo</a> and the Dutch-speaking <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agalev" title="Agalev">Agalev</a> were part of government during the late 1990s. Most successful was the <a href="/wiki/Latvian_Green_Party" title="Latvian Green Party">Latvian Green Party</a>, who supplied the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Latvia" title="Prime Minister of Latvia">Prime Minister of Latvia</a> in 2004. The Swedish Green party was a long term supporter of the social-democratic minority government until the election 2006 when the social-democratic party lost. The Irish Green Party were <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_30th_D%C3%A1il" title="Government of the 30th Dáil">in government</a> from <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_2007" title="Irish general election, 2007">2007</a> until January 2011 when the party withdrew their support for the ruling coalition. During their period in office, the Irish Green Party held two Cabinet portfolios including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minister_for_the_Environment,_Community_and_Local_Government" title="Minister for the Environment, Community and Local Government">Minister for the Environment, Heritage and Local Government</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minister_for_Communications,_Energy_and_Natural_Resources" title="Minister for Communications, Energy and Natural Resources">Minister for Communications, Energy and Natural Resources</a>.
</p>

<p>In Scandinavia, left-wing socialist parties have formed the <a href="/wiki/Nordic_Green_Left_Alliance" title="Nordic Green Left Alliance">Nordic Green Left Alliance</a>. These parties have the same ideals as European Greens. However, they do not cooperate with the <a href="/wiki/Global_Greens" title="Global Greens">Global Greens</a> or European Greens, but instead form a combined parliamentary group with the <a href="/wiki/Party_of_the_European_Left" title="Party of the European Left">Party of the European Left</a>, which unites communists and post-communists. There is one exception, in 2004 the MEP for Danish <a href="/wiki/Socialist_People%27s_Party_(Denmark)" title="Socialist People's Party (Denmark)">Socialist People's Party</a> has left the Nordic Green Left parliamentary group and has joined the Green parliamentary group in the European parliament. The Socialist People's Party is currently an observer at the <a href="/wiki/European_Green_Party" title="European Green Party">European Green Party</a> and the Global Greens. Outside of Scandinavia, in 2004, <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a> became the first country in the world to have a Green politician become Head of Government, but in 2006 the Green Party received only 16.71 percent of the vote. In the <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a> <a href="/wiki/Estonian_parliamentary_election,_2007" title="Estonian parliamentary election, 2007">2007 parliamentary elections</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Estonian_Greens" title="Estonian Greens">Estonian Greens</a> won 7.1 percent of the vote, and a mandate for six seats in the country's parliament, the <a href="/wiki/Riigikogu" title="Riigikogu">Riigikogu</a>. Other significant electoral results for European green parties include Germany's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alliance_%2790/The_Greens" title="Alliance '90/The Greens">Alliance '90/The Greens</a> in the <a href="/wiki/German_federal_election,_2002" title="German federal election, 2002">2002 federal election</a> and France's <a href="/wiki/Europe_Ecology_%E2%80%93_The_Greens" title="Europe Ecology – The Greens">Europe Ecology – The Greens</a> in the <a href="/wiki/French_legislative_election,_2012" title="French legislative election, 2012">2012 legislative election</a>, in which they both won more seats than any other <a href="/wiki/Third_party_(politics)" title="Third party (politics)">third party</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Caroline_Lucas_2010.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1243" data-file-width="872" height="214" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Caroline_Lucas_2010.jpg/150px-Caroline_Lucas_2010.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Caroline_Lucas_2010.jpg/225px-Caroline_Lucas_2010.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Caroline_Lucas_2010.jpg/300px-Caroline_Lucas_2010.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Caroline_Lucas_2010.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>British MP <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Lucas" title="Caroline Lucas">Caroline Lucas</a> is the UK's first green politician elected under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First-past-the-post" title="First-past-the-post">first-past-the-post</a> system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>In some countries Greens have found it difficult to win any representation in the national parliament. Three reasons can be found for this. It includes countries with a first past the post electoral system, such as the United Kingdom. However, despite the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_past_the_post" title="First past the post">first past the post</a> system in the United Kingdom, the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_England_and_Wales" title="Green Party of England and Wales">Green Party of England and Wales</a> won their first seat in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a> when <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Lucas" title="Caroline Lucas">Caroline Lucas</a> won the seat of <a href="/wiki/Brighton_Pavilion_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Brighton Pavilion (UK Parliament constituency)">Brighton Pavilion</a> in 2010.  The <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Green_Party" title="Scottish Green Party">Scottish Green Party</a> has had success in the devolved <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a> and the Irish <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Green Party in Northern Ireland">Green Party in Northern Ireland</a> has had success in the devolved <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a>, where the first-past-the-post system is not used. In countries where a party with similar ideals is stronger, such as <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(Norway)" title="Green Party (Norway)">Norway</a> and Denmark, Green parties tend to perform worse. In some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_European" title="Eastern European">Eastern European</a> countries, like <a href="/wiki/Ecologist_Party_of_Romania" title="Ecologist Party of Romania">Romania</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Greens_(Poland)" title="The Greens (Poland)">Poland</a>, Green parties are still in the process of formation and have therefore not gained enough support. The <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Bulgaria" title="Green Party of Bulgaria">Green Party of Bulgaria</a> is a part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left-wing" title="Left-wing">left-wing</a> <a href="/wiki/Coalition_for_Bulgaria" title="Coalition for Bulgaria">Coalition for Bulgaria</a>, currently in opposition. It has no parliamentary representation but it did supply one Deputy Minister in the government of Prime Minister <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sergey_Stanishev" title="Sergey Stanishev">Sergey Stanishev</a> from 2005 to 2009.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/European_Green_Party" title="European Green Party">European Green Party</a> has worked to support weak Green parties in European countries. Until recently, they were giving support to Green parties in the Mediterranean countries. These Green parties are now making electoral gains, e.g. in Spain, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecologists_Greens" title="Ecologists Greens">Greece</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecological_and_Environmental_Movement" title="Ecological and Environmental Movement">Republic of Cyprus</a>, or getting organized to do so, e.g. in <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Alternative_(Malta)" title="Democratic Alternative (Malta)">Malta</a>. Therefore, the EGP is now turning its attention to Eastern Europe – all these countries have Green parties, but in materially poor Eastern Europe the success of Green Parties is patchy except for Hungary, where the local Green party, <a href="/wiki/Politics_Can_Be_Different" title="Politics Can Be Different">Politics Can Be Different</a> (LMP), has been elected to parliament and many city councils.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Turkey">Turkey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Turkey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Greens and the Left Party of the Future is a left-libertarian and green party in Turkey. It was founded on 25 November 2012 as a merger of the Greens Party and the Equality and Democracy Party.
</p><p>Prominent members include Murat Belge, left-liberal political author and columnist for <i>Taraf</i>; Kutluğ Ataman, filmmaker and contemporary artist; and Ufuk Uras, former Istanbul deputy and president of the Freedom and Solidarity Party.
</p><p>The party is one of the participants in the Peoples' Democratic Congress, a political initiative instrumental in founding the Peoples' Democratic Party in 2012.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2011" title="Canadian federal election, 2011">41st general election</a> in Canada, held on May 2, 2011, there was only one federally elected member of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Canada" title="Green Party of Canada">Green Party of Canada</a> in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_Canada" title="House of Commons of Canada">House of Commons of Canada</a>, its leader <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_May" title="Elizabeth May">Elizabeth May</a>. However, at the dissolution of the <a href="/wiki/41st_Canadian_Parliament" title="41st Canadian Parliament">41st Parliament</a> on August 2, 2015, the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Canada" title="Green Party of Canada">Green Party of Canada</a> held two seats in the House of Commons, the second seat belonging to formerly Independent MP <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Hyer" title="Bruce Hyer">Bruce Hyer</a> who was elected to the House of Commons as a member of the <a href="/wiki/New_Democratic_Party" title="New Democratic Party">NDP</a> in 2011. Only May won <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2015" title="Canadian federal election, 2015">re-election</a> to the <a href="/wiki/42nd_Canadian_Parliament" title="42nd Canadian Parliament">42nd parliament</a>. There remains no federal representation by the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_the_United_States" title="Green Party of the United States">Green Party of the United States</a> in the U.S. Congress. Accordingly, in these countries, Green parties focus on <a href="/wiki/Electoral_reform" title="Electoral reform">electoral reform</a>. In Mexico, however, the <i><a href="/wiki/Ecologist_Green_Party_of_Mexico" title="Ecologist Green Party of Mexico">Partido Verde Ecologista</a>,</i> often abbreviated as PVEM, has 17 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chamber_of_Deputies_of_Mexico" title="Chamber of Deputies of Mexico">deputies</a> and four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Senate_of_Mexico" title="Senate of Mexico">senators</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congress_of_Mexico" title="Congress of Mexico">Congress</a> as a result of the 2006 elections. Nevertheless, some of its political practices such as plead in favor of the death penalty in Mexico, led to the <a href="/wiki/European_Green_Party" title="European Green Party">European Green Party</a>'s withdrawal of recognition of the PVEM as a legitimate green party. 
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The first Green parties in Canada (both federal and provincial) were founded in 1983. The strongest provincial Green parties are the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_British_Columbia" title="Green Party of British Columbia">Green Party of British Columbia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Ontario" title="Green Party of Ontario">Green Party of Ontario</a>. As of May 2013, the first ever BC elected green member was elected, in 2014 a Green MLA was elected to the New Brunswick legislature, and in 2018 <a href="/wiki/Mike_Schreiner" title="Mike Schreiner">Mike Schreiner</a> became the first Green MPP elected to the Ontario provincial legislature. Federally, the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Canada" title="Green Party of Canada">Green Party of Canada</a> received 3.91% of the popular vote in the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2011" title="Canadian federal election, 2011">2011 federal election</a> and its support and influence continues to rise. Support waned slightly in the 2011 federal election when it captured 3.91% of the popular vote, down from the 6.8% captured in the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2008" title="Canadian federal election, 2008">2008 federal election</a>; support fell another 0.46% in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election" title="Canadian federal election">2015 federal election</a>. Although Ms. May was the first elected Member of Parliament, the first seat was gained in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_Canada" title="House of Commons of Canada">House of Commons of Canada</a> on August 30, 2008 when sitting Independent MP <a href="/wiki/Blair_Wilson" title="Blair Wilson">Blair Wilson</a> joined the party. May continues to sit as a Member of Parliament for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saanich-Gulf_Islands" title="Saanich-Gulf Islands">Saanich-Gulf Islands</a> and has exerted a considerable amount of legislative influence on a number of issues ranging from denying unanimous consent for military intervention in Libya, playing a central role in exposing changes to environmental legislation hidden in the omnibus Budget Bill C-38, to introducing a <a href="/wiki/Private_member%27s_bill" title="Private member's bill">private member's bill</a> to develop a national strategy on <a href="/wiki/Lyme_disease" title="Lyme disease">Lyme disease</a>.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Vancouver_municipal_election,_2008" title="Vancouver municipal election, 2008">2008 Vancouver municipal election</a>, Stuart Mackinnon, a member of the Vancouver Green Party, was elected to the Vancouver Parks Board. Since that time former <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_British_Columbia" title="Green Party of British Columbia">Green Party of British Columbia</a> leader, and deputy-leader of the federal Green party, <a href="/wiki/Adriane_Carr" title="Adriane Carr">Adriane Carr</a> won the Greens' first seat on Vancouver City Council, in 2011 municipal elections. <i>See also: <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Canada#Provincial_parties" title="Green Party of Canada">Provincial parties in Canada</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/List_of_Green_party_leaders_in_Canada" title="List of Green party leaders in Canada">List of Green party leaders in Canada</a>.</i>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the United States, Greens first ran for public office in 1985. Since then, the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_the_United_States" title="Green Party of the United States">Green Party of the United States</a> has claimed electoral victories at the municipal, county and <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">state</a> levels.  The first U.S. Greens to be elected were <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=David_Conley_(politician)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="David Conley (politician) (page does not exist)">David Conley</a> and Frank Koehn in Wisconsin 1986. Each was elected to a position on the County Board of Supervisors in Douglas and Bayfield counties respectively. <a href="/wiki/Keiko_Bonk" title="Keiko Bonk">Keiko Bonk</a> was first elected in 1992 in Hawaii County, becoming Official Chairwoman in 1995.  The first Green Party mayor <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> was Kelly Weaverling, elected in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cordova,_AK" title="Cordova, AK">Cordova, AK</a> in 1991.
</p><p>As of April 2018, 156 Greens held elective office across the U.S.A. in 19 states. The states with the largest numbers of Green elected officials are California (68), Connecticut (15), and Pennsylvania (15). Titles of offices held include: Alderman, Auditor, Board of Appeals, Board of Finance, Board of Selectmen, Borough Council, Budget Committee, Circuit Court Judge, City Council, Common Council, Community College District Board of Trustees, Community Service Board,  Conservation Congress, Constable, County Board of Supervisors, County Supervisor, Fire Commission, Fire District Board, Inspector of Elections, Judge of Elections, Mayor, Neighborhood Council Board, Park District, Parks and Recreation District Board, Library Board, Planning Board, Public Housing Authority Resident Advisory Board, Public Service District,  Rent Stabilization Board, Sanitary District Board,  School Board, Soil and Water Conservation Board, State Representative, Town Council, Transit District Board, Village Trustee, Water District Board, and Zoning Board of Appeals  <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> As of October 2016, 100 Greens held elected office across the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The first US Green elected to a state legislature was <a href="/wiki/Audie_Bock" title="Audie Bock">Audie Bock</a> in 1999, to the <a href="/wiki/California_State_Assembly" title="California State Assembly">California State Assembly</a>, followed by <a href="/wiki/John_Eder" title="John Eder">John Eder</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Maine_House_of_Representatives" title="Maine House of Representatives">Maine House of Representatives</a> in 2002 and 2004 and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Carroll_(politician)" title="Richard Carroll (politician)">Richard Carroll</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Arkansas_House_of_Representatives" title="Arkansas House of Representatives">Arkansas House of Representatives</a> in 2008. While in office in 2003 in the <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey_General_Assembly" title="New Jersey General Assembly">New Jersey General Assembly</a>, incumbent <a href="/wiki/Matt_Ahearn" title="Matt Ahearn">Matt Ahearn</a> made a <a href="/wiki/Party_switching_in_the_United_States" title="Party switching in the United States">party switch</a> to Green for the remainder of his term.
</p><p>The Green Party has contested six U.S.A. presidential elections: in 1996 and 2000 with <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Nader" title="Ralph Nader">Ralph Nader</a> for President and <a href="/wiki/Winona_LaDuke" title="Winona LaDuke">Winona LaDuke</a> as Vice President, in 2004 with <a href="/wiki/David_Cobb" title="David Cobb">David Cobb</a> for President and <a href="/wiki/Pat_LaMarche" title="Pat LaMarche">Pat LaMarche</a> for Vice President, and in 2008 with <a href="/wiki/Cynthia_McKinney" title="Cynthia McKinney">Cynthia McKinney</a> for President and <a href="/wiki/Rosa_Clemente" title="Rosa Clemente">Rosa Clemente</a> for Vice President. In 2000, Nader received more votes for president than any Green Party candidate before or since. <a href="/wiki/Jill_Stein" title="Jill Stein">Jill Stein</a> ran for president on the Green ticket in 2012 and 2016; the vice-presidential candidates were <a href="/wiki/Cheri_Honkala" title="Cheri Honkala">Cheri Honkala</a> in 2012 and <a href="/wiki/Ajamu_Baraka" title="Ajamu Baraka">Ajamu Baraka</a> in 2016. Stein, who received over one million votes in the 2016 race, and led unsuccessful attempts toward <a href="/wiki/2016_United_States_presidential_election_recounts" title="2016 United States presidential election recounts">2016 election recounts</a> in three states: Michigan, Pennsylvania, and Wisconsin.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_America">South America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: South America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazil">Brazil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Brazil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Marina_Silva" title="Marina Silva">Marina Silva</a>'s Green Party in the <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_presidential_election,_2010" title="Brazilian presidential election, 2010">Brazilian presidential election, 2010</a> won 19.33% of the vote of the first round (more than any other third party), taking enough votes from <a href="/wiki/Dilma_Rousseff" title="Dilma Rousseff">Dilma Rousseff</a> of the incumbent <a href="/wiki/Workers%27_Party_(Brazil)" title="Workers' Party (Brazil)">PT</a> party to stop her getting the 50% needed to avoid a second round of which she went on to beat Jose Serra of the opposition PSDB party. Despite only winning numerically the vote in one state, the Green Party came second in front of Dilma Rousseff in <a href="/wiki/Amap%C3%A1" title="Amapá">Amapa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amazonas_(Brazilian_state)" title="Amazonas (Brazilian state)">Amazonas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pernambuco" title="Pernambuco">Pernambuco</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a> in front of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jose_Serra" title="Jose Serra">Jose Serra</a>. The Green Party tied with Serra in Ceara, both having 16.36% of the vote, although Dilma Rousseff won the state. <a href="/wiki/Maranh%C3%A3o" title="Maranhão">Maranhão</a> also had a 1.5% difference in vote between Serra and Marina with the Green Party in third. In <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo_(state)" title="São Paulo (state)">São Paulo</a>, the Green Part had their strongest showing with almost 5 million (20.77%) of the votes cast. Success in the legislature amounted to winning 2 more seats for a total of 15 in the Chamber of Deputies and the loss of their only senate seat.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Colombia">Colombia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Colombia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the 1990s, the <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_Green_Party" title="Oxygen Green Party">Oxygen Green Party</a> was created under the leadership of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ingrid_Betancourt" title="Ingrid Betancourt">Ingrid Betancourt</a> but dissolved after her infamous kidnapping. Later, the Visionaries Party was created by  <a href="/wiki/Antanas_Mockus" title="Antanas Mockus">Antanas Mockus</a> whose ideals earned him the Bogotá Mayoral Office twice.
In the <a href="/wiki/Colombian_presidential_election,_2010" title="Colombian presidential election, 2010">2010 Colombian presidential election</a> a green party has been created under the name <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_Party_(Colombia)" title="Green Party (Colombia)">Colombian Green Party</a>, with former <a href="/wiki/Bogot%C3%A1" title="Bogotá">Bogotá</a> mayor Antanas Mockus being the leader.
</p>
