<p><b>Royal Air Force Greenham Common</b> or <b>RAF Greenham Common</b> is a former <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force_station" title="Royal Air Force station">station</a> in <a href="/wiki/Berkshire" title="Berkshire">Berkshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. The airfield was southeast of <a href="/wiki/Newbury,_Berkshire" title="Newbury, Berkshire">Newbury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berkshire" title="Berkshire">Berkshire</a>, about 55 miles (89 km) west of London.
</p><p>Opened in 1942, it was used by both the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Air_Forces" title="United States Army Air Forces">United States Army Air Forces</a> during the Second World War and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, also as a base for nuclear weapons. After the Cold War ended, it was closed in September 1992.
</p><p>The airfield was also known for the <a href="/wiki/Greenham_Common_Women%27s_Peace_Camp" title="Greenham Common Women's Peace Camp">Greenham Common Women's Peace Camp</a> held outside its gates in the 1980s in protest against the stationing of cruise missiles on the base.
</p><p>In 1997 Greenham Common was designated as public parkland, effectively returning it to its pre-WW2 status but with restrictions. The common was made a site of <a href="/wiki/Site_of_Special_Scientific_Interest" title="Site of Special Scientific Interest">site of Special Scientific Interest (SSSI)</a> in 1985 along with Crookham Common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sssi_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-sssi-2">[2]</a></sup> The Cold War era control tower has recently been redeveloped and is now open as a visitor centre and cafe.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_World_War">Second World War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RAF_Greenham_Common&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Second World War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before the building of an airfield, Greenham Common was a massive piece of <a href="/wiki/Common_land" title="Common land">common land</a>. It was used for troop movements during the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> and in the eighteen and nineteenth centuries.
</p><p>In late 1943, Greenham Common airfield was turned over to the USAAF <a href="/wiki/Ninth_Air_Force" title="Ninth Air Force">Ninth Air Force</a>. An American advance party soon arrived to ready the airfield for the incoming units.  Greenham Common was known as <b>USAAF Station AAF-486</b> for security reasons by the USAAF during the war, and it was referred to by this name instead of by location.  Its USAAF Station Code was "GC".
</p><p>The first arrival was the <a href="/wiki/51st_Troop_Carrier_Wing" title="51st Troop Carrier Wing">51st Troop Carrier Wing</a> Headquarters, arriving in September 1942. The 51st TCW controlled the three troop carrier groups at <a href="/wiki/RAF_Keevil" title="RAF Keevil">RAF Keevil</a> (62nd TCG), <a href="/wiki/RAF_Aldermaston" title="RAF Aldermaston">RAF Aldermaston</a> (60th TCG) and <a href="/wiki/RAF_Ramsbury" title="RAF Ramsbury">RAF Ramsbury</a> (64th TCG) as part of <a href="/wiki/Twelfth_Air_Force" title="Twelfth Air Force">Twelfth Air Force</a>. The Wing Headquarters was located in requisitioned Bowdown House, a mansion on the northeast end of the airfield, and made use of the runways for its communication and courier flights.
</p><p>The 51st TCW HQ followed its groups to North Africa as part of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Torch" title="Operation Torch">Operation Torch</a> in November 1942.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="354th_Fighter_Group">354th Fighter Group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RAF_Greenham_Common&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 354th Fighter Group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:354fg-p51b.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="356" data-file-width="600" height="131" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/354fg-p51b.jpg/220px-354fg-p51b.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/354fg-p51b.jpg/330px-354fg-p51b.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/354fg-p51b.jpg/440px-354fg-p51b.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:354fg-p51b.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/North_American_Aviation" title="North American Aviation">North American</a> <a href="/wiki/North_American_P-51_Mustang#P-51B_and_P-51C" title="North American P-51 Mustang">P-51B-1-NA Mustang</a>, AAF Ser. No. 43-12408 of the <a href="/wiki/355th_Fighter_Squadron" title="355th Fighter Squadron">355th Fighter Squadron</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:P-47-d3-368f-1945.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="209" data-file-width="600" height="77" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/P-47-d3-368f-1945.jpg/220px-P-47-d3-368f-1945.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/P-47-d3-368f-1945.jpg/330px-P-47-d3-368f-1945.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/P-47-d3-368f-1945.jpg/440px-P-47-d3-368f-1945.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:P-47-d3-368f-1945.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Republic_P-47_Thunderbolt#P-47D_/_P-47G_/_XP-47K_/_XP-47L" title="Republic P-47 Thunderbolt">Republic P-47D</a> of the 397th Fighter Squadron</div></div></div>
<p>As troop carrier groups began arriving in the UK in late 1943 and deployed in the Greenham area, Greenham Common was one of the airfields used by the Ninth Air Force for fighter groups arriving from the United States.
</p><p>On 4 November the <a href="/wiki/354th_Fighter_Group" title="354th Fighter Group">354th Fighter Group</a> arrived from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portland_Army_Air_Base" title="Portland Army Air Base">Portland Army Air Field</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a> and they were informed they were to fly the <a href="/wiki/North_American_P-51_Mustang" title="North American P-51 Mustang">North American P-51 Mustang</a>. This was a change of equipment for the group, as they had trained with <a href="/wiki/Bell_P-39_Airacobra" title="Bell P-39 Airacobra">Bell P-39 Airacobras</a>. The group stayed at Greenham Common for only a few days, being transferred to <a href="/wiki/RAF_Boxted" title="RAF Boxted">RAF Boxted</a> in Essex on 13 November.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="368th_Fighter_Group">368th Fighter Group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RAF_Greenham_Common&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 368th Fighter Group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A few weeks later on 13 January 1944, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/368th_Fighter_Group" title="368th Fighter Group">368th Fighter Group</a> arrived from <a href="/wiki/Farmingdale,_New_York" title="Farmingdale, New York">Farmingdale, New York</a>, flying <a href="/wiki/Republic_Aviation" title="Republic Aviation">Republic</a> <a href="/wiki/Republic_P-47_Thunderbolt" title="Republic P-47 Thunderbolt">P-47 Thunderbolts</a>. They had the following fighter squadrons and fuselage codes:
</p>
<ul><li>395th Fighter Squadron (A7)</li>
<li>396th Fighter Squadron (C2)</li>
<li>397th Fighter Squadron (D3)</li></ul>
<p>The 368th was a group of <a href="/wiki/Ninth_Air_Force" title="Ninth Air Force">Ninth Air Force</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/71st_Fighter_Wing_(World_War_II)" title="71st Fighter Wing (World War II)">71st Fighter Wing</a>, <a href="/wiki/IX_Tactical_Air_Command" title="IX Tactical Air Command">IX Tactical Air Command</a>.  The 368th FG moved to <a href="/wiki/RAF_Chilbolton" title="RAF Chilbolton">RAF Chilbolton</a> the next day.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="438th_Troop_Carrier_Group">438th Troop Carrier Group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RAF_Greenham_Common&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 438th Troop Carrier Group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eisenhower_d-day.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="931" data-file-width="1189" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Eisenhower_d-day.jpg/220px-Eisenhower_d-day.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Eisenhower_d-day.jpg/330px-Eisenhower_d-day.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Eisenhower_d-day.jpg/440px-Eisenhower_d-day.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eisenhower_d-day.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>General <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight D. Eisenhower</a> addresses Company E, 502nd Parachute Infantry Regiment (Strike), at Greenham Common Airfield about 8:30 pm on 5 June 1944.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:C47-4u-438tcg-rafgc.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="323" data-file-width="600" height="118" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/C47-4u-438tcg-rafgc.jpg/220px-C47-4u-438tcg-rafgc.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/C47-4u-438tcg-rafgc.jpg/330px-C47-4u-438tcg-rafgc.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/C47-4u-438tcg-rafgc.jpg/440px-C47-4u-438tcg-rafgc.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:C47-4u-438tcg-rafgc.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Douglas_C-47_Skytrain" title="Douglas C-47 Skytrain">C-47</a> of the 89th Troop Carrier Squadron</div></div></div>

<p>Literally as the 368th FG was moving out, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/438th_Troop_Carrier_Group" title="438th Troop Carrier Group">438th Troop Carrier Group</a> was flying into Greenham Common from <a href="/wiki/RAF_Langar" title="RAF Langar">RAF Langar</a>. Flying <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Aircraft_Company" title="Douglas Aircraft Company">Douglas</a> <a href="/wiki/Douglas_C-47_Skytrain" title="Douglas C-47 Skytrain">C-47 Skytrains</a>, they had the following Troop Carrier squadrons and fuselage codes:
</p>
<ul><li>87th Troop Carrier Squadron (3X)</li>
<li>88th Troop Carrier Squadron (M2)</li>
<li>89th Troop Carrier Squadron (4U)</li>
<li>90th Troop Carrier Squadron (Q7)</li></ul>
<p>The 438th was a group of <a href="/wiki/Ninth_Air_Force" title="Ninth Air Force">Ninth Air Force</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/53d_Troop_Carrier_Wing_(World_War_II)" title="53d Troop Carrier Wing (World War II)">53rd Troop Carrier Wing</a> of <a href="/wiki/IX_Troop_Carrier_Command" title="IX Troop Carrier Command">IX Troop Carrier Command</a>.
</p><p>The 90th TCS stayed in the UK and operated from <a href="/wiki/RAF_Welford" title="RAF Welford">RAF Welford</a> until the rest of the groups aircraft returned from Italy on 24 August.  In the absence of the 438th, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/316th_Troop_Carrier_Group" title="316th Troop Carrier Group">316th Troop Carrier Group</a> used Greenham Common as a forward base.
</p><p>The airfield was inactive for the next five years, and would probably have remained so but for the sudden escalation in tension between the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> and its Second World War <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allies</a> in what later became known as the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cold_War">Cold War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RAF_Greenham_Common&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cold War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the 1948 <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Blockade" title="Berlin Blockade">Berlin Blockade</a> and the 1950 <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">invasion of South Korea</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Korean_People%27s_Army" title="Korean People's Army">Korean People's Army</a>, the perceived threat from the Soviet Union rose. On 23 April 1951, RAF Greenham Common was made available to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> by the British <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Defence (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Defence</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Air_Command" title="Strategic Air Command">Strategic Air Command</a> base, with joint operations with the Royal Air Force units. Formal handover to the 7th Air Division was on June 18 and massive reconstruction work began. Basically, the entire wartime airfield was demolished and replaced with a new 10,000 ft (3048 m) east/west runway with parallel taxiways north and south of it leading to extensive hardstandings. A new technical and domestic site was built to the south of the runway, involving diversion of the A339 road and demolition of several homes and two pubs. Construction work was finally completed and the base declared operational in September 1953.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strategic_Air_Command">Strategic Air Command</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RAF_Greenham_Common&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Strategic Air Command">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:KC97.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="416" data-file-width="640" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/KC97.jpg/220px-KC97.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/KC97.jpg/330px-KC97.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/KC97.jpg/440px-KC97.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:KC97.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KC-97_Stratofreighter" title="KC-97 Stratofreighter">KC-97 Stratofreighter</a>, 1951</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Usaf-b-47-usafmuseum.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="381" data-file-width="600" height="140" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Usaf-b-47-usafmuseum.jpg/220px-Usaf-b-47-usafmuseum.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Usaf-b-47-usafmuseum.jpg/330px-Usaf-b-47-usafmuseum.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Usaf-b-47-usafmuseum.jpg/440px-Usaf-b-47-usafmuseum.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Usaf-b-47-usafmuseum.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>USAF Boeing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-47_Stratojet#B-47" title="B-47 Stratojet">B-47E-50-LM Stratojet</a>, AF Ser. No. 52-3363, in flight.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:B-58_Hustler.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1378" data-file-width="1800" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/B-58_Hustler.jpg/220px-B-58_Hustler.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/B-58_Hustler.jpg/330px-B-58_Hustler.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/B-58_Hustler.jpg/440px-B-58_Hustler.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:B-58_Hustler.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-58_Hustler" title="B-58 Hustler">B-58 Hustler</a> in flight</div></div></div>
<p>In the post-Second World War years, the United States <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Air_Command" title="Strategic Air Command">Strategic Air Command</a> was based at three major airfields in eastern England: <a href="/wiki/RAF_Lakenheath" title="RAF Lakenheath">RAF Lakenheath</a>, <a href="/wiki/RAF_Marham" title="RAF Marham">RAF Marham</a> and <a href="/wiki/RAF_Sculthorpe" title="RAF Sculthorpe">RAF Sculthorpe</a>. The increasing tension of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> led to a re-evaluation of these deployments and a move further west, behind RAF fighter forces, to RAF Greenham Common, <a href="/wiki/RAF_Brize_Norton" title="RAF Brize Norton">RAF Brize Norton</a>, <a href="/wiki/RAF_Upper_Heyford" title="RAF Upper Heyford">RAF Upper Heyford</a> and <a href="/wiki/RAF_Fairford" title="RAF Fairford">RAF Fairford</a>.
</p><p>The airfield came under Strategic Air Command's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/7th_Air_Division_(United_States)" title="7th Air Division (United States)">7th Air Division</a>, with the 3909th Combat Support Group as its administrative unit on the base, responsible for all non-flying activities as well as maintenance and logistical support of the flying units attached to RAF Greenham Common.
</p><p>The initial bomber wing deployed was the 303d Bombardment Wing with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-47_Stratojet" title="B-47 Stratojet">B-47 Stratojets</a>, arriving on 17 March 1954 from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Davis-Monthan_Air_Force_Base" title="Davis-Monthan Air Force Base">Davis-Monthan Air Force Base</a> in <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a>. The 303d stayed just over a month, returning on 28 April 1954. This was the first of the short-term temporary duty deployments from home bases in the US that continued intermittently over the next 10 years. Other known SAC deployments were:
</p>
<ul><li>Greenham Common Task Force (Provisional) (1 November 1955 – UNK) (ERB-29Aa and RB-50G/Es) (Electronic Reconnaissance and Countermeasures) (TDY From 97th Bombardment Wing, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schilling_Air_Force_Base" title="Schilling Air Force Base">Schilling AFB</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a>)</li>
<li>97th Air Refueling Squadron (5 May 1956 – 13 July 1956)(KC-97)<br/>(TDY From 97th Bombardment Wing, Schilling AFB, Kansas)</li>
<li>310th Bombardment Wing (3 October 1956 – 9 January 1957) (B-47, KC-97)<br/>(TDY Smokey Hill AFB, later renamed Schilling AFB, Kansas)</li>
<li>40th Bombardment Wing (1 July 1957 – 1 October 1957) (B-47, KC-97)<br/>(TDY Schilling AFB, Kansas)</li></ul>
<p>In April 1958 the 90-day detachments were replaced by a three-week Reflex Alert rotation, during which the bombers did not fly, reducing the noise considerably. The runways and dispersals were further strengthened for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-52" title="B-52">Boeing B-52 Stratofortress</a> bomber, but none were based at Greenham.
</p><p>From August 1960 the B-52 made periodic training visits, and a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-58_Hustler" title="B-58 Hustler">Convair B-58 Hustler</a> arrived briefly in October 1963. Reflex operations by B-47 and KC-97s continued until 1 April 1964.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Air_Command" title="Strategic Air Command">Strategic Air Command</a> (SAC) departed Greenham Common on 30 June 1964, much to the relief of the local residents; the thundering jet bombers of SAC came no more, and for several years it was used for USAF storage and as a relief base.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_Air_Forces_in_Europe">United States Air Forces in Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RAF_Greenham_Common&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: United States Air Forces in Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gate_1961.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="337" data-file-width="640" height="116" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Gate_1961.jpg/220px-Gate_1961.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Gate_1961.jpg/330px-Gate_1961.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Gate_1961.jpg/440px-Gate_1961.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gate_1961.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Gate to RAF Greenham Common, 1961.</div></div></div>
<p>The closure of US bases in France forced the reopening of RAF Greenham Common for air transport operations (Operation FRELOC) to handle <a href="/wiki/Materiel" title="Materiel">materiel</a> and personnel overflow beginning in early January 1967.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In late 1967, Greenham Common was used for <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exercise_REFORGER" title="Exercise REFORGER">Exercise REFORGER</a>, again as a result of the withdrawal of France from the NATO integrated military alliance.
</p><p>On 1 November 1968 control of RAF Greenham Common was transferred from SAC to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Forces_in_Europe" title="United States Air Forces in Europe">United States Air Forces in Europe</a>, with the 7551st Combat Support Group having administrative control of the base. However, the base was little used, primarily being utilised as a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Postal_Service" title="United States Postal Service">United States Military Postal Mail</a> sorting facility, with aircraft flying mail in from the United States, being sorted at Greenham Common, then distributed to American bases in the UK and Europe. Mail from American forces in Europe was also sent to Greenham Common and sorted there, before being flown to the United States.
</p><p>Beginning in 1973 the base became the home of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Air_Tattoo" title="International Air Tattoo">International Air Tattoo</a>, a large scale international military <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airshow" title="Airshow">airshow</a>, since relocated to <a href="/wiki/RAF_Fairford" title="RAF Fairford">RAF Fairford</a>, approximately 40 miles (64 km) away.
</p><p>The 20th Tactical Fighter Wing at <a href="/wiki/RAF_Upper_Heyford" title="RAF Upper Heyford">RAF Upper Heyford</a> established "Operating Location A" at Greenham Common for its F-111 fighters in 1976, using the airfield occasionally for dispersal exercises.
</p><p>In 1977 the USAF announced plans to reactivate the base to house <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KC-135" title="KC-135">KC-135</a> <a href="/wiki/Aerial_refueling" title="Aerial refueling">aerial refueling</a> tanker aircraft, due to a lack of capacity at the KC-135's main base in Britain, <a href="/wiki/RAF_Mildenhall" title="RAF Mildenhall">RAF Mildenhall</a>. This led to widespread local opposition, and in 1978 the British Defence Secretary vetoed the plan.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="501st_Tactical_Missile_Wing">501st Tactical Missile Wing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RAF_Greenham_Common&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 501st Tactical Missile Wing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RAF_Greenham_Common_runway.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="471" data-file-width="709" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/RAF_Greenham_Common_runway.jpg/220px-RAF_Greenham_Common_runway.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/RAF_Greenham_Common_runway.jpg/330px-RAF_Greenham_Common_runway.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/RAF_Greenham_Common_runway.jpg/440px-RAF_Greenham_Common_runway.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RAF_Greenham_Common_runway.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Greenham Common in the late 80s</div></div></div>
<p>The Soviet deployment of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SS-20" title="SS-20">SS-20</a> missile from 1975 caused major concern in the <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> alliance. The longer range, greater accuracy, mobility and striking power of the new missile was perceived to alter the security of Western Europe. It was feared that the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> could launch a nuclear strike against Western Europe with a reduced threat of nuclear retaliation (i.e. compared to an attack on the continental United States). After discussions, NATO agreed to a two part strategy:
</p>
<ul><li>To pursue arms control negotiations with the Soviet Union to reduce the Soviet and American INF arsenals.</li>
<li>To deploy in Europe from 1983 up to 464 USAF <a href="/wiki/BGM-109G_Ground_Launched_Cruise_Missile" title="BGM-109G Ground Launched Cruise Missile">BGM-109G Ground Launched Cruise Missiles</a> or GLCMs, as well as 108 US Army <a href="/wiki/Pershing_II" title="Pershing II">Pershing II</a> ballistic missiles. (See also <a href="/wiki/Submarine-launched_cruise_missile" title="Submarine-launched cruise missile">SLCM</a> and <a href="/wiki/Air-launched_cruise_missile" title="Air-launched cruise missile">ALCM</a>).</li></ul>
<p>The UK's share of this total was 160 missiles, 96 based at Greenham Common with four spares, and 64 at <a href="/wiki/RAF_Molesworth" title="RAF Molesworth">RAF Molesworth</a>.  When in June 1980 it was announced that RAF Greenham Common was to become the first site for cruise missiles, there was an outcry from the <a href="/wiki/Campaign_for_Nuclear_Disarmament" title="Campaign for Nuclear Disarmament">Campaign for Nuclear Disarmament</a>. Once more a massive new construction was undertaken as the GLCM Alert and Maintenance Area (GAMA) site was built in the southwest corner of the base. GAMA was a maximum security QRA (Quick Reaction Alert) area with 6 large above ground shelters in which fully operational cruise missiles were stored.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Greenham_Common_Bunkers_-_geograph.org.uk_-_325266.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="215" data-file-width="640" height="101" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Greenham_Common_Bunkers_-_geograph.org.uk_-_325266.jpg/300px-Greenham_Common_Bunkers_-_geograph.org.uk_-_325266.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Greenham_Common_Bunkers_-_geograph.org.uk_-_325266.jpg/450px-Greenham_Common_Bunkers_-_geograph.org.uk_-_325266.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Greenham_Common_Bunkers_-_geograph.org.uk_-_325266.jpg/600px-Greenham_Common_Bunkers_-_geograph.org.uk_-_325266.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Greenham_Common_Bunkers_-_geograph.org.uk_-_325266.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The GAMA site</div></div></div>
<p>These shelters were specially designed and constructed to protect the GLCMs and crews against nuclear and conventional strikes. They were about 10 m high, with a reinforced 2 m thick concrete ceiling. Below was a massive <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a> plate, 3 m of sand and a reinforced concrete plate. The shelters were completely covered with tons of clay.
Each shelter was equipped with three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydraulic" title="Hydraulic">hydraulic</a> nuclear blast proof doors at both ends to assure a quick entry or exit.
They were designed to withstand the blast of an air-bursting nuclear explosion above the base or a direct hit from a 2,500 lb (1,100 kg) conventional bomb.
</p><p>Each shelter contained 2 LCC Launch Control Centers and 4 TEL <a href="/wiki/Transporter_erector_launcher" title="Transporter erector launcher">transporter erector launchers</a>. Units were mobile and, once deployed, would travel from the base in convoys to secret preset dispersal sites. This would happen within minutes of the alert being given; movement was via local roads through the surrounding villages.
</p><p>The first squadron of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/501st_Tactical_Missile_Wing" title="501st Tactical Missile Wing">501st Tactical Missile Wing</a> received its weapons in November 1983; they were flown onto the base by <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_C-5_Galaxy" title="Lockheed C-5 Galaxy">Lockheed C-5 Galaxys</a>.
</p><p>A series of meetings held during August and September 1986 culminated in a summit between United States President <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> and the General Secretary of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CPSU" title="CPSU">CPSU</a> <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Gorbachev" title="Mikhail Gorbachev">Mikhail Gorbachev</a> in <a href="/wiki/Reykjav%C3%ADk" title="Reykjavík">Reykjavík</a>, Iceland, on 11 October 1986. To the surprise of both men's advisers, the two agreed in principle to removing INF systems from Europe and to equal global limits of 100 INF missile warheads.
</p><p>The United States and the Soviet Union signed the <a href="/wiki/Intermediate-Range_Nuclear_Forces_Treaty" title="Intermediate-Range Nuclear Forces Treaty">Intermediate-Range Nuclear Forces Treaty</a> in 1987, which led to the removal of all nuclear missiles from the base.  The last GLCMs at RAF Greenham Common were removed in March 1991, and the 501st TMW inactivated on 4 June 1991.
</p><p>The base's last operational commander, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Group_Captain" title="Group Captain">Group Captain</a> <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Brookes" title="Andrew Brookes">Andrew Brookes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RAF" title="RAF">RAF</a> went on to become an aviation author and defence academic.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenham_Common_Peace_Camp">Greenham Common Peace Camp</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RAF_Greenham_Common&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Greenham Common Peace Camp">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Greenham_Common_Women%27s_Peace_Camp" title="Greenham Common Women's Peace Camp">Greenham Common Women's Peace Camp</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Embracing_the_base,_Greenham_Common_December_1982_-_geograph.org.uk_-_759090.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="631" data-file-width="522" height="205" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Embracing_the_base%2C_Greenham_Common_December_1982_-_geograph.org.uk_-_759090.jpg/170px-Embracing_the_base%2C_Greenham_Common_December_1982_-_geograph.org.uk_-_759090.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Embracing_the_base%2C_Greenham_Common_December_1982_-_geograph.org.uk_-_759090.jpg/255px-Embracing_the_base%2C_Greenham_Common_December_1982_-_geograph.org.uk_-_759090.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Embracing_the_base%2C_Greenham_Common_December_1982_-_geograph.org.uk_-_759090.jpg/340px-Embracing_the_base%2C_Greenham_Common_December_1982_-_geograph.org.uk_-_759090.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Embracing_the_base,_Greenham_Common_December_1982_-_geograph.org.uk_-_759090.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>On 12 December 1982, 30,000 women held hands around the 6 miles (9.7 km) perimeter of the base, in protest against the decision to site American cruise missiles there</div></div></div>
<p>From 1981 "women's <a href="/wiki/Peace_camp" title="Peace camp">peace camps</a>" were established in protest at the deployment of cruise missiles.  They came to be known as "The Greenham Women" or "peace women", and their 19-year protest drew worldwide media and public attention, often due to the peace women cutting through the fences of the base.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="USAF_departure_and_closure">USAF departure and closure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RAF_Greenham_Common&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: USAF departure and closure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Entrance_to_the_New_Greenham_Park_-_geograph.org.uk_-_29255.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="640" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Entrance_to_the_New_Greenham_Park_-_geograph.org.uk_-_29255.jpg/220px-Entrance_to_the_New_Greenham_Park_-_geograph.org.uk_-_29255.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Entrance_to_the_New_Greenham_Park_-_geograph.org.uk_-_29255.jpg/330px-Entrance_to_the_New_Greenham_Park_-_geograph.org.uk_-_29255.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Entrance_to_the_New_Greenham_Park_-_geograph.org.uk_-_29255.jpg/440px-Entrance_to_the_New_Greenham_Park_-_geograph.org.uk_-_29255.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Entrance_to_the_New_Greenham_Park_-_geograph.org.uk_-_29255.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Entrance to the New Greenham Park</div></div></div>
<p>On 11 September 1992, the USAF returned RAF Greenham Common to the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Defence (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Defence</a>.  On 9 February 1993 the Greenham Common air base was declared surplus to requirements by the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Defence" title="Secretary of State for Defence">Secretary of State for Defence</a> and the facility was closed and put up for sale.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RAF_Greenham_Common.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="1536" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/RAF_Greenham_Common.jpg/220px-RAF_Greenham_Common.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/RAF_Greenham_Common.jpg/330px-RAF_Greenham_Common.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/RAF_Greenham_Common.jpg/440px-RAF_Greenham_Common.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RAF_Greenham_Common.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Aerial view of the former runway at RAF Greenham Common, viewed east-to-west, August 2014.</div></div></div>
<p>With the departure of the cruise missiles and the subsequent closure of the base by the MOD in 1993, the peace camps remained at the site until September 2000 to ensure the base was closed and the land returned to the public. However, the protesters no longer attracted the attention of the media as they did during the 1980s when there were some 40 camps spread around the base perimeter.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alleged_nuclear_accident">Alleged nuclear accident</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RAF_Greenham_Common&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Alleged nuclear accident">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 28 February 1958, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-47" title="B-47">B-47</a>E of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/310th_Bomb_Wing" title="310th Bomb Wing">310th Bomb Wing</a> developed problems shortly after takeoff and jettisoned its two 1,700 gallon external fuel tanks. They missed their designated safe impact area, and one hit a hangar whilst the other struck the ground 65 feet (20 m) behind a parked B-47E. The parked plane, which was fuelled, had a pilot on board, and was carrying a 1.1 megaton (4.6 PJ) <a href="/wiki/B28_nuclear_bomb" title="B28 nuclear bomb">B28 nuclear bomb</a>, was engulfed by flames. The <a href="/wiki/Conflagration" title="Conflagration">conflagration</a> took sixteen hours and over a million gallons of water to extinguish, partly because of the magnesium alloys used in the aircraft. Although two men were killed and eight injured, the US and UK governments kept the accident secret: as late as 1985, the British government claimed that a taxiing aircraft had struck a parked one and that no fire was involved.
</p><p>Two scientists, F. H. Cripps and A. Stimson, who both worked for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_Weapons_Research_Establishment" title="Atomic Weapons Research Establishment">Atomic Weapons Research Establishment</a> at <a href="/wiki/Aldermaston" title="Aldermaston">Aldermaston</a>, alleged in a secret 1961 report, released by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CND" title="CND">CND</a> in 1996, that the fire detonated the high explosives in the nuclear weapon, that plutonium and uranium oxides were spread over a wide area (foliage up to 8 mi (13 km) away was contaminated with <a href="/wiki/Uranium-235" title="Uranium-235">uranium-235</a>) and that they had discovered high concentrations of radioactive contamination around the air base.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, a radiological survey commissioned in 1997 by <a href="/wiki/West_Berkshire" title="West Berkshire">Newbury District Council</a> and <a href="/wiki/Basingstoke_and_Deane" title="Basingstoke and Deane">Basingstoke and Deane</a> found no evidence of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_accident" title="Nuclear accident">nuclear accident</a> at Greenham Common, suggesting that Cripps and Stimson's claims were false. The seven-month long survey was carried out by the Geosciences Advisory Unit of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southampton_University" title="Southampton University">Southampton University</a> and combined a <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopter</a>-mounted <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">gamma ray</a> detector survey with a ground-based survey. The team analysed nearly 600 samples taken from soil, lake sediment, borehole water, house dust, runway tarmac and concrete, looking for uranium and plutonium <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a>. No evidence of an accident involving nuclear weapons damage was found at the former air force base although the ground survey detected some low-level uranium contamination around the Atomic Weapons Establishment at Aldermaston thought to be derived from that facility, and the helicopter survey found some anomalies around <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harwell_Laboratory" title="Harwell Laboratory">Harwell Laboratory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=RAF_Greenham_Common&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
