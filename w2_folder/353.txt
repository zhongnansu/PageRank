<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Green growth</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Green growth</b> is a term to describe a path of economic growth that uses natural resources in a sustainable manner. It is used globally to provide an alternative concept to typical industrial economic growth. See also <a href="/wiki/Green_economy" title="Green economy">green economy</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_growth_as_a_policy_strategy">Green growth as a policy strategy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Green growth as a policy strategy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term green growth has been used to describe national or international strategies.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organizational_efforts_on_green_growth">Organizational efforts on green growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Organizational efforts on green growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNESCAP" title="UNESCAP">UNESCAP</a></b>: In 2012, the United Nations Economic and Social Commission for Asia and the Pacific released the <a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20130803062252/http://www.unescap.org/esd/environment/lcgg/" rel="nofollow">Low Carbon Green Growth Roadmap for Asia and the Pacific</a> to explore the opportunities that a low carbon green growth path offers to the region. The roadmap articulates five tracks on which to drive the economic system change necessary to pursue low carbon green growth as a new economic development path.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/OECD" title="OECD">OECD</a></b>: In 2011 the OECD published a strategy towards green growth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNEP" title="UNEP">UNEP</a></b>: In 2008, the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) led the Green Economy Initiative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a></b>: In 2012, the World Bank published its report "Inclusive Green Growth: The Pathway to Sustainable Development".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/International_Chamber_of_Commerce" title="International Chamber of Commerce">International Chamber of Commerce</a> (ICC)</b>: In 2010, ICC launched the unique global business Task Force on Green Economy resulting in the <a class="external text" href="http://www.iccwbo.org/Products-and-Services/Trade-facilitation/Green-Economy-Roadmap/" rel="nofollow">Green Economy Roadmap</a>, a guide for business, policymakers and society published in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organizations_devoted_to_green_growth">Organizations devoted to green growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Organizations devoted to green growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Global_Green_Growth_Institute" title="Global Green Growth Institute">Global Green Growth Institute</a></b>: Founded in 2010 by Korean President Lee Myung-bak and later GGGI was first launched as a <a href="/wiki/Think_tank" title="Think tank">think tank</a> in 2010 by Korean <a href="/wiki/Lee_Myung-bak" title="Lee Myung-bak">President Lee Myung-bak</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Org_Overview_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Org_Overview-7">[7]</a></sup> and was later converted into an international treaty-based organization in 2012 at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rio%2B20" title="Rio+20">Rio+20</a> Summit in Brazil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Green Growth Knowledge Platform</b>: In January 2012, the <a href="/wiki/Global_Green_Growth_Institute" title="Global Green Growth Institute">Global Green Growth Institute</a> (GGGI), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organisation_for_Economic_Co-operation_and_Development" title="Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development">Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development</a> (OECD), <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Environment_Programme" title="United Nations Environment Programme">United Nations Environment Programme</a> (UNEP), and <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> signed a Memorandum of Understanding to formally launch the Green Growth Knowledge Platform (GGKP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The GGKP's mission is to enhance and expand efforts to identify and address major knowledge gaps in green growth theory and practice, and to help countries design and implement policies to move towards a green economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/KAIST_Graduate_School_of_Green_Growth" title="KAIST Graduate School of Green Growth">KAIST Graduate School of Green Growth</a></b>: Graduate School of Green Growth (GSGG)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> at Korea Advanced Institute of Science and Technology (KAIST) provides world-class education and research on green business, sustainable finance, and policy through Green Business and Policy Program(MS), Green Business(MS/Ph.D.), Green MBA, and Green Business and Green Finance Track programs. GSGG was ranked 4th in 'Better World MBA' by Corporate Knights in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Established in 2013, GSGG nurtures professionals in green growth to cope with and adapt to climate change.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_green_growth_efforts">National green growth efforts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: National green growth efforts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a></b>: In South Korea, green growth has become the national strategy model. Korean President <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lee_Myung_Bak" title="Lee Myung Bak">Lee Myung Bak</a> has embraced a vision of 'Low Carbon, Green Growth' as the core of the country's new vision on the 60th anniversary of the founding of the nation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a></b>: In the United States, President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> has taken several steps toward green growth. President Obama believes that by investing in the future <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> production will not only reduce the dependency on foreign energy sources but will also create jobs and 'clean-energy economy'. President Obama has a goal of installing 10 gigawatts of <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable</a> projects by 2020, doubling the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wind_energy" title="Wind energy">wind</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar</a> energy production by 2025, and to develop such policies, which will help to shape the nation's green economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li></ul>
