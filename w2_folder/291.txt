<p>The <b>Green League</b> (<b>VIHR</b>, <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a>: <i lang="fi">Vihreä liitto</i>, <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>: <i lang="sv">Gröna förbundet</i>), shortened to <b>the Greens</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">green</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_political_parties_in_Finland" title="List of political parties in Finland">political party in Finland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nordsieck_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nordsieck-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Annesley2013_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Annesley2013-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Caramani2004_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Caramani2004-4">[4]</a></sup> The Green League is among the largest political parties in <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>. The Greens hold fifteen seats in the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Finland" title="Parliament of Finland">Finnish Parliament</a> and one in the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a>. The party is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Global_Greens" title="Global Greens">Global Greens</a> and the <a href="/wiki/European_Green_Party" title="European Green Party">European Green Party</a>, while its MEP, <a href="/wiki/Heidi_Hautala" title="Heidi Hautala">Heidi Hautala</a>, sits with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Greens%E2%80%93European_Free_Alliance" title="The Greens–European Free Alliance">The Greens–European Free Alliance</a> in the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a>. Originally split on whether Finland should join the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, the Green League is <a href="/wiki/Pro-Europeanism" title="Pro-Europeanism">pro-European</a> and was the first Finnish party in favor of the <a href="/wiki/Federalisation_of_the_European_Union" title="Federalisation of the European Union">federalisation of the European Union</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Founded in 1987, the party absorbed a number of <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">green</a> organisations and their members, including four MPs elected <a href="/wiki/Finnish_parliamentary_election,_1987" title="Finnish parliamentary election, 1987">in 1987</a>. The party won ten seats in the <a href="/wiki/Finnish_parliamentary_election,_1991" title="Finnish parliamentary election, 1991">1991 election</a>. Despite falling to nine seats <a href="/wiki/Finnish_parliamentary_election,_1995" title="Finnish parliamentary election, 1995">in 1995 election</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pekka_Haavisto" title="Pekka Haavisto">Pekka Haavisto</a> joined <a href="/wiki/Paavo_Lipponen" title="Paavo Lipponen">Paavo Lipponen</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paavo_Lipponen%27s_first_cabinet" title="Paavo Lipponen's first cabinet">first cabinet</a>, which was composed of a rainbow coalition. This move made the Green League the first <a href="/wiki/Green_party" title="Green party">green party</a> in a national cabinet. The party remained in government until 2002, when it resigned from the cabinet in opposition to <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a>. <a href="/wiki/Finnish_parliamentary_election,_2007" title="Finnish parliamentary election, 2007">In 2007</a>, the party peaked at 15 seats, and joined the <a href="/wiki/Vanhanen_II_Cabinet" title="Vanhanen II Cabinet">centre-right-led</a> government. At the <a href="/wiki/Finnish_parliamentary_election,_2011" title="Finnish parliamentary election, 2011">2011 election</a>, the party fell to ten seats. Nevertheless, the Greens were invited to join the six-party <a href="/wiki/Katainen_Cabinet" title="Katainen Cabinet">Katainen Cabinet</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Finnish_parliamentary_election,_2015" title="Finnish parliamentary election, 2015">2015 parliamentary election</a>, the party returned to its previous best of 15 seats, and at 8.53%, achieved their best ever share of the overall vote. 
</p><p>Since June 2017, the party’s leader and chairman has been <a href="/wiki/Touko_Aalto" title="Touko Aalto">Touko Aalto</a>. The party is in opposition, and has provided vast criticism regarding the actions of the incumbent right-wing <a href="/wiki/Sipil%C3%A4_Cabinet" title="Sipilä Cabinet">Sipilä Cabinet</a> such as financial support for economically well-off companies, <a href="/wiki/Fortum" title="Fortum">Fortum's</a> purchase of <a href="/wiki/Uniper" title="Uniper">Uniper</a>, and the expedited process of <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Finland" title="Constitution of Finland">constitution</a>-changing surveillance laws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding">Founding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Founding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Green League was founded 28 February 1987, and was registered as a political party the next year. Political activity had begun already in the early 1980s, when <a href="/wiki/Environmentalist" title="Environmentalist">environmental activists</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feminist" title="Feminist">feminists</a>, disillusioned young politicians from the marginalized <a href="/wiki/Liberals_(Finland)" title="Liberals (Finland)">Liberal People's Party</a> and other active groups began to campaign on green issues in Finland. In 1995, it was the first <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> green party to be part of a state-level <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_(government)" title="Cabinet (government)">cabinet</a>.
</p><p>The party was founded as a popular movement, which explains its name's descriptor <i>liitto</i>, "league". Initially, there was much resistance within the movement against the founding of a political party, motivated by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Michels" title="Robert Michels">Robert Michels</a>' <a href="/wiki/Iron_law_of_oligarchy" title="Iron law of oligarchy">iron law of oligarchy</a>, which claims that movements inevitably degenerate into <a href="/wiki/Oligarchy" title="Oligarchy">oligarchies</a> when they create a formal organization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mickelsson_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mickelsson-12">[12]</a></sup> The party still especially stresses openness and democratic decision-making. Even though <i>liitto</i> has been dropped from the party's website and advertisements, the word still remains in the official name.
</p>
<h3><span id="Early_activities_.281983-1994.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_activities_(1983-1994)">Early activities (1983-1994)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early activities (1983-1994)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first two parliamentary representatives were elected even before the registration, in the <a href="/wiki/Finnish_parliamentary_election,_1983" title="Finnish parliamentary election, 1983">1983 parliamentary election</a>. These were the first independent representatives in the Finnish Parliament. In <a href="/wiki/Finnish_parliamentary_election,_1987" title="Finnish parliamentary election, 1987">1987</a> the number of seats rose to four, and in <a href="/wiki/Finnish_parliamentary_election,_1991" title="Finnish parliamentary election, 1991">1991</a> to ten.
</p><p>About half of the party’s members were against Finland joining the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> in 1994. Later, polls showed that most Greens were anti-<a href="/wiki/Eurozone" title="Eurozone">Eurozone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-euroopanunionissa_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-euroopanunionissa-13">[13]</a></sup> The party heads declined to fight against euro adoption.
</p>
<h3><span id="As_part_of_the_Lipponen_Cabinets_.281995-2003.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="As_part_of_the_Lipponen_Cabinets_(1995-2003)">As part of the Lipponen Cabinets (1995-2003)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: As part of the Lipponen Cabinets (1995-2003)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Finnish_parliamentary_election,_1995" title="Finnish parliamentary election, 1995">1995 election</a>, the Green League received a total of nine seats out of 200. The party joined the coalition cabinet led by the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Finland" title="Social Democratic Party of Finland">Social Democrats</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pekka_Haavisto" title="Pekka Haavisto">Pekka Haavisto</a> became the <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_the_Environment_(Finland)" title="Minister of the Environment (Finland)">Minister of the Environment</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> thus becoming the first green minister in Europe.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The Green League received 7.3% of the vote, and gained two additional seats in the <a href="/wiki/Finnish_parliamentary_election,_1999" title="Finnish parliamentary election, 1999">1999 election</a>, raising the total to 11. The Greens continued in <a href="/wiki/Lipponen_II_Cabinet" title="Lipponen II Cabinet">the next coalition cabinet</a>, but resigned in protest on 26 May 2002, after the cabinet's decision to allow the construction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olkiluoto_nuclear_power_plant" title="Olkiluoto nuclear power plant">a new nuclear plant</a> was accepted by Parliament.  
</p>
<h3><span id="Growth_to_mainstream_appeal_.282003-present.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth_to_mainstream_appeal_(2003-present)">Growth to mainstream appeal (2003-present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Growth to mainstream appeal (2003-present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2003, the Green League received 8.0% of the vote, receiving a total of 14 seats. They increased their seats to 15 in the <a href="/wiki/Finnish_parliamentary_election,_2007" title="Finnish parliamentary election, 2007">2007 election</a>, while receiving 8.5% of the vote. In the <a href="/wiki/Finnish_parliamentary_election,_2011" title="Finnish parliamentary election, 2011">2011 election</a>, the party lost five seats. 
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_2009_(Finland)" title="European Parliament election, 2009 (Finland)">2009 European Parliament elections</a>, the Greens gained two of the thirteen Finnish seats in the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a>, which were occupied by <a href="/wiki/Satu_Hassi" title="Satu Hassi">Satu Hassi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heidi_Hautala" title="Heidi Hautala">Heidi Hautala</a>.
</p><p>At the municipal level, the Greens are an important player in the largest cities of Finland. In the municipal election of 2008 the Greens received 8.9% of the vote; the vote share was considerably higher in <a href="/wiki/Helsinki" title="Helsinki">Helsinki</a>, where the Greens became the second largest party with 23.2% of the vote.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In several other cities, the Greens achieved the position of the third largest party. Its weak spot is the rural countryside, particularly in those municipalities experiencing strong outward migration.
</p><p>A 2012 study indicated that the Greens are the most popular party among journalists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2017, the party's then-chairperson <a href="/wiki/Ville_Niinist%C3%B6" title="Ville Niinistö">Ville Niinistö</a> announced that he would step down as chair of the Green League in June 2017. The statement caused a <a href="/wiki/Green_League_leadership_election,_2017" title="Green League leadership election, 2017">leadership election</a>. There were six candidates running for party chair, of whom MP <a href="/wiki/Touko_Aalto" title="Touko Aalto">Touko Aalto</a> won the election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ideology_and_policies">Ideology and policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ideology and policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vihreiden_vaaliteltta_Isolla_Roballa.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Vihreiden_vaaliteltta_Isolla_Roballa.jpg/220px-Vihreiden_vaaliteltta_Isolla_Roballa.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Vihreiden_vaaliteltta_Isolla_Roballa.jpg/330px-Vihreiden_vaaliteltta_Isolla_Roballa.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Vihreiden_vaaliteltta_Isolla_Roballa.jpg/440px-Vihreiden_vaaliteltta_Isolla_Roballa.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vihreiden_vaaliteltta_Isolla_Roballa.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An election canvassing tent for the Greens on Iso Roobertinkatu in Helsinki.</div></div></div>
<p>The Green League is no longer a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protest_party" title="Protest party">protest party</a>, nor an <a href="/wiki/Alternative_movement" title="Alternative movement">alternative movement</a>. Some Green candidates reject classifying the party as either left-wing or right-wing. Economic opinions of the members range between left and right.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mickelsson_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mickelsson-12">[12]</a></sup> However, on average, members of party place their party left of the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Finland" title="Social Democratic Party of Finland">Social Democrats</a> and right of the <a href="/wiki/Left_Alliance_(Finland)" title="Left Alliance (Finland)">Left Alliance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In comparison with some other European green parties, Finland's Greens are noticeably more pro-European and centrist.
</p><p>In the chamber of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finnish_Parliament" title="Finnish Parliament">Parliament</a> and assembly rooms of local councils, Green representatives sit between the <a href="/wiki/Finns_Party" title="Finns Party">Finns</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Finland" title="Social Democratic Party of Finland">Social Democrats</a>.
</p><p>The party is one of the strongest proponents for <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage_in_Finland" title="Same-sex marriage in Finland">same-sex marriage</a>. The party is also distinct in its opposition against <a href="/wiki/Conscription_in_Finland" title="Conscription in Finland">universal male conscription</a> and wants to opt for a gender-neutral, selective version. The eventual goal of the Greens is voluntary military service.
</p><p>In 2015, the party included <a href="/wiki/Basic_income" title="Basic income">universal basic income</a> as a proposal in their platform.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Presidential_elections">Presidential elections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_League&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Presidential elections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Parliamentarian and then-former <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_European_Parliament" title="Member of the European Parliament">MEP</a> <a href="/wiki/Heidi_Hautala" title="Heidi Hautala">Heidi Hautala</a> was a candidate in the presidential elections in 2000 and 2006, taking approximately a 3.5% share of votes in the first round in each. <a href="/wiki/Pekka_Haavisto" title="Pekka Haavisto">Pekka Haavisto</a> was the first Green candidate in the <a href="/wiki/Finnish_presidential_election,_2012" title="Finnish presidential election, 2012">2012 election</a> to enter the second round. Haavisto got an 18.8% share of votes in the first round, and lost to centre-right <a href="/wiki/Sauli_Niinist%C3%B6" title="Sauli Niinistö">Sauli Niinistö</a> in the second round held on 5 February.
