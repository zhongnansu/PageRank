<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Environmental impact of reservoirs</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wachusett-dam.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Wachusett-dam.jpg/220px-Wachusett-dam.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Wachusett-dam.jpg/330px-Wachusett-dam.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Wachusett-dam.jpg/440px-Wachusett-dam.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wachusett-dam.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Wachusett Dam in <a href="/wiki/Clinton,_Massachusetts" title="Clinton, Massachusetts">Clinton, Massachusetts</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>environmental impact of reservoirs</b> comes under ever-increasing scrutiny as the global demand for water and energy increases and the number and size of reservoirs increases.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Dam" title="Dam">Dams</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reservoir" title="Reservoir">reservoirs</a> can be used to supply <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a>, generate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroelectric_power" title="Hydroelectric power">hydroelectric power</a>, increase the water supply for <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>, provide recreational opportunities, and <a href="/wiki/Flood_control" title="Flood control">flood control</a>.  In 1960 the construction of <a href="/wiki/Llyn_Celyn" title="Llyn Celyn">Llyn Celyn</a> and the flooding of <a href="/wiki/Capel_Celyn" title="Capel Celyn">Capel Celyn</a> provoked political uproar which continues to this day. More recently, the construction of <a href="/wiki/Three_Gorges_Dam" title="Three Gorges Dam">Three Gorges Dam</a> and other similar projects throughout <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a> have generated considerable environmental and political debate.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Upstream_impacts">Upstream impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_reservoirs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Upstream impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lake_Nasser.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2008" data-file-width="3032" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Lake_Nasser.jpg/220px-Lake_Nasser.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Lake_Nasser.jpg/330px-Lake_Nasser.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Lake_Nasser.jpg/440px-Lake_Nasser.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lake_Nasser.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Lake_Nasser" title="Lake Nasser">Lake Nasser</a> behind the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aswan_dam" title="Aswan dam">Aswan dam</a>, Egypt, 5250 km², displaced 60,000 people<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thayer_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thayer-1">[1]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fragmentation_of_river_ecosystems">Fragmentation of river ecosystems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_reservoirs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fragmentation of river ecosystems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A dam also acts as a barrier between the upstream and downstream movement of migratory river animals, such as <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trout" title="Trout">trout</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mann_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mann-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some communities have also begun the practice of transporting migratory fish upstream to spawn via a barge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mann_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mann-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reservoir_sedimentation">Reservoir sedimentation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_reservoirs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reservoir sedimentation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rivers carry sediment down their riverbeds, allowing for the formation of depositional features such as <a href="/wiki/River_delta" title="River delta">river deltas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alluvial_fan" title="Alluvial fan">alluvial fans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Braided_river" title="Braided river">braided rivers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxbow_lake" title="Oxbow lake">oxbow lakes</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Levees" title="Levees">levees</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shore" title="Shore">coastal shores</a>. The construction of a dam blocks the flow of sediment downstream, leading to downstream erosion of these sedimentary depositional environments, and increased sediment build-up in the reservoir. While the rate of sedimentation varies for each dam and each river, eventually all reservoirs develop a reduced water-storage capacity due to the exchange of "live storage" space for sediment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Silenced_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Silenced-3">[3]</a></sup> Diminished storage capacity results in decreased ability to produce hydroelectric power, reduced availability of water for irrigation, and if left unaddressed, may ultimately result in the expiration of the dam and river.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Handbook_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Handbook-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_below_dam">Impact below dam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_reservoirs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Impact below dam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Riverline_and_coastal_erosion">Riverline and coastal erosion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_reservoirs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Riverline and coastal erosion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As all dams result in reduced sediment load downstream, a dammed river is greatly demanding for sediment as it will not have enough sediment. This is because the rate of deposition of sediment is greatly reduced since there is less to deposit but the rate of erosion remains nearly constant, the water flow erodes the river shores and riverbed, threatening shoreline ecosystems, deepening the riverbed, and narrowing the river over time. This leads to a compromised water table, reduced water levels, homogenization of the river flow and thus reduced ecosystem variability, reduced support for wildlife, and reduced amount of sediment reaching coastal plains and deltas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Handbook_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Handbook-4">[4]</a></sup> This prompts coastal erosion, as beaches are unable to replenish what waves erode without the sediment deposition of supporting river systems. Downstream channel erosion of dammed rivers is related to the morphology of the riverbed, which is different from directly studying the amounts of sedimentation because it is subject to specific long term conditions for each river system. For example, the eroded channel could create a lower water table level in the affected area, impacting bottomland crops such as <a href="/wiki/Alfalfa" title="Alfalfa">alfalfa</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zea_mays" title="Zea mays">corn</a>, and resulting in a smaller supply.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Egineering_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Egineering-5">[5]</a></sup>
In the case of the Three Gorges Dam in China the changes described above now appears to have arrived at a new balance of erosion and sedimentation over a 10-year period in the lower reaches of the river. The impacts on the tidal region have also been linked to the upstream effects of the dam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_temperature">Water temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_reservoirs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Water temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The water of a deep reservoir in temperate climates typically stratifies with a large volume of cold, oxygen poor water in the hypolimnion. Analysis of temperature profiles from 11 large dams in the Murray Darling Basin (Australia) indicated differences between surface water and bottom water temperatures up to 16.7 degrees Celsius.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> If this water is released to maintain river flow, it can cause adverse impacts on the downstream ecosystem including fish populations. Under worse case conditions (such as when the reservoir is full or near full), the stored water is strongly stratified and large volumes of water are being released to the downstream river channel via bottom level outlets, depressed temperatures can be detected 250 - 350 kilometres downstream.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The operators of Burrendong Dam on the Macquarie River (eastern Australia) are attempting to address thermal suppression by hanging a geotextile curtain around the existing outlet tower to force the selective release of surface water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_ecosystems_destroyed_by_agriculture">Natural ecosystems destroyed by agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_reservoirs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Natural ecosystems destroyed by agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many dams are built for irrigation and although there is an existing dry ecosystem downstream, it is deliberately destroyed in favor of irrigated farming. After the <a href="/wiki/Aswan_Dam" title="Aswan Dam">Aswan Dam</a> was constructed in Egypt it protected Egypt from the droughts in 1972–73 and 1983–87 that devastated East and West Africa. The dam allowed Egypt to reclaim about 840,000 hectares in the <a href="/wiki/Nile_Delta" title="Nile Delta">Nile Delta</a> and along the Nile Valley, increasing <a href="/wiki/Aswan_Dam#Irrigation_scheme" title="Aswan Dam">the country's irrigated area</a> by a third. The increase was brought about both by irrigating what used to be desert and by bringing under cultivation 385,000 hectares that were natural flood retention basins. About half a million families were settled on these new lands.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_flood-dependent_ecology_and_agriculture">Effects on flood-dependent ecology and agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_reservoirs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Effects on flood-dependent ecology and agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In many low lying developing countries the <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savanna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forest</a> ecology adjacent to <a href="/wiki/Floodplain" title="Floodplain">floodplains</a> and <a href="/wiki/River_delta" title="River delta">river deltas</a> are irrigated by wet season annual floods. Farmers annually plant flood recession crops, where the land is cultivated after floods recede to take advantage of the moist soil. Dams generally discourage this cultivation and prevent annual flooding, creating a dryer downstream ecology while providing a constant water supply for irrigation.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BaluchNomads.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="825" data-file-width="1714" height="106" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/BaluchNomads.jpg/220px-BaluchNomads.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/BaluchNomads.jpg/330px-BaluchNomads.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/BaluchNomads.jpg/440px-BaluchNomads.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BaluchNomads.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Water becomes scarce for nomadic pastoralist in Baluchistan due to new dam developments for irrigation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p><i>Case studies</i>
</p>
<ul><li>The Lake Manatali reservoir formed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manantali_dam" title="Manantali dam">Manantali dam</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mali" title="Mali">Mali</a>,  West Africa intersects the migration routes of nomadic <a href="/wiki/Pastoralism" title="Pastoralism">pastoralists</a> and withholds water from the downstream savanna. The absence of the seasonal flood cycle causes <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_irrigation#Lost_land_use_opportunities" title="Environmental impact of irrigation">depletion of grazing land</a>, and is also drying the forests on the floodplain downstream of the dam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li></ul>
<ul><li>After the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Kainji_Dam" title="Kainji Dam">Kainji Dam</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>, 50 to 70 percent of the downstream area of flood-recession cropping stopped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Potential_for_disaster">Potential for disaster</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_reservoirs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Potential for disaster">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dam_failure" title="Dam failure">Dam failure</a></div>
<p>Dams occasionally break causing catastrophic damage to communities downstream. Dams break due to engineering errors, attack or natural disaster. The <a href="/wiki/Banqiao_Dam" title="Banqiao Dam">greatest dam break disaster happened in China</a> killing 200,000 Chinese citizens. However, they <a href="/wiki/St._Francis_Dam" title="St. Francis Dam">have happened in California killing 600 people</a>, Germany during World War II and <a href="/wiki/Dam_failure#List_of_major_dam_failures" title="Dam failure">other countries</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flood_control">Flood control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_reservoirs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Flood control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The controversial <a href="/wiki/Three_Gorges_Dam" title="Three Gorges Dam">Three Gorges Dam</a> in China is able to store 22 cubic kilometres of floodwaters on the Yangtze River. The <a href="/wiki/1954_Yangtze_River_floods" title="1954 Yangtze River floods">1954 Yangtze River floods</a> killed 33,000 people and displaced 18 million people from their homes. In <a href="/wiki/1998_China_floods" title="1998 China floods">1998 a flood</a> killed 4000 people and 180 million people were affected. The flooding of the reservoir caused over a million people to relocate, then a flood in August 2009 was completely captured by the new reservoir, protecting hundreds of millions of people downstream.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_beyond_the_reservoir">Effects beyond the reservoir</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_reservoirs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Effects beyond the reservoir">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_humans">Effects on humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_reservoirs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Effects on humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Diseases</b><br/>
Whilst reservoirs are helpful to humans, they can also be harmful as well. One negative effect is  that the reservoirs can become breeding grounds for disease vectors. This holds true especially in tropical areas where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mosquitoes" title="Mosquitoes">mosquitoes</a> (which are <a href="/wiki/Vector_(epidemiology)" title="Vector (epidemiology)">vectors</a> for <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snails" title="Snails">snails</a> (which are vectors for <a href="/wiki/Schistosomiasis" title="Schistosomiasis">Schistosomiasis</a>) can take advantage of this slow flowing water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:10.34748W_13.10430N.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="702" data-file-width="1024" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/10.34748W_13.10430N.png/220px-10.34748W_13.10430N.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/10.34748W_13.10430N.png/330px-10.34748W_13.10430N.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/10.34748W_13.10430N.png/440px-10.34748W_13.10430N.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:10.34748W_13.10430N.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lake Manantali, 477 km², displaced 12,000 people.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Resettlement</b><br/>
Dams and the creation of reservoirs also require relocation of potentially large human populations if they are constructed close to residential areas. The record for the largest population relocated belongs to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three_Gorges_dam" title="Three Gorges dam">Three Gorges dam</a> built in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. Its reservoir submerged a large area of land, forcing over a million people to relocate. "Dam related relocation affects society in three ways: an economic disaster, human trauma, and social catastrophe", states Dr. Michael Cernea of the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> and Dr. Thayer Scudder, a professor at the <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">California Institute of Technology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thayer_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thayer-1">[1]</a></sup> 
As well, as resettlement of communities, care must also be taken not to irreparably damage sites of historical or cultural value. The Aswan Dam forced the movement of the Temple at Aswan to prevent its destruction by the flooding of the reservoir.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenhouse_gases">Greenhouse gases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_reservoirs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Greenhouse gases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tapaj%C3%B3s_hydroelectric_complex#Impact" title="Tapajós hydroelectric complex">Tapajós hydroelectric complex § Impact</a></div>
<p>Reservoirs may contribute to changes in the Earth's climate. Warm climate reservoirs generate <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> when the reservoirs  are stratified, in which the bottom layers are <a href="/wiki/Anoxic_waters" title="Anoxic waters">anoxic</a> (i.e. they lack oxygen), leading to degradation of <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> through anaerobic processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (August 2014)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup> At a dam in Brazil, where the flooded basin is wide and the biomass volume is high the methane produced results in a pollution potential 3.5 times more than an oil-fired power plant would be.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dirty_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dirty-16">[16]</a></sup> A theoretical study has indicated that globally hydroelectric reservoirs may emit 104 million metric tonnes of methane gas annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Methane gas is a <a href="/wiki/Global_warming#Greenhouse_gases" title="Global warming">significant contributor</a> to global climate change.
