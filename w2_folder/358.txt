<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Monadnock-Sunapee Greenway</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>Monadnock-Sunapee Greenway</b> (MSG) is a 50-mile (80 km) <a href="/wiki/Hiking" title="Hiking">hiking</a> trail that traverses the highlands of southern <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a> from <a href="/wiki/Mount_Monadnock" title="Mount Monadnock">Mount Monadnock</a> in <a href="/wiki/Jaffrey,_New_Hampshire" title="Jaffrey, New Hampshire">Jaffrey</a> to <a href="/wiki/Mount_Sunapee" title="Mount Sunapee">Mount Sunapee</a> in <a href="/wiki/Newbury,_New_Hampshire" title="Newbury, New Hampshire">Newbury</a>. Located approximately 27 miles (43 km) from the city of <a href="/wiki/Concord,_New_Hampshire" title="Concord, New Hampshire">Concord, New Hampshire</a>, the trail traverses a rural, heavily <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glaciated" title="Glaciated">glaciated</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphic</a> upland studded with lakes, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heath_(habitat)" title="Heath (habitat)">heath barrens</a>, the rocky summits of several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monadnock" title="Monadnock">monadnocks</a>, and dense woodland of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_hardwood_forest" title="Northern hardwood forest">northern hardwood forest</a> type. The trail is maintained primarily through the efforts of the Monadnock-Sunapee Greenway Trail Club.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-D-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSG_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-MSG-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Notable features along the route include the summit of Mount Monadnock; Eliza Adams Gorge; the classic New England town commons of <a href="/wiki/Nelson,_New_Hampshire" title="Nelson, New Hampshire">Nelson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Washington,_New_Hampshire" title="Washington, New Hampshire">Washington</a>; the New Hampshire Audubon Society's Nye Meadow Refuge; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Otter_Brook_(Ashuelot_River)" title="Otter Brook (Ashuelot River)">Otter Brook</a> and Andorra Forest; the high heath barrens of <a href="/wiki/Pitcher_Mountain" title="Pitcher Mountain">Pitcher Mountain</a>, Hubbard Hill, and Jackson Hill; <a href="/wiki/Lovewell_Mountain" title="Lovewell Mountain">Lovewell Mountain</a>; 8 miles (13 km) of the Sunapee Ridge; and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Solitude_(New_Hampshire)" title="Lake Solitude (New Hampshire)">Lake Solitude</a>, a mountaintop <a href="/wiki/Tarn_(lake)" title="Tarn (lake)">tarn</a> near the summit of Mount Sunapee. A number of lakes, reservoirs, ponds, and other wetlands are also located along the route.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSG_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-MSG-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trail_description">Trail description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monadnock-Sunapee_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Trail description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:142px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MountSunapeefromLake.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3008" height="93" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/MountSunapeefromLake.jpg/140px-MountSunapeefromLake.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/MountSunapeefromLake.jpg/210px-MountSunapeefromLake.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/MountSunapeefromLake.jpg/280px-MountSunapeefromLake.jpg 2x" width="140"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MountSunapeefromLake.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mount Sunapee</div></div></div><div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:142px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2007_11Nov_10_Mount_Monadnock_Summit_Rocky_Plateau.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="105" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/2007_11Nov_10_Mount_Monadnock_Summit_Rocky_Plateau.jpg/140px-2007_11Nov_10_Mount_Monadnock_Summit_Rocky_Plateau.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/2007_11Nov_10_Mount_Monadnock_Summit_Rocky_Plateau.jpg/210px-2007_11Nov_10_Mount_Monadnock_Summit_Rocky_Plateau.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/2007_11Nov_10_Mount_Monadnock_Summit_Rocky_Plateau.jpg/280px-2007_11Nov_10_Mount_Monadnock_Summit_Rocky_Plateau.jpg 2x" width="140"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2007_11Nov_10_Mount_Monadnock_Summit_Rocky_Plateau.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Summit of Mount Monadnock</div></div></div>
<p>The MSGT extends from the summit of Mount Monadnock to the summit of Mount Sunapee through <a href="/wiki/Cheshire_County,_New_Hampshire" title="Cheshire County, New Hampshire">Cheshire County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sullivan_County,_New_Hampshire" title="Sullivan County, New Hampshire">Sullivan County</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Merrimack_County,_New_Hampshire" title="Merrimack County, New Hampshire">Merrimack County</a>. The MSG passes through the following incorporated communities: <a href="/wiki/Jaffrey,_New_Hampshire" title="Jaffrey, New Hampshire">Jaffrey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dublin,_New_Hampshire" title="Dublin, New Hampshire">Dublin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harrisville,_New_Hampshire" title="Harrisville, New Hampshire">Harrisville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nelson,_New_Hampshire" title="Nelson, New Hampshire">Nelson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stoddard,_New_Hampshire" title="Stoddard, New Hampshire">Stoddard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington,_New_Hampshire" title="Washington, New Hampshire">Washington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goshen,_New_Hampshire" title="Goshen, New Hampshire">Goshen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Newbury,_New_Hampshire" title="Newbury, New Hampshire">Newbury, New Hampshire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-D-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mount Monadnock, the southern terminus of the MSG, is the most prominent peak of southeast New England. At 3,165 feet (965 m) high, it is 1,000 feet (300 m) higher than any mountain peak within 30 miles (48 km) and rises 2,000 feet (610 m) above the surrounding landscape. Its bare, rocky summit provides expansive views.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Eliza Adams Gorge is a scenic and rocky <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_hemlock" title="Eastern hemlock">eastern hemlock</a>-clad ravine located just below the dam spillway on Howe Reservoir. The Nye Meadow Refuge is an important <a href="/wiki/Great_blue_heron" title="Great blue heron">great blue heron</a> rookery managed by the <a class="external text" href="http://www.nhaudubon.org/" rel="nofollow">Audubon Society of New Hampshire</a>. Otter Brook and Andorra Forest, 15,000 acres (61 km<sup>2</sup>), collectively the largest conservation area in southwest New Hampshire, are cooperatively managed by <a href="/wiki/The_Nature_Conservancy" title="The Nature Conservancy">The Nature Conservancy</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Society_for_the_Protection_of_New_Hampshire_Forests" title="Society for the Protection of New Hampshire Forests">Society for the Protection of New Hampshire Forests</a> and include extensive tracts of <a href="/wiki/Northern_hardwood_forest" title="Northern hardwood forest">northern hardwood forest</a>, wetlands, and rare plant communities. Pitcher Mountain, 2,153 feet (656 m), Hubbard Hill, 1,846 feet (563 m), and Jackson Hill, 2,061 feet (628 m), offer wide views from upland heath barrens and <a href="/wiki/Blueberry" title="Blueberry">blueberry</a> fields.
</p><p>Lovewell Mountain, 2,473 feet (754 m), is a rugged monadnock contiguous with the southern part of Sunapee Ridge; several scenic outlooks on ledges provide views of the surrounding countryside. Mount Sunapee, 2,743 feet (836 m), is the high point of a mountainous 10-mile (16 km) ridgeline that spans four towns. The ridge offers scenic vistas from more than a dozen distinct peaks along its length and from the <a href="/wiki/Mount_Sunapee_Resort" title="Mount Sunapee Resort">Mount Sunapee ski area</a> on the summit. Other notable peaks along its length include White Ledges, 2,700 feet (820 m), which overlooks Lake Solitude; Lucia's Lookout, 2,500 feet (760 m); an unnamed southern summit, 2,230 feet (680 m); and Kittredge Hill, 2,140 feet (650 m). The commons of Washington and Nelson are small and bucolic classic New England town centers with little commercial development and with architecture dating back to the early 19th century. Washington, 1,507 feet (459 m), is the highest incorporated town in New Hampshire; it is 1-foot (0.30 m) higher than the next-highest town, the ski resort community of <a href="/wiki/Waterville_Valley,_New_Hampshire" title="Waterville Valley, New Hampshire">Waterville Valley</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSG_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-MSG-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Notable bodies of water along the trail route include Seavers Reservoir, Howe Reservoir, Chesham Pond, <a href="/wiki/Silver_Lake_(Harrisville,_New_Hampshire)" title="Silver Lake (Harrisville, New Hampshire)">Silver Lake</a>, Childs Bog, Center Pond, Halfmoon Pond, and the mountaintop Lake Solitude.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSG_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-MSG-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The MSG is primarily used for <a href="/wiki/Hiking" title="Hiking">hiking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Backpacking_(wilderness)" title="Backpacking (wilderness)">backpacking</a>, and in the winter, <a href="/wiki/Snowshoe" title="Snowshoe">snowshoeing</a>. Portions of the trail are suitable for, and are used for, <a href="/wiki/Backcountry_skiing" title="Backcountry skiing">backcountry skiing</a>. Site-specific activities enjoyed along the route include <a href="/wiki/Hunting" title="Hunting">hunting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boating" title="Boating">boating</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_swimming" title="Human swimming">swimming</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSG_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-MSG-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhp_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-nhp-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intersecting_trails">Intersecting trails</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monadnock-Sunapee_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Intersecting trails">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Andorra_Forest,_New_Hampshire.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="300" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Andorra_Forest%2C_New_Hampshire.jpg/120px-Andorra_Forest%2C_New_Hampshire.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Andorra_Forest%2C_New_Hampshire.jpg/180px-Andorra_Forest%2C_New_Hampshire.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Andorra_Forest%2C_New_Hampshire.jpg/240px-Andorra_Forest%2C_New_Hampshire.jpg 2x" width="120"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Andorra_Forest,_New_Hampshire.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cascade, Andorra Forest</div></div></div><p>The <a href="/wiki/Metacomet-Monadnock_Trail" title="Metacomet-Monadnock Trail">Metacomet-Monadnock Trail</a> in southern New Hampshire and central <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> continues south where the MSGT leaves off. This extends the overall hiking possibilities another 210 miles (340 km) via the Metacomet-Monadnock Trail and its logical extensions, the <a href="/wiki/Metacomet_Trail" title="Metacomet Trail">Metacomet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mattabesett_Trail" title="Mattabesett Trail">Mattabesett Trails</a> in <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMC2_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMC2-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> To the north, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sunapee-Ragged-Kearsarge_Greenway_Trail&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sunapee-Ragged-Kearsarge Greenway Trail (page does not exist)">Sunapee-Ragged-Kearsarge Greenway Trail</a> continues from the northern terminus of the MSG via a 75-mile (121 km) loop trail to <a href="/wiki/Mount_Kearsarge_(Merrimack_County,_New_Hampshire)" title="Mount Kearsarge (Merrimack County, New Hampshire)">Mount Kearsarge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ragged_Mountain_(New_Hampshire)" title="Ragged Mountain (New Hampshire)">Ragged Mountain</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bog_Mountain_(New_Hampshire)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bog Mountain (New Hampshire) (page does not exist)">Bog Mountain</a> and back to Mount Sunapee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monadnock-Sunapee_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The MSG was first suggested by Allen Chamberlain, president of the <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Mountain_Club" title="Appalachian Mountain Club">Appalachian Mountain Club</a>, in 1919 and was laid out in 1921 by the <a href="/wiki/Society_for_the_Protection_of_New_Hampshire_Forests" title="Society for the Protection of New Hampshire Forests">Society for the Protection of New Hampshire Forests</a> (SPNHF). But the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_England_Hurricane_of_1938" title="New England Hurricane of 1938">hurricane of 1938</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> "effectively ended the original trail; it was not maintained again." The trail was re-established after a study by the SPNHF in 1974.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSGTC_web_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-MSGTC_web-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hiking_the_trail">Hiking the trail</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monadnock-Sunapee_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hiking the trail">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:142px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LakeSolitudeHikers.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="105" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4c/LakeSolitudeHikers.JPG/140px-LakeSolitudeHikers.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4c/LakeSolitudeHikers.JPG/210px-LakeSolitudeHikers.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4c/LakeSolitudeHikers.JPG/280px-LakeSolitudeHikers.JPG 2x" width="140"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LakeSolitudeHikers.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hikers above Lake Solitude</div></div></div>
<p>The MSG is blazed with white rectangles. It is regularly maintained, and is considered moderately difficult hiking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSG_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-MSG-2">[2]</a></sup> Mount Monadnock is regarded as a challenging hike for novice hikers during favorable weather conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baldwin_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baldwin-9">[9]</a></sup> As the crow flies, the MSGT route is never more than a mile or two from a public road, however, cliffs and steep terrain make access much more difficult in some areas. Trail descriptions are available from a number of commercial and non-commercial sources, and a complete guidebook with <a href="/wiki/Topographic_map" title="Topographic map">topographic maps</a> is published by the Monadnock-Sunapee Greenway Trail Club.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSG_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-MSG-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pitcher_Mountain_New_Hampshire.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="300" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Pitcher_Mountain_New_Hampshire.jpg/120px-Pitcher_Mountain_New_Hampshire.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Pitcher_Mountain_New_Hampshire.jpg/180px-Pitcher_Mountain_New_Hampshire.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Pitcher_Mountain_New_Hampshire.jpg/240px-Pitcher_Mountain_New_Hampshire.jpg 2x" width="120"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pitcher_Mountain_New_Hampshire.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>View from Pitcher Mountain</div></div></div>
<p>Weather along the MSG is typical of southern <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a>.  Conditions on exposed hill tops and summits may be harsher during cold or stormy weather.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA-10">[10]</a></sup> Weather atop the exposed and isolated 3,100-foot (940 m) summit of Mount Monadnock can be much more severe than conditions below; high winds are common year round and winter storms on the summit can be life-threatening. <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">Lightning</a> is a hazard on exposed peaks and ledges during thunderstorms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baldwin_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Baldwin-9">[9]</a></sup> Snow is common in the winter and may necessitate the use of cross country skis or snowshoes. Ice can form on exposed ledges and summits, making hiking dangerous without special equipment. Snow and ice tend to linger on Mount Monadnock well into the spring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSG_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-MSG-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baldwin_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Baldwin-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMC_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMC-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Biting insects can be bothersome during warm weather. Parasitic <a href="/wiki/Ixodes_scapularis" title="Ixodes scapularis">deer ticks</a> (which are known to carry <a href="/wiki/Lyme_disease" title="Lyme disease">Lyme disease</a>) are a potential hazard. The trail passes through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Black_Bear" title="American Black Bear">black bear</a> habitat, although problems with bears are rare. More likely are problem encounters with domestic dogs. <a href="/wiki/Skunk" title="Skunk">Skunks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raccoon" title="Raccoon">raccoons</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Porcupine" title="Porcupine">porcupines</a> are common and active after dark. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poisonous_snake" title="Poisonous snake">Poisonous snakes</a> are considered extinct along the route. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poison_ivy" title="Poison ivy">Poison ivy</a> is uncommon on the MSGT and it does not thrive on Mount Monadnock at all.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSG_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-MSG-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baldwin_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Baldwin-9">[9]</a></sup> Some water sources along the trail flow through inhabited areas or swamps and may be contaminated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMC_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-AMC-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Camping">Camping</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monadnock-Sunapee_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Camping">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Vehicle-accessible campgrounds with basic amenities are located off the MSG via side trails at <a href="/wiki/Monadnock_State_Park" title="Monadnock State Park">Monadnock State Park</a> on the south side of Mount Monadnock, and at <a href="/wiki/Mount_Sunapee_State_Park" title="Mount Sunapee State Park">Mount Sunapee State Park</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pillsbury_State_Park" title="Pillsbury State Park">Pillsbury State Park</a> on the west side of Sunapee Ridge. The MSGTC maintains several backcountry campsites and four<a href="/wiki/Lean-to" title="Lean-to">lean-tos</a> available to backpackers. <a href="/wiki/Campfire" title="Campfire">Campfires</a> are generally prohibited on the MSG, except in established fire rings in state park campgrounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSG_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-MSG-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
