<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Green computing</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>
<b>Green computing</b>, <b>green ICT</b> as per International Federation of Global &amp; Green ICT "IFGICT", <b>green IT</b>, or <b>ICT sustainability</b>, is the study and practice of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmentally_sustainable" title="Environmentally sustainable">environmentally sustainable</a> computing or IT.
</p><p>The goals of green computing are similar to <a href="/wiki/Green_chemistry" title="Green chemistry">green chemistry</a>: reduce the use of hazardous materials, maximize <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">energy efficiency</a> during the product's lifetime,  the <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">recyclability</a> or <a href="/wiki/Biodegradation" title="Biodegradation">biodegradability</a> of defunct products and factory waste. Green computing is important for all classes of systems, ranging from handheld systems<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> to large-scale data centers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceGCS9_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceGCS9-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many corporate IT departments have green computing initiatives to reduce the environmental effect of their IT operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In 1992, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">U.S. Environmental Protection Agency</a> launched <a href="/wiki/Energy_Star" title="Energy Star">Energy Star</a>, a voluntary labeling program that is designed to promote and recognize the <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">energy efficiency</a> in monitors, climate control equipment, and other technologies. This resulted in the widespread adoption of <a href="/wiki/Sleep_mode" title="Sleep mode">sleep mode</a> among consumer electronics. Concurrently, the Swedish organization TCO Development launched the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=TCO_Certification&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="TCO Certification (page does not exist)">TCO Certification</a> program to promote low magnetic and electrical emissions from <a href="/wiki/Cathode_ray_tube" title="Cathode ray tube">CRT</a>-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_display" title="Computer display">computer displays</a>; this program was later expanded to include criteria on energy consumption, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ergonomics" title="Ergonomics">ergonomics</a>, and the use of hazardous materials in construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulations_and_industry_initiatives">Regulations and industry initiatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Regulations and industry initiatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organisation_for_Economic_Co-operation_and_Development" title="Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development">Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development</a> (OECD) has published a survey of over 90 government and industry initiatives on "Green ICTs", i.e. information and communication technologies, the environment and climate change. The report concludes that initiatives tend to concentrate on the greening ICTs themselves rather than on their actual implementation to tackle global warming and environmental degradation. In general, only 20% of initiatives have measurable targets, with government programs tending to include targets more frequently than business associations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Government">Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Governmental" title="Governmental">governmental</a> agencies have continued to implement standards and regulations that encourage green computing. The <a href="/wiki/Energy_Star" title="Energy Star">Energy Star</a> program was revised in October 2006 to include stricter efficiency requirements for computer equipment, along with a tiered ranking system for approved products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>By 2008, 26 US states established statewide recycling programs for obsolete computers and consumer electronics equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The statutes either impose an "advance recovery fee" for each unit sold at retail or require the manufacturers to reclaim the equipment at disposal.
</p><p>In 2010, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Recovery_and_Reinvestment_Act" title="American Recovery and Reinvestment Act">American Recovery and Reinvestment Act</a> (ARRA) was signed into legislation by President Obama. The bill allocated over $90 billion to be invested in green initiatives (renewable energy, smart grids, energy efficiency, etc.) In January 2010, the U.S. Energy Department granted $47 million of the ARRA money towards projects that aim to improve the energy efficiency of data centers. The projects provided research to optimize data center hardware and software, improve power supply chain, and <a href="/wiki/Computer_cooling" title="Computer cooling">data center cooling technologies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industry">Industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Climate_Savers_Computing_Initiative" title="Climate Savers Computing Initiative">Climate Savers Computing Initiative</a> (CSCI) is an effort to reduce the electric power consumption of PCs in active and inactive states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSCI_pressrel_launch_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-CSCI_pressrel_launch-10">[10]</a></sup> The CSCI provides a catalog of green products from its member organizations, and information for reducing PC power consumption. It was started on 2007-06-12. The name stems from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Wildlife_Fund" title="World Wildlife Fund">World Wildlife Fund</a>'s Climate Savers program, which was launched in 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The WWF is also a member of the Computing Initiative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSCI_pressrel_launch_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-CSCI_pressrel_launch-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Green_Electronics_Council" title="Green Electronics Council">Green Electronics Council</a> offers the <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Product_Environmental_Assessment_Tool" title="Electronic Product Environmental Assessment Tool">Electronic Product Environmental Assessment Tool</a> (EPEAT) to assist in the purchase of "greener" computing systems. The Council evaluates computing equipment on 51 criteria - 23 required and 28 optional - that measure a product's efficiency and sustainability attributes. Products are rated Gold, Silver, or Bronze, depending on how many optional criteria they meet. On 2007-01-24, President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> issued Executive Order 13423, which requires all United States Federal agencies to use EPEAT when purchasing computer systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/The_Green_Grid" title="The Green Grid">The Green Grid</a> is a global consortium dedicated to advancing energy efficiency in data centers and business computing ecosystems. It was founded in February 2007 by several key companies in the industry – <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AMD" title="AMD">AMD</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Power_Conversion" title="American Power Conversion">APC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dell" title="Dell">Dell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hewlett-Packard" title="Hewlett-Packard">HP</a>, <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a>, <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rackable_Systems" title="Rackable Systems">Rackable Systems</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=SprayCool&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="SprayCool (page does not exist)">SprayCool</a> (purchased in 2010 by <a class="external text" href="http://www.parker.com/portal/site/Market-Tech/menuitem.e9f921bc8ae21676de92b210237ad1ca/?vgnextoid=e7bfa7bee4a70310VgnVCM10000048021dacRCRD&amp;vgnextfmt=default" rel="nofollow">Parker</a>), <a href="/wiki/Sun_Microsystems" title="Sun Microsystems">Sun Microsystems</a> and <a href="/wiki/VMware" title="VMware">VMware</a>. The Green Grid has since grown to hundreds of members, including end-users and government organizations, all focused on improving <a href="/wiki/Data_center_infrastructure_efficiency" title="Data center infrastructure efficiency">data center infrastructure efficiency</a> (DCIE).</li>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green500" title="Green500">Green500</a> list rates supercomputers by energy efficiency (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megaflops" title="Megaflops">megaflops</a>/<a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">watt</a>), encouraging a focus on efficiency rather than absolute performance.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Green_Comm_Challenge" title="Green Comm Challenge">Green Comm Challenge</a> is an organization that promotes the development of energy conservation technology and practices in the field of Information and Communications Technology (ICT).</li>
<li>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Transaction_Processing_Performance_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Transaction Processing Performance Council (page does not exist)">Transaction Processing Performance Council</a> (TPC) Energy specification augments existing TPC benchmarks by allowing optional publications of energy metrics alongside performance results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/SPECpower" title="SPECpower">SPECpower</a> is the first industry standard benchmark that measures power consumption in relation to performance for server-class computers. Other benchmarks which measure energy efficiency include <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=SPECweb&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="SPECweb (page does not exist)">SPECweb</a>, <a href="/wiki/SPECvirt" title="SPECvirt">SPECvirt</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/VMmark" title="VMmark">VMmark</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Approaches">Approaches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Approaches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Modern <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">IT</a> systems rely upon a complicated mix of people, networks, and hardware; as such, a green computing initiative must cover all of these areas as well. A solution may also need to address end user satisfaction, management restructuring, regulatory compliance, and return on investment (ROI). There are also considerable fiscal motivations for companies to take control of their own power consumption; "of the power management tools available, one of the most powerful may still be simple, plain, common sense."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Product_longevity">Product longevity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Product longevity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gartner" title="Gartner">Gartner</a> maintains that the PC manufacturing process accounts for 70% of the natural resources used in the life cycle of a PC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> More recently, Fujitsu released a Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) of a desktop that show that manufacturing and end of life accounts for the majority of this desktop's ecological footprint.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Therefore, the biggest contribution to green computing usually is to prolong the equipment's lifetime. Another report from Gartner recommends to "Look for product longevity, including upgradability and modularity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> For instance, manufacturing a new PC makes a far bigger <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a> than manufacturing a new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RAM" title="RAM">RAM</a> module to upgrade an existing one.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Data_center_design">Data center design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Data center design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Data center facilities are heavy consumers of energy, accounting for between 1.1% and 1.5% of the world’s total energy use in 2010 [1]. The U.S. Department of Energy estimates that data center facilities consume up to 100 to 200 times more energy than standard office buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-https://energy.gov_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-https://energy.gov-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Energy efficient data center design should address all of the energy use aspects included in a data center: from the IT equipment to the HVAC(Heating, ventilation and air conditioning) equipment to the actual location, configuration and construction of the building.
</p><p>The U.S. Department of Energy specifies five primary areas on which to focus energy efficient data center design best practices:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Information technology (IT) systems</li>
<li>Environmental conditions</li>
<li>Air management</li>
<li>Cooling systems</li>
<li>Electrical systems</li></ul>
<p>Additional energy efficient design opportunities specified by the U.S. Department of Energy include on-site electrical generation and recycling of waste heat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Energy efficient data center design should help to better utilize a data center’s space, and increase performance and efficiency.
</p><p>In 2018, three new US Patents make use of facilities design to simultaneously cool and produce electrical power by use of internal and external waste heat. The three patents use silo design for stimulating use internal waste heat, while the recirculation of the air cooling the silo's computing racks. US Patent  <a class="external text" href="http://patft.uspto.gov/netacgi/nph-Parser?Sect1=PTO2&amp;Sect2=HITOFF&amp;p=1&amp;u=%2Fnetahtml%2FPTO%2Fsearch-bool.html&amp;r=4&amp;f=G&amp;l=50&amp;co1=AND&amp;d=PTXT&amp;s1=matteo&amp;s2=gravina&amp;OS=matteo+AND+gravina&amp;RS=matteo+AND+gravina" rel="nofollow">9,510,486</a>, uses the recirculating air for power generation, while sister patent, US Patent <a class="external text" href="http://patft.uspto.gov/netacgi/nph-Parser?Sect1=PTO2&amp;Sect2=HITOFF&amp;p=1&amp;u=%2Fnetahtml%2FPTO%2Fsearch-bool.html&amp;r=2&amp;f=G&amp;l=50&amp;co1=AND&amp;d=PTXT&amp;s1=matteo&amp;s2=gravina&amp;OS=matteo+AND+gravina&amp;RS=matteo+AND+gravina" rel="nofollow">9,907,213</a>, forces the recirculation of the same air, and sister patent, US Patent <a class="external text" href="http://patft.uspto.gov/netacgi/nph-Parser?Sect1=PTO2&amp;Sect2=HITOFF&amp;p=1&amp;u=%2Fnetahtml%2FPTO%2Fsearch-bool.html&amp;r=1&amp;f=G&amp;l=50&amp;co1=AND&amp;d=PTXT&amp;s1=matteo&amp;s2=gravina&amp;OS=matteo+AND+gravina&amp;RS=matteo+AND+gravina" rel="nofollow">10,020,436</a>, uses thermal differences in temperature resulting in negative power usage effectiveness.  Negative power usage effectiveness, makes use of extreme differences between temperatures at times running the computing facilities, that they would run only from external sources other than the power use for computing. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Software_and_deployment_optimization">Software and deployment optimization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Software and deployment optimization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Algorithmic_efficiency">Algorithmic efficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Algorithmic efficiency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Algorithmic_efficiency" title="Algorithmic efficiency">Algorithmic efficiency</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Analysis_of_algorithms" title="Analysis of algorithms">Analysis of algorithms</a></div>
<p>The efficiency of algorithms affects the amount of computer resources required for any given computing function and there are many efficiency trade-offs in writing programs.  Algorithm changes, such as switching from a slow (e.g. linear) search algorithm to a fast (e.g. hashed or indexed) search algorithm can reduce resource usage for a given task from substantial to close to zero.  In 2009, a study by a physicist at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harvard" title="Harvard">Harvard</a> estimated that the average Google search released 7 grams of carbon dioxide (CO₂).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> However, Google disputed this figure, arguing instead that a typical search produced only 0.2 grams of CO₂.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Resource_allocation">Resource allocation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Resource allocation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Resource_allocation" title="Resource allocation">Resource allocation</a></div>
<p>Algorithms can also be used to route data to data centers where electricity is less expensive. Researchers from MIT, Carnegie Mellon University, and Akamai have tested an energy allocation algorithm that successfully routes traffic to the location with the cheapest energy costs. The researchers project up to a 40 percent savings on energy costs if their proposed algorithm were to be deployed. However, this approach does not actually reduce the amount of energy being used; it reduces only the cost to the company using it. Nonetheless, a similar strategy could be used to direct traffic to rely on energy that is produced in a more environmentally friendly or efficient way. A similar approach has also been used to cut energy usage by routing traffic away from data centers experiencing warm weather; this allows computers to be shut down to avoid using air conditioning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Larger server centers are sometimes located where energy and land are inexpensive and readily available. Local availability of renewable energy, climate that allows outside air to be used for cooling, or locating them where the heat they produce may be used for other purposes could be factors in green siting decisions.
</p><p>Approaches to actually reduce the energy consumption of network devices by proper network/device management techniques are surveyed in.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The authors grouped the approaches into 4 main strategies, namely (i) Adaptive Link Rate (ALR), (ii) Interface Proxying, (iii) Energy Aware Infrastructure, and (iv) Max Energy Aware Applications.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Virtualizing">Virtualizing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Virtualizing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platform_virtualization" title="Platform virtualization">Platform virtualization</a></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Comparison_of_platform_virtualization_software" title="Comparison of platform virtualization software">Comparison of platform virtualization software</a></div>
<p>Computer virtualization refers to the abstraction of computer resources, such as the process of running two or more logical computer systems on one set of physical hardware. The concept originated with the IBM <a href="/wiki/Mainframe_computer" title="Mainframe computer">mainframe</a> operating systems of the 1960s, but was commercialized for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X86_architecture" title="X86 architecture">x86</a>-compatible computers only in the 1990s. With virtualization, a system administrator could combine several physical systems into virtual machines on one single, powerful system, thereby unplugging the original hardware and reducing power and cooling consumption. Virtualization can assist in distributing work so that servers are either busy or put in a low-power sleep state. Several commercial companies and open-source projects now offer software packages to enable a transition to virtual computing. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intel_Corporation" title="Intel Corporation">Intel Corporation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Micro_Devices" title="Advanced Micro Devices">AMD</a> have also built proprietary <a href="/wiki/X86_virtualization#Hardware_support" title="X86 virtualization">virtualization enhancements</a> to the x86 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Instruction_set" title="Instruction set">instruction set</a> into each of their CPU product lines, in order to facilitate virtual computing.
</p><p>New virtual technologies, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linux_Containers" title="Linux Containers">Linux Containers</a> can also be used to reduce energy consumption. These technologies make a ore efficient use of resources, thus reducing energy consumption by design. Also, the consolidation of virtualized technologies is more efficient than the one done in <a href="/wiki/Virtual_machine" title="Virtual machine">virtual machines</a>, so more services can be deployed in the same physical machine, reducing the amount of hardware needed. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminal_servers">Terminal servers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Terminal servers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Terminal_server" title="Terminal server">Terminal server</a></div>
<p>Terminal servers have also been used in green computing. When using the system, users at a terminal connect to a central server; all of the actual computing is done on the server, but the end user experiences the operating system on the terminal. These can be combined with <a href="/wiki/Thin_client" title="Thin client">thin clients</a>, which use up to 1/8 the amount of energy of a normal workstation, resulting in a decrease of energy costs and consumption.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> There has been an increase in using terminal services with thin clients to create virtual labs. Examples of terminal server software include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Terminal_Services" title="Terminal Services">Terminal Services</a> for Windows and the <a href="/wiki/Linux_Terminal_Server_Project" title="Linux Terminal Server Project">Linux Terminal Server Project</a> (LTSP) for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linux_operating_system" title="Linux operating system">Linux operating system</a>. Software-based remote desktop clients such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windows_Remote_Desktop" title="Windows Remote Desktop">Windows Remote Desktop</a> and <a href="/wiki/RealVNC" title="RealVNC">RealVNC</a> can provide similar thin-client functions when run on low power, commodity hardware that connects to a server.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_management">Power management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Power management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Power_management" title="Power management">Power management</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Configuration_and_Power_Interface" title="Advanced Configuration and Power Interface">Advanced Configuration and Power Interface</a> (ACPI), an open industry standard, allows an operating system to directly control the power-saving aspects of its underlying hardware. This allows a system to automatically turn off components such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_display" title="Computer display">monitors</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hard_drive" title="Hard drive">hard drives</a> after set periods of inactivity. In addition, a system may <a href="/wiki/Hibernation_(computing)" title="Hibernation (computing)">hibernate</a>, when most components (including the <a href="/wiki/Central_processing_unit" title="Central processing unit">CPU</a> and the system RAM) are turned off. ACPI is a successor to an earlier Intel-Microsoft standard called <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Power_Management" title="Advanced Power Management">Advanced Power Management</a>, which allows a computer's <a href="/wiki/BIOS" title="BIOS">BIOS</a> to control power management functions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Some programs allow the user to manually adjust the voltages supplied to the CPU, which reduces both the amount of heat produced and electricity consumed. This process is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Undervolting" title="Undervolting">undervolting</a>. Some CPUs can automatically undervolt the processor, depending on the workload; this technology is called "<a href="/wiki/SpeedStep" title="SpeedStep">SpeedStep</a>" on Intel processors, "<a href="/wiki/PowerNow!" title="PowerNow!">PowerNow!</a>"/"<a href="/wiki/Cool%27n%27Quiet" title="Cool'n'Quiet">Cool'n'Quiet</a>" on AMD chips, <a href="/wiki/LongHaul" title="LongHaul">LongHaul</a> on <a href="/wiki/VIA_Technologies" title="VIA Technologies">VIA</a> CPUs, and <a href="/wiki/LongRun" title="LongRun">LongRun</a> with <a href="/wiki/Transmeta" title="Transmeta">Transmeta</a> processors.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Data_center_power">Data center power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Data center power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Data centers, which have been criticized for their extraordinarily high energy demand, are a primary focus for proponents of green computing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceGCS9_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceGCS9-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-30">[30]</a></sup>  According to a <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a> study, data centers represent 21% of the electricity consumed by the IT sector, which is about 382 billion kWh a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Data centers can potentially improve their energy and space efficiency through techniques such as storage consolidation and virtualization. Many organizations are aiming to eliminate underutilized servers, which results in lower energy usage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The first step toward this aim will be training of data center administrators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The U.S. federal government has set a minimum 10% reduction target for data center energy usage by 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-30">[30]</a></sup> With the aid of a self-styled ultraefficient evaporative cooling technology, Google Inc. has been able to reduce its energy consumption to 50% of that of the industry average.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Operating_system_support">Operating system support</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Operating system support">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Microsoft Windows, has included limited <a href="/wiki/PC_power_management" title="PC power management">PC power management</a> features since Windows 95.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> These initially provided for stand-by (suspend-to-RAM) and a monitor low power state. Further iterations of Windows added hibernate (suspend-to-disk) and support for the <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Configuration_and_Power_Interface" title="Advanced Configuration and Power Interface">ACPI</a> standard. Windows 2000 was the first NT-based operating system to include power management. This required major changes to the underlying operating system architecture and a new hardware driver model. Windows 2000 also introduced <a href="/wiki/Group_Policy" title="Group Policy">Group Policy</a>, a technology that allowed administrators to centrally configure most Windows features. However, power management was not one of those features. This is probably because the power management settings design relied upon a connected set of per-user and per-machine binary registry values,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> effectively leaving it up to each user to configure their own power management settings.
</p><p>This approach, which is not compatible with Windows Group Policy, was repeated in Windows XP. The reasons for this design decision by Microsoft are not known, and it has resulted in heavy criticism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Microsoft significantly improved this in Windows Vista<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> by redesigning the power management system to allow basic configuration by Group Policy. The support offered is limited to a single per-computer policy. The most recent release, Windows 7 retains these limitations but does include refinements for <a href="/wiki/Timer_coalescing" title="Timer coalescing">timer coalescing</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Processor_power_management&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Processor power management (page does not exist)">processor power management</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and display panel brightness. The most significant change in Windows 7 is in the user experience. The prominence of the default High Performance power plan has been reduced with the aim of encouraging users to save power.
</p><p>There is a significant market in third-party <a href="/wiki/PC_power_management" title="PC power management">PC power management</a> software offering features beyond those present in the Windows operating system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> available. Most products offer <a href="/wiki/Active_Directory" title="Active Directory">Active Directory</a> integration and per-user/per-machine settings with the more advanced offering multiple power plans, scheduled power plans, anti-insomnia features and enterprise power usage reporting. Notable vendors include <a href="/wiki/1E" title="1E">1E</a> NightWatchman,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Data Synergy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PowerMAN_(Software)" title="PowerMAN (Software)">PowerMAN (Software)</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Faronics" title="Faronics">Faronics</a> Power Save,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Verdiem" title="Verdiem">Verdiem</a> SURVEYOR and EnviProt Auto Shutdown Manager<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a> systems started to provide laptop-optimized power-management in 2005,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> with power-management options being mainstream since 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_supply">Power supply</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Power supply">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Desktop <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_power_supply" title="Computer power supply">computer power supplies</a>  are in general 70–75% efficient,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> dissipating the remaining energy as heat. A certification program called <a href="/wiki/80_Plus" title="80 Plus">80 Plus</a> certifies PSUs that are at least 80% efficient; typically these models are drop-in replacements for older, less efficient PSUs of the same form factor. As of July 20, 2007, all new Energy Star 4.0-certified desktop PSUs must be at least 80% efficient.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Storage">Storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Smaller form factor (e.g., 2.5 inch) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hard_disk_drives" title="Hard disk drives">hard disk drives</a> often consume less power per gigabyte than physically larger drives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Unlike hard disk drives, <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_drive" title="Solid-state drive">solid-state drives</a> store data in flash memory or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DRAM" title="DRAM">DRAM</a>. With no moving parts, power consumption may be reduced somewhat for low-capacity flash-based devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>In a recent case study, <a href="/wiki/Fusion-io" title="Fusion-io">Fusion-io</a>, manufacturer of solid state storage devices, managed to reduce the energy use and operating costs of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MySpace" title="MySpace">MySpace</a> data centers by 80% while increasing performance speeds beyond that which had been attainable via multiple hard disk drives in Raid 0.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> In response, MySpace was able to retire several of their servers.
</p><p>As hard drive prices have fallen, storage farms have tended to increase in capacity to make more data available online. This includes archival and backup data that would formerly have been saved on tape or other offline storage. The increase in online storage has increased power consumption. Reducing the power consumed by large storage arrays, while still providing the benefits of online storage, is a subject of ongoing research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Video_card">Video card</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Video card">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A fast <a href="/wiki/Graphics_processing_unit" title="Graphics processing unit">GPU</a> may be the largest power consumer in a computer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p><p>Energy-efficient <a href="/wiki/Video_card" title="Video card">display</a> options include:
</p>
<ul><li>No video card - use a shared terminal, shared <a href="/wiki/Thin_client" title="Thin client">thin client</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Desktop_sharing" title="Desktop sharing">desktop sharing</a> software if display required.</li>
<li>Use motherboard video output - typically low 3D performance and low power.</li>
<li>Select a GPU based on low idle power, average wattage, or <a href="/wiki/Performance_per_watt" title="Performance per watt">performance per watt</a>.</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Display">Display</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Display">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Unlike other display technologies, <a href="/wiki/Electronic_paper" title="Electronic paper">electronic paper</a> does not use any power while displaying an image.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CRT_monitor" title="CRT monitor">CRT monitors</a> typically use more power than LCD monitors. They also contain significant amounts of lead. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LCD_monitor" title="LCD monitor">LCD monitors</a> typically use a <a href="/wiki/Cold_cathode" title="Cold cathode">cold-cathode fluorescent bulb</a> to provide light for the display. Some newer displays use an array of <a href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diode" title="Light-emitting diode">light-emitting diodes</a> (LEDs) in place of the fluorescent bulb, which reduces the amount of electricity used by the display.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>  Fluorescent back-lights also contain mercury, whereas LED back-lights do not.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Materials_recycling">Materials recycling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Materials recycling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Electronic_waste" title="Electronic waste">Electronic waste</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer_recycling" title="Computer recycling">computer recycling</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Waste_Electrical_and_Electronic_Equipment_Directive" title="Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment Directive">Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment Directive</a></div>
<p>Recycling computing equipment can keep harmful materials such as lead, mercury, and hexavalent chromium out of <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfills</a>, and can also replace equipment that otherwise would need to be manufactured, saving further energy and emissions. Computer systems that have outlived their particular function can be re-purposed, or donated to various charities and non-profit organizations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> However, many charities have recently imposed minimum system requirements for donated equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> Additionally, parts from outdated systems may be salvaged and recycled through certain retail outlets<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> and municipal or private recycling centers. Computing supplies, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ink_cartridges" title="Ink cartridges">printer cartridges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paper_recycling" title="Paper recycling">paper</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Battery_recycling" title="Battery recycling">batteries</a> may be recycled as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p><p>A drawback to many of these schemes is that computers gathered through recycling drives are often shipped to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Developing_countries" title="Developing countries">developing countries</a> where environmental standards are less strict than in North America and Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Valley_Toxics_Coalition" title="Silicon Valley Toxics Coalition">Silicon Valley Toxics Coalition</a> estimates that 80% of the post-consumer e-waste collected for recycling is shipped abroad to countries such as <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2011, the collection rate of e-waste is still very low, even in the most ecology-responsible countries like France. In this country, e-waste collection is still at a 14% annual rate between electronic equipment sold and e-waste collected for 2006 to 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p><p>The recycling of old computers raises an important privacy issue. The old storage devices still hold private information, such as emails, passwords, and credit card numbers, which can be recovered simply by someone's using software available freely on the Internet. Deletion of a file does not actually remove the file from the hard drive. Before recycling a computer, users should remove the hard drive, or hard drives if there is more than one, and physically destroy it or store it somewhere safe. There are some authorized hardware recycling companies to whom the computer may be given for recycling, and they typically sign a non-disclosure agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cloud_computing">Cloud computing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Cloud computing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cloud_computing" title="Cloud computing">Cloud computing</a></div>
<p>Cloud computing addresses two major ICT challenges related to Green computing – energy usage and <a href="/wiki/Resource_consumption" title="Resource consumption">resource consumption</a>. <a href="/wiki/Virtualization" title="Virtualization">Virtualization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_provisioning_environment" title="Dynamic provisioning environment">Dynamic provisioning environment</a>, multi-tenancy, green data center approaches are enabling cloud computing to lower carbon emissions and energy usage up to a great extent. Large enterprises and small businesses can reduce their direct energy consumption and carbon emissions by up to 30% and 90% respectively by moving certain on-premises applications into the cloud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> One common example includes <a href="/wiki/Online_shopping" title="Online shopping">Online shopping</a> that helps people purchase products and services over the Internet without requiring them to drive and waste fuel to reach out to the physical shop, which, in turn, reduces greenhouse gas emission related to travel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Google_Scholar_Citations_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-Google_Scholar_Citations-74">[74]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Edge_Computing">Edge Computing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Edge Computing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Edge_computing" title="Edge computing">Edge computing</a></div>
<p>New technologies such as <a href="/wiki/Edge_computing" title="Edge computing"> Edge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fog_computing" title="Fog computing">Fog computing</a> are a solution to reducing energy consumption. These technologies allow redistributing computation near the use, thus reducing energy costs in the network.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Furthermore, having smaller data centers, the energy used in operations such as refrigerating and maintenance gets largely reduced. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Telecommuting">Telecommuting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Telecommuting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Telecommuting" title="Telecommuting">Telecommuting</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Teleconference" title="Teleconference">Teleconferencing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Telepresence" title="Telepresence">telepresence</a> technologies are often implemented in green computing initiatives. The advantages are many; increased worker satisfaction, reduction of greenhouse gas emissions related to travel, and increased profit margins as a result of lower overhead costs for office space, heat, lighting, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> The savings are significant; the average annual energy consumption for U.S. office buildings is over 23 kilowatt hours per square foot, with heat, air conditioning and lighting accounting for 70% of all energy consumed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> Other related initiatives, such as <a href="/wiki/Hoteling" title="Hoteling">Hoteling</a>, reduce the square footage per employee as workers reserve space only when they need it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> Many types of jobs, such as sales, consulting, and field service, integrate well with this technique.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voice_over_Internet_Protocol" title="Voice over Internet Protocol">Voice over IP (VoIP)</a> reduces the telephony wiring infrastructure by sharing the existing Ethernet copper. VoIP and phone extension mobility also made <a href="/wiki/Hot_desking" title="Hot desking">hot desking</a> more practical.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Telecommunication_network_devices_energy_indices">Telecommunication network devices energy indices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Telecommunication network devices energy indices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The information and communication technologies (ICTs) energy consumption, in the USA and worldwide, has been estimated respectively at 9.4% and 5.3% of the total electricity produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> The energy consumption of ICTs is today significant even when compared with other industries. Some study tried to identify the key energy indices that allow a relevant comparison between different devices (network elements).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> This analysis was focused on how to optimise device and network consumption for carrier telecommunication by itself. The target was to  allow an immediate perception of the relationship between the network technology and the environmental effect. These studies are at the start and the gap to fill in this sector is still huge and further research will be necessary.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Supercomputers">Supercomputers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Supercomputers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The inaugural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green500" title="Green500">Green500</a> list was announced on November 15, 2007 at SC|07. As a complement to the TOP500, the unveiling of the Green500 ushered in a new era where supercomputers can be compared by performance-per-watt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Tsubame_(supercomputer)" title="Tsubame (supercomputer)">TSUBAME-KFC-GSIC Center</a> by Tokyo Institute of Technology, Made in Japan was with a great advantage to the second, the Top 1 Supercomputer in the World with 4,503.17 MFLOPS/W and 27.78 Total Power (kW)++
</p><p>Today a new supercomputer, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=L-CSC&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="L-CSC (page does not exist)">L-CSC</a> from the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=GSI_Helmholtz_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="GSI Helmholtz Center (page does not exist)">GSI Helmholtz Center</a>, Made in Germany emerged as the most energy-efficient (or greenest) supercomputer in the world. The L-CSC cluster was the first and only supercomputer on the list to surpass 5 gigaflops/watt (billions of operations per second per watt). L-CSC is a heterogeneous supercomputer that is powered by Dual Intel Xeon E5-260 and GPU accelerators, namely AMD FirePro™ S9150 GPUs. It marks the first time that a supercomputer using AMD GPUs has held the top spot. Each server has a memory of 256 gigabytes. Connected, the server via an Infiniband FDR network.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_certification">Education and certification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Education and certification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_computing_programs">Green computing programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Green computing programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Degree and postgraduate programs that provide training in a range of information technology concentrations along with sustainable strategies in an effort to educate students how to build and maintain systems while reducing its harm to the environment. The <a href="/wiki/Australian_National_University" title="Australian National University">Australian National University</a> (ANU) offers "ICT Sustainability" as part of its information technology and engineering masters programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Athabasca_University" title="Athabasca University">Athabasca University</a> offer a similar course "Green ICT Strategies",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> adapted from the ANU course notes by Tom Worthington.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> In the UK, <a href="/wiki/Leeds_Beckett_University" title="Leeds Beckett University">Leeds Beckett University</a> offers an MSc Sustainable Computing program in both full and part-time access modes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_computing_certifications">Green computing certifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Green computing certifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some certifications demonstrate that an individual has specific green computing knowledge, including:
</p>
<ul><li>Green Computing Initiative - GCI offers the Certified Green Computing User Specialist (CGCUS), Certified Green Computing Architect (CGCA) and Certified Green Computing Professional (CGCP) certifications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/CompTIA" title="CompTIA">CompTIA</a> Strata Green IT is designed for IT managers to show that they have good knowledge of green IT practices and methods and why it is important to incorporate them into an organization.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Information_Systems_Examination_Board" title="Information Systems Examination Board">Information Systems Examination Board</a> (ISEB) Foundation Certificate in Green IT is appropriate for showing an overall understanding and awareness of green computing and where its implementation can be beneficial.</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Singapore_Infocomm_Technology_Federation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Singapore Infocomm Technology Federation (page does not exist)">Singapore Infocomm Technology Federation</a> (SiTF) Singapore Certified Green IT Professional is an industry endorsed professional level certification offered with SiTF authorized training partners. Certification requires completion of a four-day instructor-led core course, plus a one-day elective from an authorized vendor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Australian_Computer_Society" title="Australian Computer Society">Australian Computer Society</a> (ACS) The ACS offers a certificate for "Green Technology Strategies" as part of the Computer Professional Education Program (CPEP). Award of a certificate requires completion of a 12-week e-learning course designed by Tom Worthington, with written assignments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup></li>
<li>International Federation of  Global &amp; Green ICT "IFGICT"- promotes Green IT Professional, Certification requires minimum 2 years in ICT industry. IFGICT is shortlisted service provider by UNFCCC - CDM.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blogs_and_Web_2.0_resources">Blogs and Web 2.0 resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Blogs and Web 2.0 resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are a lot of blogs and other user created references that can be used to gain more insights on green computing strategies, technologies and business benefits. A lot of students in Management and Engineering courses have helped in raising higher awareness about green computing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ratings">Ratings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_computing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Ratings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since 2010, <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a> has maintained a list of ratings of prominent technology companies in several countries based on how clean the energy used by that company is, ranging from A (the best) to F (the worst).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> This Rating has been certified by Dr. Jordan Kennedy from Cambridge University and his husband Professor Cory Richards. These men have done many years of research towards Green ICT.
</p>
