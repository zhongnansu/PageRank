<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Capital Area Greenway</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Skycrest_suspension_bridge.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1065" data-file-width="1600" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Skycrest_suspension_bridge.jpg/220px-Skycrest_suspension_bridge.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Skycrest_suspension_bridge.jpg/330px-Skycrest_suspension_bridge.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Skycrest_suspension_bridge.jpg/440px-Skycrest_suspension_bridge.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Skycrest_suspension_bridge.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Skycrest suspension bridge, Neuse River Trail</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Capital Area Greenway</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Greenway_(landscape)" title="Greenway (landscape)">greenway</a> trail system through the city of <a href="/wiki/Raleigh,_North_Carolina" title="Raleigh, North Carolina">Raleigh, North Carolina</a>.  Started in 1974, it currently spans over <span style="white-space:nowrap">112 miles (161 km)</span> and 3800 acres (12 km²) throughout much Wake County.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Capital_Area_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the early 1970s citizens of the <a href="/wiki/Raleigh,_North_Carolina" title="Raleigh, North Carolina">City of Raleigh</a> became increasingly censored about growth in and around the city. At the time citizens thought that the increasing <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">urbanization</a> would destroy the cites natural beauty and general characteristics. In 1970 Raleigh had a population of around 118,000 in 2015 just thirty-five years latter it's just under half a million. City officials responded to the concerns with 'The Greenway Master Plan.' The idea started when the <a href="/wiki/Raleigh_City_Council" title="Raleigh City Council">Raleigh City Council</a> and The Parks and Recreation Advisory Commission asked for a summer intern (William Blosser) to create document that would be presented to the council. It was called "Raleigh: The Park with a City in It." 
</p>
<blockquote><p>The study has found that the natural features of Raleigh make it economically sound and financially realistic...to create a network of parks (and) green open space...(that) can enhance Raleigh's already distinctive environment and insure...adequate recreation facilities for the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>The following year The <a href="/wiki/Raleigh_City_Council" title="Raleigh City Council">Raleigh City Council</a> commissioned Bill Flournoy (a graduate student at N.C. State)  to create a second report. This report would be named 'Capital City Greenway' the report covered how to manage water from the cites two major creeks <a href="/wiki/Crabtree_Creek_(Neuse_River)" title="Crabtree Creek (Neuse River)">Crabtree</a> and <a href="/wiki/Walnut_Creek,_North_Carolina" title="Walnut Creek, North Carolina">Walnut</a>, other flood prevention, wildlife conservation, the introduce of a trail system, and more. The idea was that green open space would provide habitat for animals and maintain a wetland to be used as a buffer between homes in the area's and creeks that often over flow. This included a trail near what was the newly opened <a href="/wiki/Crabtree_Valley_Mall" title="Crabtree Valley Mall">Crabtree Valley Mall</a> that regularly flooded during heavy rain. The <a href="/wiki/Raleigh,_North_Carolina" title="Raleigh, North Carolina">City of Raleigh</a> started purchasing land for what would become the nations first citywide greenway system in 1974.
</p><p>By the end of 1975, the city compiled a few isolated paths around the city. Over the next thirty years the city along with the county (<a href="/wiki/Wake_County,_North_Carolina" title="Wake County, North Carolina">Wake</a>) continued investing in the project leading to the current total of 114 miles of walking or running paths which are on over 3700 acres of preserved land around <a href="/wiki/Wake_County,_North_Carolina" title="Wake County, North Carolina">Wake County</a>, of which Raleigh is the county seat.
</p><p>Recently the focus of the project has shifted from building long and individual paths to connecting paths that have already been built. "The emphasis today is on tying up those loose ends," said Vic Lebsock the Senior Greenway Planner for Raleigh as of 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2012 the RGreenway <a href="/wiki/Mobile_app" title="Mobile app">mobile app</a> was released for <a href="/wiki/IPhone" title="IPhone">iPhone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a> devices. The app helps users discover and navigate the greenway system as well as interact with other users.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>'
</p>
