<p><i><b>Evergreen</b></i> is the seventh studio album by the English rock band <a href="/wiki/Echo_%26_the_Bunnymen" title="Echo &amp; the Bunnymen">Echo &amp; the Bunnymen</a>. It is their first album since reforming after they disbanded in 1993. Vocalist <a href="/wiki/Ian_McCulloch_(singer)" title="Ian McCulloch (singer)">Ian McCulloch</a> and guitarist <a href="/wiki/Will_Sergeant" title="Will Sergeant">Will Sergeant</a> had previously worked together as <a href="/wiki/Electrafixion" title="Electrafixion">Electrafixion</a> before they were rejoined by bassist <a href="/wiki/Les_Pattinson" title="Les Pattinson">Les Pattinson</a> under the name Echo &amp; the Bunnymen in early 1997. The album was recorded at Doghouse Studios in <a href="/wiki/Henley-on-Thames" title="Henley-on-Thames">Henley-on-Thames</a> and was produced by McCulloch and the band's manager Paul Toogood but was credited to the whole band.
</p><p>Following a successful return to live performances and the release of the single "<a href="/wiki/Nothing_Lasts_Forever_(Echo_%26_the_Bunnymen_song)" title="Nothing Lasts Forever (Echo &amp; the Bunnymen song)">Nothing Lasts Forever</a>", the album was released in July 1997. Two further singles – "<a href="/wiki/I_Want_to_Be_There_(When_You_Come)" title="I Want to Be There (When You Come)">I Want to Be There</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Let_It_Get_You_Down_(Echo_%26_the_Bunnymen_song)" title="Don't Let It Get You Down (Echo &amp; the Bunnymen song)">Don't Let It Get You Down</a>" – followed the album's release. The album received good reviews from the music press and was received well by the public, reaching number eight on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Albums_Chart" title="UK Albums Chart">UK Albums Chart</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_(Echo_%26_the_Bunnymen_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After leaving Echo &amp; the Bunnymen in 1988 to pursue a solo career, vocalist Ian McCulloch released two albums that were not commercial successes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Despite McCulloch's departure and drummer <a href="/wiki/Pete_de_Freitas" title="Pete de Freitas">Pete de Freitas</a>'s death, guitarist <a href="/wiki/Will_Sergeant" title="Will Sergeant">Will Sergeant</a> and bassist <a href="/wiki/Les_Pattinson" title="Les Pattinson">Les Pattinson</a> decided to recruit three new members – <a href="/wiki/Noel_Burke" title="Noel Burke">Noel Burke</a> (vocals), Jake Brockman (keyboards) and <a href="/wiki/Damon_Reece" title="Damon Reece">Damon Reece</a> (drums) – and continue with the same band name, which angered McCulloch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The Bunnymen released one further album, <i><a href="/wiki/Reverberation_(album)" title="Reverberation (album)">Reverberation</a></i> (1990), which critics and fans alike received poorly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams229_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-adams229-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Warner_Music_Group" title="Warner Music Group">WEA Records</a> subsequently dropped the group, who went on to break-up in early 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams229_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-adams229-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>McCulloch met former <a href="/wiki/The_Smiths" title="The Smiths">Smiths</a> guitarist <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Marr" title="Johnny Marr">Johnny Marr</a> in 1993 and they wrote and recorded an album, tentatively titled <i>Touch Down</i>. The album was to be released in early 1994;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams251_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-adams251-7">[7]</a></sup> however, despite McCulloch and Marr being happy with the album, <a href="/wiki/Rob_Dickins" title="Rob Dickins">Rob Dickins</a> at WEA felt it was missing some element. Dickins suggested to McCulloch that Sergeant be brought in to do some work. McCulloch was initially sceptical because he had not spoken with Sergeant since de Freitas's funeral; however, he did give the idea some thought.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams251_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-adams251-7">[7]</a></sup> Before McCulloch had chance to contact Sergeant, a mutual friend persuaded the pair to meet socially. While McCulloch and Sergeant were being reacquainted, the tapes from the McCulloch and Marr sessions disappeared.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams252_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-adams252-8">[8]</a></sup> McCulloch was not upset about this as he and Sergeant had started working together as <a href="/wiki/Electrafixion" title="Electrafixion">Electrafixion</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams252_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-adams252-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>With McCulloch influenced by American <a href="/wiki/Alternative_rock" title="Alternative rock">alternative rock</a> bands such as <a href="/wiki/Nirvana_(band)" title="Nirvana (band)">Nirvana</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Smashing_Pumpkins" title="The Smashing Pumpkins">The Smashing Pumpkins</a>, the group employed a heavier sound than Echo &amp; the Bunnymen's previous work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> After successfully touring the United Kingdom and refusing to play any Echo &amp; the Bunnymen material,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Electrafixion released their only album, <i><a href="/wiki/Burned_(album)" title="Burned (album)">Burned</a></i>, in September 1995. Despite critics giving the album good reviews, sales of it and the follow-up singles were disappointing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> After embarking on a tour of the United States in 1996, Electrafixion eventually gave in to fan pressure and began to introduce Echo &amp; the Bunnymen material to their live set.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Sergeant felt that as the band were playing Echo &amp; the Bunnymen songs, they might as well reform Echo &amp; the Bunnymen;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-record_collector_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-record_collector-13">[13]</a></sup> however, McCulloch was initially opposed to the idea. McCulloch changed his mind and, having persuaded Pattinson to come out of retirement, Echo &amp; the Bunnymen was reformed in mid-1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams275_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-adams275-14">[14]</a></sup> McCulloch felt Echo &amp; the Bunnymen could not reform without Pattinson and described the bassist's involvement as "integral".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grant_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-grant-15">[15]</a></sup> McCulloch went on to say it was important to "feel like the original group". He has also said, "Right from the first demo [of <i>Evergreen</i>] we realised that we'd still got that chemistry."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-staunton_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-staunton-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recording_and_packaging">Recording and packaging</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_(Echo_%26_the_Bunnymen_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Recording and packaging">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The recording of <i>Evergreen</i> started at the beginning of 1997 when Echo &amp; the Bunnymen entered Doghouse Studios in <a href="/wiki/Henley-on-Thames" title="Henley-on-Thames">Henley-on-Thames</a>. The production of the album was undertaken by McCulloch and Paul Toogood, the band's new manager,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams275_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-adams275-14">[14]</a></sup> although it was credited to the band in the liner notes to the album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> With <a href="/wiki/Oasis_(band)" title="Oasis (band)">Oasis</a> in the next studio, <a href="/wiki/Liam_Gallagher" title="Liam Gallagher">Liam Gallagher</a> contributed backing vocals to the track "<a href="/wiki/Nothing_Lasts_Forever_(Echo_%26_the_Bunnymen_song)" title="Nothing Lasts Forever (Echo &amp; the Bunnymen song)">Nothing Lasts Forever</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams275_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-adams275-14">[14]</a></sup> McCulloch said, "We just hit it off right away, and after a few beers he ended up singing on the record." McCulloch also said that Gallagher "insisted we put tambourine on ['Nothing Lasts Forever']" which "took [it] to another level".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-record_collector_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-record_collector-13">[13]</a></sup> Adam Peters, who had previously worked on the band's 1984 album <i><a href="/wiki/Ocean_Rain" title="Ocean Rain">Ocean Rain</a></i>, was brought in to provide <a href="/wiki/String_instrument" title="String instrument">string</a> <a href="/wiki/Arrangement" title="Arrangement">arrangements</a> for the album. Using musicians from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Metropolitan_Orchestra" title="London Metropolitan Orchestra">London Metropolitan Orchestra</a>, Peters recorded string passages for seven tracks from the album at <a href="/wiki/Abbey_Road_Studios" title="Abbey Road Studios">Abbey Road Studios</a> in London. With <a href="/wiki/Clif_Norrell" title="Clif Norrell">Clif Norrell</a>, who had previously worked with <a href="/wiki/R.E.M." title="R.E.M.">R.E.M.</a>, finishing the <a href="/wiki/Audio_mixing_(recorded_music)" title="Audio mixing (recorded music)">mixing</a> of the album, the recording was completed by the end of March 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams275_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-adams275-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>The photograph used on the front cover of the album was shot by Norman Watson, who also directed the <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">videos</a> for two of the singles from the album – "Nothing Lasts Forever", which was to become the lead single from the album, and "<a href="/wiki/I_Want_to_Be_There_(When_You_Come)" title="I Want to Be There (When You Come)">I Want to Be There</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams276_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-adams276-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The cover was shot in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marrakech" title="Marrakech">Marrakech</a> in early May 1997 and echoes the cover of the band's 1980 debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Crocodiles_(album)" title="Crocodiles (album)">Crocodiles</a></i>. The cover picture shows the band against a backdrop of trees at night. However, in place of the band's former drummer de Freitas, who died in a motorcycle accident, the photograph shows the remaining band members with a car.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The live debut of "Nothing Lasts Forever" was at the <a href="/wiki/Cream_(nightclub)" title="Cream (nightclub)">Cream</a> nightclub in Liverpool in early May 1997 at Echo &amp; the Bunnymen's first concert since reforming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams276_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-adams276-18">[18]</a></sup> This was followed by two sold-out concerts at the <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Lounge" title="Mercury Lounge">Mercury Lounge</a> in New York and a number of <a href="/wiki/Music_festival" title="Music festival">festival</a> appearances in the US, UK and Europe before <i>Evergreen</i> was released on 14 July 1997 by <a href="/wiki/London_Records" title="London Records">London Records</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Special_edition" title="Special edition">limited edition</a> version containing a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bonus_disc" title="Bonus disc">bonus disc</a> titled <i>History of the Peel Sessions 1979–1997</i> was released at the same time. The bonus disc contains tracks that were recorded live for <a href="/wiki/John_Peel" title="John Peel">John Peel</a>'s show on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_1" title="BBC Radio 1">BBC Radio 1</a> between 1979 and 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic_rabid_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic_rabid-24">[24]</a></sup> Following the album, two more singles were released – "I Want to Be There (When You Come)" in September 1997 and "Don't Let It Get You Down" in November 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The album was reissued in 1999 with the addition of four live tracks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic_rabid_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic_rabid-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Reviewing <i>Evergreen</i> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic">Allmusic</a>, Ned Raggett described it as "an attractive piece of work" when it "shines at its best".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-20">[20]</a></sup> Although he noted, "Replacement drummer <a href="/wiki/Michael_Lee_(musician)" title="Michael Lee (musician)">Michael Lee</a> fills in [for de Freitas] adequately but not completely, rendering what was a special group something less so." The reviewer for British music magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Melody_Maker" title="Melody Maker">Melody Maker</a></i>, called the album a "triumph" for fans as well as acknowledging that the album was unlikely to impress people who were not familiar with their work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HMV_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-HMV-22">[22]</a></sup> The album was described in <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine as "a stunning comeback".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HMV_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-HMV-22">[22]</a></sup> Jeremy Helligar for <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> was not as keen and described the reunion as having "the feel of a non-event".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ew_review_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-ew_review-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Evergreen</i> became Echo &amp; the Bunnymen's fifth album to make the Top 10 of the <a href="/wiki/UK_Albums_Chart" title="UK Albums Chart">UK Albums Chart</a> when it reached number eight during its first week of release and stayed on the chart for seven weeks. "Nothing Lasts Forever" reached number eight on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a>, although the follow-up singles "I Want to Be There (When You Come)" and "Don't Let It Get You Down" fared less well reaching numbers thirty and fifty respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guinness_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-guinness-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
