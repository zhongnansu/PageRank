<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Green building on college campuses</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b><a href="/wiki/Green_building" title="Green building">Green building</a> on college campuses</b> is the purposeful <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">construction</a> of buildings on <a href="/wiki/College" title="College">college</a> campuses that decreases resource usage in both the building process and also the future use of the building.  The goal is to reduce CO<sub>2</sub> emissions, energy use, and water use, while creating an atmosphere where students can be healthy and learn. Universities across the country are building to green standards set forth by the USGBC, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Green_Building_Council" title="United States Green Building Council">United States Green Building Council</a>.  The USGBC is a non-profit organization that promotes <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> in how buildings are designed and built.  This organization created the <a href="/wiki/Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design" title="Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design">Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design</a> (LEED) rating system, which is a certification process that provides verification that a building is environmentally sustainable. In the United States, commercial and residential buildings account for 70 percent of the electricity use and over 38 percent of <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_carbon_dioxide_emissions" title="List of countries by carbon dioxide emissions">CO<sub>2</sub> emissions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plan_B_3.0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Plan_B_3.0-1">[1]</a></sup> Because of these huge statistics regarding resource usage and emissions, the room for more efficient building practices is dramatic.  Since college campuses are where the world’s future leaders are being taught, colleges are choosing to construct new buildings to green standards in order to promote environmental stewardship to their students.  Colleges across the United States have taken leading roles in the construction of green building in order to reduce <a href="/wiki/Resource_consumption" title="Resource consumption">resource consumption</a>, save money in the long run, and instill the importance on environmental sustainability on their students.It is a better way to motivate new generation to live a sustainable life.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits_of_Green_Building_on_Campuses">Benefits of Green Building on Campuses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_on_college_campuses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Benefits of Green Building on Campuses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Green buildings on college campuses provide benefits to the campus in several different ways.  Campuses can benefit from the short and long term economic benefits.  Initially, federal and state governments will sometimes provide tax incentives for buildings constructed that surpass the standards set by the government.  There are also long term savings. According to the USGBC, with an upfront investment of 2% in green building design, the resulting life savings is 20% of the total construction costs. With many universities lacking funding, this kind of savings could dramatically help the yearly budget.  Along with this increase in monetary savings, <a href="/wiki/Green_building" title="Green building">green building</a> and <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a> has been proven to make the occupants more productive.  Studies have shown a link between improved lighting design and a 27% reduction in the incidence of headaches.  Also, students with the most daylighting in their classrooms progressed 20% faster on math tests and 26% faster on reading tests in one year than those with less daylighting.  Both of these studies show that better lighting conditions, which are one of the main features of green buildings, can increase the productivity of its occupants.  Students at colleges where green buildings are being used will benefit by increasing their potential to gain knowledge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGBC:_Green_Building_Research_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGBC:_Green_Building_Research-2">[2]</a></sup> The last important benefit of green buildings on college campuses is having the university seen as environmentally sustainable.  Students are becoming increasingly aware of the issues the Earth faces with carbon emissions and increased consumption.  These students want to attend universities that are striving to reduce their environmental impact.  Universities participating in sustainable initiatives, like constructing green buildings, will attract more highly qualified students.  Green buildings on campuses benefit both the school as well as the students.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="LEED_Rating_System">LEED Rating System</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_on_college_campuses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: LEED Rating System">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many institutions in the United States are administering the <a href="/wiki/Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design" title="Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design">LEED</a> (Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design) Green Building Rating System. The development of the LEED Rating System has been nationally recognized as the leading method to construct green buildings. The rating system incorporates the design, construction, and maintenance of the building. LEED promotes a cradle-to-cradle approach in regards to construction and design materials. The rating system is composed of six sections: Site Planning, Water Management, Energy Management, Material Use, Indoor Air Quality, and the Innovation &amp; Design Process. Each section is composed of credits and points, which ultimately determine how "green" the building is constructed, designed, and maintained.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="LEED_Certification_Levels">LEED Certification Levels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_on_college_campuses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: LEED Certification Levels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>LEED has four different levels of certification. All depending on how many credits and points were obtained through the LEED Rating System. There are 100 possible base points plus an additional 6 points for Innovation in Design and 4 points for Regional Priority.
</p><p>Buildings can qualify for 4 types of certification:
</p>
<ul><li><b>Certified</b>: 40-49 Points</li>
<li><b>Silver</b>: 50-59 Points</li>
<li><b>Gold</b>: 60-79 Points</li>
<li><b>Platinum</b>: 80 points and above</li></ul>
<h3><span id="LEED_-_NC_Application_Guide_for_Multiple_Buildings_and_On-Campus_Building_Projects_.28AGMBC.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="LEED_-_NC_Application_Guide_for_Multiple_Buildings_and_On-Campus_Building_Projects_(AGMBC)">LEED - NC Application Guide for Multiple Buildings and On-Campus Building Projects (AGMBC)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_on_college_campuses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: LEED - NC Application Guide for Multiple Buildings and On-Campus Building Projects (AGMBC)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The USGBC has issued an application guide for administration of LEED Rating System on college, corporate, or government installations that include multiple buildings. This application is designed for projects where several buildings will be constructed at once, in phases, or a single building is constructed in a setting of existing buildings with common ownership. Note, however, that the AGMBC applies to LEED Rating System Versions 2.1 and 2.2. The methods described still apply to new construction on campuses.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Issues_with_AGMBC">Issues with AGMBC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_on_college_campuses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Issues with AGMBC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The sustainable sites category is the most challenging category, and it is the most detailed section in the AGMBC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Real_Life_LEED:_Certifying_Multiple_Buildings_Together?_Part_2_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Real_Life_LEED:_Certifying_Multiple_Buildings_Together?_Part_2-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Campus settings sometimes have established property lines through campus, but share a common infrastructure between areas. (Examples include street lighting may encroach on another building, storm water routes may go into same retention areas)</li>
<li>One overall sign for LEED certification, may not appeal to college trying to market LEED dedication.</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Multi-Building_Certification_Methods">Multi-Building Certification Methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_on_college_campuses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Multi-Building Certification Methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ol><li>Certifying a new building within a setting of existing buildings that are considered a campus, i.e. there is one owner or common property management and control. Use of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retention_pond" title="Retention pond">retention pond</a> not on "site" but on campus, would still qualify for LEED credit.</li>
<li>Certifying a group of new buildings as a package where the entire building set will be rated as a package and only one rating received. These buildings may constitute the entire campus or be a subset of an existing campus.</li>
<li>Certifying new buildings where each new building is constructed to a set of standards but will receive an independent rating based on achievement of credits beyond the standards specific to that building. These buildings may constitute the entire campus or be subset of an existing campus.</li></ol>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Campus_Green_Building_Techniques">Campus Green Building Techniques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_on_college_campuses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Campus Green Building Techniques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The following methods are becoming more prevalent on campuses around the nation. Because of the large scale of college campuses, the impact of these methods are truly praise for energy savings and enhanced occupants' comfort.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_roofs" title="Green roofs">Green roofs</a> - Living, vegetative roofing alternatives; a solution to the heat island effect associated with buildings.</li>
<li>Low VOC paints - Drastically limits any odorous, harmful, or irritating emissions and enhance the occupants' comfort.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compact_fluorescent" title="Compact fluorescent">Compact fluorescent</a> bulbs - Uses less energy and give off less heat; will save energy used to cool the building.</li>
<li>Using recycled content</li>
<li>Buying and using local materials - Local materials have lower transportation costs because of the lower amount of energy needed to move materials.</li>
<li>Tree preservation and relocation</li>
<li>Low flow plumbing fixtures - Uses less water per flush.</li>
<li>Alternative transportation - Campuses utilize bike transportation, rapid bus transit, and safe pedestrian walkways. <a href="/wiki/Zipcar" title="Zipcar">Zipcar</a> is also becoming popular on many college campuses.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_Materials_used_in_Green_Building">Sustainable Materials used in Green Building</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_on_college_campuses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sustainable Materials used in Green Building">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The following are some examples<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-University_of_Florida_FP&amp;C_Student_Presentations_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-University_of_Florida_FP&amp;C_Student_Presentations-8">[8]</a></sup> of sustainable products used in green building. These materials are less harmful to the environment. Now-a-days many materials have a "green" substitute.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Division_3:_Concrete">Division 3: Concrete</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_on_college_campuses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Division 3: Concrete">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>PS 4000 Flat Wall Form:
<ul><li>Improved tongue-and-groove design simplifies installation on the job and minimizes the problems associated with <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a> spillage at the top of the wall.</li>
<li>The unique design provides strength, fire resistance, and dimensional stability</li>
<li>Provides you with a superior construction technology that delivers cost-effect, high-performance structures that are safer, quieter, comfortable, energy efficient, and more structurally secure and environmentally responsible building system available on the market today.</li></ul></li>
<li>Fly Ash
<ul><li>Because fly ash use displaces <a href="/wiki/Portland_cement" title="Portland cement">Portland cement</a> use, it also reduces the need for cement production, which is a major energy user and a leading source of "greenhouse gas" emissions.</li>
<li>Better performance without increase in cost.</li>
<li>It can replace up to 30% by mass of Portland cement, and can add to the concrete’s final strength and increase its chemical resistance and durability.</li></ul></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Division_4:_Masonry">Division 4: Masonry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_on_college_campuses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Division 4: Masonry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Cavclear Masonry Mat
<ul><li>A fluid-conducting, non-absorbent polymer mesh made from 100% recycled plastic that is installed full-height in the airspace.</li>
<li>It prevents <a href="/wiki/Mortar_(masonry)" title="Mortar (masonry)">mortar</a> from bridging the airspace and results in a continuous area for drainage and ventilation.</li>
<li>Ensures water management.</li>
<li>Reduces building's life-cycle costs.</li></ul></li>
<li>Sealtech Block
<ul><li>Certified "Green" with 10% recycled high-strength plastic powder.</li>
<li>Non-porous surface means decreased <a href="/wiki/Permeability_(earth_sciences)" title="Permeability (earth sciences)">permeability</a>, making it water-resistant.</li>
<li>Stronger than standard concrete block yet 10% lighter, translating into reduced shipping and labor costs.</li></ul></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Division_5:_Metals">Division 5: Metals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_on_college_campuses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Division 5: Metals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Maze nails
<ul><li>Made from recycled steel.</li>
<li>The scrap <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> generated while making nail heads goes right back to the steel mill for re-melting.</li>
<li>Nails are galvanized with a dual Zinc coating for durability assurance.</li></ul></li>
<li>Cold-formed metal framing
<ul><li>Lightweight, and dimensionally stable.</li>
<li>Contains 20-25% recycled material (10-15% post-consumer content, though some manufactures have in excess of 90% of recycled content.)</li>
<li>Steel studs can even be recycled at end of building’s life.</li></ul></li></ul>
<h3><span id="Division_6:_Woods.2C_Plastics.2C_and_Composites"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Division_6:_Woods,_Plastics,_and_Composites">Division 6: Woods, Plastics, and Composites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_on_college_campuses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Division 6: Woods, Plastics, and Composites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Ecosurfaces
<ul><li>Made from recycled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tires" title="Tires">tires</a>.</li>
<li>Slip-resistant.</li>
<li>Weather resistant, able to withstand extreme temperatures</li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Reclaimed_lumber" title="Reclaimed lumber">Reclaimed lumber</a>
<ul><li>If not reused, <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a> would be burned or chipped</li>
<li>Old growth forests are protected</li>
<li>Durable and aesthetically pleasing.</li>
<li>The wood has become stabilized over time, which prevents changes due to humidity.</li></ul></li>
<li>Engineered wood, Gluecam
<ul><li>Provide a significant environmental advantage over solid wood by utilizing fast-growing, small diameter trees effectively.</li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Plastic_lumber" title="Plastic lumber">Plastic lumber</a>
<ul><li>Makes use of recycled plastic and is an effective replacement for pressure-treated lumber, which also protects timber resources.</li>
<li>Will not rot, absorb water, splinter, or crack</li>
<li>Resistant to oil, salt, and chemicals</li></ul></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Division_7:_Thermal_and_Moisture_Protection">Division 7: Thermal and Moisture Protection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_on_college_campuses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Division 7: Thermal and Moisture Protection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Concrete roof tiles
<ul><li>Made from an approximate mix of 3 parts sand to 1 part <a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">cement</a> and 10% water.</li>
<li>Limited maintenance is necessary.</li>
<li>Concrete tiles are wind resistant.</li>
<li>Can last up to 100 years.</li></ul></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Division_8:_Openings">Division 8: Openings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_on_college_campuses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Division 8: Openings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Greenscreen PVC-free fabrics
<ul><li>PVC-Free construction of <a href="/wiki/Polyurethane" title="Polyurethane">polyurethane</a> and specially designed, pre-stretched <a href="/wiki/Polyester" title="Polyester">polyester</a> core.</li>
<li>5 different levels of visibility: 3%, 5%, 10%, and 25%.</li>
<li>Elimination of PVC content in production of GreenScreen fabrics mean shades contain no VOC’s and does not "offgas" during the life of the product.</li>
<li>PVC-content makes it easier and quicker to recycle GreenScreen fabrics and divert them from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfills" title="Landfills">landfills</a>.</li></ul></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Division_9:_Finishes">Division 9: Finishes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_on_college_campuses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Division 9: Finishes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Marmoleum Flooring
<ul><li>Raw materials and energy are used efficiently, waste is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recycled" title="Recycled">recycled</a> wherever possible, and emissions are kept to an absolute minimum.</li>
<li>Life-cycle analysis shows that these linoleum products are ecologically preferred floor covering.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Linoleum" title="Linoleum">Linoleum</a> is produced from renewable materials: linseed oil, <a href="/wiki/Wood_flour" title="Wood flour">wood flour</a>, jute and ecologically responsible pigments.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Organic_matter" title="Organic matter">Organic</a> product.</li></ul></li>
<li>Cork floating floor
<ul><li>Highly compressible and resilient.</li>
<li>Excellent sound and thermal insulator.</li>
<li>Lightweight and buoyant.</li>
<li>Natural fire retardant, hypoallergenic, and insect resistant.</li></ul></li>
<li>Australian Chestnut flooring
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design" title="Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design">LEED</a> Qualification: MR 7-Certified Wood</li>
<li>Product is certified according to the principles &amp; criteria of the Forest Stewardship Council (FSC), adhering to strict environmental and social standards</li>
<li>Easily meets E-1 Standard for Indoor Air Quality</li></ul></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bamboo_flooring" title="Bamboo flooring">Bamboo flooring</a>
<ul><li>Bamboo is not wood, but rather a type of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grass" title="Grass">grass</a>.</li>
<li>Quick <a href="/wiki/Renewable_resource" title="Renewable resource">renewable resource</a>, can be harvested in as little as 5 years.</li>
<li>Very strong and stable, more so than many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hardwoods" title="Hardwoods">hardwoods</a></li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organizations">Organizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_on_college_campuses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Organizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="International_Sustainable_Campus_Network">International Sustainable Campus Network</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_on_college_campuses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: International Sustainable Campus Network">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Universities have a leadership role in advancing knowledge, technology and tools to create a sustainable future. To fulfill this role effectively and with high credibility, they need to include a focus on sustainability also in their own operations and facilities. Campus projects, be they educational or corporate campus developments, present interesting sustainability challenges and opportunities. Firstly, their size is at the borderline between single building projects and small towns, a fruitful scale for innovative energy and transport solutions. And secondly, they are to a certain degree one-purpose neighborhoods focused on education, research, development or distribution of new ideas, products or services.
</p>
<ul><li>Goal 1: sustainable construction, renovation, and operation</li>
<li>Goal 2. sustainable master planning and development, mobility, and community integration</li>
<li>Goal 3. linking facilities, research and education for sustainable development</li></ul>

As part of its commitment to green and sustainable safety concepts, the Code Council is excited to develop a new set of green codes under the multi-year initiative called "IGCC: Safe and Sustainable by the Book." This initiative will include collaboration from the Council’s closest allies and pre-eminent thought leaders in green building, as well as outreach and feedback from our members and the general public.  The International Green Construction Code is committed to developing an effective and efficient code that will continue our long tradition of international code guidance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-International_Green_Construction_Code_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-International_Green_Construction_Code-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="World_Green_Building_Council">World Green Building Council</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_on_college_campuses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: World Green Building Council">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/World_Green_Building_Council" title="World Green Building Council">World Green Building Council</a> is an international organization that facilitates the green building councils of many developed and developing nations. The Council started in 1999 with its first meeting in California. Eight members attended the first meeting: <a href="/wiki/U.S._Green_Building_Council" title="U.S. Green Building Council">U.S. Green Building Council</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green_Building_Council_of_Australia" title="Green Building Council of Australia">Green Building Council of Australia</a>, Spain Green Building Council, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Green_Building_Council" title="United Kingdom Green Building Council">United Kingdom Green Building Council</a>, Japan Green Building Council, United Arab Emirates, Russia and Canada. THE WorldGBC incorporated in 2002 and operates from Toronto, Canada.  There are currently over 15 established GBCs and 35 emerging and prospective countries with GBCs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-World_Green_Building_Council_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-World_Green_Building_Council-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Stanford_University:_Knight_Management_Center">Stanford University: Knight Management Center</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_on_college_campuses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Stanford University: Knight Management Center">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stanford" title="Stanford">Stanford</a> is a leading university in the green movement and the school is striving to achieve a LEED Platinum certification for their new graduate school of business, the Knight Management Center.  The goal for this building is to open in the winter of 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sustainable_Stanford_Green_Buildings_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sustainable_Stanford_Green_Buildings-12">[12]</a></sup> The center will have eight buildings around three quadrangles with 360,000 square feet (33,000 m<sup>2</sup>) of interior space.  According to the principal architect, Stan Boles of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boora_Architects" title="Boora Architects">Boora Architects</a> in Portland, Oregon, "The orientation of the buildings is narrow in the north-south dimension.  They are designed for optimum daylighting, ventilation, and for shading of one another.  The exterior walls are designed so that areas of glass are created but shaded by exterior screens to prevent excessive heat gain."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stanford_Business_School_breaks_ground_on_LEED_Platinum_campus_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stanford_Business_School_breaks_ground_on_LEED_Platinum_campus-13">[13]</a></sup> This project aims to:
</p>
<ul><li>Reduce overall water usage by at least 40%.</li>
<li>Exceed current energy efficiency standards by at least 40%.</li>
<li>Generate at least 12% electricity on site through <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar energy</a>.</li>
<li>Use rainwater or re-circulated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gray_water" title="Gray water">gray water</a> to reduce <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Potable_water" title="Potable water">potable water</a> use for building sewage conveyance by 80%.</li>
<li>Recycle or salvage 50% to 70% of non-hazardous construction debris.</li>
<li>Use low- or non-volatile organic compound-emitting materials to ensure exceptional indoor air quality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stanford_Business_School_breaks_ground_on_LEED_Platinum_campus_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stanford_Business_School_breaks_ground_on_LEED_Platinum_campus-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sustainability:_Knight_Management_Center:_Stanford_GSB_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sustainability:_Knight_Management_Center:_Stanford_GSB-14">[14]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Stanford’s president, John L. Hennessy, said, "One of the biggest global challenges facing us today is the sustainable use of our planets natural resources.  The Graduate School of Business will play a key role in helping us address these challenges by leading the way in its sustainable development of this new campus."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stanford_Business_School_breaks_ground_on_LEED_Platinum_campus_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Stanford_Business_School_breaks_ground_on_LEED_Platinum_campus-13">[13]</a></sup> Stanford University is taking an active role in constructing green buildings on their campus and the Knight Management Center will be a great example of how a building can be sustainable.
</p>
<h4><span id="University_of_California_at_Santa_Barbara:_Donald_Bren_School_of_Environmental_Science_.26_Management"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="University_of_California_at_Santa_Barbara:_Donald_Bren_School_of_Environmental_Science_&amp;_Management">University of California at Santa Barbara: Donald Bren School of Environmental Science &amp; Management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_on_college_campuses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: University of California at Santa Barbara: Donald Bren School of Environmental Science &amp; Management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Donald Bren School of Environmental Science &amp; Management is located at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Santa_Barbara" title="University of California, Santa Barbara">University of California, Santa Barbara</a>, California.  The academic laboratory and classroom facility demonstrates cost-effective, energy-efficient technologies and operations.  The concrete and steel frame structure was complete in 2002 and cost approximately $27,500,000.  Donald Bren Hall was the first laboratory to receive LEED Platinum accreditation, the highest rating achievable through the US Green Building Council’s national rating system, with the following building design features:
</p>
<ul><li><b>Site Protection</b>: Since Donald Bren Hall is located adjacent to the ocean, a strict site protection plan was developed and implemented to ensure all storm water is retained onsite to prevent contamination of local waterways.</li>
<li><b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_Efficiency" title="Water Efficiency">Water Efficiency</a></b>: A separate <a href="/wiki/Reclaimed_water" title="Reclaimed water">reclaimed water</a> system was installed to furnish <a href="/wiki/Greywater" title="Greywater">greywater</a> to flush toilets and irrigate the landscape. Waterless urinals were also installed and it is estimated that each waterless urinal will save approximately 45,000 gallons of water per year.</li>
<li><b>Energy Efficiency</b>: Design includes a 40 KW rooftop photovoltaic system, natural ventilation linked with a window interlock system for heating, daylighting controls, energy-efficient lighting, high efficiency boiler, and chiller integrated into a virtual chilled water loop. These energy efficiency measures assisted the building to exceed Title 24 (1998 Standards) by 31%.</li>
<li><b>Materials Efficiency</b>: 93% of the construction waste generated onsite was diverted from the landfill. Recycled-content products include 12-20% flyash in the concrete, glass tiles and countertops, 100% postconsumer recycled content carpet, and tire-derived rubber flooring. Other environmentally preferable products for the interior surface materials included linoleum and natural cork flooring, bamboo cabinetry, and stained concrete flooring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sustainable_Building_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sustainable_Building-15">[15]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>According to Great Buildings, "The Donald Bren School at the University of California, Santa Barbara takes advantage of a beautiful setting near the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> to become a green building that embraces its environment not only for efficiency, but for experience. With a striking open courtyard, it provides ample opportunity for social interaction that makes the transition between indoors and outdoors much smoother and ephemeral than most buildings.  Building Bren Hall with sustainable materials and methods is estimated to have added only 2% to the building cost, which will easily be offset over time by energy savings."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donald_Bren_School_of_Environmental_Science_&amp;_Management_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Donald_Bren_School_of_Environmental_Science_&amp;_Management-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="University_of_North_Carolina_at_Chapel_Hill:_Botanical_Gardens_Education_Center">University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill: Botanical Gardens Education Center</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_on_college_campuses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill: Botanical Gardens Education Center">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The 29,656-square-foot (2,755.1 m<sup>2</sup>) Education Center is located at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Carolina" title="University of North Carolina">University of North Carolina</a> at Chapel Hill.  The building consists of three major sections connected by covered breezeways.  The central wing welcomes visitors to the education center as they enter the garden through a large breezeway.  The east wing offers classrooms for students enrolled in workshops and classrooms, and the west wing features the Reeves Auditorium.  This large multipurpose space is used for lectures, conferences, and special events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-N.C._Botanical_Garden_inaugurates_new_green_education_center_with_fall_events_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-N.C._Botanical_Garden_inaugurates_new_green_education_center_with_fall_events-17">[17]</a></sup> The Education Center plans to achieve a LEED Platinum rating, most likely the first ever in North Carolina, with these features:
</p>
<ul><li><b>Site Selection and Design</b>: The Education center was located with an efficient solar orientation.  Also, during the construction process, there was minimal disturbance to grade, and existing vegetation was well protected.</li>
<li><b>Water Efficiency</b>: The building uses water-efficient native landscaping and low-flow plumbing.  Stormwater is conserved and re-used.  Rainwater cisterns, gardens, and retention swales are also methods being used.</li>
<li><b>Energy Efficiency</b>: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geothermal" title="Geothermal">Geothermal</a> wells for efficient heating and air-conditioning are used. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" title="Photovoltaic">Photovoltaic</a> and solar cells have been installed on the building, and natural lighting is used very effectively along with day sensors that automatically dim lights when daylight is too strong.</li>
<li><b>Materials Efficiency</b>: To minimize transportation costs and carbon dioxide emissions, and to stimulate local economies, all materials were locally and sustainably produced.  No wood came from old-growth trees; all the wood came from certified sustainable forests.  At least 75% of the construction waste was recycled, and there was no non-toxic or off-gassing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-An_Environmentally_Sustainable_Education_Center_for_the_North_Carolina_Botanical_Garden_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-An_Environmentally_Sustainable_Education_Center_for_the_North_Carolina_Botanical_Garden-18">[18]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The new Education Center expresses a sense of place and celebrates relationships between humans and nature through the integration of indoor and outdoor spaces. Open breezeways, comfortable porches, natural light in every room, beautiful <a href="/wiki/Native_plant" title="Native plant">native plant</a> landscaping, and educational exhibits inform, delight, and invite visitors to the Conservation Garden. Most of all, the building is a center of learning, teaching both the science and the enjoyment of plants and <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">nature</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-An_Environmentally_Sustainable_Education_Center_for_the_North_Carolina_Botanical_Garden_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-An_Environmentally_Sustainable_Education_Center_for_the_North_Carolina_Botanical_Garden-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="University_of_Florida:_James_W._Heavener_Football_Complex">University of Florida: James W. Heavener Football Complex</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_on_college_campuses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: University of Florida: James W. Heavener Football Complex">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Florida" title="University of Florida">University of Florida</a>’s new football complex, the James W. Heavener Football Complex, was completed in 2008 and received LEED Platinum rating for the environmental sustainability of the building.  The facility contractor was PPI Construction Management and the architect was RDG Planning and Design.  The building includes offices, conference rooms, an atrium to display the football teams accomplishments, and a weight training facility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UF’s_Football_Complex_Receives_Highest_Green_Building_Certification_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-UF’s_Football_Complex_Receives_Highest_Green_Building_Certification-19">[19]</a></sup> The LEED rated the complex 52 out of the 69 available points for the certification, which gave the building the Platinum rating.  This facility is the first platinum athletic facility in the United States as well as the first platinum rated building in the state of Florida.  The $28 million building exceeded the original goal of obtaining a LEED Silver rating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Football_Complex_leads_the_state_in_sustainability_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Football_Complex_leads_the_state_in_sustainability-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>This building has many features that helped it to achieve the Platinum level.  The features dealing with water usage reduce the buildings indoor water use by 40 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UF’s_Football_Complex_Receives_Highest_Green_Building_Certification_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-UF’s_Football_Complex_Receives_Highest_Green_Building_Certification-19">[19]</a></sup> Due to all of the facility’s energy saving features the building has exceeded the state and national energy requirements by 35 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UF’s_Football_Complex_Receives_Highest_Green_Building_Certification_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-UF’s_Football_Complex_Receives_Highest_Green_Building_Certification-19">[19]</a></sup>  Another interesting fact about the construction of this building is that most of the material used in the construction came from within 500 miles of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Florida" title="University of Florida">University of Florida</a>, which reduce the emissions created form transporting the material.  Also 78 percent of the building debris was recycled.  The assistant director of LEED at UF, Bahar Armaghani, said, "Green Buildings are not exclusively concerned with saving money through more efficient technology.  They are also investments for the well-being of the people and environment."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UF’s_Football_Complex_Receives_Highest_Green_Building_Certification_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-UF’s_Football_Complex_Receives_Highest_Green_Building_Certification-19">[19]</a></sup> The University of Florida has taken on an initiative to have all new construction be LEED Gold certified or higher and with the construction of this facility the school has surpassed their own requirements by achieving the Platinum rating.
</p><p>Key Features of the Heavener Football Complex:
</p>
<ul><li>Occupancy sensors to control lighting</li>
<li>Organic carpet</li>
<li>Paint and flooring made out of recycled materials</li>
<li>Low-flow water fixture and water saving shower heads</li>
<li>Dual-flush toilets</li>
<li>Low-e glazing, insulation, and reflective material on glass</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Green_roof" title="Green roof">Green roof</a> on weight room</li>
<li>100% reclaimed water for irrigation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UF’s_Football_Complex_Receives_Highest_Green_Building_Certification_19-4"><a href="#cite_note-UF’s_Football_Complex_Receives_Highest_Green_Building_Certification-19">[19]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="High_Point_University_School_of_Education">High Point University School of Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_on_college_campuses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: High Point University School of Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/High_Point_University" title="High Point University">High Point University</a>, located in High Point, North Carolina, has a LEED-Certified building that houses the School of Education. The 31,000-square-foot building houses the education and psychology departments in technologically advanced classrooms, computer labs and offices. It features high-tech educational equipment, such as smart boards, a children’s book library, math and science touch screen games, a methods lab designed to look and feel like a real elementary school classroom, a Mac lab and psychology research booths. The School of Education building is setting an example for modern-day energy conservation with things like floor to ceiling windows for natural lighting and light sensors in the rooms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Key Statistics: 
</p>
<ul><li>Water usage is cut by 30 percent inside the building and by 50 percent in its irrigation system</li>
<li>Energy usage is decreased by 24 percent.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International">International</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_on_college_campuses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: International">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Charles_Hostler_Student_Center">Charles Hostler Student Center</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_on_college_campuses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Charles Hostler Student Center">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Charles Hostler Student Center on the campus of the <a href="/wiki/American_University_of_Beirut" title="American University of Beirut">American University of Beirut</a> provides a model for environmentally responsive design that meets the social needs of the campus and the larger region. Situated on Beirut's seafront and main public thoroughfare, the new 204,000 sq ft (19,000 m<sup>2</sup>). facility houses competitive and recreational athletic facilities for swimming, basketball, handball, volleyball, squash, exercise and weight training. The space also includes an auditorium with associated meeting rooms, cafeteria with study space, and underground parking for 200 cars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HPB_Case_Study:_Overview_-_Charles_Hostler_Student_Center_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-HPB_Case_Study:_Overview_-_Charles_Hostler_Student_Center-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Green Building methods:
</p>
<ul><li>Organized as building clusters as opposed to a single building. Allowing the building forms themselves to redistribute air, activity and shade.</li>
<li>The east-west orientation of the building forms helps to shade exterior courtyards, reducing the amount of southern exposure.</li>
<li>The orientation also directs nighttime breezes and daytime sea breezes to cool outdoor spaces.</li>
<li>Green spaces on the rooftops allow for a more pleasing physical and visual integration with the upper campus, providing usable rooftop areas for activities and reducing the amount of exposure to the sun.</li>
<li>Usable program area on the site is increased through shading and <a href="/wiki/Ventilation_(architecture)" title="Ventilation (architecture)">ventilation</a> of outdoor spaces<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Integrated_sports_centre_honoured_at_AIA_Top_Ten_Green_Projects_awards_2009_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Integrated_sports_centre_honoured_at_AIA_Top_Ten_Green_Projects_awards_2009-23">[23]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Dubai_International_Academic_City_Phase-III">Dubai International Academic City Phase-III</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_on_college_campuses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Dubai International Academic City Phase-III">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dubai_International_Academic_City" title="Dubai International Academic City">Dubai International Academic City</a> Phase-III (DIAC phase-III) comprises four academic buildings and a food court spread over a total built up area of 600,000 square feet (56,000 m<sup>2</sup>). It has received the Silver LEED certification, and is expected to save approximately AED2.3 million per year from reduced energy costs, district cooling demand changes, irrigation water costs, sewage tanker and domestic water costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dubai_International_Academic_City_Phase-III_Becomes_Middle_East’s_First_and_Largest_LEED_Certified_Academic_Facility_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dubai_International_Academic_City_Phase-III_Becomes_Middle_East’s_First_and_Largest_LEED_Certified_Academic_Facility-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Green Building component Features:
</p>
<ul><li>Heat recovery wheels</li>
<li>Enhanced levels of insulation</li>
<li>Optimization of fresh air through variable speed drives on air handling units</li>
<li>Recessed windows</li>
<li>Significantly low lighting power densities</li></ul>
<p>These features will make this cluster 21.7% more energy efficient than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Society_of_Heating,_Refrigerating_and_Air-Conditioning_Engineers" title="American Society of Heating, Refrigerating and Air-Conditioning Engineers">American Society of Heating, Refrigerating and Air-Conditioning Engineers</a> (ASHRAE) <a href="/wiki/ASHRAE_90.1" title="ASHRAE 90.1">90.1 - 2004 standards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dubai_International_Academic_City_Phase-III_Becomes_Middle_East’s_First_and_Largest_LEED_Certified_Academic_Facility_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dubai_International_Academic_City_Phase-III_Becomes_Middle_East’s_First_and_Largest_LEED_Certified_Academic_Facility-24">[24]</a></sup> will also consume 30% less water than the standards set by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="U.S. Environmental Protection Agency">U.S. Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA) as well as 40% less irrigation water. These savings have been achieved by the installation of ultra-low flow water restrictors in wash basins and dual-flush tanks in wash rooms, as well as additives in the soil for the landscape areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dubai_International_Academic_City_Phase-III_moves_closer_to_get_Leed_certification_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dubai_International_Academic_City_Phase-III_moves_closer_to_get_Leed_certification-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
