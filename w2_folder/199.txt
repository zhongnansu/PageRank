<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Performance per watt</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>In <a href="/wiki/Computing" title="Computing">computing</a>, <b>performance per watt</b> is a measure of the <a href="/wiki/Energy_conversion_efficiency" title="Energy conversion efficiency">energy efficiency</a> of a particular <a href="/wiki/Computer_architecture" title="Computer architecture">computer architecture</a> or <a href="/wiki/Computer_hardware" title="Computer hardware">computer hardware</a>. Literally, it measures the rate of computation that can be delivered by a computer for every <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">watt</a> of power consumed. This rate is typically measured by performance on the <a href="/wiki/LINPACK" title="LINPACK">LINPACK</a> benchmark when trying to compare between computing systems.
</p><p>System designers building <a href="/wiki/Parallel_computing" title="Parallel computing">parallel computers</a>, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Google_search_technology#Production_hardware" title="Google search technology">Google's hardware</a>, pick CPUs based on their (other than Green500) performance per watt of power, because the cost of powering the CPU outweighs the cost of the CPU itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performance_per_watt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The performance and power consumption metrics used depend on the definition; reasonable measures of performance are <a href="/wiki/FLOPS" title="FLOPS">FLOPS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Instructions_per_second" title="Instructions per second">MIPS</a>, or the score for any <a href="/wiki/Benchmark_(computing)" title="Benchmark (computing)">performance benchmark</a>.  Several measures of power usage may be employed, depending on the purposes of the metric; for example, a metric might only consider the electrical power delivered to a machine directly, while another might include all power necessary to run a computer, such as cooling and monitoring systems. The power measurement is often the average power used while running the benchmark, but other measures of power usage may be employed (e.g. peak power, idle power).
</p><p>For example, the early <a href="/wiki/UNIVAC_I" title="UNIVAC I">UNIVAC I</a> computer performed approximately 0.015 operations per watt-second (performing 1,905 operations per second (OPS), while consuming 125 kW). The <a href="/wiki/Fujitsu" title="Fujitsu">Fujitsu</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FR-V" title="FR-V">FR-V</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VLIW" title="VLIW">VLIW</a>/<a href="/wiki/Vector_processor" title="Vector processor">vector processor</a> <a href="/wiki/System_on_a_chip" title="System on a chip">system on a chip</a> in the 4 FR550 core variant released 2005 performs 51 Giga-OPS with 3 watts of power consumption resulting in 17 billion operations per watt-second.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This is an improvement by over a trillion times in 54 years.
</p><p>Most of the power a computer uses is converted into heat, so a system that takes fewer watts to do a job will require less cooling to maintain a given <a href="/wiki/Operating_temperature" title="Operating temperature">operating temperature</a>.  Reduced cooling demands makes it easier to <a href="/wiki/Quiet_PC" title="Quiet PC">quiet a computer</a>. Lower energy consumption can also make it less costly to run, and reduce the environmental impact of powering the computer (see <a href="/wiki/Green_computing" title="Green computing">green computing</a>).
If installed where there is limited <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_control" title="Climate control">climate control</a>, a lower power computer will operate at a lower temperature, which may make it more reliable.  In a climate controlled environment, reductions in direct power use may also create savings in climate control energy.
</p><p>Computing energy consumption is sometimes also measured by reporting the energy required to run a particular benchmark, for instance <a href="/wiki/EEMBC" title="EEMBC">EEMBC</a> EnergyBench.  Energy consumption figures for a standard workload may make it easier to judge the effect of an improvement in <a href="/wiki/Electrical_efficiency" title="Electrical efficiency">energy efficiency</a>.
</p><p>Performance (in operations/second) per watt can also be written as operations/watt-second, or operations/joule, since 1 watt = 1 joule/second.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="FLOPS_per_watt">FLOPS per watt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performance_per_watt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: FLOPS per watt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span id="MFLOPS_PER_WATT"></span><span id="GFLOPS_PER_WATT"></span>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Green500_evolution.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="540" data-file-width="720" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Green500_evolution.svg/400px-Green500_evolution.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Green500_evolution.svg/600px-Green500_evolution.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Green500_evolution.svg/800px-Green500_evolution.svg.png 2x" width="400"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Green500_evolution.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Exponential growth of supercomputer performance per watt based on data from the Green500 list. The red crosses denote the most power efficient computer, while the blue ones denote the computer ranked#500.</div></div></div>
<p><b>FLOPS per watt</b> is a common measure. Like the <a href="/wiki/FLOPS" title="FLOPS">FLOPS</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floating_point" title="Floating point">Floating Point</a> Operations Per Second) metric it is based on, the metric is usually applied to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_computing" title="Scientific computing">scientific computing</a> and simulations involving many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floating_point" title="Floating point">floating point</a> calculations.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performance_per_watt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of  June 2016<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Performance_per_watt&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the <a href="#Green500_List">Green500</a> list rates the two most efficient supercomputers highest –  those are both based on the same <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manycore" title="Manycore">manycore</a> accelerator <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PEZY-SCnp" title="PEZY-SCnp">PEZY-SCnp</a> Japanese technology in addition to Intel Xeon processors –  both at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RIKEN" title="RIKEN">RIKEN</a>, the top one at 6673.8 MFLOPS/watt; and the third ranked is the Chinese-technology <a href="/wiki/Sunway_TaihuLight" title="Sunway TaihuLight">Sunway TaihuLight</a> (a much bigger machine, that is the ranked 2nd on <a href="/wiki/TOP500" title="TOP500">TOP500</a>, the others are not on that list) at 6051.3 MFLOPS/watt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2012, the Green500 list rated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue_Gene/Q" title="Blue Gene/Q">BlueGene/Q, Power BQC 16C</a> as the most efficient supercomputer on the TOP500 in terms of FLOPS per watt, running at 2,100.88 MFLOPS/watt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 9 June 2008, CNN reported that <a href="/wiki/IBM_Roadrunner" title="IBM Roadrunner">IBM's Roadrunner</a> supercomputer achieves 376 MFLOPS/watt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 2010, IBM machine, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IBM_Blue_Gene#Blue_Gene/Q" title="IBM Blue Gene">Blue Gene/Q</a> achieves 1,684 MFLOPS/watt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>As part of <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Intel_Tera-Scale" title="Intel Tera-Scale">Tera-Scale</a> research project, the team produced an 80 core CPU that can achieve over 16,000 MFLOPS/watt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The future of that CPU is not certain.
</p><p>Microwulf, a low cost desktop <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beowulf_(computing)" title="Beowulf (computing)">Beowulf cluster</a> of four dual-core <a href="/wiki/Athlon_64_X2" title="Athlon 64 X2">Athlon 64 X2</a> 3800+ computers, runs at 58 MFLOPS/watt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-microwulf_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-microwulf-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Kalray has developed a 256-core VLIW CPU that achieves 25,000 MFLOPS/watt. Next generation is expected to achieve 75,000 MFLOPS/watt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Adapteva has announced the Epiphany-V, a 1024-core 64-bit RISC processor intended to achieve 75 GFLOPS/Watt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>US Patent <a class="external text" href="http://patft.uspto.gov/netacgi/nph-Parser?Sect1=PTO2&amp;Sect2=HITOFF&amp;p=1&amp;u=%2Fnetahtml%2FPTO%2Fsearch-bool.html&amp;r=1&amp;f=G&amp;l=50&amp;co1=AND&amp;d=PTXT&amp;s1=matteo&amp;s2=gravina&amp;OS=matteo+AND+gravina&amp;RS=matteo+AND+gravina" rel="nofollow">10,020,436</a>, July 2018 claims three intervals of 100, 300, and 600 GFLOPS/Watt.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Green500_List">Green500 List</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performance_per_watt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Green500 List">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Green500 list ranks computers from the <a href="/wiki/TOP500" title="TOP500">TOP500</a> list of supercomputers in terms of <a href="/wiki/Electrical_efficiency" title="Electrical efficiency">energy efficiency</a>, typically measured as <a href="/wiki/LINPACK" title="LINPACK">LINPACK</a> FLOPS per watt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of  November 2012<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Performance_per_watt&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Appro_International,_Inc." title="Appro International, Inc.">Appro International, Inc.</a> Xtreme-X supercomputer (<i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Beacon_(supercomputer)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Beacon (supercomputer) (page does not exist)">Beacon</a></i>) topped the Green500 list with 2499 LINPACK MFLOPS/W.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nics_green500_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-nics_green500-18">[18]</a></sup>  Beacon is deployed by NICS of the University of Tennessee and is a GreenBlade GB824M, <a href='/wiki/Xeon#E5-16xx/24xx/26xx/46xx-series_"Sandy_Bridge-EP"' title="Xeon">Xeon E5-2670</a> based, eight cores (8C), 2.6 GHz, Infiniband FDR, Intel Xeon Phi 5110P computer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-top_green500_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-top_green500-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of  June 2013<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Performance_per_watt&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the <a href="/wiki/Eurotech_(company)" title="Eurotech (company)">Eurotech</a> supercomputer Eurora at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cineca" title="Cineca">Cineca</a> topped the Green500 list with 3208 LINPACK MFLOPS/W.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eurotech_Eurora_tops_green_500_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eurotech_Eurora_tops_green_500-20">[20]</a></sup>  The Cineca Eurora supercomputer is equipped with two Intel Xeon E5-2687W CPUs and two PCI-e connected NVIDIA Tesla K20 accelerators per node. Water cooling and electronics design allows for very high densities to be reached with a peak performance of 350 TFLOPS per rack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-top_green500Aurora_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-top_green500Aurora-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of  November 2014<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Performance_per_watt&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the L-CSC supercomputer of the <a href="/wiki/Helmholtz_Association_of_German_Research_Centres" title="Helmholtz Association of German Research Centres">Helmholtz Association</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gesellschaft_f%C3%BCr_Schwerionenforschung" title="Gesellschaft für Schwerionenforschung">GSI</a> in <a href="/wiki/Darmstadt" title="Darmstadt">Darmstadt</a> Germany topped the Green500 list with 5271 MFLOPS/W and was the first cluster to surpass an efficiency of 5 GFLOPS/W. It runs on <a href="/wiki/List_of_Intel_Xeon_microprocessors#Xeon_E5-26xx_v2_(dual-processor).5B32.5D" title="List of Intel Xeon microprocessors">Intel Xeon E5-2690</a> Processors with the <a href="/wiki/Ivy_Bridge_(microarchitecture)" title="Ivy Bridge (microarchitecture)">Intel Ivy Bridge</a> Architecture and <a href="/wiki/List_of_AMD_graphics_processing_units#FirePro_Server_Series" title="List of AMD graphics processing units">AMD FirePro S9150</a> GPU Accelerators. It uses in rack watercooling and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cooling_Tower" title="Cooling Tower">Cooling Towers</a> to reduce the energy required for cooling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Green500_List_-_November_2014_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Green500_List_-_November_2014-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of  August 2015<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Performance_per_watt&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Shoubu_supercomputer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Shoubu supercomputer (page does not exist)">Shoubu supercomputer</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RIKEN" title="RIKEN">RIKEN</a> outside <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> Japan tops the Green500 list with 7032 MFLOPS/W. The top three supercomputers of the list use PEZY-SC accelerators (<a href="/wiki/General-purpose_computing_on_graphics_processing_units" title="General-purpose computing on graphics processing units">GPU</a>-like that use <a href="/wiki/OpenCL" title="OpenCL">OpenCL</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/PEZY_Computing" title="PEZY Computing">PEZY Computing</a> with 1024 cores each and 6-7 GFLOPS/W efficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="GPU_efficiency">GPU efficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performance_per_watt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: GPU efficiency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Graphics_processing_unit" title="Graphics processing unit">Graphics processing units</a> (GPU) have continued to increase in energy usage, while CPUs designers have recently focused on improving performance per watt.  High performance GPUs may draw large amount of power and hence, intelligent techniques are required to manage GPU power consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Measures like <a href="/wiki/3DMark" title="3DMark">3DMark2006 score</a> per watt can help identify more efficient GPUs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> However that may not adequately incorporate efficiency in typical use, where much time is spent doing less demanding tasks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>With modern GPUs, energy usage is an important constraint on the maximum computational capabilities that can be achieved. GPU designs are usually highly scalable, allowing the manufacturer to put multiple chips on the same video card, or to use multiple video cards that work in parallel. Peak performance of any system is essentially limited by the amount of power it can draw and the amount of heat it can dissipate. Consequently, performance per watt of a GPU design translates directly into peak performance of a system that uses that design.
</p><p>Since GPUs may also be used for some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPGPU" title="GPGPU">general purpose computation</a>, sometimes their performance is measured in terms also applied to CPUs, such as FLOPS per watt.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Challenges">Challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Performance_per_watt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While performance per watt is useful, absolute power requirements are also important.  Claims of improved performance per watt may be used to mask increasing power demands.  For instance, though newer generation GPU architectures may provide better performance per watt, continued performance increases can negate the gains in efficiency, and the GPUs continue to consume large amounts of power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Benchmarks that measure power under heavy load may not adequately reflect typical efficiency.  For instance, 3DMark stresses the 3D performance of a GPU, but many computers spend most of their time doing less intense display tasks (idle, 2D tasks, displaying video).  So the 2D or idle efficiency of the graphics system may be at least as significant for overall energy efficiency.  Likewise, systems that spend much of their time in standby or <a href="/wiki/Standby_power" title="Standby power">soft off</a> are not adequately characterized by just efficiency under load.  To help address this some benchmarks, like <a href="/wiki/SPECpower" title="SPECpower">SPECpower</a>, include measurements at a series of load levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>The efficiency of some electrical components, such as <a href="/wiki/Voltage_regulator" title="Voltage regulator">voltage regulators</a>, decreases with increasing temperature, so the power used may increase with temperature.  Power supplies, motherboards, and some video cards are some of the subsystems affected by this.  So their power draw  may depend on temperature, and the temperature or temperature dependence should be noted when measuring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPCRNewLevels_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-SPCRNewLevels-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>Performance per watt also typically does not include full <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_cycle_assessment" title="Life cycle assessment">life-cycle costs</a>.  Since computer manufacturing is energy intensive, and computers often have a relatively short lifespan, energy and materials involved in production, distribution, <a href="/wiki/Electronic_waste" title="Electronic waste">disposal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Computer_recycling" title="Computer recycling">recycling</a> often make up significant portions of their cost, energy use, and environmental impact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Energy required for climate control of the computer's surroundings is often not counted in the wattage calculation, but it can be significant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-impLowPower_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-impLowPower-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
