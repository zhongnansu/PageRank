<p><b>Green waste,</b> also called agricultural waste, is <a href="/wiki/Biodegradable_waste" title="Biodegradable waste">biodegradable waste</a> that  can be composed of <a href="/wiki/Compost" title="Compost">compostable</a> wastes, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grass" title="Grass">grass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flower</a> cuttings, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hedge_(barrier)" title="Hedge (barrier)">hedge</a> trimmings, fallen leaves, dead plant matter, as well as domestic and commercial <a href="/wiki/Food_waste" title="Food waste">food wastes</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup>. The differentiation <i>green waste</i> identifies it as waste that is high in <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, opposed to <a href="/wiki/Brown_waste" title="Brown waste"><i>brown</i> waste</a>, which is primarily composed of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon compounds</a>.  
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collection_of_Green_waste">Collection of Green waste</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_waste&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Collection of Green waste">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Green waste can be collected via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Municipal" title="Municipal">municipal</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curbside_collection" title="Curbside collection">curbside collection</a> schemes or through private <a href="/wiki/Waste_management" title="Waste management">waste management</a> businesses. Many communities, especially in the United Kingdom, have initiated green waste <a href="/wiki/Recycling_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Recycling in the United Kingdom">recycling and collection programs</a> in order to decrease the amount of <a href="/wiki/Biodegradable_waste" title="Biodegradable waste">biodegradable materials in landfills</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> . Communities are provided with, or can provide their own, compost receptacles that they fill with plant and food remains, which are then emptied on a regular basis. Programs such as this allow communities to be an active part of composting their green waste which allows them to play an active role in decreasing the amount of food being dumped into local and regional landfills<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup>. 
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Veg_waste_Hyd_Market.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Veg_waste_Hyd_Market.jpg/220px-Veg_waste_Hyd_Market.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Veg_waste_Hyd_Market.jpg/330px-Veg_waste_Hyd_Market.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Veg_waste_Hyd_Market.jpg/440px-Veg_waste_Hyd_Market.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Veg_waste_Hyd_Market.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Vegetable waste being dumped in a market in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyderabad,_India" title="Hyderabad, India">Hyderabad, India</a>.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">Biogas</a> captured from biodegradable green waste can be used as <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuel</a>. Green waste can be used as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-food_crop" title="Non-food crop">non-food crop</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Cellulosic_ethanol" title="Cellulosic ethanol">cellulosic ethanol</a>. It can also help us reduce the usage of petroleum gases which produce harmful gases on burning
</p>
