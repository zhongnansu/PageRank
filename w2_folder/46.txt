<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Green home</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Green_affordable_housing" title="Green affordable housing">Green affordable housing</a></div>
<p>A <b>green home</b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/House" title="House">house</a> designed to be <a href="/wiki/Environmentally_friendly" title="Environmentally friendly">environmentally friendly</a> and sustainable. And also focuses on the efficient use of "<a href="/wiki/Low-energy_house" title="Low-energy house">energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_conservation" title="Water conservation">water</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_architecture" title="Sustainable architecture">building materials</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> You can easily calculate the carbon footprint of your home by following the example <a class="external text" href="https://greenzerocarbonhome.com/blog/" rel="nofollow">here</a>. Green homes have become more prevalent as green affordable housing emerges. Green affordable housing shares the same aims as traditional green homes but emphasise affordability, inevitably reducing the environmental friendliness of the home.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The idea of affordable green homes is a criticized topic with some critics<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (September 2016)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> saying that it is a fallacy and that green homes are inherently more costly than traditional homes. A green home uses generally less water, less electricity but more on recycled objects, on the materials of the house and when in use.
</p><p>One of the biggest contributors to the environmental impact of a house is its carbon footprint. The carbon footprint is caused by burning fossil fuels (such as natural gas, coal, heating oil or propane) to heat the home and by burning fossil fuels in power stations to generate the electricity used by the house. A "green home" a "zero carbon home" and a "net-zero energy home" all mean essentially the same thing which is that a house uses so little energy for heating and electricity that all that energy is generated at the house. This usually means the electricity is generated by solar panels. Several architects have reported success in building zero carbon homes such as these examples:

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Components">Components</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_home&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Components">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The parts that make up a green home are not universally agreed upon. There are no national standards on what constitutes a green remodel, beyond non-profit certification. In general, a green home is a type of house that is built or remodeled in order to conserve "energy or water; improve indoor air quality; use sustainable, recycled or used materials; and produce less waste in the process." This may include buying more <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">energy-efficient</a> <a href="/wiki/Home_appliance" title="Home appliance">appliances</a> or utilizing specific building materials that are more efficient in keeping both cool and heated air inside the structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_home&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1963 hamari dost created the original major modern turn to the <a href="/wiki/Green_building" title="Green building">green building</a> movement began in the 1970s, after the price of oil began to increase sharply. In response, researchers began to look into more energy efficient processes, following in the wake of the earlier environmental movement. Many different organizations were founded in the 1990s in order to promote green buildings and some organizations intended to improve the knowledge of consumers so that they could have more green homes. The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=International_Code_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="International Code Council (page does not exist)">International Code Council</a> and the <a href="/wiki/National_Association_of_Home_Builders" title="National Association of Home Builders">National Association of Home Builders</a> began the paperwork in 2006 in order to create a "voluntary green home building standard".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Energy_Policy_Act_of_2005" title="Energy Policy Act of 2005">Energy Policy Act</a> was legalized in 2005, which allowed tax reductions for homeowners that could show their utilization of energy efficient changes to their homes, such as <a href="/wiki/Solar_panel" title="Solar panel">solar panels</a> and other solar-powered devices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Certifications">Certifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_home&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Certifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are various types of certifications globally that declare a home as a Green home. The <a href="/wiki/U.S._Green_Building_Council" title="U.S. Green Building Council">U.S. Green Building Council</a> is an example of a type of organization that gives out green home certifications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Its certification is titled <a href="/wiki/Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design" title="Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design">Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design</a>. The factors that it considers in its certification system include "the site location, use of energy and water, incorporation of healthier building and insulation materials, recycling, use of renewable energy, and protection of natural resources".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The National Association of Home Builders also independently created its Model Green Home Building Guidelines during the same period as a type of certification, along with a number of other programs for various utilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Indian Green Building Council (IGBC), part of the Confederation of Indian Industry (CII) was formed in the year 2001. The council offers a wide array of services which include developing new green building rating programmes, certification services and green building training programmes.
</p>
