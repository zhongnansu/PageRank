<p><b>Green affordable housing</b> is <a href="/wiki/Affordable_housing" title="Affordable housing">reasonably priced housing</a> that incorporates sustainable features. The phenomenon has become increasingly common in the United States with the adoption of state and local policies that favor or require green building practices for publicly owned or funded buildings. Potential benefits of green affordable housing include lower energy cost burden and improved health.  One challenge to green affordable housing is the tendency to overlook long-term benefits in the face of higher upfront cost.  The challenge for green housing advocates is to see to the <a href="/wiki/Whole-life_cost" title="Whole-life cost">life cycle cost</a> of the building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_Green_Blog_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Green_Blog-1">[1]</a></sup> Many affordable housing projects already find it a challenge to raise capital to finance basic affordable housing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwartz_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schwartz-2">[2]</a></sup> Green affordable housing has taken form in traditionally wooden homes and most recently with 'upcycling' shipping containers. 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_affordable_housing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although there is no single definition for what constitutes a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_Building" title="Green Building">Green Building</a>, some elements recur in describing the concept. A Green Building can thus be defined as a high-performance building designed, built, operated and disposed of in a resource-efficient manner with the aim to minimize the overall (negative) impact on the built environment, human health and the natural environment. Some examples of green building features are choice of site and orientation, efficient use of materials and resources, indoor environmental quality and innovation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Affordable_Housing" title="Affordable Housing">Affordable Housing</a> in the US is defined as dwelling units whose total housing costs fit within the budget of households at the local median income level.  Affordability is commonly defined as not spending more than 30 percent of household income on housing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EE_in_AffHousing_guide_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-EE_in_AffHousing_guide-4">[4]</a></sup>  Given the higher exposure of low-income households and the need for public assistance, the most salient features of green affordable housing are <a href="/wiki/Domestic_energy_consumption" title="Domestic energy consumption">energy use</a>, material use/durability and a healthy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indoor_environment#Indoor_environment" title="Indoor environment">indoor environment</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_affordable_housing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Publicly sponsored affordable housing projects use public funds or tax breaks in order to increase the stock of housing available to low income people. In addition, these projects often seek to improve the quality of the stock, in order to offer "decent, safe, sanitary, and affordable living environments".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Housing_Act_of_1968_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Housing_Act_of_1968-5">[5]</a></sup>   Green Affordable Housing projects add to those goals by lowering the lifetime cost of units and improving the quality.  The concept of the <a href="/wiki/Triple_bottom_line" title="Triple bottom line">triple bottom line</a> defines sustainability to include economics, social equity and the environment. Affordable housing already addresses economics and social equity. Sustainability measures obviously help the environment, but they can help the economics and social equity of housing projects as well. Housing units with a better design can improve health outcomes. With lower utility costs and improved health outcomes, the projects are more beneficial to low-income households, who are particularly exposed to poor housing quality and increases in general life costs, e.g. for utilities or medical expenses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwartz_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schwartz-2">[2]</a></sup> Economically, sustainable design lowers the lifetime cost of a building.  Since the preservation of affordable housing is sensitive to public funding, making affordable housing greener is a logic step to reducing long-term costs and increasing stability.  Green design increases the economics, social equity, and environmental impact of affordable housing projects.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics_of_the_existing_affordable_housing_stock">Characteristics of the existing affordable housing stock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_affordable_housing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Characteristics of the existing affordable housing stock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The affordable housing stock varies in terms of ownership, type and quality. However, financing of affordable housing has in most cases been constrained which means quality of affordable housing construction projects often has been compromised.
</p><p>Policy programs that require green building standards in new construction will contribute to greening the affordable housing  stock. However, new construction, whether green or conventional building, will in general be of higher quality, more efficient and healthier than older buildings. In contrast, the existing affordable stock constitutes a challenge from many perspectives. According to the <a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Healthy_Housing" title="National Center for Healthy Housing">National Center for Healthy Housing</a> (NCHH) 5.7 million of American families live in substandard housing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCHH_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCHH-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Existing low-cost units tend to be older than the housing stock in general. For example, in 1995, 68% of the affordable rental housing stock was constructed before 1970 and another 21% were built before 1980. A considerable share of the older affordable rental stock has been removed from the market since 1995, but many of the remaining units are in older buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JCHS_Rental_Housing_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-JCHS_Rental_Housing-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JCHS_challenges_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-JCHS_challenges-8">[8]</a></sup> Due for example to differences in building standards, technical development and historical maintenance, the older building stock is generally of worse quality than newer buildings. Deficits include cold down draft, poor insulation and inefficient windows and appliances.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Utility_expenditures_for_low-income_households">Utility expenditures for low-income households</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_affordable_housing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Utility expenditures for low-income households">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Poor housing quality and low incomes contributes to <a href="/wiki/Fuel_poverty" title="Fuel poverty">fuel poverty</a>, a phenomenomen that is more common among lower-income household than among households in general.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEA_fuel_poverty_report_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-IEA_fuel_poverty_report-9">[9]</a></sup> Over the past decade home energy costs have risen in American homes in general.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Energy.gov_Prices_and_trends_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Energy.gov_Prices_and_trends-10">[10]</a></sup> For low-income households this cost increase is even more pressing, for which home energy expenditures can be as high as a fifth of household income and more than a quarter of total housing costs. For that reason, improving energy efficiency has been pointed out as one of the challenges for American housing by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harvard-MIT_Joint_Center_for_Urban_Studies" title="Harvard-MIT Joint Center for Urban Studies">Joint Center for Housing Studies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JCHS_challenges_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-JCHS_challenges-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Energy expenditures for low income households have risen over the past decades, and the energy burden (energy expenditures as share of household income) has increased since 1997. Mean residential energy expenditures increased by 27 percent from 2001 to 2005, to $1,522, and by almost 20 percent to $1,822 by fiscal year 2009. The mean group residential energy burden (the ratio between mean energy expenditures and mean income for a given set of households) for households with incomes at or below 150 percent of <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Health_and_Human_Services" title="United States Department of Health and Human Services">U.S. Department of Health and Human Services</a>' (HHS) poverty guidelines rose from 10.7 percent in 1997 to 13.5 percent in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LIHEAP_report_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-LIHEAP_report-11">[11]</a></sup>  Utilities are included in housing expenditures in calculations of housing affordability. However, higher energy costs mean that rents will be higher and/or that more assistance is needed to cover the difference between actual and "affordable" energy expenditures, assistance that already is insufficient to cover this gap.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Home_Energy_Affordability_Gap_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Home_Energy_Affordability_Gap-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Housing_conditions_and_health_problems_in_affordable_housing">Housing conditions and health problems in affordable housing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_affordable_housing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Housing conditions and health problems in affordable housing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The deterioration of the existing stock has contributed to <a href="/wiki/Housing_quality_and_health_outcomes_in_the_United_States" title="Housing quality and health outcomes in the United States">health problems</a> for those living in affordable housing.  Green updates to existing units can improve health outcomes.  In addition, new housing units that address the causes of health issues improve the sustainability of the house.  These units will have a lower lifetime building cost (because they won't need updates) and cost less to inhabitants (because of improved health outcomes).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Costs_and_benefits_of_Green_Affordable_Housing">Costs and benefits of Green Affordable Housing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_affordable_housing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Costs and benefits of Green Affordable Housing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Upfront_cost">Upfront cost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_affordable_housing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Upfront cost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Depending on the extent of sustainability design, the <a href="/wiki/Green_building#Cost_and_payoff" title="Green building">costs of green building</a> often imply an initial <a href="/wiki/Capital_cost" title="Capital cost">capital cost</a> premium compared to the cost of conventional buildings. Newer technology and the requirement of special labor skills are examples of <a href="/wiki/Cost_driver" title="Cost driver">cost drivers</a> in green <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">building projects</a>. However, careful planning and deliberate choices can minimize these upfront costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EBN_1999_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-EBN_1999-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blueprint_Greening_Affordable_Housing_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blueprint_Greening_Affordable_Housing-15">[15]</a></sup> It is expected that the costs of building green will decrease over time, thanks to experience and the development of products and services.
</p><p>There are many new types of products in the market that offer green affordable housing. The type of area the housing is located in (rural versus suburban versus urban) can also play a big role in choosing the best resource in building affordable green housing. Other factors include the location of housing and the type of transportation options that it supports. "Dispersed, low-density housing often cannot support viable public transit, biking, or pedestrian options, all but making auto ownership a necessary cost. The true cost of housing, therefore, is the combined cost of housing plus transportation."  <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Currently, one third of the human population resides in earthen houses; in developing countries this figure is more than one half. To build shelter in these developing countries with traditional industrial building materials (i.e. It has proven impossible to fulfill the immense requirements for shelter in the developing countries with traditional industrial building materials i.e. brick, concrete and steel).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Building_with_Earth:_Design_and_Technology_of_a_Sustainable_Architecture_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Building_with_Earth:_Design_and_Technology_of_a_Sustainable_Architecture-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_cycle_cost">Life cycle cost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_affordable_housing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Life cycle cost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Funds available to the development and maintenance of affordable housing are scarce and, in order to maximize the supply of housing, low construction costs are often favored in choosing projects. However, one of the main arguments for green affordable housing is that a higher upfront cost for construction/renovation can be motivated by a lower operating cost over the life-time of the building, i.e. a lower <a href="/wiki/Whole-life_cost" title="Whole-life cost">life-cycle cost</a>. If the initial investment leads to lower <a href="/wiki/Operating_cost" title="Operating cost">operating costs</a> and/or higher durability the higher upfront capital cost needed for construction or renovation can be justified. This is especially true considering that public funding and tax breaks (like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LIHTC" title="LIHTC">low-income housing tax credit</a>) for housing often require a longer term perspective from its builders. However, the need to preserve affordability of housing sometimes restricts value enhancement over time and may thus be a barrier to investing in green features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blueprint_Greening_Affordable_Housing_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blueprint_Greening_Affordable_Housing-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits">Benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_affordable_housing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>General green building benefits include environmental, economic and social benefits. The potential <i>environmental</i> benefits are enhancement and protection of biodiversity and ecosystems; improved air and water quality; reduced waste stream and conservation and restoration of natural resources. <i>Economically</i> potential benefits include lower operation costs; a market for green products and services; enhanced occupier productivity and the optimization of life-cycle performance. <i>Social</i> potential benefits include improved health and comfort for residents, minimizing the burden on local infrastructure and improved aesthetics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-US_EPA_GB_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-US_EPA_GB-18">[18]</a></sup> Affordable housing projects emphasize all three benefits, because they replace deteriorating stock, which harm the environment most and provide the least economic and social benefit.
</p><p>The potential benefits of green affordable housing projects can be divided into direct, regional and global benefits. The direct benefits include lower utility costs, a healthier indoor environment and increased durability. Regional benefits include support for solid waste management and improved water quality. Globally benefits may include reduced energy use and carbon footprint and lower material use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Global_Green_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Global_Green-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Energy efficiency and other sustainability improvements for low-income households have external benefits as well.  Governments, energy-providers, property owners, and taxpayers all experience direct financial, indirect financial, and social welfare and livelihood co-benefits from green affordable housing projects. The improved quality and energy efficiency of these projects result in direct financial benefit in the form of reduced energy bills, reduced outlays for assistance to low-income households, reduced rates of shut-offs and thus reduced related administrative costs. Indirect financial co-benefits accrue to residents, property owners and tax-payers and society as a whole. Examples of these co-benefits are increased property values and economic activity, increased home and fire safety and reduced involuntary mobility for low-income households. Potential co-benefits for social welfare and livelihood include improved comfort, health and safety for the residents, community pride and improved health outcomes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IEA_fuel_poverty_report_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-IEA_fuel_poverty_report-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measures_and_Strategies_to_achieve_Green_Affordable_Housing">Measures and Strategies to achieve Green Affordable Housing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_affordable_housing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Measures and Strategies to achieve Green Affordable Housing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Governments, local housing authorities, and other interested parties can either begin new affordable housing projects with sustainable design, or retrofit the existing stock.  While new projects require a larger upfront cost, these projects provide a greater return on investment than retrofitting. Initiatives for Green Affordable Housing are becoming increasingly more common, supported for example by The Green Affordable Housing Coalition<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GAHC_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-GAHC-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Standards_for_New_Housing">Standards for New Housing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_affordable_housing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Standards for New Housing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To ensure the energy efficiency of affordable housing, state and local governments have implemented sustainability standards.  Washington was the first state to enact green building legislation in the U.S., with its Evergreen Sustainability Development Standard.  This standard requires that all buildings that receive public funding meet “79 criteria that safeguard health and safety, increase durability, promote sustainable living, preserve the environment, and increase energy and <a href="/wiki/Water_efficiency" title="Water efficiency">water efficiency</a>.”  This applies to all publicly subsidized affordable housing, but not private projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>The potential problem with sustainability standards is that projects will be more costly and projects will become cost prohibitive.  While units that are built will be higher efficiency, the number of available units could decrease because of costs. A 2009 study prepared by Davis Langdon examines the effect of green standards on the costs of affordable housing projects in Seattle and Portland. This study compared “standard housing projects” (i.e. projects with no explicit sustainability goals) and projects attempting to achieve Built Green or LEED certification. The study includes both public and private affordable housing projects. At the time of the study, both cities had high sustainability standards for public projects, and Washington has only increased that standard since then. Construction cost analysis (in terms of cost per square foot) showed no significant difference between standard projects and green-rated projects. The study is limited by a small sample size, because of which they couldn’t compare public and privately funded projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Updating_Existing_Housing_Stock">Updating Existing Housing Stock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_affordable_housing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Updating Existing Housing Stock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many jurisdictions offer options for updating the energy efficiency of existing housing stock.  The <a href="/wiki/Weatherization#US_Weatherization_Assistance_Program" title="Weatherization">US Weatherization Assistance Program</a> (WAP) offers the chance for low-income families to improve their housing in order to reduce energy consumption and costs.  WAP uses funds from the federal Department of Energy.  Federal funds are distributed by state offices and services are provided by local agencies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>  WAP has suffered because of increased competition for federal funds.  The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Recovery_and_Reinvestment_Act" title="American Recovery and Reinvestment Act">American Recovery and Reinvestment Act</a> (ARRA) designated funds for WAP.  The program as a whole was criticized for its failure to distribute these funds effectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Energy-efficient appliances are another method for households to decrease energy use.  Low-income households have trouble realizing this gain energy efficiency.  Energy-efficient appliances require a large up-front investment, and savings will not be realized for many years.  Local grantees have the option to use Weatherization funds to support appliance purchases.  However, unlike weatherization programs, the gains in household budgets are often not worth the investment.  A 2010 study examined the households' purchasing habits in regard to high-efficiency appliances.  Using household-level data, this study determines that homeowners are significantly more likely to own energy efficient large appliances, such as refrigerators or washing machines. However, the results show that the cost of this problem is negligible compared to overall energy consumption. This finding is important considering the increasing efficiency standards for household appliances.  A higher standard may put simple appliances out of the price range of low income households, while not offering much energy relief for households that do own them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evaluation_of_Green_Affordable_Housing_policies_and_incentives">Evaluation of Green Affordable Housing policies and incentives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_affordable_housing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Evaluation of Green Affordable Housing policies and incentives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As Green Affordable Housing still is a rather marginal phenomenon the outcomes of policy programs and specific interventions remain to be examined and confirmed. However, a number of studies are underway to examine the effects on health, energy savings and project costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Other research initiatives seek to develop Green Affordable Housing while continuously evaluating the efforts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Affordable_Housing_Initiative_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Affordable_Housing_Initiative-30">[30]</a></sup>  Some early learning point to the importance of training, outreach and resource coordination for the success of a green affordable housing program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
