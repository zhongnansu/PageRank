<p><i><b>Greenhouse</b></i> is a 1997 album by British pop group <a href="/wiki/Brotherhood_of_Man" title="Brotherhood of Man">Brotherhood of Man</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The album was released independently by the group themselves on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compact_cassette" title="Compact cassette">cassette</a> only and was available to buy at their shows.  The songs contained included a mix of re-recordings of their own hit singles and cover versions.  It also contained one new track "Greenhouse" - a song recorded a few years earlier in 1991 with Dutch producer <a href="/wiki/Eddy_Ouwens" title="Eddy Ouwens">Eddy Ouwens</a>, but never released.  The song was later included on the download-only album <i>The Definitive Collection</i> in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  Brotherhood of Man themselves were credited as producers for the rest of the album (member <a href="/wiki/Lee_Sheriden" title="Lee Sheriden">Lee Sheriden</a> had been the group's musical director on their recordings for many years).  The line-up of tracks formed part of their then current live show.
</p><p>Among the cover versions were the <a href="/wiki/Prince_(musician)" title="Prince (musician)">Prince</a> song, "<a href="/wiki/1999_(song)" title="1999 (song)">1999</a>", the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beatles" title="Beatles">Beatles</a>' "<a href="/wiki/Got_to_Get_You_into_My_Life" title="Got to Get You into My Life">Got to Get You into My Life</a>" (albeit based on the 1970s hit version by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_Wind_and_Fire" title="Earth Wind and Fire">Earth Wind and Fire</a>), <a href="/wiki/Huey_Lewis_and_the_News" title="Huey Lewis and the News">Huey Lewis and the News</a> 1987 hit  "<a href="/wiki/Hip_to_Be_Square" title="Hip to Be Square">Hip to Be Square</a>" and <a href="/wiki/Foreigner_(band)" title="Foreigner (band)">Foreigner's</a> "<a href="/wiki/Juke_Box_Hero" title="Juke Box Hero">Juke Box Hero</a>".  The latter of these featured a heavy rock sound that was unusual for Brotherhood of Man, and at over five minutes; the longest song they ever recorded.
</p>
