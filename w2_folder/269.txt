<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Evergreen Review</i></h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><i><b>Evergreen Review</b></i> is a U.S.-based literary magazine directed by editor-in-chief <a href="/wiki/Dale_Peck" title="Dale Peck">Dale Peck</a>.  The <i>Evergreen Review</i> was founded by <a href="/wiki/Barney_Rosset" title="Barney Rosset">Barney Rosset</a>, publisher of  <a href="/wiki/Grove_Press" title="Grove Press">Grove Press</a>. It existed in print from 1957<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> through 1973, and was re-launched online in 1998, and again in 2017. Its lasting impact can be seen in the March–April 1960 issue, which included work by <a href="/wiki/Albert_Camus" title="Albert Camus">Albert Camus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Ferlinghetti" title="Lawrence Ferlinghetti">Lawrence Ferlinghetti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bertolt_Brecht" title="Bertolt Brecht">Bertolt Brecht</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LeRoi_Jones" title="LeRoi Jones">LeRoi Jones</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Edward_Albee" title="Edward Albee">Edward Albee</a>'s first play, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Zoo_Story" title="The Zoo Story">The Zoo Story</a></i>. The Camus piece was a reprint of "Reflections on the Guillotine", first published in English in the <i>Review</i> in 1957 and reprinted on this occasion as the magazine's "contribution to the worldwide debate on the problem of <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment" title="Capital punishment">capital punishment</a> and, more specifically, the case of <a href="/wiki/Caryl_Chessman" title="Caryl Chessman">Caryl Whittier Chessman</a>."
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writers">Writers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_Review&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Writers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Evergreen Review</i> debuted pivotal works by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Beckett" title="Samuel Beckett">Samuel Beckett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jorge_Luis_Borges" title="Jorge Luis Borges">Jorge Luis Borges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bukowski" title="Charles Bukowski">Charles Bukowski</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_S._Burroughs" title="William S. Burroughs">William S. Burroughs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marguerite_Duras" title="Marguerite Duras">Marguerite Duras</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean_Genet" title="Jean Genet">Jean Genet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Allen_Ginsberg" title="Allen Ginsberg">Allen Ginsberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/G%C3%BCnter_Grass" title="Günter Grass">Günter Grass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kerouac" title="Jack Kerouac">Jack Kerouac</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norman_Mailer" title="Norman Mailer">Norman Mailer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Miller" title="Henry Miller">Henry Miller</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Neruda" title="Pablo Neruda">Pablo Neruda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Nabokov" title="Vladimir Nabokov">Vladimir Nabokov</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frank_O%E2%80%99Hara" title="Frank O’Hara">Frank O’Hara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenzabur%C5%8D_%C5%8Ce" title="Kenzaburō Ōe">Kenzaburō Ōe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Octavio_Paz" title="Octavio Paz">Octavio Paz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harold_Pinter" title="Harold Pinter">Harold Pinter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Susan_Sontag" title="Susan Sontag">Susan Sontag</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Stoppard" title="Tom Stoppard">Tom Stoppard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Derek_Walcott" title="Derek Walcott">Derek Walcott</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_X" title="Malcolm X">Malcolm X</a>. United States Supreme Court Justice <a href="/wiki/William_O._Douglas" title="William O. Douglas">William O. Douglas</a> wrote a controversial piece for the magazine in 1969. Kerouac and Ginsberg regularly had their writing published in the magazine.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Illustrators">Illustrators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_Review&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Illustrators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although primarily a literary magazine, <i>Evergreen Review</i> always contained numerous illustrations. In its early years, these included a small number of <a href="/wiki/Cartoon" title="Cartoon">cartoons</a>. By the mid-1960s, many illustrations and photographs were of an <a href="/wiki/Erotica" title="Erotica">erotic</a> nature, including a serialized graphic novel, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Phoebe_Zeit-Geist" title="The Adventures of Phoebe Zeit-Geist">The Adventures of Phoebe Zeit-Geist</a></i> by writer <a href="/wiki/Michael_O%27Donoghue" title="Michael O'Donoghue">Michael O'Donoghue</a> and artist <a href="/wiki/Frank_Springer" title="Frank Springer">Frank Springer</a>. It was later published as a Grove Press hardcover in 1968 and trade paperback in 1969.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evergreen_evolution"><i>Evergreen</i> evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_Review&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Evergreen evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ken Jordan, writing in the introduction to <i>Evergreen Review Reader, 1957-1996</i>, described the counter-cultural contents and the impact of the publication on readers:
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Evergreenreview11.jpg"><img alt="Evergreenreview11.jpg" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="390" data-file-width="255" height="352" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/5c/Evergreenreview11.jpg/230px-Evergreenreview11.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/5/5c/Evergreenreview11.jpg 1.5x" width="230"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Evergreenreview11.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div>
<dl><dd>The first issue featured an essay by <a href="/wiki/Jean-Paul_Sartre" title="Jean-Paul Sartre">Jean-Paul Sartre</a> and an interview with the great New Orleans jazz drummer <a href="/wiki/Baby_Dodds" title="Baby Dodds">Baby Dodds</a>. It also included a story of <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Beckett" title="Samuel Beckett">Samuel Beckett</a>'s <i>Dante and the Lobster</i>, the first of his many appearances in <i>Evergreen's</i> pages; these continued through the last issue published.</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>The second issue was a landmark. A banner across the cover declared "San Francisco Scene," and inside held the first collection of work by the new Beat writers - including Lawrence Ferlinghetti, <a href="/wiki/Gary_Snyder" title="Gary Snyder">Gary Snyder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_McClure" title="Michael McClure">Michael McClure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philip_Whalen" title="Philip Whalen">Philip Whalen</a>, Jack Kerouac (before the publication of <i><a href="/wiki/On_the_Road" title="On the Road">On the Road</a></i>) and Allen Ginsberg, whose <i>Howl</i> had already been  published as a pamphlet by Ferlinghetti's press, <a href="/wiki/City_Lights" title="City Lights">City Lights</a>, and was confiscated by customs officials and faced trial for obscenity in San Francisco. The issue brought the Beats and <i>Evergreen Review</i> to the forefront of the American stage...</dd></dl>
<dl><dd><i>Evergreen</i> published writing that was literally counter to the culture, and if it was sexy, so much the better. In the context of the time, sex was politics, and the powers-that-be made the suppression of sexuality a political issue. The court battles that Grove Press fought for the legal publication of <i><a href="/wiki/Lady_Chatterley%27s_Lover" title="Lady Chatterley's Lover">Lady Chatterly's Lover</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Cancer_(novel)" title="Tropic of Cancer (novel)">Tropic of Cancer</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Naked_Lunch" title="Naked Lunch">Naked Lunch</a></i>, and for the legal distribution of the film <i><a href="/wiki/I_Am_Curious_(Yellow)" title="I Am Curious (Yellow)">I Am Curious: Yellow</a></i>, spilled onto the pages of <i>Evergreen Review</i>, and in 1964, an issue of <i>Evergreen</i> itself was confiscated in New York State by the <a href="/wiki/Nassau_County,_New_York" title="Nassau County, New York">Nassau County</a> District Attorney on obscenity charges...</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>All of this was done on a shoestring budget by a tiny staff. Barney Rosset started the magazine with editor <a href="/wiki/Don_Allen" title="Don Allen">Don Allen</a> and Fred Jordan, who was nominally the business manager in its early days. <a href="/wiki/Richard_Seaver" title="Richard Seaver">Richard Seaver</a> joined the editorial team with the ninth issue, and Don Allen stepped back to become a contributing editor. Publication increased from quarterly to bimonthly to, in the late sixties, monthly, and the format changed from trade paperback to a full-sized, glossy magazine attaining a subscription base of some 40,000 copies and a newsstand circulation of 100,000. The final print issue, number 96, came out in 1973.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Online">Online</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_Review&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Online">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The original <i>Evergreen Review</i> ceased publication in 1973, but the magazine was revived in 1998 in an online edition edited by founder <a href="/wiki/Barney_Rosset" title="Barney Rosset">Barney Rosset</a> and his wife Astrid Myers. The online version ceased publication in 2013 and was revived in March 2017 with <a href="/wiki/OR_Books" title="OR Books">OR Books</a> co-publisher John Oakes as publisher and writer and critic <a href="/wiki/Dale_Peck" title="Dale Peck">Dale Peck</a> as editor-in-chief.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
